# See and Release the Permanence
### who you are beyond your roles
*Method VS · See and Release · Vivianne dos Santos*

> *You are not the leaves that fall with the seasons. You are the tree that remains when they drop.*

## Who this book is for

This book is for you, if there is a sentence you have been saying about yourself for so long that it no longer feels like a sentence, it feels like your face. The strong one. The responsible one. The one who endures. The one who fixes things. The one others turn to when the ground shakes, and whom no one turns to on the good days. It is for you if, at some quiet hour, perhaps late at night when the house has fallen asleep, you feel that sentence tightening, like clothes belonging to a body you no longer have, and you hold it anyway with both hands, because who would you be without it.

It is for you if changing course, changing your mind, changing your life, feels like a betrayal. If it costs you to ask for help or to show yourself fragile, because your place was always the one who does not collapse. If your identity is so glued to a function, mother, daughter, professional, the carer, the one who decides, that the mere idea of letting it go opens a clench of emptiness in you, as though releasing the role meant dying a little.

This is not a self-help book with easy promises, nor a manual for becoming someone else. It is the opposite of that. I will not ask you to change who you are. I will ask you only to look, with more honesty and less harshness, at the form you mistook yourself for, and to discover, beneath it, what in you never needed defending because it could never be lost.

There is a question that has stayed with me for years. Why does someone lucid, who sees clearly that a certain rigidity is suffocating her, go on wearing it every single day? For a long time I called this weakness, lack of character, not wanting it badly enough. I looked at my own cycles and concluded that, if I were more aware, I would stop tripping over the same stone. And still I repeated. That was when I understood I was looking in the wrong place. The problem was not that I could not see. It was that I saw and, even so, could not. We do not repeat for lack of will, nor of awareness. We repeat because, at some point, that was the most intelligent answer a body could find in order to survive, to belong, to be loved. Awareness learns quickly, from a single sentence. The body learns slowly, and unlearns slower still. That is why knowing is not enough, and that is why this book will not ask you to know more, but to see better.

I want this to be clear from the very first page, because it is the opposite of almost everything you will be told: there is nothing in you to fix. You are not broken. You will not find here a method to repair yourself, because nothing is faulty. You will find a way to see what makes you repeat, and to release, slowly, what no longer serves you, so that what you always were may finally have room to breathe. One thing at a time, without haste, without noise.

I do not write to you from a place of arrival. I write from a path still under way, my own. I am someone who clung for years to an image of herself, who confused rigidity with strength and function with identity, and who came to need names for what she felt. I still study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as someone collecting certainties, but as someone who needs to understand. Everything I share here passed first through me, and only then did I recognise it in what echoes in so many women who walk the same circles, convinced they are the only ones.

This is one of seven veils of a method I call Method VS, See and Release. The initials are mine, Vivianne dos Santos, but they are also the only two movements of the whole path: see what holds you, release what makes you repeat. You can read this book without ever having read the mother book, *The Seven Veils*, and use it whole. It is a short, deep manual, meant to be read in one breath and carried into life, which is where everything is decided. Read it slowly. Underline. Go back. And, above all, carry the method into your days, because it is there, and not on these pages, that the unravelling happens.

There is a condition without which none of this works, and I want to make it clear before anything else: tenderness toward yourself. Most of us learned to change by force of harshness, of demand, of inner criticism. We point out what is missing, we scold ourselves as no one from outside would dare to. And then we are surprised that nothing truly changes. Harshness is itself a veil, perhaps the one that dresses all the others, and with it on there is no seeing that is not judgement. So, whenever along these pages you catch yourself judging yourself for what you see, swap harshness for curiosity, shame for tenderness. Treat yourself as you would treat your dearest friend learning this. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself, and the rest becomes possible.

A word about how to read this, because the way you read is part of the method. Do not read in a hurry, in one sitting, to reach the end. This is not a book to finish, it is a book to inhabit. Read slowly, let each part work in you for a few days, notice where, in your concrete life, the pattern appears. Do the practices, even the ones that seem too small, because it is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here. And come back. Come back to these pages in a few months and reread them, and you will find them different, because you will be different. The books that matter are not read once. They accompany us, and tell us new things at each age of the soul.

## The veil of permanence

You introduce yourself, and the same sentence always comes out. Your name, what you do, whose daughter you are, what you have already lost, what you became good at. You have said it so many times that it stopped being an introduction and became an outline, and inside that outline you learned to fit. You are the strong one. You are the responsible one. You are the one who endures, who does not collapse, the one others turn to when the ground shakes. And there is some hour, perhaps late at night when the house has fallen asleep and there is no one left needing you, when you feel that sentence tightening, like clothes belonging to a body you no longer have. But you hold it with both hands, because who would you be without it. This is the first veil, and the most stubborn: the belief that you are a fixed thing, an identity you must carry and defend as though your life depended on it.

I call a veil a conditioning so old that it already seems like your skin. It is not a flaw, not an illness, not a fault. It is a thin layer that placed itself between you and what you are, early, often by hands that had also inherited it, and that, after so many years, stopped being seen as a veil. It is seen as truth. It is seen as "this is just how I am". And this one, in particular, is the veil that clings to the very notion of who you are, and therefore the hardest to notice, because to see it you must look at the thing you look with.

I think of a woman, and she could be so many. She was, her whole life, the pillar of the family, the one who solved things, the one everyone turned to in crises and no one turned to on the good days. When the children left and she was alone, she did not first weep for their absence. She wept for something else, harder to confess: she did not know who she was without someone to hold up. She had spent so long being the pillar that she had forgotten she was also a house, with rooms inside waiting to be lived in. She was not broken. She was only discovering, late, that she was far more than the function that had made her indispensable.

Perhaps you recognise yourself in her, or perhaps your role has another face. The one who never trembles. The one who always has a firm opinion and cannot bear the thought of changing it. The one who defines herself entirely by a profession, and who, when retirement or a redundancy takes it away, feels she has ceased to exist. The consistent one at all costs, who would rather defend an old choice she no longer believes in than admit, in front of others, that she changed. They are different faces of the same veil, and beneath them all lives the same silent belief: that there is a version of you that you must keep intact, at any price, or you will be less, or you will be nothing.

I think of another woman, younger, who introduced herself to everyone as "the strong one of the group", the one who never cried, who always had the right advice for friends in crisis. She was so proud of it that she did not even notice the price. When her father fell ill and she herself needed, for the first time, a shoulder, she discovered she did not know how to ask. She would sit with her friends and, instead of saying "I am falling apart", she listened to their problems, gave advice, went home and cried alone, ashamed of needing. The role that had made her loved had become a cell. She was loved for being strong, and so she did not dare stop being it, even when being strong was costing her soul. Notice how the veil works: it does not tighten from outside, it tightens from within, and it dresses itself in the clothing of your best qualities.

This veil does not harm you out of cruelty. It harms you out of loyalty to a time that has passed. At some point, being a fixed and firm version of yourself was the most sensible thing a body could find in order to belong, to be loved, to be safe. Pleasing may have been what kept you safe in a home where love was conditional. Being unshakeable may have been the only way not to fall apart when, as a child, there was no one to cushion your fall. The veil protected you, and it did it well. The problem is not that it existed. It is that it goes on dressing you long after the threat has gone, and choosing for you in places where you could, at last, choose alone. There are no enemies here to fight. There is an old, intelligent strategy that did its job and was never told the danger had passed.

### How do you recognise this veil active in you?

None of these signs is an illness or a failure of character. They are only the invitation to discover, beneath the form, the one in you who never changes.

You recognise it by always introducing yourself with the same sentence, the same roles, as though without them no one would be left. By feeling that changing course, changing your mind, changing your life, would be betraying yourself or letting down those who count on you. By how it costs you to ask for help or show fragility, because your place was always the strong one, the one who endures, the one who does not collapse.

You recognise it by an identity so glued to a function that the mere idea of letting it go opens a clench of emptiness in you. By defending who you are with a firmness that, seen from outside, looks like security, and that on the inside is, so often, fear of not knowing who you would be without it. By preferring to stay the same your whole life rather than admit you changed your mind, as though changing were lying about who you were. And by feeling, in certain silences, that the sentence you repeat about yourself already tightens like clothes belonging to a body that has since changed.

This veil has ways of hiding worth knowing, because they tend to pass for virtue, and so they are the hardest to catch. There is the disguise of the strong one, who never trembles, who confuses rigidity with character and dreads being seen fragile. There is the one who defines herself entirely by a role, and who, when that role changes or ends, feels she has lost her footing. There is the consistent one, who sees in change a kind of moral weakness, when changing is only a sign that you are alive and learning. And there is the most silent disguise of all, that of loyalty, which I will speak of next, because it is the deepest root of this veil. There is no malice in any of these disguises. There is a little girl who learned, long ago, that only one version of her was safe, and who goes on protecting it as she can.

Recognising this takes nothing away from what you are. It only loosens, a little, the knot you hold it with. And it is from that loosening that everything is born. Do not rush to change anything yet. For now, it is enough to begin to see. A pattern seen no longer catches you in the same way, because it has stopped acting in the dark. The first liberation, in this veil as in all of them, is never to change. It is to see.

## The root

To understand why this veil holds, even against your will, even after you have seen it, it helps to look at its root. Because seeing, on its own, rarely is enough the first time, and it is there that so many women judge themselves weak when they were only human. The rigidity that governs you has three foods: a body that learned early that only one version of you was safe, an inheritance you received without choosing, and a zone of yourself you do not look at, where the veil decides in the dark. Let us look at all three, because what leaves the shadow stops ruling.

Where is this veil born? Almost always in a home where only one version of you was welcome.

The child who receives love when she is strong learns to hide her fragility. The one who is only seen when she stands out learns that existing means performing. The one who had to grow up fast clings to the role of the responsible one, because it was that role that secured her a place. You did not build a self out of vanity. You built it to survive, to belong to those whose love you needed. When the love of a home comes conditioned, when it arrives wrapped in a "how well you cope", "thank goodness there is always you", the child makes a very simple and very intelligent calculation: I will be this, because this is what keeps me wanted. And then she spends a lifetime paying an invoice she signed before she could read.

Notice that there was no crime here, not yours nor that of those who raised you. A child does not choose the version of herself she presents to the world. She deduces it, in the most intelligent way a child has, which is by observing what makes the grown-ups' eyes shine and what makes the house go tense. If your firmness, your early maturity, your capacity to cope were what earned a smile, that is what you learned to be, and that is what your body kept as though life depended on it. The identity you defend today is not vanity or stubbornness. It is an old strategy of a clever girl doing everything she knew to have a place. And it is precisely because it was so intelligent, so effective, that it clung so hard and lasted so long.

Perhaps you had to grow up fast, to be the little adult of a house in disorder, to care for those who should have cared for you. Perhaps you became your mother's confidante before you were old enough to carry that, or the one who held the siblings together, or the one who had to be always fine so as not to be one more problem for already weary parents. Each of these stories teaches the body the same lesson: your fragility has no place here, put it away. And the child puts it away so well that, years later, the woman no longer knows where she left it. That is why, today, it costs you so much to ask, to show yourself low, to let yourself be supported. It is not pride. It is a little girl who learned, very early, that her turn would not come, and who arranged never to have to wait for it again. To see this with tenderness is the beginning of letting her, at last, have her turn.

Notice that it was not only your own choice. Almost always, the rigidity did not even begin in you. It was here that systemic family constellation opened my eyes in a way I did not expect: much of what we carry was not born in us, it crossed generations before reaching us. There are women behind you who had to keep themselves whole at all costs, in times and homes where stepping out of line was dangerous, where a crack could cost dearly. A grandmother who could not allow herself to tremble. A mother who held everything alone and never set it down. They learned to be made of stone because life asked them for stone, and they passed you the gesture without words, the way one hands down a sacred inheritance.

Defending your identity today tooth and nail may be, without your knowing, a loyalty to them. A way of not betraying them, of not being softer than they were, of honouring what they endured by going on enduring the same. Notice how, deep down, being happy in a lighter, more open, more fragile way can sound almost like abandonment, as though resting were accusing them of not having known how to rest, as though changing were saying their path had been wrong. There are women who stay rigid their whole lives out of a faithfulness they never named, bound to a family fate they mistook for identity. And the body keeps that loyalty long before the head understands it.

But there is a very particular rest that arrives only when you say to them, inwardly, slowly: I saw what you had to bear. I honour you. And, from me onward, I can set down the part that is no longer needed. This is not betrayal. It is exactly the opposite. It is letting the current of rigidity stop with you, so that it need not go on running in those who come after you. To honour where you come from does not oblige you to repeat what hurt there. You can keep their strength as inheritance and give back the weight that did not have to be yours. To belong to your line does not oblige you to carry its hardness.

So, before you go on, stay a moment with these questions, without rushing to answer. From whom did you learn, back then, that only one version of you was welcome at home? Which woman before you had to be made of stone in order to survive? And how much of the firmness you defend as yours is, deep down, a loyalty to someone who no longer needs you to carry it. Do not answer from the head. Let the answers arrive over the days, often when you least expect, in some gesture of yours that you suddenly recognise as being, after all, someone else's gesture.

## See

To see this veil is to recognise that the problem was never that you had an identity. The problem is that you mistook yourself for it, to the point where letting go of a role felt like dying.

There is an enormous difference, and it is here that all the freedom of this veil lives, between having a form and being the form. Think of a tree. The roots keep where you come from. The trunk holds the roles, what keeps you standing in the world. The leaves are the facets that change with the seasons, that turn green and dry and will fall. If a branch breaks, if all the leaves drop in one autumn, the tree does not stop being a tree. You are not the leaves. You are what remains when they fall.

Notice how the tree is not afraid of autumn. It does not cling to its leaves in terror, it does not spend the winter in panic asking who it is without them. It lets them fall, stands bare, rests, and in spring it gives new leaves, and none of those leaves is less true because the previous one fell. The tree trusts the trunk. It knows, without needing to know, that what holds it is not in the leaves. You too have a trunk like that, an axis that crossed every season of your life, every version you have been and let go of, and that went on being you while everything changed. What this veil did was make you mistake yourself for the leaves, and so every autumn feels like the end of the world. To see is to bring the attention down from the leaves to the trunk, and to discover, with some astonishment, that it was always there, firm and silent, while you wept the loss of each leaf as though it were the loss of you.

And you can prove it now, with no theory at all, just by looking. Notice a thought you are having this instant. There is in you someone who notices, and that someone is not the thought, otherwise it could not see it. Notice a role you play, mother, daughter, professional. There is in you someone who plays it, and who is not the role, because that someone is still there between one role and the next, in the gap where you are being nothing for anyone. The one who sees has no name and no function. It was not born when you took on your first role, and it does not die when you let the role go. And, because it cannot be lost, it does not need defending. Everything you defend full time is, by definition, what you are afraid of losing. What remains in you does not fit inside that fear.

See what this veil, deep down, hides. It hides the belief in a fixed self, a self you must keep always the same, always consistent, always equal to the image, on pain of ceasing to be you. It is a belief so old that you do not even feel it as a belief, you feel it as an obvious fact, as the backdrop of all your experience. And yet, if you look honestly, you realise that this fixed self never actually existed. The woman you were at twenty is not the one you are today, and neither of them was false. You changed your tastes, your opinions, your body, your beliefs, many times, and something in you went on being you through all those changes. That something is none of the versions. It is the thread that runs through them all. The fixed self you defend is a photograph you took of yourself at a certain moment, and mistook for the whole river. To see this veil is to discover that you were never the photograph. You were always the river.

There is one more thing seeing gives you back, and it is a kind of relief you may not know you need. As long as you believe you are a fixed thing, you live in a state of permanent defence, always watching whether your image is holding, always ready to correct whoever sees you differently, always tired of sustaining yourself. It is a full-time job, and it never ends, because the image needs to be renewed at every encounter, every conversation, every glance. When you see that what you defend is not your core, but only a layer, that work begins, on its own, to lose its urgency. You do not have to let it go by force. You see that it was not necessary, and the guard lowers by itself.

To see is not to think about this. It is not to build the perfect theory about your identity, to dissect your childhood, to find someone to blame. That, often, is just the mind using introspection as one more way to defend yourself, and you can spend a lifetime understanding yourself without ever seeing yourself. To see is simpler and harder. It is catching yourself in the act, at the exact moment you are defending an image of yourself, and staying with it without rushing to judge it or fix it. It is noticing, in the middle of a conversation, that you are proving a point no one asked you to prove. It is noticing, in the middle of an "this is just how I am", the fear that holds that sentence up underneath. To see hurts a little, always, because it removes the anaesthetic of certainty about who you are. And it is that small burning that, in the end, opens the hand.

Let me make clear what seeing does not ask of you, because this is where many stumble. Seeing does not ask you to stop being firm when firmness is true, nor to give up the roles you love, nor to become soft or undefined. Seeing asks you only one thing: that you stop mistaking the clothing for the skin. That you notice the difference, within yourself, between the firmness you choose and the firmness that governs you. The first is yours, it comes from within, and you can set it down when you wish. The second is the veil, and you can tell because, beneath it, there is always a clench, a cannot, a fear of what would happen if you yielded. Where there is that clench, there is the veil. Where there is freedom to be and not to be, there is you.

And there is a small, sure sign for recognising that you are truly seeing, and not only thinking about yourself. When you really see, there is an instant of space, almost silent, in which the pattern hangs suspended, without catching you completely. It is not a great revelation. It is a millimetre of distance between you and the automatic reaction, and it is in that millimetre that all the freedom you gain lives. You need no more than that. Do not chase great experiences. Do the simple gesture of noticing, and let the rest work in the dark, in its own time.

## Release

To release this veil is not to throw away who you are, nor to stop being capable, nor to lose the roles you love. It is to return identity to the place it should never have left: clothing, and not skin.

You go on being daughter, mother, professional, friend, everything you are. You only stop dying a little each time one of those roles changes, ends or is taken from you. The children leave, and you do not collapse with the empty nest, because you were not only the nest. A job ends, and you do not end with it. A version of you stops making sense, and you are not lost, you change. To release is to discover that there was always, beneath every form, someone wearing them, and that this someone remains.

What releasing reveals, deep down, is a simple and liberating truth: identity is flux, not a prison. You are not a statue that must stay identical to prove it is true. You are a river, and a river that stopped flowing would cease to be a river, it would become a stagnant pool. Your capacity to change is not a flaw in your identity, it is the proof that you are alive. The women we most admire, deep down, are not the ones who never changed. They are the ones who knew how to cross many lives within a single one, shedding skins without losing themselves, faithful not to a fixed image of themselves, but to something deeper and more mobile that accompanied them through every crossing. To release this veil is to give yourself permission to be many, over time, without ceasing to be one.

To release is not an effort. It is not tearing out of yourself, by force of will, the woman of stone you learned to be, because what is torn out by violence grows back with a deeper root, and you wake up fighting yourself. To release is more like slowly opening a hand that had been clenched for so long it had begun to ache, and discovering what it was gripping, and what lay beneath it, waiting for you. It is to stop feeding the pattern with your attention and your fear, and to notice that, without that fuel, it loses strength on its own.

There is a paradox here worth knowing, because it spares you much useless struggle. The more you fight a rigid identity, the more you feed it. If you decide, with all your willpower, "from today I will stop being the strong one", you have only created one more role to defend: that of the one who is no longer strong, the one who is freeing herself, and you will defend it with the same rigidity as ever, now only in different clothes. The harshness has come back disguised as growth. So releasing is not done against you. It is done in your favour, with curiosity instead of combat. You do not say "I have to stop being this way". You say "let me see this", and you stay looking, without haste, and what is seen with attention and without violence loosens by itself, like a knot that comes undone when you stop pulling both ends at once. Often there is not even a dramatic moment of liberation. There is rather a day on which you notice that what bound you for years no longer tightens as before, that you responded differently without even trying, that the thing came undone in the dark while you were living.

And there is an immense rest on the other side. If you are not the name you defend, then you do not have to defend it full time, permanently on guard. You can lower your arms. You can make mistakes. You can not know. You can change your mind at forty, at fifty, at sixty, without feeling you betray yourself, because you understand that to change belongs to whoever is alive. Losses begin to hurt without destroying you, because you discover in yourself a depth no loss can reach. You become curious about yourself again. You stop being a statue intent on staying the same and become a river once more. You do not become no one. You become free within who you are.

Notice the hidden economy of this rest. Sustaining a fixed image costs, and it costs the whole time, even while you sleep. You have to watch how you are seen, correct whoever sees you differently, keep the consistency of the character in every conversation, not contradict yourself, not fail the role. It is a full-time job, badly paid, from which you never take a holiday. To release this veil is, above all, to resign from that job. And what you do with the energy it devoured is your own affair. But you will notice that, suddenly, there is more of you available for life, more free attention, more room to feel, to play, to surprise yourself. The woman who does not have to defend herself full time has, at last, time to live.

To release often has the taste of a small grief, and I want you to know this so it does not frighten you. Even the prisons we grow used to have the comfort of the familiar, and to let go of a hard identity is also to say goodbye to who we were inside it, to the one who endured, to the one no one ever saw tremble. Let it hurt what it must hurt. Sadness, here, is not a sign that it went wrong. It is a sign that something true is finally being released.

There is also, in this releasing, a brief emptiness worth announcing to you, so you do not read it as a mistake. When you let go of a version of yourself that defined you for decades, there is a strange interval in which you are no longer the old one and not yet anything new. The self no longer leans on the usual role, but has not yet found other ground. The mind, used to always knowing who it is, does not know how to inhabit that open space, and may take fright, may think it has lost itself. Do not be frightened. It is an emptiness of gestation, not of lack. It is the fertile territory between the form you let go of and the life you have not yet named, and it is precisely there, without the comfort of the old certainties about yourself, that something truly new can, for the first time, begin to be born. Do not rush to fill it with a new role just as tight. Stay in the open long enough to discover that you were none of the forms, you were the one who inhabited them.

## The protocol

So far, we have seen the veil. Now you need something concrete to do with it when it appears, because the veil does not appear when you are sitting reading, calm. It appears in the middle of an argument, in a comment that stung you, in a moment when someone saw you weak and you rushed to recompose the image. It is in those instants, and not on these pages, that the method is proven. So this is the serious heart of this book: a protocol, with named steps, to apply at the exact moment the pattern of defending the identity fires.

When the pattern fires, when you feel the old firmness rising to defend the image, prove a point, hold the role, you do not need a book in front of you. You need a gesture the body knows by heart. I call it the pocket protocol of permanence, and it has five steps. It fits inside a minute, and you use it at the exact instant you catch yourself defending who you think you have to be. The steps are named on purpose, because a gesture with a name comes back to the hand faster. Learn them, not in order to do them perfectly, but so they are there, available, the next time the firmness rises.

**One: Name the veil.** Stop, even for a second, and say inwardly, without judging yourself: here is the permanence. Here it is defending an image of me. To name the pattern is to take it out of the shadow, and what leaves the shadow stops ruling in the dark. You need nothing more than this to begin: recognising, in the act, that it is not you speaking, it is the veil.

**Two: Find the one who sees.** Instead of staying inside the role, step back to the one who is playing it. Notice that there is, in this moment, someone in you noticing the firmness rise, and that someone is not the firmness. Breathe once slowly and stay, for an instant, with that one who watches. It is your true ground, and it is always there, even when the role is shouting.

**Three: Ask whose it is.** Ask the question of the root, softly: is this really mine, or am I being loyal to someone who came before? Which woman taught me that I had to be made of stone? Often you will feel, with a shiver, that the rigidity is not yours, it is borrowed. And what is borrowed can be given back with respect.

**Four: Ask what it protects.** Before releasing, honour. Ask: what am I trying to protect by acting this way? Almost always you will find, beneath the strength, a child afraid of not being loved if she stops being useful or firm. Do not scold her. Place, inwardly, a hand on her shoulder. The veil was protection before it was prison, and you can only release what you first thank.

**Five: Release one finger.** Not everything, not by force. Open the hand just a little. Say a small sentence that goes against the veil without tearing it out: "I don't know", "I changed my mind", "I need help", "I can't today", "you do it". Just one. And notice that you do not collapse. That you remain whole even without being right, even without knowing, even without being the strong one. What you cannot let go of today, leave it be. Come back tomorrow. It releases one finger at a time.

This is the protocol, and it is only this. Stop and name. Find the one who sees. Ask whose it is. Ask what it protects. Release one finger. It is not a complicated ritual, it is a simple gesture, repeated until the body knows it on its own, until you catch it earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you.

Let me show you the protocol at work in a real situation, of the kind life brings. Imagine you are in a conversation and someone disagrees with a decision of yours, and at once you feel the heat rise to your chest, the urge to defend yourself, to explain, to prove you are right. It is the veil firing. First, you name it inwardly: here is the permanence, I am defending my image. This alone already slows the impulse for half a second. Then you step back to the one who sees: you notice there is in you someone observing the urge to defend yourself, and that someone is calm. You breathe. Then you ask whose it is: where does this urgency to always be right come from, who taught me that yielding was losing. Perhaps you remember a home where yielding meant being swallowed. Then you ask what it protects: I am trying not to feel small, not to feel disposable. And, finally, you release one finger: you say, out loud, "look, you may be right, let me think about it", and you notice the world does not end, that you did not dissolve for not having won the argument. It was a minute. And in that minute you did not change your personality, you only did not let the veil choose for you.

Notice that the protocol does not ask you to stop feeling the impulse. The impulse will go on coming, with the same force, for a long time, because the body learned it decades ago. What changes is that, between the impulse and the action, you open a millimetre of space, and it is in that millimetre that you live your freedom. With repetition, the millimetre widens. At first you will remember the protocol only hours later, the argument already over, and that is fine, that too is the path. Then you will remember halfway through. And one day, without warning, you will see the veil coming before it catches you, and be able, for the first time, to choose something else. It is for that instant of choice that this whole protocol works.

## The practices

The protocol is for the instant the veil fires. The practices are for serene training, outside the heat of the moment, so that the body slowly learns a new path. Do them with tenderness, not as one more task you can fail. Choose the ones that call you. Repeat them.

Before you begin, stay with these questions, without rushing to answer. Who would you be if you let go, for a single day, of the role that most defines you? What do you fear would happen if you stopped being the strong one, the one who endures, the one who fixes everything? From whom did you learn, back then, that only one version of you was welcome at home? And what in you remains, even when all the roles change, end or are taken from you? Do not answer from the head, in a rush. Let them rest in you while you do the practices, and notice the answers that arrive on their own, often without words.

The practices that follow have a gentle order, but not an obligatory one. The first train the seeing, the recognising of who you are beneath the form. The last train the releasing, in life and in the line of women you come from. Do one at a time. You need no more than a few minutes a day, and one practice done with presence is worth more than five done in a rush to be done with them. Remember: there is no grade here, no advanced pupil. There is only your own pace.

**Practice one: the name and the one who hears it.** Find a calm moment, alone, and say your name softly, slowly, three times. On the third, instead of thinking about the name, notice who is hearing it. There is someone who hears the name, and who is not the name. Stay with that one who hears for the space of three breaths, doing nothing, changing nothing. It is the simplest exercise in this book and the deepest: to move, for an instant, from being the name to being the one who hears it. If you feel nothing special, good. The practice is not for feeling anything spectacular, it is for training the gaze. The rest works in the dark, in its own time.

**Practice two: the three "I am".** On a sheet of paper, write three sentences beginning with "I am", the first that come, the ones you most use to present yourself to the world. Beside each one, write, slowly, what would happen if it stopped being true. Who you would be if you were no longer that. Observe which one tightens your chest as you write. That is not your deepest identity. It is your most defended veil, the first your hand learns to set down. Do not fight it. Just notice that you hold it tightly, and ask yourself why. And then, carefully, ask that sentence one last question: whose voice is this? Often you will discover, with a shiver, that the sentence you say about yourself most firmly was not born in you. It belongs to a mother who called you strong, to a father who only praised your performance, to someone who spoke first and whom you went on repeating inwardly for decades, doing their work for them. When you recognise the voice, in silence, give the sentence back. You do not have to fight it. Only to notice that it was a loan, and that you no longer have to keep paying it for them.

**Practice three: the tree.** On a sheet of paper, draw a simple tree, without worrying about the drawing. In the roots, write where you come from, what formed you, the names of those who came before you. In the trunk, write the roles that hold you up today, mother, daughter, professional, what you are for others. In the leaves, write the facets that have already changed over your life, the ones that fell, the ones that turned green in other seasons, the girl you were and no longer are, the tastes that changed, the certainties you let drop. Then look at the trunk and the roots, without the leaves, and ask: what of me would remain if all the leaves fell at once? That remainder is what stays. It is who you are beneath the roles. Do this practice once a year, if you can, on your birthday perhaps, and notice how the leaves change from year to year, and how the tree goes on being your tree. It is the living proof, in your own handwriting, that you changed greatly and never ceased to be you.

**Practice four: changing your mind out loud.** This one leaves the page and enters life. Choose, during the week, a small occasion to change your mind out loud in front of someone, or to say "I don't know", or "actually I was wrong", without justifying yourself, without a speech to defend. Then pay attention to what rises inside: the clench, the urge to explain, the fear of seeming fragile. Do not cover that discomfort. Just notice it, and notice too that you did not collapse. That you remained whole without being right. Each time you do this, you teach the body, and not only the head, that to change is not to die. Begin with small things, weightless, a film you did not actually like that much, a restaurant you would not actually recommend. The body learns the permission in the small things first, and only then extends it to the great ones. You do not have to begin with the decision of your life. Begin by letting drop, without drama, an opinion about nothing at all, and notice the tiny rest of not having to sustain it.

**Practice five: returning the inheritance.** This one is slower and asks for silence. Sit alone and bring to mind a woman of your line who had to keep herself whole at all costs, a grandmother, a mother, a great-grandmother you were told about. Imagine her before you. Say to her, inwardly, without haste: I saw what you had to bear. I honour your strength. And I tell you that, from me onward, there is no longer any need to be made of stone. I can tremble, I can ask, I can change, and that does not betray you. Notice what moves in you as you say these words. Often it is here, and not in the will, that the rigidity finally begins to set down.

**Practice six: the minute of the veil.** This is the only one I ask of you every day, and it is the smallest of all. Once a day, at night perhaps, stop for a minute and ask: which version of me was I defending today? Where did I hold the role when I could have set it down? Not to correct yourself, only to see. Name the moment, thank the veil for its old protection, and release one finger, inwardly, in your imagination if need be. One minute a day. It is enough that, after weeks, you begin to catch the veil earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you. One minute a day changes a life, not because it is much, but because it is every day.

## Seven days of releasing the form

Seven days do not remove a veil forever, and that is not the promise. What they do is teach the body a path, one small movement a day, so that it is there, available, the next time the veil settles. Do one a day, without rushing, without grading yourself. If one day asks for more time, stay with it. There is no possible delay on this journey, because the destination is exactly where you already are.

Each day asks of you a few minutes and a single gesture. It is not a programme to fulfil with rigour, it is an invitation to inhabit the veil long enough to know it from within, and not only from ideas. If you can, keep a notebook of your own at hand, not to find right answers, but to see yourself thinking. And remember, each day, the golden rule: first to see, without judging yourself. Releasing comes after, and comes more on its own than you expect. The order matters, and never reverses: the rush to change without first recognising is itself the veil dressed in good intention.

**Day 1, seeing the outline.** Today you only notice. Walk through the day with a loose question in your pocket: where am I defending an image of myself? Change nothing, correct nothing, do not try to be different. Just notice the times you prove a point, correct the image others have of you, hold the role of the strong one even when you needed to set it down. Notice, without scolding yourself, the clench that appears whenever your image is at risk. At night, recall one of those times, with curiosity and not with harshness, as one observes an old acquaintance. Seeing is the first movement, and for today that is enough. There is nothing to release yet. There is only to recognise.

**Day 2, the name and the one who hears it.** Do the first practice. Say your name three times, and on the third notice who hears it. Stay three breaths with that one who hears. Carry with you, through the rest of the day, the memory that there is in you someone prior to the name and to all the roles.

**Day 3, releasing one finger.** Today you say a small sentence that goes against the veil: "I don't know", "I changed my mind", "I need help", or you leave, without picking it up, a task you would do out of habit to show that you cope. Just one. Notice the discomfort that rises, and notice that you remain whole.

**Day 4, whose it is.** Today you look at the root. Choose a firmness of yours, a way you are hard or unshakeable, and ask: whose is this? Which woman before me had to be this way? You do not need the right answer. It is enough to open the question and let it work.

**Day 5, returning the inheritance.** Do the practice of returning the inheritance, with the woman of your line who comes to mind. Say to her, inwardly, that you honour her strength and that, from you onward, the current can set down. Notice what grows lighter.

**Day 6, showing a fragility.** Today you let someone see you not whole. Ask for help with a small thing. Say you are tired, instead of saying everything is fine. Accept a kindness without returning it at once, and practise saying only "thank you", without giving it back, without minimising it. Practise existing without being useful, without being the strong one. A discomfort will rise, perhaps even a shame, the sense of being a burden, of standing exposed without the armour. Do not flee from it. Notice that love does not flee with you. The people who truly love you do not love you for your strength, they love you, and often they are relieved to be able, at last, to give you something back.

**Day 7, what remains.** Today close the week with the practice of the tree, or just with its question: what of me would remain if all the leaves fell at once? Sit with the answer, not as a conclusion, but as a seed. Write, if you wish, a short letter to yourself, to read in a few months, with what you discovered about who you are beneath the roles and what you want to remember on the days you forget everything. Keep it. You will need it again, and it will be there, patient, waiting for you.

## What changes

What changes, when this veil lifts, is that life grows lighter, and that lightness is no small thing.

You can change course at any age without feeling you betray yourself, because you understand that to change belongs to whoever is alive, not a disloyalty to who you were. Losses begin to hurt without destroying you, because you discover in yourself a depth no loss can reach, a ground that stays when the leaves fall. You set down the permanent guard, that weariness of one who must defend the image full time, and you discover a rest you did not know: that of not having to prove that you are, because you already are.

The change here does not have the dramatic form you may expect. You will not wake one day transformed, with the veil on the floor and a permanent serenity. What you will notice, after months, is more subtle and more solid than that. You will notice that you admitted a mistake without the whole defence speech behind it. That you changed plans without the old guilt. That someone saw you low and you did not rush to recompose the mask. That you said "I need help" and the word came out easier than last time. These are small victories, almost invisible, and often you only notice them later, when you realise that what would once have cost you a week of torment now took only an afternoon. True transformation is not a lightning bolt, it is a tide that rises so slowly that you only notice it when you realise the whole landscape has changed.

You become curious about yourself again. You stop being a statue intent on staying the same and become a river once more, able to change its course without ceasing to be water. You learn to ask for help, to say "I don't know", to show yourself fragile, and you discover that the love that came only through strength was a small love, and that there is another, larger, that comes because you exist. Your relationships breathe more easily, because they no longer rest on your firmness or your function, but on your true presence.

Something also changes in the body, and this surprises those who did not expect it. The permanent guard, that state of being always tense in order to sustain an image, spends a silent energy you did not even notice you were spending. When you set it down, there is a background weariness that begins to give way, not because you started doing less, but because you stopped carrying, twenty-four hours a day, the weight of being someone. The shoulders drop. The breath widens. There are women who only realise how contracted they were when, for the first time, they let themselves be nothing for anyone for an afternoon, and feel the body release a tension they had had since childhood and had long mistaken for their natural posture.

And there is, above all, a reconciliation that often happens without being sought: with those who came before you. When you see that you inherited the rigidity without choosing it, that those women of stone were of stone because life asked them for stone, you stop judging them and begin to understand them. And in that understanding something is released in you too. You are no longer loyal to their hardness, you are loyal to the love that lay beneath the hardness, and that love does not ask you to go on being stone. It asks you, perhaps, the exact opposite: to be the first who can, at last, rest.

And, beneath it all, this remains: you stop dying a little each time a role ends. You do not become no one. You become free within who you are. The change does not come like a lightning bolt, with all the veils on the floor at once. It comes like a tide rising slowly, until you notice, on some ordinary day, that you let go of the role without collapsing, that you changed your mind without guilt, that you were fragile and remained whole. These are small victories, almost invisible, and it is because they are small and everyday that they are real.

A word about relapses, because you will have them, and I do not want them to take you by surprise nor for you to take them as failure. This is not a straight line, it is a spiral, and on a spiral you pass several times through the same point, but never at the same height. You will recognise yourself defending the old image, holding the role with both hands, on a bad day, in a fright, when tired, and you will be tempted to think you learned nothing, that you went back to square one. You did not go back. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before, you would not even be aware, and now you see yourself doing it, perhaps still in the middle of the gesture, perhaps already halfway. That seeing, earlier and earlier, is the progress, though it does not have the form you expected. The veil does not vanish overnight as if by magic. It goes on losing strength, catching you fewer times and for less time, until one day, with no celebration at all, you notice it barely comes anymore. Be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades and generations to weave.

## To close

This was one veil, just one, of seven.

Before I say goodbye, let me leave you an image of the path, so you do not read it as a staircase to climb. This is not a straight line with a summit at the end. It is a spiral. You will return to this veil at different moments of your life, and find it each time a little thinner. At thirty, perhaps it will be the professional you are afraid to stop being. At fifty, perhaps it will be the mother whose children no longer need her in the same way. Later, perhaps it will be the body itself that changes and forces you to release the image of yourself you had. Each time, the same veil, and each time you a little more able to see it and set it down. It is not useless repetition. It is the spiral doing its work, bringing you to the same point at an always new height. So, when you catch yourself defending an image again, do not think you learned nothing. Think: here I am again, in this veil, and this time I see it sooner.

Permanence is the first, the one that clings to the idea of who you are, and so it is often here that many begin. But it does not live alone. It interweaves with the others, takes them by the hand. It often walks with the horizon, the one that always projects you toward a "when I will finally be happy", and the two together rob you of the present entirely, one binding you to who you once were, the other pushing you toward what you are not yet. It walks too with the effort, because the identity of the strong one sustains itself by doing, by holding, by proving, and when you work one, the other loosens. Beneath them all there is a deeper veil, the sense of separation, of being alone in having to defend who you are, and it is from that veil that all this firmness, in the end, is born.

You do not have to cross them all, nor in order, nor at once. You pulled this thread because it was this one that called you, and in releasing it you will feel the others loosen on their own. The web does not come undone knot by knot by force. It comes undone when you stop clenching it. The whole map, the seven veils and the path between them, is in *The Seven Veils*. When you want to go deeper, or when another veil begins to tighten, it waits for you, patient, like a friend who is in no hurry.

Before you close, let me remind you of how this is lived day to day, because it is there, and not on these pages, that the method is proven. You will not transform yourself in a day of revelation. You will transform yourself in thousands of small instants in which you choose to see instead of reacting, and to release instead of holding. Imagine an ordinary day. Someone comments on a choice of yours and you feel the old firmness rising to defend yourself. Instead of embarking on it, you notice: ah, here is the permanence. You do not scold yourself, you do not force yourself to change, you only see. That seeing alone already opens a millimetre between you and the pattern. Later, you catch yourself saying "this is just how I am" to justify not trying something new, and you notice again, with tenderness: ah, here is the veil speaking for me. It is in this way, and not in some more grandiose way, that a life transforms.

There will be days when you manage, and days when you will defend the old image the whole day and only realise it in bed. The bad days do not erase the path walked, just as a storm does not erase an entire spring. On those days, the method itself has one last fold, the most important: to see, and release, your own demand to do it well. Because the rush to already be the free and light woman you imagine is itself one more veil trying to come in through the back door. There is no advanced pupil in this school. There are only women walking, each at her own pace, seeing and releasing what they can on the day they have. And one thing I want to make clear, because it matters: this path does not replace the support you may need. There are wounds that ask for professional hands, and to seek that help is not weakness, it is itself a gesture of seeing and of caring, and perhaps the first great step toward releasing the strong one who never let herself be held.

There is a difference worth keeping, between this method and the quick fixes you may already have tried. Quick fixes promise to take away the discomfort fast: affirm every morning that you are free, repeat that you changed, force the new image over the old. They work for a few days, and then the old firmness comes back, intact, because it was never seen, only painted over. The method is the opposite, and so it is slower and more solid. It does not give you a new and better identity to defend in place of the old one. It teaches you to stop defending any identity full time, and that cannot be faked, it is built slowly, in the seeing and not in the flight. From outside it may look as though you are going slowly, while others promise lightning transformations. On the inside, you are building the only kind of change that lasts. Trust that slow rhythm, especially on the days when the old rush tells you that you should already be free. That rush is itself one more veil. See it. And release it too.

For now, stay only with this, which is the heart of everything said here. You are not the name you defend. You are not the roles you carry. You are not the strong one, nor the responsible one, nor the one who endures, although you are also all of that when you choose to be. You are what remains when the leaves fall. And what remains does not need defending, because never, at any moment, was it at risk of being lost.

Trust this, even on the days it is hard to believe: beneath every role you will yet set down, beneath every version of you that you will yet release, there is something that was never bound. Something whole, serene, that does not need to be conquered or deserved, that was not born with your first role and will not die with your last, and that waited for you all this time, with the patience of things that are in no hurry because they have no end. You do not have to build it. Only to make enough silence to notice that it was always there, beneath every form, wearing them one by one without ever being any of them.

I do not wish for you to remove this veil forever, nor that you reach a definitive serenity, because that would only return you to one more veil, that of arrival. I wish you something else, smaller and truer: that, the next time you catch yourself holding the old role with both hands, instead of the old clench and the old shame, you feel an almost tenderness toward yourself, and think, with a tired smile, "ah, here you are again". And that you see. And that you release what you can that day, even if only a single finger.

See. And release. One finger at a time. And remember, on the days the old firmness tightens again, that to recognise it is already half the path, and that on the other side of it, waiting for you for a long time, is your own rest.

With all my care,
Vivianne
