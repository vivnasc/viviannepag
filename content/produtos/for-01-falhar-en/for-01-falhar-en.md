# The woman who cannot fail

**For you, who made yourself flawless in order to be safe. Because your perfection is not vanity, it is an armor, and you are no longer in that danger.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a woman whose perfection is a defense that was once necessary for survival, and that is a wound that often asks for more than understanding. If you recognize in yourself a constant anxiety about making mistakes, a panic in the face of failure, or the exhaustion of someone who can never lower her guard, perhaps you need not only to understand yourself, but to be supported. Seeking out a professional is not failing. It is caring for a wound you deserve to see heal.*

---

## 1. I cannot fail

Notice what happens inside you when you face the possibility of making a mistake.

It isn't just worry. It's panic. A tightness in your chest, an urgency, a terror out of all proportion, as if failing were not a setback, but a danger.

That's why you do everything well. More than well. Flawlessly. You leave no margin for error. You check, you control, you anticipate, you repeat, until you're certain that nothing will go wrong. And even so the anxiety doesn't pass, because there's always a way to fail that you haven't yet foreseen.

You live in permanent tension. The tension of someone who can't afford to make a mistake. Of someone who has to be always equal to the task, always right, always flawless, because failing, in your inner world, is not an acceptable possibility.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not vanity. It isn't perfectionism out of pride, out of wanting to be the best, out of liking to shine. People may think you're demanding, controlling, a perfectionist. But what drives you isn't the desire to be perfect. It's the terror of what happens if you aren't.

Because your perfection is an armor. You built it, without knowing it, to protect yourself from a real danger. You learned, somewhere, that failing came at a cost. A serious cost. And you made yourself flawless in order to be safe, because perfection was your way of avoiding a consequence that, one day, truly was dangerous.

This book is different from others you may have read. Because what brought you here isn't only a pattern to understand. It is, very often, a response to something you lived through, an adaptation that kept you safe in a time when making a mistake had real consequences. And a wound born under that kind of danger deserves more than to be understood. It deserves care. So, throughout these pages, I will tell you, plainly and without it being a failure, that perhaps you need not only to understand yourself, but to be supported.

And I'll tell you now where we are going, so you can read without your heart on edge. You can make a mistake and remain safe. You are no longer in the danger where a slip of yours cost dearly. The armor of perfection protected you back then, but today it tightens your chest at every typo, every delivery, every mirror, and you can, slowly, and perhaps with help, learn to set it down.

Perhaps the armor reveals itself in small things, in slips so ordinary you never named them.

In feeling a disproportionate anxiety in the face of the possibility of making a mistake, even a small one.

In being unable to rest until you're certain that everything is perfect, controlled, with no possible flaw.

In experiencing a mistake, when it happens, not as a setback, but as a catastrophe, with a weight that doesn't match the real size of what went wrong.

In being exhausted by a vigilance that never stops, the vigilance of someone who has to guarantee, at every instant, that she doesn't fail.

They are all the same plate of the same armor. Perfection as armor. The terror of making a mistake, which comes from a time when making a mistake cost dearly.

I want to show you how this perfection shows up in daily life, in things you may already take for who you are. You redo what was already fine, because it could be better, because it could have a hidden mistake you haven't yet seen. You delay handing things in, not out of laziness, but because while it isn't perfect, you don't let it go, and it's never perfect enough for your standard. You check, and you check again, and you check one more time, searching for the flaw you're certain is somewhere, lying in wait.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way you experience a mistake when it happens. It isn't proportional. A small slip, that another person would forget within a minute, stays with you for hours, days, churning, reliving, reproaching yourself. You wake in the night with it. You feel a shame that doesn't match the real size of what went wrong. Because, for you, a mistake is not a setback. It's a threat, a crack in the armor, a sign that maybe you aren't, after all, safe.

Notice, too, the tension with which you live, even on good days. There's always a part of you on alert, anticipating what could go wrong, preparing for the mistake that's coming. You never truly relax, because relaxing is lowering your guard, and lowering your guard is risking that a flaw gets through. You live in a state of permanent vigilance, exhausted, without knowing that most people don't live this way, don't have to live this way, and that this vigilance is not normal, it is the price of an armor.

And there's something the world doesn't see, beneath your flawlessness. It sees a capable, organized, reliable person, and it admires you for it. It doesn't see the terror that drives you, the anxiety that grips you, the immense effort it costs you to keep everything perfect. It sees the result, not the price. And you, who receive praise for your perfection, become even more bound to it, because now there are also others expecting you never to fail, and disappointing them adds to your own terror.

## 2. Failing cost dearly

Go back. To the beginning. To when failing became dangerous.

Because no one fears making mistakes this way without a reason. Your panic about failure was not born from nothing. It was born from an experience in which failing did, in fact, bring a consequence that left its mark on you.

Perhaps you grew up with someone who punished mistakes harshly. A father, a mother, whose reaction to a slip of yours was rage, punishment, humiliation, the withdrawal of affection. And you learned, in your body, that making a mistake brought danger, and that the way to avoid it was to never make one.

Perhaps you lived in a house where you had to be perfect to keep the peace. Where a mistake of yours could set off a storm, a conflict, an explosion. And you made yourself flawless, not out of pride, but so as not to light the danger that a mistake could bring.

Perhaps a mistake, in your house, had consequences that went far beyond a scolding. Perhaps there was violence, unpredictability, real danger, and your perfection was your way of giving no cause, of not summoning the storm, of keeping yourself safe in a place where failing could cost a great deal.

Think about what a little girl learns in a house where a slip lights the storm. She learns that the notebook has to be without a smudge, that the bed has to be left without a wrinkle, that the answer has to come out right the first time, because a mistake, there, does not stay a mistake. She learns that the only way to be safe is to give no cause, to anticipate everything, to leave not a single loose end unchecked. And that learning is not engraved as an idea, it is engraved as terror in the body, tied to survival.

And that little girl did exactly what she had to do to come through unharmed. She made herself flawless. She built, toy by tidied toy, test by perfect test, the armor of perfection, and it protected her, in that time, from that danger. Making herself without fault was, then, an intelligent response, perhaps a saving one, to a house where making a mistake was, in fact, risky.

But the armor did not come undone when the danger passed. It stayed. It became your way of being in the world. And today, as an adult, in a world where making a mistake no longer brings the consequence it once did, you continue to live as if it did. You remain flawless, vigilant, in panic in the face of failure, defended against a danger that no longer exists, but that your body continues to fear.

I want you to see this as clearly as a notebook held up to the light, with no smudge to hide. Your perfection is not a defect of yours. It is an intelligent response to a real danger. You did not come into the world needing to check everything twice. You are a survivor, who learned to protect herself in the only way she could. And recognizing this is not excusing those who hurt you. It is honoring the child who found, in perfection, a way to be safe.

I want to add a root you may recognize, because perfection is not always born from punishment. Sometimes it is born from conditional love. From a house where you were seen, praised, cherished, when you brought results, when you were the best, when you didn't fail. And where, without the results, without the excellence, you became invisible, or a disappointment. You learned, then, that love came through perfection, and that to fail was to risk losing love, not through punishment, but through the disappearance of affection.

A child who learns this becomes perfect not out of fear of pain, but out of fear of ceasing to be loved. And she carries, into adult life, the equation that only the flawless are lovable, that each failure makes her a little less worthy of love. So your perfection serves not only to protect you from a danger. It serves to keep you deserving of the love you felt only excellence could give you.

And sometimes there is yet another root: that of having been the child of whom everything was expected. The clever one, the capable one, the one who would go far, the family's pride. And that expectation, which seems like praise, is an enormous weight, because it turns each failure into a betrayal of the hopes placed in you. You became perfect so as not to disappoint, to live up to what they expected, to carry the future they projected onto you. And failing, in that equation, is not just making a mistake, it is disappointing everyone who bet on you.

In any of these roots, the lesson is similar: your worth, your place, your love, depend on your not failing. And it is that equation, engraved early, that governs you today, and that turns each mistake, however small, into a threat to your very right to be loved and to have a place.

It matters that you see where the terror of making a mistake came to be lodged, because that is what explains why it costs so much to undo it. This alarm of failure does not live only in what you remember, in the memory you know how to tell. It lives in a register older than words, engraved in the instant when your body associated a mistake with a consequence. When a little girl learns, early, that failing brings danger, her nervous system files that warning below consciousness, and no explanation erases it on its own. That's why, today, the panic that rises when you see a flaw does not pass through a decision of yours. It arrives before thought, in a tightening of the throat, in an ice in the hands, in an urge to correct, because it is the body reacting, and not reason choosing.

That's why you so often tell yourself that it's absurd, that no one dies from a typo or from a less than perfect delivery, and even so the fear does not go down. It does not go down because it was not born from a reasoning. It was born from a memory the body keeps, ancient, that still treats each mistake as if it were the mistake that once brought the storm. And that memory is not dismantled by good arguments about proportion and common sense. It is relearned, slowly, by making mistakes in small ways and discovering, time after time, that this time no punishment came.

I tell you this not to frighten you with the difficulty, but to lift off you one more burden of guilt: the guilt of not being able, simply by wishing it, to stop being afraid. No amount of willing the alarm quiet will switch it off. It is not weakness. It is the nature of a wound that lives in the body, and that therefore asks for a path that passes through the body, and often through hands that know how to accompany it, and not only through your good will to change.

## 3. The armor of perfection

There is something about your perfection that you need to see. It protects you, yes. But it also crushes you.

Notice the weight of living flawlessly. The vigilance that never stops. The tension of having to be always right. The immense energy you spend controlling, foreseeing, checking, guaranteeing that nothing fails. It is a full-time job, exhausting, that no one sees, and that consumes you from within.

And there is a cost to the size of the life you allow yourself. Because someone who cannot fail cannot take risks. She cannot experiment, try, dare, because all of that brings the possibility of making a mistake. You stay bound to the safe, the controlled, what you already master, because the new, the uncertain, what could go wrong, is too threatening. Perfection, which protects you, also imprisons you in what you already know how to do without failing.

And there is a cost to relationships. Because perfection creates distance. Someone who is always flawless is hard to reach, to know, to love up close, because she never shows the fragility, the mistake, the humanity that brings people closer. People admire you, but perhaps they don't feel close to you, because the armor that protects you also keeps them out.

I want to show you something deeper. Perfection robs you of the possibility of being loved as you are. Because you believe, deep down, that you are only acceptable when you are flawless. That your worth, your safety, your place, depend on your not failing. And so you never let yourself be seen fallible, human, wrong, and you never come to discover whether you would be loved that way, because you never risk showing it.

There is also a particular loneliness in this. The loneliness of someone who cannot share her mistakes, her doubts, her failures, with anyone. Of someone who must keep, always, the flawless facade, and who therefore carries alone everything she cannot show. Perfection is a solitary armor, because no one comes inside it, and you stay in there, alone, with only the weight of holding it up.

I want you to feel the tiredness of a guard who has never once been relieved of her post. The tiredness of an entire life of not being allowed to fail. The endless vigilance. The permanent panic. The impossibility of resting, of making a mistake, of being, simply, human. It is a deep exhaustion, and perhaps you already take it for normal, so used are you to living with your armor on.

I want to go deeper into what perfection costs you, because the price is greater than it seems, and it is paid in silence.

It costs you rest. Because to rest is to stop being on guard, and you can't stop being on guard, afraid that the moment you set down your guard, a flaw will slip through. Even on holiday, even while sleeping, there's a part of you on watch, and so you never truly replenish, and you live in a background tiredness that rest does not cure, because your rest is not rest, it's a guarded pause.

It costs you pleasure. Because to enjoy something, you have to give yourself over to it, to lower control, and you don't lower control. You're always evaluating, correcting, improving, even in what should be pure enjoyment. And pleasure, which demands surrender, escapes you, because you don't allow yourself the surrender it asks for.

It costs you the courage to create. Because creating something new means making mistakes, trying, failing, starting over. And you, who cannot bear mistakes, stay bound to what you already master, to the safe, to what you know how to do without failing. The new, the risky, what could be yours if you dared, goes undone, because the path there passes, inevitably, through mistakes your armor does not allow.

And it costs you yourself, in the way you treat yourself. Because you've become your own jailer, the harshest guard, the most relentless judge. What others, in your past, did to you, you now do to yourself, without respite. You don't need anyone to punish you for a mistake. You punish yourself, with a harshness you would never apply to anyone else. And living this way, under your own merciless vigilance, is a tiredness of the soul, ancient, that perhaps you no longer even know how to name.

I want you to see that none of this is vanity, nor healthy demand, nor ambition. It is the functioning of an armor, and the armor has a cost, and you have been paying it, in silence, for far too long.

I want to add something about control, because it is the visible face of your perfection. You control everything, not because you like to be in charge, but because control is your way of preventing the mistake, and the mistake, in your body, is danger. To control is your way of feeling, for a moment, safe: if I foresee everything, if I watch over everything, if I hold everything, then nothing bad can catch me by surprise.

But total control does not exist, and so your attempt to have it is an endless source of anxiety. Because life is, by nature, uncertain, and there is always what you don't control: other people, chance, the unforeseen. And you, who need to control in order to feel safe, live in permanent war with the uncertainty of life, trying to hold what won't let itself be held, and exhausting yourself in that impossible struggle.

And there is a cost to relationships, because control, applied to people, suffocates. Those who love you feel watched, corrected, managed. And even if you do it out of anxiety, and not out of domination, the effect is the same: the people around you lose their air, they feel that nothing they do is right, that there's always a better way, yours. And they pull away, or shrink, tired of living under a measuring stick that never lets them be at peace.

Letting go of control a little is, therefore, part of this crossing. Not so you become careless, but so you stop exhausting yourself holding the unholdable, and so you let those who live with you breathe. To trust that not everything depends on you, that the world holds together without your permanent vigilance, that you can loosen the reins a little and nothing catastrophic happens, is a concrete way to lower the armor, and to discover that safety did not come, after all, from control, it came from a trust you never allowed yourself to have.

## 4. Who am I when I fail

I'll set a single question down in the middle of the page, like a flaw you are not allowed to correct. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you when you fail? When you aren't flawless? When you fail, simply, as any human being fails?

Stay with it. Notice the terror it brings.

For you, this question touches the deepest fear, because your entire identity was built on being flawless. You are the one who doesn't fail, the one who delivers without a flaw, the competent one, the reliable one, the one others turn to when they want something done well. Take that away, imagine yourself making mistakes in front of everyone, not coping, not being equal to the task, and an abyss opens. Then what am I, if not the one who never fails? What safety is left to me, if I fail? What worth do I have, if I'm not perfect?

The shadow hiding here is your own human fallibility. The possibility of making a mistake, of not coping, of being, simply, human, with the flaws all humans have. That part of you, the one that can fail, terrifies you, because you associated failure with danger, and so you live fighting your own humanity, as if it were a threat lodged inside you.

Notice how this locks you inside the armor with no door out. You cannot allow yourself to be human. You cannot allow yourself the mistake, the doubt, the imperfection that everyone has. You demand of yourself a superhuman perfection, and punish yourself, with immense harshness, whenever you fall short of it. You have become, in the face of your own failure, the harshest judge there is, harsher than anyone who ever hurt you.

And there is a cruelty in this that perhaps you don't see. You treat yourself the way that old danger treated you. You internalized the harshness, the judgment, the punishment in the face of failure, and you apply them to yourself, today, without anyone needing to. The danger from outside became a danger from within, and you continue to live under threat, now your own, your own armed guard against any slip of yours.

I want to tell you something about this terror, because it is here that it begins to lose its force. Making a mistake does not make you unsafe, today. This is the truth your body has not yet learned. In the time when the armor was born, failing brought danger, and your terror was just, it was the only sensible response to that house. But today, in your adult life, a mistake is, most of the time, simply human. It does not bring the catastrophe your body anticipates. People make mistakes, and remain safe, loved, worthy. You can too. The danger of that time has passed, even though your nervous system keeps sounding the fire alarm at the smallest spark, as if the house were still burning.

Don't answer the question of who you are when you fail just yet. It isn't answered with the head, and sometimes it needs more than a book. But leave it open. Because that is the one crack in the armor through which freedom enters. On the day your body learns that failing is no longer dangerous, you stop needing the armor to be safe. And there, only there, can you begin to allow yourself to be human.

And notice how this belief makes you strangely fragile, despite all your competence. Because if your worth depends on not failing, then any failure, however small, is a threat to your very worth. You cannot afford a single mistake, because a mistake, in your inner world, is not a detail, it's a crack in everything that holds you up. You live, therefore, always one mistake away from ceasing to have worth, and that fragility, hidden behind your apparent solidity, explains the permanent tension with which you live.

A person whose worth did not depend on perfection could make a mistake and remain whole. You don't give yourself that right, because you built your entire worth on something you have to prove, without fail, time after time, endlessly. And that is a terrible place to live, because no rest is possible: the worth you have to prove at every instant is never proved once and for all, and so the proof begins again every day, without ever ending.

There is a small sign where this fragility betrays itself, and perhaps you'll recognize it. It is the way you receive a compliment. When someone tells you that you did flawless work, you don't feel clean joy. You feel a stab of fear, because now there is one more person who expects perfection from you, and one more height from which you can fall. The compliment, which should warm, tightens, because you read it as an order for the next success. And you notice, too, how you can't hear praise without dismantling it inside, without thinking that if they knew the effort it cost, or the flaw that nearly slipped through, they wouldn't admire you so. You live receiving applause for a facade, and alone with the certainty that the facade will, one day, crack.

And there is another face of this same belief, which is what you do with the mistakes of others. Because someone who forbids herself to make mistakes tends, without meaning to, to watch the mistakes of others with the same magnifying glass. You notice the colleague's slip, the failure of someone who works with you, the carelessness of someone you love, not out of malice, but because your eye was trained, from early on, to look for the crack through which danger enters. And that attention, which seems like rigor, is the same terror turned outward. When you learn to treat your own failure with gentleness, you will notice that the gaze with which you hem in the failures of others also, slowly, softens, and that the people around you begin, at last, to breathe.

## 5. Perhaps you need more than to understand

I have to lay down my pen and stand still here, where the danger really lived. With all seriousness. This is the most important chapter, and it is different from everything else.

Because what brought you here is not, most of the time, only a personality pattern. It is, very probably, a response to something you lived through. An adaptation to danger. And there is an enormous difference between a wound of character and a trauma response, and that difference changes what you need.

A wound of character is worked through by understanding. You see the pattern, you understand the origin, and that, in time, helps to change it.

A trauma response is something else. It does not live only in the head, it lives in the body. In the nervous system. In the way your organism learned to react to danger, at a level that understanding, alone, does not reach. And a trauma response needs more. It needs understanding, yes, but also the body, time, support, and sometimes treatment.

I want to name, plainly, what may lie beneath your panic about failing. If you grew up with real danger, with violence, with unpredictability, with harsh punishment, with humiliation, then your perfection is not just a trait. It is a survival response to trauma. And the panic you feel in the face of failure is not an exaggeration. It is your nervous system reliving an old danger, engraved deep.

And I want to tell you this with all firmness, and with all tenderness, because it is precisely you, who cannot bear to fail, who finds it hardest to hear. This is not your fault, and it is not resolved by effort alone, nor only by understanding. If your terror of making a mistake is a trauma response, you deserve more than this book. You deserve the support of a professional, a psychologist, a therapist, someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, your nervous system, the memory of danger you carry.

Seeking that help is not admitting that you failed. It is the very reverse of a crack in the armor. It is the most lucid and most courageous act you can have, that of caring for a wound that deserves to be cared for by trained hands. You, who can never fail, perhaps need, more than anyone, to learn that asking for help is not a failure, it is the wisest thing there is.

If you recognize, in your story, real danger, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Don't skip it, you who have the habit of jumping to the next level, to the next demand. The understanding of this book accompanies you. But there are wounds that ask for more than a well-done reading, and the terror of making a mistake, when it comes from a dangerous house, is perhaps one of them. Don't treat it as one more task to carry out perfectly. Treat it as a wound that deserves hands that know how to hold it.

I want you to see how perfection keeps people at a distance, even though it seems the opposite. From the outside, your flawlessness commands respect, admiration. But it creates distance. Because someone who is always perfect is hard to reach, to know, to love up close. She does not show the fragility, the mistake, the doubt, the humanity that brings people closer. And people, faced with a facade without cracks, keep their distance, because there's no way in.

And there is a particular loneliness sealed inside the armor. The loneliness of someone who cannot share her mistakes, her doubts, her fears, with anyone. Of someone who must keep, always, the appearance of someone who handles everything, and who therefore carries alone everything she cannot show. You are surrounded by people who admire you, and alone with the weight of holding up the image they admire. Perfection is a solitary armor, because no one comes inside it.

I want to show you, too, what your perfection does to those who love you, or to those who work with you. They live with the measuring stick you apply to yourself, applied also to them, or at least felt. They feel that it's never good enough, that there's always something to improve, that mistakes are not tolerated. And that, even if you don't say it, weighs, because to live beside someone who doesn't allow herself to make mistakes is to live afraid of making mistakes too. Without meaning to, your armor makes the air around you tense, watched, demanding.

And there is something moving beneath all of this. Perfection is, at bottom, an attempt to be loved and to be safe. You learned that only flawless were you safe, only flawless were you accepted. And you keep trying, with every well-done thing, to win the safety and the love you felt only perfection could give you. But it's an endless race, because perfection is never reached once and for all, and so the safety and the love it promises never come to stay. With every success, you pursue a peace that the next success calls into question again.

I want to tell you something about perfection that may set you free: it does not exist, in any human being. Everyone makes mistakes, fails, falls short. Those who seem flawless either hide their failings, or you don't see them. By demanding of yourself a perfection no one has, you demand the impossible, and you condemn yourself to permanent failure, because the goal, being impossible, is never reached.

Notice the mathematical cruelty of this. You defined your worth by an unreachable goal. Therefore, by definition, you never reach your worth. You live always in deficit, always falling short, always one mistake away from ceasing to have worth, because the measuring stick you use, perfection, is not a human one, it is an impossible one, and no human meets it, and you, who are human, don't either.

And I want you to see the difference between the mistake and the flaw of character, because you confuse them. A mistake is something you do. A flaw of character is something you are. And you, faced with a mistake, do not think "I did something wrong." You think "I am wrong." You turn each slip into proof of your unworthiness, into confirmation that you're no good after all. But a mistake says nothing about your worth. It only says that you are human, like everyone who makes mistakes and still has worth.

The wisest person you know makes mistakes. The most competent one makes mistakes. The one you admire most makes mistakes. And they are not worth less for it. They make mistakes, they learn, they go on, and they remain worthy, capable, loved. Mistakes are part of being a person, and they are not the opposite of worth, they are only the mark of being human. And you have a right to that humanity, as much as anyone you admire who, like you, also fails.

I want to tell you a truth that perhaps no one ever told you: a mistake is not the opposite of good work. It is part of it. No one makes anything of worth without making mistakes along the way. The best in any field make more mistakes than the others, because they try more, risk more, explore more. A mistake is not the sign that something went wrong. It is, very often, the sign that you are attempting something worthwhile.

Your armor taught you the opposite: that a mistake is failure, shame, danger. And so you avoid it, and by avoiding it, you avoid also everything that can only be reached by making mistakes: the new, the difficult, what would make you grow. A life without mistakes is not a perfect life. It is a small life, bound to the safe, to what you already master. And you deserve a big life, and to a big life belong mistakes, many of them, because it is by making mistakes that one goes far.

I want you to begin to see your mistakes differently. Not as proof of your unworthiness, but as signs that you are alive, trying, learning, growing. Each mistake is information, not a sentence. It tells you something about what to adjust, not about who you are. And a person who treats mistakes as information, and not as condemnation, learns faster, risks more, lives more, than one bound to the terror of failing.

## 6. Make a mistake and remain safe

If your perfection is an armor against an old danger, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can make a mistake and remain safe.

Read this slowly, because your body does not yet believe it. For you, making a mistake and danger are the same thing, fused since childhood. And I propose to separate them. Today, in your adult life, making a mistake and danger no longer go together. You can fail, and nothing terrible happens. You can not be equal to the task, and remain safe, loved, worthy. The link between failure and danger, which was real in your past, is no longer real in your present.

I want to be honest, the way the checking part of you wishes everyone would be. This separation is not made simply by convincing yourself of it. If your response is a trauma one, your body will keep sounding the alarm in the face of failure, however much your head knows there's no danger. So this path, very often, is made with help, with someone to accompany you in teaching your nervous system, slowly, that the danger has passed. Needing that is not weakness. It is what this wound asks for.

But there is a path, and it does not pass through one more effort of will, it passes through giving your body new experiences that contradict, slowly, the old lesson. Your nervous system learned that failure equals danger in a concrete house, in concrete situations. It will unlearn in the same way: in concrete situations, small ones, in which failure stops bringing punishment. You don't argue with the body. You show it.

And when this path begins to open, the first sign is not a thought, it is a physical sensation of relief. It is the day you let a mistake slip, and instead of the usual tightness, you feel, for an instant, your shoulders drop, the air come in deeper, a strange calm where the panic used to be. Nothing terrible happened. You failed, and you are still here, whole, safe. It is a short instant, but it is enormous, because it is your body registering, perhaps for the first time in your life, that this time the failure did not bring the storm.

I want to show you how, in practice, the link between making a mistake and danger comes undone, because it is engraved in the body, and the body learns slowly, with evidence.

Begin by allowing yourself small mistakes, on purpose. Things with no real consequence, in which you consciously leave something imperfect. A message with a typo you don't fix. A task you hand in good, but not perfect. A detail you leave unrefined. And you observe what happens, outside and inside.

Outside, almost certainly, nothing terrible. The world does not collapse over that mistake. People barely notice, or they notice and don't mind. The catastrophe your body anticipated does not come. Inside, the alarm rises, the terror, the shame, the urgent desire to correct. And the work is to stay with that alarm, without obeying it, letting the mistake be, until the body discovers that it made a mistake and remained safe.

Each time you do this, you give your system a new piece of evidence. That failing, today, does not bring the danger of back then. And it is with evidence, repeated, in the body, and not only with ideas, in the head, that the armor, slowly, loosens. Perfection is not undone by convincing yourself you can make mistakes. It is undone by making mistakes, in a small way, and surviving, time after time, until the body believes it.

I want to be honest about one thing, because it matters. If yours is a deep trauma response, this exercise, alone, may not be enough, and the alarm may be too strong for you to face by yourself. So this path, very often, is made with help, with someone to accompany you in approaching the mistake safely, at your own pace. Needing that is not weakness. It is what a survival wound asks for.

I want you to see what it truly is to allow yourself to be human, because it is not lowering your standards nor ceasing to care. It is something else, deeper. It is to stop demanding of yourself a superhuman perfection, and to begin treating your mistakes with the understanding you would treat the mistakes of someone you love.

Notice this asymmetry. If a friend of yours made a mistake, what would you tell her? Almost certainly: it's all right, it happens, you're human, that doesn't define you. And to yourself, what do you say, faced with the same mistake? Almost certainly the opposite: you're a disaster, how could you, this proves you're no good. You treat yourself with a harshness you would never use with those you love. Allowing yourself to be human is, first of all, beginning to treat yourself the way you would treat a friend: with gentleness, with understanding, with the certainty that a mistake does not erase the worth of a person.

And allowing yourself to be human is ceasing to be, to yourself, the danger that others were. Because you internalized the harshness, the judgment, the punishment in the face of failure, and you apply them to yourself, today, with no need. There is no one punishing you. It's you. And stopping punishing yourself, ceasing to be your own jailer, is perhaps the most freeing act of this crossing: ceasing to continue, against yourself, the war that others began.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that points to the failure and punishes, is engraved deep, and it will leap up at each mistake with its usual harshness, telling you that you shouldn't have failed, that now they'll see, that this proves what you always feared. Don't expect to silence it all at once, nor would that be realistic, it would just be one more perfection demanding itself of you. Expect, rather, to stop obeying it. To hear it say "you're a disaster, you'll be found out," and to answer it, slowly, "I made a mistake, I am human, and I still have worth, and no one is coming." And each time you choose this gentleness instead of punishment, the old voice weakens a little, and another grows, more your own, more tender, the voice no one gave you in the house where you learned not to fail.

## 7. Allowing myself to be human

There is one verb that takes off the armor, and it is the one your whole life was built against. Allow.

To allow yourself to make mistakes. To allow yourself to be human. To allow yourself the fallibility everyone has, and that you forbade yourself in the name of safety.

Notice that I don't say strive, nor manage, nor correct. I say allow, which is the opposite of the verb that governed your life. All of it was made of guaranteeing, controlling, preventing, letting nothing through. To allow is to open your hand, and to you, who learned that opening your hand was letting danger in, this is the most difficult gesture of all. So I want to show you, slowly, what it means.

To allow yourself to be human is to stop demanding of yourself a superhuman perfection. It is to stop punishing yourself, with immense harshness, for each failure. It is to treat your mistakes like the mistakes of any person, with understanding, and not with the terror and the punishment of someone living under threat.

It is, above all, to stop being, to yourself, the danger that others were. Because you internalized the harshness, the judgment, the punishment in the face of failure, and you apply them to yourself, today, with no need. To allow yourself to be human is to stop treating yourself the way that danger treated you, and to begin treating yourself with the gentleness you deserve.

It will cost you. You will feel, with each mistake you allow yourself, the old terror, the old impulse to correct, to control, to make sure it never happens again. And you will want to put the armor back on, to make yourself flawless, so the terror passes.

But it is precisely there, in that allowing yourself the failure despite the terror, that your healing lies. Because each time you make a mistake, and treat yourself with understanding instead of punishment, you teach yourself something new. That you can fail and remain worthy. That a mistake does not take away your worth. That being human, fallible, imperfect, is not the danger you learned to fear.

I want to tell you something about perfection that may set you free. The perfection you seek does not exist, in any human being. Everyone makes mistakes, fails, falls short. Those who seem flawless either hide their failings, or you don't see them. By demanding of yourself a perfection no one has, you demand the impossible, and you condemn yourself to permanent failure, because the goal, being impossible, is never reached. To allow yourself to be human is, also, to surrender to the fact that perfection is not a real goal, it is an armor, and that setting it down is not giving up on being good, it is ceasing to demand of yourself what is not human.

I won't give you a method, and this path, more than others, is often made with help. A method would only be one more thing to perform flawlessly. So I leave you the principle instead. Allow yourself to make mistakes. Treat your mistakes with the gentleness you would treat the mistakes of someone you love. And discover, slowly, and perhaps with hands to accompany you, that being human, fallible, imperfect, does not take away your safety, it gives you back the life that the armor of perfection was taking from you.

I want to speak to you of what opens up when making a mistake stops being danger, because it is a different life from the one you have.

The courage to risk opens up. Because someone who can make mistakes can try, experiment, dare, create. You stop being bound to the safe, to what you already master, and you can go toward the new, the uncertain, what could be yours if you tried. Perfection bound you to what you knew how to do without failing. The freedom to make mistakes opens up to you everything you don't yet know, and that is learned only by making mistakes.

The possibility of being loved as you are opens up. Because you stop hiding behind the flawless facade, and you allow yourself to be seen fallible, human, imperfect. And you discover that people don't love you less for it, they love you more, because fragility brings people closer, and perfection drives them away. What you feared would take away love, your humanity, is precisely what makes you lovable up close.

And above all, rest opens up. The rest of not having to be flawless at every instant. Of being able to lower your guard. Of living without the endless vigilance of someone who cannot fail. It is a rest you perhaps never knew, the rest of someone who sets down, at last, an armor she had been carrying since childhood, and discovers how light the body is without it.

And I want you to see that allowing yourself to make mistakes does not make you less competent, less careful, less good at what you do. The competence the world admires in you does not fall away with the armor; it stays whole. What changes is that it stops coming from terror, and starts coming from enjoyment. You keep doing things well, but because you want to, and not because you're afraid. And what you do from enjoyment is, in the end, better, freer, more your own, than what you did from fear, because fear shrinks, and enjoyment expands.

I want you to take one thing with you, about those who watch you, if there are those who learn by looking at you. If they see you live in perfection, in panic of mistakes, punishing yourself for each failure, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it's likely they'll put on the same armor, and live the same terror. The most freeing thing you can do for those who watch you is not to give them the example of flawlessness. It is to show them a woman who makes mistakes, and treats herself with gentleness, and remains worthy. Sometimes a woman learns to allow herself to make mistakes, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn those she loves to the same terror of failing.

And I want you to understand that setting down the armor is not giving up on excellence, nor becoming careless. It is ceasing to live in terror, and beginning to do things well out of enjoyment, and not out of panic. The difference may not be seen from the outside, perhaps. But inside, it is the difference between a life and a prison. Between creating and watching over. Between living and surviving. And you deserve the first, after an entire life trapped in the second.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding where the terror of making a mistake comes from already brings relief. But, in the case of someone who made herself flawless in order to survive, understanding may not be enough, and telling you this, instead of promising you an easy cure, is the most honorable way to care for you.

Understanding does not take off the whole armor at once. It does not make you, tomorrow morning, hand in something imperfect without your heart racing, nor leave a typo without the urge to correct it. But it changes enough so that you stop judging yourself for not reaching a perfection no one reaches, and begin to look at your panic about failing not as a defect of temperament, but as a wound that asks for care.

Now you know that you cannot fail, and that this is not vanity, it is an armor.

You know that the armor was born from a real danger, from a time when failing cost dearly.

You know that perfection protects you and crushes you, isolates you and exhausts you.

You know whom you fear to be when you fail, and that you became, to yourself, the harshest judge, the danger from within.

You know that perhaps you need more than to understand, and that seeking help is not failing, it is the wisest thing there is.

You know that you can make a mistake and remain safe, and that the verb is to allow, to allow yourself to be human.

I want to leave you with one picture to carry past the last page.

Imagine someone who lived through a war, and who learned to be always on alert, always in armor, always ready for danger, because lowering her guard, there, could cost her life. And the war ended, long ago, but she still goes about in armor, vigilant, in panic, because her body never knew the war was over.

The crossing is your body learning that the war is over. That you are no longer in that danger. That you can, slowly, and perhaps with someone to help you, set down the armor, lower your guard, and discover that the world, today, is not the battlefield it was. It isn't easy, and sometimes it isn't done alone. But it is possible. And on the other side of the armor is a life you can, at last, live, instead of merely surviving.

I want you to know, too, what to expect from this crossing, so you don't live it as one more thing you have to be perfect at. There will be setbacks. Days when you allow yourself to make mistakes, and days when you put the whole armor back on, in panic, as always. And your old voice will tell you that you failed even at this, that you can't even manage to set down the armor. Don't believe it. The setbacks are not failures along the way, they are part of the way. No one sets down all at once a defense of a whole lifetime. You set it down a little, you put it back on, you set it down again, a little longer, and it is this way, back and forth, slowly, that the body learns. Allowing yourself to retreat without judging yourself for it is, in itself, part of the healing.

And I want you to know that you don't have to make this crossing alone, nor make it perfect. In fact, wanting it perfect would be to put the armor at the service of taking it off, and that doesn't work. This is, perhaps, the first thing in your life that you need to do badly, slowly, stumbling, with mistakes, with help. And perhaps that is exactly why it heals: because it forces you, at last, to give up perfection even in the way you free yourself from it.

Finally, I want to tell you something simple, to close. You were not wrong. The girl who made herself flawless in order to be safe did what she had to do, and she did it well. There is nothing to correct in her, nothing to reproach. There is only one thing to say to her, slowly, time after time, until the body believes it: it has passed. You are safe now. You can set that down now.

And if you take a single sentence closed in your chest as you leave these pages, take this one. You do not have to deserve rest by being perfect first. Rest is not a prize you earn at the end of a flawless life. It is a right you have now, today, with all the mistakes you will still make, simply for being human, and for being no longer in the war where failing cost dearly.

And you already recognize, deep down, the sentence that holds everything I have told you here. I can make a mistake and remain safe.

Allowing yourself to read it was the first step. There is a second, and that one does not fit in a book. It is made in life, by allowing yourself, slowly, the failure you always forbade yourself. And, very often, it is made with help, with someone prepared to accompany your body in learning that the danger has passed. Because a survival wound deserves hands that know how to hold it, and seeking them is not failing. It is caring, at last, for someone who learned she could never fail.

And before you close the book, I leave you a single request. I know that, for you, it is one of the hardest there is.

This week, leave something imperfect, on purpose. Small, with no real consequence. A mistake you would normally correct, a detail you would leave unrefined. And don't correct it. And observe your body, the alarm that rises, the terror, the urge to fix it. And stay with that, without fixing it. Because it is in that allowing, endured despite the terror, that your body begins, slowly, to learn that it made a mistake and remained safe, and that the war, after all, is over.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
