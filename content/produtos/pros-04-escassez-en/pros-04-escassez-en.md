# The woman who inherited scarcity

**For the woman who comes from where there was lack and does not let herself have. Because prospering does not erase your origin nor betray those who came before you.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks about carries an ancient loyalty to lack, and there is a point where that loyalty stops being memory and becomes an anguish that tightens whenever life wants to give her more. If you recognize in yourself a persistent anxiety around having, a guilt that robs you of sleep, or a weight that does not lift, this may have roots that deserve the care of a professional. Asking for help betrays no one. It is beginning to let yourself have that too.*

---

## 1. The fear of having

There is a fear you carry and that you have perhaps never recognized as fear, because it dresses itself as prudence, as modesty, as good sense: the fear of having. Not the fear of not having, that one you know well, that one you inherited from those who raised you. I speak of the other, the one almost no one names: the fear of truly having, of prospering, of receiving abundance, of life giving you more than what you are used to. Because when abundance draws near to you, instead of embracing it, you draw back. Instead of joy, you feel a tightening. Instead of "finally," you feel "no, this is not for me."

Notice how this governs you, in the small things and the large. You have difficulty spending on yourself, even when you can, even when you need to, because there is a voice that says it is not for you, that it is wasteful, that you should save, that you don't have the right. When money starts to be left over, you find, without realizing it, ways of making it disappear, expenses, helping others, unexpected costs, as if a comfortable balance left you uncomfortable. You sabotage opportunities to grow even when you want them, you draw back at the edge of more, you find reasons not to advance precisely when life was opening the door for you. And when, despite everything, some abundance comes in, you don't enjoy it in peace, you live it with a dull guilt, looking over your shoulder, like someone who received something she shouldn't have.

And there is a larger pattern beneath all this, which is the heart of this book: you do not let yourself have. It is not that life does not want to give to you. It is that you, at the entrance, refuse, sabotage, shrink, because having more than where you came from fills you, deep down, not with joy, but with an anguish you do not understand. As if prospering were dangerous. As if there were, in the act of having, a betrayal, an abandonment, a breaking of something sacred. And that anguish, ancient and silent, is stronger than any conscious will to grow, and so you draw back time after time, without realizing that it is you, and not life, who shuts the door.

I want to name where this comes from, because it is the heart of everything: you come from where there was lack. From a family, a lineage, an origin where money was scarce, where pennies were counted, where deprivation was known, the fear of the end of the month, the weight of not having enough. And that lack was not only a material circumstance, it was an emotional ground, a way of seeing the world that engraved itself in you before words: the world is a place of scarcity, having is difficult, money is a worry, abundance belongs to others, to a class, to a world that is not yours. You grew up inside that truth, and it became yours, so much yours that prospering sounds to you, even today, like leaving your place, like becoming a person who is no longer from there.

I want you to know, right at the start, that this book does not come to tell you that your origin was a mistake, that you should renounce it, that the poverty you came from is something to rid yourself of in shame. On the contrary. Your origin is yours, and there is dignity in it, strength, and a history that deserves respect. I came to look at something else: at the silent loyalty that binds you to lack, and that turns every possibility of prospering into a kind of betrayal, making of you a woman who could have and does not let herself, who sees abundance at the door and sends it away, faithful to a scarcity that is no longer hers, but that she goes on feeling as the only place where she belongs.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read with a tight chest: prospering does not erase your origin. It does not separate you from those who came before, it does not betray those who had less, it does not turn you into a person who forgot her own. This idea, which seems simple, goes against a very deep fear, and so it will cost you to accept it. But it is the one that opens the door to something you never allowed yourself: to have without guilt, to prosper without betrayal, to let life give you more without that meaning leaving behind those you came from, and that, deep down, it is out of love for them that you do not let yourself have.

There is a way of thinking that you may recognize, and that is scarcity speaking inside you even when there is plenty in front of you. It is the mind that, faced with abundance, can only see what might be lacking: you have money but you live afraid of losing it, you have security but you feel it is fragile, you have enough but you go on feeling that it is not enough, that it might run out, that it is better not to trust. The scarcity you inherited is not only a lack of goods, it is a way of inhabiting the world, a lens through which even abundance is seen as precarious, threatened, about to disappear. And so, even when life gives to you, you cannot rest in what you have, because your system goes on operating as if lack were always at the door. Scarcity has become an inner state, and an inner state travels with you even into plenty, and spoils it, and keeps you from living it in peace.

And I want you to notice something that distinguishes this wound from the others in this collection, because it is important: your problem is not not knowing how to earn, nor not knowing how to receive in general. There are women who earn well, who rise, who achieve, and who even so feel this tightening, this guilt, this discomfort with having, because the wound is not in the capacity to produce, it is in the permission to have more than the origin had. You can be very capable, very competent, very successful in the eyes of the world, and still sabotage the enjoyment, shrink what you show, live your own abundance as if it were lent, or stolen, or about to be taken from you. It is not a problem of competence. It is a problem of belonging. And that is why no increase in income, by itself, resolves it: because what is at stake is not how much you earn, it is whether you give yourself permission to keep what you earned.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in concrete scenes. It's feeling uncomfortable when you earn more than your parents ever earned, as if there were something wrong in surpassing them. It's not being able to buy something good for yourself without hearing, inside, the voice of those who never could. It's minimizing what you have when you are with your family, dressing more simply, talking less about what you achieved, so as not to stand out, so as not to make them feel bad, so as to go on being one of them. It's having, and feeling, instead of enjoyment, a dull guilt of someone who received what others lacked. They are all the same: the loyalty to lack, which binds you to the place you came from and does not let you advance toward where you could go.

## 2. Loyalty to lack

There is something that needs to be understood, because it explains your resistance to more, and it is one of the most beautiful and most painful things there is in families: loyalty. We belong to those who raised us, to those who came before, to the lineage we came out of, and that belonging is one of the deepest needs of the human being. And one of the ways our love guarantees that belonging is likeness: we are like our own, we share their destiny, their ground, their difficulties, and in that sharing we feel ourselves part, connected, loyal. To be like them is a way of saying to them "I am yours, I belong here, I have not separated from you."

Now notice what this does when you come from where there was lack. If your people went through deprivations, if they knew scarcity, if lack was their ground, then a part of you, without deciding to, feels that to be like them is also to share that lack. That to have little is to be with them, and to have much is to separate. That scarcity is the territory of the family, and abundance is an exile. And so, when abundance draws near, an ancient alarm goes off: if I have what they did not have, I stop being one of them, I betray their experience, I abandon the common destiny, I become a stranger, one who "got above herself," one who rose and forgot where she came from. And that alarm is so strong, because it touches belonging, that you prefer to draw back, to stay in the lack, to keep yourself loyal, rather than risk the separation that prospering seems to bring.

In the way I learned to look at these stories, there is a hidden question here that governs everything, and it is worth bringing it to light: do I have permission to have what they did not have? Notice the question. It is not "am I capable of having," because capable you are. It is "do I have permission," "do I have the right," "may I have what was lacking to those I love without betraying them by it?". And the answer, engraved deep, is no. A part of you feels that to have what they did not have is a kind of disloyalty, almost a cruelty, as if prospering were rubbing their lack in their face, as if your abundance were an accusation against their poverty. And out of love, out of loyalty, out of not wanting to wound them nor separate from them, you do not give yourself that permission, and you stay, voluntarily, within the measure of what they had, so as to go on being one of them.

Notice that this loyalty can be so strong that it makes you, without your knowing it, recreate the lack even when life gives you plenty. You earn well and, mysteriously, the money disappears. Your income rises and, in the same step, expenses appear that consume it. You draw near to a comfortable life and, without realizing it, you make decisions that bring you back to the familiar tightness where you feel at home. It is not bad luck, nor is it a lack of skill with figures. It is the loyalty to lack operating behind the scenes, always leading you back to the level of scarcity you know, because it is at that level that you feel yourself one of them, it is at that level that belonging is guaranteed. Your system has a thermostat set to lack, and whenever life heats you above it, it cools you back down, brings you to the familiar temperature, even though that temperature makes you suffer. To see this thermostat is the first step toward being able, slowly, to set it to another number.

And there is a particularly moving form of this, when someone in the family suffered greatly from lack. Perhaps you have a mother who went without everything, a father who worked a lifetime and never had, grandparents who knew hunger. And there is in you a sense that you do not have the right to have what they could not have, that it would be unfair, that how are you to live well if they lived so badly. It is as if prospering would make their pain even greater by contrast, and your loyalty, your love, told you not to increase it, to stay close, in the lack, with them, instead of rising into a comfort they never knew and that, by having it, would separate you from them forever. You stay poor, or tight, or without letting yourself have, as a way of not leaving them alone in their deprivation.

There is a way of seeing families that sheds light on this, and that is worth knowing. To belong to a group, to a family, requires, without anyone saying it, sharing in its common destiny. There is a silent conscience, a kind of invisible scale, that watches who stays and who moves away, who shares the group's fortune and who separates from it. And that conscience does not distinguish between moving away for the worse and moving away for the better: whoever falls below the group betrays it as much as whoever rises above it, because both break the equality that maintains belonging. That is why prospering triggers the same guilt that falling into disgrace would: in both cases, you stop being equal to your own, and the conscience of the group registers that as a separation. Understanding this helps, because it shows you that your guilt of prospering is neither irrational nor a defect of yours, it is the working of an ancient mechanism of belonging, which confuses rising with abandoning. And mechanisms, when they are seen, can be revised.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because the tendency will be to think yourself foolish for it, or to feel anger toward those who transmitted scarcity to you. It is no foolishness at all, it is love, it is the deepest form of loyalty, and whoever transmitted it to you did not want to trap you, they transmitted to you the only relationship with having that they knew. The loyalty to lack is not a defect of yours. It is the proof of how much you love and of how much you need to belong. Except that that love, carried like this, turned against you and against them: against you, because it keeps you from living; and against them, because your voluntary deprivation gives them nothing back, does not diminish their past pain, helps them in nothing. Your lack of today does not cure the lack of yesterday. It only multiplies scarcity by one more generation, yours, with no benefit to anyone.

## 3. Abundance threatens belonging

I am going to tell you something that may seem strange to you, but that is the heart of the mechanism: your shadow is not poverty. It is abundance. What truly frightens you, deep down, is not to go on having little, with that you even feel at home. What frightens you is to have much, to prosper, to rise, because it is having, and not lack, that threatens the thing you most fear losing: belonging.

Notice this inversion, because it explains so much. You would expect a person who came from scarcity to desire abundance without reservation, to run toward it, to embrace it as soon as it appeared. And yet that is not what happens with you. When abundance arrives, you hesitate, draw back, sabotage, feel uncomfortable, because it brings with it a threat that poverty never brought: that of separating you from your own. Poverty kept you with them, in the same boat, in the same struggle. Abundance takes you out of the boat, puts you in another place, transforms you into someone different, someone who has what they do not have, and that difference, for someone who loves belonging so much, is terrifying. That is why your shadow is having: because having is separating, and separating is your greatest wound.

Notice that there is a subtle difference here in relation to other wounds with money, and it is worth seeing. It is not that you think yourself incapable of having, nor that you think you do not deserve it for not striving enough. It is something more relational, more tied to belonging: you could have, you know you deserve it, but you do not let yourself, because having would separate you. The obstacle is not between you and money, it is between you and your own, in the fear that money will come between you, that abundance will create a distance that lack never created. That is why many women in this situation can only prosper when they find a way of not separating themselves while prospering: by sharing everything, sustaining the whole family, living modestly despite being able to do more, as if they had to pay, with the apparent permanence in lack, for the right to have risen. Notice whether you do this: whether your way of allowing yourself to have more is not to show it, not to enjoy it, to distribute it all, so that no one notices that you actually separated yourself. It is the loyalty to lack negotiating with you, letting you have provided you do not seem to have.

And I want to show you how this shows itself in ways you may never have connected to this fear. It is the strange sadness you feel when something goes too well for you. It is the discomfort of being the one who "made it" in the family, the one who prospered, and the sense that this isolates you, sets you apart, transforms you into the stranger around whom others are no longer completely at ease. It is the way you, without meaning to, minimize your success, hide your comfort, share too much of what you earn, as if apologizing for having, buying the right to go on belonging despite no longer being in the lack. All of this is abundance being lived as a threat to belonging, and your response trying to repair the separation that having seems to cause.

There is a scene that repeats itself in many families, and perhaps in yours, and it is worth naming it. It is the person who prospers and who, because of that, comes to be treated differently by the family. Who is seen as "the rich one," who is the target of a quiet envy, of a new distance, of comments, of requests, of a mixture of pride and resentment. And that person, feeling the separation that her success created, either pulls away, or pays, or shrinks, to try to recover the place she had when she was equal to the others. If you saw this, you learned early the price of prospering: that prospering costs belonging, that whoever rises is treated like someone who betrayed, and you drew, without deciding to, the conclusion that it was safer not to rise, to stay equal, to keep yourself within the common measure, so as not to pay that price.

I want to tell you something about this threat, because it begins to disarm itself when it is seen. The separation you fear is not caused by abundance. It is caused by a way of prospering that abandons, that pulls away, that forgets, that looks down. But that is not the only way of prospering. There is another, and it is the one this book wants to show you: that of prospering while staying connected, of having without separating yourself, of rising taking your own with you, in your heart, in respect, in connection. The fear tells you that there are only two options: either you stay in the lack and belong, or you prosper and separate. It is a lie. There is a third, and it is the only one that frees you: to prosper and to belong at the same time, because your belonging, the true one, never depended on your having little, it depended on your loving, and love you can do the same, or more, from abundance.

## 4. Betraying those who had less

I am going to let fall here, with weight, the question that lies beneath all your resistance to more, and I ask you not to flee from it, even if it tightens your chest, because it is the question that decides everything.

How can I have what was lacking to those I love?

Stay with it. Notice the guilt it brings, the sense that prospering is, somehow, a betrayal.

This is the turning point of the book, and I want you to live it slowly. Beneath your fear of having, your loyalty to lack, the way abundance threatens you, there is a guilt, and it is the guilt of prospering beyond the origin. The sense that, by having more than your own had, you do them a kind of injustice, you leave them behind, you rise at the cost of a difference that diminishes them. As if abundance were a fixed quantity, and what you have extra were what they lacked. As if prospering were, deep down, taking something from them, or at least abandoning them in their lack while you go on toward comfort.

I want you to feel the weight of this guilt, because it is what you have been carrying without knowing. Imagine what it is to desire a better life and, at the same time, to feel that having it is betraying those you love. To want to prosper and to feel that prospering is a cruelty toward those who could not. It is a terrible trap, because it binds you on both sides: if you do not prosper, you suffer the frustration of a tight life that did not need to be so; if you prosper, you suffer the guilt of having surpassed your own. And in doubt, your love almost always chooses the lack, chooses to stay with them in the deprivation, because the guilt of betraying them is more unbearable than the frustration of not living. You sacrifice your life on the altar of a loyalty that no one asked of you, and that benefits no one.

And here is what needs to be said, and said with all clarity: your deprivation gives nothing back to anyone. This is the truth that the guilt hides. If your mother went without, your being deprived too does not give her back what she lost. If your grandparents went hungry, your going through tightness does not feed them retroactively. Your lack of today does not cure nor honor the lack of yesterday, it has no effect on it, it changes nothing for those who suffered. The only thing your deprivation does is extend scarcity by one more generation, with no benefit to those who came before and with enormous harm to you, and to those who come after. You are paying a debt that does not exist, on an account that no one opened, with a currency, your own life, that buys no relief for those you say you want to honor.

I want to stop here a moment, because there is a point at which this guilt stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If the anguish around having robs you of sleep, if there is an anxiety that tightens whenever life wants to give you more, if you feel a weight that does not lift, a deep sadness tied to your origin, or if you recognize stories of real deprivation, of hunger, of material trauma in your family that still today run through your body, then this deserves to be cared for with support. There are wounds of scarcity that were engraved by true suffering, over generations, and that are not resolved by reading alone nor by force of will. To say it is the most honest way of taking care of you: the understanding in this book opens the door, but there are crossings that are better made accompanied, with someone to help you undo, safely, a knot that comes from very far and that weighs a great deal.

I want you to feel too the injustice of this guilt, because seeing it as unjust helps to let it go. Notice: no one in fact asked you to stay in the lack. Those who love you did not tell you "please, suffer too, do not have more than us." It was you, out of love, out of loyalty, out of an ancient mechanism of belonging, who made that silent decision for them, in their name, without their having asked it. The guilt of prospering is, almost always, an imagined, projected guilt, an account that you opened in their place and that you insist on paying as if they were collecting on it, when in truth most of them, if they knew, would be horrified to learn that their daughter, their granddaughter, deprives herself out of loyalty to their pain. You turned their love into a demand that they never made, and that invented demand is what binds you, not them.

Let me tell you where this usually comes from with the most force. Sometimes there is, in the family, a phrase, a look, an implicit message that prospering is betraying. "You think you're better than us." "You've gotten full of luxuries." "You're not the same anymore." "You forgot where you came from." Perhaps you heard this, or you fear hearing it, or you saw it said to someone in the family who dared to rise. And that threat, real or feared, taught you that the price of prospering is the symbolic expulsion from the clan, the loss of the love of your own, the transformation into a stranger. And a child, or a young woman, or even an adult woman, faced with the choice between prospering and being loved by her own, almost always chooses to be loved, and stays small, stays in the lack, stays loyal, because no material comfort makes up for the loss of belonging to one's own blood.

## 5. Prospering does not erase the origin

If there is one sentence I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it carefully, because it dismantles the guilt that binds you: prospering does not erase your origin. It does not separate you from those who came before, it does not renounce where you came from, it does not betray the lack you knew. On the contrary. Your origin goes with you, it is part of who you are, it is in your strength, in your resilience, in your capacity to value what you have precisely for having known what it is not to have. Prospering does not take your origin from you. It honors it, if you know how to prosper carrying it with you.

Notice that there is a difference between the separation you fear and the differentiation that life asks for. To separate would be to cut, to forget, to look down, to renounce where you came from, and that, yes, would be a painful loss, for you and for them. But that is not what prospering requires. What prospering requires is differentiation: becoming yourself, with your life, your level, your path, distinct from that of your own, without ceasing to love them nor to belong. To differentiate is not to separate. A child who grows and has a life different from the parents' does not abandon them, he becomes an adult, and that is what healthy parents want. You confused differentiation with betrayal, growing with abandoning, and so you stayed small so as not to differentiate yourself. But you can be different from them and deeply of them at the same time, you can have a life they did not have and remain entirely connected to who they are. Differentiation is, in fact, the sign of a mature belonging: I belong so much that I can be myself without fear of losing myself from you.

Notice what is freeing in this idea. Your whole resistance rested on a false equation: to have much equals to separate myself from my own. And now I propose that you see the truth: what connects you to your own was never the lack. It was the love, the shared history, the blood, the memory, the gratitude. And none of that disappears when you prosper. You can have a better house than your parents' and love them exactly the same. You can earn more than anyone in your family ever earned and remain deeply one of them. Abundance does not erase a single memory, does not cut a single bond, does not diminish a gram of love. The separation you feared was an illusion of the guilt, not a real consequence of having.

There is a movement that frees, and I want to show it to you without making it a recipe, because it is the opposite of staying in the lack. First, to see: that you do not let yourself have, and where, and how you sabotage. Then, to recognize where the loyalty to lack comes from, and that it was your way of belonging, not a defect. Then, and this is the hard step, to give back the guilt that does not belong to you: to look, inside, at those who went through deprivation, and to say something like "your lack was yours, and I honor it, but it is not mine to repeat; I do not owe you my smallness, I owe you my harvest; I receive, in your name, what you could not have." Then, to take life with both hands: to let yourself have, to fill what was always empty, without apologizing. And finally, to transmit: to make of the abundance you receive a new inheritance, so that those who come after you do not inherit the lack, but the possibility. A loyalty that comes from so many generations does not unravel at the turn of a page. It is a crossing. But the direction is this, and it is the opposite of shrinking.

I'll say it another way, deeper. Imagine what your people who went through lack would truly want for you, if you could ask them with the heart. A mother who went without, a father who toiled, grandparents who knew hunger, what would they want for the daughter, the granddaughter, the descendant? Would they want her to go through the same? Would they want her to stay in the lack out of loyalty to them? Or would they want, with all their soul, for her to have what they could not have, to live better, to prosper, for their struggle to have served precisely so that someone after them could, at last, not go without? Almost always, this is what those who truly love us want: that we be the proof that it was worth it. Your prosperity is not a betrayal of their sacrifice. It is its harvest. It is what gives meaning to all that they went through.

And here is the inversion that frees, and I want you to feel it: it is staying in the lack that betrays their wish. To go on tight, deprived, without letting yourself have, out of a misunderstood loyalty, is what makes their sacrifice useless, is what causes everything they toiled at to have served for nothing, is what perpetuates the scarcity they so wanted to end. The true loyalty is not to repeat the lack. It is to receive them in your abundance, it is to prosper in their name, it is to make the deprivation they knew stop in you, and turn, in your life and in that of your children, into the plenty they dreamed of and did not have. Prospering is, after all, the highest way of honoring them.

The awakening, when it comes, does not come like an idea, it comes like a deep permission, almost a forgiveness. It is the moment when you feel, inside, even if only for an instant, that you have permission to have. That prospering does not make you bad, does not separate you, does not turn you into a stranger. That you can rise and go on loving, have and go on belonging, live well and honor in exactly that way those who lived badly. It is an instant that can bring tears, because it undoes a guilt of a whole lifetime, and because you feel, perhaps for the first time, that your own, instead of reproaching you from the past, smile at you, relieved that the lack, at last, in you, has found its end.

## 6. Honoring without repeating

There is a confusion at the center of everything, and that I want to undo carefully, because it is from it that your prison is born: you confused honoring your origin with repeating your origin. As if the only way of respecting the lack you came from were to go on in the lack. As if honoring the poverty of your own required remaining poor, or tight, or without letting yourself have. And it is not. There is an enormous difference between honoring and repeating, and it is in that difference that your freedom dwells.

Notice the difference. To honor your origin is to recognize it, to respect it, to carry it with you with gratitude, not to forget where you came from, to value the struggle of those who preceded you, to keep in your heart the memory of the lack as a root, not as a chain. To repeat your origin is to stay in it, to reproduce the scarcity, to live the same deprivation, as if changing were disrespect. The first is mature love, the second is blind loyalty. And you, without knowing it, chose the second, thinking it was love, when true love asked precisely the opposite: that you receive what they could not, so that their struggle would bear fruit.

Notice that repeating the lack has yet another cost that is rarely seen: it robs you of the capacity to be grateful for abundance. Whoever stays in scarcity out of loyalty never gets to savor what she has, because she lives always looking at what might be lacking, distrusting what she receives, sabotaging what grows. And so she loses not only the material plenty, but the joy, the peace, the gratitude, which are, deep down, the true wealth. To honor the origin by repeating the lack does not give you the purity you imagine, it gives you a tight and anxious life, the same one your own had, but without the necessity that at least justified it in them. They went without because they had to go without. You go without out of loyalty to a necessity that no longer exists, and so your lack is, of the two, the saddest, because it is the only one that could have been avoided.

And I want to warn you of a subtle trap that usually appears here, and that is the reverse of the guilt: the romanticizing of poverty. There are those who, in order to keep themselves loyal to lack, beautify it, turn it into a virtue, into a sign of purity, of authenticity, of moral superiority. "It's the poor who are happy." "Money spoils people." "We had nothing but we were more united." And there is partial truth in this, of course, but when it becomes a way of not prospering, of despising abundance so as not to have to seek it, it is a trap. Because to romanticize the lack is another way of being loyal to it, it is to make of scarcity a noble choice instead of recognizing it as what it was, a real difficulty that caused real suffering. To honor poverty is not to romanticize it. It is to respect those who lived it without pretending it was good, and to want, precisely because it was not good, for it to end.

And I want to show you what it is to truly honor, because it is beautiful and it is the opposite of repeating. To honor is to say, inside, to those who came before: "I saw your lack. I know what you went through. I carry you with me, with gratitude and with respect, in everything I am. And it is precisely because I love you and because I know your struggle that I am going to have what you did not have, live what you could not live, prosper where you went without. Not to forget you, but so that your sacrifice, at last, bears fruit. My abundance is your harvest. I receive it in your name." This is honor. And notice how it does not bind you to the lack, it frees you from it, and frees them too, because it transforms their deprivation from a meaningless pain into a seed that finally germinated.

I want to leave you with a question that can guide you, because it cuts straight to the root: if whoever suffered most from lack in my family saw me now, and loved me as I know they love me, would they want me to repeat their deprivation, or to leave it behind? Ask yourself this question when the guilt of prospering tightens around you. And listen to the answer honestly, because it is almost always the same: whoever loves you does not want you to suffer what they suffered, they want you free of it. The guilt tells you that prospering offends them. Their love tells you the opposite: that your full life is the meaning of everything they endured. And between the voice of the guilt and the voice of the love, you can, slowly, learn to choose the second, because it is the second, and not the first, that tells the truth about what those who preceded you truly wished for you.

I am not going to give you a method for honoring without repeating, because it would be one more task, and we would be feeding your tendency to complicate what asks only for permission. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because undoing a loyalty of generations is not done in a page. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: carry your origin in your heart, not in your wallet. Honor it with memory and gratitude, not with deprivation. Let the lack you came from be the root that gives you strength, and not the chain that binds you, and discover, slowly, that prospering with your origin in your arms is the most complete way of respecting it.

---

## 7. Prospering with the origin in your arms

There is an image I want to leave you with, because it is the destination of this crossing: to prosper with the origin in your arms. Not to prosper leaving your own behind, which is what you feared. Not to stay in the lack so as not to leave them, which is what you were doing. But a third thing, the only one that frees: to advance toward abundance taking them with you, in your heart, in respect, in connection, like someone climbing a mountain without letting go of the hand of the one she loves.

I want you to think, for a moment, of all the women of your lineage who came before you, in a line, mother behind mother, grandmother behind grandmother, each with her lack, her struggle, her not-being-able. And imagine that each of them, in her time, dreamed, even without saying it, of a better life, a life in which there was no lack, in which one could rest, in which there was plenty. That dream passed from hand to hand, generation after generation, without being realized, because the time and the world did not allow it. And now you arrive, and in you, perhaps for the first time, that dream is possible. You are the point where the dream of all of them can, at last, come true. To refuse it, to stay in the lack out of loyalty, is to let the desire of generations die with you. To realize it, to prosper, to have, to live with room to breathe, is to do it for all of them, it is the harvest of a sowing of centuries. When you see it this way, prospering stops being a selfish act and becomes an act of love for your whole lineage, the way of honoring not one, but all those who dreamed of what you can now live.

Notice what this resolves. Your fear told you that you had to choose between prospering and belonging, between comfort and your own, between abundance and loyalty. And that choice was impossible, because you wanted both things, and any renunciation cost you too much. To prosper with the origin in your arms dissolves the choice: you no longer have to abandon anyone in order to rise, nor to stay below in order to belong. You take them with you. Your abundance does not leave them behind, it honors them; your rise does not separate you, it connects you even more, because now you can even, if you want and if it makes sense, share what you have, relieve those you love, be in the family the one who broke the cycle and opened the way for the others.

And I want to show you a concrete way of doing it, that changes the relationship with money itself. For you, money is loaded with fear, with guilt, with tightness, it is a source of anxiety and not of freedom, because you associated it with lack, with struggle, with the weight of those who never have enough. To carry the origin in your arms includes changing your relationship with money itself: letting it be a neutral resource, an energy that circulates, a tool of life, and not a minefield of loyalties and guilts. Notice how you talk about money, how you treat it, how you react when it comes and when it goes. You probably inherited a tense, distrustful, almost superstitious relationship with it. And that relationship you can begin to remake, slowly, letting money come in without alarm, stay without guilt, be used with joy and managed with calm, instead of always being a threat or a betrayal. When money stops carrying the scarcity of your own, it begins, at last, to be able to circulate freely in your life, because you stopped blocking it with the weight of a history that does not have to be your relationship with it.

And I want to speak to you about a concrete way of carrying the origin in your arms, which is to let the scarcity stop in you. You are, perhaps, the first of your lineage who can truly prosper. And that gives you an enormous power and a beautiful responsibility: that of being the point where the lack ends. Where the inheritance of scarcity, passed from generation to generation, finally interrupts itself, and gives way to a new inheritance, of abundance, of possibility, of "you can have," that you will pass on to those who come after. Your children, or whoever comes next, will not inherit the fear of having, the guilt of prospering, the loyalty to lack, because you, in yourself, transformed the scarcity into plenty. This is not betraying the origin. It is being its best descendant, the one who received the struggle of all those who came before and converted it, at last, into fruit.

I want to speak to you about a true generosity that only abundance permits, because it answers your guilt in another way. When you are in the lack, you want to help your own and cannot, or you help by taking from yourself, emptying yourself, sharing a scarcity that is not enough for anyone. When you prosper, you gain, for the first time, the possibility of truly helping, without destroying yourself: of supporting those you love, of relieving those who need, of being, in the family, a source and not one more mouth. Your guilt tells you that having more than your own is abandoning them. But notice the inversion: it is precisely by prospering that you gain the power to take care of them, whereas by staying in the lack you can only offer them company in the deprivation. Whoever loves and prospers can become the shoulder of the whole family. Whoever loves and stays small can only share the smallness. Abundance does not pull you away from those you love, it gives you, at last, the means to support them.

There is a fear you may have, and I want to go to meet it: the fear that prospering will change you, make you frivolous, materialistic, one of those people whom money spoils. Notice that this fear is the romanticizing of poverty speaking, and answer it calmly. Money is not going to turn you into another person. It is going to amplify who you already are. If you are generous, you will have more to give. If you are connected to your own, you will have more to support them. If you value what you have precisely for having known lack, you will prosper with a gratitude that those who never went without rarely have. Your origin, far from being erased by abundance, is precisely what guarantees that abundance does not spoil you, because you know what it is not to have, and that, no one takes from you, and it is your greatest protection against the frivolity you fear.

I want you to think about what you are transmitting to those who come after you, because it may move you more than anything. If you have children, or if there are children who see you live, notice what they are learning from you about having. If they see you shrink, sabotage yourself, live with fear of abundance and guilt of comfort, they are inheriting the same loyalty to lack, and it is likely that one day they will wear it, and that they will deprive themselves too, out of loyalty to you, as you deprive yourself out of loyalty to your own. Scarcity is passed on like this, from generation to generation, not in the genes, but in the example, in the atmosphere, in the relationship with having that is breathed at home. And you have the power to break that transmission. When you let yourself prosper with peace, with gratitude, without guilt, you are teaching those who come after that having is possible, that abundance is not betrayal, that one can live well without ceasing to be a good person nor to love where one comes from. It is perhaps the greatest gift you can give: not romanticized scarcity, but the permission of a full life.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that you may not even remember wanting, so used are you to the lack: the joy of having without guilt. Of receiving and enjoying. Of living with room to breathe without looking over your shoulder. Of buying something good for yourself and feeling enjoyment, not shame. Of seeing the balance grow and feeling security, not discomfort. Of prospering and sleeping in peace, knowing that you betrayed no one, that you honored those you love precisely by having dared to have what was lacking to them. That joy is your right, as much yours as anyone's, and it is also, notice, the most beautiful gift you can give to those who came before you: the living proof that their struggle was worth it.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in a simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything at once, and it doesn't undo a loyalty of generations in a day. But it changes enough for you to stop feeling bad, or like a traitor, or like a stranger, for desiring and for having a better life than the one you came from.

Now you know that you are afraid of having, and not only of not having, and that this fear is a loyalty to lack. You know that the question that governs you is whether you have permission to have what they did not have, and that your engraved answer was no. You know that your shadow is not poverty, it is abundance, because it is having that threatens belonging. You know, and this was the hardest, that you carry the guilt of prospering beyond the origin, and that your deprivation gives nothing back to anyone, it only extends scarcity by one more generation. You know that prospering does not erase the origin, and that it is staying in the lack that betrays the wish of those who loved you. You know how to distinguish honoring from repeating, and that romanticizing poverty is another way of being loyal to it. And you know that you can prosper with the origin in your arms, being the point where the lack, at last, ends.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it sums everything up. Imagine a chain of people, your lineage, passing from hand to hand, over generations, one same nearly empty bucket, with a little water at the bottom, and each one drinking only a sip, carefully, so that there is some left for the next, all of them thirsty, all loyal to the little there is. And you arrive, and in front of you, for the first time, there is a spring, water gushing, abundant, for everyone. And you, out of loyalty, hesitate to drink, hesitate to fill the bucket, because those who came before never could, and to drink your fill seems to you to betray them. But notice: they passed that nearly empty bucket precisely in the hope that one day someone would reach the spring. You are that person. And the way of honoring them is not to drink only a sip as they drank. It is to fill the bucket, to quench your thirst, and to leave the spring open for all who come after you, so that no one in your lineage ever has to count the sips again. The crossing is to drink. In the name of all those who were thirsty.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can prosper without abandoning those who came before.

Seeing this was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not made by reading, it is made by undoing, slowly, a loyalty of generations, and by giving yourself, time after time, the permission to have.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about money, nor is it going to teach you to earn more. It is a space for you to look at the way you keep value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the deepest and most moving ways: that of not letting yourself have, out of loyalty to those who had less. There, the crossing is to undo the loyalty to lack, to give yourself permission to prosper, and to learn to honor your origin without repeating it, to carry it in your arms instead of using it as a chain. Not so that you forget where you come from, but so that where you come from stops being the measure of how far you can go.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There, the crossing includes ceasing to level your life downward, to shrink it so as not to stand out, to keep yourself small out of loyalty to the group. Here, the crossing is to stop leveling your life by the lack you came from, to keep it tight out of loyalty to the lineage. One shrinks for the peers, the other for the origin. But deep down it is the same journey: the permission to be greater than what loyalty confined you to, without ceasing to belong to anyone.

If you feel that it is the moment, you can find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is all right, and you betrayed no one by reading. What you have read is already yours, and it stays with you, without guilt, waiting, with no hurry, for the day you give yourself permission to have the rest too.

And before you close these pages, I ask you one thing only, and it will be, for you, one of the hardest there is, and that is why I ask it of you. Today, or in these days, receive or buy or accept something good that comes to you in excess, something your origin did not know, and instead of living it with guilt, dedicate it, inside, to those who came before you. Say, in silence: "This is for you, and in your name. Your lack ends here." Notice the guilt trying to appear, and answer it with this dedication. Because it is in that receiving dedicated to your own that the loyalty to lack begins to come undone, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that prospering was never betraying those you love, it was, after all, the only way of making their struggle have, at last, been worth it.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
