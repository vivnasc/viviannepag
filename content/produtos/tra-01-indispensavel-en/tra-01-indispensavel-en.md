# The woman who became indispensable

**For you, who made yourself indispensable at work to justify that you deserve your place. Because you can have worth without being needed, and your place does not have to be paid for with indispensability.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the woman who makes herself indispensable at work to justify her place, and there is a point at which that stops being dedication and leads to burnout. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion that doesn't pass, an anxiety tied to always having to be needed, or an inability to stop, this may be more than tiredness, and it deserves the care of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I made myself indispensable

Notice the place you occupy at work. You are the indispensable one. The one without whom nothing works.

You are the one who knows everything, who solves everything, who is in everything. The one everyone turns to, without whom the project stalls, the team grinds to a halt, the company feels your absence. You have become the center on which everything depends, the part without which the machine doesn't run. And there is a part of you that feels safe like this, and another that can't take it anymore.

And you can't stop. You can't delegate, because you think nobody does it the way you do. You can't be away, because everything collapses in your absence. You can't go on holiday without your phone, without answering, without being present, because your absence, you feel, would bring chaos.

You carry everything. The tasks that are yours and the ones that aren't. What falls to you and what should belong to others. You accumulate, you centralize, you hold it all, in an endless effort to be the part on which everything depends.

And notice that saying no is almost impossible for you. When they ask one more thing of you, even already drowning, you accept, because refusing seems to risk your place, seems to stop being needed, seems to give someone else the chance to be necessary in your stead. And so you accumulate without end, unable to set a limit, because every limit you set would confront you with the fear that, without doing everything, perhaps they would no longer need you. Your no stays caught in your throat, not out of kindness, but out of terror that refusing would take from you the place that only your limitless availability seems to guarantee.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only dedication, even though it looks like it. Everyone praises you, they say you are tireless, that they don't know what they would do without you, that you are essential. And there is real dedication in you, yes. But underneath there is something else, deeper, that needs to be seen.

There is a need to make yourself indispensable. And that need is not about the work. It's about you. About your right to occupy that place. You made yourself indispensable, without knowing it, to justify that you deserve to be there, so that nobody can question your place, because without you everything falls.

This book is not about career. I haven't come to give you tips on management, productivity, leadership. I've come to look at something else, something deeper. At the reason you need to make yourself indispensable. At the wound, existential and not professional, that makes you believe your place has to be justified, paid for, guaranteed, with your indispensability.

And I'll tell you up front where we are going, so you can read without dread. You can have worth without being needed. Your place at work, and in the world, does not have to be bought at the weight of indispensability. And that hunger to be essential, which leaves you breathless, comes from a wound we are going to look at calmly, and which is not healed by piling up even more tasks.

Before we go on, separate with me what shows on the outside from what is really happening. What shows, from the outside, is a dedicated, devoted professional who loves what she does and so bends over backward. What it is, on the inside, very often, is a woman who doesn't feel secure of her place, and who bends over backward not out of love for the work, but out of fear of not deserving to be there. The difference can't be seen from the outside, but it's felt in everything: the dedication that comes from enjoyment has limits, it rests, it brings joy; the one that comes from fear has no end, it doesn't rest, it exhausts. And it's likely that yours, however real the enjoyment, is mixed with a good dose of fear, and it is from that fear, and not from the enjoyment, that this book wants to free you.

This book hasn't come to tell you to work less, or to stop caring, or that your devotion is worth nothing. Your competence is real, your worth is real, and the enjoyment you take in what you do is a good thing. It has come to tell you that you don't have to pay for your place with your exhaustion, that you deserve to be there without having to become the part on which everything depends, and that there is a way to work well, and even better, without consuming yourself proving, without end, that you are needed.

Maybe you recognize yourself in small things.

In not being able to delegate, even drowning in work, because you think only you do it well, and because letting go seems to risk your place.

In feeling a strange satisfaction when something fails in your absence, because it confirms that you are needed.

In not being able to switch off, to rest, to be away, because your absence seems to risk your place.

In measuring your worth, at work, by the number of things that depend on you, as if you were worth exactly as much as you are needed.

They are all the same thing. Indispensability as a way to justify your place. The exhaustion of someone who has to always be needed in order to feel entitled to be there.

I want to show you how this shows up day to day, in things you perhaps already take for professionalism. You receive a request you could pass to someone else, and instead of passing it on, you keep it, because this way you are the one who solves it, you are the one they depend on. You see a colleague doing something differently from you, and you correct it, you redo it, you step in, because you have to be at the center of everything. You go on holiday, and you bring the computer, you answer messages on the beach, you call to ask how things are going, unable to trust that they carry on without you, or perhaps afraid to discover that they do.

And there is a detail that reveals everything: what you feel when something goes wrong in your absence. Instead of the dismay that would be natural, a secret satisfaction rises in you, an almost guilty relief, because the failure confirms what you need to believe: that without you it doesn't work, that you are needed, that your place is guaranteed. And when, on the contrary, everything goes well without you, instead of feeling reassured, you feel strangely uneasy, as if your dispensability were a threat. Notice this, because it is revealing: you would rather things go worse with you absent than better without you, and that is not dedication, it is the wound speaking.

And there is something the workplace doesn't see, beneath your devotion. It sees a tireless, dedicated, trustworthy professional, who is loyal to the cause, who is always there, and it praises you, and it leans on you, and it settles into letting you bear everything. It doesn't see the exhaustion underneath, the anxiety of someone who can never stop, the doubt that forces you to prove, without end, that you deserve to be there. It sees the dedication, not the fear that feeds it. And you, who receive the praise for your devotion, become even more bound to it, because now there are also others counting on you never to fail, never to stop, never to cease being the central part.

## 2. Justifying that I deserve the place

Go back. Beneath the professional surface, there is an existential question. Do I deserve to be here?

Because your need to make yourself indispensable does not arise from the work. It arises from an older doubt about your right to occupy a place. And indispensability is your answer to that doubt: if everything depends on me, nobody can question that I deserve to be here.

Notice the mechanism. Someone who feels, without doubt, entitled to her place doesn't need to justify it. She occupies it, simply, does her work, and goes home, without feeling she has to prove, at every instant, that she deserves to be there. You don't. You feel that your place is fragile, that it has to be earned, justified, guaranteed, and the way you found to guarantee it was to make yourself indispensable.

Where does this doubt about your right to be there come from? Almost always, from far away. From a time when your place, at home, in life, was not guaranteed by your being who you were, but had to be earned, conquered, justified. Perhaps you learned, early on, that you only had worth when you were useful, when you produced, when you were needed. And you carried that lesson into work, where you continue to justify your place by making yourself necessary.

Maybe you are a woman in an environment where you had to prove twice as much to get half the recognition. Where your place was always questioned, and where you grew used to justifying it with an effort, a devotion, an indispensability that others didn't need to demonstrate. And that need to prove became a way of existing, at work and beyond it.

Notice what you learned. That your place is not given, it is earned. That to have the right to be there you have to be needed. That without your indispensability, no one guarantees you stay. And that belief carved itself deep, and it governs you, and it makes you accumulate, centralize, make yourself indispensable, in an endless effort to justify a place that you feel that, otherwise, you wouldn't have.

I want to add a root you may recognize. In many homes, love and attention came through usefulness. You were seen, praised, valued, when you helped, when you delivered, when you were needed, and you went unnoticed when you simply were. A child who learns that she is loved for what she does, and not for who she is, grows up turning herself into a machine for being useful, because she learned that this is how, and only how, she deserves a place in others' affection. And she carries that equation into work, where she makes herself indispensable, not out of ambition, but because, deep down, she keeps trying to earn, through usefulness, the place she never felt was given to her freely.

And there is, sometimes, a root even deeper: that of having felt, early on, that your place in the world was conditional. That you had to justify your existence, to make up for the inconvenience of your being there, to pay, somehow, for the space you occupied. Perhaps because you were a child who felt she was a burden, who weighed on others, who had to make herself useful to be tolerated. And that sense, that existing has to be earned, is the soil from which your adult indispensability springs: you keep paying, with endless work, for a place that you learned was never, simply, yours.

I want you to see this without guilt. Your need to make yourself indispensable is not vanity, nor control, nor an inability to trust others. It is a way of guaranteeing a place that, somewhere, you learned had to be paid for. You are not controlling by nature. You are someone who doubts her right to be there, and who pays for that place with the only currency she knows: that of making herself necessary.

## 3. Usefulness is not worth

There is a confusion at the center of everything, and I want to undo it. You confused being useful with having worth.

Notice the difference. Being useful is being needed, being necessary, solving, producing. Having worth is something else: it is being worth something for who you are, for your competence, for your presence, regardless of how much others depend on you. Usefulness is circumstantial, it changes with the context, it can end tomorrow. Worth is yours, it goes with you, it doesn't depend on who needs you today.

And you built your whole worth upon usefulness. Upon being needed. Upon being necessary. To the point that, if you are not needed, if you are not necessary, you feel you are worth nothing, that you have no place, that there is no reason to stay.

There is a small test that gives this confusion away. Notice what you answer, on the inside, when someone asks how you are. You almost never say how you are. You say what you've been doing, how many projects you have on your plate, how full everything is. As if your state of mind were a to-do list, and your existence were measured by the volume of what you carry. Whoever confuses being worth with being needed loses even the language to say she exists without producing, and answers a question about being with an inventory of doing.

Notice what this confusion does to you, day to day. You can't simply be, without producing. Rest gives you guilt, because resting is not being useful. Holidays unsettle you, because far from being needed you feel you are losing worth. Even at a dinner with friends, or in a moment of leisure, there is a part of you that needs to be useful, to help, to solve, because you don't know how to be worth something without doing. You confused what you are worth with what you produce so completely that you lost the ability to exist at rest, and that is, perhaps, the deepest loss: that of being able, simply, to be, without having to justify your presence with usefulness.

And I want you to see that this confusion is, deep down, a sadness, not a flaw. Because it reveals that, somewhere, you were not allowed to feel that you were worth something just for being. That you had to earn your place, instead of receiving it. A person who knows, deep down, that she is worth something for existing doesn't need to make herself indispensable, she rests in her worth. You don't rest because they never let you rest in it, and so you chase it without end, doing, proving, being necessary, in the hope that one day the sense of having a right to be there will finally arrive. That sense doesn't come from doing more. It comes from healing the wound that made you believe you had to earn it.

And there is an enormous cost in this. Because you live making yourself ever more necessary, accumulating, centralizing, not delegating, and that exhausts you. You carry the work of several people, you refuse help, you let go of nothing, because letting go is risking ceasing to be needed, and ceasing to be needed is, in your world, ceasing to have worth.

And there is a cost to your work, too. Because a person who centralizes everything, who doesn't delegate, who becomes the single point on which everything depends, is, paradoxically, a problem for the organization. She creates dependency, bottlenecks, fragility. A healthy team doesn't depend on a single person. And your indispensability, which you judge to be your worth, is often an obstacle to everyone's growth, including your own, because bound to being necessary, you don't grow beyond it.

And there is yet another cost around you, in the people who could grow and don't. Every task you centralize is a task someone doesn't learn to do. Every decision you withhold is a decision another doesn't exercise. By making yourself the center of everything, you keep them, without meaning to, small, dependent, incapable, not because they are, but because you never gave them the room to stop being so. And then you complain that nobody does it like you, that you have to be in everything, without seeing that it was you who, by not letting go, prevented them from learning. Indispensability feeds on itself: the more you centralize, the more incapable others become, and the more necessary you seem, in a cycle that only you can break, by letting go.

Notice this trap. The more indispensable you make yourself, the more trapped you become. Because you can't stop, be away, rest, change, grow, without risking the place that indispensability guarantees you. You have become a hostage to your own need to be needed. And what looked like power, being the central part, is in truth a prison, that of someone who can never leave her post, ever, on pain of discovering that her place was not, after all, guaranteed.

And there is a cruel irony in this trap. Indispensability promises you security, but gives you the opposite. Because the more everything depends on you, the more anxiously you live, the more afraid you are of failing, of falling ill, of not coping, because your failure is no longer only yours, it drags everything down with it. What ought to reassure you, being needed, is precisely what takes away your peace, because it places on your shoulders a weight that can't be set down. True security doesn't come from everything depending on you. It comes from knowing that your worth doesn't depend on that, and that you can fail, stop, be away, and still be worth something.

I also want you to see the difference between being the central part and being a good professional. The central part is irreplaceable because she created dependency, because she centralized, because she didn't let anyone learn. The good professional is valuable because she is competent, because she solves problems, because she raises up those around her. The first seems more powerful, but she is more fragile, because her worth is in withholding, not in creating. The second seems less central, but she is more solid, because her worth is in her, and goes with her wherever she goes. You can choose which you want to be, and the one that gives you peace, and a future, is not the first.

And there is a cost that extends beyond work, to your whole life. Because someone who makes herself indispensable at her job is rarely so only there. The same need to be needed, to be necessary, to be unable to stop, contaminates everything: the home, the family, the friendships. You become the indispensable one everywhere, the one who holds everything together, the one nobody can do without, and you never rest anywhere, because nowhere do you allow yourself simply to be, without having to do, to serve, to prove. And your whole life turns into a permanent workplace, with no schedule, no day off, where you can never, finally, set things down.

And it costs you the possibility of being loved for who you are, and not for what you do. Because, by defining yourself through usefulness, you never come to know whether you would be wanted without it. You make yourself so necessary that people bond with you for what you solve, for what you give, for what you hold together, and you never discover whether they would stay if you stopped doing it. And there is a deep loneliness in this: that of someone who is very necessary and little known, very useful and rarely seen, surrounded by people who depend on her and uncertain whether anyone wants her beyond what she does.

I want you to feel the weight of this. The weight of always having to be necessary. Of never being able to stop. Of carrying everything, alone, to justify a place that feels fragile. It is a deep exhaustion, and perhaps you already take it for dedication, when it is, deep down, the endless effort of someone who doubts her right to be there, and who pays for it by making herself indispensable.

## 4. Who am I if I am not needed

I'll leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who are you, at work, if you are not needed? If things work without you? If you are not the part on which everything depends?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness, or the fear, that it opens up. Maybe you feel that, without indispensability, you wouldn't know who you are, at work, nor what place you would occupy. That emptiness says a lot: it says you built your entire professional identity upon being needed, to the point of no longer knowing who you would be if you weren't. And it is precisely that emptiness this crossing invites you to inhabit, without filling it in a rush with more work, so that you can discover, slowly, that beneath the indispensable one there is a professional of worth who exists even when she is not needed.

In you, this question touches a very deep wound. Because your worth, at work, has stuck to your indispensability. You are the needed one, the essential one, the central part. Take that away from you, imagine that things work without you, and a terror appears. So what worth do I have? What place do I occupy, if not that of the indispensable one? If I am not needed, is there a reason to stay?

And the shadow that hides here is the doubt about your right to be there. The fear that, without indispensability, your place would reveal itself as fragile, questionable, undeserved. That you only have the right to be there as long as you are needed, and that without it you would be dispensable, replaceable, set aside.

Notice how this imprisons you. You can't delegate, because to delegate is to make yourself less necessary. You can't rest, because your absence is risking your place. You can't grow beyond your post, because your post is what guarantees your worth. You live bound to an indispensability that exhausts you, because letting go of it confronts you with the doubt it covers: that perhaps you don't, in fact, have a right to your place.

And there is an enormous fragility in this. Because a worth that depends on being needed is a worth forever at risk. All it takes is for another capable person to appear, for the organization to change, for you to stop being the only one who knows, and your worth, resting on indispensability, threatens to collapse. You live, therefore, in permanent tension, that of someone who has to keep being needed in order to keep being worth something, and who can never rest in that worth, because it depends on a usefulness she has to renew without end.

Look closely at the terror this question raises, because looking at it already weakens it. What terrifies you is not, truly, ceasing to be needed. It is the doubt that this awakens: that perhaps you don't have a right to your place. But notice, those two things are not the same. You can stop being the part on which everything depends and still have every right to be there, by your competence, by your story, by who you are. Your right to your place never depended, truly, on your indispensability. It was the wound that glued them together, and separating them is the beginning of your liberation: discovering that you deserve to be there, even when you are not needed.

I also want you to think about whom you admire, in your field. The people whose presence conveys to you authority, calm, worth. Notice: they are almost never the ones running from one side to the other putting out fires, the ones who do everything, the ones nobody can do without because they centralized everything. They are, very often, the ones who occupy their place with serenity, who delegate, who trust, who don't need to prove every minute that they are needed. True authority doesn't come from anxious indispensability. It comes from the quiet security of someone who knows her worth. And it is that serenity, and not your scrambling, that would give you, after all, the recognition you seek by exhausting yourself.

There is a sentence that, if you let it in, dismantles this fear from the inside. Your worth doesn't depend on being needed. This is the truth your wound hides. You have worth, as a professional and as a person, by your competence, by your experience, by who you are, and that worth doesn't disappear if you delegate, if you rest, if things work without you. On the contrary. A professional who knows how to delegate, who builds capable teams, who doesn't create dependency, is more valuable, not less. Your real worth is greater than your indispensability, and discovering it is freeing yourself from it.

And I want you to notice something, about what this indispensability prevents you from being. There is, in you, probably, a greater professional than the one who fits into the role of central part. Someone who could lead, create, think big, train others, leave a mark that went beyond solving the day to day. But that person doesn't fit while you are bound to being necessary in everything, busy putting out fires, centralizing tasks, letting go of nothing. Indispensability not only exhausts you, it shrinks you: it binds you to the operational, to the urgent, to what only you do, and it robs you of the room to become what you would be if you didn't have to prove, every single day, that you are needed.

Don't answer the question of who you are if you are not needed just yet. But leave it open. Because it is there that the door lies. On the day you discover that you have worth even without being indispensable, you stop having to exhaust yourself making yourself necessary. And then, only then, can you begin to delegate, to rest, to occupy your place without having to pay for it with your exhaustion.

## 5. When indispensability leads to burnout

I'm going to set the book down for a moment, because what comes now asks for another gravity. There is a point at which the need to be indispensable stops being a pattern and becomes an exhaustion that calls for care.

If you carry everything, alone, without delegating, without stopping, for years, there is a cost that the body and the mind exact. And I want to name the signs, plainly, because knowing them matters.

If the tiredness you carry no longer yields to a night's sleep or a weekend. If Monday arrives before you've recovered from Friday, if you come back from holiday as though you'd never left. If there is a background anxiety, clinging to work, that won't let you go, that tightens your chest, that won't let you switch off. If you have lost the pleasure in what you do, and everything has become obligation, weight, survival. If your body is beginning to give signs, to fall ill, to fail, under the weight of a pace it can no longer bear.

If you have become irritable, or distant, or numbed, with no room for your loved ones, because work consumes everything you have. If your head never switches off, if you take the work to bed, to the shower, to the moments that should be yours. If you feel, at times, a desire to disappear, for everything to stop, a tiredness that is no longer only of the body, it is of the soul. These are serious signs, and they deserve to be taken seriously, not pushed forward with one more effort.

These may be signs of exhaustion, of burnout, and that is not solved by doing more, by enduring more, by being even more indispensable. It is solved by stopping, and often with support, from a doctor, from a psychologist, from someone who helps you climb out of the pit.

And I want to name the specific trap of someone who has made herself indispensable. It is that you, precisely because you are the part on which everything depends, are the one who has the hardest time stopping, being away, taking care of herself. Because stopping seems to risk everything, to let everything fall, to fail everyone. And so you endure beyond your limit, you exhaust yourself sustaining what only you sustain, until the body, or the mind, gives way, without warning.

I want to tell you, too, a hard truth about the place you hold so tightly. However indispensable you feel, the organization would survive your absence. I don't say this to hurt you, I say it to free you. Companies, projects, teams, reorganize, they find ways to carry on, because they were made for that. The only person who doesn't survive the pace you keep is you. And there is a profound injustice in spending your health, your life, your years, sustaining something that, on the day you fall, will carry on without you, perhaps with a word of pity and a replacement on the way. That place does not deserve your exhaustion. You deserve to be cared for before your body forces you to stop.

Don't wait for that point. If these signs of burnout are yours, let this be the chapter that stays with you. Stop. Seek support. Your indispensability is not worth more than your health, than your life. And seeking help is not failing anyone. It is the most lucid thing you can do, for yourself, and even for the work you hold together so tightly, because an exhausted person sustains nothing, and taking care of yourself is the only way you can, one day, continue.

## 6. To have worth without being needed

If you confused usefulness with worth, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can have worth without being needed.

Read this slowly, because it contradicts your deepest belief. For you, worth comes from indispensability. Without being needed, you are worth nothing. And I propose that you separate the two things. Your worth, as a professional and as a person, is not in how much others depend on you. It is in your competence, in your experience, in what you know, in who you are. And that worth remains, intact, even if you delegate, even if you rest, even if things work without you.

Let me put it another way. Imagine two equally competent professionals. One centralizes everything, doesn't delegate, makes herself the part on which everything depends, and exhausts herself. The other builds a capable team, delegates, and things work even in her absence. Which of the two is more valuable? The second, by far. Because her worth is not in creating dependency, it is in creating capability. Indispensability is not the peak of professional worth. It is, very often, its opposite.

And I want you to think about what happens, over time, to each one. The first gets stuck: she can't grow, because she can't let go of what she centralized; she can't be promoted, because nobody can replace her in what she does; she falls ill, sooner or later, under the weight of it all. The second rises: because she left things running, she is free to take on the next challenge; because she trained people, she is seen as someone who makes others grow; because she didn't exhaust herself, she lasts. Indispensability, which seems to protect you, is in the end what binds you to the same place, exhausted, with no future. And the ability to make yourself dispensable in what you have mastered is, paradoxically, what opens the doors for you to more.

I want you to feel the weight of this trade you make without realizing it. You trade your growth for the security of being needed. You trade your rest for the certainty of being necessary. You trade your life out there for the position of central part. And none of those trades, when you see them from the outside, are worth it. Because what you gain, indispensability, is fragile and exhausting; and what you lose, growth, rest, life, is precious and doesn't come back. It is worth stopping and asking: what am I sacrificing to keep being necessary, and is it really worth it?

And there is an enormous liberation in this. When your worth stops depending on being needed, you can, at last, delegate. You can build, around you, capable people, instead of keeping them dependent on you. You can rest, be away, go on holiday, without the terror of everything collapsing. You can grow beyond your post, because you no longer need to cling to it to have worth. Your life, and your work, breathe.

I want to show you, further, that indispensability deceives even in terms of professional worth. The people who rise most, who grow most, who leave the deepest mark, are rarely the ones who do everything alone. They are the ones who know how to surround themselves, to train, to trust, to multiply themselves in others. Whoever centralizes everything stays stuck doing, and whoever delegates frees herself to think, to create, to lead. By clinging to being the only one who knows, you are not protecting your worth, you are limiting it, keeping it small, occupied in the operational, with no room to grow. The true mark of a great professional is not that nothing works without her. It is that so much works, and so well, thanks to what she built, that it can work even in her absence.

And I want to undo a fear of yours: that of, if things work without you, ceasing to be needed, and being set aside. It is the opposite. Whoever builds capable teams, whoever solves problems in a way that they don't come back, whoever leaves things running, becomes more valuable, not less, because she demonstrates a rare ability: that of creating value that lasts beyond her own presence. Nobody does without someone who makes the organization better. What gets done away with, over time, is whoever creates bottlenecks, whoever stalls things, whoever keeps everything dependent on herself and therefore falls ill and blocks the way. The indispensability you think protects you is, over time, more fragile than the competence that frees others.

I want to speak to you of the verb of this crossing. To delegate. To delegate is not to lose your place, nor your worth. It is to entrust to others what doesn't have to be only yours. It is to build capability around you, instead of dependency. It is to free yourself from the weight of everything, so you can occupy your real place, which is greater than that of the central part on which everything depends. It is, deep down, an act of trust: in others, who are capable; and in yourself, who has worth even when you don't do everything.

And delegating demands something that costs you: tolerating that others don't do it exactly as you do, and that it's fine. That things get done differently, perhaps less perfect to your measure, but that they get done, and that the organization doesn't depend only on you. To delegate is to let go of control, and letting go of control, for someone who uses it as the seatbelt of her place, is terribly frightening. But it is exactly in the opening of that closed fist that your freedom begins.

I want to be honest with you about one thing, so you don't fool yourself: at first, delegating will seem inefficient to you, and even more laborious. You will think it was faster to do it yourself, that you have to explain, to correct, to follow up, and that in the end you could almost have done the work in half the time. It's true, in the short term. But delegating is not about the short term. It is an investment: you spend more now to reap much later, when that person knows how to do it, and you are free of that task forever. Whoever doesn't delegate because she thinks it's faster to do it alone condemns herself to doing it alone forever. Today's haste is tomorrow's prison.

And I want to warn you of what you will feel when you delegate for real. You will feel a strange emptiness, almost a discomfort, on seeing someone else do what was yours, on having less in your hands, on not being the center of that task. That emptiness is not a sign that you lost your place. It is the space that opens when you let go of what filled you and exhausted you, and which you don't yet know how to fill. Don't rush to fill it with more work, by reflex. Leave it open, for a while, and you will discover that into that space fit rest, thought, growth, and even the life out there that indispensability had been robbing from you.

The awakening, when it comes, is a relief. It is the day you delegate something, or you are away, or you rest, and you discover that things worked without you, and that, instead of the terror you expected, you feel relief. That your worth didn't collapse because you weren't needed in that moment. That you keep being worth something, even when you're not needed. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves the belief that without your indispensability you have no place.

And I want you to understand that this relief is not only yours. When you stop centralizing everything, the people around you grow. The colleagues you kept dependent gain autonomy, confidence, competence. The team, which was fragile because everything passed through you, becomes stronger, more capable, more resilient. What you thought was generosity, doing everything for everyone, was, without your knowing, a brake on their growth. And by delegating, by trusting, by letting go, you give others what your indispensability took from them: the space to become, themselves too, capable. The best thing you can do for a team is not to be irreplaceable. It is to make it capable of doing without you.

## 7. To delegate and occupy my real place

There is a destination for this crossing. To stop clinging to indispensability and to occupy your real place.

And I want to show you the difference between the two places. The place of indispensability is fragile, exhausting, and is, deep down, a prison. You have to renew it without end, always being necessary, never being able to stop. Your real place is another. It is the place of someone who has worth for who she is and for what she knows, and who can therefore delegate, rest, grow, without risking her legitimacy.

To occupy your real place is to stop justifying your right to be there. It is to recognize that you deserve your place, not for being indispensable, but for being competent, for having earned what you have, for being who you are. It is to occupy your chair with the calm of someone who doesn't have to prove, at every instant, that she deserves to be there.

It is also learning to set limits without guilt. To say no to one more task that doesn't fall to you. To end the working day when it ends. To go on holiday without the phone. To trust that your place doesn't disappear because you set a limit. Limits, for someone who learned to guarantee her place with endless availability, seem dangerous, seem to risk everything. But it is precisely by setting them, and by seeing that your place stays there, that your body learns the new truth: that you have a right to be there even without giving yourself entirely, even without doing everything, even when you keep something for yourself.

And I want you to see the difference between occupying your place and gripping it. To grip the place is what you do now: you hold on to it tooth and nail, making yourself necessary, afraid of losing it. To occupy the place is something else, it is to inhabit it with calm, with the security of someone who knows it is hers, without having to defend it at every instant. Whoever grips lives in tension, always fearing loss. Whoever occupies lives in peace, because the place is not at risk, it is simply being lived. And there is an enormous difference between spending your life defending a place and, at last, being able to inhabit it.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you delegate, that you rest, that you stop being the central part, the old fear of losing the place, the doubt about your right to be there, the urge to make yourself necessary again so the anxiety will pass.

But it is precisely there, in that delegating endured in spite of the fear, that your liberation lies. Because each time you delegate, and your place doesn't disappear, you learn that it didn't depend on your indispensability. That you had worth beyond being needed. That you can occupy your place without paying for it with your exhaustion.

I want you to see what opens up when you occupy your real place. Rest opens up, because you no longer have to be needed at every instant. Growth opens up, because you are no longer bound to your post. A better way of working opens up, in which you build capability around you instead of dependency, and in which your worth multiplies instead of exhausting you. And above all, a peace opens up: that of someone who knows she deserves her place, and who doesn't have to justify it with her indispensability.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that tells you that without being needed you are worth nothing, is carved deep, and it will appear each time you delegate, each time you rest, each time things move along without you. It will tell you that you are losing ground, becoming dispensable, risking your place. You don't have to silence it in one blow, it's enough to stop doing what it commands. To hear it say make yourself necessary, and to answer it, slowly, I have worth even without being needed. And each time you occupy your place without paying for it with exhaustion, you weaken the old voice, and you strengthen a new one, more your own, more firm, which knows that you deserve to be there for who you are, and not for how much others depend on you.

And I want to leave you with something, about who sees you, especially if there are those who learn by looking at you, children, younger people you guide. If they see you live bound to work, unable to stop, measuring your worth by how much you are needed, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it is likely they will carry, too, the same exhaustion, the same confusion between usefulness and worth. The most freeing thing you can teach those who watch you is not the example of someone who never stops. It is that of a woman who works well and also rests, who has worth and also has limits, who knows she deserves her place without having to pay for it with her life. Sometimes a woman learns to let go, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn those she loves to the same exhaustion.

And I want you to see that occupying your real place doesn't make you less dedicated, less competent, less good at what you do. Your ability stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops coming from the fear of not deserving to be there, and comes instead from the security of someone who knows she deserves it. And a professional who works from security, and not from fear, is better, more creative, more free, than one who exhausts herself proving her place. Fear shrinks, recognized worth expands. And what you will do from knowing yourself to be valuable, without having to prove it at every instant, will be greater than everything you did from doubt.

I won't give you a method of management, because this book is not about career. But I leave you the principle. Delegate what doesn't have to be only yours. Build capability, not dependency. And discover, slowly, that your worth was not in being the part on which everything depends, but in who you are, and in what you know, and that this, unlike indispensability, nobody can take from you, and it doesn't exhaust you to maintain.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, let go of an indispensability built over an entire career. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your exhaustion with dedication, and your indispensability with your worth.

Now you know that you made yourself indispensable, and that this is not only dedication, it is a way of justifying your place, a mixture of genuine enjoyment with an old fear of not deserving to be there.

You know that beneath the professional surface there is an existential doubt about your right to be there, which comes from far away, from a time when your place had to be earned instead of given.

You know that you confused usefulness with worth, and that indispensability, far from being your worth, is often a prison, and even an obstacle to your growth and to that of others.

You know whom you fear being if you are not needed, and that underneath lies the doubt about your right to your place, a doubt that is healed by recognizing your worth, and not by doing even more.

You know that indispensability can lead to burnout, and that seeking help is not failing anyone, it is taking care of yourself before your body forces you to stop.

You know that you can have worth without being needed, and that the verb is to delegate, so you can occupy your real place, with the calm of someone who no longer has to defend it at every instant.

I want to leave you with an image.

Imagine someone who, to make sure she is not fired, deliberately, without realizing it, makes everything depend on her. Who hides information, centralizes tasks, teaches no one, so that nobody can replace her. And who thus becomes indispensable, and feels secure, because without her everything stops. But who lives bound to that post, unable to be away, unable to rest, unable to grow, hostage to the very indispensability that was meant to protect her.

The crossing is discovering that your place doesn't have to be guaranteed like that. That you have worth without having to make everything depend on you. That you can teach, delegate, build capability, and occupy your place not for being the only one who knows, but for being good at what you do, and for deserving to be there. And that, free of indispensability, you can, at last, be away, rest, grow, without the ground falling away beneath you.

Let me prepare you for what comes, so you don't turn this crossing into one more task where you have to get top marks. There will be setbacks. Days when you delegate and rest, and days when you go back to gripping everything, to centralizing, to letting go of nothing, in a panic of ceasing to be needed. And your old voice will tell you that without that you are worth nothing, that you have to be necessary again. Don't believe it the first time. Setbacks are part of it. Nobody lets go all at once of a way of being that sustained an entire career. It is let go little by little, and each time you delegate and your place doesn't disappear, a new proof is left, and it is from those proofs, added together, that the confidence in your real worth, slowly, is built.

I have one last thing left, simple, before I close the door. You always had worth. The woman who made herself indispensable to deserve her place did what she learned to do, and did it with an enormous dedication, worthy of respect. But she never needed to do it to be worth something. She was already worth something, from the start, for who she is, for what she knows, for the person she is beyond everything she produces. There is nothing to correct in her devotion. There is only one thing to remind her, slowly, until she believes it: your place is yours, you earned it, and you don't have to keep paying for it to be able to occupy it in peace.

Deep down, you already know the sentence that holds everything I have written together. I can have worth without being indispensable. And you know that your place, conquered and deserved, is yours, and that you can, at last, inhabit it in peace, instead of defending it, exhausted, at every instant.

Delegating was the first step. The second, that one, doesn't fit on these pages nor is it learned by reading. It is done by letting go, slowly, of control. By building capability around you. By occupying your real place, without justifying it with your exhaustion. And there is a deeper work, with someone who walks alongside you, to undo the doubt about your right to be there, because that doubt comes from far away, and rarely undoes itself alone.

If from all of this you keep a single thought, keep this one. Your worth is not what you produce. It is who you are. The most valuable people you know are not worth something for the number of things that depend on them, they are worth something for the person they are, for the competence they have, for the presence they bring. And so are you. You have a right to a place at your work, and in the world, not for making yourself necessary, but simply for being someone of worth, who doesn't need to prove it, without end, exhausting herself. Learning to rest in that worth, instead of chasing it, is the true crossing, and you deserve to make it.

And before you set this book down, I leave you a single request. I know that, for you, it is one of the hardest.

This week, delegate one thing you would normally do yourself. Entrust it to someone, and don't redo it, don't control it, don't take it back. Let it be done without you. And observe two things. What happens, which almost certainly is not the disaster you fear. And what you feel, the fear of losing the place, the doubt about your worth, the urge to make yourself necessary again. Stay with that, without taking back control. Because it is in that delegating, endured in spite of the fear, that you begin to discover that your worth didn't depend on being indispensable, and that your place, after all, was always yours.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are being lazy, neglectful, risking everything you built, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you are learning to work in a healthier, freer, and more valuable way, and that resting, delegating, setting limits, doesn't take away your worth, it gives you back the life that indispensability had been charging you.

Because you have worth, and always had it, far beyond everything you do.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
