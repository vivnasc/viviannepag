# The woman who carries the company

**For you, whose identity has fused with what you create, to the point of not knowing where you end and your project begins. Because you can stay whole without holding everything.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the woman who fused with what she created, to the point of being unable to let go, and there is a point at which that stops being dedication and leads to exhaustion. If you recognize in yourself an inability to disconnect, an anxiety tied to your project that doesn't pass, or the sense that without it you are nothing, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I carry everything on my shoulders

Notice the relationship you have with what you created. The company, the project, the business, the work. It isn't only your work. It is you. You fused with it, to the point of no longer knowing where you end and your creation begins.

You carry it all on your shoulders. Every decision, every problem, every detail passes through you, depends on you, weighs on you. You can't let go of anything, delegate anything, let anything run without your hand. Because that thing is yours, it is part of you, and to let go of a part seems to risk your own integrity.

And notice that this weight has no schedule and no end. It is not like the work of someone who closes the door and goes home, leaving what remains for the next day. Your work has no door to close, because it goes with you, it is part of you. You wake up with it, you fall asleep with it, and in the hours when you should be living something else, it is there, underneath, pulling your attention, robbing you of the present. You carry the work not like someone carrying a bag, which can be set down, but like someone carrying her own name, which can't be let go. And that permanent weight, which you never set down, is one of the clearest marks of the fusion.

And you don't switch off. Ever. Even at home, even on holiday, even asleep, the project is with you, in your head, in your chest. You carry the work everywhere, because it isn't work, it is you, and you can't separate yourself from yourself.

And maybe you don't even remember it being different. Remember a time when you ended the day and it was over, when there were parts of your life that had nothing to do with what you produced. Everything gradually became one, slowly, without your noticing, until the work occupied all the space, and you took that for normal, for dedication, for being who you are. But it is not normal to live with no border between work and life, it is not healthy to exist only inside one thing, however much you love it. It is only what became so habitual that you no longer see it as a problem, but as your natural way of being.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only ambition, nor love for what you do, even though it looks like it. People admire your dedication, they say you live for your project, that it wouldn't exist without you. And there is real love in what you create, yes. But underneath there is something else, that needs to be seen.

There is a fusion. Your identity has fused with your creation. You don't have a company, you are the company. You didn't make a project, you are the project. And that fusion, which looks like total dedication, is, deep down, the loss of the border between you and what you create, to the point of no longer knowing how to exist separate from it.

This book is not about management, nor about entrepreneurship, nor about how to lead better. I've come to open the door behind the office, the one nobody audits. At the fusion between you and what you create. At the fact that you placed your whole identity in what you do, to the point of not knowing who you are without it. And at what that costs you, and even costs the very creation you carry so much and which, without your knowing, you bind by not letting it grow without you.

And I'll tell you the direction of this book up front, so you can read it without your foot on the brake. You can stay a whole person even without your hand on every decision of the company. You can love what you create, dedicate yourself to it, without fusing with it, without losing the border between you and your work. And that ability to let go, to separate who you are from what you create, doesn't take the project from you. It gives you back yourself.

I want you to understand, from the start, the difference between loving what you create and fusing with it, because they are different things, and this book only wants to take the second from you. To love the work is to dedicate yourself to it, to care, to give it your best, and at the same time to remain a whole person, with a life of your own, who exists when she steps away from it. To fuse with the work is to stop existing outside of it, it is for the work to become your skin, your worth, your whole identity. Love sustains, fusion consumes. And it is possible, and desirable, to deeply love what you create without losing yourself in it, and that is what these pages have come to give back to you.

This book hasn't come to ask you to love your work less, nor to dedicate yourself less, nor for it to count less for you. Your creation can keep being one of the most important things in your life. It asks you only that it not be the only one, that it not be you, that it not carry the impossible weight of being your whole identity. Because a work loved by a whole person flourishes more than a work carried by a person who lost herself in it.

Maybe you see your own face in the smallest details of the day.

In not being able to delegate anything, because everything is yours, everything is personal, everything passes through you, as if each detail were a part of your own body.

In feeling every problem of the project as a problem of yours, personal, existential, that takes away your sleep and tightens your chest as if it were your life at risk.

In not being able to disconnect, to rest, to have a life outside of work, because work is all you have, it is who you are, and the silence outside of it gives you an emptiness that frightens you.

In being afraid, deep down, that without the project you would be no one, you would have no identity, you wouldn't know who you are.

In feeling that speaking ill of the work, or hearing a criticism of it, is speaking ill of you, as if there were no difference between judging what you do and judging who you are.

They are all the same root. The identity that got stuck to what it produces, to the point of not distinguishing what happened to the company from what happened to the one who founded it. The carrying everything on the shoulders of someone who doesn't know how to separate herself from her own work.

I want to show you how this shows up day to day, in things you perhaps already take for devotion. You receive a criticism of your project, and you don't feel it as a criticism of the work, you feel it as an attack on you, it hurts you as if they had said you are no good. Something goes wrong in the business, and instead of seeing it as a problem to solve, you sink, as if the problem were a failure of yours, personal, existential. Someone suggests a different way of doing it, and it costs you to listen, because to touch the work is to touch you.

And there is a detail that reveals everything: you can't speak of the project without saying I. It isn't the company that grew, it was me. It wasn't the product that failed, it was me who failed. It isn't the business that is at risk, it is me who is at risk. The language betrays the fusion: there is no longer, in your head, a border between what happened to the work and what happened to you. And that is why each high and each low of the project is lived as a high or a low of your own person, without distance, without respite, without the relief of knowing that you are one thing and it is another.

And there is something nobody sees, beneath your total dedication. People admire you, they say the project is your face, that it wouldn't exist without you, and there is pride in that, but there is also a prison. They don't see that you no longer have a life outside of it, that you don't know who you are when you are not working, that rest gives you guilt and silence gives you emptiness. They see the passion, not the fusion that consumes you. And you, praised for living for the work, become even more bound to it, because now to let go of it, or even to step away, seems to betray who everyone expects you to be.

## 2. I am what I create

Go back. Beneath the professional surface, there is a fusion of identity. I am what I create.

Because your relationship with your project is not only one of dedication. It is one of identity. You placed, in what you create, your whole sense of who you are. Your worth, your existence, your place in the world, all of that got stuck to your creation. And that is why you can't let go of it, because to let go of it is to let go of a part of you.

Where does this fusion come from? Almost always, from a need to exist through what you do. From a time when you learned that your worth was in what you produced, in what you achieved, in what you created, and not in who you were. And so you placed, in your work, your own existence, because it was there, in what you did, that you felt you had worth, had a place, had a reason to be.

Maybe what you created is, for you, the proof of your worth. The demonstration that you are capable, that you are worth something, that you deserve to exist. And that is why you can't let go of it, because it is not only a project, it is the evidence of your reason to be, and to let go of it is to risk losing the proof that you are worth something.

Maybe what you created is the place where you finally felt you were someone. Where you had worth, recognition, identity. And you cling to it with all your strength, because outside of it you don't know who you are, you have nowhere to feel you are someone, you have no identity of your own that doesn't depend on the work.

Or maybe the work was your way of fleeing. From an emptiness, from a pain, from a life that in other areas was not going well, from questions about yourself that you didn't want to face. To plunge into work is, for many people, a socially applauded way of not being with oneself, of filling every hour so that there is no room for what hurts. And if it was also like that for you, then letting go of the work frightens you twice over: because it confronts you not only with the loss of identity, but with everything the work was letting you flee from. And that, though it hurts to look at, is precisely what deserves, at last, to be cared for, instead of covered over with more work.

Notice the figure this leaves on your own books. You confuse yourself with what you create. The success of the work is your worth, the failure of the work is your personal failure. Each problem of the project is a problem of yours, existential, and not only professional. You live the work as if it were your own skin, and so it weighs on you with a weight that no project, by itself, should have.

Maybe what you created was born, too, from a need to have control over something, in a world where, in other areas, you felt powerless. The work is your territory, the place where you are in charge, where you decide, where things depend on you. And there is a security in that, that of someone who, having felt without control in other areas of life, found in creation a space entirely her own. But that same security becomes a prison, because letting go of control of the work reactivates the old powerlessness, and that is why you cling to every detail, not only out of love, but out of fear of feeling again that nothing is in your hands.

I want you to read this entry without writing it down as a debt against yourself. Your fusion with what you create is not a flaw, nor excess of ego. It is a way of existing that you learned, in a time when your worth seemed to be in what you did, and not in who you were. You are not obsessive by nature. You are someone who placed her existence in her work, because it was there that she learned to find worth, and who therefore doesn't know how to separate herself from it.

And I want to show you in what layer of yourself this got inscribed, because that is where the difficulty lies. The fusion is not only an idea, a conscious choice. It is a way of feeling yourself that carved itself deep. That is why it isn't enough to understand, rationally, that you are not the company, to stop feeling it. The fusion lives below reason, in a layer where your worth and your work are so intertwined that your reason can't separate them alone. That is why even knowing that you should rest, let go, delegate, you can't: because it isn't a matter of knowing, it is a matter of an identity that got confused, and that disentangles itself slowly, with time, experience, and, very often, help.

## 3. The border that was lost

There is a line on the floor you've walked over so many times you no longer see it. Between you and what you create, the border was lost. And without a border, there is no health, neither for you nor for the work.

Notice what a healthy border would be. You, on one side, with your identity, your worth, your life. The work, on the other, as something you create, love, dedicate yourself to, but which is not you. Connected, yes, but distinct. You would exist even if the work failed. The work would have a life of its own, capable of existing, in part, without you.

But in you there is no such border. You and the work are the same thing. And without a border, everything gets confused. The problem of the work is your existential problem. The risk of the work is the risk of your identity. You can't separate your peace from the health of the project, because there is no separation between you and it.

And this carries a bill no balance sheet records. It costs you your peace, because you never rest, since the work never rests, and you are the work. It costs you your health, because you carry, in your body, the weight of everything that concerns the project. It costs you your life, because you have no life outside of it, you have no identity of your own, you have no you that doesn't depend on what you create.

And it costs, paradoxically, the work itself. Because a creation fused with its creator, that depends on her for everything, that can't grow without her, is a fragile, limited, trapped creation. You can't delegate, build a team, let the work gain a life of its own, because that would be separating it from you, and you don't know how to separate yourself. And so the work stays hostage to your fusion, unable to grow beyond you, just as you stay hostage to it.

Notice this trap. The more you fuse with the work, the more it depends on you, and the more you depend on it. It is a fusion that imprisons them both. You can't let go, because to let go is to lose yourself. The work can't grow, because growing would demand autonomy in relation to you. And they stay, both, bound to each other, in an embrace that suffocates as much as it unites.

And there is a cost, still, that is paid in health, and that very often is only seen late. The body of someone who fuses with the work lives in permanent tension, because there is no respite, there is no end of the workday, there is no separation between work and the rest. And the body exacts that bill: in insomnia, in anxiety, in illnesses, in ever louder signs that it was not made to live like this, without setting down. Many dedicated creators fall ill, not out of weakness, but for having lived years without the border that protects, fused with something that never gave them rest. And when the body, at last, forces them to stop, they discover, too late, that the work they wouldn't let go of carries on without them, and that the only irreplaceable thing, after all, was the body they sacrificed to hold it.

I want you to see the difference between healthy dedication and fusion. Healthy dedication gives a lot to the work, and at the same time keeps something for itself, has limits, rests, lives. Fusion gives everything, keeps nothing, has no limits, doesn't rest, doesn't live outside of it. The first sustains itself over time, because it renews itself; the second exhausts itself, because it consumes itself. And what distinguishes one from the other is not love for the work, which can be equal in both, it is the border: healthy dedication keeps it, fusion lost it. Recovering it won't make you love less. It will only make your love for the work stop destroying you.

And it costs your relationships, too. Because someone fused with a work is rarely present for anything else. The people who love you compete, without knowing it, with the project, and they always lose, because the project is everything, it is everywhere, it has no schedule. You are with them but your head is on the work. You hear but you don't listen, because a part of you is always solving, planning, worrying. And those who love you feel that, feel that they come in second place, always, behind something they can never rival, and they pull away, or resign themselves, or are hurt in silence.

It costs you, too, parts of you that went unlived. Because when your whole identity is in a work, everything else atrophies: the interests you had, the friendships you cultivated, the dreams that didn't fit in the project, the parts of you that have nothing to do with what you produce. You gradually shrank until you fit entirely into the work, and the rest of you, what existed beyond it, went unlived, forgotten, waiting. There is a mourning in this, that of a wider life you traded for a whole life inside one single thing, however bigger and more beautiful that thing may be.

And there is a cost that almost nobody speaks of, hidden beneath the numbers that look good. A company fused with the one who founded it has a facade value and a real fragility, because everything that makes it run lives in one single head, in yours. The decisions that are written down nowhere, the contacts only you have, the way of solving things that no one beside you learned, because you never gave them room to learn. If someone looked seriously at what you built, they would see a work that doesn't survive a week of your absence, and that, which you live as proof of how necessary you are, is, in truth, the greatest weakness of everything you raised. A creation that only exists while you are stuck to it is not protected by your presence. It is hostage to it.

I want you to feel the load on your shoulders, the one you've stopped naming because you've carried it so long. The weight of carrying a work as if it were your own existence. Of never being able to rest from it, because it is from yourself that you can't rest. Of not knowing who you would be without it. It is a deep exhaustion, and perhaps you already take it for dedication, when it is, deep down, the loss of yourself in what you create.

## 4. Who am I without what I create

I'll set a question on the desk between us, like a paper you can't file away. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who are you without what you create? If the project ended, if the company closed, if the work ceased to exist, who would remain?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness, or the terror, that it opens up.

For you, who put your name on the door and your blood into every year of the company, this question opens a trapdoor. Because your whole identity has fused with your creation. Take it away, imagine yourself without it, and an abyss appears. So who am I? What worth do I have, if not that of the work? What identity is left to me, if mine was all there?

Notice that the force of that abyss is the exact measure of how much you fused. For a person with a border, the question who would you be without your work has an answer, even if it cost to lose the project: I would still be me, with my life, my loved ones, my person. For you, the question opens the emptiness, because you placed everything there, and so the answer seems to be nothing. But that nothing is not true, it is only the effect of having let everything else atrophy. There is not, in fact, an emptiness beneath the work. There is a whole life to rebuild, parts of you asleep, waiting for you to return to them.

And the shadow that hides here is the emptiness of having no identity of your own, separate from what you create. The sense that, without the work, there is no person there, only an emptiness. That you, without the project, don't exist, because you placed your whole existence in it.

See how this locks you in, like a founder who can't hand over a single key. You can't let go of the work, even if it exhausts you, because to let go of it is to confront the emptiness of who you are without it. You cling to it with all your strength, not only out of love for what you create, but out of terror of what would be left of you if you let it go. The fusion is not only dedication. It is also a flight from the question of who you would be, without the work defining you.

And what looks from outside like a thriving company hides a crack down its foundation. Because an identity fused with a work is an identity forever at risk. All it takes is for the work to fail, change, end, and your identity threatens to collapse with it. You live, therefore, with a background fear, that of losing the work, because to lose the work is to lose yourself. And that fear makes you unable to take risks, to change, to let the work evolve, because any threat to it is a threat to your own existence.

I want to tell you something about the terror this question raises, because it begins to disarm it. What terrifies you is not, truly, letting go of the work. It is the emptiness you imagine finding beneath it, the sense that, without the project, there is no one there. But that emptiness is an illusion of the fusion, not a truth about you. Beneath the work there is no emptiness, there is a person, you, with a story, with tastes, with loved ones, with parts that existed before the work and that will exist after it. The fusion made you forget that person, but it didn't erase her. She is there, waiting to be found again, and finding her again is not falling into emptiness, it is returning to yourself.

Notice, moreover, that you existed before this work. There was a version of you before you created it, with dreams, interests, relationships, a life. That version was not an emptiness, it was a whole person. The work didn't create you, it was you who created it, and so you are prior to it, greater than it, capable of existing without it, as you already existed before it was. Remembering who you were before the work is one of the ways to recover the certainty that you exist beyond it.

And there is a truth about that emptiness which, the moment you see it, undoes it from within. You exist beyond what you create. This is the truth the fusion hides. You have worth, identity, existence, that don't depend on the work. You existed before it, and you would exist after it. The work is something you create, an expression of you, but it is not you. And discovering the you that exists beyond the work is not losing the work. It is recovering yourself, who had been lost in it.

And I want you to see how this fusion makes you hostage, too, of the fear of ending. Every work, every project, has a possible end: it can fail, it can change, it can reach its natural close. But if you are the work, then the end of the work is your end, and so you live with a background terror, that of it ending, which makes you unable to accept changes, to close cycles, to let die what has to die so that something else can be born. You cling to the work like someone clinging to her own life, and that rigidity, that fear of the end, harms not only your peace, but the very ability of the work to evolve.

Don't answer the question of who you are without what you create just yet. But leave it open. Because that is the door at the back of the company nobody else can open for you. On the day you discover that you exist beyond the work, that you have an identity of your own, that you are not only what you create, you stop having to fuse with it in order to exist. And then, only then, can you let go, without losing yourself.

## 5. When the fusion leads to burnout

I need to lower my voice here, and ask for your full attention. Because there is a point at which the fusion with the work stops being a pattern and becomes an exhaustion that calls for care.

If you carry everything, without letting go, without disconnecting, for years, there is a cost that the body and the mind exact. If your peace depends entirely on the work, if each problem of it takes away your sleep, if you live in a permanent anxiety tied to the project, if you can't rest, ever, because you can't separate yourself from what you create.

If the exhaustion no longer passes. If there is a background anxiety that won't let you go. If you have lost the pleasure in the very work you loved so much, and it has become only weight, obligation, survival. If your body gives signs, under the pace of someone who never stops.

If your head never switches off from the project, not in bed, not in the shower, not in the moments that should be yours. If you no longer know how to have a conversation that doesn't end in work, a weekend that isn't invaded by it, a night's sleep that it doesn't steal. If you feel, at times, that you are disappearing inside the work, that there is no longer a person there, only a function, a cog that can't stop. These are serious signs, and they deserve to be taken seriously, not pushed forward with more work.

These may be signs of exhaustion, and that is not solved by carrying more, by holding more, by fusing even more with the work. It is solved by letting go, by separating, by resting, and very often with support, from a professional who helps you recover the border between you and what you create.

And I want to name the trap of someone who fused with the work. It is that stopping, resting, letting go, seems to abandon the work, and abandoning the work seems to abandon yourself. And that is why you endure, beyond your limit, fused with the project, until the body or the mind gives way. Don't wait for the day the whole structure forces the stop. Seeking help is not admitting that you are not capable, it is taking care of the person the work most depends on, who is you, and doing it before exhaustion decides for you.

And I want to tell you a truth that costs, but that frees. If you fall, the work falls with you. It is precisely because everything is fused in you that your exhaustion is also the greatest risk to the project. You, who carry everything to protect it, are, at your limit, its greatest fragility, because a work that depends entirely on an exhausted person is one step from collapsing with her. Taking care of yourself, recovering the border, is not betraying the work. It is, paradoxically, the most responsible way to protect it, because a whole and rested creator sustains what a fused and exhausted creator lets fall.

If the company has brought you to your limit and you recognize your signs here, let these be the lines that stay with you from the whole book. Stop. Seek support. Your work is not worth more than your health, than your life. And recovering the border between you and what you create is not abandoning the work. It is the only way you can, in good health, keep creating it.

## 6. To stay whole without holding everything

If you fused with what you create, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can stay whole without holding everything.

Read this slowly. For you, letting go of the work, ceasing to hold everything, seems to undo you, to lose your integrity, to let a part of you fall. As if your wholeness depended on holding every detail, and as if letting go would break you.

And I put the opposite motion before you. Your wholeness doesn't depend on holding everything. On the contrary. While you are fused with the work, you are not whole, you are dispersed in it, lost in it, with no border of your own. To stay whole is, precisely, to recover the border, to separate yourself from what you create enough to exist on your own, and not dispersed in the project.

Let me draft the clause differently. A healthy creator is not the one who fuses with the work. She is the one who loves the work and remains distinct from it. Who dedicates herself without losing herself. Who creates without confusing herself with the creation. Who can let go, delegate, rest, because her identity is not all there, but in herself. And it is that creator, whole and separate, who can create in good health, for a long time, without exhausting herself.

And notice: the creators you most admire, the ones who last, the ones who create work after work over an entire lifetime, are not the ones who burned themselves out on a single creation. They are the ones who knew how to stay whole, who had a life beyond work, who didn't place their whole existence in a single project. The longevity of a creator depends, precisely, on not fusing, because whoever fuses exhausts herself and stops, and whoever stays distinct renews herself and continues. Letting go is not the opposite of creating a lot. It is the condition for creating over time, without one work consuming you before the next is born.

And there is an enormous liberation in this, for the work too. When you recover the border, when you stop holding everything, the work can, at last, grow. You can delegate, build a team, let it gain a life of its own, instead of keeping it dependent on you. The work that separates from the creator is a work that can grow beyond her. And by letting go, you don't lose it, you free it, and you free yourself.

Think of the works that last, that grow, that become greater than those who created them. None of them stayed stuck to the creator forever. All reached a point at which they gained a life of their own, at which other hands held them, at which they could carry on beyond a single person. That is what makes a creation truly great: not depending, forever, on the one who made it. And by keeping the work fused with you, without wanting to, you condemn it to always be small, limited to what you, alone, can hold. Letting go is not the end of the work. It is the condition for it to be able to be greater than you.

I want you to see, too, that a work fused with the creator transmits, without meaning to, that fusion to those who work in it. People feel that nothing is theirs, that everything passes through you, that there is no room for them to own anything, and so they don't give themselves, they don't create, they don't grow, they wait for your orders. By letting go, by letting them own parts of the work, you give them what your fusion took from them: the room to care as if it were theirs. And a work cared for by many who feel it is theirs is stronger than a work carried by one who feels it is all hers.

I want to name the one action this whole company rests on. To let go. To let go is not to abandon the work. It is to stop holding it with your life, to stop fusing with it, to stop carrying it as if it were your own existence. It is to love it while remaining distinct from it. It is to trust that it can exist, in part, without your hand on every detail. It is to recover the border between who you are and what you create.

And letting go demands something that costs you: tolerating that the work gets done without you, perhaps differently from your way, and that it's fine. That you delegate, and that others don't do it exactly as you do, but that they do it. That the work gains a life of its own, and that this, far from taking the work from you, makes it stronger, and you freer. Letting go of control, for someone who fused with the work, is frightening. But it is in taking your hands off the helm, and seeing that the boat doesn't capsize, that you slowly go back to being your own.

I want to be honest with you about one thing: letting go is not done all at once, nor in a total way. You won't go from carrying everything to releasing everything. Letting go is done little by little, starting with what is easiest to delegate, with what least frightens you to release, and gaining confidence each time you see that the work holds up without your hand on it. It is a muscle that is trained slowly: today you release a small task, and you see that it goes well; tomorrow you release a bigger one. Don't demand of yourself that you let go of everything at once, which would be putting the fusion at the service of undoing it. Demand of yourself only to take the first step, and then the next, trusting that each proof that the work carries on without you will make the next step less frightening.

And I want to warn you of what you will feel, on letting go. You will feel an emptiness, almost a mourning, as if you were losing something. It is natural: for years, holding everything was the way to feel present, necessary, alive in the project. Releasing leaves a space where before there was that constant effort. Don't rush to fill it by grabbing everything again. Let the emptiness be, for a while, and you will discover that into it fits what the fusion took from you: rest, life outside the work, the parts of you that had been forgotten. The emptiness of letting go is not loss. It is the space where you, who had been lost in the work, will, slowly, reappear.

The awakening, when it comes, is a deep relief. It is the day you let go of something, or you delegate, or you rest, when the work carries on without you, and you discover that you didn't come undone, that you remain whole, that you exist even when you are not holding everything. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves a lifetime of fusion, and gives you back yourself, who had been lost in the work.

And I want you to see that recovering the border doesn't make you cold, nor distant, nor less committed to what you create. You keep caring, giving your best, loving the work. What changes is that you stop living it as if each thing were a matter of life or death for you. You gain the right distance, the one that lets you see with clarity, decide with calm, err without collapsing, because the work's error stops being your end. And, paradoxically, creating from that healthy distance is creating better, because fusion, which looks like dedication, clouds judgment, and the border, which looks like distance, gives you back the lucidity of someone who loves without blinding herself.

## 7. To recover the border

This crossing has a place it is heading toward. To recover the border between you and what you create.

And I want to put this to you concretely, in the ground of your working days.

To recover the border is to begin to distinguish yourself from the work. To recognize that you are one thing, and the project is another, connected but distinct. To separate your worth from the success of the work, your identity from the existence of the project, your peace from the health of the business. To be able to love what you create without confusing yourself with it.

It is also learning to speak of the work in the third person, so to speak. To say the project had a problem, and not I failed. The company needs to change, and not I am at risk. It seems a detail of language, but it is more than that: it is training your mind to see the work as something you have, and not as something you are. Each time you separate, in words, what happened to the work from what happened to you, you are rebuilding, little by little, the border that was lost, and teaching yourself that you can take care of the project without each thing of it happening to you, inside, like a personal wound.

And it is, above all, giving back to other areas of your life the place the work took from them. The relationships, the body, the rest, the pleasure, the interests that have nothing to do with work. Not as a flight from the work, but as a rebuilding of a whole life, in which creation is an important part, but only a part. Because a person who has only the work is one collapse away from having nothing, and a person who has a whole life can love the work without it being the only thing holding her up.

It is also recovering a life outside the work. An identity that doesn't depend on it. Interests, relationships, parts of you that exist independently of what you create. Because a person whose identity is all in a work is a fragile person, hostage to that work. And recovering a life of your own, outside the project, is recovering a ground that doesn't collapse if the work collapses.

And don't think that this takes you away from the work, or makes you less dedicated. It is the opposite. A creator who has a life of her own, who rests, who nourishes herself from other sources besides work, brings to the work a freshness, a perspective, an energy that an exhausted and fused creator can't have. The best ideas often come from far from the work, in rest, on a walk, in the conversation that has nothing to do with the project. By recovering a life outside the work, you don't abandon it, you nourish it, because you bring to it a more whole person, more alive, more able to create.

And there is a simple sign, day to day, that the border is coming back. It is when you manage to be away, a whole day, without your phone, and discover, on returning, that there were things decided without you, perhaps not as you would have done them, but decided, and that the company didn't stop waiting for you. The first time, that hurts, because it touches the illusion of being irreplaceable. But, if you let it settle, you discover a deep relief beneath the pain: that of knowing that what you built no longer binds you to every hour, that it can breathe without you, and that you, therefore, can too. The border is not measured by how much you hold. It is measured by how much you manage to release and see that it holds up.

I want to give you a concrete image of what the healthy border is. Imagine that the work is a child. A good father, a good mother, loves the child deeply, gives the best, and at the same time knows that the child is not him, that the child has a life of his own, that he will grow up and follow his own path. Healthy love doesn't fuse, it lets the other be other. And your project, like a child, deserves that love that cares without possessing, that dedicates itself without fusing, that loves enough to let it grow. Letting go is not loving less. It is loving in a way that lets the work, and you, breathe.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you let go, that you delegate, that you recover life outside the work, the old fear of losing yourself, the sense of abandoning the project, the emptiness of who you are without it. And you will want to fuse again, to hold everything, to plunge into the work, so the emptiness will pass.

But it is precisely there, in that letting go endured in spite of the fear, that your liberation lies. Because each time you let go, and the work carries on, and you remain whole, you learn that you were not the work, that you exist beyond it, that you can love it without losing yourself in it.

And I want you to understand that recovering the border doesn't happen in a day, it is a path with comings and goings. There will be times when you plunge in whole again, above all in the moments of greatest pressure, of greatest risk to the project, because it is there that the fusion pulls most. That doesn't mean you failed. It means only that the wound is old and that it reactivates when fear presses. The work is not to never get entangled again, it is to learn to notice when you are fusing again, and to take, slowly, a step back, recovering the distance that gives you back yourself. The difference is not in never losing yourself in the work again, it is in knowing how to return to yourself ever more quickly.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that tells you that you are the work, is carved deep, and it will appear each time you let go, each time you rest, each time you recover a life of your own. It will tell you that you are abandoning what you built, neglecting yourself, risking everything. You don't have to silence it all at once, that voice that confuses the company with you, it is enough that you stop doing what it commands. To hear it say you are this, don't separate, and to answer it, slowly, I exist beyond this, and I can let go without losing myself. And each time you let go despite it, and you remain whole, you weaken the old voice, and you strengthen a new one, more your own, which knows that you are worth something for who you are, and not only for what you create.

I want you to see what opens up when you recover the border. Rest opens up, because you no longer have to hold everything. Life opens up, because you already have an identity outside the work. The growth of the work itself opens up, which can, at last, gain a life of its own. And a peace opens up: that of someone who knows she exists, with worth, beyond what she creates, and who therefore can create freely, without the work being a matter of life or death for her identity.

And I want to leave you with something, about who sees you, above all if there are children, or close people who learn by looking at you. If they see you fused with the work, with no life outside of it, unable to stop, living as if you were only your work, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it is likely they will carry, too, the same fusion, the same emptiness outside of what they produce. The most freeing thing you can teach those who watch you is not the example of someone who gave herself entirely to a work. It is that of a woman who creates with passion and also lives, who dedicates herself and also rests, who is more than what she does. Sometimes a woman learns to let go, at last, not only for herself, but to show those she loves that one can love what one does without disappearing into it.

I won't give you a method of management, because this book is not about companies. But I hand you the founding clause to keep. Love what you create, but don't fuse with it. Recover the border between who you are and what you do. And discover, slowly, that you stay whole without holding everything, and that you exist, with worth, far beyond the work you carried so much.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It won't turn the whole operation around in a single morning. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, let go of a work you fused with your whole life. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your exhaustion with dedication, and your work with who you are.

Now you know that you carry the company, the project, the work, on your shoulders, and that this is not only ambition nor love for what you do, it is a fusion between you and what you create.

You know that you placed your identity in what you create, to the point of not knowing who you are without it, and that you existed before the work, you created it, and you exist, whole, beyond it.

You know that you lost the border between you and the work, and that without a border there is no health, neither for you nor for it, because the fusion imprisons them both.

You know whom you fear being without what you create, and that underneath lies the emptiness of having no identity of your own, an emptiness that is an illusion of the fusion, and not a truth about you.

You know that the fusion can lead to burnout, and that recovering the border is not abandoning the work, it is the most responsible way to protect it.

You know that you can stay whole without holding everything, and that the verb is to let go, to recover the border, to love the work without fusing with it.

And you know that letting go is not abandoning, it is the healthiest way to love what you create: the one that cares without possessing, that dedicates itself without losing itself, that lets the work, and you, breathe.

I leave you with an image to keep, and to return to on the hard days.

Imagine someone who planted a tree, and who loved it so much that she stuck to it, embracing the trunk, day and night, year after year, never letting go, convinced that, if she let go, the tree would die. And who, from being so stuck, stopped living her life, and the tree, with someone stuck to the trunk, couldn't grow freely.

The crossing is to unstick yourself from the trunk. To take a step back. To discover that the tree has roots of its own, that it doesn't die from your letting go, that it even grows better with space. And to recover your life, beside the tree, caring for it, loving it, but without being the tree, without being stuck to it, whole and distinct, at last, yourself again.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more demand. There will be setbacks. Days when you let go, when you have a life of your own, when you rest, and days when you plunge whole into the work again, carrying everything, disappearing into it, as always. And the old voice will tell you that you can't after all, that without your fusing nothing moves. Don't believe it. The relapses are written into the ledger of this crossing. Nobody undoes all at once a fusion of years. It is undone little by little, and each time you step away and the work carries on, and you remain whole, a new proof is left, and it is from those proofs, added together, that the border between you and what you create, slowly, is rebuilt.

There remains one thing for me to tell you, without detours, to close. You exist. Not as an extension of the work, not as a function of the project, but as a person, whole, of worth, who happened to create something big, but who is worth something for who she is, and not only for what she made. The woman who fused with her creation did so because there she found worth, meaning, place, and that is worthy of tenderness. But the worth she sought in the work was in her all along, before the work, beyond the work. There is nothing to correct in her dedication. There is only one thing to remind her, slowly, until she believes it: you are not what you create, you are the one who creates, and that is much greater, and nobody can take it from you.

And you already carry with you, deep down, the sentence on which everything said here rests. I can stay whole without holding everything. And you know that letting go doesn't take the work from you, it gives you back yourself.

To let go was the first step. But the step that follows doesn't fit in these pages, no book takes it for you. It is done by unsticking, slowly, from the work. By recovering the border, the life of your own, the identity that doesn't depend on what you create. And there is a deeper work, with someone who walks alongside you, to discover the you that exists beyond the work, because that identity, which had been lost in the creation, rarely recovers itself alone.

If of all this you keep a single idea, when you close the book and go back to the company tomorrow, keep this one. You are not your work. You are the one who made it. And whoever makes is always greater than what is made, because she can make that, and also something else, and also nothing, and keep being, in any case, someone of worth. The work is one of the things you do with your life, perhaps the most important, but it is not your life, nor is it you. Remembering this, slowly, time after time, is what gives you back the freedom to love it without losing yourself in it, and to exist, whole, with or without it.

And before you set down the book and go back to the rush, stay with a request of mine. Small to say, hard to do, for someone who is like you.

This week, devote some time to something that is only yours, that has nothing to do with the work, with the job, with what you create. Something that reminds you who you are beyond the project. And notice the discomfort, the urge to return to the work, the sense that you are wasting time, abandoning what matters. And stay with what is only yours anyway. Because it is in that recovering of a life of your own, endured in spite of the fear, that you begin to unstick yourself from the work, and to discover, at last, that you exist, whole, far beyond what you create.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are being irresponsible, neglecting what you built, risking everything, remember: that thought is the fusion speaking, not the truth. The truth is that taking care of yourself, recovering a life of your own, letting go a little, doesn't put the work at risk, it protects it, because a whole and rested creator sustains what a fused and exhausted creator would, sooner or later, let fall.

Because you are worth something, and you exist, far beyond everything you may ever create.

Because the work is yours, but you are not the work, and that difference is your freedom.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
