# The woman who does not know how to receive help

**For you, who refuse support even when it arrives, because counting on someone, one day, ended in pain. Because you can lean without losing your strength.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who does not know how to lean because counting on someone ended, one day, in pain, and that is a memory of trauma, not pride. If you recognize in yourself a distrust that won't let you accept support, an isolation that costs you dearly, or old wounds from having trusted and been let fall, perhaps you need not only understanding, but support. Seeking out a professional does not take away your strength. It is letting yourself, at last, be supported.*

---

## 1. I don't know how to receive help

Notice what happens when someone offers you support.

You can't accept it. Even when you need it. Even when the support is genuine, offered from the heart, with no price. You refuse, with a smile, you say it isn't necessary, that you'll handle it, that you're fine, and you keep enduring alone what you could share.

It isn't that no one offers you help. Sometimes they do. It's that you don't know how to receive it. Something in you recoils, distrusts, refuses. As if accepting support were dangerous, or a trap, or a risk not worth taking, even when, deep down, it was exactly what you needed.

And when, rarely, you accept, you do it with discomfort, with vigilance, waiting for the support to fail, or to demand a price, or to let you fall halfway. You don't give yourself over to the support, you don't rest in it, you don't let yourself, truly, be supported. You're always half outside, ready to go back to doing things alone the moment the support, as you expect, fails.

Notice that there is here a subtle, but enormous, difference between you and those who never carried this wound. It isn't that you don't know that support exists, nor that you don't recognize it when it appears. It's that you can't trust it. You know, rationally, that that person would help you, that they're good, that they wouldn't betray you. And even so, at the moment of leaning, something brakes. The problem isn't one of information, it's one of trust, and trust isn't decided with the head, it's felt with the body, or it isn't felt. And your body, marked by a fall, doesn't feel it, however much your reason justifies it.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not pride. People may think you're proud, stubborn, incapable of accepting a hand. But what keeps you from receiving support is not pride. It is memory. The memory of a time when you counted on someone, and that someone let you fall.

Because you learned, somewhere, that counting on others ends in pain. You leaned, and the support gave way. You trusted, and you were betrayed. You needed, and you were left alone, in the moment when you most needed. And your body kept that pain, and decided, to protect you, not to lean on anyone again.

This book is different. Because your refusal of support is not only a trait to correct. It is, very often, a trauma response. An adaptation to a time when counting on someone ended in pain. And that deserves more than understanding. So, throughout these pages, I will tell you that perhaps you need not only to understand yourself, but support, of the right kind.

And I'll tell you now where this leads, so you read without arming yourself against the book the way you arm yourself against an outstretched hand. There is a firm bridge where you can set down your foot without losing any of the strength that brought you here. Accepting support does not make you weak, nor put you at someone's mercy, if it is the right support, from someone trustworthy. What taught you not to lean was a real pain, and that pain can, slowly, and perhaps with help, stop governing you.

I want you to distinguish, right away, the woman others see lean on no one from the woman who, inside, cannot let herself be supported. What it seems, from the outside, is an independent woman, who prefers to do everything alone, who doesn't like to owe favors. What it is, inside, is a woman who leaned, one day, and was let fall, and who learned, from that fall, not to entrust her weight to anyone again. Your refusal of support is not, at its origin, a preference. It is a scar. And there is an enormous difference between choosing to do things alone because you want to, and refusing support because trusting terrifies you. The first is freedom. The second is a prison, even if it looks like strength.

This book does not come to tell you that you have to trust everyone, nor that your distrust is all wrong. There are people who don't deserve your trust, and you do well to guard it from them. It comes to tell you that your distrust, by refusing everyone alike, is also punishing those who would support you, and leaving you alone in the face of a life no one should cross alone. And it comes to propose to you not that you lower your guard blindly, but that you recover the discernment that lets you distinguish the firm bridges from the one that collapsed, and lean, at last, on those that hold.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In refusing help even when you're sinking, saying that everything's fine.

In feeling distrust when someone offers you support, searching for the trap, the price, the catch.

In not being able to give yourself over to the support you accept, always staying on guard, waiting for it to fail.

In preferring to do everything alone, whatever it costs, to risking counting on someone who can let you fall.

They are all the same. The refusal of support. The memory that counting on someone ended in pain.

I want to show you how the refusal of support disguises itself as habit, in gestures so much your own that you no longer even notice them. Someone tells you count on me, and you smile and thank them, but inside you've already decided you won't count, because counting is risking. You're in a difficult moment, and when they ask if you need anything, you say no, it's all handled, even when nothing is handled and you're sinking. You accept, at most, the smallest help, the one that doesn't put you in anyone's hands, the one you can withdraw at any moment, and you refuse the one that would require you to truly trust.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way you watch those who help you, waiting for the catch. When someone supports you, you don't rest in the support, you monitor. You search for the hidden price, the interest behind it, the moment it will fail. You can't simply receive, because to receive without distrust would be to lower a guard you learned to never lower. And so, even the support you accept doesn't truly relieve you, because you receive it with one hand and push it away with the other, always half-expecting to regret having accepted it.

And there's something the world doesn't see, beneath your refusal. It sees an independent, capable woman, who is no trouble, who solves everything, and it admires you for it, and perhaps even settles into your never having to be helped. It doesn't see the distrust that governs you, the weight you carry alone for not daring to trust, the loneliness of someone who never lets herself be supported. It sees the strength, not the wound. And you, who receive admiration for your self-sufficiency, become even more bound to it, because now there are also others used to you never needing anything, and going against that image costs you even more.

## 2. Counting on someone ended in pain

Go back. To the beginning. To when counting on someone cost you dearly.

Because no one refuses support this way without a reason. Your distrust was not born from nothing. It was born from a real experience, in which you leaned, and the support failed in a way that left its mark on you.

Perhaps you counted on someone who should have been there, and that person was not there. A father, a mother, someone in whom you placed your trust as a child, and who failed you in the moment when you most needed. And you learned that one can't count on anyone, that support gives way precisely when you most lean on it.

Perhaps you leaned, and the support became a betrayal. You trusted, you opened up, you let yourself depend, and you were hurt in that place of trust. And you learned that leaning is exposing yourself, and that whoever exposes herself is hurt.

Perhaps you needed help, as a child, and it came with a price. With a demand, a debt, a manipulation. Whoever helped you, helped in order to later demand, to have power over you. And you learned that accepting support puts you in debt, at the mercy of the one who helps, and that it's safer not to accept than to be in someone's hands.

Or perhaps the fall was not a single moment, but a drop by drop. Perhaps you grew up with adults who promised and didn't deliver, who were there and suddenly weren't, who now held you up and now let you fall, without your being able to predict which it would be. And that unpredictability teaches something even more corrosive than a clear betrayal: it teaches that support is never reliable, that you can't lean on it because you don't know if it will be there. A child who grows up not knowing whether she can count learns not to count at all, because hope that fails hurts more than the absence of hope.

Notice what a child, or a young woman, learns in any of these places. She learns that counting on others is dangerous. That support gives way, or betrays, or demands a price. That the only way to be safe is to lean on no one, to do everything alone, not to depend on a support that will fail. And that lesson is engraved deep, in the body, as a memory of pain.

And that person did what she had to do. She stopped leaning. She became self-sufficient, distrustful of support, capable of enduring everything alone. And that protected her, in that time, from the pain of counting on those who failed. It was an intelligent adaptation to a real experience of being let fall.

I want to pause, for a moment, on the pain there is in this origin, because it's easy to pass over it quickly. To be let fall by those who should hold you is not just any setback. It is one of the deepest wounds there is, because it touches the most basic trust, that of being able to count on someone, that of the world having hands when one falls. When that trust breaks early, something very deep breaks, and the distrust that remained is not a defect of yours, it is the scar of a real wound. Recognizing this is not dwelling on the past, it is ceasing to blame yourself for a defense that pain, and not your ill will, raised.

But the foot you learned to take off any bridge did not come back down with time. It stayed suspended, forever. And today, as an adult, in a world where perhaps there is trustworthy support, you continue to refuse it, defended against an old pain, and deprived of a present comfort you never let yourself receive.

I want you to see this clearly. Your refusal of support is not pride, nor stubbornness, nor an incapacity of character. It is an intelligent response to a real pain. You are not distrustful by temperament. You are someone who leaned and was let fall, and who learned, from that pain, not to lean again. And recognizing this is honoring the memory that protects you, and aching, at the same time, that it was necessary.

I want you to see where the warning not to lean gets engraved, because that is what explains why wanting to trust is not enough. The lesson that every bridge falls does not live only in the memory you can recount. It lives in the body, in that recoil that grips your chest before you think, deeper than any word. When someone we leaned on lets us fall, above all early, the body engraves that moment as a warning of survival: leaning is dangerous. And that warning fires, afterward, on its own, whenever someone offers us support, before any thought, in a recoil, in a distrust, in an automatic no. So, today, your refusal does not pass through a decision. It happens before you decide, because it is your body protecting you from an old fall.

That's why you so often tell yourself that this person is trustworthy, that there's no reason to distrust, and even so you can't give yourself over. You can't because the distrust is not born from reasoning about this person, it is born from a body memory, ancient, that treats all support as the bridge that collapsed. And a body that has already fallen once does not let itself be convinced by guarantees that this bridge is firm. It is relearned, slowly, through new experiences, repeated, in which the body discovers that it leaned and, this time, did not fall. I tell you this to lift off you the guilt of not being able, simply by wishing it, to trust. It is not a lack of good will. It is that the fear of leaning lives in a place words don't reach, and that calms only by setting down the foot, slowly, on bridges that hold, often with someone who knows how to be on the other side holding you while you try.

## 3. The distrust that protects and isolates

There is something about your distrust of support that you need to see. It protects you, yes, from a possible betrayal. But it also isolates you from a real comfort. And, with time, what promised to be a protection became your greatest prison, because it closes you not only to danger, but to all the life that happens only in connection.

Notice. Your distrust treats all support as suspect. Every outstretched hand as a possible trap. Every offering as a possible price. And by treating all support as dangerous, you refuse, indiscriminately, both the support that would betray you and the one that would truly comfort you. You close the door to everyone, by reflex, without distinguishing.

And there is an enormous cost in this. It costs you facing life alone. Everything that is difficult, illness, loss, despair, the weight, you face it alone, because you lean on no one. And there are moments in life when no one should be alone, when accepting support is not weakness, it is what makes the unbearable bearable. And you, in those moments, refuse the comfort, and carry alone what no one should carry alone.

It costs you intimacy. Because true closeness is born, in part, from letting ourselves be comforted by one another. From entrusting our weight to someone, and from holding someone's weight. Someone who leans on no one keeps everyone at a distance, in adversity, and so never knows the deep intimacy of someone who let herself, truly, be held.

And there is a particular loneliness in this, different from that of someone physically alone. It is the loneliness of someone who is surrounded by people and, even so, alone within, because she lets no one reach the part of her that would need comfort. You can have people around, who love you, who would be there, and continue to feel alone in adversity, because your distrust does not let them in where it hurts. It is a loneliness chosen without being chosen, imposed by the memory of a fall, and perhaps it is, more than anything, the highest price this shell exacts from you: that of living accompanied and, within, always alone.

And it costs you the tiredness of someone who endures everything alone. Because there is a human limit to what one carries alone. And you, by refusing support, take yourself to that limit, and beyond it, exhausted, at the brink of breaking, instead of sharing the weight with those who would share it. Your distrust spares you the pain of a possible betrayal, at the price of the certain exhaustion of someone who never lets herself be comforted.

Notice this loneliness. The loneliness of someone who, in adversity, is always alone. Of someone who faces what is difficult with no one to hold her. Of someone who, even surrounded by people who would help her, refuses, and endures, and sinks, alone. It is a loneliness chosen, not because you like it, but because you learned it as the only safe way to be, after a time when you leaned and were let fall.

And it costs you, too, the injustice you do to those who are trustworthy. Because your distrust does not distinguish. It treats those who truly love you, those who would never betray you, those who would be there without demanding a price, the same way it would treat those who hurt you. And those people, who deserved your trust, feel kept at a distance, put to the test, suspected of a betrayal they did not commit. Without meaning to, you make those who wish you well pay the bill of those who did you wrong. And there is, sometimes, a sadness in those who love you, that of wanting to be there for you and never being let in.

I want you to see the difference between healthy prudence and your distrust. Healthy prudence evaluates each person by what they show, and opens itself to those who deserve trust. It has both doors: it distrusts those who give cause, it trusts those who deserve it. Yours doesn't. Yours closed both doors, it refuses everyone alike, by reflex, without evaluating. And a distrust that does not distinguish is not prudence, it is a shell, that protects you from those who would betray you at the price of isolating you from those who would comfort you. The work of this crossing is not to make you naive, it is to give you back the discernment that fear took from you.

I want you to feel the weight of this. The weight of facing your whole life without being able to lean on anyone. Of carrying alone what was meant to be shared. Of refusing, time after time, the comfort that would do you good, out of fear of a pain that perhaps would not come. It is a deep tiredness, and perhaps you already take it for independence, when it is, at bottom, the loneliness of someone who was let fall and never again dared to lean.

## 4. Who am I if I lean

I'll leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you if you lean on someone? If you accept help? If you stop, for a moment, facing everything alone?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Perhaps a distrust, perhaps a fear.

For you, this question touches a memory of pain. Because leaning, in your past, ended in a fall. And imagining yourself leaning again awakens the echo of that fall, the fear of trusting again and being let fall, of exposing yourself and being betrayed, of depending and being at someone's mercy.

And the shadow hiding here is the memory of trust betrayed. The time you leaned, and the support gave way. That memory lives in you, in the body, and fires whenever someone offers you support, telling you: careful, you leaned once, and look how it ended. And you, so as not to relive the fall, refuse the support, even the one that would comfort you.

Notice how this imprisons you. You can't lean, even while sinking, because leaning awakens the fear of the old fall. You live facing everything alone, at an enormous cost, because the fear of being let fall again is greater than the weight you carry alone. The memory of one betrayal governs your entire relationship with support, today, with people who perhaps would never betray you.

And there is a sadness very much its own in someone who refuses comfort, and I want to say it out loud. It is that of knowing, deep down, that there are people who would want to comfort you, and of not being able to let them, however much you wish it. It isn't that you don't want to be held. You would want it, perhaps desperately. It's that your body won't let you, it fires the alarm, recoils, closes the door, even when your deepest part longs to enter through it. To live this way, desiring comfort and refusing it at the same time, is a silent form of suffering, and it deserves all tenderness, not the harshness of those who call you proud without knowing what it costs you.

And there is an enormous fragility in this self-sufficiency of yours. Because a person who cannot lean on anyone is alone in the face of everything. And that loneliness, in adversity, is not strength, it is helplessness. True strength includes the capacity to lean, when you need to, on those who are trustworthy. Refusing all support is not being strong. It is being trapped in a memory that does not let you be comforted.

And I want you to see the painful irony of this position. You, who refuse support so as not to be let fall again, end up, by doing so, letting yourself fall, every day. Because the helplessness of someone who faces everything alone is, itself, a form of fall, slow, continuous, with no one underneath. You avoided the fall of trusting and being betrayed, but you fell into the fall of trusting no one and being left without a net. Both hurt. And perhaps the second, for being permanent, hurts, in the end, even more than the first, the one you so fear to repeat.

And I want to tell you something about the terror it gives you just to imagine yourself leaning on someone, because it is here that it begins to lose its force. What terrifies you is not, truly, leaning. It is the fall you learned that support brought. For your body, the two things are one, fused in that moment when the bridge collapsed. But they are separable. Leaning, in itself, is not falling. It is entrusting the weight to something, and most bridges, unlike that one, hold. The fall belonged to that bridge, that person, that time. Today, with someone trustworthy, leaning and falling can, at last, stop going together.

I want to tell you something about this memory, because it begins to disarm itself. That person who let you fall is not all people. That betrayal is not what all support will do to you. Your body learned, once, that leaning ends in pain, and generalized that lesson to everyone, forever. But it was the lesson of one person, in one moment, and not a truth about all the support that exists. There are people who won't let you fall. And refusing everyone because of the one who betrayed you is letting that person, even today, govern your life.

And there is a hidden cruelty in refusing all support, that perhaps you've never seen this way. That person who let you fall did you harm once. But the memory of them, by governing your whole life, continues to do you harm every day, by depriving you of all the support you could have. It is as if the betrayal continued to happen, repeated, not by the person who committed it, but by the shell they left you. And freeing yourself from this is not only ceasing to suffer that fall. It is ceasing to continue, against yourself, the harm another person began, and that you, without meaning to, have prolonged for years by refusing everything.

Don't answer the question of who you are if you lean just yet. It isn't decided with the head, it is felt in the body on the day it dares to set down its weight, and sometimes it needs more than a book to get there. But leave it open. Because that is where the door is. On the day your body learns that leaning, today, on those who are trustworthy, does not end in the fall of before, you stop having to face everything alone. And there, only there, can you begin to let yourself be comforted.

## 5. Perhaps you need more than to understand

I have to stop the book here. With all seriousness. Because your refusal of support is not, most of the time, only a personality trait. It is a memory of trauma, and that changes what you need.

If you learned not to lean because you counted on someone and were let fall, betrayed, or made to pay, then your distrust is not pride. It is a trauma response. A scar of a trust that broke. And the fear of leaning is not undone simply by understanding where it comes from. It lives in the body, in that recoil that grips you when a hand reaches out, in the certainty engraved deep that every bridge ends up giving way.

I want to name, plainly, what kind of fall may lie beneath your refusal. If there was, in your story, grave betrayal, abandonment in the moment of need, abuse of trust by those who should have cared for you, then your difficulty in receiving support is a serious wound, and it deserves to be cared for with specialized support.

And here I'll ask you to hear me with firmness and tenderness at the same time. There is a paradox that, to you more than anyone, will be hard to swallow, but that is important. The healing of your difficulty in receiving support passes, itself, through receiving support, that of a professional. And I know that is precisely what costs you. But the helping relationship, with a psychologist, a therapist, can be, for you, the place where you learn, slowly, safely, that leaning does not always end in the fall of before.

Because in a well-conducted helping relationship, you experience something new. You lean, and the support does not give way. You trust, and the trust is not betrayed. You depend, for a moment, and you are not made to pay, nor let fall. And your body learns, with that experience, repeated, safe, that there exists support one can, in fact, trust. It is the healing of the wound of this book, done in the very place of support.

I want to separate two things for you, because that is what knowing which support to seek depends on. A distrust of temperament is worked through by understanding: you see the pattern of refusing help, you understand the origin, and that, in time, helps to change it. A fall engraved as trauma is something else. It does not live only in the head, it lives in the body, in the recoil that fires before thought, the moment someone reaches out a hand to you. And a wound like that needs more than understanding. It needs time, the body, a safe relationship where the system relearns, slowly, that leaning no longer ends in the fall of before. That, alone, is rarely achieved. It is done with accompaniment.

And I want to take away the fear that, in this book more than in any other, will hold you back: the fear that seeking help is, itself, one more occasion to be let fall. I know that precisely this costs you, trusting a professional, leaning on someone you don't yet know. But notice: a good helping relationship does not ask you to trust blindly, from the start. It asks you only to take a first small step, and lets trust be built slowly, as that support proves, time after time, that it holds. You have every right to advance with caution, to evaluate, to trust gradually at your own pace. You don't have to throw yourself in. You only have to allow yourself to begin.

And I want to tell you something with all firmness and all tenderness: if your refusal of support comes from a grave betrayal, from an abandonment in the moment of greatest need, from an abuse of trust by those who should have cared for you, this is not your fault, and it is not resolved by effort alone. You deserve the support of someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, your distrust, the memory of the fall you carry engraved deep. And seeking that support is not risking one more fall. It is giving yourself the chance to, in a safe and trained place, relearn what that betrayal took from you: that there is support one can trust.

If you recognize, in your story, the pain of having been let fall, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Seeking help does not take away your strength, and it is not one more fall waiting to happen. It is the opportunity to learn, at last, that there is support that holds. And you deserve that learning.

## 6. Leaning without losing my strength

If your refusal of support is a memory of a fall, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can lean without losing your strength.

Notice what you fear. For you, leaning seems to lose your strength, to become dependent, weak, at the mercy of the one who supports you. As if accepting comfort were giving up the self-sufficiency that kept you upright, and ending up, again, in the vulnerable position where you were, one day, let fall.

And I propose to question this. Leaning is not losing your strength. Your strength stays with you, intact. Leaning is, keeping your strength, letting someone hold with you what is too heavy for one pair of hands alone. It is interdependence, in which one is strong and accepts support, according to what life asks. It is not the opposite of strength. It is strength knowing, at last, how to rest in the hands of those who are trustworthy.

Notice that the people you most admire, the ones who seem most whole, most firm in life, are not the ones who never lean on anyone. They are, almost always, the ones who have around them people they trust, networks that sustain them, people they turn to when life tightens. Their solidity does not come from needing no one, it comes precisely from knowing whom to lean on. The image of the strong person who crosses everything alone is a myth, and a myth that costs you dearly, because it makes you refuse exactly what makes people truly strong: the capacity not to be alone.

I want to distinguish, because it's important. There is dependence, in which one loses one's own capacity, hangs on someone, at their mercy. That is not what I propose to you. There is leaning, in which one keeps one's strength, and accepts, now and then, the comfort of those who are trustworthy, without ceasing to be capable. The first takes away strength. The second shares it. And they are very different things, which your fear confuses into one.

Notice that it is precisely your fear of dependence that pushes you toward the opposite extreme, isolation, and that neither of the extremes is healthy. At one end, total dependence, in which one hands everything over to another and loses the ground. At the other, total isolation, in which one entrusts no one the slightest weight. You live at the second end, thinking it is the safe place. But the healthy place is neither of the extremes, it is the middle: interdependence, in which one keeps one's own strength and accepts, and offers, support, according to what life asks. It is toward that middle that this crossing calls you, not toward the dependence you fear, but toward a balance in which trusting does not mean losing yourself.

I want to be honest. If yours is a deep wound of a fall, agreeing with this is not enough to make you feel it. Your body will keep distrusting, fearing the fall, however much your head knows there is trustworthy support. So this path, very often, is made with help, with someone to accompany you in rebuilding, slowly, the trust that broke.

But there are small steps you can take, with the foot still fearful. You can begin by accepting support in a small way, from those who, over time, showed themselves trustworthy. A small help. A comfort in a difficult moment. And observe what happens. Outside, with the right person, almost certainly, the support does not give way, does not demand a price, does not let you fall. Inside, the distrust, the fear of the fall, the desire to go back to doing things alone. And to stay with that, to let yourself lean, is to give your body a new piece of evidence. That you leaned, and didn't fall. That there was, this time, someone to hold you.

Each time you lean, in a small way, and the bridge holds, you give your system a new piece of evidence. That leaning, today, does not end in the fall of back then. And it is with evidence, repeated, in the body, and not only with ideas, in the head, that the distrust, slowly, loosens. The shell is not undone by convincing yourself you can trust. It is undone by leaning, in a small way, on those who deserve it, and discovering that the bridge held, time after time, until the body believes it. It is slow work, of many small pieces of evidence, and so don't demand of yourself to trust all at once: the body learns at its own pace, and your task is only to give it, repeatedly, the experience of leaning and not falling.

I want to warn you of what will happen inside you at the first hand you accept, so it doesn't frighten you. The first time you lean, even on a small thing, the distrust will likely rise strong, you'll feel an urgent desire to withdraw, to go back to doing things alone, to regret having accepted. You'll search, almost avidly, for the first sign that the support will fail, so as to be proved right, so as to be able to retreat. That is not a sign that you did wrong in accepting. It is your old system trying to avoid the fall, sabotaging the support before it can disappoint you. Don't obey it right away. Let the support happen, stay with the distrust without obeying it, and give your body the chance to discover that, this time, the bridge held.

And I want you to choose well whom you lean on, in this phase, because it matters a great deal. Don't begin with the person who already failed you, with the one who would give you reason to distrust. Begin with whoever, over time, showed themselves trustworthy, with small consistent gestures, with a presence that did not fail. It is not naivety, it is the opposite: it is using your discernment to choose where your body has the best chance of learning the new truth. There is time, afterward, to risk more. First, the body needs evidence that leaning can go well.

The awakening, when it comes, is felt in the body before you think it. It is the time you set down the weight and the bridge does not give way, in which you lean, and the support holds. In which you trust, and you are not betrayed. In which you depend, for a moment, and you discover, with astonishment, that you didn't fall, that you were comforted, that this time was different. It is a small instant, but it is enormous, because it is your body learning, perhaps for the first time since that fall, that there is support one can trust.

## 7. Letting myself be comforted

There is a verb for this crossing. To lean.

To lean. To let yourself be comforted. To accept support, in adversity, from those who are trustworthy. Not to face everything alone, but to share the weight, to rest, for a moment, in another's hands.

And I want to show you, slowly, what fits inside this verb you so fear.

Leaning is not becoming dependent, nor losing your strength, nor ending up at anyone's mercy. It is, keeping your strength, letting it rest, now and then, in a trustworthy support. It is ceasing to carry everything alone, and allowing someone to help you carry, when the weight is great. It is being, you too, comforted, and not only the one who endures alone.

And it is, also, ceasing to treat yourself the way the one who let you fall treated you. Because, by refusing all comfort, it is you, now, who deny yourself support, who leave yourself alone in adversity, who repeat, against yourself, the helplessness of back then. Leaning is ceasing to continue, yourself, the abandonment another began, and giving yourself, at last, the care that was denied you.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you lean, the old distrust, the fear of the fall, the memory of the betrayal, the desire to go back to doing things alone. And you will want to refuse the support, take back control, not expose yourself, so as not to risk the pain of before.

But it is precisely there, in that leaning despite the fear, that your healing lies. Because each time you lean, on those who are trustworthy, and the support holds, you teach your body something new. That leaning, today, with this person, does not end in the fall of back then. That there is support one can trust. That you don't have to face everything alone.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that told you to distrust everyone, is engraved deep, and it will appear with each support you accept, with its usual harshness. It will tell you careful, you'll fall again, don't trust. The work is not to silence it all at once, it is to stop obeying it blindly. To hear it say don't trust, and to answer it, slowly, this person has already shown me they're trustworthy, I'm going to risk leaning a little. And each time you lean despite it, and the bridge holds, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more your own, wiser, that knows how to distinguish, instead of refusing everything out of fear.

And don't confuse, along the way, a setback with proof that you were right. There will be days when someone disappoints you in a small thing, when the support doesn't come exactly as you expected, and your old voice will shout: you see, one can't trust. But a small disappointment is not a fall, and not every human failing is a betrayal. People, even trustworthy ones, sometimes fail, forget, can't manage. Learning to distinguish a small, reparable failing, within a relationship of trust, from a betrayal that justifies closing the door, is part of the discernment this crossing gives you back. Not everything that hurts a little proves the bridge will collapse.

I want to tell you something important. Leaning requires discernment, and that is a strength, not a naivety. I don't ask you to trust just anyone, to accept support from whoever it may be, to lower your guard blindly. I ask you to learn to distinguish those who are trustworthy from those who are not, and to lean on the former. Your distrust, in the past, did not distinguish, it refused everyone. Mature trust distinguishes, and leans on those who earned trust. And that discernment is what gives you back the safety to lean.

And I want to teach you to read the signs, because your fear took away the use of them. Those who are trustworthy show it with time, not with promises. It is there in the small things, consistently. They keep what they say. They don't make you pay for what they give. They respect your no. They don't use your fragility against you afterward. They don't disappear when the weight increases. These signs are not seen in an instant, they are seen over months, over years, and that is why mature trust is built slowly, and is not demanded right away. You, who learned to close the door to everyone alike, can relearn to open it little by little, observing, letting each person show you, over time, whether they are a firm bridge or one that will collapse.

And there is something freeing in realizing this: your distrust, instead of an enemy, can become an ally, if you tune it. Instead of a wall that refuses everyone, it can become a sharpened sense that distinguishes. Your capacity to detect danger, which was born from pain, is real and is valuable. The problem was never having it, it was having it pointed at everyone, without exception. When you use it to choose well, and not to refuse everything, it stops isolating you and starts truly protecting you, letting you lean on those who deserve it and guard yourself from those who don't. That is not lowering your guard. It is putting the guard at the service of life, instead of at the service of isolation.

I want to show you, too, that leaning is a gift to those who love you, and not only something you receive. Think of how you feel when someone you care for leans on you, entrusts you their weight, lets you help. You don't feel used, you feel trusted, close, chosen. Well, that is exactly what you deny those who love you, each time you refuse the support: the joy of comforting you, the closeness of being let in, the trust of being chosen as your bridge. Leaning on those who are trustworthy takes nothing from them. It gives them the opportunity to love you in a way you never allowed them.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that perhaps you don't even remember wanting, so used are you to enduring alone. The joy of not being alone in adversity. Of sharing the weight. Of being comforted, held, helped, in a difficult moment. Of discovering that there are people who won't let you fall, and of letting yourself, at last, be held by those hands. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day you lean, and discover that you didn't fall.

And I want to undo, further, a fear that will appear: the fear that, if you lean once, you'll become dependent, clinging, incapable of fending for yourself again. It won't happen. Your capacity to be enough on your own, which you built over an entire lifetime, does not disappear by accepting a hand now and then. It stays yours, whole, ready to fend for yourself whenever you need it. What changes is that enduring everything alone stops being your only path, your prison. Leaning does not replace your strength, it adds to it a choice. And a woman who knows how to fend for herself and knows how to lean on those who are trustworthy is freer, and stronger, than one who only knows the first half.

I won't give you a recipe for trusting, and this crossing is, very often, made with hands that accompany you on the other side of the bridge. But I'll leave you the principle. Lean, in a small way, on those who are trustworthy. Let yourself be comforted, in adversity. And discover, slowly, and perhaps with hands to accompany you, that leaning does not take away your strength, and that you don't have to face, alone, everything life brings.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. But, with a fall engraved in the body, understanding may not be enough to let you set down your foot on a bridge, and to hide that from you would be to let you fall again.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It does not make you, tomorrow, accept the support you refused your whole life. But it changes enough so that you stop confusing your refusal of support with strength, and your isolation with independence.

Now you know that you don't know how to receive help, and that this is not pride, it is memory, the memory of a fall your body never forgot.

You know that counting on someone ended, one day, in pain, and that you learned, from that pain, not to lean again, and that this is a scar, not a defect of yours.

You know that your distrust protects you from a possible betrayal, at the price of the certain helplessness of someone who faces everything alone, and that it does not distinguish, it refuses everyone alike, even those who would comfort you.

You know whom you fear to be if you lean, and that beneath it lies the memory of trust betrayed, which separates itself, over time, from the safe present.

You know that perhaps you need more than to understand, and that the helping relationship can be, itself, the first firm bridge where you learn that there is support that holds, that does not give way, that does not demand a price, and that heals, in the very place of support, the wound of having been let fall.

You know that you can lean without losing your strength, and that the verb is to lean, to let yourself be comforted by those who are trustworthy.

And you know, now, that the discernment fear took from you can return, tuned: that you can learn to distinguish the firm bridges from the one that collapsed, and lean on those that hold, without letting an old betrayal close you, forever, to all the hands that would want to hold you.

I want to leave you an image.

Imagine someone who, crossing a river, leaned on a bridge, and the bridge collapsed, and that person fell, and was hurt. And who learned, that time, never again to trust any bridge, to always cross by swimming, alone, exhausted, against the current, even when, ahead, there were firm, solid bridges, that would have held her.

The crossing is learning to distinguish the firm bridges from the one that collapsed. To trust that not all bridges fall. To put your foot, slowly, on a solid bridge, and to discover that it holds, that you don't fall, that you can cross supported, instead of swimming, alone, always. To put your foot on a bridge after the one that collapsed is not easy, and sometimes it's done with someone to hold your hand on the first step. But on the other side is the end of an entire life's solitary crossing.

I want to tell you what you'll find along the way, so you don't cross this crossing swimming and alone, like everything else. There will be setbacks. Days when you lean, and days when you close the door to everything again, distrusting everyone, doing everything alone, as always. And your old voice will tell you that you've seen it, that one can't trust anyone, that it's safer the way you always did it. Don't believe it at first. To distrust again for a few days is not having failed the crossing, it is part of making it. No one rebuilds all at once a trust that broke. It is rebuilt little by little, one support at a time, and each time you lean and the bridge holds, even if you retreat afterward, a new piece of evidence stays in the body, and it is from that evidence, accumulated, that trust, slowly, is reborn.

And I want to speak to you of those who grow up watching you endure everything alone, if there are children, people close to you, who learn by looking at you. If they see you always refuse support, distrust everyone, face everything alone, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it's likely they'll carry, too, the same distrust, the same loneliness. The most freeing thing you can do for those who watch you is not to give them the example of the woman who never needs anyone. It is to show them a woman who knows how to choose well whom to trust, and who lets herself, at last, be comforted by those who deserve it. Sometimes a woman learns to lean, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn those she loves to the same distrust of someone who never again dared to count on anyone.

And one last thing remains, simple, before I let you go. You were not wrong to distrust. The person who protected herself, after having been let fall, did what she had to do so as not to be hurt again. There is nothing to reproach in her. There is only one thing to say to her, slowly, time after time, until the body believes it: that bridge collapsed, but it is not all bridges. You can cross again, with care, and discover that there are people who hold you.

You know, deep down, what the firm bridge is that holds this whole book. I can lean without losing my strength. And you know that leaning, with discernment, on those who are trustworthy, is not repeating the fall, it is discovering, at last, that there are people who hold you.

Recognizing that you don't know how to receive help was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not crossed only by reading. It is crossed by setting down the foot, slowly, on bridges of trust. And, very often, it is made with help, with someone prepared to be, themselves, the first firm bridge on which you learn that support, today, may not fall. Because a wound of someone who was let fall deserves hands that know how to, this time, hold her.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. The person who let you fall does not have to be the last word on what support is. They showed you a bridge that collapsed, but they did not show you all bridges, and there are, in the world, firm bridges that they never let you know, because the fear they left you made you refuse them before you tried them. Giving yourself back the possibility of crossing them is not forgetting what you suffered. It is refusing to let the one who hurt you continue, today, to choose for you whom you can let in.

And before you close these pages, I ask you only one gesture, of the kind you usually refuse. Difficult, for you.

This week, accept a support you usually refuse. From someone who, over time, showed themselves trustworthy. A small help, a comfort in a difficult moment. And instead of refusing, by reflex, accept. Let yourself lean. And notice the distrust that rises, the fear of the fall, the desire to go back to doing things alone. And let yourself lean anyway. Because it is in that letting yourself be comforted, endured despite the fear, that your body begins, slowly, to learn that not all support gives way, and that you don't have to, after all, cross everything alone.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are risking too much, exposing yourself, preparing the next fall, remember: that thought is the memory, not the truth. The truth is that you chose a firm bridge, with care, and that letting yourself cross it is not repeating the old fall, it is beginning, at last, to discover that there are people who hold you.

Because you deserve, after everything, to discover that not all bridges collapse.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
