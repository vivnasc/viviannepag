# The one who carries the family

**For you, who became the backbone of the household before you were old enough to be. Because holding everything up is not the same as loving, and you can set down what you carry without abandoning anyone.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who became the pillar of a family, and there is a point at which carrying everything stops being devotion and starts to crush you. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion that never lifts, a background anxiety tied to keeping everything standing, or a weight that is taking away your own life, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding what you carry is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. If I don't hold it up, who will

There is a sentence you may never have said out loud, but that organizes your entire life: if I don't hold this up, who will? It is a sentence you carry deep down, before any choice, and it makes you rush to everything, without even thinking, like someone answering a reflex older than reason.

Look at your family. And notice who holds it up.

It's you. You're the one who solves the crises. Who soothes the conflicts. Who is present when someone falls ill, when there's a problem, when everything threatens to collapse. You're the one who endures, the one who can't be missing, the column the whole house leans on.

And it's not just now. It's always been this way. For as long as you can remember, you were the one who held things up. The one who took care of things. The one who solved them. The one who carried an entire family on her shoulders, often before she even had shoulders for it.

Notice the weight of this, which perhaps you no longer even feel, so used to it are you. You carry everyone's worries. The crises of your parents, of your siblings, of whoever in your family needs you. You're always available, always alert, always ready to come running. And never, never, do you allow yourself to stop, because stopping means, in your mind, letting fall the house that only you hold up.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not your strength. Everyone tells you that you're strong, that you're a pillar, that they don't know what they'd do without you. And there is strength in you, yes. But what makes you carry everything is not strength. It's something older, and sadder.

It's a place you took up far too early. The place of the one who holds everything up. And which you never let go of, because you became, without deciding to, the person the entire family depends on.

I want to set this apart from other things, because it matters. It's not the mother who carries her children, which is another wound, from another world. It's not the professional who carries the company, which is yet another. Here it's deeper. Here you became the substitute pillar of an entire family system, almost always because someone was missing. A father who fell ill, or vanished, or absented himself in his heart. A mother who sank, who couldn't cope, who needed someone to hold her up. A family that lived through instability, and where a child, you, filled the void the adults left behind.

This book is not going to tell you to abandon your family, to stop caring, to cut off the people you love. The opposite of that. I have come to look at the weight you carry, and to ask whether it is really yours to carry. I have come to show you that holding an entire family on your shoulders is not love, it's a loyalty to its suffering, and that this loyalty, however noble it may seem, is costing you your own life.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can set down what you carry without abandoning anyone. The house you hold up existed before you, and it will go on existing after you. You are not the one keeping it standing alone, even though you learned to believe you are. And setting the weight down is not the same as no longer loving. It's no longer confusing love with the obligation to hold everything up.

I want you to understand, right away, the difference between what it looks like and what it is. What it looks like, from the outside, is a generous, devoted person, who loves her family so much that she gives herself entirely to it. What it is, on the inside, is a child who took up an adult's place far too early, and who never managed to leave it. Your giving is not, at its root, the free choice of someone who loves. It's a survival adaptation of someone who felt that, if she didn't hold things up, everything would fall. And there is an enormous difference between caring for your family because you want to and from the right place, and carrying it because you're terrified of what happens if you let go. The first is love. The second is a prison dressed up as love.

This book does not come to pull you away from your family, nor to tell you that caring for your own people is wrong. Caring for those we love is one of the most beautiful things there is. It comes to distinguish caring from carrying, loving from holding up, being present from crushing yourself under the weight of everyone. It comes to give you back the right to a life of your own, inside a family you still love, but which you no longer have to hold up alone on your shoulders.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In being the first one everyone calls when there's a problem in the family, and the last to ask for anything for yourself.

In being unable to rest while you feel there's someone in your family in need, even when that person is an adult and perfectly capable.

In feeling an enormous guilt at the mere thought of stepping away, of living your own life, of putting yourself ahead of the family you hold up.

In being exhausted, for years, from carrying a weight that nobody asked you for in words, but that you feel is yours, and yours alone, to hold up.

In feeling guilty when something good happens to you, as if you had no right to be happy while there is, in your family, someone who isn't well.

They are all the same thing. The place of the one who carries the family. The pillar that became a prison.

I want to show you how this shows up in everyday life, in things you may already take for normal. The phone rings, it's the name of a relative, and before you even answer your body is already bracing for one more problem to solve. You have a plan of your own, a trip, a rest, and a family crisis comes up, and you cancel, without hesitating, because yours always comes last. You receive good news in your life, and the first thing you feel is not pure joy, it's the calculation of how it will be received by a family where not everyone is well.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way you feel guilty for things you don't control. If a relative is unwell, you feel you should have done more. If there's a conflict, you feel you should have prevented it. If someone suffers, you feel it as a failing of yours, as though it were your job to guarantee everyone's well-being, and any unhappiness in the family were proof that you didn't hold things up well enough. You carry not only the weights, but the responsibility for outcomes that were never, truly, in your hands.

And there's something nobody sees, beneath your devotion. The family sees someone strong, always available, someone they can trust, and they lean on you the way one leans on solid ground, without imagining what it costs you. They don't see the exhaustion, the postponed life, the tired person carrying alone what should belong to many. They see the pillar, not the weight of being the pillar. And you, who receive the recognition of being the strong one, become even more bound to the post, because now there's also the image everyone has of you to hold up, and disappointing it feels to you like betraying who you are.

## 2. The substitute pillar

Go back. To the beginning. To when this weight first landed on your shoulders.

Almost always, it was early. Far too early.

There was, in your family, a void. Someone who should have held things up, and didn't. A father who was missing, through death, through absence, through illness, through addiction, through some weakness or other. A mother who sank into a pain, a depression, an incapacity, and who could no longer be the adult the house needed. Or both, each absent in their own way, leaving a hole at the center of the family.

And nature does not tolerate a void. A family system without a pillar searches, desperately, for someone to fill the empty place. And very often that someone is a child. The most responsible one. The most sensitive one. The one who understood, without words, that if nobody held that up, the house would fall.

It was you. You filled the empty place. You became the substitute pillar, the column that was missing, the adult the family didn't have. And you did it, most likely, without anyone asking you directly. You felt the void, you felt the danger, and you took up the post, because it was that or watch the house crumble.

Notice what this meant. A child should not hold up a family. She should be held up by it. The natural order is for parents to support their children, not the other way around. When that order is reversed, when it's the child who supports the system, something breaks, and the child pays a price she carries for the rest of her life.

You paid with your childhood. You weren't, truly, a child, because there was no room for it. While the other children played, you kept watch. You were attentive to the mood of the house, to the pain of the adults, to the danger of everything collapsing. You grew up too fast, because the house needed an adult, and you were the adult it didn't have.

Notice what this left in you, and what you may still carry. An enormous difficulty in playing, in resting, in being light, because lightness was never safe, there was always something to watch over. A hypervigilance toward other people's moods, trained from early on to read the climate of the house in order to anticipate danger, and which today doesn't switch off, making you feel everything others feel, as though it were your job to manage it. And an inability to simply receive care, because your place was always to give, and receiving brings you discomfort, almost a shame, as though you had no right to it.

And there is a grief here that you may never have wept: that of the childhood you didn't have. There is, in you, a child who never got to be a child, who carried what she shouldn't have, who grew up before her time. And that child deserves to be acknowledged, and wept for, not so that you sink into the sorrow, but so that she, at last, can be seen, and so that you, the adult, can give her now the care that was missing then. Acknowledging what you lost is not dwelling on the past, it's honoring the price you paid, and it is, very often, the beginning of being able, finally, to set things down.

In the way I have learned to look at these stories, there is a loyalty here that needs to be seen. You did not hold up the family only out of responsibility. You held it up out of love. Out of an enormous, childlike love, that saw the suffering of its own people and decided, without deciding, that it would hold it up, that it would ease it, that it would not let the house fall. That love is moving. And it is also what binds you, still today, to a weight that no longer fits you.

Because what you learned, deep down, was an equation. To hold up is to love. If I hold up the family, I love the family. If I let go, it would be abandonment. And that equation, etched in childhood, governs you still. That's why you can't set things down. Setting down, for you, is not resting. It's betraying. It's abandoning the people you love. It's letting fall the house you swore, as a child, to hold up.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing those who raised you. Whoever left the void you filled, most of the time, didn't do it out of malice. They were sunk in their own pain, in their own incapacity, and had no way of being the pillar they should have been. They didn't mean to steal your childhood. But they stole it, without meaning to, by letting you fill a place that wasn't yours. And acknowledging this is not hating anyone. It's, finally, seeing what happened to you.

I want you to notice something that may be hard to admit, because it touches the pride you take in being the pillar. Your family, in many cases, organized itself around your availability. It got used to your being the one who held things up, and so it stopped developing other ways of standing on its own. Not out of malice, but because, when there's someone who holds things up, nobody else needs to. You became so reliable, so present, so capable, that your family came to count on you the way one counts on a law of nature, and your constant presence became the silent excuse for no one else to grow. Without meaning to, by holding everything up, you taught the others to hold nothing up. And today, when you think of setting things down, you feel the doubled weight of a family that, in fact, no longer knows how to stand without you, precisely because you never gave it room to learn.

Notice, too, a very particular loneliness, that you may know well. It's the loneliness of someone who holds everyone up and has no one to hold her. You are everyone's shoulder, and you have no shoulder of your own. You're the one who listens, who solves, who is there, and when it's you who needs, there is no one to turn to, because everyone sees you as the strong one, the one who doesn't need, the one who always endures. You built, around yourself, a world in which you are the source and never the destination, and then, on the bad nights, you feel a loneliness you don't understand, without seeing that it was you who, by becoming everyone's pillar, left yourself with no one to be a pillar for you.

## 3. Loyalty to suffering

There is something deeper here, and I want to name it carefully, because it is the heart of your bond. Your connection to your family is not only responsibility. It is loyalty to its suffering.

Notice. You don't just hold up the tasks, the crises, the practical problems. You carry the pain. The pain of your own people. The suffering of the family, old and present, weighs on you as though it were yours to resolve.

And there is, in this, a very particular form of love, and one very hard to see. It's the love of someone who does not allow herself to be well while her own people are unwell. Of someone who feels that to be happy, to prosper, to live lightly, while the family suffers, would be a betrayal, an abandonment, almost a cruelty.

That's why you carry. That's why you don't set things down. Because setting the weight down, living your own life, being happy, feels to you like leaving your own people alone in their pain. And your loyalty, your love, won't let you. You'd rather carry alongside them, suffer alongside them, stay bound to the family's pain, than move forward and leave them behind.

I want to show you what this loyalty costs you, because the price is enormously high.

It costs you your own life. While you hold up the family, you don't live your own. You postpone your dreams, your projects, your happiness, waiting for a moment when the family is well, when it no longer needs you, when you can, at last, set things down. And that moment never comes, because there is always a new crisis, a new problem, a new reason to keep holding things up.

It costs you your health. Because carrying an entire family, year after year, drains you. The body tires of a weight it never sets down. The mind tires of a vigilance that never stops. And many people who carry their families fall ill, not out of weakness, but out of the exhaustion of an effort that has no end.

It costs you your most intimate relationships, too. Because someone who carries her family of origin arrives at her other relationships already tired, already divided, already with her heart occupied. The partner, the children, the friends, receive what is left over from a person who spends herself supporting her own people. And often conflicts arise, resentments, distances, because the one at your side feels they compete, always, with a family that seems to come first. Carrying your family of origin costs not only you, it also costs the relationships you build, which live in the shadow of a weight that won't let them flourish.

It costs you, as well, the possibility of being simply a daughter, a sister, a member, and not the one responsible for everyone. There is a lightness in family relationships that you have never known: that of someone who is with her own people without having to manage them, to fix them, to save them. You never sat at your family's table as an equal among equals, you always sat as the one who keeps watch, who cares, who holds up. And you lost, with that, the simple pleasure of belonging without weight, of being with your own people without being their column, of being one of them instead of the one who carries them all.

And it costs you, deep down, joy. Because loyalty to suffering forbids you joy. How can you be joyful, if your own people suffer? How can you delight, if someone needs you? And so you live with a muffled guilt whenever you're happy, as though your happiness were stolen from your family's pain, as though you had no right to be well while they are unwell.

There is a hard truth here that needs to be said. Your pain does not heal their pain. Your shared suffering does not ease your family's suffering. Carrying alongside them does not help them carry less. It only multiplies the weight by one more person, you. And a family with one more person sinking is not lighter. It has one more person sinking.

Imagine someone drowning, and you throwing yourself into the water to save them, but with nothing to brace against for the two of you. What happens is not that you save them. It's that you both drown. To help someone who is drowning, you must, first, have solid ground yourself, keep yourself afloat, from where you can reach out a hand. And that is what your loyalty to suffering keeps you from doing: instead of holding yourself steady, so that from there you can help, you throw yourself in to sink with your own people, and so you don't save them, you only lose yourself with them. Keeping yourself steady is not abandoning them, it's the only position from which, in fact, anyone can help.

Notice a still more subtle loyalty, that you may never have seen. It's not only that you don't allow yourself to be well while your own people are unwell. It's that, deep down, prospering, being happy, having a light life, seems to you a way of separating from them, of rising above the family, of ceasing to be one of them. As though your happiness would exile you from the clan of suffering you belong to. And so you stay, voluntarily, in the weight, in the pain, in the effort, because that is where the family lives, and being there is your way of continuing to belong. Loyalty to suffering is, deep down, a loyalty to belonging: so that you stay one of them, you suffer like them, you carry like them, and you never let yourself go further than the shared pain allows.

But notice the reversal this hides. Those who truly love you, in your family, do not want you to suffer along with them. They want you free, light, happy, even if they themselves can't be. And the best thing you can do for a family that suffers is not to sink along with it. It's to be the one who shows that it is possible to set things down, to live, to be happy, and to open, by your example, a door through which, perhaps, one day, the others will pass too. Carrying alongside them doesn't pull them out of the pit. Your own climbing out, without abandoning them, may be what shows them there is a way out.

I want you to see, as well, a form of hidden pride within this loyalty, because it too binds you. There is, in being the pillar, a place of importance, of identity, of meaning. You are the strong one, the indispensable one, the one who holds everything up, and that, however much it weighs on you, gives you a clear place in the world, a reason for being. And setting things down means not only risking the family's collapse, but losing that place, that identity, that importance. Who will you be, if you are no longer the one who carries? And that question frightens, because it threatens not only your rest, but your sense of who you are. Acknowledging this pride is not an accusation, it's a tenderness: it shows how, even within the weight, you found a place, and how setting things down requires, too, finding a new place, one that is not about holding everything up.

And I want to tell you something about guilt, because it is going to be your greatest adversary in this crossing. The guilt you feel when you think of setting things down is not a sign that you're doing something wrong. It's a sign that you're challenging an old loyalty, etched deep. Guilt, here, does not measure the harm you do, it measures the strength of the bond you're trying to change. So don't take it as proof that you shouldn't set things down. Take it for what it is: the natural resistance of a lifelong loyalty, that will cry out louder the closer you get to freeing yourself. To cross through the guilt, without obeying it, is the heart of this crossing.

## 4. Who am I if I'm not the column

I'm going to leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it, even if it brings you vertigo.

Who are you if you stop being the backbone of the household?

Stay with it. Notice what it opens up. Perhaps an emptiness. Perhaps a terror.

For you, this question touches a deep fear, and it's worth understanding it. It's that you don't know who you are without the weight. Your entire identity was built on being the one who holds things up, the strong one, the pillar. Take that away from you, imagine yourself carrying nothing, and a terrifying question appears. Then what am I? What worth do I have, if not that of holding things up? What place do I occupy, if not that of the column?

And the shadow hiding here is not weakness. It's the fear of collapse. The terror that, if you let go, everything crumbles. The house falls. The family comes apart. And it will be your fault, because you were the one who let go.

That fear is so old, and so deep, that even exhausted you won't allow yourself to set things down. Because setting down, in your imagination, is not resting. It's bringing about the disaster you spent your life avoiding. It's letting fall what only you hold up. And you'd rather crush yourself under the weight than risk the collapse you fear.

Notice how this imprisons you. You're bound to a post you took up as a child, and which you never again left. Not because it's yours, but because you're afraid of what happens if you abandon it. You are the guardian of a house that perhaps no longer needs you as it once did, but you remain on sentry duty, because letting down your guard feels to you like risking everything.

There is also a fragility hidden within this strength of yours as a pillar. Because a pillar does not live. It supports. It is always in the service of the structure, never of its own life. And you, from being a pillar for so long, forgot that you are also a person. That you too have the right to be supported, to lean, to rest, to live. The pillar has no life of its own. And you, by becoming a pillar, slowly lost yours.

And there is a particular sorrow in discovering, at a certain point in life, that you spent so long holding up other people's lives that you forgot to live your own. That the years passed, taken up with solving, with caring, with supporting, and that your dreams, your desires, your life, were always left for later, for when the family was well, for a later that never came. I don't tell you this to sink you into that grief, but so that it becomes a reason, perhaps the strongest one, to set things down now, while there is life left to live. Because the years you have left deserve to be yours, and not just one more chapter of a whole life in the service of everyone but you.

I want to tell you something about the fear of collapse, because it begins to disarm it. The house is not going to fall just because you set things down. This is the belief that binds you, and it is, most of the time, false. You grew up believing you were the only thing between the family and disaster. But that was the perception of a child, terrified, who felt the weight of the world on her shoulders. The adult reality is different. Families are more resilient than they seem. People, even those who seem to depend on you, have more resources than you credit them with. And often, when you finally set things down, you discover that the house doesn't fall, that the others manage, that what you carried alone was, after all, possible to share, or to bear, or even to do without.

And I want you to distinguish something important: one thing is your real responsibility, another is the responsibility the wound makes you feel. The child you were felt responsible for everything, because for a child, in the midst of chaos, feeling responsible is, paradoxically, more bearable than feeling powerless. If it's my job to hold things up, then I can do something; if it isn't, then I am at the mercy of it all. And so you took on a total responsibility, which gave you an illusion of control over a world that terrified you. But that total responsibility was a child's illusion, not a truth. You were not, and are not, responsible for the lives, the choices, the pain of the adults in your family. They were, and are, responsible for themselves. And freeing yourself from the weight begins with seeing that much of what you carry was never, in fact, your responsibility, it was a load that a frightened child took on so as not to feel powerless.

Don't answer the question of who you are without the weight just yet. It isn't answered all at once. But leave it open. Because it is in that question that your liberation lies. On the day you discover that there is, beneath the pillar, a person, with the right to a life of her own, you stop needing to carry everything in order to have a place. And then, only then, can you begin to set things down.

## 5. The house existed before me

If you carry the family out of an old loyalty, then there is a truth that needs to reach you, and that changes everything. The house existed before you, and it will go on existing after you.

Read this slowly, because it contradicts the perception that governs you. You feel yourself to be the center that holds everything up. Without you, nothing stands. You are the column without which the house falls. But this, however real it may seem, is not true.

Your family existed before you were born. It had its pains, its problems, its way of standing on its own, or of not standing, long before you arrived to hold it up. And it will go on existing, one way or another, after you. You are not the foundation of the house. You arrived at a house that was already standing, or already falling, before you.

What happened was that, as a child, faced with a void, you took on a weight that wasn't yours. You took for yourself a responsibility that belonged to the adults. And you came to believe, because a child believes what she lives, that you were the one holding everything up, when in truth you were carrying something that was never yours to carry.

I want to show you a distinction that frees you. There is the system, and there is your place within it. The system, your family, with its history, its pains, its destiny, is bigger than you, and does not depend on you to exist. Your place, the place that belongs to you, is that of a member, a daughter, a sister, and not that of a pillar, a savior, an adult to everyone. When you confuse your place with that of supporting the entire system, you take up a post too big for you, and you crush yourself beneath it.

Giving that post back is not abandoning the family. It's taking up, at last, the place that was yours, and letting the system take up its own. It's acknowledging that it is not your job to hold everything up, that there is a whole bigger than you, that existed before and will continue after, and that you, within that whole, are a person, and not a column.

In the way I have learned to read these stories, there is a movement that frees you, and I want to show it to you. It's looking at your own people, inwardly, and giving back to them, with respect, the weight you took on out of love. Saying, in silence, something like: this pain was yours, this house was yours to hold up, and I, as a child, carried it for you because I loved you and was afraid of seeing you fall. But it was not mine to carry. I give you back your life, your pain, your responsibility, with love, and I keep my own. There is an immense strength in this gesture, and no abandonment. Because giving back what was not yours is not the same as no longer loving. It is loving from the right place.

The awakening, when it comes, does not arrive like an explosion. It arrives like an almost physical relief. It's the moment when you feel, for an instant, that perhaps you don't have to hold everything up. That perhaps the house can stand without you crushing yourself beneath it. That perhaps you can exist, and love, without being the column. It's a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves a lifelong belief: that without you, everything falls.

I want to pause here for a moment, because there is a point at which this weight stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If carrying your family has led you to an exhaustion that is already making your body ill, to an anxiety that won't let go of you, or to a deep sadness of someone who never got to live her own life, that deserves to be cared for with professional support. And if, beneath your having taken up the place of an adult far too early, there are experiences of neglect, of severe instability, of abandonment, that still run through you today, then we are in the presence of a wound that does not undo itself just by reading. To say so is the most honest way of caring for you: the understanding in this book opens the door, but there are old weights, especially the ones that come from childhood, that are set down better with support, with someone trained to hold you while, slowly, you learn to lower your arms.

And I want to add a distinction that frees you, about what it means to give back. Giving the weight back to your family is not presenting them with an invoice, throwing in their faces everything you carried, turning them into the guilty ones. That would be trading a weight for a resentment, and resentment binds as much as the weight. Giving back is an inner gesture, quiet, of respect: acknowledging that what you carried was not yours to carry, and slowly ceasing to hold it up, without war, without accusation, only with the firmness of someone reclaiming her place. You don't need to convince your family of anything, nor to wait for them to acknowledge what you did for them. It's enough that you, inwardly, stop taking on what isn't yours. The giving back is done in you first, and only afterward, if it makes sense, in the world.

## 6. Loving is not holding up

There is a confusion at the center of everything, and I want to undo it clearly, because it is from this that your weight comes. You confused loving with holding up.

For you, loving your family means holding it up. Solving its problems, carrying its pains, always being available, never failing. And holding up became your only way of loving, to the point where you no longer know how to love without carrying.

But they are different things. Very different.

Holding up is carrying the other's weight. It's doing for them what they should do for themselves. It's taking on the responsibility for their life. It's holding them up, so that they don't fall.

Loving is something else. It's wanting what is good for the other. It's being present, yes, but without substituting yourself for them. It's accompanying, without carrying. It's loving the person, and respecting that their life, their responsibility, their weight, are theirs.

And there is an enormous difference in the consequences. Holding someone up keeps them dependent, infantilized, unable to grow, because you do for them what they would learn to do. Loving someone, without holding them up, lets them grow, become capable, live their own life, with their own weight. The love that holds up imprisons both. The love that accompanies frees.

Notice what your holding up does to your family. By carrying everything, you keep the others from carrying what is theirs. By solving everything, you keep them from learning to solve. By being the column, you keep them leaning on you, without developing their own column. Your devotion, without your knowing it, keeps your family dependent on you, and you bound to it, in a tie that looks like love but that is, deep down, a web imprisoning everyone.

I want you to see this, because it's freeing. When you stop holding up, you're not ceasing to love. You're loving in a better way. A way that respects the others as capable adults, and not as dependents who need you to survive. A way that lets them grow, instead of keeping them small. A way that frees both, instead of imprisoning both.

And when you stop holding up, you discover something surprising. That perhaps the others are more capable than you thought. That what you carried alone, they can, after all, hold up, when you leave them room. That your family, without you carrying it, doesn't collapse, it finds other ways to stand on its own. And that your holding up, however well-intentioned, was perhaps keeping them small more than it was helping.

I want to warn you about something, so that it neither frightens you nor makes you draw back. When you begin to set things down, it's likely the family will resist. Not out of malice, but because it got used to your being the one who carried, and change is unsettling. There may be complaints, pressures, attempts to hand the weight back to you, sentences that make you feel selfish, absent, a bad daughter or a bad sister. That is not a sign that you're doing wrong. It's the natural reaction of a system reorganizing itself, and that will test whether the change is really for real. If you give in to that pressure, you go back to the post; if you cross through it, with firmness and without war, the family, slowly, finds a new balance, in which you are not the one holding everything up. The resistance is part of it, and it is not proof that you were wrong, it is proof of how much needed to change.

And I want you to see that, by setting things down, you're not harming your own people, even if guilt tells you otherwise. You're, deep down, doing them a good that your holding up denied them: that of discovering their own strength. A person who is always propped up never discovers what she's capable of. By ceasing to carry your own people, you give them the opportunity, that your devotion took from them, to grow, to stand on their own, to find within themselves the resources they delegated to you. Setting things down is not only a care for yourself. It is, paradoxically, a care for them, that of treating them as capable adults, and not as eternal dependents on your strength.

I want you to see, too, the difference between a child who grows up propped up and one who grows up propping others up. The first learns that the world supports her, that she can lean, that she has the right to be cared for, and she carries that confidence into life. The second, you, learned the opposite: that the world leans on her, that she can't lean on anyone, that her place is to give and never to receive. And that reversal left you with a deep difficulty in letting yourself be cared for, in asking, in receiving, because receiving was never safe, nor even permitted. Part of this crossing is, therefore, not only setting down the weight of others, but learning, slowly, to let yourself be supported too, to receive the care you always gave and never received.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Love, but don't carry. Accompany, but don't hold up. Be present, but leave to each one the weight that is theirs. And discover, slowly, that the love that doesn't carry is lighter, freer, and, in the end, more love, than the one that crushes itself holding everything up.

## 7. Setting down without abandoning

There is a verb for this crossing. To set down.

To set the weight down. Not to throw it to the ground, not to run from it, not to cut off anyone. To set it down. With care, with love, but to set it down. To stop carrying on your shoulders what isn't yours to carry.

And I want to be very clear, because your fear is going to distort this. Setting down is not abandoning.

Abandoning would be turning your back, disappearing, ceasing to care, cutting off your family. That is not it. Setting down is something else. It's continuing to love, to be present, to care, but without carrying everyone's weight on your shoulders. It's ceasing to be the column without ceasing to be family.

Notice the difference. The column cannot leave, because the house falls. The member of the family can live her own life, and be present, and love, without being the structure everything depends on. Setting things down is moving from column to person. From pillar to member. From the one who holds everything up to someone who loves, simply, without the weight.

It's going to cost you. You're going to feel, each time you set something down, the old guilt, the old fear of collapse, the old loyalty to suffering telling you that you're abandoning your own people. And you're going to want to pick the weight back up, to solve, to hold up, so that the guilt will pass.

But it's precisely there, in that setting down endured despite the guilt, that your liberation lies. Because each time you set something down, and the house doesn't fall, you learn a new truth. That you weren't the only thing holding it up. That the others are more capable than you thought. That setting down is not abandoning, it's trusting. And that you have the right to a life that is not only about holding up other people's lives.

And I want to teach you to set things down little by little, because setting everything down all at once would be impossible, and even irresponsible. Begin with the lightest, with what clearly is not yours: a crisis belonging to an adult capable of solving it, a problem that isn't yours, a request you can refuse without anyone being put at real risk. Leave those weights, first, and observe that the house stands. As you gain confidence, and as you see that setting things down does not bring disaster, you become able to set down bigger weights. Don't demand of yourself that you let go of everything right away, which would be putting the old perfectionism in the service of setting things down. Demand of yourself only to take the first step, and to trust that each weight set down without catastrophe makes the next one less frightening.

And distinguish, as you set things down, what is legitimate care from what is too much holding up. There are things that make sense for you to do for your own people, out of love, out of choice, and that bring you joy, and those you don't have to set down. And there is the compulsive carrying, the solving of everything, the not being able to be missing, the crushing of yourself under the weight of everyone, and it is that which is destroying you. Setting things down is not ceasing to do anything for the family. It's ceasing to do everything, to be the one responsible for everyone, to carry what is each person's to carry. The difference lies in choosing, from the right place, what you do out of love, instead of carrying everything out of terror that, if you don't, the house will fall.

I want to speak to you about what opens up when you set things down. For the first time, space opens up for you. For your dreams, which you postponed waiting for the family to no longer need you. For your joy, which you forbade yourself while your own people suffered. For your own life, which was left suspended while you held up everyone else's. Setting things down doesn't take anything essential from you. It gives you back your life.

And there also opens up, paradoxically, a better way of loving your family. Because when you stop carrying them, you can be with them in another way. Lighter, more present, less resentful. Without the weight of holding up contaminating the love. You can love them as people, and not as burdens. And they can love you as a person, and not as a pillar. The relationship breathes, freed from the weight that was crushing it.

There is a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of living your own life without guilt. Of being happy without feeling that you're stealing happiness from your family's pain. Of setting down the weight and discovering that there still exists, there, a person, with dreams, with desires, with a life left to live. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day you set things down, for the first time, and discover that the house, after all, didn't fall.

And I want to leave you something, about the ones who watch you, especially if you have children. Because this place is passed on, from generation to generation, as it perhaps reached you. If your children see you carry everything, sink, live for the family without ever living for yourself, they learn that this is how one loves, and they risk becoming, themselves too, the pillars who crush themselves. The most freeing thing you can do for them is not to give them the example of the one who carries everything. It's to show them a woman who loves and also lives, who cares and also sets things down, who belongs to a family without sacrificing herself for it. Sometimes a woman learns to set things down, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to pass on to those she loves the weight that was passed on to her.

And I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more task to be done to perfection. It's going to be slow, and it's going to have setbacks. Days when you set things down, and days when you pick everything up again, in a panic, as always. And the old guilt will use each setback to tell you, you see, your place is to hold things up. Don't believe it. The setbacks are part of it. No one lowers all at once the arms she raised as a child. She lowers them little by little, one weight at a time, and each time you set things down and the house doesn't fall, a new piece of proof remains, and it's from those proofs, added together, that the confidence that you can live, slowly, is built.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, set down a lifelong weight. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your exhaustion with love, and your weight with duty.

Now you know that you carry the family, and that this is not your strength, it's a place you took up far too early, out of love and out of fear, and which you never again left.

You know that you became the substitute pillar of a system where someone was missing, and that you paid with your childhood for a place that was never yours.

You know that your bond is a loyalty to the family's suffering, a loyalty to belonging, and that your pain does not heal their pain, it only multiplies the weight.

You know who you are without being the column, or you know that this question gives you vertigo, and that beneath it lies the fear of collapse.

You know that the house existed before you and will continue after, and that you can give back, with respect and without war, the weight you took on out of love, and that was never yours to carry.

You know that loving is not holding up, and that the verb is to set down, without abandoning, slowly, one weight at a time.

And you know that, on the other side of this fear, there is a person, beneath the pillar, with dreams, with desire, with a life left to live, waiting for you, at last, to lower your arms.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to keep it.

Imagine that you walked in, still a child, small, beneath a roof that threatened to fall, and that you raised your frail arms to hold it up. And that you stayed like that, arms raised, holding up the roof, for years, decades, never able to lower your arms, never able to live, because lowering your arms, you thought, was letting the roof fall on everyone.

The crossing is discovering that the roof, after all, has other columns. That it didn't depend only on your child's arms. And that you can, slowly, with care, lower your arms, and see that the roof stands, and that you can, at last, step out from under it and live, instead of spending your whole life with your arms raised holding up what was never yours to hold up.

And perhaps you'll discover, as you lower your arms, that they are numb, sore, from so many years raised, and that it will be hard, at first, to use them again for your own life, to embrace yourself, to reach for what is yours. That's natural. Arms that only ever knew how to hold up the weight of others have to relearn how to serve you. Give them time, and tenderness, because they too, like you, carried far too early, and they deserve, now, to rest, and then to learn, slowly, to embrace your life instead of other people's.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds up this book. I can set down without abandoning. Setting down is not the same as no longer loving, it's loving from the right place, at last.

Setting down was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by lowering, slowly, your arms. By giving back, with love, the weight that wasn't yours. By discovering, weight by weight, that the house stands without you crushing yourself beneath it, and that you still belong to it without having to hold it up.

And there is a place to make that deeper crossing, with another or in a group, with people to accompany you, because giving back a lifelong weight is rarely done alone. That is the work that continues beyond this book. The book shows you the weight. The deep crossing is done with hands that help you set it down.

And before you close these pages, I ask one thing of you only. A hard thing, for you.

This week, leave one thing unheld. A family crisis that isn't yours to solve. A problem belonging to an adult who can solve it alone. A weight you usually pick up without thinking. Don't pick it up. And observe two things. What happens, which almost certainly is not the disaster you fear. And what you feel, the guilt, the fear, the urge to rush in and hold it up. Stay with that, without picking up the weight. Because it is in that setting down, endured despite the guilt, that your liberation begins, and that you discover, at last, that loving was never carrying everything, and that you have, you too, the right to a life that is yours.

And if, in doing so, you feel that you're abandoning your own people, that you're being selfish, that you're failing the people you love, remember: that thought is the old loyalty, not the truth. The truth is that you're giving back, with love, what was never yours to carry, and opening, for you and perhaps for them, the possibility of a life that is not only weight. Setting down is not abandoning. It's loving from the right place, at last.

Because you, who held up so much and so early, deserve, now, to be held up, and deserve a life that is yours.

Because the house, after all, has other columns, and you deserve, at last, to step out from under the roof and live.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
