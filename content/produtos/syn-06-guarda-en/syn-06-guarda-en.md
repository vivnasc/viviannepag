# The woman who never lowers her guard

**For you, who love with your armor on, always ready to defend yourself. Because there is a way of trusting that doesn't leave you defenseless, and you can trust without losing yourself.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a way of loving with a permanent guard, that never lets the other truly come in, and there is a point where that defense stops protecting and begins to isolate. If you recognize in yourself an inability to trust anyone, a vigilance that exhausts you, or old wounds of betrayal that still tighten you today, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole path.*

---

## 1. Always ready to defend myself

Notice something, with honesty.

You love with your guard up.

Even when you love, there is a part of you that never relaxes. That observes. That assesses. That is ready, at any moment, to defend itself, pull back, protect itself.

You don't give yourself over entirely. You always keep a part on the outside, an exit, a reserve. Like someone who enters a house without taking off her coat, ready to leave if she has to.

Look at your relationships. There is always a point up to which you let people reach, and no further. An intimacy you allow, and one you don't. A wall, somewhere, that no one crosses.

And you control. Without realizing it. You test the other, to see if he's trustworthy. You assess the signs, looking for the betrayal you're sure is coming. You keep the helm, so as never to be at anyone's mercy.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not coldness. People find you, perhaps, closed, difficult, distant. It isn't that.

Beneath the guard, there is a woman who feels a great deal, who wants very much to love, who has an enormous tenderness kept inside. The guard is not an absence of feeling. It is the protection of feeling. You defend precisely what is most fragile in you.

And perhaps that is why you suffer from a contradiction you don't understand: you want, profoundly, to be loved, to be known, to be reached, and at the same time you defend yourself with everything against whoever comes close. You wish someone would cross the wall, and you build it ever higher. You dream of intimacy, and you become impenetrable. It isn't that you don't want love. It is that you are so afraid of the pain love can bring, that you defend the door through which it would have to come in. And so you remain, desiring from afar what you push away up close.

This book is not going to teach you to lower your guard before everyone, to trust blindly, to open yourself without discernment. That would be dangerous, and foolish.

I have come to show you something else. That your guard, which protects you from being hurt, also protects you from being loved. That the wall that doesn't let the pain in doesn't let the love in. And that there is a way of trusting that is not lowering your whole guard before just anyone, but opening it, slowly, before whoever deserves it.

I'll tell you the direction, so you can read in peace. You can trust without losing yourself. Lowering your guard is not becoming defenseless. It is choosing, with discernment, to let in whoever has earned coming in. And what keeps you in your armor is not strength. It is an old wound, that we are going to look at.

Perhaps your armor shows itself in small daily gestures.

In never showing those you love how much you care, for fear that it would make you vulnerable.

In testing the other, in creating little tests, in pulling back when he comes too close, only to confirm that you can't trust.

In feeling that, if you really lowered your guard, if you gave yourself over entirely, you would be at someone's mercy, and that no one is worthy of that risk.

In being in a relationship for years and the other still saying that he doesn't really know you, that there is always a part of you that is closed.

All of them are the same wall by another name. The guard. The armor you never take off, not even for those you love.

I want you to notice something important, before we go on. Your guard is not malice, nor hardness of heart. It is the opposite. Only someone who has, deep inside, something very precious and very fragile to protect builds a wall of this size. The wall is the measure of the tenderness you keep. The higher the guard, the larger the heart it hides. And that is why this book has not come to ask you to become harder, nor more open to everyone. It has come to help you protect that heart another way: not with a wall that closes everything, but with a discernment that chooses to whom to open it.

Because the problem with the wall is not protecting. It is protecting too much, indiscriminately, forever. A wall protects the city, but if it never opens its gates, the city dies on the inside, without trade, without visits, without new life. And your heart, behind your wall, is like that: protected, and withering for lack of everything that would only come in if the gates, sometimes, opened.

I want to show you how the guard shows itself in daily life, in things you may never have connected to an armor.

You test. Without realizing it, you place little tests in the path of whoever comes close. You see if he notices, if he insists, if he withstands your coldness, if he passes the exam you set without saying so. And when he fails a test he didn't even know he was taking, you say to yourself: I knew it, he wasn't trustworthy.

You control. You need to know where he is, what he thinks, where the relationship is going, because the unknown, for you, is danger. Uncertainty tightens you, and so you want to anticipate, foresee, control, so as never to be caught by surprise by a betrayal you're sure is coming.

You keep an exit. You never give yourself over entirely, you never let yourself depend to the point of not being able to leave. You always keep a reserve, a foot outside, an emotional plan B, so that, if he hurts you, it won't catch you unprepared, won't destroy you, because you were already halfway out the door.

And you observe. You are always reading the signs, looking for the proof that, after all, you couldn't trust. And as whoever looks finds, you always find something: a tone, a silence, a gesture, that confirms your distrust and justifies pulling back. Your vigilance doesn't look for reasons to trust. It looks for reasons to defend yourself, and it always finds them.

Notice what all of this has in common. It is protection. Each test, each control, each kept exit, each watched sign, serves to protect you from being hurt. And it does protect, in fact. But by protecting you from pain, it also protects you from love, because true love demands precisely the opposite of all of this: it demands that you stop testing, that you let go of control, that you close the exit, that you trust without proof. And that, your guard doesn't allow.

And there is a detail you may recognize, and that hurts. People tell you it's hard to get close to you. That there is always a wall. That they never know you fully. And you, on the inside, would like to be reached, you wished someone would cross the wall, but at the same time you defend it with everything, and so whoever comes close gets tired of beating against a wall, and leaves. And you confirm, once again, that you can't trust, without seeing that it was your guard, and not their lack, that pushed them away.

I want you to notice still something that may surprise you: the guard is silent. It doesn't shout, it doesn't knock on the door, it doesn't announce itself. It shows itself in millimeters. It is in the extra half second you take to answer when he asks you what you feel. It is in the joke you make to deflect a conversation that was getting serious. It is in your changing the subject when someone touches you too deep. It is in your saying "I'm fine" in a tone that warns, without words, not to insist. They are gestures so small, so automatic, that you yourself don't see them. But whoever loves you, that one sees. He feels a door closing slowly, without a sound, right in the middle of an intimacy. And he learns, with time, not to come near that door, because he knows it always closes.

And there is a moment, in particular, when the guard reveals itself: right after a good closeness. Notice this, because it is one of the clearest signs. There was a night when you laughed together, when you felt close, when you lowered, without meaning to, a little of the guard. And the next day you pulled back. You became cold, distant, busy, you invented a reason to move away. It wasn't by chance. It was the guard correcting a slip. Coming too close lit the old alarm, and your defense pulled you back behind the wall, to repair the breach. That is why your relationships go in fits and starts, one step forward and two back, and whoever loves you never understands why, whenever you are well, you disappear. The answer is that closeness, for you, is danger itself, and the guard never lets you stay inside it for long.

## 2. The guard you learned not to lower

Go back. To the years before the first stone of this wall was laid, long before any boyfriend.

The guard wasn't born with you. It was built. Brick by brick, in a time when lowering it cost you dearly.

Because no one builds a wall without reason. Walls are built where there was invasion. And yours was built where, somewhere, you lowered your guard and were hurt. Almost always, there is a founding betrayal. A moment, or a time, in which you trusted, gave yourself over, showed yourself, and were wounded in a way that taught you never again.

Ask yourself. When was it that trusting, in you, became dangerous?

Perhaps you trusted, as a child, in someone who should have protected you, and who betrayed you, hurt you, failed you in the deepest place. And you learned that to trust is to risk being wounded by the one who should most have cared for you.

Perhaps you showed your fragility, your open heart, and that was used against you. Ridiculed. Exposed. Despised. And you learned that showing what you feel is handing weapons to whoever can wound you.

Perhaps you saw, at home, what happened to whoever gave themselves over. A mother betrayed, someone destroyed for having loved too much, trusted too much, lowered her guard before someone who didn't deserve it. And you swore, without words, never to be like that. Never to be at anyone's mercy. Never to lower your guard as she lowered hers.

Notice what a person learns in any of these places. She learns that the world is not a safe place. That people hurt. That trusting is a weakness that gets paid for dearly. That the only possible defense is not to let anyone reach the center. And that lesson doesn't stay only in the head. It engraves itself in the body, in the shoulders that tense when someone comes too close, in the stomach that tightens before tenderness. It rises up as a wall.

And the wall saved you, in that time. It protected you from a pain that would have come back if you had trusted again before whoever had betrayed you. It was intelligent, it was necessary, it was perhaps what kept you on your feet. Don't despise it. It took care of you when no one else did.

But the armor, made for a time of danger, didn't come undone when the danger passed. It stayed on. It became your way of being in the world, and above all in love.

And today, as an adult, before men who may be trustworthy, before a love that might be safe, you remain in your armor, defending yourself from a danger that belongs to the past, and losing, in the present, the love the armor protects you from.

I want you to see this with gratitude and with clarity. With gratitude, because the guard protected you, and did well, in a time when the danger was real. And with clarity, because today it no longer protects you only from pain. It also protects you from love. And that protection, which was salvation, became a prison.

I want you to notice something you may never have noticed: the guard is not only with men, it is with everything.

With friends, there is always a part you don't show. With family, you keep what you feel. At work, no one really knows you. You are always, everywhere, with your coat on, ready to leave, with a reserve no one reaches.

That is why you can have many people around you and no one who knows you. Many relationships, and none deep. Because depth demands lowering the guard, and you don't lower it, with anyone, ever. And that loneliness in the midst of people is the mark of someone who lives in armor: surrounded, and alone.

Notice also something about what you show. It isn't that you show nothing. You show what is safe. The strength, the competence, the humor, the part of you that doesn't expose you. What you keep is the fragile: the fear, the tenderness, the need, how much you care. You show the armor, and you hide the person. And so whoever loves you, loves the armor, and never reaches the woman it protects.

And there is a specific fear you may recognize: the fear that, if you show how much you care, you lose your power. As if caring less kept you safe, and caring more put you at someone's mercy. That is why you feign indifference, you pull back when you feel too much, you hide your affection, so as never to give the other the proof that he could hurt you. But that game, which protects you, also prevents you from loving, because love demands precisely that you show how much you care, and you keep that as the greatest of secrets.

I want to show you a form of guard that is rarely seen as such: anticipation. Before a relationship begins, you have already imagined its end. You have already rehearsed, in your head, the scene in which he betrays you, leaves you, disappoints you. You call this realism, prudence, knowing how life goes. But it is guard. It is preparing yourself for a pain that hasn't come yet, and in doing so, living a little of it already. You rehearse the loss so that, when it comes, it won't catch you whole. Only this way you never come to live, completely, what is happening, because a part of you is always at the end, protecting itself from an outcome that may never come. You rob the present of its joy to pay, in advance, an insurance against a pain that may not even exist.

And there is another form, even more subtle: self-sufficiency. You trained yourself not to need anyone. You solve everything alone, you carry everything alone, and you take pride in it. But notice where that pride comes from. It comes from a child who learned that needing is dangerous, that asking is exposing yourself, that depending is handing someone the power to disappoint you. So you made not needing a virtue, when it is, deep down, a scar. The woman who needs no one is not free. She is someone who, one day, needed so much and was so failed, that she decided never to run that risk again.

I want you to see what this does to whoever loves you. He feels the wall, but doesn't understand it. He feels that you care, but sees you pretend you don't. He senses there is a whole woman there, but you only let him touch the armor. And, with time, either he gives up, tired of loving someone who doesn't let herself be loved, or he stays, but at a distance that never closes. In both cases, your guard wins: no one came in. But you lost, because no one came in.

## 3. The armor also locks from the inside

There is something about armor you need to see. It doesn't only lock from the outside. It locks from the inside.

Your guard keeps the pain out, it's true. But it keeps you trapped on the inside. Because a wall that doesn't let anyone in also doesn't let you out. You are left shut in with your tenderness, with your fear, with everything you keep, without ever being able to show it, share it, let it breathe. The armor protects what you have inside, and at the same time imprisons it, because it prevents it from coming out, from being seen, from being loved.

Notice what the armor costs you.

It costs you intimacy. Because intimacy is born of giving over, of showing oneself, of letting be seen what is most fragile. And you don't show. You keep. So true intimacy, the intimacy of being known fully, never happens, because there is always a part of you that no one reaches.

It costs you love. Because no one can truly love someone who doesn't let herself be seen. Your loves love what you show them, which is only a part. The whole woman, the one behind the guard, that one is never loved, because she is never shown.

And it costs you loneliness. The particular loneliness of someone who is always surrounded by walls. You can be in a relationship, beside someone, and feel profoundly alone, because between you and the other there is always the armor, and through metal one touches no one.

There is a cruel irony in this. You built the guard so as not to suffer. But the guard brings you a suffering of its own, that of loneliness, that of the love that never reaches the depths, that of living your whole life protected and never found. You traded the pain of the possible betrayal for the certain pain of isolation. So as not to run the risk of being hurt, you guaranteed that you would not be, truly, loved. And I don't know if it's the better bargain.

There is a cost that is almost never spoken of, and that I want to name: tiredness. To live on guard is full-time work. It is always reading the room, measuring people, calculating the risk, controlling what you show and what you hide. It is a muscle that never relaxes. And at night, when you should rest, your head keeps reviewing conversations, looking for what might have been a sign, building defenses for the next day. That vigilance consumes an immense energy, an energy that could be tenderness, creation, pleasure, life. But that is spent entirely guarding a door that no one, after all, is trying to break down.

And there is a cost in the body, worth seeing. The body of someone who lives in alert knows no true rest. The shoulders rise, the jaw locks, the breath grows short. You learned to be tense in a way so old that you already confuse it with your normal state. You don't remember what it is like to let go of all the weight, to let the body sink into someone's arms without a part of you calculating. That abandon, that letting go, is a language you forgot, or that perhaps you never learned. And your body pays, in tension, the price of a protection your mind decided on long ago.

Notice also something subtle. Your guard, often, produces exactly what you most fear. You fear being betrayed, abandoned, hurt. And your distrust, your coldness, your tests, your control, tire whoever loves you, push him away, make him give up. And when he leaves, you say you knew you couldn't trust. But it wasn't trust that failed. It was distrust that pushed away. The prophecy fulfilled itself because you, without knowing it, provoked it.

It is a mechanism, and it is important to see it. You expect the betrayal, and so you defend yourself. Your defense hurts the other, or pushes him away. And his distancing confirms, in your eyes, that you were right to defend yourself. And so the guard feeds itself: it creates the distance it then uses as proof that the distance was necessary. It is a perfect circle, and perfectly closed, and what it locks inside is you.

I want to give you a concrete example of how this happens, because by seeing it from the outside perhaps you'll recognize it in yourself. Imagine he is late, without warning. For a woman without a guard, it is a delay, and she wonders what happened. For you, it is proof. The heart races, the head fires off: he doesn't care, he's with someone else, I knew it. When he arrives, with a banal and true excuse, you are already armed, you already treat him with coldness, you already punish him for the crime he committed only in your head. And he, who arrived tired and happy to see you, finds a wall, doesn't understand why, and moves away, hurt. You see the distancing and think: see, I was right to distrust. But it wasn't the delay that created the distance. It was your reaction to the delay. You turned a nothing into a wound, and then used the wound as proof that you had to defend yourself. That is how the guard works: it fabricates the threats it then protects you from.

And notice how this robs you of the present. Even on good days, even when nothing is wrong, a part of you is always somewhere else, waiting. Waiting for the moment when the thing will go wrong, when his mask will fall, when you'll have to defend yourself. You are never, entirely, in the happiness in front of you, because a sentinel inside you never leaves her post. And so, even when life gives you love, you live, beside him, guarding yourself from him. You taste the present always with the flavor of future loss mixed in, and you never know it whole.

## 4. Who am I without the armor

I am going to leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it.

With the steel set down and the wall undone, who is the woman left standing there?

Stay with it. Notice the fear it brings, the vertigo of imagining yourself without the guard that defines you.

This question touches you in a deep place, because the armor isn't only something you wear. It fused, over the years, into the very shape of you.

You are the woman in armor, the unbreached one. The one who needs no one to hold her wall. The one who isn't shaken. The independent one, the controlled one, the one no one hurts. And that identity, built on the guard, is your way of feeling safe in the world. Take away the armor, imagine yourself without the guard, shown, vulnerable, at someone's mercy, and a terror appears. So who am I? What stands guard at the gate, if I lower the bridge myself? And if I collapse, and there is no one to hold me?

The shadow that hides here is precisely what the armor covers. The fragile woman. The one who feels, who needs, who can be hurt. The vulnerable one, whom you hid so deep that you almost no longer recognize her.

You are afraid of her. Of your own fragility. Because it was by being fragile, one day, that you were wounded. And you associated fragility with danger, and armed yourself against your own most tender part, as if it were a threat, when it is, deep down, the part of you most capable of loving.

Notice this loss. By arming yourself against your fragility, you lost access to the part of you that feels most, loves most, connects most. The armor didn't only close the door to others. It closed off your own access to your own heart, which was left locked inside, protected and alone.

I want you to see how far this goes, because it is deeper than it seems. It isn't only that you hide the fragility from others. It is that you hide it from yourself. There are feelings you don't even let yourself feel, because feeling them would already be a way of lowering your guard before yourself. The longing, you let it harden into coldness. The need, you turn it into contempt for whoever needs. The wish for a lap to rest on, you disguise it as independence. You became a stranger to your own tenderness, to the point of no longer knowing, sometimes, what you truly feel, because it has been so long since you let yourself reach there.

I want you to come close to her with care, to that fragile woman, because she isn't your enemy. She is the girl who trusted, one day, and was betrayed. It is she who cries, still today, deep down, behind all the steel. And you, instead of consoling her, locked her up, silenced her, were ashamed of her, because you blamed her for the wound: if she hadn't been so open, so trusting, so needy, we wouldn't have been hurt. But that is punishing the victim for the crime. She didn't betray you. She was betrayed. And what she needs from you, today, isn't one more prison, it is a lap. It is that someone, at last, tells her it wasn't her fault, that having trusted wasn't stupidity, and that she deserves, still, to be cared for. That someone can only be you. The reconciliation with your fragility begins there, on the day you stop treating it as the mistake that cost you dearly and begin to treat it as the part of you that suffered most.

And notice something you may never have thought of: beneath the woman who needs no one, there is one who needs immensely. Not less than others, more. The guard isn't born in someone who needs little. It is born in someone who needed so much, and was so failed, that the need became unbearable to feel. So you buried it. But it didn't die. It is there, alive, hungry, pushing against the steel, and sometimes it bursts out in a nameless longing, in tears with no reason, in an emptiness no achievement fills. That emptiness isn't weakness. It is your legitimate need to be loved, knocking at the door you yourself locked. And it doesn't fall silent until you hear it.

And there is a fragility hidden in your strength, that must be seen. A person who can't trust, who can't lower her guard, isn't strong. She is hostage to fear. True strength isn't the shell. It is the capacity to open yourself, knowing you can be hurt, and to risk it just the same. The armor isn't courage. It is fear dressed in steel. And living in armor isn't being strong, it is being so frightened that you never, ever, allow yourself to be exposed. True courage is that of the woman who, having been wounded, chooses to trust again, in whoever deserves it, knowing there are no guarantees. That courage, your guard never let you have.

And I want to tell you something that begins to disarm the fear. Today, there are those who can hold you. In the time when the armor was born, lowering your guard was dangerous, and your defense was just. But today, in your adult life, there may already be people who would catch you if you fell, firm hands that weren't there when you were a child. The danger of lowering the bridge and being betrayed was real then. Outside today's gate, that enemy may no longer be standing. And the only way to find out is to risk, slowly, with whoever deserves it, lowering the wall a little.

And there is something I want you to see about what fragility does to relationships. Contrary to what you fear, showing yourself fragile doesn't push people away. It draws them closer. People don't connect to our strength, they connect to our truth, and our truth includes fragility. Think of the people you most love: it isn't their perfection that moves you, it is the moment when you saw them tremble, hesitate, need. It was there that you truly loved them. Well, the same happens with you. When you allow yourself to be vulnerable before whoever is trustworthy, you don't become smaller in that person's eyes. You become real, close, lovable in a new way. The shell protects you from exposure, but it deprives you of intimacy. And perhaps the day will come when you discover that it was worth risking the exposure, because on the other side of it is precisely what you most lack: being loved whole, and not only in the strong half.

Don't answer this question right away. It isn't answered all at once, and the answer isn't given before just anyone. But leave it open. Because that question is the very gate in your wall. The day you come to know, and accept, the fragile woman you hid, you stop having to defend her with steel. And then, only then, you can begin to let in whoever deserves her.

## 5. When the guard is more than a pattern

Here I must lower my voice and halt the book at the wall. With seriousness.

There is a difference between a defensive guard and a wound that asks for help. And it is worth knowing which side of it you are on.

There is the side we have been talking about. You love with your armor on, you find it hard to trust, you defend yourself from love. It hurts, but it has a path, and part of that path is the understanding these pages bring you.

And there is another side, more serious. And I want to name it, without going around it.

If your distrust is so great that it prevents you from connecting to anyone at all, and leaves you profoundly alone.

If there is, at the origin of your guard, a grave betrayal, an abuse, a wound that still assaults you today, in thoughts, in nightmares, in a tightness that doesn't pass.

If watching, controlling, distrusting has become an anxiety that consumes you, that takes away your sleep, that dominates the relationship.

If you no longer distinguish who is trustworthy from who isn't, and treat everyone as a threat, even those who love you and never failed you.

This may be more than a pattern to understand. It may be the echo of a trauma, a wound of trust that asks for help. And serious help, from a trained professional who can stand watch with you while the wall comes down.

Wounds of betrayal, of abuse, of abandonment, engrave themselves deep, in the body, and don't come undone only by understanding. They need to be cared for, with time, with someone trained to hold you while, slowly, you learn again that the world, or at least a part of it, can be safe.

I want to tell you something about this, with tenderness. The relationship with whoever helps you can be, for you, the first place where you experience lowering your guard in safety. Notice the good strangeness it has: it is a relationship where the giving over comes first, and where the other person is trained, and committed, not to use against you what you show. Where you open yourself, and are not betrayed. Where you show your fragility, and it is kept, and not used as a weapon. And your body learns, there, in a safe relationship and slowly, that trusting, with the right person, doesn't always end in the wound of before. It is healing, in the very place where the wound opened.

And I want to add one thing, because it can make it easier. Seeking help doesn't oblige you to lower your whole guard on the first day. You can come in with your armor on, and say exactly that, that you find it hard to trust, that you are afraid to expose yourself. Whoever helps you well doesn't force your door. They wait, on the landing, for as long as it takes, and let you open, millimeter by millimeter, at your own pace. Distrust itself can be the first subject, instead of an obstacle. You don't have to arrive healed. You arrive as you are, with your guard up, and it is precisely from there that one begins.

If you recognize yourself in the signs I named, it is this chapter I want you to remember above the others. Seek support. It is not weakness. It is the most courageous way of caring for a wound you deserve, at last, to see heal.

I want to tell you still one cruel thing the guard does, and that has to do with confusing the past with the present. Not everyone is the person who betrayed you. The betrayal that marked you was by someone, in a moment, in a context. It isn't what everyone will do to you. But your guard doesn't distinguish. It treats the next man as if he were the one who hurt you. And it makes whoever did you no harm pay the debt of whoever wounded you. That isn't justice, nor protection. It is letting whoever betrayed you keep ruining your relationships, even today, years later, through the fear he left you.

I want you to see that forgiving the past, or at least letting it go, isn't for the benefit of whoever hurt you. It is for your benefit, and for that of whoever today would want to love you. As long as your guard serves the old betrayal, it is you who remains trapped in it, and it is the present that pays. To release the guard is, deep down, to stop giving whoever betrayed you the power to govern your life, long after he left it. It is taking from him, at last, the keys to your house.

## 6. To trust is not to be defenseless

If your guard protects you and isolates you at the same time, then there is a new truth waiting for you. Read it slowly.

To trust is not to be defenseless.

On your map, there are only two positions. Guard all the way up, and you are safe. Or guard all the way down, and you are at someone's mercy, defenseless, waiting to be wounded. And because the second terrifies you, you always choose the first, and you live armed.

But there is a third position, and it is the one you lack. Trust with discernment. Lowering your guard before whoever deserves it, and keeping it before whoever doesn't. It isn't all or nothing. It is choice.

Notice the difference. Distrust treats everyone as a threat, indiscriminately. Blind trust treats everyone as safe, indiscriminately. Both are blind, because neither distinguishes. Mature trust distinguishes. It looks at each person, observes over time, and decides, with judgment, to whom it opens and to whom it doesn't.

You think your distrust is discernment. It isn't. Discernment sees who is trustworthy and opens to that person. Distrust sees nothing, closes itself to all by reflex. It is the opposite of discernment, it is a defensive blindness that treats the good man and the dangerous man exactly the same way: outside the wall.

I'll say this another way. The permanent guard doesn't make you safer. It makes you less able to read whoever is in front of you. Because, busy defending yourself from everyone, you don't distinguish who would hurt you from who would love you. And you end up closing the door precisely to whoever would do you good, with the same steel with which you close it to whoever would do you harm.

I want to show you how to recognize, in practice, who is trustworthy, because discernment isn't a hunch, it is a patient reading. Notice not what the person promises, but what they do, over time. Whoever is trustworthy is coherent: what they say one day holds the next. They keep the small things, and that is why you can believe the big ones. When they err, they acknowledge it, instead of turning the blame against you. They respect a no of yours without punishment, without a tantrum, without making you pay for it. They grow curious about your fragility, and care for it, instead of using it. These are not signs you see on a date. You see them over months, in small tests that life makes on its own. And learning to read them is turning your vigilance, which always looked for threats, to begin to look, also, for proofs of safety.

And I want to warn you of a common mistake. You will want to test before trusting. You will want the guarantee, the absolute certainty that you won't be betrayed, before lowering your guard. But that guarantee doesn't exist. To trust is always to risk, because there is never certainty. And if you wait for the absolute certainty, you never trust, because it never comes. Courage isn't trusting when you have the certainty. It is trusting despite not having it, in whoever, from what you see, deserves it.

I'll show you how the guard is lowered, in practice, because it isn't in one stroke, nor before everyone. It is lowered slowly, in steps, and before whoever has shown, over time, that they are worthy of trust. It isn't a leap into the dark. It is a series of small risks, each one a little larger than the last, with someone who, at each step, proves to you that they are safe.

Begin with something small. Showing a minimal fragility to whoever is trustworthy. Saying that something hurt you, instead of feigning indifference. Asking, when you need to, instead of bearing it alone. Letting be seen how much you care, in a gesture, instead of hiding it. And observing what happens.

Almost certainly, with the right person, not the wound you expect, but a welcome. A care. A new closeness. And each time you lower your guard, a little, and are not hurt, you give your body a new proof: that lowering your guard, with this person, today, doesn't bring the betrayal of before. And it is with proofs, repeated, that the wall, slowly, loosens.

I want to leave you a distinction worth gold. There is a difference between lowering the guard and throwing the guard away. Lowering the guard is a reversible, gradual gesture, that you control: you open a little, you see what comes, and you decide whether to open more. Throwing the guard away is handing over everything at once, without a net, and that is what terrifies you, and rightly so. No one with good sense would do the second. But you, in your map of two positions, confuse the two, and so you refuse even the first. I want you to see that you can lower a centimeter and raise it again, if you have to. That the opening isn't a door that, once open, never closes again. It is a helm, that you hold. And it is because you hold it that you can, at last, dare to move it.

And I want you to see that your capacity to read people, which today you use to defend yourself, is, in truth, a gift. You sense others, you read the signs, you perceive who is genuine and who isn't. That gift, put at the service of fear, closes you to all. Put at the service of discernment, it helps you trust well, to choose whom to open to. The healing isn't losing your sharpened perception. It is turning it from fear toward wisdom, from looking for threats to recognizing who is safe.

## 7. To trust without losing myself

Beyond the wall, this crossing has a destination. And it fits in a sentence. To trust without losing myself.

Notice the two parts. To trust. And without losing myself.

The first is the courage to open the guard. The second is the guarantee that opening it doesn't mean disappearing, erasing yourself, giving over everything, being at someone's mercy. You can lower your guard and remain whole, with your judgment, with your boundaries, with your ground.

Because there is a fear, beneath your armor, that is greater than the fear of being hurt. It is the fear of losing yourself. That, if you trust, if you give yourself over, you'll give so much of yourself that you'll stop belonging to yourself, you'll end up in the other's hands, you'll lose your own center.

And perhaps you are right to have that fear, if your experience of lowering your guard was always that: giving yourself over so much that you lost yourself. But that isn't trusting. It is dissolving. And to trust with discernment is the opposite: it is opening yourself while remaining yours. It is showing yourself fragile without becoming hostage. It is giving your trust, but not your helm. It is lowering the guard of the heart without lowering your discernment, your values, your boundaries.

I want you to see why these two things, opening and losing yourself, seem to you the same. Almost certainly, at the origin, trusting and disappearing came together. Perhaps you loved someone who only wanted you if you were what he needed, and to be loved you had to erase yourself. Perhaps you learned that giving yourself over was obeying, molding yourself, ceasing to have your own shape. So your body kept an equation: love equals annihilation. And it is that equation, and not love itself, that your guard fights. You defend yourself, with reason, from a way of loving that demanded you disappear. Only you confused that way with all ways, and closed the door to a love that, that one, would want you whole, with shape, with voice, with boundaries of your own.

To trust without losing yourself is to let in without ceasing to exist. It is discovering that there is someone who wants you precisely for your contours, for your no when it is no, for your shape that doesn't yield. That mature love doesn't want you dissolved, it wants you present. And that showing your fragility to someone like that doesn't hand you over to him, because he doesn't want it to dominate you, he wants it to know you, and there is an abyss between the two things.

I want you to see that true safety isn't in the wall. It is in you. It is in knowing that, whatever happens, even if you are hurt, you have yourself. That you aren't going to disappear. That you will survive, as you survived before. That inner safety is what allows you to lower your guard, because you no longer depend on the wall to feel whole. Your ground is you, not the steel. And that is why you can risk opening yourself, because even if they hurt you, you don't lose yourself. You have yourself. You always had.

Notice the freedom this brings. If your ground is you, then you no longer need the other to be perfect, infallible, an absolute guarantee that he'll never do you harm. You can love someone human, who sometimes fails, because your safety doesn't depend on his never failing. It depends on your knowing that you can bear it, that you recover, that you go on being yourself if the thing goes wrong. And this changes everything, because it frees the other from an impossible demand, and frees you from an endless vigilance. You no longer have to inspect his every gesture looking for the proof that you'll have to defend yourself. You trust him a little, and you trust yourself a lot, and it is that lot that lets you, at last, rest.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, and perhaps you don't even remember that it exists. The joy of being, at last, known. Of someone seeing the whole woman, the fragile one included, and staying. Of no longer being alone behind walls. Of touching and being touched, without metal in between. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the risk, on the day you trust enough, in whoever deserves it, to lower, slowly, a lifetime's guard.

And the joy of resting. Because living on guard is living in permanent tension, always alert, always watching, always ready. It is exhausting. And lowering your guard, with whoever is safe, is being able, at last, to relax, to stop watching, to rest in someone's arms without expecting the knife. That rest, whoever lives in armor rarely knows it, and it is among the sweetest there is.

And there is a joy you may not even imagine, that of being helped. All your life you carried everything alone, because asking was risking a disappointment. But there is a particular sweetness in letting, once, someone carry along with you. In saying I can't, and hearing I'll help you. In setting down the weight, after so many years, and discovering that the ground didn't give way, that there were hands. You don't know, yet, how that feels, because you never let yourself experience it. But it is one of the most moving experiences of a life, that of not being alone in bearing it. And it is, also, waiting for you, on the other side of the guard you insist on not lowering.

And there is the simplest joy of all, and perhaps the deepest: that of being you, whole, before someone, and not having to manage anything. Not measuring your words, not hiding what you feel, not calculating what to show. Being simply present, with your highs and lows, with your tenderness and your fear, and being accepted like that. It is the exact opposite of the life you know, in which everything, in you, is watched and administered. And it is, perhaps, what you most deeply desire, without knowing how to name it: a place, with someone, where you can, at last, stop defending yourself. A place where the sentinel, after a whole life of vigil, can lower her arms, sit down, and rest.

When trusting stops meaning losing yourself, it stops being so terrifying. Because you no longer risk everything by opening yourself. You risk being hurt, yes, but not being destroyed, because you have yourself, whatever happens. And it is that inner safety, and not the wall, that allows you, at last, to let someone in.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of one simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't bring the whole wall down at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, lower a lifetime's guard. But it changes enough so you can stop confusing your armor with strength, and your isolation with safety.

Now you know that you love with your guard on, and that this isn't coldness, it is the protection of a tenderness you hide.

You know that the guard was built in a time when trusting cost you dearly, and that it remains on long after the danger passed.

You know that the armor locks from the inside, and that it protects you from pain at the price of isolating you from love, from rest, and from your own heart.

You know who you are without the armor, or at least you know that this question gives you vertigo, and that behind the steel is the fragile woman you hid and of whom you are afraid.

You know how to distinguish the defensive guard from the wound that asks for help, and to seek it if that is the case.

And you know that to trust is not to be defenseless, that the verb is to trust without losing yourself, and that your ground, after all, is you.

Let me hand you an image of walls and a bridge.

You live inside a castle with all the walls up, the bridge raised, the gates locked. Inside you are safe. No one comes in, no one hurts you. And also no one visits you, no one touches you, no one knows you. You are safe, and you are alone, and the two at the same time.

The crossing isn't to bring the walls down and be left exposed to any invader. It is to learn to lower the bridge. To open it to whoever comes in peace, and to raise it before whoever comes in war. To have walls, yes, but with a bridge that you control, and that you know how, at last, to lower to let in whoever has earned coming in.

Because a castle without a bridge isn't a fortress. It is a prison. And you deserve more than to live trapped in your own protection.

Notice, in this image, who stands on the bridge. It is you. It isn't the other who brings it down, nor force, nor invasion. It is you who decides to lower it, and it is you who can raise it again. That is the whole difference between the giving over you fear and the trust I propose to you. In the giving over you fear, someone takes the fortress from you. In mature trust, it is you, always you, who opens, with your own hand, in your own time, to whoever you chose. The bridge goes on being yours. And it is because it goes on being yours that you can, at last, dare to lower it.

The awakening, when it comes, is a risk lived. It is the time when, with someone who deserved it, you lower your guard a little. You show something you usually hide. You let a fragility be seen. And instead of the wound you expected, the opposite comes: he welcomes, cares, stays. And you feel, with astonishment, that lowering your guard, with the right person, didn't leave you defenseless. It left you, for the first time, found. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it contradicts, with the force of what you felt, the law that trusting always ends in pain.

And there is something that gratitude changes. When you stop fighting your guard as a defect, and begin to recognize it as a protection that was necessary, it itself begins to be able to loosen. A wall that is fought defends itself even more. A wall that is understood, and to which one gives thanks, can, slowly, be invited to open. You don't declare war on your armor. You thank it for what it did, and teach it, with patience, that the time of danger has passed, and that it can, in safety, rest.

I want to leave you still one thing about whoever watches you grow and love. There are those who learn, looking at you, what love is and what one can expect from it. If they see you on permanent guard, unable to trust, pushing away whoever comes close, they learn that love is dangerous, that defense is the only safety. And it is likely that they will put on, one day, the same armor. The most liberating thing you can do for whoever watches you is to show that one can trust again, after having been wounded. That the courage to love, despite the risk, is possible. Sometimes a woman lowers her guard, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn whoever she loves to the same protected loneliness.

You know, deep down, the phrase that holds together everything I wrote here. I can trust without losing myself.

Trusting was the first step. The second, that one, isn't made by reading. It is made by lowering, slowly, the bridge, before whoever deserves it. By risking the risk. And, if there is someone at your side, by learning, the two of you, to build the safety that makes the armor, at last, unnecessary.

That is the work of SyncHim. The book names. SyncHim crosses. Each in its own time.

And I leave you, to close, a small request. Difficult, for you.

This week, with someone in whom, from what you see, you can trust, show one small thing you usually hide. A fragility. A fear. A how much you care. Not everything, not all at once, not before just anyone. Only a little, before whoever deserves it. And notice what happens. Almost certainly, not the wound you expected, but a touch, a welcome, a new closeness. Because it is in that small lowering of the guard, with the right person, that you begin to learn, at last, that to trust doesn't leave you defenseless. It leaves you, finally, found.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
