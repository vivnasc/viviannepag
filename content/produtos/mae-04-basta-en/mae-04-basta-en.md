# To love and say enough

**For the mother who is afraid to set boundaries with an adult child who hurts her. Because understanding his pain does not oblige you to endure it.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. I want to tell you something right here, with all firmness and all care: if you are living with violence, threats, coercion, or abuse from your child, whether physical, financial, or psychological, this book is not enough, and it does not leave you alone. Seek professional support, and if you are in danger, seek safety support, through the services and helplines in your country. Understanding someone's pain never obliges you to remain exposed to what they do. If you feel that your sadness or your fear are too great to carry alone, please seek help. It is not weakness. It is the way.*

## 1. If I set boundaries, I stop being a mother

There's a sentence that may have held you captive for years, even if you've never said it that way, out loud. The sentence is this: if I set boundaries, I stop being a mother.

This sentence is one of the most effective prisons there is, because it uses your own love against you. The more you love, the tighter it grips you, because the idea of not being a good mother is, for you, perhaps the worst of all. And your child, consciously or not, has learned to use that sentence, or to make you feel it, whenever you're about to protect yourself. The mere fear of ceasing to be a good mother is enough, and you back down, you give in, you go back to enduring. The sentence works for you, on its own, without anyone having to say it, because it's been installed in your head for so long that it has become your own voice. One of this book's tasks is to help you realize that this voice is not the truth, it's an old learning, and that it can be unlearned.

You have an adult child who hurts you. I'm not talking about ordinary disagreements, those frictions that exist in any family. I'm talking about something that leaves you, after each encounter, smaller, more tired, more empty. Perhaps it's words that wound. Perhaps it's the way he demands, the way he holds things over you, the way he makes you feel in debt for things you never asked for. Perhaps it's the coldness that appears when you don't do what he wants, and the warmth that returns, calculated, when you give in again. Perhaps it's worse than that, and you know exactly what I'm talking about, and even here, as you read, you feel your chest tighten.

And even so, every time you think of setting a boundary, of saying no, of protecting yourself, this old and cruel certainty appears: if I do it, I stop being a good mother. If I refuse, he'll think I don't love him. If I draw away, what kind of mother am I? And you stay. You give in. You endure one more time. Because the guilt of setting the boundary seems greater to you than the pain of not setting it. And so the years pass, and you keep getting more worn down, and the relationship doesn't improve, and you don't understand why.

And there's a particular tiredness in this relationship, different from any other. It's the tiredness of someone who is always calculating. You calculate his mood before you call. You calculate whether today it's better to say yes to avoid the storm, or whether you still have the strength for the no. You calculate what to say, how to say it, when to say it, so as not to wake the part of him that hurts. You live in a state of constant vigilance within your own relationship with a child, and that vigilance wears you down in a way no one sees, because from the outside you look only like a devoted mother, always available, always helping. No one sees the price you pay on the inside, and you've learned not to show it, perhaps because you're ashamed, perhaps because you won't even let yourself see it in full.

And there's the loneliness of not being able to say it. How do you say, in a conversation between mothers, that you're afraid of a child? That a child hurts you, exploits you, leaves you exhausted? Society has a script for mothers of sick children, for mothers of children who have passed away, but it has no script at all for the mother whose adult child treats her badly. That mother stays silent, because she feels no one would understand, that they would judge her, that they would say she did something wrong for the child to be that way. And in the silence, the pain grows, and the guilt grows, and the idea that maybe the guilt really is hers grows too. I want to take that weight off you right now: the fact that your child treats you badly, he being an adult, is his responsibility, not yours.

I want to begin by telling you something that perhaps no one has told you. What you feel, that tightening, that tiredness, that sense of always being in the wrong with someone who treats you badly, is not a sign that you're a bad mother. It's a sign that you're being hurt, and that a part of you, beneath the guilt, knows it. The pain you feel is not proof that you failed. It's information. It's your own being telling you that something in this relationship is not right, and that you deserve to be treated differently.

And I want to tell you too, with all care, that this book is going to be different from the others you may have read about motherhood. I'm not going to ask you to have more patience. I'm not going to ask you to understand your child better, to try harder, to love more perfectly. You've already done all of that, and a great deal, and for too long. What I'm going to do is the opposite: I'm going to help you see that there's a point, and you may have crossed it long ago, where understanding more stops being love and becomes a way of erasing yourself.

There's something you need to hear before everything else, and it's the heart of this whole book: understanding someone does not oblige you to remain exposed to what they do. You can understand your child's pain, his story, his wounds, and at the same time decide that you do not accept being treated the way he treats you. The two things fit together. You don't have to choose between loving him and protecting yourself. And learning to hold both at the same time is, perhaps, the most important thing you can do, for yourself and, as strange as it may seem, also for him.

I'm going to ask you one thing throughout these pages: that you read with tenderness for yourself. That, whenever the voice appears that says "but I'm the mother, I have to endure," you stop for a moment and ask yourself whether you would say that to a friend in your situation. Because you deserve the same care you would give her. And perhaps, by the end of this book, you'll begin to believe that setting a boundary with someone who hurts you is not ceasing to be a mother. It's the way to keep being one without losing yourself entirely along the way.

## 2. When the love began to cost only me

There's something many mothers in your situation can't do, and it's the first step of everything: to name what's going on. Because when you love someone, and a child no less, the mind does an enormous amount of work not to see. It finds excuses, contexts, mitigating factors. "He's going through a hard time." "He didn't mean to." "I wasn't perfect either." And so, year after year, what's going on stays without a name, and what has no name can't be changed.

So let's, slowly and without rushing, begin to name. Not to condemn your child, but so that you can see clearly what you've been living in the fog.

And I want to ask you one thing before we continue: that while you read, you notice what your body tells you. The body doesn't lie the way the mind lies. The mind finds excuses, puts things in context, forgives before time. The body, though, tightens, shrinks, goes on alert, loses sleep, gains a weight in the chest. If there's a relationship that leaves your body like this, repeatedly, over years, your body is telling you a truth that your mind may not yet let itself hear. To name what's going on is, in part, to listen to the body again, after so many years of silencing it in the name of understanding.

There's guilt used as a tool. It's when your child, instead of asking, makes you feel bad if you don't give. When he turns each no of yours into proof that you're selfish, that you don't love him enough, that you owe him. Guilt, which should be your inner compass, becomes a lever in his hands, and each time he pulls it, you give in, not out of conviction, but to make it stop.

There's the threat, veiled or open. "If you don't help me, I don't know what I'll do." "Then it's better I disappear from your life." "You'll regret it." Phrases that set your heart racing, that force you to give in out of fear of what might happen if you don't. Fear becomes the permanent climate of the relationship, and you keep learning to anticipate it, to do everything to avoid it.

And there's conditional affection, perhaps the most confusing of all. It's when your child's tenderness appears and disappears depending on whether you do what he wants. When you give in, he's sweet, present, and you feel an enormous relief, almost a happiness. When you refuse, the coldness comes, the silence, the punishment. And your system, without your realizing it, learns a terrible lesson: to have your child's love, you have to submit. So affection, which should be free, becomes a bargaining chip, and you keep doing everything not to lose it.

There's also a more subtle form, and perhaps the most confusing of all: emotional blackmail disguised as suffering. Your child doesn't threaten you openly, he presents himself in suffering, and makes you feel that, if you don't do what he wants, that suffering will be your fault. "I don't know what's going to become of me." "You're the only one I have left." "After everything, you still do this to me." And you, who can't bear to see a child suffer, give in, not because he demanded it, but because you were led to feel responsible for his pain. It's one of the hardest forms to name, because it looks like love, it looks like concern, and in truth it's a way of keeping you giving without ever being able to refuse. His suffering may be real, and still it isn't your responsibility to solve it at the expense of yourself.

And notice something these dynamics have in common: they all leave you feeling that the problem is you. That if you suffer it's because you're too sensitive, that if you're hurt it's because you misinterpret, that if you refuse it's because you're a bad mother. The confusion sets in to such a degree that you come to doubt your own perception, to ask yourself whether you might be exaggerating, whether you might be the unfair one. This doubt is no accident, it's one of the deepest effects of these dynamics: they make you distrust yourself, and the more you distrust yourself, the more you depend on the version of the facts the other gives you. To trust your own perception again, what you feel, what you see, is one of the first steps out of the fog.

When you look at this as a whole, you begin to see the reversal that happened, slowly, over the years, without anyone announcing it: the love came to cost everything to you and nothing to him. You give, you give in, you sacrifice, you set yourself aside, you spend yourself, and he receives, demands, and gives in return only the minimum needed to keep you giving. The relationship, which should be an exchange, has become a one-way pipe, and you're the one feeding, exhausted, a flow that only goes in one direction.

I want to stop here and tell you something important, because I know what's going on in your head right now. Naming this is not turning your child into a monster, a villain, a bad person. It isn't that, and it would be unfair and simplistic. Your child may have his own pain, his own story, his own wounds, and probably does. We'll talk about that, and seriously, in the next chapter. But naming the behaviors that hurt you, seeing them for what they are, is not condemning the person he is. It's stopping pretending that you're not being wounded. And stopping pretending is the beginning of everything, because you can only change what you first manage, with courage, to look at head on.

## 3. Pain explains, but does not justify

We come to the heart of this book, and to the distinction that can change everything. Read this chapter slowly. If you need to, read it more than once.

Your child has, most likely, pain. Perhaps he carries a trauma, a depression, an anxiety, an emotional dependence, an old wound that never healed. Perhaps he has suffered things you know about, and perhaps things he never told you. And a part of you, the part that is a mother to the bone, clings to that pain of his as the explanation for everything. "He's like that because he suffered." "He hurts because he's hurt." "If I understood him better, if I loved him more, he'd change." And in that immense understanding, you find the reason to keep enduring.

I want to offer you a sentence that, for many mothers, is a liberation and an enormous relief. It's this: pain explains, but does not justify.

Let me open this sentence, because it contains everything. Your child's pain explains his behaviors. It helps to understand where they come from, why he reacts that way, what lies beneath. The understanding is real and has value, and you, who love him, do well to want to understand. But to explain is not the same as to justify. That a behavior has an origin does not mean it's acceptable, nor that you're obliged to endure it. The origin of someone's pain illuminates the behavior. It does not oblige you to be the place where that behavior is discharged.

I want you to notice something your compassion may never have let you see: you suffer too, and your pain matters as much as his. You've spent so long looking at your child's wound that you stopped counting your own. But you are a person, with a limit to how much you can endure, with a life that deserves to be lived and not only spent absorbing another's pain. Understanding your child's pain never obliged you to leave your own pain voiceless. And perhaps one of the most important things this book can give you is the permission to put your pain into the equation, to treat it as real, to stop sacrificing it always in the name of his. Both pains exist. His doesn't cancel yours. Yours doesn't cancel his. But yours, for years, has been the only one that doesn't count, and that's going to change.

Think about this clearly, because it's decisive. An adult, no matter how much he suffers, remains responsible for how he treats others. Pain does not remove his responsibility, the illness that prevents him from choosing removes it, and that's another thing, it's clinical matter, and in those cases the one who treats it is a professional, not the mother. But the general rule, the one that frees so many trapped mothers, is this: your child may have reasons for his pain, and still be responsible for not dumping it on you. To understand where what he does comes from does not turn you into the bin where he can throw it.

Let me give you an image that many mothers find useful. Imagine that someone, because they're in great physical pain, steps on your foot by accident. You understand the pain, you don't get angry, and even so you pull your foot away, because continuing to be stepped on helps neither that person nor you. Now imagine that this person, knowing they're stepping on you, keeps doing it on purpose because it gives them some relief. Their pain remains real. But your obligation is not to keep your foot under theirs. Understanding someone's pain never turned you into the floor where that pain can be discharged endlessly. This simple image contains almost everything: see the pain, and pull your foot away. The two things at the same time.

And I want to add an uncomfortable but freeing truth: continuing to accept what hurts you, in the name of his pain, very often does not help your child. On the contrary. A child who discharges his pain onto a mother who endures everything is never confronted with the need to take care of that pain in another way, to take it to those who can treat it, to take responsibility for it. Your infinite tolerance may be, without your wanting it, postponing the moment when he would have to face his own pain seriously. Sometimes, the boundary you set is not only protection for you, it's also the only mirror that can show your child that something needs to change in him. You don't set the boundary to cure him, that's not yours to do. But the boundary may be, for him, the beginning of a truth that your tolerance kept postponed.

And there's a trap here I want you not to fall into, and into which I myself will not fall in this book: that of diagnosing your child. You are not his clinician. It's not yours to discover whether he has this or that, nor to treat him, nor to cure him. If he has a pain that needs care, the one who cares is a professional, and the best love you can have for him, in that case, is to direct him there and not to try to be the treatment yourself. Carrying the role of an adult child's therapist is one of the surest ways to deplete yourself without helping him, because a mother's love, however great, does not replace what only professional care can give.

I want you to see, too, how this confusion between explaining and justifying is fed from the outside, not only by you. The whole culture likes to explain people's behavior by their wounds, and there's value in that, but there's a side effect: it begins to seem that whoever suffered has license to hurt, and that whoever is hurt by someone who suffers has an obligation to understand and endure. It's a trap. Understanding someone's wounds should increase compassion, not cancel responsibility. That your child has suffered does not transfer to you the obligation to be the place where he repeats the suffering. Both things are true: he deserves compassion for his pain, and you deserve not to be hurt because of it. Neither cancels the other.

Notice what this distinction gives you back. As long as you believed that understanding his pain obliged you to endure his behavior, you were trapped, with no way out, condemned to endure in the name of love. But the moment you separate the two things, the pain you understand and the behavior you don't accept, a new space opens. You can keep having compassion for your child's wound. And you can, at the same time, say that you don't accept being treated the way he treats you. You no longer have to choose between your heart and your dignity. And that, you'll see, is the key to everything that follows.

## 4. Compassion is not unlimited tolerance

There are two words that, in your life, have merged into one, and that confusion has cost you dearly. The words are compassion and tolerance. They seem like sisters, and they are, in fact, very different things. Separating them is the work of this chapter.

Compassion is seeing the other's pain and caring about it. It's keeping your heart open, it's not hardening, it's continuing to wish your child well even when he hurts you. Compassion is one of the most beautiful things there is, and you have a lot of it, perhaps even too much. Compassion looks at your child and says: I see your pain, and it matters to me.

Unlimited tolerance is something else. It's enduring everything, without limit, without end. It's letting his behavior continue, and continue, and continue, without ever saying enough. It's confusing loving with enduring, caring with submitting. Endless tolerance looks at your child and says: do what you want with me, I'll endure it, because I love you.

And here is what you need to see: you can have compassion without having unlimited tolerance. You can keep your heart open and, at the same time, close the door to a behavior that wounds you. The sentence that brings the two together, and that I want you to keep, is this: I see your pain, and I do not accept this way of treating me. Notice that both halves fit in the same sentence, without canceling each other out. The first is compassion. The second is a boundary. And a whole mother can say both at the same time, with the same voice, with the same love.

Let's look at what endless tolerance, contrary to what you believe, teaches your child. You always give in out of love, thinking that this way you help him, that this way you show him he can count on you no matter what. But what he learns, without either of you wanting it, is that he can treat you however he wants with no consequence. That you'll be there all the same. That a mother's love is a floor that endures any tread. And a child who learns this doesn't become better, he becomes worse, because he never finds the wall that would tell him where the limit of the acceptable lies. Your infinite tolerance, born of the purest love, ends up confirming for him that his behavior has no cost, and what has no cost tends to repeat itself and to grow.

And look at what that tolerance does to you. Each time you endure the unacceptable, you disappear a little more. Your self-esteem keeps wearing down, because a part of you registers, even if you silence it, that you're being treated in a way you don't deserve, and that you're allowing it. Resentment accumulates beneath the love, and resentment poisons. And there comes a point where you no longer know where you end and where the function of absorbing another person's pain and anger begins. Unlimited tolerance doesn't save the relationship. It kills you, slowly, and the relationship too.

There's a question that helps you know which side you're on, in each moment. It isn't "am I being understanding?", because you always are, too much even. The question is: in accepting this, what am I teaching, and what am I losing? If in accepting you're keeping your dignity and the relationship breathes, it's healthy compassion. If in accepting you're disappearing a little more, swallowing one more piece of yourself, confirming to the other that anything goes, then it's no longer compassion, it's tolerance that costs you your soul. The same answer, "never mind, I'll endure it," can be love in one case and self-destruction in the other, and only you, looking honestly at what it costs you, can tell them apart.

Notice too something about guilt, because it's going to appear the moment you think of a boundary. You'll feel guilt for refusing, and you'll read that guilt as proof that you're doing wrong. You're not. Guilt, in someone trained to endure everything, fires precisely when she begins to protect herself, because the old system reads self-protection as selfishness. Feeling guilt when setting a boundary does not mean the boundary is wrong. It means, almost always, that you're doing something new, something you were taught not to do. Guilt, here, is not a sign that you erred. It's the sound of the old habit protesting against your new freedom. With time, as you protect yourself and the world doesn't end, that guilt keeps going down, and in its place something else grows: respect for yourself.

So, in this book, I'm not asking you to have less compassion. I'm asking you to have the same compassion, intact, and to add to it something you always lacked: a boundary. Because compassion without a boundary wears you down to the end, and a boundary without compassion hardens you. The two together, and only the two together, are what allow you to love your child and remain whole. The heart open and the door with a lock. Tenderness for him and respect for yourself. It's possible to hold both things in the same hand. And learning to hold them is the way.

## 5. To say enough is not to stop loving

I'm going to let fall here the sentence that can break the chain that has held you for years. Read it slowly, and stay with it.

To say enough is not to stop loving.

Stay with this a moment. Feel what stirs.

Your whole life as a mother you believed the opposite. You believed that saying no, setting a boundary, refusing, drawing away, was the opposite of loving. That a mother who loves endures, welcomes, always forgives, is always available. And so, each time your body asked you to stop, to protect yourself, to say enough, your mind held you back with the same sentence: if you do that, you stop being a good mother. And you stayed. And you suffered. And the boundary, which was your salvation, stayed forbidden by a belief you never got to question.

I want to question it with you now. Where does the idea come from that to love is to have no boundaries? Look around you, look at the relationships you admire. Healthy love has boundaries. A good mother sets boundaries for a small child, and no one doubts that she loves him, because we know that the boundary is a form of care, of guidance, of protection. Why, when the child grows up, would the boundary become a lack of love? It doesn't. It remains care. The difference is that now the boundary also protects the mother, and that's what you were taught to think you had no right to do.

Think of a house with a door, and let this image settle in you. Love is the house: your child has a place there, always, in your heart, and that doesn't change. The door is the boundary: you decide when it opens, on what conditions, who comes in and how. Having a door doesn't mean not having a house. It means having a house that protects you, instead of a house exposed to every wind, where anyone comes in at any hour and does what they want to you. For years you lived in a house without a door, in the name of love, as if putting up a door were denying the house. But the door doesn't deny the house. It protects it. And a mother can have her heart entirely open to her child and, at the same time, a door that decides what enters her life. Love is unconditional. Access to you doesn't have to be.

Here is the awakening of this book, the thing that changes everything: understanding your child does not oblige you to remain exposed to him. You can love him deeply and, at the same time, decide that there are things you no longer accept. To say enough is not to close your heart. It's to close the door to a behavior, while keeping the heart open to the person. It's possible to say, with whole love: I love you, and I will not keep allowing you to treat me this way. This is not rejection. It's not abandonment. It's not cruelty. It's health. It's the health of someone who understood that you can love without letting yourself be destroyed.

I want to help you see where the belief comes from that setting a boundary with a child takes away your motherhood, because seeing it be born helps to let it go. For many women, this belief was inherited, passed down through lines of mothers and grandmothers who sacrificed themselves entirely and who were never allowed to refuse a child anything. You grew up watching women who gave everything and asked for nothing, who endured everything and said nothing, and you learned, without words, that this was what a mother did. Your difficulty in saying enough did not begin with you. It comes from before, from an idea of motherhood built on sacrifice without limit. And part of what you're doing now, in questioning it, is interrupting an inheritance, deciding that in you that line can change, that one can love a child without disappearing for him.

And there's a confusion at the root worth undoing: the confusion between being a mother and being endlessly available. You are your child's mother forever, that's not in question, it's not something a boundary can take from you. To be a mother is a bond, it's not a door always open to be wounded. You can be entirely a mother and, at the same time, unavailable for what hurts you. The two things don't touch. The boundary doesn't affect the fact that you're his mother, it only affects the conditions under which you're available, and having conditions is not ceasing to love, it's loving the way a whole person loves, and not the way someone who has erased herself loves.

And I need to be honest with you about one thing, because I don't want to sell you an illusion. Saying enough protects you. It doesn't guarantee that your child will change. Perhaps he will, perhaps your firmness opens in him a door that your tolerance kept closed. But perhaps not, at least not yet, and you have to be able to set the boundary even without the guarantee that he'll react well. Because the boundary is not a strategy to transform him. It's a protection for you. If he changes, it will be a gift. But you set the boundary for yourself, for your dignity, for your survival, and not on the condition that he reward you with the change you hope for. To expect the boundary to change the other is, deep down, one more way of putting your life in his hands.

There's another thing you need to know about saying enough, and that frees many mothers from the burden of doing it perfectly. To say enough doesn't have to be a grand gesture, a declaration, a dramatic rupture. It can be small, quiet, almost invisible. It can be no longer answering a call made only to dump anger on you. It can be not entering an argument you know only serves to diminish you. It can be leaving a place when the tone changes. It can be not giving an immediate answer to a demand, and giving yourself time. Each of these small enoughs is a way of protecting yourself, and together they keep building a boundary that, a few months ago, seemed impossible to you. You don't need courage for one big scene. You need firmness for a thousand small moments, and that firmness is trained, it grows, and one day you discover you already have it.

And I want to tell you something about what may appear inside you when you begin to say enough: anger may come up. Old anger, accumulated over years of tolerance, anger for everything you endured, for everything you swallowed, for all the times you stayed silent. That anger may frighten you, because you were taught to think that a good mother doesn't feel anger toward a child. But anger, here, is not malice, it's inner justice, it's the part of you that always knew you were being treated unfairly and that finally has room to speak. You don't have to act on it, you don't have to dump it on anyone. But you have the right to feel it, to recognize it, to let it tell you what it has to tell you. Anger, well listened to, is often the guardian of the boundary, the force that lets you keep the door closed when guilt wants to make you open it again.

I know this frightens. I know that the mere idea of saying enough to a child tightens your chest, because it goes against everything you learned. But I want you to know that this fear is not a sign that you're wrong. It's a sign of how much you were taught that your worth depended on enduring. You're unlearning one of the oldest and cruelest lessons you were given, that to love is to disappear. And unlearning that is hard, and it's courageous, and you, just by being here, reading this, have already begun.

## 6. The boundary as protection

If saying enough is not ceasing to love, then what, after all, is a boundary? It's worth looking closely, because the word frightens, and what frightens is usually misunderstood.

A boundary is not a punishment. You're not punishing your child when you say no, you're not making him pay, you're not giving him a lesson out of revenge for what he did to you. A boundary has nothing to do with retaliation. Someone who sets a boundary in order to punish remains trapped in the sick relationship, only on the other side of it. The healthy boundary is not born of anger, it's born of care. It's born of wishing yourself well enough not to keep accepting what hurts you.

A boundary is not rejection. When you say "I don't accept being spoken to that way," you're not rejecting your child, you're rejecting a behavior. The person keeps your love. What changes is that this love stops coming accompanied by unlimited availability to be wounded. There's an enormous difference between "I don't want you in my life" and "I don't want to be treated that way in my life," and it's in that difference that all the possibility of continuing to love without destroying yourself lives.

What a boundary truly is, is protection. Protection of yourself, of your peace, of your dignity, of the person you are and who has kept fading over the years of tolerance. And, however paradoxical it may seem, it's also protection of the only relationship that may still be possible between the two of you. Because a relationship in which one person spends herself entirely enduring the other is not a relationship, it's a slow destruction, and those don't survive, or they survive as ruins. Sometimes, the boundary is the only thing that can save any bond, because it creates the minimum conditions for respect, one day, to be possible.

I want to talk to you about the fear that usually holds back the boundary even when everything in you knows it's needed: the fear of losing him. You think that, if you set boundaries, your child will draw away, will cut you off, you'll lose him. And that fear is real, and it hurts, and it deserves to be looked at honestly. But ask yourself one thing: what do you have, right now, to lose? A relationship in which you're hurt, in which you're exploited, in which you disappear, is already a form of loss, only a silent one, in which you lose pieces of yourself instead of losing his presence. Sometimes, the choice is not between having your child and losing your child. It's between losing yourself, slowly, keeping a relationship that wounds you, or risking a distance that may, with time, open space for a healthier bond. There are no guarantees. But continuing to disappear guarantees nothing either, except your own erasure.

And there's a form of love you may never have considered, and that I want to offer you: loving from afar. There are children who, up close, can only hurt, at least in this phase of their lives. And there are mothers who discover that the only way to keep loving a child like that is at a certain distance, with a certain protection, without the unlimited access he used to wound. To love from afar is not to give up loving. It's to love in the only way that doesn't kill you. It's keeping the door of your heart ajar, while keeping the door of your house with the lock that protects you. And this, for some mothers, is not a defeat. It's the most mature and most courageous way to keep loving someone whom, up close, they can't have without losing themselves.

And there's something I want you to know about what you feel when you finally set a boundary, because no one warns you and it can confuse you. At first, setting a boundary may not bring relief, it may bring more anguish. You may feel terrible, cruel, you may want to go back and give in just to make the discomfort stop. This is normal, and it's not a sign that you erred. It's what happens when you do, for the first time, something that goes against a habit of a whole lifetime. The body finds it strange. The guilt screams. And often, from the other side, comes a reaction that tries to make you back down, more coldness, more drama, more demands, precisely because the boundary threatens a pattern that worked for the other. Resisting that pull back, in the early times, is the hardest part, and it's also where your freedom is decided. Each time you endure the discomfort of keeping a boundary, instead of undoing it to calm the other, you become a little more whole.

And the boundary is rarely a single conversation, a grand decisive moment. It's more a new posture, kept in a thousand small moments: the call you don't answer in that state, the request you say no to calmly, the subject you no longer enter into, the home where you no longer receive what hurts you. The boundary is built like this, gesture by gesture, and each gesture keeps teaching, you and the other, that there is now a border where before there was none. You don't need a final scene. You need a quiet firmness, repeated, that with time redraws the whole relationship.

And there's an inner sign that tells you whether the boundary you're setting is healthy or whether it's revenge in disguise, and it's worth knowing. The healthy boundary brings, in the end, a feeling of peace, of solid ground, of "I did what I had to do for myself," even if with sadness. Revenge brings something else: an agitation, a wish for the other to suffer, a satisfaction in his pain. If you notice that what you want is not to protect yourself but to punish him, to make him feel what he made you feel, then it's not yet a boundary, it's your pain wanting payback, and that, while human, doesn't free you, it binds you even more to the sick relationship. Don't judge yourself if you feel that wish, it's understandable after so much. But the boundary that frees is not born of it. It's born of care for yourself, not of the wish for harm to the other. One points toward your peace. The other keeps you trapped, now by anger instead of by guilt.

There's something I'm not going to do in this chapter, and I want to tell you why. I'm not going to give you a script of how to confront your child, exact phrases to say to him, steps for a decisive conversation. And I don't do it for a serious reason: because I don't know your specific situation, and in some situations, above all where there's the possibility of a violent reaction, a poorly prepared confrontation can increase your risk instead of decreasing it. Setting boundaries, when there's danger, is not a matter of courage to confront, it's a matter of safety to plan, and that's done with support, with someone who knows your case, not with general instructions from a book. What this book gives you is the permission and the understanding. The how, above all if there's risk, is done with accompaniment. And that's exactly what the next chapter is about, which is the most important of all.

## 7. First protect, then (perhaps) understand

This is the most important chapter of this book, and I want you to read it with all your attention, because it's here that your safety lives.

There's a line that changes everything, and that needs to be said without beating around the bush. On one side of that line are the difficult, hurtful, draining relationships, but where there's no danger to your integrity. On the other side is abuse and violence. And in the face of abuse and violence, everything I've said until now about understanding your child stops applying, and a single rule comes to hold: first protect.

I'm going to be very clear about what that line is, because many mothers live it without giving it a name. If your child physically assaults you, or threatens to. If he threatens you, or others, to get what he wants. If he takes money, possessions from you, controls your resources, leaves you without means. If he humiliates you systematically, isolates you from other people, makes you feel that you're crazy, that it's your fault, that you deserve it. If he intimidates you, if you live in fear inside your own home. This is not a difficult relationship. This is abuse. And abuse is not solved with more understanding, with more love, with more patience. Abuse is solved with protection and with help.

In the face of abuse, I will not ask you, in any line of this book, to seek to understand the pain of the one who does this to you. I will not ask you to think of his wound, his story, what led him to be this way. I will not ask you to look at your part, at what you might have done, at your shared responsibility, because in the face of abuse there is no shared responsibility on the victim's part, and whoever is abused did not provoke the abuse. All of that, which in other situations may have a place, in the face of violence goes completely off the stage. What remains is a single priority, and it's your safety. First protect. To understand, if ever, will be much later, and only if it's safe, and never before you are protected.

I want to say a word about the fear of reporting, or of seeking help, when it's a child. Many mothers, in the face of a child's abuse, feel that asking for help is a betrayal, is a handing over, is doing to the child what a mother should never do. And they stay, and they keep silent, and they protect the aggressor for being their child, even while he destroys them. I want you to think of this differently: seeking help is not betraying your child, it's interrupting a situation that harms both of you. For a child who abuses, it does no good either to keep abusing with nothing to stop it, because each time he does it with no consequence he sinks deeper into what he's doing. Asking for help, putting on a brake, may be, however paradoxical it may seem, the most loving thing you can still do for him: refusing to keep being the place where he becomes worse. Your protection may be, for him, the first real boundary anyone sets, and boundaries, even the ones that hurt, are sometimes the beginning of a change.

And protecting yourself is not something you have to do alone, nor should you. If you live with any of these situations, the most important step you can take is not a conversation with your child, it's seeking help from outside. A mental health professional, who can help you see clearly and recover your footing. The support services of your country, the helplines for victims, which exist precisely for situations like yours and where there are trained people to guide you safely. Someone you trust who knows what's going on, so that you're not alone with this. Seeking this help is not exaggeration, it's not betraying your child, it's not making a drama. It's what one does when one is in danger, and you deserve to do it with the same naturalness with which you'd seek help for any other threat to your life.

I want to tell you about a confusion that keeps many mothers trapped in situations of abuse for years, because knowing it can free you. It's the hope that love will cure him. The mother thinks: if I endure a little longer, if I love him enough, if I show him I won't give up on him, he'll change, he'll stop, he'll go back to being who he was. And that hope, born of the deepest love, is precisely what traps her, because it makes her endure the unbearable while waiting for a transformation that her sacrifice cannot produce. Abuse does not stop because the victim endures more. It usually does the opposite: the less consequence it has, the more it sets in. A mother's love, however great, is not treatment for someone who assaults, and to believe that it is keeps you exposed while you wait for a miracle that doesn't come by that route.

I also want to name something about shame, because it's one of the biggest prisons here. A mother who is mistreated by a child often feels a shame so great that she prefers to hide everything rather than ask for help. Shame that it will be known, shame at what they'll think of the child, shame at what they'll think of her, the mother who "raised that." That shame is unfair and it's dangerous, because it's exactly what keeps you silent, and silence is abuse's best ally. You have nothing to be ashamed of. You are not responsible for what an adult chooses to do, even if that adult is your child, even if you raised him. Asking for help does not expose you as a bad mother. It exposes the truth of a situation, and the truth, told to those who can protect you, is the beginning of the end of the danger.

I want to tell you something you may need to hear more than anything else in this book. It is not a lack of a mother's love to protect yourself from a child who harms you. It is not. A mother can love a child and, at the same time, recognize that near him she is in danger, and draw away to survive. That doesn't make you a bad mother. It makes you a person who chose to live. And your life, your safety, your integrity, are worth it. They're worth it for your own sake, not only for what you can still give to others. You matter, and your protection doesn't need to be justified by anything or anyone.

And I want to tell you why the order matters so much, why here you protect first and only understand afterward, contrary to what you may have learned your whole life. It's that, in the face of danger, stopping to understand the pain of the one who threatens you can cost you your safety. To understand requires that you lower your guard, that you open your heart, that you draw closer, and that, in the face of someone who harms you, leaves you more exposed, not less. So the order reverses: first you get yourself to safety, first you ensure your integrity, first you seek those who can protect you. Understanding, if it comes, comes much later, from a position of safety, and never as a condition for you to remain exposed. Whoever tells you to first understand the pain of the one who harms you, however well-intentioned, is putting the order backwards, and that backward order has kept many women in danger. First your life. The rest comes afterward, if it comes.

If none of this applies to you, if your relationship is difficult but not dangerous, then this chapter serves you as a map of the boundaries you should not cross in enduring. And if any of these lines applies, then I want this to be the chapter you remember: you are not alone, it is not your fault, and there is help, and you deserve to seek it today.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one thing: understanding changes things. And in this book, more than in any other, understanding can be the beginning of a different life, a safer one, a more whole one.

Now you know how to name what you live, instead of leaving it in the fog. You know that your child's pain explains, but does not justify, what he does. You know that compassion is not unlimited tolerance, and that you can say "I see your pain" and "I do not accept how you treat me" in the same breath. You know that to say enough is not to stop loving, and that the boundary is protection, not punishment. And you know, above all, that in the face of violence or abuse, the first thing, the only thing, is to protect, and to seek help.

And you know, perhaps for the first time, that your pain counts. That you are not only the mother who exists to absorb what the child doesn't know how to carry. That you have a right to a life in which you are treated with respect, and that claiming that right takes nothing away from your motherhood, it gives you back the dignity that motherhood should never have cost you.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book together: I can love you and say enough.

Understanding was the first step. But there's a second, and the second, in this book, is more than self-awareness. It's protection, and sometimes it's professional care, and it's a network around you.

And I want you to carry with you the coexistence that runs through this whole book, because it's in it that your freedom lives. I see your pain, and I do not accept this way of treating me. Repeat it until it stops sounding like a contradiction and begins to sound like the truth. Because it was the impossibility of holding these two things at the same time that trapped you for years: you thought you had to choose between compassion and protection, between being a good mother and being a whole person. You don't. You can keep both, in the same hand, with the same love. And the day you manage to say both things, and to keep them, is the day you stop losing yourself and begin to be able to love without disappearing.

There's a path of deeper, guided transformation that I created, which I call **FreeMe**, and that exists to help mothers set down what they carry and return to themselves. And I want to be honest with you, because honesty is the highest form of care: if what you live is a difficult but safe relationship, FreeMe can accompany you to find your firmness and your voice. But if what you live is abuse or violence, then the main path is not a journey of self-awareness, it's professional support and, if needed, safety support. In that case, FreeMe can come afterward, as part of rebuilding yourself, but first comes your protection, and that's done with those who are trained to protect you.

And I want to tell you one last thing about your child, because I know it lives at the bottom of everything you feel. Setting a boundary, protecting yourself, even drawing away, doesn't close the door forever, doesn't decree that he'll never change, doesn't erase the love. There are children who, confronted with a firm boundary, find, with time and often with help, a different way back, a healthier one. And there are others who don't, at least not now, and that's a pain you may have to learn to carry, the pain of loving someone who can't yet be loved up close without wounding. But in neither case is your protection the mistake. Keeping yourself whole is what leaves you available for a reconnection, if it one day becomes possible and safe. A destroyed mother has no way to receive a child who returns. A mother who protected herself does.

If you feel it's the moment to accompany yourself more deeply, and that it's safe to do so, you'll find FreeMe at [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com). And if what you need, before everything, is protection, then the most important step you can take today is to seek the professional help and the support services of your country. That is the first step, and the most courageous.

And if this book reached your hands and left you with more questions than answers about your specific situation, that is not a failing of yours nor of the book. It's a sign that your situation deserves a look made to your measure, and that a book cannot give, but a professional can. Taking what you felt in reading these pages to someone trained to listen to you, a psychologist, a support service, is not exaggeration, it's giving your pain the seriousness it deserves. You deserve to be heard in full, with your case, your child, your story, and not only to recognize yourself in a general portrait. This book is a door. To cross it, in your concrete life, may call for company, and seeking it is an act of strength, not of weakness.

I want to leave you with one last thing, which is perhaps the most important of the whole book. You deserve to be treated with respect, including, and above all, by those you say you love and by those who love you. True love does not demand that you erase yourself. It does not ask you to endure what hurts you. It does not charge your dignity as the price of admission. And if there is, in your life, a love that asks all of this of you, then it is not love that is lacking. It's you who have been paying a price that no true love would ever charge. And you can stop paying. You can love, and say enough. Today may be the day you begin.

With all my tenderness, and with all my firmness,
*Vivianne*
