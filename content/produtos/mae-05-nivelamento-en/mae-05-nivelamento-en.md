# The mother who wanted to unite

**For the mother who tries to keep her children the same. How the love that wants to unite can keep each child from following their own destiny.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. Before you begin, I want to tell you what this book is not. It does not come to teach you that everyone should fend for themselves, nor to treat mutual help between siblings as a mistake. Families love each other by helping each other, and that is beautiful. There are also children who do, in fact, need more support, because of illness, disability, or a real fragility, and there the family's solidarity is right and there is nothing to correct. This book speaks of something else, more subtle: of the moment when the love that wants to keep everyone the same begins, without meaning to, to keep each child from having their own life. If in reading this you feel a family suffering too great to carry alone, please seek support. It is not weakness. It is care.*

---

## 1. The balance that weighs downward

You didn't want to leave anyone behind. That was always your compass, from when they were little. That none should have more than the other, that none should feel less, that none should be left out. You divided everything into equal parts, your time, your lap, your attention, with an almost religious fairness. And when they grew up, you kept doing the same, because that was how you knew how to love: giving everyone the same, keeping everyone together, not letting life open between them distances that frightened you.

When they were small, this way of loving worked, and worked well. Children really do need to feel that they receive the same, that they're equally loved, that none is worth more. The fairness of equality, in childhood, is a form of security, and you did well to give it. The problem is that this way of loving, perfect for children who depend on you equally, was not made for adults who follow lives of their own. And nobody warned you that it had to change. You kept loving by the ruler of equality long after your children had stopped being equal, because it was the ruler you knew, the one that had served you so well. The difficulty is not that you loved badly. It's that you kept using, with different adults, the measure that was right for dependent children.

But children grow up, and life does with them what it does with everyone: it takes them down different paths. One studies more, another less. One prospers, another struggles. One marries, another stays alone. One goes far, another stays close. And those differences, which for you are almost unbearable, begin to appear, and you, without quite knowing how, keep trying to manage them, to balance them, to soften them, so that the family keeps looking like what you always wanted it to be: united, equal, with no one ahead and no one behind.

And this is where a pattern appears that you may never have seen clearly, because it disguises itself as fairness. When one child advances, instead of letting him advance freely, you feel a need to bring him back closer to the others. You call the one who prospered to take part in the problems of the one who struggles. You ask the one who has more to help the one who has less. You expect the one who went far not to draw away, not to show himself different, not to live his own life too much, so that the other doesn't feel left behind. And you call this balance. But notice something: this balance almost always works in the same direction. It doesn't pull the one who struggles upward. It asks the one who advances to slow down. It levels downward.

Perhaps you recognize this in small scenes, the size of family life. The good news of one child that, at the table, lasts only a moment, because someone soon changes the subject to another's problem. The gift you give to one and have to balance with an equal gift to the other, even when their lives no longer ask for the same thing. The visit in which you measure, without meaning to, how much time you spent with each one, so that none feels passed over. The conversation in which you avoid talking too much about one's achievements in front of the other, so as not to hurt. Each of these gestures is so small that it seems harmless, and taken one by one it even is. But together, repeated over years, they sketch a silent rule that everyone in the family has learned: here, no one may stand out too much.

And there's a hidden tiredness in all of this, that you may feel without naming it. Keeping the balance is exhausting work. You're always measuring, comparing, calculating, adjusting. Always attentive to who is well and who is unwell, to who needs more and who has to hold back. You carry, alone, the entire emotional accounting of the family, as if you were responsible for ensuring the scale never tips too far to any side. It's an enormous weight, and no one explicitly asked it of you, you took it on yourself, out of love, thinking that was what a good mother did. But no one can balance forever the lives of several adult people without depleting themselves, because those lives are not yours to balance.

You don't do it out of ill will, and I want this to be clear from the first page: you don't wake up thinking of holding any child back. You wake up thinking of not leaving anyone behind, which is one of the most beautiful things a mother's heart can want. The problem is not in your intention, which is pure love. The problem is in an effect no one showed you: that in trying for no one to be left behind, you may be, without meaning to, keeping someone from going forward. And that in trying for everyone to be equal, you may be asking each one to be less than he is, to fit the measure of all.

I want to tell you too that there's no villain here. Not you, who love and try to hold the family with both hands. Not the child who prospered, who isn't to blame for having gone down an easier or luckier path. Not the child who struggles, who didn't choose to struggle and who deserves all your care. Each one, in his way, is trying to serve love and belonging, and none is doing anything wrong. If throughout this book you feel I'm pointing at someone to blame, then I went off the path, and I ask you to come back to this page. Here there are no culprits. There's a love that learned to unite in a way that, now that the children are adults, no longer serves, and that has costs worth seeing.

And I want you to know, before we move on, that this book may reach you in a different way from the others. It's possible you didn't come looking for it, because the mother who levels rarely knows that she levels, rarely feels there's a problem of hers here, she sees only a family that loves and tries to unite. It's also possible you arrived from another side, from the side of a son or daughter, recognizing in your own story the weight of having been held back, or the weight of having always been the one to whom others had to give. Whatever door you came in through, I want you to know that here no one is looking for a culprit, neither on the mother's side, nor on the side of any child. What is sought is to understand a pattern of love that hurts without meaning to, so that it can, slowly, give way to another.

For now, I only ask you one thing: that you begin to look at your wish to keep everyone the same, and that you ask yourself, with tenderness, whether that wish, born of love, isn't sometimes asking some of your children to carry a weight that isn't theirs. Not to blame yourself. To begin to see. Because seeing is always the first step, and you, in opening this book, have already begun to take it.

## 2. The difference that looks like a threat

To understand what's going on, we have to go to the root, and the root is not control, it's fear. A very specific, very human fear, that you may never have named: the fear that difference separates. That, if one child goes too far, he stops belonging. That, if the lives become too distinct, the family, as you knew it, stops existing.

Notice that small differences don't bother you. One child likes one thing more, another something else, and it's all fine. What's hard, what tightens, is when the difference becomes big and visible. When one child prospers in an obvious way, gains autonomy, builds a life that no longer resembles his siblings'. That's when something inside you is activated, a kind of alarm, and you feel an urgency to bring him back closer, to rebalance, not to let the distance grow. It's not envy, it's not malice. It's fear. The fear that that child, in rising, draws away, and that in drawing away he loses himself from the family.

There's a sentence that lives beneath this fear, one almost never said out loud, but that organizes a great deal: if you go too far, you stop being ours. No one says it like that. But it's felt, in the air, in the reactions, in the silences. It's felt when a child tells of an achievement and the reaction is not pure joy, it's a joy with a shadow, with a "don't forget where you came from" stuck underneath. It's felt when one's success is received with less celebration than it would deserve, because celebrating it too much would be, somehow, leaving the others behind. The child who advances learns, without words, that to keep belonging he can't shine too much.

There's another way the difference frightens, more intimate and less spoken of: the fear that, if a child becomes very different from you, he'll stop understanding you, stop needing you, become someone from a world that's no longer yours. A mother who stayed in one place and sees a child go to another, with another language, other habits, another life, can feel a particular loneliness, that of no longer fitting entirely into her own child's world. And that loneliness, which is understandable and painful, can lead you, without meaning to, to pull him back toward common ground, to underline what keeps you the same, to devalue his new world a little, so as not to lose him. It's not malice, it's fear of distance. But the child feels that his new life is not welcome, and is torn between growing and belonging. To be able to love him in his world, even if that world is no longer yours, is one of the most generous ways to let him be free without losing him.

And the way you bring him back closer is rarely open control, the command, the prohibition. It's something much more subtle and much harder to see. It's responsibility. It's guilt. It's obligation. It's calling the child who prospered to always be present in the problems of those who struggle, to contribute, to solve, to carry. It's making him feel that, having more, he owes more, and that his good life is only legitimate if it's put at the service of balancing the others' lives. You don't need to forbid him to advance. It's enough to load him with responsibility for his siblings, and he, who loves you and loves his siblings, slows down on his own, stays, doesn't allow himself to go too far, so as not to betray the family.

And there's a painful detail in this fear: it usually comes disguised as virtue. You don't feel "I'm afraid he'll draw away," you feel "I'm keeping him humble," "I'm reminding him of his values," "I'm preventing him from acting superior." The fear dresses itself as a moral principle, and that's why it's so hard to see. It looks as if you're defending something good, humility, unity, loyalty to your origins, when underneath it's only the fear that difference separates. To see that, sometimes, what you call values is fear, is not an accusation against you, it's a liberation, because fear, once recognized, can be calmed, while a false principle only traps you more.

I want to stop here, once again, so as not to leave you alone with a reading that blames you. This fear that a child who rises will be lost from the family is profoundly human, and it has old roots. In many families, above all those that came from below, from sacrifice, from little, unity was literally what allowed them to survive. Keeping everyone together, everyone equal, everyone pulling for the same thing, was for generations a matter of survival, not a whim. You inherited that old wisdom, that strength lies in unity and that standing out is dangerous. It protected your family in hard times. What this book asks is not whether it was good, it was. It's whether, now that your children are adults and live in another time, it still serves, or whether it has begun to cost each one the freedom to have their own destiny.

## 3. The attempt at fairness that creates unfairness

We come to the mechanism, to the heart of how a love so well-intentioned ends up producing the opposite of what it seeks. And I want to cross it with you slowly, always from what you feel, not from what the others feel, because it's you that this book wants to see.

You look at your children and you see an inequality that hurts you. One has more, another has less. One is well, another struggles. And that difference won't leave you in peace, because your mother's heart wants everyone to be well, equally well. This is a pain of love, don't be mistaken about it. You can't bear to see one child suffer while another prospers, and you want to do something, you want to correct it, you want to balance it. Up to here, all of this is love.

And I want you to notice something about this pain of inequality: it is, in part, yours, and not your children's. Very often, the child who prospers doesn't feel in debt until you show it to him, and the child who struggles doesn't feel diminished by the difference until it's underlined for him. The comparison, the scale, the discomfort with difference, live first in you, and it's from you that they're passed to the children. This isn't to blame you, it's to give you back a power: if the pain of difference begins in you, then in you its healing can also begin. When you stop living the difference between your children as a problem to solve, they too stop living it that way. The peace you make, inside, with the fact that your children are different, is perhaps the greatest gift you can give them, because it frees them from a comparison that, deep down, was never theirs, it was yours.

The problem appears in the next step, and it's a step you take without meaning to, almost without noticing. To balance the difference, you'd have two options: to give more strength to the one who struggles, or to take freedom away from the one who advances. The first is hard, sometimes impossible, because a child's strength can't be transferred, it's not in your hands to give it, he has to find it on his own path. So, without a conscious decision, you end up doing the second, which is more within your reach: you slowly hold back the one who advances. You ask him not to shine so much, not to draw away, to share the weight, to stay connected to his siblings' problems. And so, trying to do justice, you create, without meaning to, a new injustice: you ask one child to pay for the difference he didn't create.

And I want you to understand why you choose, without knowing it, the option of holding back instead of strengthening. It's that holding back seems within your reach, and strengthening seems not to be. You can't give, with your hands, strength to the child who struggles, you can't solve his life, you can't make the crossing for him, and that leaves you powerless, and powerlessness is unbearable for a mother's heart. To hold back the one who advances, to ask him to share, to slow down, to help, that does seem to be in your hands, it gives you the feeling of doing something for the family's fairness. But it's a false solution, because it relieves your powerlessness without helping anyone: the one who struggles keeps struggling, and the one who advances takes on a guilt. The true path, that of supporting each one in his place without leveling, requires you to bear the powerlessness of not being able to equalize your children's lives, and that's one of the hardest things a mother is asked to do.

See the concrete portraits of this, without judging yourself in any of them. It's a child's success that's never openly celebrated at home, because celebrating it would make the others feel less, and so his achievement is received with a contained smile, almost with regret at showing it. It's the child who succeeded, called, time after time, to enter the problems of those who didn't succeed, to give money, to give time, to give solutions, as if his good life were a kind of common fund to which the others have a right. It's the family conversation that, when that child arrives full of his own news, always slips, quickly, to the others' difficulties, because giving too much space to what's going well seems, in the family's silent economy, a lack of solidarity.

In the systemic reading I study, there's an idea that helps to see this without guilt: that, in a family system, when someone tries to carry what belongs to another, the whole system goes out of alignment. Each person has their place and their destiny, and healthy love respects that order. A child's suffering belongs to him, it's part of his path, and however much it hurts, it's not yours to solve by transferring it onto another child's shoulders. When you take one's weight and put it on the other, you don't relieve the first, who keeps his pain, and you overload the second, who comes to carry something that isn't his. The system goes crooked, and everyone feels, without knowing why, that something is out of place. To give each one back what is his, his pain, his path, his life, is not coldness. It's restoring the order that lets love circulate without crushing anyone.

And here I need to tell you, with all clarity, that this doesn't make you an unfair mother. The unfairness I describe is not born of an unfair heart, it's born of a heart that loves two children at the same time and that knows no other way to hold both without one feeling less. You're not trying to harm the child who advances. You're trying to protect the one who struggles, and the only tool you have at hand ends up being to hold the other back. It's a love that stumbles, not a malice that acts. And seeing it like this, as a stumble of love and not as a defect of yours, is what will let you change it without sinking into guilt, because guilt would only trap you, and what you need here is not guilt, it's clarity.

There's one more thing I want to name with great care, because it's the true knot. The child who prospers, most of the time, doesn't want more money, more possessions, more recognition from the family. What he wants, and rarely gets, is to be received for his own story, without it being immediately mixed with the others' problems. He wants to be able to arrive and tell his joy and have his joy be only his for a moment, whole, without the shadow of those who don't have it. It's a simple and profound wish: to exist in his mother's eyes as a person with his own life, and not only as a piece in a balance he has to maintain. And when that's denied him, time after time, what he feels is not that he has too much. It's that his own life has no place.

And here's a truth that's hard, but that frees: what that child most wants from you is not to be relieved of the responsibility for his siblings, it's to be seen and celebrated by you. It's your joy in his life that he seeks, and that he rarely receives without reservation, because your joy always comes accompanied by the shadow of the others. When you finally manage to rejoice with him fully, you give him something worth more than any relief of tasks: the experience of being, for his mother, a pure joy, and not a problem of balance. That is the gift that leveling steals from him, and that blessing gives him back.

## 4. The debt that isn't his

Let's look now at what all of this does to the child who advances, not to accuse you, but because seeing the cost is what makes change possible. And I'm going to ask that, while you read, you always remember one thing: you never wanted this. You wanted to unite. What I'm going to describe is an effect that happened without your wanting it, and seeing it is not taking on a guilt, it's recovering the possibility of doing differently.

The child who prospers, within a family that levels, learns a lesson that accompanies him his whole life: I don't have permission to have my life while my sibling suffers. Notice the weight of this sentence. He doesn't learn that his life is his. He learns that his life is conditional on the others', that he can only be well to the extent that the others are well too, that every step he takes forward has to be paid for with guilt, or with responsibility for those who stay behind. His prosperity, instead of being a joy, becomes a debt. A debt he didn't contract, that no one formalized for him, but that he feels in every achievement, in every moment of happiness he doesn't allow himself to live in full.

I want you to see, too, what this does to the child who struggles, because leveling doesn't serve him either, however much it seems made to protect him. The child who struggles, within a family that levels, receives an equally heavy silent message: you are the one who needs, you are the fragile one, you are the one to whom the others have to give. And that message, repeated over the years, can trap him in the place of the one who can't, confirm to him that alone he can't cope, take from him the stimulus and the dignity of finding his own strength. In trying to protect him with his siblings' help, you may, without meaning to, keep him small, make him forever the one who receives, never the one who is capable. None of your children is well served by leveling: neither the one who advances, held back by a debt, nor the one who struggles, fixed in the place of the one who needs. The love that levels, however well-intentioned, ends up shrinking them both.

And there's something he's denied, perhaps the most important of all: the authorship of his own story. The right to have a life that is only his, with his clean victories, without the others' problems inside them diluting them. When everything he builds has to be immediately shared, divided up, put at the service of the family balance, he never gets to feel that his life belongs to him. He lives his own story as if it were a common good, always in debt, always owing, and that, however much he prospers on the outside, leaves him strangely poor on the inside: poor in belonging to himself.

And there's a long-term cost you may not yet have seen: the debt that isn't his ends up, very often, driving the siblings apart from one another, exactly the opposite of what you wanted. Because the child who is always called to give begins, with time, to feel a tiredness, perhaps even a resentment, toward the siblings who receive, even while loving them. And the child who receives may feel, underneath, a humiliation, a diminishment, in being always the one who must be given to. These tensions, born of the economy of debts that leveling creates, keep eroding the love between them, slowly, beneath the surface. What was meant to unite them ends up creating between them accounts that don't balance. And often it's only when the mother sets down that accounting, and lets them relate without debts, that the siblings discover they can love each other freely, without the weight of who owes and who is owed.

Remember, again, what you believed you were doing. You didn't wake up thinking "today I'm going to take from this child the right to his own life." You woke up thinking "I don't want any of my children to feel alone, I don't want any to be left behind, I want them to stay united when I'm no longer here." Your intention was belonging, was unity, was the deepest love a mother can have. What happened was that this most beautiful intention, on meeting the inequality of life, translated into a request that weighed on one of your children. Do you see the difference? The intention was belonging. The effect was debt. And they're different things, and you can change the effect without renouncing the intention.

And there's an even more hidden effect, that touches the future. A child who grows up feeling he doesn't have permission for his own life while a sibling suffers, carries that pattern outside the family. He learns to feel guilt whenever he's well, not to allow himself to prosper without giving a part away, not to be able to enjoy what he achieves without thinking of those who don't have it. The leveling he lived at home becomes the way he relates to his own success for the rest of his life, and he carries, out in the world, an invisible debt that no one charges him except himself. To free him from that debt, to give him back the right to be well without guilt, is not only to heal today's relationship. It's to release a weight he would carry his whole life, and perhaps would pass, without meaning to, to his own children.

And a question begins to form, perhaps. A question that hurts and at the same time opens a door. Could I be asking a child to pay a debt that isn't his? Could I be, without meaning to, making one child's good life be lived with guilt, in the name of a fairness that in the end doesn't help the other and hurts this one? Don't answer in a hurry, and above all don't answer with guilt, because guilt, here, would only be one more weight, and this book didn't come to add weights, it came to show you which ones you can set down. The question is not an accusation. It's the beginning of a liberation, for you and for your children, all of them.

## 5. To unite is not to merge

I'm going to let fall here the realization that changes everything. Read slowly, and stay with it as long as it asks.

Your effort to unite your children may be keeping each one from occupying their own place.

Stay with this a moment.

Your whole life as a mother, you united. And uniting was good, it was necessary, it was love. But there's a confusion that set in, slowly, without anyone noticing it, and that is at the root of all the pain in this book: the confusion between uniting and merging. They're not the same thing. To unite is to keep connected beings who remain distinct, each with their form, their path, their destiny, connected by love and by belonging. To merge is to dilute the differences until everyone looks like one, until no one can be entirely himself without threatening the whole. You wanted to unite. But the fear of difference led you, without meaning to, to merge, and merging, however much it looks like love, suffocates.

Think of the families you know that seem truly united, the ones where the siblings seek each other out, like each other, support each other of their own accord. Notice that they're almost never families of equal children. They're families where each one followed their path, sometimes very different from one another, one far away, another nearby, one in one life, another in another, and where even so, or perhaps because of it, the bond held. Because what unites them is not the likeness, it's the love that didn't demand likeness. No one had to give up being who they are in order to keep belonging, and so no one needed to draw away in order to free themselves. The unity that respects difference doesn't have to be defended with vigilance, it defends itself, because no one wants to flee a place where they can be free.

Because a united family is not an equal family. It's a family where different people fit. Where one can prosper and another struggle, and both keep belonging, without one's success being a betrayal and the other's difficulty an accusation. True unity doesn't demand that everyone be in the same place, at the same height, with the same life. It demands only that everyone know they belong, whatever the place life puts them in. And that unity, the one that respects differences, is much stronger and much freer than forced equality, because it doesn't need to hold anyone back in order to maintain itself.

Notice that merging, contrary to how it seems, is not the height of love, it's its most subtle disguise. When we truly love someone, we want that person to be more themselves, to flourish in their own way, to follow their path. Merging wants the opposite: it wants the other to be the same, close, predictable, part of a whole where they don't stand out. So merging, even dressed as love and as unity, suffocates, while true love frees. And the question that distinguishes the two is simple, even if hard to answer honestly: when my child becomes more himself, more different, further from the mold, do I rejoice or do I take fright? If you rejoice, it's love. If you take fright and want to bring him back closer, it's the fear speaking, and the fear, recognized, can be calmed, so that the love can breathe.

There's an old loyalty that usually lies beneath all of this, and that's worth looking at with respect before questioning it. It's loyalty to equality, passed down in phrases you may have heard your whole life: "no one is better than anyone," "don't act high and mighty," "don't forget where you came from," "here we're all equal." These phrases were not born of ill will. They were born of the wish for belonging, of keeping people together, of protecting humility and connection. They have a real beauty, and they honor a value that matters. But they also have a flip side, when applied rigidly: they can hold back individuation, they can make growing, standing out, following your own path, be lived as a betrayal of the family. And no child should have to choose between belonging and becoming who they are.

There's an image that can help you feel the difference between uniting and merging. Think of a hand. The fingers are different, they have different sizes, different functions, and none tries to be the same as the others. And yet they all belong to the same hand, they move together, they do together what none would do alone. If you tried to make all the fingers the same, in the name of fairness, the hand would stop working, it would lose precisely the strength that comes from difference. A family is like that: its strength lies not in the children being the same, it lies in their being different and belonging to the same whole. To unite is to be a hand. To merge is to want all the fingers to be the same. And life, wise, made your children different precisely so that, together, they could be more than the sum of copies.

This is where the verb of this book comes in, the hardest of the whole series, and I want to introduce it with care: to bless. To bless is not only to permit, not only to tolerate difference reluctantly. It's something deeper and more beautiful: it's to truly wish each child well on their own path, to rejoice in each one's destiny, even when those destinies are different, even when one goes farther than the other. To bless is to say, with a whole heart, to each child: your life is yours, and I rejoice in it. It's the opposite of leveling. And it is, perhaps, the highest form of maternal love: to love not the children's equality, but the truth of each one.

## 6. To bless is to let each one go to their own destiny

If to unite is not to merge, then what, in practice, is to bless? Let's look closely, because it's here that love changes its shape without ceasing to be love.

But before I show you what blessing does, I want to take a weight off you, because I know it has already appeared: the fear that to bless the difference is to abandon the child who suffers. It isn't. Blessing doesn't ask you to stop caring about the child who struggles, to turn your back on him, to treat him with indifference. It asks only that you stop trying to solve his pain at the cost of the other's freedom. You can keep supporting the one who struggles, with all your love, with everything genuinely within your reach, and at the same time let the one who advances live freely. The two things fit together. To bless is not to choose one child against the other, it's to stop putting them in competition for an impossible balance, and to love each one, entirely, in the place where life has put him.

To bless is, first of all, to stop distributing emotional responsibilities among your children. To stop making the one who has more responsible for the one who has less. To stop turning the love of siblings into an obligation, a debt, a duty to balance lives. Not so that they stop helping each other, mind you, but so that the help, when it comes, is a choice and not an imposition. There's an enormous difference between a sibling who helps another because he wants to, with a free heart, and a sibling who helps because he feels his good life obliges him, because he carries the guilt of having more. The first is solidarity. The second is a debt that poisons, slowly, the love between them.

To bless is also to allow each child to have his own story, without having to compensate for it with his siblings'. To let the one who prospers prosper freely, without guilt, without having to hide his joy. To let the one who struggles fight his own fight, with your support, but without turning him into a cause that holds the others back. Each one with his life, each one with his destiny, all belonging to the same family, but none chained to the others' place. It's giving each child back the authorship that leveling took from him: the right for his life to be his.

To bless is also, and this is subtle, to let the child who struggles have the dignity of his own fight. It seems that protecting him is sparing him, is solving things for him, is mobilizing the siblings to shelter him. But there's a way of protecting him that diminishes him, that says to him "you can't, let us take care of you," and there's another that honors him, that says to him "I believe in you, I'm here, and your life is yours to live." To bless the child who struggles is not to leave him alone, it's to trust him enough not to make him, forever, the fragile one of the family. It's to support him without reducing him to his problem, to see him whole, with his strength and not only with his difficulty. Each child, the one who advances and the one who struggles, deserves to be seen for his own life, and not for the place he occupies on the scale you've been trying to balance.

And there's a part of blessing that's the hardest of all, and I want to name it plainly, because it's where many mothers discover their own limit. To bless is to be able to rejoice, truly, in what one child has built, without, the next second, your thoughts running to the child who suffers. It's to let the joy for one be whole, without cutting it with worry for the other. Many mothers can love all their children, but they can't fully rejoice in one's success without immediately feeling a pang of guilt because of the one who doesn't have the same. And that inability to feel full joy, however much it looks like loyalty to the one who suffers, helps no one: it gives nothing more to the one who struggles, and takes from the one who succeeded the right to be celebrated. To learn to rejoice fully in each child, one at a time, without compensations, is perhaps the most mature gesture of love this book asks of you.

I want to talk to you about why full joy is so hard, because understanding it helps to release it. When you don't allow yourself to rejoice entirely in one child because of the other, there's an old belief underneath: that joy is a limited quantity, and that celebrating one is taking away from the other. As if there were a fixed reserve of love and celebration, and what you give to one were necessarily missing from the other. But love doesn't work that way. It's not a cake divided into slices, where one's slice diminishes the other's. It's more like a light, that doesn't grow smaller for illuminating more than one person. You can rejoice fully in one child today and fully in another tomorrow, and neither is left with less for it. The joy you give one is not stolen from the other. To believe that it is, that's the very root of leveling, and undoing that belief is a large part of the crossing.

And now I need to restate, with all clarity, the rule that holds this whole book together, because without it everything I've said can be misunderstood. To bless is not to teach "every one for themselves." It's not telling your children to fend for themselves, not to help each other, each to care only for his own life. Healthy families help each other, and mutual help between siblings is one of the most beautiful things there is. What this book questions is not the help, it's help turned into an obligation of identity, into a debt, into a condition for belonging. And I restate too, with the same clarity, that there are children who really do need more support, because of illness, disability, a real and lasting fragility. In those cases there's no leveling to correct, there's love to honor, and the family's added solidarity is right and beautiful. You know the difference between a child who truly needs and a difference you're trying to level out of fear. Trust that discernment.

## 7. Each one in their place

If you've made it this far, you've already seen a lot: the balance that weighs downward, the fear of difference, the fairness that creates unfairness, the debt that isn't his, the confusion between uniting and merging. Now I want to show you, in broad strokes, where one goes from here. Not the step by step, which doesn't fit in a book and isn't done alone. But the map, the direction.

And I want to name a resistance you may feel here: the fear that, by letting each child follow their path, the family will fall apart, scatter, stop being a family. It's the original fear of this book, and it appears again, now at the threshold of change. But notice what we learned along the way: it's not difference that undoes families, it's obligation that wears them down. To let go is not to scatter. You keep being the emotional center, the home to return to, the mother of all. You only stop being the manager who keeps everything forcibly balanced. And a family that gathers out of love, and not out of duty, gathers better, lasts longer, seeks each other out more. What you fear losing by letting go is precisely what letting go protects.

The destination is simple to say and hard to live: each one in their place. Each child belonging through their own place, and not by paying for the others. In a family like this, belonging stops depending on equality. One can have more, another less, one can go far, another stay close, and all keep being equally of the family, equally loved, equally at home. The difference stops being a debt to settle and becomes only what it always was: difference. Distinct lives of distinct people who love each other and who belong to each other without having to be the same.

And I want you to see that this doesn't betray your original intention, on the contrary, it fulfills it. You always wanted belonging, unity, for your children to stay connected when you're no longer here. Well, it's precisely forced belonging, the one that demands equality, that most threatens that unity in the long term. Because a belonging that demands that each one be less than he is, that holds some back and overloads others, generates resentment, and resentment, with time, drives apart. The siblings who grew up in an economy of debts and compensations tend to draw apart when the mother is gone, because what united them was her, was the obligation she maintained, not a free love. Whereas the siblings who were left free to be different, who helped each other by choice and not by duty, those tend to stay connected, because what unites them is love, and free love lasts longer than obligation. To bless the difference does not undo the family. It's what can keep it truly united.

And I want to tell you how this is seen, in practice, in small gestures, without giving you a step by step, because the deep path is FreeMe's. It's seen when a child tells of a joy and you let it last, whole, without cutting it with worry for another. It's seen when you stop making the child who has more the automatic solver of the problems of those who have less, and let the help, if it comes, come by his choice and not by your order. It's seen when you stop measuring your love in equal portions and begin to give it according to what each one, at this moment, needs, knowing that giving differently is not loving unequally. It's seen when you receive each child for his own story, without immediately mixing in the others' affairs, giving each one the rare experience of existing, for a moment, as the center and not as a piece of the whole. They're small gestures, and each one says, without words: your life is yours, and I rejoice in it.

Honor, therefore, your intention, because it was always good. To want to unite your children is one of the most beautiful loves there is. What changes is not the intention, it's the form. You learn that the best way to keep them united is not to keep them equal, it's to bless each one's difference. That the best way to care for the one who struggles is not to hold back the one who advances, it's to support each one on his own path. That the best inheritance you can leave them is not a web of mutual debts, it's the freedom for each one to be who he is, connected by love and not by obligation.

And there's a return of yours, too, hidden in this path, and worth naming. Managing the balance among your children was, for years, a full-time job, a constant weight on your shoulders, a permanent vigilance over who is well, who is unwell, who needs more, who is drawing away. When you bless, when you give each one back their own life, you set down that weight too. You stop being the manager of the family balance and become, simply, the mother again. And you discover, perhaps with surprise, that there's a rest in it, a relief in letting each life follow its course, in trusting that the love between your children doesn't need you to balance it in order to exist. That rest is yours too, and you deserve to receive it.

And a word about time and guilt, before we close this chapter. Perhaps, in reading, a regret came over you, an "if only I'd understood this twenty years ago." Don't let that regret turn into a new leveling, now against yourself, in which you punish yourself to balance out a guilt. You did what you knew with the love you had, and the love you had was enormous, and the intention was always good. There's nothing here to atone for. There is, rather, a new way of loving you can still practice, from today, with the children you have, in the phase they're in. It's never too late to begin to bless. Children feel when a mother sets down the weight of balancing them and begins to celebrate them, each one for himself, and that change, even late, even imperfect, reaches them and changes something.

These are the outlines. The map, not the journey. The journey is deeper, it's done slowly, and sometimes it needs company. But the map already shows that there's a path, and that it leads to a freer family and a lighter mother. You, now, already know the direction.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in something simple: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything at once, and it doesn't take the pain away overnight. But it changes enough so that you can stop feeling like a bad mother for something that was always love, and begin to see another way of loving that doesn't ask any child to be less than he is.

And I want you to know that this change doesn't come too late, whatever the age of your children, however long this way of loving has already lasted. The blessing has no deadline. A mother can, in her seventies, eighties, look at a child and tell him, at last, with a whole heart, that she rejoices in his life, that his life is his, that he doesn't have to pay anything to anyone. And that sentence, even said late, even after decades of leveling, reaches the child and undoes old knots. It's never too late to set a child free. And there are few more beautiful inheritances a mother can leave than this: having, at last, blessed each of her children to be entirely who they are.

Now you know how to see the balance that weighs downward, and the fear of difference that lives beneath it. You know how the attempt at fairness can create unfairness, and what the debt that isn't his does to the child who advances. You know, and this was the realization that changes everything, that to unite is not to merge, and that your intention was always belonging, not harm. You know that to bless is to let each child have their own story, and that this is not "every one for themselves" nor is it ceasing to honor the one who truly needs. And you know that the best way to keep your family united is, after all, to let each one be free.

And you know, perhaps, that loving equally never meant loving in the same way. Your children are different, and so they need loves with different shapes, one more space, another more lap, one to be celebrated, another to be supported. To give each one what each one needs, and not the same to all, is not loving unequally. It's loving with more truth, it's seeing each child in his difference and answering him, and not an abstract measure of equality. The highest justice, between children, is not to give everyone the same. It's to give each one what is his.

You know, deep down, the sentence where this whole book weeps: I don't need to make you the same in order to love you equally.

And perhaps, deep down, the hardest sentence of all begins to make sense: I can rejoice in what you have built. Each one, one at a time, fully, without guilt.

Understanding has already opened a crack. But cracks aren't crossed by reading: they're crossed by taking the first step on the other side.

It was for that crossing that I created **FreeMe**.

FreeMe is not information to file away in your head. It's a path to be walked with your whole body. It's a guided crossing that you do from the inside, at your own pace, to set down what you carry and let each child follow their destiny. For a mother like you, who loved so much she wanted to keep everything united, there's a path designed for your pain: the fear of difference, the guilt over inequality, the debt you distributed without meaning to, and the courage to bless instead of to level. Not to stop loving your children, nor to keep them connected, since that's your most beautiful intention. To discover that the strongest unity is the one that lets each one be free.

And I want to tell you one last thing about what this crossing gives back to you, because it's not only for your children. For years you were the guardian of the balance, the one who measured, who adjusted, who carried the emotional accounting of the entire family. When you set down that role, when you let each life follow its course, you receive back something you had lost: the lightness of being a mother without being a manager. You can finally enjoy each child without the weight of having to balance him with the others. You can hear one's joy without agonizing over another's sadness in the same instant. You can love in peace, each one fully, without the permanent tension of keeping the scale level. And you discover that this peace, which seemed impossible, was always waiting for you on the other side of the fear of difference.

There, the sentences you read here in silence are spoken slowly, with space for the body to welcome them. "Your success takes nothing from your brother." "Your life belongs to you." "Each one occupies their place." What was a map here, there becomes a living crossing. To bless stops being an idea and becomes a gesture. And difference stops frightening and begins to seem only what it always was: life, in each of your children, taking its own shape.

If you feel it's the moment, I'm there. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't now, that's all right. What you've read stays with you, a patient seed, and the path awaits, whole, your turn.

And I want to leave you with an image that may stay with you. Think of a mother bird with several young in the nest. The time comes for them to fly, and each one flies in their own way: one early, another late, one high, another low, one far away, another stays nearby. The mother doesn't force them all to fly at the same height, nor hold back the one who flies higher to wait for the one who still hesitates. She lets each one find their own sky. And she keeps being the mother of all, of the one who went far and the one who stayed close, without the distance of one being a betrayal or the hesitation of another a shame. To bless is this: to let each child find their own sky, and to rejoice in each flight, even the ones that take them far, even the ones that take their time, because each one is the right flight for that child.

But before you close these pages, I ask you just one thing. Today, think of one of your children, and of one good thing he built, and let yourself rejoice in it, fully, for one minute, without letting your thoughts run to any of the others. Only that joy, whole, for that child. And notice what stirs in you. Because it's there, in that joy you allow yourself to have without compensation, that the blessing begins, and your children's freedom begins, and yours.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
