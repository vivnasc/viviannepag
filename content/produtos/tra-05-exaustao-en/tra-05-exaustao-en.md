# The woman who confuses exhaustion with merit

**For you, who use tiredness as proof that you dedicated yourself and deserve it. Because your worth is not measured in exhaustion, and you can rest without ceasing to be worth something.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the woman who uses exhaustion as a medal, and that leads, frequently, to burnout. If you recognize in yourself a tiredness that no longer passes with rest, persistent anxiety, an inability to stop, or a loss of pleasure in everything, this may be burnout, and it deserves the care of a health professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. The more exhausted, the more I deserve

Notice an equation that perhaps governs you, without your ever having put it into words. The more exhausted, the more I deserve. The more I drain myself, the more I am worth. Tiredness, for you, is not a warning. It is a medal.

You finish the day destroyed, and instead of alarm, you feel a strange satisfaction. I worked hard. I gave everything. I deserve my place. Exhaustion is your proof of dedication, your receipt of merit, the evidence that you gave yourself enough to have a right to what you have.

Notice how this satisfaction is, deep down, a relief from guilt, and not a joy. You don't feel good because the day was good, or because you created something you are proud of. You feel good because you drained yourself enough for the background guilt, that of not deserving, to give you a respite for a few hours. It is a peace bought with exhaustion, one that lasts only until the next day, when the guilt returns and demands new proof. And that is why you never truly rest: your peace is not repose, it is the short interval between two exhaustions, always waiting to have to prove, again, that you deserve.

And that is why you can't stop. Resting seems to you laziness. Time off seems to you a luxury you don't deserve. A day on which you didn't drain yourself seems to you a day on which you didn't do enough, on which you didn't deserve, on which you failed. You only feel at peace when you are exhausted, because only exhaustion confirms to you that you gave what you had to give.

And maybe you can no longer even recall a time of respite. A time when a Sunday afternoon with nothing to fulfill didn't set off your guilt, when lying down to stare at the ceiling was rest and not a crime, when your worth was not at stake at every hour the clock marked. For many people, this way of living has become so habitual that it seems the only possible way, it seems to be who one is. But it is not. It is not normal to live confusing tiredness with worth, nor healthy to find peace only in exhaustion. It is only an equation so old and so repeated that you stopped seeing it as a problem, and began to take it for a virtue.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only dedication, nor a work ethic. It is a deep confusion. You confused exhaustion with merit. Tiredness with worth. Draining yourself with proof that you deserve. And that confusion is destroying you, slowly, while you call it dedication. And it is so subtle that you have perhaps never seen it as a problem, but as a quality of yours.

Because exhaustion proves nothing. You are no more deserving for draining yourself. You are not worth more for ending up destroyed. Tiredness is not the measure of your worth, nor of your merit, nor of your dedication. It is, only, tiredness, and taken to the extreme, it is illness. A medal that, instead of honoring you, gradually consumes you.

This book is not about productivity, nor about time management, nor about how to work in a more sustainable way. I've come to look beneath the tiredness, at what feeds it. At the equation that makes you confuse exhaustion with merit. At the reason why you need to drain yourself to feel that you deserve. And at the possibility of resting, at last, without guilt, without that taking from you the worth that, deep down, never depended on your tiredness.

And I'll leave you the compass right away, so that you read without the tension of someone bracing for the blow. Your worth is not measured in exhaustion. You can rest without ceasing to be worth something. And that need to drain yourself in order to deserve comes from a wound we are going to look at, and which, if it has already taken you to the limit, also deserves the care of someone who knows how to walk alongside it.

There is a woman others see and a woman who drains herself within, and they do not coincide. What it looks like, from the outside, is a dedicated, hardworking woman, with an admirable ethic. What it is, inside, is a woman who doesn't feel entitled to be worth something without draining herself, and who destroys herself not out of love for the work, but out of fear of not deserving if she stops. The difference can't be seen from the outside, but it is felt: the dedication that comes from enjoyment has limits and brings joy; the one that comes from the fear of not deserving has no end and consumes. And it is likely that yours, however real the dedication, is mixed with that fear, and it is from that fear, not from the enjoyment, that this book wants to free you.

This book hasn't come to ask you to work less, or to stop dedicating yourself, or to tell you that your devotion is worth nothing. Your dedication is real and it is beautiful. It has come to ask you to stop measuring it in suffering, to stop needing to drain yourself in order to feel worthy, and to discover that you can give your best and also rest, without rest taking from you the worth that effort, alone, never truly gave you.

Maybe you recognize the equation in the small habits of your days.

In feeling guilt when you rest, as if you were doing something wrong, failing, being lazy, even when you are clearly exhausted and need to stop.

In measuring the worth of your day by the tiredness with which you end it, and in only feeling good when you are destroyed, as if a light day were a failed day.

In using your exhaustion, without realizing it, as proof of your dedication, almost with pride in how tired you are, like someone showing off a medal.

In postponing rest until you deserve it, and in that moment of having deserved it never arriving, because there is always more to do, more to prove.

In not being able to stop, even drained, because stopping seems to you to stop deserving.

In feeling a strange unease on days of rest, as if something were wrong, instead of the relief that rest should bring.

They are all the same equation wearing different clothes. Exhaustion confused with merit. The draining of oneself in order to have a right to be worth something.

I want to show you how this shows up day to day, in things you perhaps already take for normal. When they ask you how you are, you answer utterly exhausted, with a hint of pride in your voice, like someone showing off a medal. When you see someone rest, take time off, take time for herself, a judgment rises in you, an envy mixed with reproach, as if that person were doing something you don't allow yourself. And when, by chance, you have a light day, on which you didn't drain yourself, instead of savoring it, you feel uncomfortable, empty, as if you hadn't done enough to have a right to exist on that day.

Notice, too, a small thing you do without realizing it: the accounting you keep, in silence, of your own effort. You know exactly what time you went to bed and what time you got up, how many meals you skipped, how many times you were the last to leave. You keep those numbers like someone keeping receipts, ready to present, even if only to yourself, at the end of the day, as proof that you earned the right to be there. It is not vanity. It is the equation needing to be fed, needing accounts that confirm there was enough effort. And as long as you keep that accounting, no rest will seem paid for, because there is always a column where you could have given more.

And there is a detail that reveals everything: the way you plan rest. You don't rest because you need to, you rest because you deserved it, after having drained yourself enough to have earned it. Rest, for you, is not a need, it is a reward, and a reward that only comes after exhaustion. That is why you never rest in time, only at the end, destroyed, when it is already late, and even then with the sense that perhaps you haven't deserved enough.

And there is something the world doesn't see, beneath your dedication. People admire your devotion, they say you are tireless, that you work like no one, and that praise binds you even tighter to the equation, because now draining yourself is also being admired. They don't see the tiredness that doesn't pass, the guilt that prevents you from stopping, the equation that is destroying you slowly. They see the medal, not the wound. And you, rewarded for your exhaustion, become even more bound to it, because to let go of it seems to risk the admiration it brings you.

## 2. I have to drain myself to deserve

Go back. Beneath the professional surface, there is a belief. I have to drain myself to deserve my place.

Because your need to exhaust yourself doesn't arise only from the work. It arises from an older belief, that your worth has to be proven with effort, and the greater the effort, the greater the proof. Exhaustion is the extreme form of that proof: I gave so much that I destroyed myself, therefore, I deserve.

Where does this belief come from? Almost always, from a childhood where rest was suspect. From a time when your worth was not given, but had to be earned, and earned with effort, with sacrifice, with visible dedication. Maybe you learned, early on, that you were only valued when you strove to the limit, when you gave everything, when you sacrificed yourself. And that rest, idleness, time off, were suspect, signs of someone who doesn't deserve.

Maybe you come from a lineage where hard work was the supreme virtue, where whoever rested was frowned upon, where sweat was the measure of a person's worth. And you inherited that equation: worth is measured in effort, and the maximum effort, exhaustion, is the maximum proof of worth.

Maybe you learned that you only had a right to stop when you could go no further. That rest had to be earned with prior exhaustion. That you couldn't take time off before having destroyed yourself working. And you carried that lesson into your life, where you drain yourself in order to have, at last, a right to a rest you never allow yourself fully.

And maybe you live, still, in a world that reinforces this equation every day. A world that applauds whoever is always busy, that treats exhaustion as a sign of importance, that makes being tired a form of status. Where to say that you rested sounds like an excuse, and to say that you are drained sounds like a virtue. That world didn't create your wound, but it feeds it, gives it reason, makes you feel that your equation is the truth, and not a distortion. So part of your crossing is also learning to distrust that culture of exhaustion, to see that not everything the world rewards is good for you, and that being worth something is not the same as always being tired, however much, around you, one thing is confused with the other.

Notice what you learned, in any of these roots. That worth is proven in effort. That rest has to be earned. That exhaustion is the proof that you dedicated yourself. And it is that equation, carved early and reinforced everywhere, that governs you today, and that makes each moment of rest a debt to pay with guilt, instead of a right to live with peace. It makes you drain yourself without end, because to stop before exhaustion seems to you not to have deserved, and to deserve, in your world, costs you your skin.

Maybe you grew up seeing, at home, love and approval arrive through effort. The child who helped, who sacrificed herself, who tired herself, was the good daughter, the one who made others proud; the one who rested, who played, who did nothing, was the lazy one, the one who disappointed. And you learned, early, the equation that governs you today: love is earned with effort, and rest pushes it away. That is why resting brings you not only guilt, it brings an old fear, that of, by stopping, ceasing to be worthy of the affection you felt only effort gave you.

I want you to look at this without adding more guilt to the pile. Your confusion between exhaustion and merit is not a flaw, nor masochism. It is an inherited equation, learned in a time when your worth seemed to depend on your visible effort. You are not unable to rest by temperament. You are someone who learned that resting was not deserving, and who drains herself in order to have a right to be worth something.

And I want you to see in what part of you this equation lives, because without that nothing comes undone. This equation is not only an idea in your head, that changes with an argument. It is a way of feeling yourself, carved deep, in which rest triggers guilt and exhaustion soothes. That is why it isn't enough to understand, rationally, that your worth doesn't depend on effort, to stop feeling the guilt when you stop. The guilt doesn't obey reason, because it isn't born from it, it is born from an old equation, that is undone slowly, with the repeated experience of resting and discovering that you didn't cease to be worth something, and very often with the help of someone who walks alongside you to disarm it.

## 3. The rest that feels like betrayal

There is something hidden beneath your tiredness that you need to see. For you, resting is not resting. It is guilt. It is betrayal. It is failure.

Notice what you feel when you stop. It is not relief. It is unease. A voice that says you should be doing something, that you are wasting time, that you don't deserve that rest, that you are lazy. And that voice won't let you go, and it ruins your rest, and it pushes you back to work, to exhaustion, to the only thing that gives you peace: draining yourself.

And notice, with honesty, that often you don't even truly rest. You carry the work into the rest, in thought. You are on holiday but planning the return. You are with the family but brooding over what was left undone. Your body stops, but your mind doesn't, and so the rest doesn't restore, because there is no real rest where the head keeps working. It is like trying to charge a battery without turning off the device: it never fills. And that is why, however much you rest like this, you always wake up tired, because your rest is only a guarded pause, and not the repose of someone who, at last, switched off.

And the bill for this equation is enormous. Because a person who can't rest without guilt never truly rests. Your body never restores, your mind never stops, and you go on accumulating a tiredness that never resolves, because the rest that would resolve it is forbidden to you by guilt. You drain yourself without end, and the exhaustion worsens, because the only remedy, rest, you don't allow yourself to take.

And there is a cost in your relationship with life. Because living is not only working. It is also resting, enjoying, being, playing, doing nothing. And you, by forbidding yourself rest, by only allowing yourself effort, live an amputated life, reduced to work, without the idleness, the pleasure, the time off, which are part of what makes a life full. You complete life as a task, and never live it as a delight.

And there is a cost, above all, in the body, which pays in silence the bill of an equation that doesn't respect its limits. The body was not made to give without restoring, to spend itself without resting, to function always in the red. And when you force it to that, in the name of merit, it exacts the bill, sooner or later: in tension that won't let go, in sleep that doesn't repose, in illnesses, in ever louder signs that it can't bear the pace. You call it dedication. Your body calls it mistreatment, and it warns you, in its way, that the exhaustion you take for a medal is, for it, a wound that accumulates.

And there is a cruel irony in your equation: it ends up taking from you precisely what you seek. You seek to be worth something, to deserve, and exhaustion, taken to the limit, leads you to burnout, to the point where you can no longer do anything, where you fail at everything, where you become, by dint of draining yourself for merit, unable to fulfill whatever it may be. The equation promises you worth through draining yourself, and delivers you, in the end, collapse and incapacity. What you do to deserve ends up taking from you the possibility of doing anything at all.

Notice this trap. The more you drain yourself in order to deserve, the more tired you become, and the more tired you become, the more you need to rest, but the more guilty you feel if you rest, and so the more you drain yourself to compensate for the guilt. It is a circle that tightens, and that ends, if nothing breaks it, in burnout, at the point where the body, or the mind, can no longer bear it, and stops, without asking your permission.

And there is something cruel in this circle: it never gives you the sense of arrival you seek. You drain yourself to deserve, but the deserving is never guaranteed, because tomorrow there is more to prove, more to do, more exhaustion to accumulate. The goal always moves, and you run after it without ever reaching it, because it is not a real goal, it is an equation without end. That is why, however much you drain yourself, the peace of having, at last, deserved never comes. That peace doesn't come from effort, however much you do, it comes from an inner change: that of ceasing to need to deserve a worth that is already yours.

I want you to see, still, that this equation robs you of the ability to feel healthy pride in what you do. Because true pride comes from the worth of what one does, and you only feel merit in how much it cost you. You could have done something extraordinary with lightness, with pleasure, in little time, and you wouldn't value it, because it didn't hurt enough. And you could have done something mediocre at the cost of an enormous exhaustion, and feel deserving, because you suffered. The equation corrupts your judgment about your own work, making you value the suffering instead of the worth, the cost instead of the fruit.

And it costs you, too, the people you love, and you with them. Because the time that should be theirs, and yours, is swallowed by work, or by an exhaustion that no longer lets you be present. You are with them but destroyed, with no energy for the conversation, for the game, for the affection, because you spent everything deserving. And when, rarely, you stop, the guilt won't let you be whole even there. So your dedication, which looks like a virtue, gradually robs the moments that matter most, and those who love you always receive your leftovers, what is left after exhaustion has taken the best of you.

I want you to feel the full weight you have been carrying as if it were a medal. The weight of never being able to rest in peace. Of carrying a guilt whenever you stop. Of draining yourself without end, because only exhaustion gives you the right to be worth something. It is a deep exhaustion, and perhaps you already take it for dedication, or for a work ethic, when it is, deep down, a wound that forbids you rest and pushes you toward burnout.

## 4. Who am I when I rest

I'll set a question down here, the way you set down a load you can't carry any further. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who are you when you rest? When you are not producing, striving, draining yourself? Who are you, simply stopped, doing nothing?

Stay with it. Notice the guilt, or the emptiness, that it brings.

For you, this question touches a place that hurts, and that is why you run from it. Because your worth stuck to your effort, to your exhaustion. You are the one who dedicates herself, who gives everything, who drains herself. Take that away from you, imagine yourself resting, not producing, not striving, and a guilt appears, an emptiness, a sense of not deserving, of not being worth something, of failing.

And the shadow that hides here is the belief that, without the effort, without the exhaustion, you have no worth. That to rest is not to deserve. That your right to be worth something depends on always giving, draining yourself, proving, and that the moment you stop, that right is suspended, and you cease to be worth something.

Notice how that belief becomes the chain that keeps you draining yourself. You can't rest, even destroyed, because resting suspends your worth. You drain yourself without end, because only exhaustion confirms it. You live bound to an equation that forbids you time off, because time off, in your world, is to stop deserving. And rest, which is a basic human need, becomes, for you, a transgression, a guilty luxury, a failure.

And a worth bought with exhaustion is a worth built on sand. Because a worth that depends on constant effort is a worth forever at risk, forever needing to be renewed with more exhaustion. You can never rest in that worth, because it depends on your continuing to drain yourself. You live, therefore, in a race without end, proving, each day, with more tiredness, a worth that is never guaranteed, because it rests on an exhaustion you have to repeat without stopping.

Compare with someone who has a worth that rests in herself. That person can work and stop, give and restore, without her sense of being worth something fluctuating with the amount of effort of each day. She is worth something when she produces and worth something when she rests, because the worth is not in the doing, it is in being. And so she lives with a peace you lack: that of not having to prove anything, at every hour, in order to keep deserving. It is that peace this crossing wants to give you back, not that of doing less, but that of ceasing to make your worth depend on how much you drain yourself.

I want to tell you something about this belief, because it begins to disarm itself. Your worth doesn't depend on your effort, nor on your exhaustion. You are worth something, whether you rest or not, whether you drain yourself or not. Rest doesn't take your worth, just as exhaustion doesn't add to it. Your worth is prior to your effort, and independent of it. And resting, far from making you less worthy, is simply taking care of yourself, as any person has a right to do.

And I want to tell you something about the fine panic this question awakens, that tightening that makes you immediately look for a task. What frightens you in rest is not rest itself, it is the emptiness it uncovers, the question about your worth that effort covered. While you drain yourself, you don't have to face that question, because exhaustion answers for you: I gave everything, therefore I am worth something. When you stop, the automatic answer disappears, and the bare question remains: am I worth something, even without giving anything? And it is from that question that you flee, by draining yourself, because facing it, without the crutch of effort, terrifies you. But it is precisely that question that needs a new answer, and the answer is yes, you are worth something, and rest is the place where, slowly, you can learn it.

Don't answer the question of who you are when you rest just yet. But leave it open. Because it is in that bare question, and not in any effort, that the way out of the equation lies. On the day you discover that you are worth something even when you rest, you stop having to drain yourself in order to deserve. And then, only then, can you rest, at last, without guilt.

## 5. When the exhaustion is burnout

I have to halt this whole machine of merit for a moment. With all seriousness. Because there is a point at which exhaustion stops being a medal and becomes an illness, and it is vital for you to know how to recognize it.

When one confuses exhaustion with merit, one drains oneself without end, without stopping, to the limit. And on the other side of that limit is burnout, a serious exhaustion, that the body and the mind exact, and that is not a medal, it is a wound.

I want to name the signs, plainly. If the tiredness you carry no longer knows a bottom, if no amount of sleep is enough for it, if the holidays end and you return as empty as you left. If a tightness tied to work accompanies you even as you lie down, and wakes you at dawn with the list unfulfilled. If what once gave you enjoyment has gone out, and the days have come to taste of ash, of task, of nothing. If you catch yourself living on autopilot, going through the motions without being there. If your body has begun to break down, to fall ill, to scream what you insist on silencing.

These are signs of burnout, and burnout is not solved by resting a weekend, nor by striving more, nor by enduring. It is solved by stopping seriously, and with support, from a doctor, from a psychologist, from someone who helps you recover. And the sooner you recognize it, the better, because installed burnout is deep, and it is very hard to come out of it.

And I want to distinguish for you normal tiredness from burnout, because it matters. Normal tiredness passes with rest: you sleep, you stop for a few days, and you restore yourself. Burnout doesn't pass like that, because it is not only of the body, it is also of the mind, of the spirit, of meaning. It is an exhaustion that has installed itself deep, that persists even after rest, that takes away your pleasure and your energy in a way that a holiday doesn't cure. If you notice that you have already rested and you remain empty, exhausted, without spirit, that is not laziness nor a lack of rest, it is a sign that the exhaustion is already deep, and that you need more than to stop for a few days: you need support.

And I want you to hear this with all seriousness: burnout is not only a matter of tiredness, it can bring, or worsen, deep suffering, and even very dark thoughts, of someone who no longer sees a way out. If you have reached that point, if you feel that the weight is too great, that you can no longer bear it, please, don't stay alone with that. Don't put it off as you put off rest. Tell someone today, contact a doctor, a support line, a person you trust, this is the one task that cannot wait for deserving. Your life is worth infinitely more than any proof of merit, and there is a way out, and there are those who know how to help you find it.

And I want to name the specific trap of someone who confuses exhaustion with merit. It is that you, precisely because you value draining yourself, are the one who most ignores the signs of burnout, or even lives them with pride. "I'm exhausted because I dedicated myself." And so you continue, beyond the limit, draining yourself, when you should stop, because your equation turns the very alarm into a medal.

I want to ask you to see this with clarity. Your exhaustion is not a proof of merit. It is, very often, a warning, and at times a symptom. And confusing it with virtue can cost you your health, and more than that. If you recognize, in yourself, the signs of burnout, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Stop. Seek help. Your worth, your dedication, are not worth more than your health, than your life. And stopping in time is not failing. It is, of all that you can do, the most lucid gesture, even if your equation swears to you otherwise.

And I want to take away a fear that may be preventing you from seeking help: that of asking for support being, in itself, a proof that you didn't endure, that you weren't strong enough. That is, precisely, the equation speaking, turning even care into a matter of merit. But seeking help when you are drained is not weakness, it is lucidity. The people we most admire for their resilience are not the ones who destroyed themselves in silence, they are the ones who knew how to recognize when they needed to stop and to be helped. Taking care of yourself doesn't take your merit. It guarantees that there will still be a you, in good health, to keep doing what she loves.

I want you to understand, too, that burnout is not a character flaw, nor a lack of ability. It is what happens to dedicated, competent people, who gave too much for too long, without restoring. It happens, precisely, to the best, to those who give themselves most, and not to the weak. So, if you have reached that point, it is not because you are less, it is because you gave more than was humanly sustainable. And coming out of it is not a matter of striving more, since it was the excess effort that brought you here, it is a matter of stopping, of restoring, and of being accompanied as you remake yourself.

## 6. To rest without ceasing to be worth something

If you confused exhaustion with merit, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can rest without ceasing to be worth something.

Read this slowly. For you, resting and being worth something are incompatible. You are only worth something when you strive, when you drain yourself. To rest is to suspend your worth. And I propose to you that you separate the two things. Your worth doesn't depend on your effort. You are worth something, whether you rest or not. Rest doesn't diminish you. It is only rest, a human need, a right, and not a transgression.

Let me weigh it on a fairer scale than exhaustion. Imagine two equally dedicated, equally competent people. One drains herself without stopping, and ends in burnout, unable to continue. The other works with dedication and rests, and so can keep going, in good health, for a long time. Which of the two is more valuable, in the long run? The second, by far. Because sustainable worth is not in draining yourself, it is in working and resting, at a pace that can be maintained. Exhaustion is not the peak of dedication. It is its collapse.

Think of this as you would think of an athlete. No real athlete trains until collapsing every day, without rest, because she knows that rest is part of the training, it is when the body rebuilds itself and becomes stronger. Whoever trained without resting would not be more dedicated, she would be more foolish, and she would injure herself quickly. Rest is not the opposite of performance, it is a condition of it. And the same goes for you: resting doesn't make you less dedicated, it makes you able to dedicate yourself for longer, better, without breaking yourself. Your equation confuses draining yourself with excellence, when true excellence always includes the rest that sustains it.

And I want you to see that there is a hidden arrogance in your equation, that you perhaps haven't noticed: that of thinking you can bear what no human body bears without restoring. As if you were the exception, the one who can give without end, the one who doesn't need to rest like the others. But you are not the exception, nor is your body different from other bodies. Recognizing your limits is not weakness, it is truth, it is humility in the best sense: that of accepting that you are human, that you need to restore, that you are not a machine. And there is an enormous relief in being able, at last, to be only human, with everyone's limits, instead of demanding of yourself a superhuman endurance that no one has.

And the moment the equation breaks, what floods in is liberation. When resting stops taking your worth, you can, at last, rest without guilt. You can take time off, stop, do nothing, without the voice that tells you that you don't deserve it. You can take care of yourself, restore yourself, live beyond work. And, paradoxically, by resting, you work better, with more energy, more clarity, more pleasure, because a rested person performs more and better than an exhausted one.

Notice this truth, which contradicts your equation: rest is not the enemy of good work, it is part of it. The clearest ideas come after a good night's sleep, not after a dawn of exhaustion. Creativity is born in repose, in the pause, in idleness, and not in draining yourself. Mistakes multiply when you are destroyed, and decrease when you are rested. So, by draining yourself in the name of dedication, you harm, after all, the very work to which you dedicate yourself. Resting doesn't rob you of the work. It gives you back to it better, and it is your exhaustion, and not your rest, that makes you perform less than you could.

And I want you to see that measuring worth by exhaustion is a ruler not only cruel, but false. Because it doesn't reward the result, it rewards the suffering. By that ruler, whoever drained herself doing badly would be worth more than whoever, rested, did well with lightness. It is absurd, when you see it from the outside, but it is the ruler by which you assess yourself: not by what you do, but by how much it cost you. To change that ruler, and to begin measuring yourself by the worth of what you create and by the life you live, instead of by the tiredness you accumulate, is one of the most freeing changes of this crossing.

I want to give you the one verb that undoes the whole equation. To rest. To rest is not laziness, nor failure, nor ceasing to deserve. It is taking care of yourself. It is restoring what work spends. It is living the part of life that is not work. It is giving your body, your mind, your being, the time off they need to stay whole. To rest is an act of health, and of dignity, and not a transgression to atone for with guilt.

And resting demands something that costs you: tolerating the guilt that rises when you stop, without obeying it. The voice that tells you that you don't deserve it, that you should be doing something, that you are lazy, will appear. And resting, for you, is staying in the rest despite that voice, letting it speak without following it, until it, slowly, loses force. Each time you rest despite the guilt, you teach yourself that you are worth something even resting.

I want to be honest with you about one thing: at first, resting may give you more anxiety than peace. It is common. When you stop, after so much time running, the guilt rises, the unease tightens, and rest, instead of relieving, seems a torment. It is not a sign that rest is bad for you. It is a sign of how rooted the equation is, and that your system still associates stopping with danger. Cross through that discomfort, stay in the rest despite it, and you will discover that, with repetition, the guilt lowers and rest, slowly, begins to taste of peace. The first rest is the hardest; each one after is a little lighter.

And I want to give you a way to begin, that may help you. Don't try, at first, the great rest, the whole day doing nothing, which will set off all the guilt at once. Begin with the small: a ten-minute pause without guilt, an evening without work, a Sunday morning without producing. Small doses of rest, in which you train staying still despite the voice, and in which your system gradually learns that resting doesn't bring the catastrophe it fears. Rest, like everything that is relearned, is done little by little, and each small dose endured makes the next easier.

And I want to give you a small sentence, to use in the exact instant the guilt rises and demands of you the bill of your effort. I don't have to pay for this. Repeat it when you sit down and the voice asks you what you did to deserve that couch. I don't have to pay for this. Rest is not a loan you took out and have to settle with tiredness. It is yours, from the start, with no debt at all. And there is a quiet liberation in discovering that you can stop without opening an account, without staying in debt, without the clock of merit running beneath your pause.

The day the equation first loosens its grip, you feel it as pure relief. It is the day you rest, when you stop, when you do nothing, and instead of the usual guilt, you feel, for an instant, that it is fine. That you deserve that rest. That you didn't cease to be worth something for having stopped. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves a lifetime's equation, and gives you back the right to the rest you always denied yourself.

## 7. My worth is not measured in tiredness

There is a place this crossing is taking you, and it is not the next finish line. To recognize that your worth is not measured in tiredness.

And I want to translate this for you into the gestures of an ordinary day, because that is where it is lived.

It is to stop using exhaustion as proof of merit. To stop measuring the worth of your day by the tiredness with which you end it. To stop only feeling good when you are destroyed. It is to begin to measure your day, and your worth, by something other than exhaustion: by what you lived, by what you created with pleasure, by the balance you kept, and not by how much you drained yourself.

It is to recognize that resting is part of a dignified life, and is not its opposite. That taking time off is not failing. That idleness, pleasure, time off, are part of what makes a life full, and not waste to atone for with guilt. It is to give yourself back the right to rest, which is a basic human right, and not a luxury to deserve with prior draining.

It is, too, learning to rest before you need to, and not only when you can go no further. Because preventive rest, the one taken in time, restores and protects; emergency rest, the one that only comes at collapse, already arrives late, is already a patch. To change from one to the other, to rest out of care and not out of exhaustion, is one of the deepest transformations of this crossing. It means treating yourself as you would treat someone in your care, with attention to needs before they become a crisis, instead of pushing yourself to the limit and only then stopping.

And it is separating your worth from your productivity, which is, deep down, the heart of everything. You are not worth something for what you produce. You are worth something for being who you are, and that doesn't increase when you produce more, nor decrease when you produce less or nothing. There is a worth of yours that is prior to any doing, that was there before you became useful, that would continue to be there if one day you could do nothing. To find again that worth, which doesn't depend on effort, is what frees you from the need to prove it, without end, with your exhaustion.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you rest without having drained yourself, the old guilt, the sense of not deserving, the voice that calls you lazy. And you will want to go back to the effort, to the exhaustion, to the only thing that gives you the guilty peace of someone who has fulfilled.

But it is precisely there, in that resting endured in spite of the guilt, that your liberation lies. Because each time you rest, and you don't cease to be worth something, and the world doesn't punish you, and your life doesn't collapse, you learn that your worth didn't depend on your exhaustion. That you could rest, all this time, and be worth something just the same.

And I want you to treat the guilt that rises with the gentleness you would have for a friend, and not with the harshness with which you treat yourself. If a friend told you that she feels guilty for resting, that she thinks herself lazy for stopping, what would you tell her? Almost certainly: you have every right to rest, it is not laziness, it is care, you deserve to stop. Well, that is, exactly that, what you need to tell yourself, in the voice of the guilt. Not to fight the guilt with more harshness, which only feeds it, but to answer it with the tenderness with which you would treat those you love. Because the guilt of resting is not won with force, it is won with compassion, with the gentleness of someone who treats herself, at last, as she would treat someone in her care.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that tells you that without draining yourself you don't deserve, is carved deep, and it will appear each time you rest, with its usual guilt. It will tell you that you are being lazy, that you should be doing something, that you didn't do enough. Don't expect to silence it in a day, expect only to stop obeying it. To let it say you don't deserve to stop, and to answer it, slowly, from the depth of the armchair where you chose to stay, I am worth something even resting. And each time you rest despite it, and you don't cease to be worth something, you weaken the old voice, and you strengthen a new one, more your own, which knows that your worth is not measured in tiredness.

I want you to see what opens up when your worth stops being measured in tiredness. Rest opens up, without guilt. Health opens up, because you stop draining yourself to burnout. A fuller life opens up, with pleasure, with time off, with parts that are not work. And a better work opens up, paradoxically, because a rested person creates better than an exhausted one. By ceasing to drain yourself, you are not worth less. You live, and work, and are worth something, better.

And I want to leave you with something, about who sees you, above all if there are children, or younger people who observe you. If they see you live exhausted, confusing tiredness with worth, feeling guilt whenever you stop, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it is likely they will carry, too, the same equation that destroys you. The most freeing thing you can teach those who watch you is not the example of someone who drains herself. It is that of a woman who works with enjoyment and also rests without guilt, who knows that her worth is not in how much she tires herself. Sometimes a woman learns to rest, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to leave those she loves the inheritance of a life measured in exhaustion.

And I want you to see that resting without guilt doesn't make you less dedicated, less serious, less good at what you do. Your dedication stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops coming from the fear of not deserving, and comes instead from enjoyment, from purpose, from choice. And what is done from enjoyment, and not from fear, is better, more creative, more sustainable, than what is wrenched out at the cost of exhaustion. Fear drains and impoverishes; enjoyment, fed by rest, sustains and flourishes. To stop draining yourself won't make you give less to the world. It will make what you give come from a more whole place, and last longer.

I won't give you a method of energy management, because this book is not about productivity. But I leave you the equation that replaces the old one. Your worth is not measured in tiredness. Rest, without guilt, as someone who takes care of herself. And discover, slowly, that you are worth something even resting, and that the exhaustion, which you took for a medal, was, after all, the path to burnout, and not the proof of your worth.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't undo the equation in a single day. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, rest without guilt for the first time. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your exhaustion with merit, and your draining yourself with dedication, and to begin to look at the guilt of resting as a wound, and not as a truth.

Now you know that you confuse exhaustion with merit, and that this is not only a work ethic, it is an equation that destroys you slowly.

You know that you learned that you had to drain yourself in order to deserve, in an equation inherited from far away, and reinforced by a world that rewards exhaustion and confuses being busy with having worth.

You know that rest seems to you betrayal, and a guilt, and that that guilt forbids you time off and pushes you toward draining yourself, in a circle that only ends, if nothing breaks it, in collapse.

You know whom you fear being when you rest, and that underneath lies the belief that without the effort you have no worth, and the emptiness of the question that exhaustion covered.

You know that exhaustion, taken to the limit, is burnout, and that confusing it with virtue can cost you your health, and more than that, and that stopping in time is the most lucid act there is.

You know that your worth is not measured in tiredness, and that the verb is to rest, without ceasing to be worth something, as someone who takes care of herself, and not as someone who receives a prize.

And you know that rest is not the opposite of dedication, it is part of it, and that a rested person lives, and works, and is worth something, better than an exhausted one.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want it to come back to your mind the next time the guilt pushes you back to work.

Imagine someone who believes she only deserves to eat if she has run a marathon first. And who, therefore, runs, and runs, until she collapses, and only then, exhausted, allows herself to eat, with the guilt of someone who perhaps didn't run enough. And who lives like this, draining herself to deserve the basics, confusing draining herself with the right to live.

The crossing is to discover that you have a right to eat without running the marathon. That you deserve the basics, the rest, the care, for existing, and not for draining yourself. That your worth is not in the marathon, it is in you, and that you can stop running until you collapse, and rest, and eat, and live, without having to deserve it with your exhaustion.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't turn it into one more marathon to run to perfection, one more proof of merit to add to the list. There will be setbacks. Days when you rest without guilt, and days when you drain yourself again, feeling that you are only worth something if you destroy yourself, as always. And the old voice will use each setback to tell you you see, you need to drain yourself. Don't believe it. A setback is not the equation winning, it is the equation fading unevenly. Nobody undoes all at once a lifetime's equation. It is undone little by little, and each time you rest and you don't cease to be worth something, a new proof is left, and it is from those proofs, added together, that the new truth, slowly, takes root.

And I want to leave you, before you set these pages down to rest, one last simple thing. You are worth something. Not for how much you strive, not for the tiredness with which you end, not for the exhaustion you accumulate. You are worth something for existing, for who you are, before and independent of any effort. The girl who learned that she had to tire herself in order to deserve learned a lie, even if told, perhaps, by those who loved her. And undoing that lie is not ceasing to dedicate yourself, it is dedicating yourself from enjoyment, and not from fear, and resting knowing that your worth was guaranteed from the start, without your having to pay for it with your exhaustion.

You already recognize it, the sentence that holds this whole book together. I can rest without ceasing to be worth something. My worth was guaranteed from the start, and I don't have to pay for it with my exhaustion.

Understanding was the first stretch of the crossing. But there is a second, and that one is not done sitting and reading, it is done sitting and doing nothing. It is done by resting, slowly, despite the guilt. By measuring your day by something other than tiredness. And, if you have already reached burnout, it is done with help, with someone who walks alongside you as you recover, because installed exhaustion is deep, and one doesn't come out of it only by wanting to.

And if from all of this you keep a single line, the next time you are exhausted and calling it dedication, let it be this. Rest is not the prize for your worth, it is part of your dignity. You don't have to deserve it by draining yourself first, because it is not a reward, it is a human need, as basic as eating or sleeping, and you have a right to it simply for being a person. Life was not given to you to spend it proving that you deserve to be alive. It was given to you to live it, and living includes resting, without guilt, as someone who knows that she is already worth something, always, without having to pay for it with her exhaustion.

And before you set this book down and get up, out of habit, to go do something, I ask one gesture of you only. A hard one, for you, harder than any task.

This week, rest without having deserved it with exhaustion. Stop, take time off, do nothing, in a moment when you are not destroyed, when you would still have energy for more. Rest not as a reward for draining yourself, but as care, as a right. And notice the guilt that rises, the voice that calls you lazy, the urge to go back to work. And rest anyway. Because it is in that rest, endured in spite of the guilt, that you begin to discover that you are worth something even without draining yourself, and that rest was never betrayal, it was, after all, the care you always denied yourself.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are being lazy, wasting time, not deserving, remember: that thought is the equation, not the truth. The truth is that you are taking care of yourself, restoring what needs to be restored, living, at last, a part of life that is not work. And that this doesn't take your worth, it gives you back the health, and the life, that exhaustion had been charging you.

Because you are worth something, and always were, far beyond any tiredness.

Because you deserve to rest, today, without having run any marathon.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
