# The woman who disappears in love

**For you, who dissolve inside the one you love until you can no longer find yourself. Because giving yourself is not disappearing, and you can remain with yourself while you love.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a way of loving in which a person dilutes herself in the other until she is lost, and there is a point at which this stops being giving and becomes a loss of self: isolation from everything that was yours, dependency, persistent loss of identity. If you recognize yourself in this, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. Where did I end up

Notice what happens to you when you love.

You disappear.

At first, it's you. You have your life, your tastes, your friends, your routine, your opinions.

And then a love comes in. And little by little, without noticing, you go on diluting yourself.

You adopt his tastes. His music. His shows. His friends become yours, and yours are left behind.

His schedule organizes yours. His wishes go ahead of yours. His opinions become, without your noticing, your opinions.

And one day, after a while, you look for yourself, and you can't find yourself.

Where did I end up? What did I like to do, before? Who were my friends? What did I think about this, before I thought what he thinks?

You don't know. You diluted yourself so much that there's no longer an outline between you and him. And where you were, there's now a we in which you, individual, almost no longer exist.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not loving too much. Everyone says you love too much. It isn't true.

To love is not to disappear. You can love immensely without diluting yourself. What happens to you is not an excess of love. It's a lack of you.

There's a difference between giving yourself and dissolving yourself. Giving yourself is giving yourself to someone while remaining whole. Dissolving yourself is ceasing to exist as a separate person.

You don't give yourself. You dissolve yourself. And you call that love, when it's, deep down, a disappearance.

This book will not teach you to love less, to hold yourself back, to give yourself by halves out of calculation. I didn't come to teach you to defend yourself from love, to close off a part, to not give yourself. That would be to mistake the whole current for its danger. Your capacity to give yourself is beautiful. The problem is not giving yourself. It's giving all of yourself, until no one is left to be loved.

I came to help you do something much harder. To remain with yourself while you love. To not lose yourself in the other. To keep existing, you, inside the relationship.

I'll point you toward the far bank now, so you can read in peace. You can love without abandoning yourself. You can have a we without ceasing to have an I. And the path is not to love less. It's to return to yourself.

Maybe the dilution shows itself first in small things.

In not knowing how to answer, on your own, what you want for dinner, what you want to do on the weekend, because you got used to wanting what he wants.

In having let drop, one by one, your friends, your hobbies, your places, without even noticing when.

In feeling a disproportionate panic when he moves away, because without him you no longer really know who you are.

In looking at photos of yourself from before this relationship and barely recognizing that woman, who had so much that was only hers.

They all flow into the same current. The disappearance. The slow dilution of you inside the one you love.

There's something you'll want to say in your defense. "But I like his things. I'm not pretending."

And it's true, in part. You really do begin to like them. The problem isn't liking what's his. It's ceasing to know what was yours.

To adopt the tastes of the one we love is natural, and beautiful. What isn't natural is for your own collection of tastes to disappear, without replacement, until only his remain.

Notice the difference. In a healthy sharing, worlds are traded. He enters yours, you enter his, and each of you becomes larger. In dissolution, only one world survives, his, and yours evaporates.

In one, you become larger. In the other, you disappear. And you, without noticing, always disappear.

There's another thing you might recognize. You don't negotiate.

In a relationship, there are differences. They want different things, and they negotiate, one gives, the other gives, a middle ground is found. That's how two people live together.

You don't negotiate, because you don't bring your part to the table. You give everything, from the start, before the negotiation begins. You agree before you know whether you agree. You adapt before you feel what you wanted.

And by not negotiating, it seems there's no conflict, that you're so compatible, that everything goes well. But there's no peace. There's your absence. The peace of a relationship where one person disappeared is not peace. It's silence.

I want to give you an example, one of those that may seem too small to mean anything. You were invited to a dinner. He accepted right away, cheerful, without asking you. And you, in that instant, felt a shiver of weariness, because you didn't feel like that dinner at all, that crowd, that night. And you didn't say so. You smiled, you made yourself pretty, you faked a willingness you didn't have. And you came home exhausted, without understanding why. The reason is simple. You spent the entire night performing a woman who wanted to be there. And performing who we are not tires us more than any work.

Notice who decides. Where they go to dinner, what they watch, how they spend the weekend, who they go out with. If the answer is, almost always, he, notice. Not because he's bossy. But because you stopped bringing a will. You say "whatever," "whatever you want," "you decide," and over time you stopped having, even, a preference.

"Whatever" looks like flexibility, looks like easy love. But it hides a woman who no longer knows what she wants, because she switched off the channel that told her.

Notice your emotions. You walk into a room and your first reading is how he is. If he's well, you're well. If he's badly, your day collapses. Your inner state became a reflection of his, with no life of its own.

They call this being sensitive, empathetic, devoted. And there's some of that. But when your state depends entirely on the other's state, you're not only empathetic. You're dissolved. You don't have your own emotional center, only a mirror of his.

And notice your friends, your spaces, your things. You let them drop, one by one, as you plunged into the relationship. And one day you look for your life, the one that was only yours, and you can't find it, because you gave it away, bit by bit, until none was left.

There's a word you probably use a lot, and I want you to notice it: "we." When you tell how your weekend was, you say we. When you talk about your plans, you say we. When someone asks how you are, you answer how you two are. Notice how many times, in a day, there's a we and how many times there's an I. For many people, the we lives well alongside the I, they alternate according to what's at stake. In you, the I almost disappeared from your speech, because it disappeared from your life. And the speech is honest: we say we when there's no longer a separate I to name. I'm not asking you to cut out the we, which is beautiful when there are two. I'm asking you to notice whether there's still, in your mouth, an I with a life of its own, or whether it dissolved too into the grammar of the couple.

I want you to notice one last thing, before we go to the origin. There's a panic you might know, and that's the reverse of dissolution: the panic that he'll move away.

When someone dissolves into the other, she loses her own center, and so her entire stability comes to depend on the other's presence. If he moves away, even a little, even for a day, you feel not only longing. You feel a kind of disappearance of yourself, as if, without him, you ceased to exist.

That panic is not too much love. It's a lack of center. It's the sign that you put, in the other, the ground you should have in yourself. And that's why you cling, you control, you can't bear the distance: not because you love him so much, but because, without him, you can't find yourself.

When you recover a center of your own, that panic drops. Not because you love less, but because you no longer depend on his presence to exist. You can love him, and miss him, without disappearing in his absence. And that is, perhaps, the greatest proof that you returned to yourself: you already feel longing, but you no longer disappear.

There's a moment of the day when all of this is seen with particular clarity, and I want you to notice it. It's the night, when he falls asleep and you're still awake, or the hours when he's out and you're alone at home. For a woman who has a center, that time is hers, and it's good. She reads, rests, busies herself with what she feels like, is with herself without discomfort. For you, that time is usually anguish. You turn on the television so as not to feel the silence. You fiddle with your phone waiting for a message from him. You wander the house without direction, like someone who lost a piece and doesn't know which. Notice this without judging yourself, only as information. The way you live the hours when you have no one to mold yourself to tells you almost everything about how much you dissolved. Not to frighten you. To show you where, exactly, there is work to be done.

## 2. Giving yourself or dissolving yourself

At the source of all of this, two waters are mistaken for one. And I want to separate them carefully, because it's from there that your loss comes.

You confused giving yourself with dissolving yourself. As if to truly love were to renounce yourself. As if keeping a place of your own were not loving enough.

But they are opposite things.

To give yourself is two who give themselves to each other, and who continue, each one, to exist. They draw close, they share, they merge in many things, but they keep, each one, a center of their own, a place that is theirs alone.

To dissolve yourself is one who disappears inside the other. Who gives over her center entirely. Who ceases to have her own place, her own tastes, her own life, and becomes an extension of the one she loves.

In the first, there are two. In the second, there's one and a half.

And you do the second. You give over your whole place. You give, not a part of you, but you entirely, until no outline is left.

Notice what this dilution drains from you.

When you dissolve yourself, you stop bringing a person to the relationship. You bring a mirror. You agree with everything, you want what he wants, you adapt to everything, and at first that looks like harmony, looks like easy love, looks like you two are so compatible.

But it isn't compatibility. It's your absence. You two are not compatible. You're the one who disappeared, and where there were two people negotiating, diverging, meeting, there came to be only one, he, and your echo.

And a relationship with a mirror, however comfortable it seems, ends up emptying itself. Because the other, deep down, wants someone in front of him. Someone with their own will, with opinion, with life. Not an echo. And an echo is loved badly, however convenient it may be.

There's something more painful still. In dissolving yourself, you lose the capacity to be truly loved.

Because to love you, they have to see you. And if you disappeared, if there's no person there, only adaptation, then there's no one to love. He loves a reflection of himself, projected onto you. Not you, who ceased to be present.

The dissolution, which you do in the name of love, is precisely what keeps love from reaching you. Because love needs someone to reach, and you, from dissolving so much, ceased to be there to receive it.

I want to take you to the exact moment when the dissolution begins, because it tends to be silent and even sweet. It's the beginning of a relationship. The phase when everything is discovery. He likes a band, and you, who never heard it, put it on and discover that you like it. He prefers the mountains to the sea, and you, who were always a sea person, begin to think the mountains have something after all. At first it's genuine, it's openness, it's the beauty of someone who loves letting another person's world in. The problem is not that first step. It's that, in you, it never has a return. He enters your world, but your world doesn't enter his. The trade, which should go in both directions, goes in only one. And after months only his landscape is left, with your name on top.

There's a more subtle way still of disappearing. To want is to risk. Whoever wants can be opposed, can enter into conflict, can hear a no. You, to avoid all that, stopped wanting. And a person who wants nothing is a person who, slowly, ceases to be there.

I want you to see something that distinguishes dissolving yourself from loving a lot, because everyone will tell you that your problem is loving too much, and it isn't.

Whoever loves a lot, and is whole, gives immensely, and continues to be herself. She has will, opinion, life, and shares them generously. The lot that she gives comes from a full center, and so it doesn't empty her, it fills her.

Whoever dissolves herself doesn't give from a full center. She gives herself, her very center, until none is left. And that's not loving a lot. It's having so little center that, on meeting someone with a firm center, she dilutes herself in him, the way water dilutes in a glass.

The difference isn't in the quantity of love. It's in whether or not there's someone there, distinct, loving. In whole love, there are two people. In dissolution, there's one and her echo. And confusing the two makes you proud of a wound, thinking your capacity to disappear is the proof of your greatness in love, when it's, in truth, the mark of a center you never came to inhabit.

There's something else you might recognize, and it's one of the most subtle. You're not like this only in romantic love. You're like this with everyone strong who enters your life.

With a dominant friend, you become what she wants. With a boss, you dilute yourself into what she expects. With your family, you're whoever each one needs you to be. You adapt, always, to whoever has more center than you, because without your own center, you're absorbed by whoever has one.

Notice this, because it shows that the problem isn't this man, nor love. It's the lack of a ground of your own, firm, that doesn't dissolve in whoever is in front of you. And until you build that ground, you'll keep disappearing, in every relationship, in every group, in every place where there's someone stronger to absorb you.

The good news is that, since the root is the same, the healing also serves for everything. When you build a center of your own, you don't dissolve only less in love. You remain more yourself everywhere, with everyone, because you come to have, at last, a place from which you're not dragged away.

I want to undo one more misunderstanding that you probably carry, and that makes everything harder. You believe that your pliability is a virtue. That being easygoing, that not creating problems, that adapting to everything, is an elevated form of goodness. And that's how everyone praises you: you're so easy, with you everything is simple, you never cause any trouble. You learned to hear this as proof that you're a good person. But notice what that praise hides. No one praises you for who you are, because no one knows who you are. They praise you for disappearing well. For not weighing on anyone, for not taking up space, for not demanding. It's praise for your absence, not for your presence. And a whole life spent collecting praise for one's own absence is a life in which you never actually came to be present before anyone. When you begin to appear, some of that praise will end. Let it end. In exchange, you'll gain something you never had: that they love you for being there, and not for not getting in the way.

## 3. Who am I when I'm alone

I'm going to lay a question here, and I ask you to stay with it longer than you feel like. Don't let it drain away too quickly.

Who are you when you're alone?

Stay with it. Notice what it opens, or what it can't open.

For you, this question may bring a blank. Because you don't really know who you are when you're not with someone.

You know who you are with him. You know how to adapt, to merge, to be what the relationship asks. But you, alone, with no one to mold yourself to, who are you?

Which tastes are yours, and not borrowed? Which opinions are yours, formed by you? What gives you joy, you, when there's no one for whom the joy is convenient?

If these questions bring silence, it's not because you're empty. It's because your own center was never cultivated, or it was, and then given over, time after time, to those you loved.

And there's a wound hidden beneath all of this, which is precisely that: the absence of a center of your own. The feeling that, without someone to bind yourself to, there's nothing there, in the middle, that is firmly you.

That's why you dissolve yourself. Not from loving too much. From having so little center that, on meeting someone with a firm center, you dilute yourself in him, the way water dilutes in the glass that holds it.

Notice how this makes you terrified of solitude.

For many people, being alone is uncomfortable, but bearable. For you, it can be almost unbearable, because in solitude there's no one to bind yourself to, and without that bond you feel you disappear, that you cease to exist.

That's why you jump from relationship to relationship, with no interval. That's why you endure bad relationships, just so as not to be alone. That's why, when one ends, the panic isn't only longing, it's a kind of dissolution of yourself, as if without the other you yourself went out.

And here is the deep fragility. If you only exist in connection, then your existence is always in another's hands. You depend on someone's presence to feel real. And it's a terrible place, because it makes you a hostage to whoever stays, and in panic before whoever leaves.

Notice how this makes you accept things that, seen from outside, no one would accept. You stay with someone who treats you badly, because the alternative, being alone, seems worse than the mistreatment. You forgive the unforgivable, not out of greatness, but because your fear of being left with no one is greater than your pain. You make excuses for behaviors that would hurt you to see happen to a friend. And it's not a lack of intelligence, nor a lack of self-worth in the sense it's usually spoken of. It's that, without a center, being alone is not discomfort, it's annihilation. And a person who feels she'll cease to exist if she's left alone does everything, even what destroys her, so as not to be left. That's why building a center of your own is not a luxury, nor a whim of self-esteem. It's what gives you back the possibility of choosing, instead of merely grabbing whatever there is so as not to fall into the void.

I want to go deeper into the origin, because seeing it being born helps to release it.

There's an order in families worth knowing. Each person has the right to a place of their own, to a will of their own, to a center. In a healthy family, a child can disagree, have her own tastes, be different, and continue to belong. Growing is not betraying. Differentiating oneself is not abandoning.

When that isn't allowed, when having a center of one's own is lived as disloyalty, as an affront, as a danger to the bond, the child learns to give up her center to keep the tie.

Maybe you had a mother who needed you to be an extension of her, who couldn't bear that you had a will different from hers. Maybe you grew up where disagreeing brought conflict, coldness, or the withdrawal of love, and where peace was bought by erasing yourself, agreeing, dissolving your will into others'.

I want to give a name to a very common form of this, because maybe you'll recognize it without ever having thought of it this way. There are mothers who don't ask their daughter to do things. They ask her to feel what they feel. If the mother was sad, the whole house had to be sad, and a cheerful daughter was a betrayal. If the mother was anxious, the daughter had to share the anxiety, or she was accused of not caring. You grew up tuning yourself to her state, minute by minute, like an instrument that never plays its own note. And you learned, without words, that feeling something different from the one you love is dangerous. You carried that into adult love. That's why you walk into a room and read his state first. It's not a flaw carved into you. It's an old training, that began long before you met this man.

A child like that learns a terrible equation. To have the bond, disappear. To be loved, don't be too much. To stay, dilute yourself. And she chooses, always, the bond, because for a child the bond is life. She gives up herself so as not to be alone. And that trade, made early, becomes her way of loving forever.

If this is what you learned, notice: you didn't choose your loves badly. You applied, in all of them, the only equation you knew. To have the love, disappear. And you disappeared, time after time, thinking that was the price of love.

It wasn't. It was the price of a love of origin that didn't let you exist. And that price, you can stop paying it to those who, today, might want you precisely whole.

I want you to look at this without loading yourself with guilt, and without needing to point a finger at whoever raised you. Whoever didn't let you have a center of your own, almost always, didn't have one either. They repeated, in you, what had been done in them. They didn't mean to erase you. But they erased you, without meaning to, and you carry it still, in the way you disappear in every love.

Don't answer right away the question of who you are alone. It isn't answered in one pouring. It's built, slowly, as you cultivate a center that is yours alone.

But leave it open. Because it's in that question that your return begins. The day there's a firm you, even without anyone at your side, you stop needing to dissolve yourself in order to love. And then, only then, can you love whole. That blank is not your truth. It's your center yet to be built, waiting to be, at last, inhabited.

## 4. The love that erases

We reach the deepest water now. And I want you to wade in slowly.

You love by erasing yourself. For you, loving and disappearing have become the same thing.

As if love demanded the sacrifice of yourself. As if, to fit in the other, you had to dissolve yourself. As if the proof of love were how much of yourself you're willing to lose.

And there's a belief here, old and painful. The belief that you, whole, with your will, your tastes, your center, are too much. That to be loved you have to shrink, to dilute yourself, to not take up too much space.

That's why, the moment you enter a relationship, you begin to erase yourself. To give in. To agree. To disappear. Not because the other demands it, often, but because you learned that this is how love is guaranteed: by not bothering, not diverging, not being too much.

I want you to feel how much of you this dilutes away. Imagine what it is to not be able to exist and love at the same time. To have to choose between being yourself and being loved. And to always choose love, at the cost of yourself, until you no longer know who you are.

It's an extremely high price. You pay with your entire identity for your staying in a relationship. And even so you don't rest, because a part of you knows that, if you disappeared in order to be loved, then it isn't you who's being loved, it's your erasure.

There's a trap here worth seeing in the clear light of day. Your erasure never gives you the security it promised. You erase yourself thinking that this way he'll never leave you, that a woman so easy to love, so without rough edges, so always available, is a woman no one abandons. But the truth is the opposite. You live in permanent alert, always measuring his mood, always afraid you said something too much, always correcting yourself. The erasure doesn't take away your fear of losing him. It multiplies it. Because whoever disappeared to be loved knows, deep down, that one instant of reappearing is enough for the castle to fall. And no one lives in peace on such fragile ground.

Let me trace this back to its spring. Almost always it rises from a love of origin that left no place for you.

Where your will was a problem. Where having your own opinion was a challenge. Where the way to be loved, or at least tolerated, was to be what others needed you to be, and never what you were.

A child like that learns a terrible equation. I, whole, am not lovable. Erased, yes. And she comes to erase herself, her whole life, in exchange for crumbs of bond.

And today, as an adult, in love, she repeats it. She erases herself. She dissolves herself. She gives over her place. Because she learned that her place, occupied, pushes away, and that only empty, moldable, without outlines, can she be kept close.

I want to show you a particularly painful way in which this belief takes its revenge on you. When, rarely, he does something that hurts you, you don't get angry. You look for what you did wrong. You turn your own behavior over in search of the flaw that provoked his coldness, his impatience, his forgetting. You became the only one responsible for everything that goes wrong between you, because admitting that he erred would be to bring yourself forward, with a right to complain, with the place of someone who also counts. And that place, on your map, is forbidden. That's why you prefer guilt. Guilt, at least, keeps you erased and safe. Indignation, that one, would make you appear, and appearing frightens you more than suffering.

There's another place where this erasure becomes especially cruel, and it's inside your own body. A woman who dissolves into the other usually loses, along with her identity, access to her own physical signals. You stop knowing whether you're hungry, because you eat at his rhythm. You stop knowing whether you're tired, because your body learned to hold on until he wants to rest. In desire itself, you stop knowing whether you want, because you got used to being available when he wants, without asking yourself whether you want too. The body is the last territory of identity, the most intimate, and when even it comes to respond to the other and not to you, the dissolution has reached the bone. Reappearing often begins here, in the simplest and the most yours: going back to asking the body what it wants, and daring to answer it.

But notice this hard and liberating truth. The love that demands you disappear is not love. It's something else. And real love, that one, wants you present, with will, with center, whole. It doesn't want you erased. It wants you there. And the day you find, or build, a love that wants you whole, you'll discover that you never had to disappear, ever, and that the disappearance, far from making you more lovable, made you, only, more absent.

## 5. When disappearing is danger

Here the current has to be held back. With seriousness.

There's a border between dissolving yourself a little in love and truly losing yourself. And it's vital to know which side of it you're on.

On one side is everything we've been talking about. You dilute yourself, you give over your center, you lose yourself in the other. It hurts, but there's a path, and the path is these pages.

On the other side, the dilution has gone past the point of return. And I want to name it plainly, without letting it slip past.

If you let drop everything that was yours, and today you have no life at all outside the relationship. No friends, no activities, no network, only him.

If you isolated yourself, or were isolated, from everything that reminded you who you were before him.

If you lost your identity to such an extent that you no longer know what you feel, what you want, what you think, without consulting what he would feel, would want, would think.

If the idea of being alone brings you a physical panic, a sense that without him you don't survive, don't function, aren't.

If you depend on him for everything, and that dependency already takes away your capacity to live on your own.

This is no longer only a dilution to understand. It may be a serious emotional dependency, a loss of identity that asks for help. And help with firm ground beneath it, from a professional.

And I want to say one thing with all firmness. If your dissolution was accompanied by someone who kept you from your own, who controls you, who decides for you, who doesn't let you have a life of your own, that is not love, and it may be a relationship of control, or abuse.

There, understanding isn't enough, and it isn't the priority. What matters first is keeping you from washing away entirely. Seek help. Talk with someone you trust, with a professional, with a support line. You're not crazy, and you're not alone.

I want to give you a concrete way to orient yourself, because the border isn't always felt, and dissolution is skilled at disguising itself as love. Ask yourself three questions, and answer slowly. If you disappeared from this relationship tomorrow, would any life be left to you? Someone to call, somewhere to go, some taste to return to? Second question. When was the last time you were with someone who wasn't him, without having to explain, without having to ask, without guilt? And the third, the hardest. If you wanted to leave, could you? Do you have money of your own, somewhere to stay, people to turn to, or is everything tied to him? If these questions bring you an emptiness, or a physical fear, don't stay alone with the answer. Take it to a professional. There are answers that should not be held in silence.

I want you to see what your dissolution does, too, to the relationship, because it may surprise you.

You dissolve yourself thinking that this way you love more, that you give yourself entirely, that you give everything. But notice what the other receives. He doesn't receive a person. He receives a mirror. Someone who agrees with everything, who wants what he wants, who brings no divergence, no will, no outline.

There's a cruel paradox here. You erase yourself to hold him, and in erasing yourself you make the very person he fell in love with disappear. He fell in love with you, with your tastes, your life, your way of being. And you, in dissolving yourself into him, make that person disappear, and leave, in her place, an echo he didn't choose.

And there's an irony deeper still, that it matters to see. Often, the coldness you so fear in him is born, without either of you noticing, from your own absence. He doesn't grow cold because you stopped pleasing him. He grows cold because there's no longer anyone there to challenge him, to surprise him, to resist him a little. Desire needs a border, an other who isn't merely his reflection. When you dissolve yourself, you dissolve too what kept him alive near you. And then, on feeling the coldness, you erase yourself even more, trying to win him back, when it's exactly the opposite that would bring him back. It's the cruelest cycle of dissolution: the remedy you choose is the disease.

I want to stop here a moment, because there's a point at which this stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding.

If you recognize yourself in the signs I named, it's this chapter I want you to remember above all. Seek support. It's not weakness. It's the beginning of your return to yourself. Understanding where your tendency to disappear comes from is precious, but it doesn't treat an established dependency, nor does it protect you from a relationship that annuls you. For that, hands closer than mine are needed.

## 6. Loving without abandoning myself

If you confused loving with disappearing, then there's a new truth waiting for you. Read it slowly.

You can love without abandoning yourself.

You can give yourself to someone while continuing to exist. You can have a we without losing your I. You can love immensely and, at the same time, remain whole.

I know this sounds almost impossible to you. Your map says that to love is to merge, that to fit in the other you have to dilute yourself, that keeping a place of your own is loving less.

But your map was drawn in a place where existing and being loved didn't fit at the same time. It was accurate then. It's not a truth about love.

Mature love is not two who merge into one. It's two who remain two, and who meet. The distance between them is not a lack of love. It's the space where love happens, where one can look at the other, desire him, choose him, because there's another there, and not an extension of oneself.

When you dissolve yourself, you kill precisely that space. You stick yourself so closely that there are no longer two to meet. And without two, there's no meeting, no desire, no true love. There's fusion, which looks like love, but which is, deep down, the disappearance of one of the people.

Let me pour the same truth from another vessel. The other fell in love with a person. With you, with your tastes, your life, your way of being, your center. When you dissolve yourself into him, you make precisely the person he fell in love with disappear. Your dissolution, which serves to guarantee love, is what, slowly, empties it.

I want to tell you something about desire, because it's where dissolution charges a price that few women connect to their loss of self. Desire needs mystery, and mystery needs an interior the other doesn't know in full. When you dissolve yourself, you keep nothing for yourself. You anticipate what he wants, you give before you're asked, you're always available, always predictable, always there. And desire, which lives on the tension between two worlds, goes on fading, not from a lack of love, but from a lack of two people. Whoever returns to herself goes back to having an interior of her own, a world that isn't given over all at once. And, without wanting to, she becomes desirable again, because she becomes an other again.

The awakening, when it comes, doesn't break like a flood. It arrives like a reunion.

It's the day you go back to doing something that was only yours. That you say an opinion that's yours, and not his. That you spend time with your friends, in your world, and feel, with surprise, that there's still a person there, beneath the dilution. You.

And you notice that, far from moving you away from love, that return to yourself gives something back to the love. Something to offer. Someone to be loved. Because a relationship only breathes when there are two, and in reappearing, you give the relationship back the second person it had lost.

It's a small instant. But it changes everything. Because it disproves, with the force of what you felt, the law that to love you have to disappear. And from it on, even if the old habit pulls you back toward dissolution, you already know there's another path, and that it doesn't take love away from you, it gives you back yourself inside it.

I want to warn you about one thing, because it's going to happen when you begin to reappear. You're going to feel selfish.

Each time you say what you want, that you recover a space of your own, that you disagree, a guilt will rise. "I'm being difficult. I'm ruining the harmony. I'm putting myself first."

That guilt is the old equation defending itself. For you, having a center was always synonymous with selfishness, because that's how they taught it to you. But existing is not selfishness. It's health.

A woman with a center is not selfish. She's a person. And a relationship needs two people, not a person and a mirror. To reappear is not to take love away from the other. It's to bring, at last, someone for him to love.

I want to anticipate for you the exact form that guilt is going to take, because knowing it beforehand takes away half its force. It won't arrive as a voice of yours. It'll arrive disguised as generosity, as goodness, even as love. It'll tell you that you're being demanding, that others have bigger problems, that it costs nothing to give in one more time. It'll make it seem that reappearing is a whim, and that erasing yourself is maturity. Don't believe it. That voice doesn't want your good. It wants your disappearance, because it's the only terrain it knows. When you hear it, thank it for the old protection, and make, even so, the gesture that brings you back.

There's something else you're going to feel. Fear that, in reappearing, you'll discover that you two, after all, want different things.

While you were dissolving yourself, there was no divergence, because there weren't two people. In reappearing, differences will emerge that were covered by your absence. And that frightens, because difference brings conflict, and conflict, in your map, threatens the bond.

But difference is not the end of love. It's the beginning of true love, the love made between two who are not the same and who, even so, choose each other. A love without any difference is not harmony. It's proof that one of the people disappeared.

And I want to give you a sign to guide you, because your head is going to want to complicate things. In a healthy relationship, you feel more yourself, not less. The right love expands you, doesn't shrink you. It makes your center grow, not dissolve. If, with someone, you feel yourself disappearing, shrinking, losing yourself, that's information. It's not proof that you love a lot. It's proof that you're paying for the love with your existence, and no love worth having charges that price.

## 7. Returning to yourself inside love

There's a single verb that carries you across these waters. To return.

To return to yourself. Not outside love, but inside it. To remain with yourself while you love.

And I want to show you what this means, in practice, without making this into a technique.

Returning begins by recovering what you gave away. Your tastes. Your friends. Your places. Your activities. Everything you let drop, one by one, in diluting yourself.

Not against him. In favor of you. To go back to having a life that's yours, beyond the relationship. A world where you exist without being in function of someone.

To return is also to go back to having an opinion. To disagree, when you disagree. To want what you want, even when it differs from what he wants. To bring back the person you had erased in order to fit.

To return is, also, to go back to feeling what you feel, and not what he feels. To have an inner state that's yours, that doesn't collapse when his collapses. That's built slowly, by noticing, over the course of the day, what you feel, separate from what he feels, until you have again an inside that's yours alone.

It will frighten you. You'll fear that, in going back to occupying your place, the relationship will shudder. That he won't like the woman with a will of her own. That having a center will cost you the love.

And maybe it really will shudder, at first. A relationship used to a mirror finds strange the person who appears where there was an echo. But notice: if the relationship only held up your erasure, then it wasn't you it loved. And it's better to know.

Almost always, though, the opposite happens. The other, who'd been loving an echo without knowing it, finds a person again, and the relationship gains life, depth, desire. Because there come to be two, and two is where love breathes.

I want to give you an image of what it is, concretely, to begin to reappear, because said in the abstract it seems too big, and in truth it's done in the smallest things. Reappearing can be, on one day, choosing the restaurant, even if your hands tremble saying the name. On another, it's keeping a plan with a friend even though he'd prefer you stayed. On another still, it's leaving a series unfinished because, after all, you weren't enjoying it, and saying so out loud. They are small gestures on purpose. You don't need a revolution. You need one preference a day, recovered and spoken, until the muscle of wanting, which atrophied from so much disuse, slowly goes back to having strength.

I want to tell you about something that's called, simply, having a ground of your own. It's the opposite of dissolving yourself.

To have a ground of your own is to have a life that sustains itself in you, and not in the other. Friends of yours. A job, or a project, that's yours. Interests, places, people, that exist independently of the relationship.

Not to defend yourself from love. But so that love is a choice, and not a dependency. Whoever has a ground of her own loves out of wanting. Whoever doesn't loves out of needing, and love out of need is never free.

Notice: when you have a ground of your own, the relationship becomes lighter, not more distant. Because you stop putting on the other the impossible weight of being your entire life. He doesn't have to be everything. You already have a life. He's the love inside it, not its only content.

And there's a liberation in this for him too. No one can endure, for very long, being someone's entire life. It's a crushing weight. When you recover your ground, you relieve him of that weight, and your love breathes.

To build a ground of your own is not to move away. It's to become someone who can love without drowning. And that, far from threatening the relationship, is what can give it, at last, a future.

I want to anticipate for you a trap of this phase, because it's easy to fall into it without noticing. When you begin to recover your life, the old habit is going to try to turn that, too, into a form of pleasing. You'll want to choose friends he approves of, projects he thinks well of, tastes that don't bother him. Notice: that's not a ground of your own. It's dissolution dressed as return. A ground of your own is what exists whether he likes it or not. It doesn't have to be against him, but it also can't depend on his approval to exist. The question that guides you is not "does he think it's good?", but "is this mine?". And it costs to ask that question, because for years the only one you knew how to ask was the first. But it's in the second that your freedom lives.

I want to leave you, too, a tender warning about pace. Don't try to return to everything at once. A woman who spent years erasing herself and who, suddenly, decides to reappear in force, usually frightens herself with her own courage and retreats back behind the starting point. The return is done slowly, with tolerance for the days when you give in again, when you say "whatever" again, when you catch yourself molding yourself. Those days don't erase the others. They're part of the path. A center is built the way any muscle is built: with repetition, with rest, and with the patience of someone who knows that strength doesn't come in a single effort, but in the sum of many small ones.

I want to leave you, also, a joy that's on the other side. The joy of being loved whole. Of someone seeing you, you, with your tastes, your life, your will, and staying. Of not disappearing in order to be wanted, but of being wanted for appearing. Of being in a relationship of two, and not of one and a half. That joy is your right, and it's on the other side of the fear, on the day you reappear, and discover that real love didn't want you erased, it wanted you there.

And notice what you teach, if there are those who watch you love. If there are daughters, nieces, girls who observe you, they learn love by watching you love. If they see you disappear in every relationship, dissolve yourself, lose yourself, they learn that this is how you love, and they carry that lesson into their own loves. The most liberating thing you can do for the one who learns by looking at you is to show a woman who loves without erasing herself. Who remains whole inside love. Who is, herself, the proof that you can love and continue to exist.

I won't hand you a set of rules to dissolve into. It would be one more way of dissolving yourself, now into some rules. But I leave you the place where the river starts. Remain with yourself while you love him. Don't disappear in order to fit. Bring yourself whole to the relationship, and let love be between two people, and not the dilution of one into the other. And where you need help to build a center that was never cultivated, seek it, because no one raises a ground of their own from nothing, alone, overnight.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't bring you back to the surface all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, reappear whole. But it changes enough for you to stop mistaking your disappearance for love.

Now you know that you dissolve yourself in love, and that this isn't loving too much, it's a lack of you.

You know the difference between giving yourself and dissolving yourself. Between two who meet and one who disappears in the other.

You know who you are when you're alone, or you know that this question brings a blank, and that this blank is your center yet to be cultivated.

You know that you love by erasing yourself, and that you learned this in a place where existing and being loved didn't fit together.

You know how to distinguish dilution from the dangerous loss of yourself, and to seek help if that's the case.

And you know that you can love without abandoning yourself. That the verb is to return. To remain with yourself while you love.

I want, before the end, to give you back a concrete hope, so you don't leave these pages with only the diagnosis. Women who dissolve themselves have, almost always, a capacity that few people have: they know how to love with a rare devotion, to feel the other with a subtlety many people never reach, to give themselves without reserve. None of this is to be thrown away. It's a gift. What happened is that this gift, without a center to hold it, turned against you and became disappearance. But the day you have a ground of your own, that same capacity for devotion, now from a whole place, will make you capable of a love that most people don't even imagine. You won't lose your tenderness in reappearing. You'll, finally, have it to offer with full hands, without it costing you your existence. Your sensitivity is not the problem. The problem was that there was no one to feel it from within. And that changes.

And there's a care I want to leave you, about time. To build a center that never existed isn't done in a day, nor with a book. You're going to relapse, you're going to dissolve yourself again, you're going to lose yourself again. It's not failure. It's the old habit still being there, while the new one is built. What transforms, even in the relapses, is that now you see. And seeing, even in the middle of the dissolution, is the beginning of being able, one day, to return more quickly. Be patient with yourself, and where you need hands to help you raise that ground, accept them.

I want to leave you with water in your hands.

You're like a river that, on reaching the sea, gives itself so entirely that it ceases to be a river. It loses the name, the bank, the course. It dissolves into the salt water and disappears.

But there's another way for the river to meet the sea. To meet it while continuing to be a river to the end. To carry its fresh water to the sea without ceasing to be who it is along the way. To mingle, yes, but bringing something of its own, a current, a name, a life.

The crossing is that. Not to cease reaching love. But to arrive there whole, with your course, your name, your center. To mingle without disappearing. To love without ceasing to be a river.

And there's one last distinction that protects everything. To return to yourself is not to become cold, closed, or selfish in love. It's not to love less. It's to love from a whole place, instead of a dissolved place. You keep giving yourself, surrendering, drawing close, as much as before, or more. But you bring, to the relationship, a person, and not an echo. And a whole person loves better than a dissolved one, because there is, in fact, someone there, present, loving. To return doesn't move you away from love. It gives you back to it, at last, as two people, and not as one and her shadow.

The sentence that holds everything I've told you here fits in a line, and I want you to carry it with you. I can remain with myself while I love you.

And there's a step that these pages can't take for you, because it isn't done by reading. It's done by recovering, slowly, what you gave away. By reappearing where you had erased yourself. And, if there's someone at your side, by learning, together, to be two, and not one and a half. That's the work that continues after you close the book. The book names. The crossing is crossed with your feet, in daily life, one gesture at a time.

And I ask you one thing only, now, before you close these pages. A hard one, for a woman so used to dissolving.

This week, do something that's only yours. That has nothing to do with him, with the relationship, with pleasing someone. Something that you, alone, like. And notice what you feel. Maybe guilt, maybe strangeness, maybe a panic of separating yourself. Stay with it, and do it anyway. Because it's in that gesture only yours, kept despite the fear, that your return begins, and that you discover, at last, that there's still someone there. You.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
