# The woman who chases the next mountain

**For the woman who already knows the goal is never enough, and yet always needs the next one. Because the addiction is not the goal, it is the movement.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks about lives in permanent movement, and movement without pause has a real cost. If you recognize in yourself a restlessness that does not rest even after success, an inability to stop, persistent anxiety, or an exhaustion that no longer lifts, this may be more than the pace of life, and it deserves the care of a professional. Stopping in time, and letting yourself be helped, is not giving up. It is the wisest thing you can do for yourself.*

---

## 1. The moment I arrive, I am already looking at the next one

There is something that always happens to you, and that you perhaps no longer even notice, so much your own has it become. The moment you reach a thing, the moment you set foot on the summit you fought for, your gaze is already searching for the next mountain. You never even get to sit down on what you conquered. You never even get to breathe the air of the top. The instant when you should be savoring the arrival is, for you, the instant when you already begin to plan the next departure. As if arriving were merely the sign that it is time to look for another place to go.

And perhaps you have already noticed a cruel detail: the higher you climb, the shorter the joy. The first achievements, years ago, still gave a pleasure that lasted a few days. Today, you reach far greater things and the pleasure lasts hours, or minutes, or not even that. It is as if your capacity to feel the arrival were wearing out, and you had to climb higher and higher to feel less and less. This is not ingratitude on your part nor a lack of capacity to enjoy. It is the sign that what you are looking for is not at the top, and that each time you go to fetch it at the top and do not find it, you become a little more convinced, deep down, that no top will ever be enough, and just as dependent, all the same, on continuing to climb. The diminishing of pleasure as the achievement grows is the signature of flight, and you, if you are honest, recognize it.

It is not that you cannot arrive. You do arrive, and you reach high places, places many people never reach. The problem is not the arrival. It is that the arrival does not detain you. It does not give you rest, it does not give you a place to stay, it does not give you the feeling of "this is it, I have arrived, I can stop." Instead, it gives you an immediate restlessness, an almost physical need to already be on your way to something else. And so your life has become a succession of summits, always climbing, always reaching, always departing again, without ever inhabiting any of the places you reached.

And here is something that sets you apart, and that we are going to look at carefully throughout these pages: you no longer believe, deep down, that the next mountain is going to give you what you are looking for. You have already reached enough to know that the top does not bring what it seemed to promise. You have lived enough times the anticlimax of arrival, the emptiness that comes right after victory, to no longer fool yourself with the idea that this time will be different. And even so you continue. Even knowing that the mountain does not resolve anything, you always need one. You keep climbing, not because you believe in the top, but because you do not know what stopping is like.

And perhaps you can already make out these scenes, the way one picks out trails on a slope. It is finishing a huge project and, instead of the satisfaction you deserved, feeling an almost immediate emptiness, a strange sadness, and throwing yourself straight at the next idea so as not to feel it. It is reaching a place you wanted for years, and finding yourself, weeks later, already bored, already looking for the next challenge, as if the place had lost its shine the moment you touched it. It is being unable to be on holiday without planning the next thing, without feeling restless, without needing an objective even for the rest. It is looking at everything you have already reached and, instead of pride, feeling a "and now, what's next?" They are all symptoms of the same thing: an inability to rest in what is, a permanent need for what comes next.

And there is a particular loneliness in this, that few people around you understand. Because from the outside they see a woman of admirable energy, always reaching, always growing, and they envy you, perhaps, that strength. They do not see the tiredness of someone who can never stop, the restlessness that does not rest, the feeling of being on a treadmill that never stops. They do not see that, behind the woman who seems to have everything and to conquer everything, there is someone who cannot simply sit down and be, who does not know how to inhabit her own life, who lives always a step ahead of herself, in the next project, in the next goal, without ever reaching the present. That loneliness, of not being understood precisely in the thing that is hardest for you, is one of the most silent there is.

I want you to know, right from the start, that this book is not about reining in your ambition, nor about telling you that wanting more is a problem. It is not. There is an energy in you that is real and that is beautiful, a capacity to move, to create, to arrive, that many people would like to have. What I came to look at with you is not that energy. It is something else: the reason why that energy no longer takes you anywhere where you can stay. The reason why, having everything you need to rest in what you have built, you cannot, and you always have to set off again.

There is an image that may help you feel what is happening. Imagine someone in a boat rowing with all their strength, convinced they are rowing toward an island. And the island, whenever they approach it, reveals itself to be a mirage, and another appears in the distance, and the person rows toward that one, and that one also dissolves, and there is always another. At a certain point, the person no longer truly believes in any island. But they keep rowing, with the same strength, because stopping rowing brings a greater terror than the disappointment of the islands: the terror of being still in the middle of the water, with no destination, alone with the sea and with themselves. They row, then, not to arrive, but so as not to have to stop. You are that person. And this book does not come to tell you the islands do not exist, nor to take away your oars. It comes to ask you whether you could, for a moment, set down the oars and be still in the middle of the water, and discover that the boat does not sink when you stop, and that the sea, which you feared so much, holds you up.

Because there is a difference between moving out of desire and moving because you cannot stop. Between choosing a new mountain because it calls you, and needing a new mountain because without one you do not know how to exist. And it is that second thing, that dependence on movement itself, that we are going to look at head on. Not to take away your strength, but for you to discover whether it is you who uses it, or it that uses you. And for you to find, perhaps for the first time, the possibility of existing without always having to be departing.

## 2. I already know it doesn't work

There is a sibling book to this one, about the woman who never arrives, and the difference between her and you is subtle but important, and it is worth seeing, because it explains why the usual advice does not serve you. That woman runs after the next goal because she believes in it. She tells herself "when I get there, I will finally feel it." She has hope. You no longer have it. You already know you will not feel it, because you have arrived so many times and not felt it that you have lost the illusion. And it is here that your situation is, in a way, deeper: because you keep running without even the excuse of hope.

Notice the difference, because it changes the diagnosis. Whoever runs with hope is pursuing a destination: they believe there is a place where everything is resolved, and they move toward it. You are not pursuing a destination, you are fleeing from stillness. Your problem is not believing too much in the goal, it is not being able to bear staying still. The goal, for you, is not even the point anymore. It is only an excuse to keep moving, because it is the movement, and not the destination, that gives you the feeling of being alive, of existing, of being who you are.

That is why changing goals never resolves anything for you, although it is always your temptation. When a mountain disappoints you, and they all disappoint you, your response is not to stop and ask what is happening. It is to look for another mountain, higher, harder, more exciting, convinced that the problem was that particular mountain and not the race itself. And you throw yourself in with renewed energy, and for a while the novelty fills you, and then you arrive, and the emptiness returns, the same, and you look for the next one. You change mountains without ever questioning the act of climbing. And since the problem was never in the mountain, changing mountains never resolves it.

And it is important to understand that novelty is your favorite drug, because it is the one that best disguises the flight. Each new mountain brings with it a phase of enthusiasm, of learning, of discovery, in which you feel alive, absorbed, full. And you interpret that enthusiasm as proof that this one is, finally, the right mountain. But notice the pattern: the enthusiasm is always greater at the start and always disappears as you master the thing, until, once you have mastered it, it loses its charm and you need another. It is not the mountain that runs out, it is the novelty. You are not addicted to success, you are addicted to the beginning, to the new phase, to the launch, because that is where the movement is most intense and the flight most effective. And a life organized around novelty is condemned to start over forever, without ever deepening anything, because deepening requires permanence, and permanence is precisely what you cannot bear.

I want to tell you this without any judgment, because there is nothing wrong with you here, there is only a pattern you have not yet seen clearly. You are not an insatiable person by defect, nor a success addict out of vanity. You are a woman who, somewhere, learned that she was only well in movement, and who built an entire life on that learning, with so much talent that it even worked, for a long time, in everyone's eyes and perhaps even in your own. It worked, in the sense that it took you far. Only it took you far from everyone, including yourself, because a person who never stops never truly gets to be with anything or anyone, not even herself.

There is a word for this, and it helps to see clearly: what you have is not desire, it is dependence. Desire wants something, obtains it, and rests, satisfied. Dependence does not truly want the thing, it wants the state the thing produces, and so it never rests, it always needs the next dose. You are not dependent on a particular goal, you are dependent on the state of being in movement, on the adrenaline of the climb, on the feeling of moving forward. And like any dependence, it works the opposite way to pleasure: it does not give you satisfaction, it gives you temporary relief from a discomfort, the discomfort of stopping, and it demands larger and larger doses for the same relief that grows smaller and smaller. That is why you need higher and higher mountains to feel the same, and why the interval between them becomes more and more intolerable.

And recognizing this as dependence, and not as ambition, is not a humiliation, it is a liberation, because it completely changes what there is to do. If it were ambition, the solution would be to choose your goals better, and you have already tried that a thousand times without result. Being a dependence on movement, the solution is another: it is not to change mountains, it is to learn to tolerate stillness, to detoxify slowly from the need to move forward, to discover that one can exist without the next dose. It is a different work, deeper, but it is the only one that touches the root, instead of feeding the pattern with one more climb.

And that is why recognizing "I already know it doesn't work" is, paradoxically, good news. Because it means you no longer need to spend energy chasing an illusion. You have seen, in the flesh, that the top does not give what it promised. You only lack the next step, which is the hardest of all for you: instead of looking for another mountain, to stop, and to look at the thing you are, in truth, fleeing from. It is to that place that this book wants to take you, slowly, with care, because I know it is precisely the place where you never pause.

## 3. I only know how to exist in movement

Let us now descend to the root, and the root is not in your goals, it is in your identity. Because the truth is this: you built who you are on movement. Not on a goal, not on a place of arrival, but on the very fact of always moving forward, expanding, growing, going farther. You became the woman who is always in movement, and that image, that being permanently on the way, is not only what you do, it is what you feel you are.

See how this appears even in the language you use about yourself. You define yourself by what you are doing, by the projects under way, by what you are going to reach next, rarely by who you are at rest. When someone asks how you are, you answer with what you are doing. When you imagine the future, you imagine achievements, never a life simply lived. When you think about who you are, the answer always comes in verbs of action, never in states of being. It is as if, without the movement, there were no noun, no person, only a series of actions in progress. And that is the mark of an identity built on doing: the inability to say who one is without saying what one does. Relearning to exist as a noun, as someone who is, and not only as someone who does, is a deep part of this crossing.

Notice what happens when you imagine stopping. You do not feel relief, you feel threat. Stopping, for you, is not rest, it is a kind of small death, as if by ceasing to move you ceased to exist. That is why, even exhausted, you do not stop. That is why, the moment one thing ends, you immediately need another, not out of greed, but because the interval between two mountains, that empty space in which there is nothing to conquer, is for you unbearable. It is a space where you are confronted with a question you do not know how to answer: if I am not moving forward toward anything, who am I? Do I exist, when I am not expanding?

This is the shadow of this book, and I want to name it with care: you only know how to exist in expansion. You learned to feel alive, valuable, real, through movement and growth, and so stillness frightens you as losing the ground would frighten anyone. When you are growing, conquering, expanding, you feel that you exist. When you stop, you feel that you disappear. And an identity built like this is condemned never to rest, because resting is, for it, ceasing to be. It is as if your existence were a bicycle that only stays upright while it moves, and that falls the moment it stops.

Notice too how this identity of movement makes you devalue everything you already are and already have. Because if you only exist in expansion, then what is still, consolidated, conquered, ceases to count, loses value in your eyes the instant it becomes stable. What you already have does not impress you, because it is no longer movement. Your qualities, your relationships, your achievements, everything that is secure and present, escapes you for being static, and your attention always turns to what is not yet, to what remains to be conquered. That is why you live with a chronic sense of insufficiency despite having so much: not because you lack things, but because you can only value movement, and what you already have, by being still, becomes invisible to you. Learning to see and to value what already is, what you already have, what you already are, is part of learning to inhabit, and it is the opposite of the gaze that only sees the next mountain.

And there is a particular exhaustion in this, different from the exhaustion of someone who works hard. It is the exhaustion of someone who can never stop, not because of the tasks, but because of who she has become. Because even when the body can no longer take it, even when everything in you asks for rest, the identity does not allow it, because stopping threatens it in its very existence. And so you keep moving, worn out, empty, fleeing a stillness that your being interprets as annihilation, when in truth that stillness is, precisely, the only place where you could finally find yourself.

And notice how this identity makes you strangely fragile, despite all your strength. Because if you are your movement, then any halt, any forced pause, any period in which you are not moving forward, threatens you in what you are. An illness that forces you to stop, a phase of life that asks for stillness, a moment in which there is simply no mountain in sight, become, for you, small identity crises, not merely inconveniences, but threats to your very sense of existing. That is why you are so afraid of stopping, of falling ill, of aging, of anything that reduces your movement: because it is not only the movement that is at stake, it is you, your entire existence, built on something that, by definition, cannot last forever. An identity that depends on always being in expansion is a condemned identity, because no human life expands indefinitely, and sooner or later life asks you to stop, and you do not know how.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because you are going to want to turn this into one more defect to correct, and that would be just one more mountain. The identity built on movement was not born from a failing of yours. It was born, almost always, from a real, old need, from a child who learned that she was loved when she moved forward, seen when she stood out, safe when she grew, and who therefore made movement her way of existing and of belonging. That child was not wrong, she was surviving the only way she knew. But you are no longer that child, and the movement that saved you then has become, now, the prison that does not let you stop. And getting out of it is not through running better. It is through learning, slowly, that you exist even when you do not move.

## 4. What lies beneath the restlessness

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you not to flee from it toward the next mountain, even though that is exactly your impulse.

What is it that you flee from when you cannot stop?

Sit with it the way you would sit on a ridge before the next ascent. Do not resolve it. Just stay.

Because your restlessness, the one that rests in nothing you reach, is not a hunger for more, it is a flight from something. Beneath the permanent need to move forward, there is a thing that the movement allows you not to feel, not to see, not to face. And while you move, you manage to keep it at a distance. But the moment you stop, in the silence of the interval between two mountains, it appears. And it is from it that you flee, mountain after mountain, without ever saying it, perhaps without ever knowing it. Your race is not so much toward the top. It is away from what awaits you in the stillness.

Notice that this is the deepest difference between this book and the one about the woman who never arrives. She still believes there is a top that resolves things, and so her cure is in seeing that the top is not the point. You no longer believe in the top, and even so you run, and so your cure is another, harder one: it is not giving up an illusion, it is facing a flight. She has to stop hoping. You have to stop fleeing. And stopping fleeing is harder than stopping hoping, because it requires that you look at the thing you flee from, instead of simply letting go of a vain hope. That is why this book takes you, more than the other, directly to the confrontation with stillness, because it is only there, and not in a new understanding about goals, that your race can finally stop.

And what awaits you in the stillness? For each woman it is a different thing, and only you can discover yours, but there are themes that repeat. Sometimes it is an emptiness, a feeling that, without the movement, there is nothing there, no ground, no meaning, and that emptiness terrifies. Sometimes it is an old sadness, a pain that was never felt because there was never time, and that the movement keeps buried. Sometimes it is a question about who one is, that frightens because it has no easy answer. Sometimes it is a loneliness, a missed encounter with oneself, that one only feels when everything else stops. Whatever it is, it is that, and not the next achievement, that is the true engine of your race. You are moved by the fear of being still with yourself.

And here is what changes everything: the fear of inhabiting is greater than the desire to conquer. You do not pursue the next mountain because you want it so much. You pursue it because staying still, inhabiting the present, being with yourself with nothing to do, frightens you more than any climb. Conquering, however tiring, is known territory, it is where you feel competent, in control, safe. Inhabiting is strange territory, it is where there is nothing to do, nothing to reach, nothing to prove, and where you find yourself, finally, alone with yourself. And it is that encounter, and not the lack of goals, that you have spent your life avoiding.

And there is often an old root in this, worth looking at carefully. For many women who live in permanent movement, stillness was, at some point in their story, dangerous. Perhaps you grew up in an environment where stopping meant being exposed to something difficult, where being still let in a pain, a conflict, a lack, and where keeping yourself busy, useful, in movement, was the way to survive, not to feel, not to be available to what hurt. If that was the case, your movement is not a whim, it is a survival strategy you learned early and that served you, and that you keep using long after the danger has passed. Your system still believes that stopping is dangerous, because once it was. Knowing this does not dismantle the race in an instant, but it removes the guilt, and it opens the possibility of showing, slowly, that part of you, that the time when you had to be always in movement to protect yourself has perhaps already passed.

I want to tell you something about the thing you flee from, because it changes the way you look at it. The thing that awaits you in the stillness is not as terrible as the fear paints it. The emptiness, the sadness, the question, the loneliness, are things that, looked at head on, with time and sometimes with company, can be crossed and transformed. What makes them monstrous is precisely the flight, because what is never faced grows in the imagination until it seems bigger than life. Many women discover, when they finally stop, that the thing they fled their whole life from was far less terrifying than the exhausting race they set up to avoid it. Stillness is not your enemy. It is only the place where the thing you have not yet wanted to see awaits you, and that, once seen, ceases to govern you.

## 5. When movement is flight

I have to stop here and make an important distinction, because I do not want you to leave this book thinking that all movement is illness and that wanting to grow is a problem. It is not. There is a movement that is life, and there is a movement that is flight, and the difference between the two is everything. Learning to distinguish them is one of the most important steps of this crossing.

Healthy movement is born from an overflowing. It is when you are full, whole, and what you have built inside wants to express itself in the world, to create, to expand, to go farther, not to fill yourself, but because you are already full and what is in you wants to come out. That movement has a different quality: it is not anxious, it is not compulsive, it lets you rest when you need to, and it can stop and savor what it reaches before continuing. It comes from desire, not from fear. It moves toward something, not away from something. And, above all, it is not afraid of stillness, because it is not fleeing from anything.

The movement that is flight is something else. It is born from an emptiness, not from an overflowing. It is when you move not because you are full, but because you cannot bear the feeling of what is there, or of what is not there, when you stop. That movement is anxious, compulsive, it does not let you rest, it does not let you savor anything, because its function is not to arrive anywhere, it is to keep you away from yourself. It comes from fear. It moves away from stillness. And it is recognized precisely in this: in the inability to stop, in the panic of the interval, in the restlessness that does not cease even when, objectively, you have already reached everything you wanted.

Notice that the same action, seen from the outside, can be one thing or the other, and only you, on the inside, know which. Two women can throw themselves into the same ambitious project: one does it because she overflows, with joy, able to stop if she wanted; the other does it because she panics if she has no project, unable to stop. From the outside, they look the same, two ambitious and capable women. On the inside, they are opposites: one lives, the other flees. That is why no one can tell you, looking from the outside, whether your movement is healthy or is flight, nor can you know it by looking at what you do. You only know it by looking at what you feel when you imagine stopping. The answer is not in the action, it is in the relationship you have with stillness.

How to know which of the two you are in? There is a question that helps, and that you can ask yourself honestly: if I could stop now, without losing anything, without guilt, without consequences, could I? Could I be still, present, with myself, without anxiety, savoring what I have? If the answer is yes, your movement is probably healthy, and wanting more is just your life expanding. If the answer is no, if the mere idea of stopping brings you restlessness, emptiness, or panic, then your movement is, at least in part, flight, and the next mountain is not a desire, it is a flight disguised as ambition.

And I want to add something important: the movement that is flight does not deceive only others, it deceives you, because it disguises itself as the most admirable things. It disguises itself as ambition, as dedication, as excellence, as passion for what one does. You can spend your life being praised for your energy, for your grit, for your capacity never to stop, and take that praise as proof that you are on the right path, when in truth you are being rewarded for your flight. It is one of the hardest things to see, because everything, the world, the culture, the people around you, conspires to tell you that your race is a virtue. But you feel, in your honest moments, that there is something that does not add up, that all that admiration does not reach the empty place, that beneath the tireless woman there is someone exhausted who would like to be able, just once, to stop without guilt. Listen to that part. It knows the truth that the applause drowns out.

And I want to be careful here, and serious, because there is a point at which this stops being a pattern to understand and becomes something that asks for help. The woman who lives in permanent movement is at real risk of exhaustion, of burnout, of clinical anxiety, and sometimes of a depression hidden beneath the productivity. If your restlessness has become a constant agitation that lets you neither sleep nor rest, if there is an anxiety that tightens your chest, if the exhaustion no longer lifts, if you have lost pleasure in everything and only the movement keeps you on your feet, then what you need is not another mountain nor only a change of perspective. You need care, professional support, someone who can help you stop safely. Reaching for that help is not the climber giving in; it is the climber who knows when the mountain has become too much to take alone. It is the most lucid gesture of someone who has already realized that running more will not save her.

## 6. Inhabiting frightens more than conquering

The verb of this crossing is to inhabit, and I want to present it with care, because for you it is, probably, more frightening than any mountain. To inhabit is to stay. It is to be in a place without using it as a springboard to the next. It is to exist in the present, in what already is, without the next achievement calling you. And for someone who built her life on movement, inhabiting does not sound like rest, it sounds like imprisonment, like stagnation, like disappearance.

And I want you to see why, for you, stopping seems so dangerous, more dangerous than any climb. It is that, while you move, you have the illusion of control. You are doing something, moving forward, building, and that gives you the feeling that you are in command, that you steer your life, that, if there is a problem, you solve it. Stopping means giving up that illusion, being left with nothing to do, exposed to what you do not control, to what simply is. For someone who feels safe in doing, that surrender frightens as letting go of the helm in the middle of a storm would frighten. But there is a truth that is only discovered when you let go: often the storm was only in the head of the one rowing, and the sea, when you stop fighting it, holds you up. The control that movement gives you is, in large part, an illusion, and letting it go does not leave you adrift, it leaves you, for the first time, afloat.

Notice this: you, who face mountains that would terrify most people, are afraid of being still. You, who have an enormous courage to move forward, to risk, to conquer, do not have the courage to stop. And this is not a contradiction, it is the key to everything: because conquering, however difficult, is your comfort zone, and inhabiting is your zone of terror. Conquering keeps you busy, competent, in control, away from yourself. Inhabiting exposes you to what the movement hides. That is why you prefer the highest mountain to the simplest couch, the hardest climb to the shortest silence. You are not less brave. You are brave in the wrong place, in the place that keeps you in flight.

And I want to name a trap specific to those who are very capable, because it is dangerous: you can turn anything into a mountain, including this. You can take this book, the idea of inhabiting, of stopping, of making peace with yourself, and turn it into one more project to be carried out with excellence, one more goal to be reached, "becoming a person who inhabits," with objectives, progress, self-assessment. And suddenly you are climbing the mountain of learning not to climb, running after stillness with the same compulsion as always. Notice this when it happens, and smile, with tenderness, because it is your old pattern trying to survive dressed up as the cure. Inhabiting is not one more thing to do well. It is, precisely, the place where there is nothing to do well, where you can be, without performance, without progress, without a goal. The day you stop trying to inhabit well and simply stay, you will have begun to inhabit for real.

Because inhabiting exposes. When you stop, when you stay, when you inhabit the present with nothing to conquer, everything the movement kept at a distance appears: the emptiness, the question, the sadness, the loneliness, whatever it is that awaits you in the stillness. And that is why inhabiting frightens more than conquering, because conquering is doing, and inhabiting is feeling, and you learned to do precisely so as not to have to feel. To inhabit is to set down all the tools, all the competence, all the race, and to stay, simply, with what is there. And what is there, avoided for so long, seems, at first, unbearable.

And I want you to know that the enthusiasm of the beginning, the one you chase, does not disappear when you learn to inhabit, it only changes form. There is a different joy, deeper, that is not in the beginning of things but in their permanence: the joy of deepening, of knowing better and better, of seeing grow over time what one tends with constancy. A relationship that lasts, a place one comes to know season after season, a work one masters and refines over years, give a joy that novelty can never give, because it is a joy of root, not of launch. You never knew it, because you always departed before letting it be born. To inhabit is, among other things, to discover that second joy, the joy of depth, that is given only to those who stay, and that is, in the end, richer and more lasting than the always brief euphoria of starting over.

And there is something that permanent movement robs you of, without your seeing it, and that is perhaps the most painful of all: your own life. Because life does not happen on the summits, it happens on the path, in the present, in the ordinary moments that you always cross in a hurry toward the next one. The people you love, the days that pass, the small joys, all of that lives in the present, and you live always a step ahead, in what comes next, and so you pass by your own life while you climb it. Your children, if you have them, grow while you look at the next goal. Relationships wear out with your present-but-distant absence. The years pass, full of achievements and empty of life lived. And one day, perhaps, you stop long enough to see that you reached everything and inhabited nothing, and that is a pain that no summit makes up for.

But there is something that is only discovered on the other side of the fear of inhabiting, and that I want you to know, even without being able to feel it yet: to inhabit is not to stagnate. It is not to give up living, growing, creating. It is to discover that one can exist without always departing, that there is a way of being alive that does not depend on movement, that the present, truly inhabited, is much richer than the race let you see. People who learn to inhabit do not cease to have a life, to do, to accomplish. But they begin to do it from a ground, from a center, from a place to which they can return, instead of an endless flight. And they discover that inhabiting, far from being the small death they feared, is where life truly happens, because life only happens in the present, and the present is only lived when one stops fleeing from it.

I am not going to ask you, in this book, to stop suddenly, to drop everything, to sit in the stillness and face all at once what you avoided your whole life. That would be violent, and it would be, in fact, one more mountain, the mountain of stopping well. Inhabiting is learned slowly, in small intervals borne, in short moments of stillness that gradually lengthen, and often with someone to accompany you, because what awaits you in the stillness is not always crossed alone. But the path begins with your seeing that your fear is not of stagnation, it is of the encounter with yourself. And that that encounter, which you fear so much, is precisely what you lack.

---

## 7. Inhabiting without conquering

If your movement is, in part, flight, and if inhabiting is what you lack, then the crossing has a clear direction, however much it costs: learning to exist without the next mountain. To inhabit where you are without needing to set off again. I am not going to map out a marked trail for you nor a summit to reach, for that would be just one more climb in disguise. I am going to point you in the direction, and the terrain is discovered on foot, slowly.

Begin by accepting that you are going to have to bear some discomfort, because inhabiting, at first, is uncomfortable for someone who only knows how to move. The first intervals of stillness will bring restlessness, will make you want to get up and do something, anything. That is normal, it is your pattern protesting. The path is not to eliminate that discomfort, it is to learn to stay with it a little longer each time, without fleeing, until it begins to subside. Each time you bear a moment of stillness without filling it, you teach your system, slowly, that stopping is not dying, that you can be still and continue to exist, that the interval between mountains is not an abyss, it is just a place where one can also live.

And I want to give you a concrete way to begin, without this becoming one more goal: begin with the interval. You do not have to drop all the mountains nor stop your life. You only have to learn to inhabit the intervals, those spaces between one thing and the next that you usually fill immediately. When you finish something, instead of leaping straight to the next, stay in the interval a little longer. Do not fill it in a hurry. Let it exist. Feel what appears in it, the restlessness, the emptiness, whatever it is, and stay. It is in the intervals, and not in a great dramatic halt, that inhabiting is trained, because the intervals already exist in your life, it is you who cross them at a run. Learning to linger in them, one at a time, is the gentlest and most real path to unlearning the flight.

Then, begin to separate your worth from your movement. This is the deepest part, because it touches your identity. Inhabiting requires that you discover that you are worth something even when you are not moving forward, that you exist even when you are not conquering, that you are someone even when you are not going anywhere. It is not an idea that one decides, it is an experience that one builds, moment by moment, each time you are still and the world does not end, each time you conquer nothing and continue to be you. Slowly, your existence comes unstuck from movement, and you discover a ground that does not depend on your always departing, a ground that was there beneath the whole race, waiting for you.

Then, learn to make peace with the idea that there are mountains you are not going to climb, and that this does not diminish you. For someone who lives in movement, each possibility not pursued seems a loss, a failure, a lesser life. But the truth is the opposite: a life that pursues everything never gets to live anything, and choosing not to climb certain mountains, letting them pass without guilt, is what allows you to truly inhabit the ones that matter, or simply to inhabit your life. You do not have to climb every mountain that appears just because you are capable. Your capacity is not an obligation. You can see a mountain, recognize that you would know how to climb it, and choose to stay where you are, not out of incapacity, but out of wisdom. That is a power the race never gave you: that of saying no to a climb, not because you cannot, but because you no longer need to.

And there is a small but revealing sign that you are learning to inhabit: you begin to be able to feel pleasure in what you already have, without the immediate impulse to use it as a base for the next leap. A coffee drunk without hurry, and that is just a coffee, not an interval between tasks. A conversation in which you are whole, without half of you in the next project. An evening in which you do nothing and do not feel you are wasting time. These moments, which for you were always almost impossible, are the ground where inhabiting is learned, and each of them, however small, is a greater victory than any summit, because it is a victory over the race, not within it. Notice them when they happen, and let them last. They are your body relearning that one can exist while still.

And there is an inhabiting that is the hardest and the most liberating: inhabiting what you have already conquered. You have, behind you, a life of summits reached that you never got to savor, because the moment you arrived you were already departing. To inhabit is to be able, finally, to return to them, not to conquer more, but to receive what is already done, to feel what you did not feel then, to let what you built give you, now, what the hurry did not allow. It is a form of reconciliation with your own life, with everything you did and never inhabited. And it is an immense richness, that was always there, waiting for you, on the inside of the stillness you feared so much.

And I want to tell you something about what you gain, and not only about what you let go of, because this crossing is not a loss, it is an exchange. You let go of the exhausting race, yes, but you gain your life back. You gain the capacity to be present with those you love, to savor what you conquered, to live the days instead of crossing them at a run. You gain a ground that does not depend on the next achievement, a peace that does not dissolve the moment it is reached, because it is not a summit, it is a place where one stays. You gain the possibility that your strength, which continues to be yours, comes to serve a life that you inhabit, instead of an endless flight. You do not become less. You become whole, for the first time, because you stop living always a step ahead of yourself and you begin to be where you are. And you discover that the woman who exists when she stops, far from being lesser than the one who runs, is more true, more profound, and finally reachable, for others and for you.

I do not promise you that this will be easy, nor fast, because unlearning a whole life of movement is not a thing of one page. But I promise you that it is possible, and that on the other side there is something the race could never give you: the peace of not having to set off again. The possibility of arriving and staying. Of existing without always being on the way. Of inhabiting your life, instead of climbing it endlessly. And that peace, unlike all the summits, does not disappoint you the instant you reach it, because it is not a place one arrives at. It is a place one stays in.

---

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not stop the race at the first summit sighted, nor does it make you set down the oars in a single afternoon. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling crazy for reaching so much and resting so little.

Before we descend from this mountain, stay with the truth that holds up everything else. There is a difference between the life one climbs and the life one inhabits, and you know the first profoundly and almost nothing of the second. The life one climbs is a succession of objectives, always ahead, always to be reached, and it has its grandeur, but it is a life lived always in the future, always on the way, never arrived. The life one inhabits is another: it is a life lived in the present, in what is, with depth instead of height, with presence instead of hurry. It is not a lesser life, it is a more whole life, because it is the only one truly lived, since life only happens now, and the now is only inhabited when one stops running toward the next thing. Learning to inhabit is not giving up climbing. It is discovering that there is a way of living that does not need height to have meaning, and that this way, unlike all the mountains, does not leave you empty when you arrive, because it is not a place one arrives at, it is a place one is in.

Now you know that your gaze always flees toward the next mountain, and why. You know that you no longer run out of hope, you run out of dependence on movement. You know that you built your identity on moving forward, and that is why stopping frightens you like disappearing. You know that beneath the restlessness there is something you flee from, and that inhabiting frightens you more than conquering precisely because it exposes you to it. You know how to distinguish the movement that is life from the movement that is flight. And you know that inhabiting is not stagnating, it is beginning, at last, to live.

And you know, perhaps, that the restlessness that brought you to this book, the one that rests in nothing, is not your enemy, it is your messenger. It has been trying to tell you, for a long time, that there is something beneath the movement that asks to be seen, and you have always answered the same way, with one more mountain. If instead of silencing it with one more climb, you listen to it, it leads you, slowly, to the place you flee from, and that place, faced, ceases to govern you. Your restlessness does not want more movement. It wants, deep down, for you to stop, and to finally listen to it.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I do not need a new mountain to exist.

Seeing your race was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not reached, it is not one more summit. It is done the opposite of everything you know: it is done by stopping, by staying, by inhabiting what you have been fleeing from inhabiting.

It was for that crossing that I created **Infonte**.

Infonte is not one more place to run to, nor one more mountain to climb. It is, precisely, the opposite: a space for you to stop, and to discover what moves you beneath the movement, what awaits you in the stillness you flee from, who you are when you are not moving forward toward anything. Not so you cease to have strength, to create, to go far, but so that those come to be born from a ground, and not from a flight. So that you can, at last, arrive at a place and stay.

There, the question "what do I flee from when I cannot stop" ceases to be an abyss and becomes a door. The stillness ceases to be a threat and begins to be home. And inhabiting ceases to look like the small death you feared and reveals itself as what it always was: the only place where your life, finally, happens.

If you feel this is the moment, you will find Infonte at [infonte.viviannedossantos.com](https://infonte.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, there is no hurry here, no goal to fulfill, no summit waiting. What you have read stays with you, serene, until the day you decide that you have climbed enough, and that it is time, at last, to stay.

And I want to leave you with an image to keep. Think of a house you built, room by room, over years, with much effort and much talent. A beautiful house, solid, yours. And think that you never slept in it, because the moment you finished a room you were already planning the next house, somewhere else, and you departed before inhabiting the one you had made. You have, behind you, a row of empty houses, all yours, all uninhabited. To inhabit is to stop building houses and to go and live in one. It is to turn on the light, to set the table, to sleep there, to live there. It is not to stop knowing how to build, it is to discover that houses are made to be lived in, not to be collected. And there is a house waiting for you, perhaps the one you have now, ready to be inhabited the moment you decide you have built enough and that it is time, at last, to dwell.

But before you close these pages, I ask of you a single step, and for you it will feel steeper than any summit you have ever taken, and that is precisely why I ask it. Today, stay. Five minutes still, doing nothing, planning nothing next, going nowhere. And when the restlessness comes, and it comes, the urge to get up, to move forward, to flee, do not flee. Stay a little longer. Because it is in that staying, borne without departing, that the race of a whole lifetime begins to undo itself, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that you do not need another mountain to exist. That you already exist, here, still, whole, now.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
