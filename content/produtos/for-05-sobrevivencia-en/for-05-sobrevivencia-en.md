# The woman who lives in survival mode

**For you, whose body never left the alert, as if the danger had not yet passed. Because you can live beyond the danger, and what you feel has a name, has an explanation, and has help.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and I want to be clear with you from the very first line: this one, more than any other, does not replace professional support. Living in survival mode is, most of the time, a trauma response, a phenomenon of the body and the nervous system, and it is not resolved only by understanding. If you recognize in yourself an alert that never passes, a body that never relaxes, memories that assail you, or the feeling of living at the edge of danger even in safety, please, seek the support of a health professional. It is not weakness. It is what this wound, truly, asks for.*

## 1. I never leave the alert

Notice your body, now. Probably, even at rest, there is a tension in it. An alert. A background readiness, as if something were about to happen, as if you had to be prepared.

You don't truly relax, almost ever. Even in safety, even at home, even with no danger in sight, there's a part of you that is always on watch. Attentive to noises, to changes, to signs of threat. Ready to react, to flee, to defend yourself, or to freeze, at the slightest hint.

And perhaps you don't even remember it ever being different. For many people who live this way, this state does not seem like a problem, it seems the normal way to exist, because they never knew another. Only when they stop to notice do they realize that what they call their temperament, their nervousness, their way of being, is, after all, a body that could never rest. If, reading this, you feel a recognition, a kind of relief at someone finally naming what you feel, it is likely that this is what we're talking about, and that you deserve, more than you think, the care this book speaks of.

Perhaps you startle easily. Perhaps you sleep badly, on alert. Perhaps you feel your heart racing, your chest tight, for no apparent reason. Perhaps you live with a background anxiety that does not pass, a constant nervousness, a feeling that danger is always lurking, even though, rationally, you know you are safe.

Or perhaps it's the opposite, and you recognize yourself more in a shutting-down than in an agitation. In a feeling of distance, of living through a pane of glass, of not quite feeling things, neither the good nor the bad. In a tiredness that does not pass with sleep, in a difficulty moving, starting, caring. In an emptiness. This too is survival mode, in its other face, that of disconnection, and it is as real, and as deserving of care, as the agitated alert. If this is where you recognize yourself, this book is as much yours as it is for those who live accelerated.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, and this is the most important thing in this whole book: what you feel is not an exaggeration, not drama, not a nervous temperament. It has a name. It is called living in survival mode. It is your nervous system trapped in a state of alert, as if the danger had not yet passed, even though it, in the world out there, passed long ago.

And this is not a weakness of character, nor a lack of positivity, nor something that is resolved by thinking positive or by trying hard to relax. It is a phenomenon of the body. A response of your nervous system, engraved by a time when the danger was real, and that remains switched on, on alert, even now that you are safe.

That is why this book is different from all the others. And I want to be very clear with you, right away, plainly. What you live is not cured only by understanding. Understanding helps, and we will do it. But living in survival mode is a trauma response, and trauma is treated with support, with time, with the body, and sometimes with treatment. This book accompanies you in understanding. But, more than any other, it asks that you also seek out real help.

And I'll give you the direction now, so you can read with some calm. You can live beyond the danger. Your body can, slowly, with help, learn that the danger has passed, and leave the alert in which it got trapped. It is not a matter of will, nor of effort. It is a matter of care, of the kind of care this wound deserves.

I want you to know why this book is different from the others in this collection, and why I insist so much on help. The other books speak of defenses, of armors, of wounds that understanding and work, very often accompanied, can ease. This one speaks of something that lives even deeper, in the very functioning of your nervous system, and that, therefore, rarely yields only to understanding. I do not write you this book to convince you that understanding yourself is enough. I write it to give you a name for what you live, to lift the guilt off you, and to point you, with all clarity, to the path that truly helps. It would be dishonest of me to make you believe these pages are enough. They are not, and I tell you so out of respect, not out of a lack of hope.

Because there is hope, and a great deal of it. What you live has a name, has an explanation, and has real help. You are not broken, you are not doomed, you are not a lost cause. You are a person whose body got stuck protecting you from a danger that has already passed, and that, today, is known how to treat. Read this book, then, not as a cure, but as a hand that takes hold of you and points you in the right direction, and that tells you, from beginning to end: it is worth it, you deserve it, and you don't have to do this alone.

Maybe the alert shows itself to you in concrete, everyday things.

In a tension in the body that never lets go, shoulders, jaw, chest, always contracted.

In an easy startle, in an exaggerated reaction to noises, to surprises, to changes.

In a difficulty relaxing, resting, ever feeling truly safe.

In a background anxiety that has no visible cause, but that does not let go of you, as if there were always a danger about to arrive.

Different shapes, one same alarm. Survival mode. The body trapped in an alert that the danger no longer justifies.

I want to show you how this shows up in daily life, in things you may already take for who you are. You enter a new place, and before any thought, your body has already swept the exits, already measured the people, already searched for the threat. Someone changes their tone, frowns, closes a door hard, and your body fires, your heart races, your stomach tightens, even if rationally you know there's no danger. You have days when you feel exhausted without having done anything special, and you don't understand why, without knowing that being on alert all day long consumes as much as an enormous physical effort.

And there's one detail you may recognize: the difficulty of simply being well. When things are calm, instead of resting, you wait for something to go wrong, almost uncomfortable with the peace, as if calm were only the interval before the next blow. For a body that lived in danger, calm is not repose, it is suspicion, because it learned that lowering the guard is when danger catches you. And so even the good moments are hard for you to inhabit, watched over by a part of you that doesn't believe they will last.

I want you to hear this clearly, because it is the heart of this book: none of this is an exaggeration of yours, nor drama, nor a lack of positivity. It is your nervous system doing what it was trained to do, in a time when the danger was real. Your body is not broken. It is protecting you from a threat that has already passed, with a loyalty no one taught it to switch off. And understanding this, though it does not cure, changes everything in the way you look at yourself: you stop being a nervous person by temperament and become a person with a wound in the body, who deserves care, and not reproach.

## 2. The body that stayed in the danger

I want to explain to you what is happening, because understanding the mechanism, though it isn't enough to cure it, brings relief, and lifts the guilt off you.

When we face a danger, the body enters survival mode. The nervous system activates, prepares us to fight, to flee, or to freeze. The heart races, the muscles tense, the senses on alert. It is a brilliant, automatic response, that serves to save us in the moment of danger. And, once the danger has passed, the body is supposed to return to calm, to repose, to safety.

But when the danger is great, or prolonged, or happens early, in childhood, when the nervous system is still forming, sometimes the body does not return. It stays trapped in survival mode. It continues on alert, in readiness, in defense, as if the danger continued, even after it has passed. The switch that should turn off the alarm gets jammed in the on position.

And that is what happens to you. Your body lived through a danger, or many, or a prolonged danger, and got trapped in survival mode. It continues to react as if the threat were present, even though, today, you are safe. It isn't your head that decides this. It is your body, your nervous system, that learned to live on alert, and that does not yet know it can switch off.

I want you to understand something that changes everything: this alarm does not pass through your will, because it happens before it. There is a part of your nervous system, very ancient, very fast, whose only function is to detect danger and fire the response, and it does this in a fraction of a second, before your thinking part even realizes what's happening. So, when your body reacts, it has already reacted, and ordering it to stop with reason is like trying to brake a reflex: the command arrives late. It isn't that you have no control out of weakness. It's that this response was made to be faster than thought, precisely to save you when there was no time to think.

It is also why being told to calm down, or think positive, or look on the bright side, doesn't work, and sometimes even makes things worse. Because those orders are addressed to the thinking part, and the alarm isn't there, it's in a deeper layer, one that doesn't speak the language of reason. Your body doesn't need to be convinced that it's safe. It needs to feel it, repeatedly, until it relearns. And feeling safe cannot be commanded, it is built, slowly, with experiences, with regulation, with the accompaniment of someone who knows how to speak that deeper language that words cannot reach.

Perhaps the danger that put you in survival mode was a childhood of violence, of instability, of fear. Perhaps it was a traumatic event, an abuse, an accident, a loss, a threat. Perhaps it was a prolonged danger, years of living on alert, in a place where you were never, in fact, safe. In any case, your body learned that the world is dangerous, and it stood guard, and it did not lower its guard when the danger passed.

I want you to hear this with all clarity. It is not your fault. You did not choose to live on alert. It is not weakness, nor a lack of effort, nor an inability to see the bright side of life. It is a physiological, automatic response, of your body, to a danger that was real. Your nervous system did what it had to do to protect you, and it got stuck doing it, and that is not a defect of yours, it is a wound, that deserves care, and not judgment.

I want to explain to you, further, that survival mode has several faces, because perhaps you don't recognize yourself in only one. There is the face of acceleration: the racing heart, the agitation, the anxiety, the desire to flee, the body ready to fight or run. And there is the opposite face, that of disconnection: the numbness, the emptiness, the feeling of watching life from outside, the tiredness that does not pass, the difficulty feeling anything at all. They seem contrary, but they are both the same: responses of the nervous system to a danger that was too great. Some people live more in one, others in the other, many oscillate between the two. If you recognize yourself in the disconnection, and not only in the acceleration, this too is about you.

And I want you to understand why danger in childhood marks so deeply. When the danger happens early, in a time when the nervous system is still forming, it does not register the threat as an episode, it registers it as the way the world is. The child who grows up on alert does not learn to be on alert in the face of danger, she learns to be on alert always, because for her the normal state of things is the threat. And that is engraved at a level so deep, so prior to words, that it is not reached by adult thought alone. That is why it isn't enough to know, today, that you are safe. A part of you, more ancient than reason, continues to live in the dangerous world where it formed.

In the way I learned to look at these stories, there is room to understand where, in your family, in your life, the danger came from. That helps to contextualize, to give meaning, to lift the guilt. But I want to be honest with you: contextualizing is not enough to cure this. Because this does not live only in the story, nor only in understanding. It lives in the body. And the body needs more than to understand. It needs to be cared for, with help, to learn, slowly, that the danger has passed.

## 3. Living at the edge of the precipice

There is an enormous cost to living in survival mode, and I want to name it, because perhaps you already take it for normal, so used are you to it. And naming the cost is not to weigh you down, it is so that you recognize, at last, how much you have been carrying, and how much you deserve to set it down.

The first price is paid by the body itself. Because a body in permanent alert never rests. The tense muscles, the racing heart, the system always activated, wear out. And prolonged survival mode has real physical consequences, on health, on sleep, on energy, on the immune system. Your body pays, in silence, the price of an alarm that never switches off.

It costs you peace. Because you never feel, truly, safe. Even in safety, there's always a part of you waiting for the danger, on guard, on alert. You don't know the deep rest of someone who feels safe, because your body never learned that it is safe. You live at the edge of the precipice, even when the ground, around you, is firm.

And there is a particular cruelty in this: the danger changed places. Before it came from outside, from a person, a place, a situation. Now it comes from within, from your own body, which fires the alarm when there is no threat. And from a danger within there is no fleeing, because it goes with you everywhere. There is no safe house, no distance that is enough, because what fires the fear is no longer in the world, it is in you. That is why changing your life, your city, your relationship, rarely resolves it: the alarm travels with you. What needs to be cared for is not the out there, it is the system that got trapped, and that one only calms with the right care, from within.

It costs you life. Because to live in survival mode is, at bottom, to survive, not to live. It is to be always defending yourself from danger, and never enjoying, creating, loving, being present. All the energy goes to vigilance, to defense, to readiness, and little is left for the life that is, in fact, happening, and that you can barely feel, busy as you are watching for the threat.

And it costs you the years. Because time passes, in survival mode, without being truly lived. The days fill with tasks, with vigilance, with enduring, and they drain away without your having felt yourself in them. You look back and it's hard to find the moments of pure joy, of full presence, because you were always half absent, on sentry. And there is a grief in this, the grief of a life crossed in defense, and the tenderness for yourself asks that you recognize that grief, not to sink into it, but so that it becomes one more reason to seek, now, the care that lets the years to come be, at last, lived.

And it costs you relationships. Because survival mode makes it hard to trust, to lower the guard, to let yourself go, to give yourself. You're always in defense, even with those who love you, even in safety, because your body does not yet distinguish the past danger from the safe present. And that defense, which protected you one day, keeps at a distance, today, precisely those who could give you the safety you so lack.

It costs you, too, the relationship with your own body. Because a body that is only a source of alarm, of tension, of unpleasant sensations, becomes a place from which one wants to flee. Many people who live in survival learn not to inhabit the body, to live from the head up, to anesthetize what they feel, because feeling is dangerous, it is opening the door to the alarm. And so they stay exiled from themselves, with no home anywhere, neither in the world, which is threat, nor in the body, which is alarm. To rediscover the body as a place where one can, at last, be, is part of what the right help gives back.

It costs you memory and attention. Because a brain busy watching for the threat has fewer resources for the rest. You forget things, you lose the thread, it's hard to concentrate, and perhaps you reproach yourself for it, thinking yourself distracted or incapable. You are not. It's that part of you is always elsewhere, on sentry, and what's left for ordinary life is less. This is not a defect of your mind. It is the price of a mind that could never dedicate itself fully to the present, because a part of it never left the danger.

Notice this exhaustion. The exhaustion of someone who never rests, not even while sleeping. Of someone who lives an entire life on alert, in readiness, waiting for a danger that, in the world, is no longer there, but that in the body never ceased to be there. It is a deep tiredness, physical and of the soul, and it is, in no way, a lack of strength of yours. It is the depletion of a system that could never switch off.

I want you to see, too, what this does to the way you judge yourself, because there is here an injustice you carry against yourself. You look at other people who seem to live with lightness, who rest, who trust, who give themselves, and you reproach yourself for not managing the same, as if it were a lack of will of yours, or of strength, or of faith. It isn't. They don't have a body trapped on alert. You do. You're trying to live with a nervous system functioning in emergency mode, and even so you keep going, you work, you care, you carry on. That is not weakness. It is a daily feat that no one sees, and for which you deserve compassion, and not the harshness with which you treat yourself.

I want to tell you, here, with all tenderness: you are not living badly by choice, nor for lack of trying. You are surviving, with a body trapped in the danger, and doing it, probably, with an immense courage that no one sees. And that does not ask you to try harder. It asks that you be, at last, cared for.

And I want to add a truth that may relieve you: part of your exhaustion comes not only from what you live today, it comes from how much you strive to seem normal. You spend an enormous energy hiding the alert, faking a calm you don't feel, functioning on the outside as if on the inside there weren't an alarm ringing without stop. That effort of disguise, added to the alert itself, is part of what depletes you. And one of the first things that changes, when you seek help, is being able, at last, to stop pretending, at least with someone, and discovering the enormous rest there is in no longer having to hide.

## 4. Who am I beyond the danger

I'll set down a question here, the way one sets down a guard. Alone. Don't run from it, even though it is difficult.

Who would you be, if your body knew, at last, that the danger has passed? If you could live, and not only survive?

Stay with it. Notice what it opens. Perhaps a longing for something you never had.

For you, this question touches a deep place, because perhaps you don't even know what it is to live without the alert. Survival mode is so old, so much your own, that it blends with you. You don't know who you would be without it, because you were never, or almost never, outside of it.

And there is, beneath the alert, a person who barely had the chance to exist. The one who could rest, play, create, love, be present, if the body weren't always on guard. That person is in you, alive, waiting, beneath the vigilance. But survival mode never gave her room, because all the energy went to defending, watching, surviving.

I want you to see that this is not a failing of who you are. It is a life that stayed suspended, waiting for safety. It isn't that you are an anxious, alert, defensive person, by nature. It's that your nature got covered over by survival, and beneath it there is someone who never came, yet, to live in peace.

And I want you to feel the tenderness that this person beneath deserves. She did not disappear. She is there, patient, waiting for years, perhaps forever, for a world where she could, at last, lower her guard. You did not lose her. She was only never given the safety she needed in order to come out. And there is something moving in knowing this: that within you, beneath all the alert, there is a version of you who knows how to rest, who knows how to play, who knows how to trust, and who only waits for the body to learn that it can. You don't have to invent her. You have to let her, at last, appear, and that is done by creating, with help, the safety that lets her come out.

I want also to tell you something about time, because it matters. Perhaps you feel that it's already late, that you've lived this way too many years, that your body is marked forever. It isn't. The nervous system has no expiration date for relearning safety. There are people who began this path late, after an entire life on alert, and found, even so, places of calm they had never known. There is no point past which it's no longer worth it. As long as there is life, there is the possibility of the body relearning, and each year that remains to you deserves to be lived with more peace than the ones that passed.

And I want to tell you something that is, at the same time, hard and full of hope. Survival mode is not permanent. The nervous system can, with help, with the right care, with time, learn to leave the alert. It can regulate itself. It can discover that the danger has passed. It is not a life sentence. It is a treatable wound. And the person who is beneath, the one who stayed waiting, can, at last, begin to live.

I want you to hear this with care, because it is one of the most important things I can tell you: what you feel does not tell the truth about the present, it tells the truth about the past. When your body fires the alarm, it is not warning you of a real danger, now. It is repeating an old warning, engraved in a time when the danger was real. The feeling is true, the sensation is true, the terror is true, but the danger, today, most of the time, is not. Learning to distinguish these two things, the alarm from the danger, is a central part of the work, and it is not done only by telling yourself you are safe, it is done slowly, with help, until the body, and not only the head, begins to believe it.

And there is something I want you to know, about the hope I offer you, so that it doesn't seem empty to you. I don't tell you this improves because it would be lovely to say it. I tell you because it is what is known today about the nervous system and trauma: that the body has a real capacity to relearn safety, to reorganize itself, to leave states in which it seemed trapped forever. People who lived for decades on alert found, with the right care, places of calm they thought impossible. It is not magic, it is not quick, and it is not done alone. But it is real, and that is why it is worth so much to take the step of seeking help: because on the other side there is, in fact, a possible change.

But, and here I am very honest with you, that does not happen only by reading, nor only by understanding, nor only by wanting. It happens with help. With a professional trained to work with trauma and the body. Because your nervous system is not disarmed by will, it is disarmed by the right care, and that care, you deserve to seek it.

And if, in imagining that life, you feel that you don't deserve it, or that it isn't for you, notice that that very thought is part of the wound. Someone who lived in danger learns, very often, that she has no right to the good, that peace is for others. It is not true. Peace, safety, rest, are not prizes reserved for those who were lucky. They are human needs, yours as much as anyone's. You don't have to deserve calm. You have a right to it, by the simple fact of being someone who suffered too much and who deserves, at last, to suffer less.

Don't answer the question of who you would be beyond the danger just yet. But leave it open, like a hope. Because it points to a real possibility: that there exists, in you, a life waiting, and that it can, with help, at last, begin.

## 5. This asks for help, and that is not weakness

I have to stop the book here, and this is the most important chapter I have written in any book. Read it with attention, and take it seriously, because it can change your life.

Living in survival mode is a trauma response. It is not a personality trait, it is not a lack of positivity, it is not something resolved alone, by effort, by reading, by good will. It is a phenomenon of the body and the nervous system, and treating it asks for more than understanding.

I want to tell you, with all clarity, what this means. It means that you deserve, and need, the support of a health professional. Of a psychologist, of a psychiatrist, of a therapist specialized in trauma. Someone trained to work not only your story, but your body, your nervous system, the alert in which you got trapped.

There are today ways to help those who live in survival mode. Approaches that work the trauma, the body, the nervous system, and that help, slowly, to leave the alert, to regulate, to feel, at last, safety. I am not telling you this as a vague possibility. I am telling you as the path, the path that this wound, truly, asks for.

I want to take away some fears that perhaps hold you back from seeking help, because I know them. Perhaps you think that what you lived through was not grave enough to deserve support, that others went through worse, that you're exaggerating. That thought is, itself, part of the wound: someone who lived in danger learns to minimize it in order to bear it. It is not the objective gravity that decides whether you deserve help. It is the fact that your body got trapped in the alert. If you live this way, you deserve support, full stop, regardless of whether your story seems grave or small next to others'.

Perhaps you fear, too, that seeking help means reopening everything, reliving the danger, being forced to tell and to feel what you spent your life fleeing. I want to reassure you: work with trauma, done by trained hands, is not throwing you back into the pain. It is the opposite. It is building, first, safety, and only at the pace of what your body can bear approaching what hurts, always with ground underneath. A good professional does not push you into the abyss, they accompany you in approaching it slowly, with care, in a way that heals instead of harming. You don't have to be afraid of asking for help out of fear of what it will demand of you.

And if the first help you find doesn't serve, don't give up. Not everyone finds right away the right person, the right approach. Sometimes you have to search more than once. That doesn't mean there's no help for you. It means only that you haven't yet found yours, and that it's worth continuing, because it exists.

And I want to dismantle, with all firmness, the idea that seeking help is weakness. It is not. It is the opposite. You, who survived, who endured, who carried alone a body on alert for years, perhaps your whole life, have an immense strength. And the greatest proof of that strength is not to keep enduring alone. It is to seek, at last, the care you deserve. Asking for help, for someone who lived in survival, is the most courageous act there is.

I want also to tell you, because it matters: if there are moments when the weight is too great, when the pain is unbearable, when you think you can't endure, please, don't stay alone with it. Talk to someone. Seek immediate help, a doctor, a support line, someone you trust. Your life matters, and there are people who know how to help, and there is a way out, even when, in the midst of the alert, it seems there isn't.

I want to tell you, further, something practical, because sometimes what holds you back is not a lack of will, it is not knowing where to begin. You don't have to find, on the first try, the perfect professional, nor to understand everything about trauma before seeking. A first small step is enough: talking to your family doctor and asking for guidance, looking for a psychologist, contacting a support service. That first step doesn't have to resolve everything. It only has to open the door. Once it's open, there are people who will help you find the next path. The hard part is only the first gesture, and even that, you can ask someone you trust to take it with you, to accompany you to the appointment, to help you make the booking. You don't have to do even this alone.

If there is one chapter of this book, of this whole collection, that I want you to remember, it is this one. Don't skip it. Don't postpone it. Understanding accompanies you, but this asks for trained hands. And seeking them is not admitting defeat. It is beginning, at last, to leave the danger where your body got trapped.

## 6. Living, and not only surviving

If your body got trapped in the danger, then there is a direction, a hope, and I want to show it to you with honesty. You can live beyond the danger. Not only survive. Live.

Surviving is what you have been doing. It is defending yourself, watching, enduring, keeping yourself upright against the threat. It is a feat, and you did it with an enormous courage. But it is exhausting, and it is not full life. It is life reduced to defense.

Living is something else. It is being able to rest, to feel, to enjoy, to create, to love, to be present. It is the energy, that went entirely to vigilance, being able to go, at last, to life. It is the body being able to relax, to feel safe, to open itself to pleasure, to connection, to peace. It is the person who is beneath the alert being able, finally, to exist.

I want you to understand that this is not trading one personality for another, nor ceasing to be who you are. Your sensitivity, your attention to what surrounds you, your capacity to perceive what others don't perceive, all of that, which was born from vigilance, stays with you, and can become a gift instead of a burden, when it stops coming from terror. Leaving survival mode does not erase you. It frees what in you was at the service of defense to be at the service of life. The same attention you used to search for the threat can come to search for beauty. The same intensity with which you feared can come to be the intensity with which you love.

And I want to be honest with you about the path, so you don't live it as one more demand. There will be better days and worse days. There will be advances and setbacks, moments when the alarm seems to quiet and moments when it returns with everything. That is not failing, it is how the body learns, back and forth, not in a straight line. Don't ask of yourself, here too, a perfect recovery, which would be putting the old perfectionism at the service of healing. Ask of yourself only that you not give up, that you keep giving your body, with help, evidence of safety, and that you treat the setbacks with the same gentleness you would treat the setbacks of someone you love who is healing.

And I want to be honest with you about how this passage is made, from surviving to living. It is not made by effort, nor by will, nor by this book. It is made with help. With the right care, over time, with someone trained to accompany your nervous system in learning, slowly, that the danger has passed. It is a path, and it has steps, and it has time, and it has hands that accompany it.

But it is a real path. Survival mode is not a sentence. Bodies can learn to leave the alert. Nervous systems can regulate themselves, with the right care. People who lived for years in survival can, with help, reach a place where they feel, at last, safe, where they rest, where they live. It is not an empty promise. It is what help trained for trauma makes possible.

I want you to understand, too, that the safety you lack is not only something built from within, in your body, it is also something received in relationship. The human nervous system regulates itself in contact with other nervous systems that are safe, calm, present. That is why the relationship with a good professional heals so much: it isn't only for what they teach you, it's because your body, beside a regulated and safe body, comes to learn, by contagion, to regulate itself too. And that is why this is not done alone, by force of will: because the very leaving of the alert needs, in part, to happen with another, in the safety of a relationship where your body, slowly, learns that it can set itself down.

So, when I tell you to seek help, I am not telling you to resolve a problem alone with the guidance of a technician. I am inviting you to enter a relationship of care, where you will not only be learning about yourself, but being accompanied, held, regulated, until your body discovers, in the presence of another, the calm it could not find alone. There is something profoundly reparative in this, especially for someone for whom the danger came, in the beginning, from other people: discovering that a relationship can be, at last, the place of safety, and not of threat.

I want to leave you, more than an awakening, a hope. There is, on the other side of this path, a life you perhaps never knew. A life in which your body rests. In which you feel safe. In which the energy you spend watching for danger can go to what you love. That life exists, and it is possible, and it is not reserved for other people stronger or luckier. It is within your reach, with the care you deserve to seek.

And the first step toward that life is not a superhuman effort. It is a gesture of care. It is seeking help. It is letting someone trained accompany you in leaving the danger where you got trapped. That gesture, small and enormous, is the beginning of living, and not only of surviving.

I want also to tell you what living, truly, can come to mean, so that you have somewhere to walk toward. It can come to mean sleeping a whole night without the body on sentry. Feeling an embrace without a part of you on watch. Being in a room without mapping the exits. Laughing without reserve. Letting yourself be loved without expecting the blow. Setting yourself down, at the end of a day, without the feeling that something is coming. Small things, that to those who never lived in danger seem ordinary, and that for you would be a new country. It is toward that country that one walks, and it exists, and there are people who can take you there.

I want you to know, further, that getting better is not erasing what you lived, nor returning to who you were before the danger, because perhaps there never was a before. Getting better is your nervous system learning to distinguish the past from the present, to release the alarm when there's no threat, to find calm as a possible state, and not as a foreign land. You will not stop having a story. You will stop living it as if it were happening now. And that difference, between carrying a memory and living inside it, is all the difference in the world.

## 7. Teaching the body that the danger has passed

There is a verb that waits on the far side of the smoke. To live. And there is a path to get there, that I want to name for you with honesty, without promising you shortcuts.

The path is your body learning that the danger has passed. Not your head, which perhaps already knows it. Your body, your nervous system, which continues to react as if the danger were present. And the body does not learn by effort, nor by reason. It learns slowly, with experiences of safety, repeated, in the body, very often with the help of someone trained to facilitate them.

I want to be clear with you, once again, because it is important and because I respect you too much to delude you. This chapter is not going to give you the cure. There is not, in a book, the cure for survival mode. What there is, in a book, is understanding, and the indication of the path. And the path passes through specialized help, through work with the body and trauma, over time.

But I want to give you, even so, some things that can accompany that path, without replacing it. Small signs of safety that you can give your body, alongside the work with whoever accompanies you.

Noticing, throughout the day, the moments in which you are, in fact, safe. Naming, inside, slowly: now, in this moment, I am safe. Not to force yourself to believe it, but to begin giving your body, in small doses, the information that the danger, in this instant, is not present.

Noticing your body, with gentleness. Where the tension is. How the breathing is. Without judging yourself, without forcing yourself, only noticing, beginning to inhabit a body you perhaps learned to flee. And if noticing the body, alone, fires the alarm for you, and sometimes it does, don't insist by force: that is precisely one of the things best done with someone beside you, who helps you approach the body without frightening you. Do only what is safe, and leave the rest for when you have trained company.

Seeking, and allowing yourself, moments and places of safety. People with whom you feel, even a little, calmer. Places where the alert lowers, even if only a little. And letting yourself be in them, letting the body register, slowly, that safe places exist.

Returning to the body through the simple things, when you can and without forcing yourself. Feeling your feet on the ground. Noticing five things you see, four you hear, three you touch, to bring your attention from the imagined danger to the concrete present. Breathing slowly, with the exhale longer than the inhale, which is a way of telling the body, in its own language, that it can calm down. They are not cures, they are only small bridges to the present, gestures you can keep at hand while the deeper work is done with whoever accompanies you.

Treating yourself with the gentleness you would have for someone you loved, and who lived what you live. Not demanding calm of yourself, not reproaching yourself for the alarm, not calling yourself exaggerated when the body fires. Speaking to yourself as you would speak to a frightened friend: slowly, with tenderness, without haste. Because harshness with yourself is one more alarm, and gentleness is, in itself, a sign of safety that your body needs to receive, above all from you.

But, and I repeat it because it is the most important, these things accompany, they do not replace. The deeper work, of teaching your body that the danger has passed, is done with help. And seeking it is the bigger step, the one that truly changes your life.

I want, further, to warn you of one thing, so it doesn't discourage you. As the body begins to feel a little safer, sometimes, instead of getting better right away, what was held comes to the surface: old emotions, memories, accumulated tiredness. It can seem that you're getting worse, when, in truth, it is the body releasing what it held for years, because at last it feels safe enough to do it. It is one more reason for this to be done accompanied, with someone who knows how to hold those moments and explain to you that they are part of the path, and not a setback. Alone, that surfacing can frighten; accompanied, it becomes part of the healing.

I won't give you a method, because it would be irresponsible to make you believe that a method, in a book, is enough for this. I give you a direction, and a request. The direction is to live, beyond the danger. The request is that you not try to get there alone, because you don't have to, and because this wound, truly, deserves hands that know how to hold it.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book with a different care from all the others. Because, in this one, understanding is not enough, and it would be dishonest to pretend it is. Here, more than accompanying you in seeing, I want to lead you to seek the care you deserve.

It doesn't change everything by reading. But it changes one thing, and it is important: it lifts the guilt off you, and gives you a name, and points you to a path.

Now you know that you live in survival mode, and that this is not an exaggeration, nor a temperament, nor a weakness, but a response of the body to a danger that was real.

You know that your body got trapped in the danger, in an alert that the danger, in the world, no longer justifies, and that this alarm speaks of the past, not of the present.

You know that this costs you the body, peace, life, relationships, and the years that pass without being lived, and that none of this is a lack of strength of yours, it is the depletion of a system that could never switch off.

You know that there is, beneath the alert, a person waiting to live, patient, intact, and that survival mode is not a sentence, it is a treatable wound, at any age.

You know, and this is the most important, that this asks for help, real help, from a professional trained for trauma, and that seeking it is not weakness, it is the most courageous and most lucid act there is.

And you know that you can live, and not only survive, and that the path there, though it is not easy, is real, and begins with a gesture of care.

And you know, above all, that you are not broken, nor doomed, nor alone. That what you live has a name, has an explanation, and has help. And that the courage to survive, which you have already proven more than enough, has now a sweeter sister waiting for you: the courage to let yourself, at last, be cared for.

I want to leave you an image, and only one.

Imagine someone who crossed a fire, and who saved herself, and who stayed, forever, smelling smoke in the air, seeing flames where there are none, with the body ready to flee, even years later, even in a house where there was never fire again. The fire ended. But the body never knew.

The crossing is your body learning that the fire ended. And that, no one does alone looking at the imagined smoke. It is done with someone who helps you see, slowly, that the air, today, is clean. That the flames stayed in the past. That you can, at last, breathe. It isn't easy, and it isn't quick, and it isn't done only with a book. But it is possible. And on the other side is a life in which one lives, and not only survives.

And I want to leave you something, about those who live with you, above all if there are children, or people close who learn by looking at you. A body in survival is transmitted, without words: children feel the alert of those who care for them, and they learn, themselves, to live on guard, even without danger. I don't tell you this to weigh on your conscience further, which already carries too much. I tell you as one more reason, perhaps the strongest, to seek help: because caring for your nervous system is not only for yourself, it is also so as not to pass, without meaning to, the alert to those you love. The most protective thing you can do for them is to give yourself the care that helps you find calm. An adult who regulates herself teaches, just by existing nearby, a small body to feel safe.

And before the smoke clears from these pages, let me tell you one last thing, with all tenderness. What happened to you was not your fault, and the state in which you got stuck is not a defect of yours. You are someone who survived something the body could not, alone, leave behind. There is no shame in that. There is, rather, an enormous dignity in your having continued, in your having endured, and there is a courage greater still waiting for you: that of letting yourself, at last, be helped to set down an alert you have carried for too long.

Beneath the alert, your body already carries the one sentence that holds this book. I can live beyond the danger. Not as a promise you have to keep alone, but as a real direction, toward which there are trained hands that can take you.

And you know, too, what the first step is, and it is not an effort, it is a care. It is seeking help. It is letting someone trained accompany you in leaving the fire that ended everywhere except in your body. That step is not weakness. It is the beginning of living.

And before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing, and it is different from all the other requests I made in these books. I don't ask you for an exercise. I ask you for a gesture of care. This week, seek help. Book an appointment. Talk to a doctor, to a psychologist, to a support line. Take the first step so that someone trained can accompany you. Because you don't have to keep surviving alone, and there is a way out, and there are people who know the path, and you deserve, after everything you endured, to let yourself, at last, be helped to live.

And if, in thinking of seeking help, you feel fear, or shame, or the certainty that you won't manage, remember: that fear is the alarm, it is not the truth. The truth is that you deserve to be cared for, that there are people who know how, and that the most courageous step of your life can be, simply, letting yourself be helped.

With all my tenderness, and with all my care,
*Vivianne*
