# The woman who is afraid to receive

**For the woman who takes care of everyone and deflects everything she is given. Because receiving does not put you in debt, and it too is a way of loving.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks about lives in the place of someone who takes care and cannot bear the place of someone who is taken care of, and there is a point where this stops being modesty and becomes an inability to let herself be supported that costs dearly, to the body and to the soul. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion of someone who always gives and never receives, a vulnerability that terrifies you, or a loneliness that already weighs on you, this may have roots that deserve the care of a professional. Letting yourself be cared for is not weakness. It is the form of being loved that you are missing.*

---

## 1. Giving is easy, receiving takes the ground from under me

There is a gesture you make so often that you no longer even notice it: deflecting. Someone gives you a compliment, and you deflect, you give it back, you minimize it, "oh, it was nothing," "you're the one," and the compliment never arrives. Someone wants to give you attention, care, a treat, and you deflect, you change the subject, you turn it toward the other person, "and you, how are you?", as if a gesture directed at you were too much for you to bear. Someone offers you something, and you deflect, you refuse, or you accept with such discomfort that you ruin the moment. Everything that comes in your direction, you deflect, like someone parrying a blow, even though what comes is love.

Notice the contrast, because it is glaring. At giving, you are masterful, you are whole, you are generous without effort. You know how to take care, to anticipate, to be there, to give your best, and you do it naturally, with pleasure even. Giving is your territory, the place where you feel competent, safe, at home. But turn the direction, put you in the position of receiving, and everything changes: you become uncomfortable, not knowing where to put yourself, wanting to flee, to give back, to balance, to leave that place as quickly as possible. Giving is easy. Receiving takes the ground from under you.

Notice a detail that reveals everything: what you feel when you are the center of attention. At a party of yours, in a moment when you are celebrated, at a dinner in your honor, in an instant when all eyes and gestures turn toward you, what appears is not pleasure, it is an acute discomfort, an urge to get up, to serve, to deflect the attention onto another person, to leave that focus as quickly as possible. For most people, being celebrated is pleasant, even if with some shyness. For you, it is almost unbearable, because to be the center is to be exposed, uncovered, in the position of someone who receives everyone's attention with nothing to do with her hands except to let herself be received. And that acute discomfort at the center of attention is the same, amplified, that you feel in every small gesture directed at you: the impossibility of being, simply, on the side of someone who receives.

And I want to be precise right at the start, because this can be confused with other things and it is none of them. It is not that you think you have no value, or that you have to pay for your place, or that you don't deserve it. None of that may even be true in you. Your problem is more specific, and more physical, almost: it is the position. You know how to be on the side of someone who gives, and you don't know how, you can't, you can't bear, to be on the side of someone who receives. It is not a matter of merit, it is a matter of place. There is a place, that of someone who receives, that you never learned to occupy, and that is as strange to you as a language you have never spoken.

And there is a reason beneath this, which is the heart of this book: for you, receiving exposes. When you give, you are in command, you are in the strong position, you are controlling the situation, it is you who decides, who offers, who directs the gesture. When you receive, you lose that control, you are in the position of someone who needs, who depends, who is exposed to the other's gesture, vulnerable, uncovered. And it is that exposure, that vulnerability, that is unbearable to you. It is not what you receive that bothers you. It is the place of someone who receives, that uncovered place where you are no longer the one who takes care, but the one who is taken care of, and where you feel, therefore, dangerously on display.

I want you to know, right at the start, that this book does not come to tell you that taking care of others is bad, that you should give less, that your generosity is a defect. Your capacity to take care is real and it is beautiful, and the world needs women like you. I came to look at something else: at your inability to receive, to let yourself be cared for, to occupy, for once, the place of someone who receives without ruining it. Because that inability, which looks like humility, is exhausting you, isolating you, and keeping you from one of the deepest experiences there is: that of being loved in the vulnerable position of someone who receives, and not only respected in the strong position of someone who gives.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read on the defensive: receiving too is a form of loving. It is not the opposite of giving, it is not the weak side of the relationship, it is not the inferior position. It is the other half of love, as dignified as the first, and without it love is left lame, running in only one direction. This idea, which seems simple, goes against the way you built yourself, and so it will cost you. But it is the one that opens the door to something you never allowed yourself: to occupy the place of someone who receives with dignity, to let them love you without deflecting, to receive without apologizing for being, for once, on the side of someone who is cared for.

There is a way of feeling that you may never have connected to this, and that comes from the same place: the tiredness of someone who always gives and never replenishes. You give, and give, and the reservoir keeps lowering, and since you don't let yourself receive, it never fills up again. And there comes a point, that you may already know, where you give out of obligation and not out of pleasure, where you take care with an empty tank, where you go on serving even with nothing left inside, because to stop giving would be to abandon the only position you know how to occupy. That exhaustion of the chronic caregiver is not a lack of love, it is a lack of replenishment: no one can give indefinitely without receiving, and you try to do it, and what looks like infinite dedication is, in truth, a sure path to exhaustion, because a source that is never fed ends up, sooner or later, running dry, however deep it may be.

And I want you to notice something that distinguishes this wound from the others, because it is subtle. It is not that you think you don't deserve, nor that you have to pay for your place, nor that receiving imprisons you. It is something more bodily, more immediate: it is the physical discomfort of being in the place of someone who receives, the vertigo of not controlling, the itch to leave it for the safe place of someone who gives. You may know, rationally, that you deserve to receive, that that person wants to give to you from the heart, that there is no debt at all, and still your body refuses, deflects, flees, because the position of receiving is, for you, a physically intolerable place, and not a matter of ideas. That is why it is not enough to convince yourself that you deserve it: the path goes through getting the body used, slowly, to tolerating a place it learned to feel as dangerous.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in concrete scenes. It's at a birthday, at a party of yours, feeling uncomfortable for being the center, and immediately wanting to serve others to get out of that place. It's falling ill and finding it almost impossible to let them take care of you, wanting to get up to handle everything anyway. It's in a difficult moment not being able to receive comfort, deflecting into "I'm fine," "don't worry about me." It's receiving help and being left so much in debt that the gesture, instead of relieving you, weighs on you. They are all the same: the flight from the place of someone who receives, the deflection of everything that comes in your direction, as if receiving were an exposure you cannot bear.

## 2. The position, not the belonging

There is a distinction that needs to be made, because it is what makes your case specific, and few people make it. Your difficulty is not one of belonging, it is not thinking that you have to deserve a place, that you have to pay to stay. There may be women like that, and that is another wound. Yours is different: it is a difficulty of position. You know that you belong, you know that you have value, you know that you are wanted. What you don't know is how to be in the place of someone who receives that wanting. You know how to be loved with open arms, offering; you don't know how to be loved with empty hands, welcoming.

Notice what this means. There are, in a relationship, two positions, and both are necessary: that of someone who gives and that of someone who receives. In a healthy relationship, people move between the two, now they give, now they receive, according to the moment, and that alternation is the very breathing of love. You got stuck in only one: that of someone who gives. You got used to it, you specialized in it, you made it your way of being in the world, and you lost access to the other. When life pushes you toward the position of someone who receives, you feel like a fish out of water, in a place that is not yours, that you don't master, where you don't know how to be. And your immediate reaction is to go back to the known position: to give, to take care, to serve, so as to leave the place of someone who receives and return to the firm ground of someone who gives.

And I want to show you what breaks, inside you, when you receive, because that is where the key is. When you give, you maintain a certain image of yourself: the strong one, the capable one, the one who doesn't need, the one who has something to give. When you receive, that image cracks. Suddenly, you are the one who needs, the one who doesn't have, the one who depends on the other's gesture. And that new image is so strange to you, so uncomfortable, that you prefer to refuse what they give you rather than have to inhabit it. It is not the gift you refuse. It is the version of yourself that receiving forces you to be: the vulnerable version, needy, uncovered, that contradicts the image of a strong and self-sufficient woman that you built and in which you recognize yourself.

And why is giving so much easier? Because giving keeps you in control. Whoever gives decides how much she gives, when she gives, to whom she gives, how she gives. Whoever gives is in command of the gesture, in the active, safe position. Whoever receives, not. Whoever receives is at the mercy of the other's gesture, in the passive position, dependent on what the other decides to offer. And for a woman who needs to control, who feels safe in command, who has difficulty letting go of the reins, the position of someone who receives is terrifying precisely because it is the position of someone who does not control, who hands herself over, who lets the other lead. Giving is keeping the steering wheel. Receiving is letting another drive. And you don't know how to ride in the passenger seat.

And here I ask you something that goes against your instinct: do not turn this page into one more place where you have to account for yourself. This is no failing of yours. The specialization in giving was, somewhere, a way of protecting yourself from the vulnerability of receiving, which in the past perhaps cost you dearly. You became the one who gives because giving was safe and receiving hurt, or exposed, or disappointed. And that choice, made long ago, made of you a woman immensely capable of taking care and incapable of being taken care of, and that half you are missing is not a defect of character, it is a position you never learned to occupy, and that, like everything that was not learned, can still be learned.

There is a family root worth looking at, because it sheds a great deal of light. Many women who don't know how to receive were, early, the ones who took care. The eldest daughter who looked after her siblings. The child who took care of a sick father, of a mother in suffering, of a house that needed her. The one who learned, before her time, to be the strong one, the one who supports, the one who gives, because someone had to be it and it fell to her. And that child who took care early never had enough experience of being taken care of, of receiving, of being in the place of someone who is supported, and so she grew up fluent in the language of giving and mute in the language of receiving. If you were that child, your difficulty in receiving is no mystery at all: it is the mark of someone who had to give before having received enough, and who never again knew how to be on the side of someone who receives.

## 3. Who am I when I'm not the caregiver

I am going to let a question fall here, on its own, and I ask you not to dispatch it quickly by going to take care of someone, because it is precisely that flight that we are going to look at.

Who am I when I'm not the caregiver?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness it opens, the sense that without the caretaking you don't quite know what is left.

For a woman who built herself whole in the position of someone who gives, this question touches a deep fear, and it is worth understanding it. It is that your identity stuck itself to the role of caregiver. You are the one who takes care, the one who supports, the one who is there for others, the strong one on whom everyone leans, and that role is not only what you do, it has become who you are, your way of having value, of being needed, of occupying a recognizable place in the world. Take that role away from you, put you in a situation where you are not the one taking care but the one being taken care of, and a vertigo appears, the sense that there, without the role, you don't know who you are, you don't know what you are worth, you don't know what place you occupy.

And the shadow hiding here has a name, and it is vulnerability. Your greatest fear, though you rarely name it, is to be vulnerable, uncovered, exposed, in the position of someone who needs and depends. Being the caregiver protects you from that: while you take care, you are covered, you are in the strong position, it is you who sees the fragility of others and never they yours. Receiving takes that protection from you, turns the focus onto you, shows your fragility, your need, your not-having, and that is unbearable to you, because you got used to being the one who sees and never the one who is seen, the one who supports and never the one who needs support. The position of caregiver was, your whole life, your perfect hiding place: by taking care of others, you never had to show that you too, underneath, were fragile and in need.

Notice how this way of existing makes you strangely alone, despite being always surrounded by people you take care of. Because to take care of everyone and let no one take care of you is to be always on the outside of intimacy. You know everyone's fragility and no one knows yours. You see everyone uncovered and no one sees you. And that asymmetry, which gives you security, robs you of true closeness, because intimacy requires reciprocity, requires that both show themselves, expose themselves, let themselves be seen fragile by each other. A relationship in which you only take care and are never taken care of is not intimate, however close it may seem, because half of you, the fragile one, the one who needs, was never there, was never shown, was never loved, because you never let it appear.

And there is a cost you pay, and that those who love you pay. The people who care about you would like to be able to take care of you, to support you, to give to you, to repay the great deal you give. That is how love wants to flow, in both directions. By never letting them take care of you, by always deflecting, by always refusing the place of someone who receives, you deprive those who love you of the possibility of loving you back, and you leave them eternally in debt, always receiving and never able to give you anything. There are those who tire of this, who feel useless beside you, who pull away because they never manage to reach you, because they feel that you are not needed and that therefore their tenderness has nowhere to land. Your self-sufficiency as a caregiver, which you think a gift, is sometimes a door shut in the face of those who wanted to love you.

There is a movement that frees, and I want to show it to you without making it a recipe, because it is the opposite of deflecting. First, to see: that you deflect everything that comes in your direction, and where, and how. Then, to recognize where the specialization in giving comes from, and that it was your armor against an exposure that hurt, not a defect. Then, and this is the hard step, to give back what made you close: to look, inside, at the child who took care too early, or who exposed herself and was hurt, and to tell her that that time has passed, that she no longer has to always be the strong one, that she can, at last, lower her guard. Then, to tolerate: to stay in the place of someone who receives without fleeing, to bear the vertigo, to let the gesture arrive. And finally, to occupy with dignity the place of someone who receives, without apology, as one of the two legitimate positions of love. None of these movements is made because you read it and agreed; they are made in the body, in the real instant when someone reaches out a hand to you and you, instead of deflecting it, hold it. The direction is this, and it is the exact opposite of going on deflecting.

Notice too a trap of someone who took care early: that of having become so good at reading others' needs that you lost contact with your own. You are so trained at seeing what others need, at anticipating, at providing, that you no longer even know what you yourself need, or whether you need anything. They ask you "what do you want?" and you go blank, because you spent your life sensing what others want and silencing what you wanted. This disconnection from yourself is a deep cost of the caregiver position: not only do you not let yourself receive, you no longer even know what you would have to receive, because you switched off, long ago, the channel that told you what you yourself needed. To relearn to receive begins, sometimes, before anything, with relearning to feel that you too have needs, that you too want, that you too, beneath the caregiver, are someone hungry to be cared for.

I want to tell you something about this fear of vulnerability, because it begins to disarm it. Vulnerability is not your weak point. It is your most human place, and it is, paradoxically, where true connection is made. People do not connect deeply to our strength, they connect to our truth, and our truth includes the fragility, the need, the not knowing, the needing. When you allow yourself to be vulnerable before someone, when you let them see you fragile, take care of you, you do not become smaller in that person's eyes, you become real, close, lovable in a new way. The shell of the caregiver protects you from exposure, but it deprives you of intimacy. And perhaps the day will come when you discover that it would be worth risking the exposure, because on the other side of it is precisely what you most lack: to be loved whole, and not only in the strong half.

## 4. Receiving exposes

Take off now all the layers, one by one, down to the bone, because it is beneath everything else that the true reason dwells. Beneath the deflecting, the specialization in giving, the fear of not being the caregiver, there is one truth only, and it is this: receiving frightens you more than giving because receiving exposes. Giving is safe because it keeps you covered. Receiving is terrifying because it uncovers you. And you, somewhere, learned that being uncovered is dangerous, and you made of giving your permanent armor against the exposure of receiving.

Notice what happens, exactly, in the moment you receive. You lower your guard. You show that you need. You hand yourself over to the other's gesture, to their care, to their wish to give to you. And in that instant you are uncovered in a way that giving never forces you to be: you are dependent on what the other is going to do, exposed to the possibility of their failing, of not corresponding, of disappointing you, of using your need against you. Giving does not have this risk, because at giving it is you who controls. Receiving has all of it, because at receiving you hand over the control. And for someone who fears exposure, that instant of handing over is so threatening that it is better to refuse the gesture and stay covered, even if that means staying alone.

And here I want to let fall, with weight, the root of this, because it almost always comes from a real wound. At some moment, probably early, you received, you lowered your guard, you needed, and you were hurt in that uncovered place. You showed a need and it was ignored, or ridiculed, or used. You leaned on someone and the support gave way. You entrusted your fragility to someone who should have guarded it and it was betrayed, or exposed, or devalued. And the part of you that lived that drew a survival conclusion: receiving is dangerous, showing that I need is dangerous, being uncovered is dangerous, therefore I don't receive, I don't need, I don't show, I stay on the safe side, on the side of someone who gives, where never again can anyone hurt me in the vulnerable place of someone who receives.

Rest a moment, and feel the weight of this, because it is a load you have been dragging for so long that you already confuse it with your own body. Imagine what it is to never be able to lower your guard. To never be able to simply receive, to rest in another's care, to let yourself be supported without vigilance. Imagine the loneliness of being always in the position of someone who gives, always covered, always strong, without ever being able to be, for once, the one who is held. It is not only tiredness, it is a hunger, the hunger to be you, for once, the one cared for, the one received, the one supported, and the terror, at the same time, of exposing yourself by doing it. You live between this hunger and this terror, and the terror has always won, and so the hunger is never satisfied, and you go on giving everyone the water that no one lets you drink, because you yourself do not let them give it to you.

I want to stop here a moment, because there is a point at which this difficulty stops being a trait and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If you recognize, beneath the deflecting, ancient experiences in which showing need cost you dearly in truth, in which you were hurt, betrayed, or abandoned in the vulnerable place of someone who needs, if the exhaustion of always giving and never receiving already robs you of body and spirit, or if the loneliness of never letting yourself be supported weighs on you in a way that does not lift, then this deserves to be cared for with support. There are wounds in the capacity to receive that were engraved by real pain, and that are not resolved by reading alone nor by force of will. To say it is the most honest way of taking care of you: sometimes, the first place where a woman who never knew how to receive learns, at last, to do it, is precisely in a relationship of help, where someone trained holds her without ever using her fragility against her, and where she can, perhaps for the first time, lower her guard in safety.

Let me tell you something that changes the way of looking at this. The exposure you so fear, the vulnerability of receiving, is not, today, the danger it was then. At that time, with that person, in that context, showing your need may indeed have been dangerous. But you are no longer that child, you are no longer in that place, you no longer depend on those people. Today you can choose to whom you expose yourself, you can receive from those who can be trusted, you can lower your guard before those who deserved you to lower it. Your shell protects you from a danger that belongs to the past, and in doing so it deprives you of a richness that is available in the present. To recognize that the time of that danger has passed is the first step toward allowing yourself, at last, to receive without the old terror.

## 5. Receiving too is loving

If there is one sentence I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it carefully, because it turns your deepest belief upside down: receiving too is a form of loving. It is not the passive, weak, inferior side of love. It is the other half, as active and as generous as giving, and without it love is not complete. When you refuse to receive, you think you are being humble, sparing the other, not weighing on them. You are, in truth, refusing them half of love, keeping them from the joy of giving to you, leaving them out of the reciprocity that makes a relationship alive.

Turn this sentence around and look closely at its reverse, because it is there that it shakes you. You see giving as the gesture of love, and receiving as a kind of weakness that is tolerated. And I propose that you see the truth: to receive well is an act of love as great as to give well. Because when you receive, you give the other the place of someone who gives, you honor their gesture, you let their love reach you and fulfill its purpose. Think about how you feel when you give something to someone and that person receives it with pleasure, with gratitude, with joy, without deflecting, without minimizing, without refusing. You feel good, you feel that your gesture arrived, that it was worth it. Now think about how you feel when you give and the person refuses, deflects, gives back. You feel the gesture fall into the void, your offer being refused, a frustration. Well, this is exactly what you do to others when you don't receive: you let their gestures fall into the void, you refuse their offers, you frustrate the love they want to give you.

And there is a wisdom in this that comes from very far, from the way balance works between people. Between parents and children, the exchange is unequal and it is right that way: the parents give, the children receive. But between adults, between equals, love lives off an exchange that circulates in both directions, and whoever only gives and never receives places herself, without knowing it, always in the upper position, in the position of someone who provides, and leaves the other always below, in the position of someone who only receives and can never repay. This, which seems like humility, is in truth a way of keeping yourself always above, always in the strong position, and it prevents the equality from which intimacy is born. There is no meeting between equals when one of the people refuses, always, to come down to the place of someone who receives. To let yourself receive is, paradoxically, an act of true humility: it is accepting to come down from the position of someone who only gives and to be, for once, at the same level, in the back-and-forth in which both give and both receive.

Look now at the same truth from the side of those who love you, which is where it hurts most. You, who take such pride in taking care of others, in making them feel good, are, without knowing it, depriving them of one of the greatest joys there is: that of taking care of you. Each time you deflect a compliment, refuse a help, don't let them pamper you, you are telling the other "your love for me has nowhere to land." And that hurts those who love you, even if they don't say it, because no one likes to have their love refused, to never manage to give anything to someone they love. Your inability to receive, which you think a virtue, is an involuntary way of keeping those who love you always on the outside, always in the position of someone who cannot reach you. To learn to receive is, after all, a gift you give to those who wish you well: the gift of letting them love you.

And I want you to see how this connects to money and to value, because it is the heart of this collection. Your difficulty in receiving does not stay in compliments and care, it extends to everything that could reach you: to recognition, to payment, to abundance, to money itself. You are capable of generating a great deal of value, of taking care, of serving, of producing, and at the moment of receiving what that is worth, you deflect, minimize, lower, let it pass, because receiving the value back puts you in the same exposed position of someone who receives. That is why you can work immensely and receive little, give a great deal and have little back, not for lack of value, but out of an inability to let it return to you. The intake tap is shut for material value too, and until you open it, you will go on being a source that gushes for everyone and that never waters itself. To learn to receive, in your case, is not only an emotional cure, it is the practical condition for the abundance you generate to be able, at last, to stay with you too.

And I want to show you what opens when you receive, because it is precious. When you receive well, without deflecting, the relationship breathes in a new way. The other feels useful, important, capable of giving you something, and that brings you closer. The reciprocity, which was blocked by your only giving, begins to flow, and the relationship becomes more equal, more alive, more true. And you, who lived always covered, experience something you may never have known: the rest of being supported, the sweetness of being cared for, the relief of not having to be always the strong one. It is a new experience, and it will cost you to believe in it each time, but each time you do it, the hardened caregiver in you learns something she never knew: that she too could be cared for, that she too had the right to receive, that receiving did not make her smaller, it made her, at last, part of love in both directions.

The awakening, when it comes, does not come like an idea, it comes like a sweet surrender. It is the moment when you let, for once, someone take care of you, give you something, support you, and instead of the usual deflection, the discomfort, the hurry to repay, you stay. You receive. You let the gesture arrive whole. And you notice, with amazement, that nothing terrible happened, that you exposed yourself to no danger, that you did not become smaller, that on the contrary, that moment of receiving warmed you, brought you closer, reminded you that you too deserve to be cared for. It is a small and enormous instant, because it disproves, with the force of experience, a belief of a whole lifetime, and opens the door to a way of being loved that you had always refused yourself.

## 6. Receiving is not debt, it is circulation

There is a belief at the center of everything, and that I want to undo carefully, because it is from it that part of your deflecting is born: the idea that receiving is being left in debt. That when you receive, you are left owing, and that being left owing tightens around you, weighs on you, puts you in a position you have to settle quickly. And so, either you refuse so as not to be left owing, or you receive and immediately give back to erase the debt. But this idea, that receiving creates a debt, is an impoverished way of seeing what happens, and I want to offer you another.

Notice that the word "debt" carries, for you, a weight that perhaps comes from far. In a family where everything was counted, where you had to pay for what you received, where being left owing was dangerous or humiliating, the child learns that receiving opens an account that has to be closed, and grows up with the tense relationship you have with receiving. But there are families, and there are relationships, in which giving and receiving generate no debt at all, in which one gives and receives with the lightness of someone who knows that love is not accounted for. Perhaps you never lived that, and so it costs you to believe that it exists. But it exists, and it is precisely what this book wants to show you: that there is a receiving that does not put you in debt, that does not oblige you, that does not tighten around you, because it happens within a circulation of affection where no one keeps the account. And your work is not to learn to pay more quickly the debts that receiving creates, it is to learn that, with the right people, receiving creates no debt at all.

Receiving is not being left in debt. It is entering a circulation. Imagine love, care, value, not as an account of credit and debit, but as a water that circulates between people, that passes from some to others and comes back, in a continuous movement, without anyone keeping the account. When you receive, you are not left owing anything, you merely receive the water that is circulating, and that water, naturally, will go out from you again to others, not as payment of a debt, but as the natural continuation of the flow. The circulation has neither creditors nor debtors. It has only movement, life, exchange. And your error, the error that makes you refuse, is seeing everything as debt when it is, in truth, circulation.

Notice the difference this makes. If receiving is debt, then each gesture you accept tightens around you, obliges you, diminishes you, and it is better to refuse. If receiving is circulation, then each gesture you accept is merely your turn to be on the side that receives, knowing that there will be other times when you will be on the side that gives, and that what matters is not to keep the accounts balanced at every instant, but to let the water flow. Whoever is in this second vision receives with lightness, without the tightness of debt, because she knows that she is not contracting any commitment, she is only taking part in the flow of life, which gives and receives, gives and receives, endlessly and without account.

And I want to show you how this vision connects to the theme of this whole collection. When you refuse to receive, you are not only refusing compliments and care, you are shutting the door through which value would come into your life. You are a woman who knows how to make value go out, give, distribute, take care, and does not know how to let it in, stay, be received. And the value that cannot come in ends up being scarce, not because you deserve it less, but because you shut the door of entry. Your life has an outflow tap always open and an inflow tap almost shut, and so, however much you give, you are always running dry, always tired, always giving from a reservoir that no one lets you fill. To learn to receive is to open the inflow tap, it is to let the water you give to others also come back to you, so that you have, at last, somewhere to give from without exhausting yourself.

Notice that there is a difference between this vision and the way you were taught to see exchanges, and that it frees. You were taught, probably, a rigorous accounting: don't be left owing, pay for what you received, keep the accounts balanced, don't be taken advantage of. And there is prudence in that, in certain contexts. But applied to love, to close relationships, to care, that accounting kills the circulation, because it turns each gesture into a transaction that has to close, and love does not close, love flows. Among those who truly love each other, no one keeps the account of who gave more, because the account does not matter, what matters is the flow, the back-and-forth, the life that passes from one to the other. When you learn to receive without accounting, to let the water flow without measuring it, you are going to discover that your relationships breathe in a new way, lighter, more alive, free of the weight of an accounting that only served to keep you always on the creditor side, always covered, always alone in your sufficiency.

I am not going to give you a method for receiving without feeling it as a debt, because it would be one more task, and we would be feeding your tendency to turn everything into something to manage well. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because unlearning the deflecting of a whole lifetime is not done in a page. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: let the water flow inward too. When someone gives you something, do not calculate the debt, do not hurry to repay, do not deflect. Receive, let it in, give thanks, and trust that the flow continues, that there will be your turn to give, and that for now your task, the hardest of all for you, is simply to let yourself receive.

## 7. Receiving without apologizing

There is a destination for this crossing, and it fits in an image: to receive without apologizing. Without the "oh, it wasn't necessary," without the "I feel so bad," without the deflection, without the hurry to repay, without the discomfort of someone who occupied a place she shouldn't have. To receive like someone who is, with dignity and right, in the place of someone who receives, as legitimate as that of someone who gives. And I want to show you what this is, because it is the opposite of your whole life, and it is beautiful.

Notice how you receive today, when you are forced to receive. You apologize. Not in these words, perhaps, but with your whole posture: you shrink, you minimize, you justify yourself, you repay quickly, you do everything to show that you are not comfortable in that place, that you know it is not yours, that you are going to leave it as soon as you can. It is as if receiving were a transgression for which you have to do penance. And that penance ruins the gesture, for you and for whoever gives, because it turns a moment that should be one of sweetness into a moment of discomfort, of apologies, of hurried balancing.

To receive without apologizing is the opposite. It is accepting the gesture whole, with pleasure, with gratitude, without shrinking, without justifying yourself, without giving it back immediately. It is occupying the place of someone who receives with the same naturalness and the same dignity with which you occupy the place of someone who gives. It is saying "thank you" and staying there, in the received, without fleeing. There is nothing wrong with being on the side of someone who receives. It is not an inferior place, it is not a failing, it is not a debt. It is one of the two natural positions of love, and to occupy it with dignity is as beautiful, and as necessary, as to give with generosity. To apologize for receiving is to apologize for being loved, and for being loved you should never, ever, have to apologize.

I want to leave you with a question that can serve as a compass, because it cuts straight to the habit: when someone gives me something and I deflect, whom am I taking care of in that moment? You are going to discover, almost always, that you are not taking care of the other, you are protecting yourself, fleeing the discomfort of receiving, even though you disguise it as modesty or as not wanting to bother. The deflecting, which looks like a generous gesture, is in truth a gesture of self-protection: you are refusing the exposure, not sparing the other person. And when you see this clearly, the deflecting loses its noble justification, and you can begin to choose, instead of it, the braver and more loving gesture of staying and receiving. Because receiving, in your case, is not the easy path, it is the hard path, it is the act of courage, it is the surrender that costs you, and that is why, precisely, it is what has the most value for you.

And I want to speak to you about what this requires, because it requires something that frightens you: tolerating vulnerability without fleeing from it. To receive without apologizing means staying in the exposed, uncovered place of someone who receives, and bearing the vertigo it gives you, without running at once to cover yourself again with a gesture of giving. It means allowing yourself to be seen fragile, needy, supported, and not dying of it, and discovering, slowly, that being seen that way does not diminish you, it brings you closer. It is a training in tolerance of exposure, and it is made in small steps: a compliment received without deflection, a help accepted without immediate repayment, a care allowed without your getting up to take care back. Each of these small acts is a time when you stay in the vulnerable place and survive, and each survival teaches your body that receiving is no longer the danger it was.

Think now of those who watch you closely and learn to live by seeing you, because perhaps this is what moves you when nothing else does. If you have children, or people who learn by looking at you, notice what you show them about receiving. If they see you always give and never accept, take care of everyone and never let yourself be taken care of, deflect each gesture directed at them, they are learning from you that receiving is suspect, that the dignified position is only that of someone who gives, that showing need is weakness. And it is likely that they will wear the same shell, and that they will exhaust themselves one day as you exhaust yourself. The most freeing thing you can do for those who learn to live by looking at you is not to give them even more. It is to let yourself, in front of them, receive, be cared for, accept with pleasure, so that they learn that receiving is dignified, that needing is human, that love flows in both directions. Sometimes a woman who never knew how to receive learns it, at last, so as not to condemn the same loneliness to those she loves.

And I want to tell you something about reciprocity, because it may touch you. You want true, close, intimate relationships. But true intimacy is, by definition, reciprocal: two who give to and receive from each other, who show themselves and welcome each other, who take care of each other mutually. A relationship in which only you give and never receive is not intimate, it is one of assistance, however much love it has, because it lacks the half in which you hand yourself over, show yourself, let yourself be supported. By refusing to receive, you are, without meaning to, condemning your dearest relationships to being always unequal, always with only one giving herself, always without the reciprocity that would make them deep. To learn to receive is not only a care for yourself. It is what is missing for your relationships to be able, at last, to become what you always wanted them to be: meetings of two, and not of one who takes care and another who is taken care of forever.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that you may not even remember wanting, so used are you to giving: the joy of being cared for. Of falling ill and letting someone tend to you without getting up. Of being down and letting someone support you without deflecting. Of receiving a treat and enjoying it whole, without guilt, without hurry. Of being, for once, the center of someone's care, and resting there, instead of fleeing to take care. That joy is your right, as much yours as anyone's, and it is, notice, also a gift to those who love you, because you finally give them what they always wanted to be able to give you: their care, their love, their turn to be, for you, the side that gives.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in a simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything at once, and it doesn't make you receive without discomfort from one day to the next. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your deflecting with humility, and to begin to suspect that receiving, far from diminishing you, was precisely what was missing for you to be loved whole.

Now you know that you deflect everything that comes in your direction, and that giving is easy because it keeps you covered and receiving takes the ground from under you because it exposes you. You know that your difficulty is not one of belonging, it is one of position: you know how to be on the side of someone who gives and not on that of someone who receives. You know that the shadow that pushes you is vulnerability, and that the caregiver was your perfect hiding place against it. You know, and this was the hardest, that receiving frightens more than giving because receiving exposes, and that that exposure hurt you in the past but is no longer the danger it was. You know that receiving too is loving, and that by refusing you are refusing half of love to those who want to give to you. You know that receiving is not debt, it is circulation. And you know that you can receive without apologizing, occupying the place of someone who receives with the same dignity with which you always occupied that of someone who gives.

I leave you with a figure to take with you, because what we said fits whole inside it. For years you were like a doctor who treats everyone and never lets herself be examined, who knows everyone's illness and hides her own, who is always on the side of someone who heals and never on the side of someone who is healed. And a doctor who never lets herself be cared for falls ill alone, in secret, while treating others, until one day the body gives way in the middle of an appointment. The crossing I propose to you is not to stop taking care. It is to lie down, for once, on the table. It is to let someone examine you, treat you, support you. It is to discover that being cared for does not take from you the value of someone who takes care, it merely gives you back the human half that you had amputated, that of someone who also needs, also falls ill, also deserves to be treated with the same tenderness with which you treat everyone except yourself.

Before closing, keep a distinction that everything else needs so as not to be twisted. To learn to receive is not to stop taking care, giving, being generous. Your gift of taking care stays with you whole, intact, no one comes to take it from you. It is only a matter of recovering the half you are missing, without giving up the one you already have. You go on being the one who takes care, when it makes sense, with the pleasure of always. You merely stop being stuck in that single position, and come to be able to also receive, be cared for, let yourself be supported, as life asks. You stop having half of love and come to have love whole, in both directions. And a woman who knows how to give and receive is not less generous than one who only gives. She is more whole, and she is, at last, less alone.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can receive without apologizing.

Seeing this was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not made by reading, it is made by staying, slowly, in the exposed place of someone who receives, and by discovering, time after time, that there you do not die, you are loved.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about money, nor is it going to teach you to earn more. It is a space for you to look at the way you keep value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the most physical ways: that of only knowing how to be on the side that gives, deflecting everything that comes in your direction, with the inflow tap almost shut. There, the crossing is to learn to occupy the place of someone who receives, to tolerate vulnerability without fleeing, to let the water flow inward too. Not so that you stop taking care, but so that care, at last, reaches you too.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There is a mother there who fears weighing, who does not let herself be cared for so as not to be a burden, who erases herself so as not to be any trouble. You are from the same family, but your question is not old age nor the fear of weighing, it is the position of someone who receives value, the uncovered place that you don't know how to occupy. One fears weighing, the other fears exposing herself. But deep down it is the same journey: to learn that we too have the right to be the side that receives, and that letting ourselves be cared for does not make us a burden nor diminish us, it completes us.

If you feel that it is the moment, you can find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is all right, and you do not have to repay this reading with anything. What you have read is already yours, received, with no debt, waiting, with no hurry, for the day you allow yourself to stay, without deflecting, in the place of someone who receives.

Let me ask you one last thing, one only, and the very one that will cost you most, and it is exactly because it costs you so much that I ask it of you. The next time someone gives you something, a compliment, a help, a care, a treat, do not deflect. Do not minimize, do not give back, do not hurry to repay, do not apologize. Look the person in the eyes, say "thank you," and stay. Receive it whole. Let the gesture reach all the way down. Notice the discomfort, the urge to flee to the place of someone who gives, and do not flee. Because it is in that staying, in the exposed place of someone who receives, without apologizing, that the deflecting of a whole lifetime begins to come undone, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that receiving did not diminish you, it was the turn for you too to be, at last, loved.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
