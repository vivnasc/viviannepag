# The solo mother

**For the mother who raises her children alone and believes she has to be enough for two. You do not have to carry the weight of two in order to be enough.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This book hasn't come to teach you how to organize your life as a solo mother better, because your problem was never a lack of organization, you organize the impossible every single day. It has come to look at the wound underneath the effort. And I want to tell you, before we begin, something important: raising children alone is a continuous effort, and continuous effort, without replenishment, can lead to burnout, which is real and which has a limit. If you feel that you no longer recover even when you stop, that you've lost the pleasure, the sleep, the hope, please seek support. It is not weakness. It is the most necessary thing you can do, for yourself and for those who depend on you.*

---

## 1. The one who cannot fall

There is a sentence that organizes your life from the inside, even if you've never said it to anyone: I cannot fall. I cannot get sick, I cannot give up, I cannot stop, I cannot collapse, because if I fall, there is no one else underneath. Others can have a bad day, can hand the night watch over to someone, can break a little and be held. Not you. You are the only pillar, and a pillar can't afford the luxury of giving way, because the entire roof depends on it.

And so you endure. You endure the exhaustion, you endure the fever, you endure the sadness, you endure the badly slept night and the day that begins all the same at the same hour. You solve everything: the bills, the schedules, the school, the food, the clothes, the illnesses, their fears and yours, which you swallow so they won't see them. You do the work of a whole household with a single pair of hands, and you still go to work, and you still smile, and you still show up. You are the one who never misses, the one who never fails, the one who is always there, the one who copes. And the world around you has grown so accustomed to your coping that it no longer even notices the price.

There is an image you may recognize: the end of the day, when the children finally fall asleep, and instead of the relief you deserved, another list arrives. The laundry, the dishes, tomorrow's appointment, the bill still to be paid, the note from school. You sit down for a moment and immediately get up again, because there is always one more thing, and the idea of simply stopping seems to you a luxury you can't allow yourself, almost an irresponsibility. And when you finally lie down, exhausted, your head won't switch off, it keeps spinning around what's missing, what could go wrong, what you'll have to solve in the morning. You rest your body without resting your mind, and you wake up already tired, and you start over. There is not, in your day, a single moment that is yours, without a task, without vigilance, without the weight of being the one responsible for everything.

Because there is something almost no one asks you: and you, how are you? People ask about your children, about their school, about their health. Sometimes they ask if you need something practical. But rarely does anyone stop, look you in the eyes, and ask how you are, truly, on the inside, not the mother, not the one who solves things, you. And since no one asks, you stop asking yourself too, because there's no time or space for an answer that might hurt too much. You go on living on the surface of doing, of the next task, of what's left to solve, and the question of how you are stays postponed for a day that never comes.

And I want to tell you something that perhaps no one tells you: what you do is heroic, and at the same time you shouldn't have to be a heroine. Both things are true. It is right to recognize the dimension of what you carry, the strength you show, the great deal you give, and I don't want, on any page, to minimize that. But recognizing the heroism is not the same as celebrating it as if it should be this way. You shouldn't have to be a heroine every single day. No one should. Daily heroism is not a healthy state in which to live an entire life, it is an emergency response, and you've been living in a permanent emergency for far too long. That is why, when along this book I ask you to receive, to set things down, to let yourself be helped, I am not taking away the merit of what you've done. I am telling you that you deserve to come out of the state of emergency, that you deserve a life in which you don't have to be a heroine in order to survive, and that this is possible, even if right now it seems unthinkable.

I want you to know, right from the start, that this book hasn't come to join those that treat you like a manager who lacks method. I haven't come to give you tips for organizing your days better, for balancing things better, for being a more efficient solo mother. You already have an efficiency no one imagines, you do daily what would seem impossible seen from outside. The problem was never your capability. I've come to look at something else, deeper and less spoken of: at the wound that lives beneath all that effort, and that no organization can solve, because it isn't a question of logistics, it is a question of a belief you carry and that costs you dearly.

I want you to know, too, that this book hasn't come to romanticize your situation or to pretend it's easy. Raising children alone is, very often, genuinely hard, and there are real difficulties, material, practical, that no change of perspective resolves completely. I am not going to tell you that it's all a matter of attitude, because it isn't, and it would be a lack of respect for what you carry. What this book can do is not take away the real difficulties of life. It is to take away one extra weight, which adds to all the others and which, that one, yes, is avoidable: the weight of believing you have to carry everything absolutely alone, without needing, without asking, without receiving, proving at all times that you can endure. That weight, the weight of the belief, is what we are going to set down. The concrete difficulties will go on asking for concrete solutions, and concrete support. But without the weight of the belief on top, even those become lighter to carry.

That belief, which we are going to take apart slowly throughout these pages, has a simple and devastating form: I am alone. I'm not speaking only of the concrete absence of a partner, although it is real. I'm speaking of a deeper loneliness, the loneliness of someone who believes she has to carry everything, who can't ask, who can't need, whose whole existence rests on her shoulders and on no one else's. It is that belief that drains you, more than the tasks. And that is why resting doesn't rest you, why even in the rare moments of pause you don't truly set yourself down, because the belief stays lit, telling you that you can't lower your guard, that if you relax, everything falls.

And I want to name a very particular loneliness of the solo mother, because it is rarely spoken: the loneliness of having no one with whom to share the small joys and the small fears of your children. When your child takes his first step, says his first word, has a victory at school, there is a part of you that turns to the side to share it, and there's no one beside you. When at night you worry about a fever, a behavior, a decision, there is no one with whom to divide the worry at two in the morning, no one who worries about that specific child as much as you do. You carry alone not only the tasks, but the whole emotion of motherhood, the joys no one divides and the fears no one eases. That emotional loneliness is, very often, heavier than the logistics, and it is one of the ones that most deserves to be heard, and one of the ones that eases most when you begin to let other people in, in other ways, into your life and into your children's.

I am not going to ask you, in this book, to do less, nor to feel guilty about the great deal you do. What I am going to do is help you see that being enough never meant being two people, that needing is not failing, and that there is a way to ease the weight that does not pass through organizing your endurance better, but through finally, and perhaps for the first time in a long while, letting yourself also receive. It is a path against everything you've learned. But it is the only one that truly takes away the weight.

## 2. The weight of two places

I want to look with you at a task you took on without ever consciously choosing it, and which may be the greatest source of your exhaustion: the task of being two. Of occupying, alone, two places. Of trying to be, at the same time, the mother and what is missing, of giving your children not only everything a mother gives, but also everything they don't have from the other side.

And I want to tell you something with a great deal of tenderness, because I know the effort there is in this: you can be an excellent mother. You can give your children love, security, values, presence, everything that is within a mother's reach to give. What you can't do, because no one can, is turn yourself into two people. You can't be, at the same time, yourself and what is missing. And when you try, when you carry on your shoulders not only your place but also the one that is empty, you take on an impossible task, and impossible tasks always have the same ending: they drain whoever attempts them, and they leave that person, on top of it all, with the feeling that she failed, when what failed was the impossibility of the task, not the person.

Think of the effort of duplicating yourself as a person trying to hold up, alone, a table that was made to have four legs and has only two, yours. So the table won't fall, you have to stay there always, your hands serving as the missing legs, never able to let go, never able to do anything else, because the instant you let go, everything would come crashing down. It is exhausting, and it is impossible to keep up forever, and on top of that it traps you there, without freedom, hostage to a table that depends entirely on your not moving. But what if the table, after all, were more stable than you think? What if it had other supports that you aren't seeing because you're too busy being all the legs yourself? A large part of this crossing is discovering that you don't have to be, alone, all the legs of the table, that there are other possible supports, and that the table, with them, holds up without your having to live glued to it, never able to take your hands away.

There is an invisible guilt that usually lies beneath this effort, and it's worth naming: the guilt of thinking that your children have less because of you, and that it falls to you to compensate for that entire lack. You see other families with two parents, and you feel that your children are at a disadvantage, and that it is your duty to fill the hole, to be so good that they don't even feel what's missing. And so you duplicate yourself, you push yourself twice as hard, you give until there's nothing left, in the attempt to keep the absence from weighing on them. It is an enormous love, this. But it is also a crushing weight, and it is born of an idea we need to look at head on: the idea that it falls to you, alone, to compensate for everything that's missing.

I want to tell you, too, something about what your children actually register, because it may relieve you of an impossible race. Children don't do the accounting you do. They don't go around comparing your home with the homes of two parents, tallying up what they lack in relation to others, in the obsessive way you do it for them. What they register, what actually stays, is whether they felt loved, seen, safe, whether there was someone present and reliable in their lives. And that, a solo mother gives as well as any family, sometimes better, because the love of a present mother is worth more than the presence of two who are absent. You are draining yourself compensating for a lack that you measure by your adult yardstick, when what your children need, and what you already give them, is something much simpler: a mother who loves them and who is there. That, you are. And that is enough, far more than your guilt lets you believe.

Because the truth, which we are going to deepen in the coming chapters, is that there are things you don't have to compensate for, because they aren't yours to compensate for. There is a place that is not yours to fill, however hard you try, and trying to fill it, besides draining you, doesn't even help your children the way you think. But for now keep only this: that being enough for your children never depended on your becoming two. A whole mother, present, who takes care of herself, is worth infinitely more to a child than a duplicated mother who wears herself out until she disappears in the attempt to also be what's missing.

And there is an effect of this impossible task that touches your children, and that may surprise you, because it is the opposite of what you intended. When you push yourself to compensate for everything, to be so complete that they don't even feel what's missing, you may, without meaning to, pass on to them a heavy message: that there is an enormous hole that has to be covered at all costs, and that you are sacrificing yourself entirely to cover it. Children feel the effort, they feel the sacrifice, and very often they begin to carry, themselves, a guilt over what you cost yourself. In trying to spare them the lack, you may end up giving them something else to carry: the weight of a mother who wears herself out for them. A mother who accepts her own limits with serenity, on the other hand, who doesn't kill herself compensating, teaches her children something precious, that one can live well even with what's missing, that the lack is not a catastrophe to be hidden, but a reality one crosses with dignity.

Notice what this impossible task does to you, day after day. It keeps you in permanent effort, without ever reaching the feeling of having done enough, because enough, in the logic of compensating for an entire absence, never comes. However much you do, there is always one side left uncovered, and the guilt takes note. And so you live in a race without a finish line, trying to reach an enough that always flees, and that is one of the deepest tirednesses there is: not the tiredness of someone who does a lot, but the tiredness of someone who does a lot and feels, all the same, that it wasn't enough. That tiredness is not cured with more effort. It is cured by dismantling the idea that you had to be two.

## 3. The place that cannot be filled

There is a place, in your children's lives, that you have been trying to fill, and I want to speak of it with the utmost care, because it is delicate ground. It is the place of the father, or of the figure who is missing, whatever the story that brought you here. And there is a distinction here that can relieve you greatly, if you let it in: the distinction between the concrete man and the symbolic place.

The concrete man is a person, with his own story, and he may have failed, may have left, may have made himself absent, may have done harm, may simply not be there. That is a reality, and you don't have to beautify it nor pretend it was different from what it was. But there is, beyond the concrete man, a symbolic place, the place of your children's other origin, the place from which half of who they are comes. And that place, unlike the man, does not disappear when the man disappears. It goes on existing inside your children, because they do, in fact, come from two, even when they grow up with one.

The task that drains you is the task of trying to fill that place, of being the other half yourself, of occupying the space that was left empty. And I want to tell you, carefully, that this task not only drains you but isn't even what your children need. Because what harms a child is not the lack of an idealized father, perfect, present. It is living in a system where half of his origin was erased, denied, turned into silence or into poison. The child does not need you to be the father. He needs to be able to exist whole, with his two origins acknowledged, even if one of them is not present.

In the way I have learned to look at families, there is an idea that helps here: the idea that each child needs to be able to honor his two origins in order to feel whole, even if one of them is not present, even if it failed. Not because the absent origin deserves it, but because it is part of the child, and when one half of the origin is erased with contempt, the child, who knows himself to be made of both, may feel, without knowing why, that half of himself is also unworthy. That is why the care is not to praise someone who doesn't deserve it, it is not to turn half of your child's origin into a void full of rage or of silence. To let it exist, simply, as a fact, even a painful one, instead of an open wound. This is subtle, and every case is its own case, and sometimes it is done better with help. But the direction relieves you too, because it takes off your shoulders the task of being the one to cover, with your effort, the whole place of the other.

The movement, then, is not to fill the place. It is to stop trying to fill it. It is to recognize, within yourself and for your children, to the measure of what is healthy and true, that this place exists, that they come from two, without that obliging you to idealize the one who failed nor to erase him from the map. It is not about praising an absent father, nor about forcing a contact that is not possible or safe. It is only about ceasing to carry the weight of two places yourself, and letting the other's place be what it is, a place, even if empty, instead of a vacancy that you demand of yourself, alone, to fill.

In the way I have learned to look at families, there is a delicate idea here that may relieve you, and that touches not the presence of the other, but his place. When someone is erased completely from a family system, struck out with rage or with silence as if he had never existed, that erasure, however justified it may seem, tends to weigh, and to weigh precisely upon the child, who knows himself, in his body, to be made of two. To include the other's place is not, in any way, to forgive the one who failed, nor to bring him back, nor to pretend that what was bad was good. It is only to acknowledge, within yourself, a simple fact: that your child came from two, and that half of his origin, even absent, even painful, is part of who he is. There is an inner sentence that many mothers find relieving, said only to themselves, with no need to share it: "I see that he also comes from you, and that belongs to him, even though you are not here." To acknowledge the place is not to give it a worth it didn't have, it is to take off your back the weight of having to erase half of the story, and to spare your child the silent weight of an origin struck out with iron. And, in doing so, you become lighter too, because you stop carrying, on top of everything else, the impossible task of pretending he came from you alone.

I want to be careful here, because every story is different, and you know yours. There are situations in which acknowledging the place of the other origin is simple, and there are others in which it is painful, complicated, or even impossible, because there was violence, abandonment, danger. I am not asking you to speak well of someone who did harm, nor to force a presence where it is not safe, nor to lie to your children about what happened. To acknowledge the symbolic place is not to rehabilitate the concrete man. It is only not to make your children beings of a single origin, not to erase half of where they come from as if it had never existed, because that half lives in them, whether you like it or not, and to deny it entirely is to deny a part of them. How to do this, with what words, to what measure, depends on your story, and sometimes it is matter to be done with help. But the direction is this: neither idealize, nor erase. Let the place be what it is, so that you stop having to fill it yourself.

And there is an enormous relief hidden in this, if you let it arrive. When you stop trying to also be the other half, you set down half of the weight you were carrying. You stop having to be two, and you go back to being able to be, simply, a mother, whole, present, enough in your own place, which is large and is good and is enough. Your children don't need you to be perfect nor double. They need a whole mother in the place of mother, and that, yes, you can be, and probably already are, far more than you believe.

## 4. The identity of the one who endures

I want now to look at something that is not about what you do, but about who you have become. Because over all the years of enduring, of resisting, of not falling, an identity has formed in you, a way of being in the world, and that identity has a name: the one who endures. The strong one. The one who copes. The one who doesn't need. The one who bears everything.

Notice how, in other mothers' stories, identity is built around motherhood, around caring, around being needed. In yours, there is another layer on top: the identity was built around endurance, around the capacity to bear what seemed unbearable. You became the woman who doesn't collapse, and that image, however much it costs you to sustain, has also become a source of pride, of identity, of worth. When people tell you "I don't know how you manage," there is a part of you that recognizes itself there, that is exactly that, the one who manages, the one who endures what others wouldn't endure.

And there is a shadow in this identity, a hidden belief that sustains it and that is worth looking at head on: if I stop, everything falls. This belief makes stopping, for you, unthinkable, dangerous, almost a betrayal. And it makes needing something, asking for help, showing fragility, seem like a flaw in your very identity, as if by needing you stopped being who you are. There is another belief, sister to this one, equally hidden: I have no permission to need. As if needing were a luxury you can't allow yourself, a weakness your role doesn't permit, something others can do but you can't.

There is an origin to this identity that often comes from before, from before you were a solo mother, and it's worth looking at. Perhaps you learned early, as a child, that you couldn't count on anyone, that you had to fend for yourself, that asking brought no answer or brought punishment. Perhaps you grew up in a home where you were the one who took care of others, the most responsible, the one who held things together, long before you had children. If that's how it was, solo motherhood didn't create your identity of the one who endures, it found it already formed and pushed it to the extreme. And that is important to know, because it means that your difficulty in receiving didn't begin with your current situation, it is older, it is a way of being in the world that you learned in order to survive when you were small and truly had no one to lean on. Today, you are no longer that child without anyone. But your system goes on working as if you were, and part of this crossing is to show it, slowly, that the time of having to do everything alone, with no one to count on, may already have passed.

I want to tell you something about this identity of the one who endures, with all my respect for the real strength there is in it: it is an armor, not your essence. The strength you showed, and you showed a great deal, is true, and it saved you, and it saved your children, in times when you truly had to endure alone. But an armor that is never taken off stops protecting and begins to crush. And there is a risk in confusing the armor with yourself, in believing that you are the one who endures, and nothing more, because then to lose the endurance would be to lose yourself, and you cling to it even when it is already harming you.

Notice a subtle cost of this identity, one that touches your relationship with other adults. The one who endures, the one who never needs, the one who copes with everything alone, becomes, without meaning to, hard to help and hard to love up close. Because she doesn't let anyone in, doesn't show fragility, doesn't ask, and people, even those who care for you, end up pulling away, not because they don't like you, but because they can't find a door to come in by, a place where they'd be useful, a space where the relationship is an exchange and not just admiration from a distance. So the armor that protects you also isolates you, and your isolation confirms your belief that you are alone, and the belief reinforces the armor, in a circle that closes over you. Lowering your guard, showing that you too need, does not make you more fragile in the eyes of those who love you. It makes you more reachable, closer, more possible to love. People draw near to those who let them in, not to those who never need anything.

And that is why the question of this chapter is one of the hardest you can be asked, and I want to leave it with you gently: who are you when you're not surviving? Who are you beneath the armor, when you aren't enduring, solving, resisting? Perhaps you don't know, and it's natural that you don't, because you've lived in survival mode for so long that you've almost forgotten there is a woman beneath the one who endures. But she exists. She has desires, tirednesses, longings, a life that is not only carrying. And part of this crossing, the deepest part, is finding her again, the woman who exists beyond the one who endures.

## 5. Tiredness is not burnout

This is the most important chapter of this book for your health, and I want you to read it with all your attention, because it can make the difference between staying on your feet and collapsing without seeing the fall coming.

There is a difference, which many solo mothers don't know, between tiredness and burnout, and knowing it can save you. Tiredness is normal, it is human, it is what you feel at the end of a hard day, of a difficult week, of an intense phase. Tiredness has one essential characteristic: it passes with rest. You stop, you sleep, you recover, and you have energy again. Burnout is another thing. In burnout, you no longer recover even when you stop. You sleep and wake up tired. The weekend goes by and doesn't replenish you. The energy doesn't come back, the pleasure doesn't come back, the hope doesn't come back. It is a well that rest no longer fills, because you crossed a limit that the body and the soul have, and that you, in enduring, ignored for too long.

And there is a particular reason why the solo mother is so vulnerable to burnout, and it isn't any weakness of yours, it is mathematics. Burnout is born of a prolonged imbalance between what is spent and what is replenished. Those who have someone to share the load with have, at least, some moments of replenishment: someone who takes over the watch, a protected night of sleep, a relieved weekend, a shoulder at the end of the day. You, very often, have none of that, or have very little. You spend full-time and you replenish almost nothing, and that imbalance, kept up over years, is the perfect ground for burnout, not because you are fragile, but because no human being replenishes energy without pauses. That is why, in your case, taking care of yourself is not an extra you do when there's time left over. It is the only way to keep the imbalance from taking you to the bottom. Replenishment, for you, is not a luxury. It is essential maintenance, like putting fuel in a car that can't stop driving.

I want you to know the signs, because burnout settles in slowly and disguises itself as "it's normal, I'm a solo mother, it has to be this way." It doesn't have to be this way. Pay attention if there is a constant irritability, in which everything sets you off, in which you no longer have patience even for what you used to bear. If you cry often, or if, on the contrary, you feel you can no longer cry, that you're anesthetized, distant from everything. If you can't sleep even when exhausted, or if all you want to do is sleep and nothing gets you out of bed. If you've lost the pleasure in things, even in your children, even in what used to gladden you. If there is a background hopelessness, a "this is never going to change," a weight that won't lift. If you feel you can't go on, and that there's no way out. These are signs of burnout, and some of them are already signs of depression.

I want you to understand why it is so easy, for you, not to see the burnout coming. It's that your whole identity was built on the capacity to endure, and so your system learned to ignore the signs that you can no longer endure, because to admit them would be to threaten who you are. You push the tiredness aside, you silence the body, you ignore the warnings, and you go on, because stopping is not in your vocabulary. And so burnout settles in without your seeing it, masked as "it's just this phase," as "I'll rest later," as "I can't right now." Learning to read your own signs, to take seriously what the body tells you, not to silence them in the name of endurance, is one of the most important lessons of this crossing. Your body is not your enemy when it asks you to stop. It is your most honest ally, telling you a truth that your identity of the one who endures keeps you from hearing.

And here I have to tell you, with all firmness and all warmth, something you may not want to hear but that you need to: this is not solved with more endurance. It is not solved by enduring more, by pushing yourself more, by being stronger. Burnout is precisely the result of having endured beyond what was humanly possible to endure, and more endurance only deepens it. What this asks for is the opposite of endurance: it asks you to stop, to ask for help, to seek professional support, a doctor, a psychologist, someone to help you out of the well. It is not an exaggeration. It is not weakness. It is not failing as a mother. It is the most responsible and most urgent care you can take, because your children need you alive, whole, present, and not a mother who wore herself out to the very end proving she could endure.

And I want to speak to you of the fear many solo mothers have of admitting burnout, because it is specific and it is dangerous. It is the fear that, if you say you can't go on, if you show that you're down, someone might think you are not capable of caring for your children, that there could be consequences, judgment, loss. That fear makes you hide the burnout even more, makes you put on a mask that everything is fine precisely when it least is, and keeps you from seeking the help you most need, for fear of what admitting might bring. I want to tell you that asking for help when you're burned out is the opposite of being a bad mother, it is the proof of a responsible mother who recognizes her limits and takes care of herself so she can keep caring. The professionals who help burned-out mothers are not there to judge or to take away, they are there to hold you up, to replenish you, to give you back to yourself. Hiding the burnout out of fear is what truly puts at risk, both you and your children. Asking for help is what protects.

If you recognize yourself in these signs, stop for a moment, and hear me: you deserve to be cared for. Not only your children, you. Your health, your rest, your life, matter, not only for what you can still give, but for yourself, because you are worth it. And seeking help when you're burned out is not abandoning your post, it is the only way to be able to go on holding it without breaking. Please, if any of these lines spoke of you, don't stay alone with it. There are people who can help you carry, and letting yourself be helped, here, is not a luxury, it is a necessity.

## 6. The guilt of a human limit

We are going to arrive now at the point that changes everything, and that touches a very particular guilt, yours, different from the guilt of other mothers. Most mothers feel guilty about things they did or didn't do, about mistakes, about moments. Your guilt is of another kind. You don't blame yourself for a mistake. You blame yourself for a limit. For being human. For not managing to be infinite.

Notice this carefully, because it is subtle and it is important. Your guilt doesn't say "I did wrong." It says "it is never enough." However much you do, and you do an enormous amount, there is always the feeling that it wasn't enough, that you should have given more, been more, endured more. And that guilt has no possible end, because it is not linked to any concrete failure you could repair. It is linked to the fact that you have limits, that you are a single person, that you can't do the work of two without something, somewhere, being left undone. You blame yourself, deep down, for not managing to transcend the human condition. And that is a guilt impossible to settle, because no one transcends the human condition, and you are living as if you were obliged to.

Notice how this guilt is different, and crueler, than the guilt over a mistake. Guilt over a mistake has a cure: you can repair, apologize, do differently, and the guilt drops. Guilt over a limit has no possible cure, because there is nothing to repair, the limit is not a mistake to correct, it is a condition to accept. And so, while you treat your limit as a failure, you stay in a guilt with no way out, trying to repair something that isn't broken, trying to correct the fact of being human. The only way out is not to do more so as to stop having limits, it is to change the way you see limits: not as failures of yours, but as the natural boundary of any person. The day you stop apologizing for being a single person, and begin to treat your limits with respect instead of shame, that guilt, which seemed eternal, finally begins to be able to set itself down.

I want to let fall here the truth that frees, and I ask you to read it slowly: insufficiency is not a failure. It is the human condition. No human being is enough for everything, none copes with everything, none is infinite. To have limits is not a failure of yours, it is what it means to be a person and not a god. And you have lived, for years, demanding of yourself a sufficiency that no human being has, and then punishing yourself for not reaching it. It is like demanding of yourself that you fly and then blaming yourself for not having wings. The problem was never that you didn't reach it. The problem was the impossible measure by which you've been evaluating yourself.

I want to show you where this demand to be infinite comes from, because seeing it born helps to let it go. Society has, for the solo mother, a cruel and contradictory expectation: it expects her to cope with everything alone, without complaining, and then judges her if something fails. People say "she is so strong," and in that praise a trap is hidden, because the praise of strength turns into the obligation never to falter. You internalized that expectation, and you made it your own voice, which now demands of you, from within, a perfection no one would demand of two people together, let alone of one alone. And when you don't reach it, the voice doesn't say "it was impossible," it says "you didn't try hard enough." To recognize that that voice is not the truth, that it is an unjust social demand you made your own, is the beginning of freeing yourself from it. You don't have to be the heroine the world likes to admire from afar without ever helping. You have the right to be just a woman doing her best, with limits, like everyone.

When you let that impossible measure fall, something enormous happens. You stop living in permanent debt to yourself. You realize that you didn't fail, that you did far more than it would be reasonable to expect of a single person, and that what was left undone was not left undone by your fault, it was left undone because you were one and the task was for two. That forgiveness of yourself, that recognition that insufficiency is human and not a defect of yours, is the ground on which everything else can be built. Because as long as you consider yourself wanting, you won't allow yourself to receive, you'll think you first have to compensate for your insufficiency. And when you realize that insufficiency is not a failure but a condition, the door opens to the thing this book has come to bring you.

I want you to notice how this guilt of the human limit feeds on comparison. You look at couples who share the load and you feel that, with two, they ought to do double what you do, and since you do almost what they do at two, alone, you deduce that you're falling short, that you ought to manage more. But the right calculation is another: you do alone what was designed to be done by two people, and to do it halfway, or two thirds, or with things left undone, is not an insufficiency of yours, it is the mathematical sign that one person does not produce as much as two. The fair measure by which to evaluate yourself is not what two people do together. It is what it would be human to expect of one alone. And by that measure, the only fair one, you don't fall short, you go far beyond, you do more than it would be reasonable to ask of any person. To trade the impossible measure for the fair measure is not to lower the bar. It is to stop evaluating yourself by a yardstick that was never made for you.

That thing has a name, and it is the verb of this crossing: to receive. Your whole life has been a movement in a single direction, the direction of giving. Giving, giving, giving, without stopping, without receiving in return, because you thought you had no right to, that receiving was weakness, that you had to be the one to sustain everything. The cure, for you, is the first gesture in the opposite direction. It is learning, or relearning, to receive. And it is of this that the chapters that follow speak. But it begins here, with this forgiveness: you don't have to be enough for two. It is enough to be yourself, human, with limits, and that already is, and always was, enough.

## 7. Relearning to receive

If insufficiency is not failure, and if your deepest tiredness comes from carrying alone what was not made to be carried by a single person, then the way back has a clear direction, and it is the opposite of the one you've been going in for years. I'm going to show it to you in broad strokes. Not step by step, which doesn't fit in a book and is done with time and company. But the map.

The verb is to receive, and I want you to understand the size of what this means for you, because for a solo mother it is perhaps the hardest thing of all. You unlearned how to receive. From so much giving, from so much enduring, from so much not being allowed to need, your system closed the door of entry and left open only the door of exit. When someone offers you help, you refuse, out of habit, before you even think. When you need something, you keep quiet, because asking seems to you weakness or imposition. When you do receive, you feel immediately in debt, in a hurry to give back, unable to simply take it in without paying. Relearning to receive is, therefore, reopening a door you closed long ago, and at first it will hurt, as everything that was closed and is used again hurts.

I want to anticipate the excuses your mind will give for not receiving, because they will all show up. "I don't want to be a burden to anyone." "People have their own lives." "Then I'll owe them." "Better to do it myself, it's faster." "No one does it like me." Do you recognize these voices? Each of them seems reasonable, and each of them is the armor defending itself, keeping the door closed. Notice that they all have one thing in common: they always put you in the position of someone who can't receive, someone who can only give or fend for herself alone. And notice another thing: you, probably, like to help others, you feel good when you do it, you don't live the help you give as an unbearable weight. Why would it be different when you're the one receiving? If helping others does you good, then letting them help you is, also, giving someone the joy of doing good. Receiving is not only taking. It is allowing the other the gesture of giving, which is a form of love that you, from refusing so much, have been denying to those who want to be near you.

To receive is many things, and they all cost you, and they all are things you lack. It is to receive concrete help, to let someone stay with your children, bring you a meal, relieve you of a task, without feeling that you failed for needing. It is to receive emotional support, to let someone ask how you are and to answer the truth, to let yourself be held, to cry before another without being ashamed. It is to receive limits, to recognize that you can't do everything and that it's all right not to. It is to receive humanity, your own, the right to be fragile, tired, imperfect, in need, like any person. And it is, at the bottom of everything, to receive life, to let it sustain you too, instead of feeling yourself the only one sustaining everything.

And here is the belief this book has come, at its core, to dismantle, the belief that lies beneath all your effort: I am alone. I want to tell you, with care and with truth, that even when the responsibility is yours, even when you are in fact the only adult of the household, the entire existence doesn't have to rest on your shoulders alone. There is always, or almost always, more than you imagine: people who would help if you let them, networks that exist if you seek them out, life that sustains in ways you don't control. The loneliness you feel is, in part, real, and in part it is the belief keeping you from seeing and receiving the support that exists. You are not as alone as the belief tells you. But to discover it, you have to lower, a little at a time, the guard that keeps you refusing everything.

And I want to speak to you of building a network, because receiving, in practice, passes largely through here. A solo mother doesn't have to go on being solo in everything, even being the only adult of the household. There are other solo mothers who live the same and with whom you can share, exchange, take turns. There is family that would help if you left it room, there are friends who don't know how to help but would if you asked something concrete, there are neighbors, there are groups, there are social and institutional supports that exist and that many mothers don't seek out because they think they should cope alone. To build a network is not weakness, it is intelligence and it is survival. Human beings were never made to raise children in isolation, it always took a village, and the fact that modern life has undone the villages doesn't change the need, it only makes it harder to meet. To seek and to build your village, piece by piece, is one of the most important acts you can perform, for yourself and for your children, who also gain from having more than one person to bond with.

You have permission to need. Read this slowly, because perhaps no one ever told you. You have permission to need, to ask, to rest, to not cope with everything, to be cared for too. You don't have to earn the care by first proving you can endure everything alone. Care is not a prize for endurance. It is a right of yours, simply by being human. And every time you receive, instead of resisting, you are not failing in your mission, you are filling the place from which you give to your children, because a mother who receives has somewhere to give from, and a mother who only gives, without ever receiving, empties herself until there is nothing left for anyone.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything all at once, and it doesn't take away the weight from one day to the next. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling alone, a failure, or obliged to be infinite.

And I want, first, to speak to you of a way of receiving that is usually the first one possible, and that may be easier for you than receiving from others: receiving from yourself. You, who take care of everyone, can begin to take care also of the one who cares, which is you. It isn't much, at first, and it doesn't need to be: it is giving yourself ten minutes a day that are only yours, it is treating yourself with the same gentleness with which you treat your children when they are tired, it is stopping speaking to yourself inwardly like a taskmaster who always demands more and beginning to speak to yourself as you would to an exhausted friend you love. This self-compassion, this receiving from yourself the care you give to others, is very often the gateway to later being able to receive from others, because someone who doesn't consider herself worthy of care, not even from herself, will hardly accept it from outside. Begin, then, here, with what is most within your reach: treat yourself with the tenderness with which you treat those you love. You too are someone you love, or can come back to being.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it says what so many words try to say. Think of a spring that gives water to a whole village. For years, it gives, and gives, and everyone drinks, and no one wonders where its water comes from. But even a spring needs to be fed, by the rain, by the underground waters, by the water that comes down from the mountain. A spring that only gave, without ever being fed again, would dry up, and with it the whole village would dry up. You have been that spring, giving to everyone without stopping. Receiving is not selfishness of the spring, it is what keeps it flowing. Letting life feed you again, letting the rain fall on you too, letting other waters fill you, is not taking from the village, it is ensuring that you can go on giving. A mother who lets herself be sustained is a spring that doesn't dry up. And your children need, more than anything, for your spring not to dry up.

Now you know that being the one who cannot fall has a price, and that this price you don't have to pay in full alone. You know that you can be an excellent mother without becoming two people. You know that there is a place that is not yours to fill, and that ceasing to try to fill it relieves you of half the weight. You know that your identity of the one who endures is an armor, not your essence. You know how to tell tiredness from burnout, and you know that burnout asks for help, not more endurance. You know that insufficiency is not failure, it is the human condition. And you know that the way back has a name you had almost forgotten: to receive.

And you know, perhaps for the first time, that your loneliness was not a destiny, it was in part a belief, and that beliefs, unlike destinies, can change. That there is, around you, more support than the belief let you see, more willing hands, more life willing to sustain you, if you lower, a little at a time, the guard you learned to keep when you were small and truly had no one. You are no longer that one. And the world, now, has more to give you than your heart, hardened by endurance, dares to expect.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I do not need to carry the weight of two in order to be enough.

And perhaps, deep down, the hardest sentence of all is beginning to make sense, the one that touches exactly what a solo mother rarely believes: I can let life sustain me too.

To understand was already to set down a bit of the weight. But the rest is not set down by reading a page: it is set down by living, slowly, in another way.

It was for that crossing that I created **FreeMe**.

FreeMe is not one more task for your list nor more information. It is, precisely, the opposite of everything that drains you. It is a guided crossing that you do from the inside, at your own pace, to set down the weight that was not made to be carried by a single person and to relearn how to receive. For a mother like you, who raises children alone and became the one who never falls, there is a path designed for your wound: the belief that you are alone, the guilt of a human limit, the armor of the one who endures, and the enormous courage of letting yourself be sustained too. Not to do less for your children, for your love for them is immense. To discover that you don't have to become two, and that you deserve to be cared for as much as you care.

And I want to tell you one last thing about your children, because I know it is for them that you endure everything, and so it is for them that you'll want to hear this. Your children don't need a mother who sacrificed herself into burnout to prove her love to them. They need a mother who is alive, present, with light in her eyes, who is well enough to be able to see them, hear them, embrace them with presence and not only with the leftover energy at the end of the day. When you take care of yourself, when you receive, when you stop spending yourself to the very end, you are not taking anything from them, you are giving them the one thing that truly cannot be missing: a whole mother. And you are teaching them, by example, something they will carry for life, that a person can give without destroying herself, that caring for others does not require disappearing, that one can love and, at the same time, let oneself be loved. It is one of the most valuable lessons you can leave them, and you can only give it to them by living it yourself first.

There, the sentences you read here alone are spoken slowly, with space to, for the first time, let them enter into you. "I have permission to need." "Insufficiency is not failure, it is the human condition." "I can be a whole mother without being two people." What here was a map, there becomes an accompanied path. Receiving stops being an idea and becomes a gesture. And the loneliness begins, slowly, to open itself to the support that was always nearer than the belief let you see.

If you feel this is the moment, I'm there. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't yet the moment, that's all right, and no one is rushing you. What you've read is already yours, and the path waits for you, without haste and without demand.

And I want you to know that this crossing does not ask you to stop being strong, because your strength is real and it is yours and you don't have to hand it over. It asks you only to free it from the obligation of being permanent. There is a difference between having strength and having to be strong at all times, without ever being able to set it down. The strength you choose to use, when it is needed, is a gift. The strength you are obliged to keep up, without rest, without alternative, without ever being able to be fragile, is a prison. This crossing does not take away your strength, it gives you back the choice: the choice of being strong when it is needed, and of also being able to rest, to need, to receive, to be cared for, without that taking anything from who you are. You go on being a strong woman. You only stop being condemned to be strong every minute, alone, forever.

But before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing, and it is a thing that goes against everything you've learned, and that is why I ask it of you. Today, let yourself receive one thing. Just one. It could be accepting a help you would normally refuse. It could be asking for something small you need and usually keep quiet about. It could be sitting for five minutes and letting someone take care of you, even if it's only a cup of tea another person made. And notice what stirs in you, the discomfort, the urge to give back at once, the voice that says you shouldn't. And receive all the same. Because it is in that small gesture of receiving, borne without fleeing, that the belief that you are alone begins to come undone, and your crossing back to a life that also sustains you begins.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
