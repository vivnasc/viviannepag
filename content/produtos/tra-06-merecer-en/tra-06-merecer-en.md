# The woman who works to deserve

**For you, for whom work has become the proof that you deserve to exist. Because your worth comes before what you produce, and you exist before you do anything at all.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the deepest wound of work, that of someone who produces to prove she deserves to exist, and that, taken to the limit, exhausts and makes ill. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion that doesn't pass, an anxiety tied to having to produce in order to matter, or a sense that without producing you're nothing, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole path.*

---

## 1. I work to have the right to exist

Notice something you may never have named, but that lies beneath everything. You don't work only to live, nor only because you like what you do. You work to have the right to exist.

Work has become, for you, the proof that you deserve your place in the world. The justification of your existence. The way to have the right to take up space, to breathe, to be. You produce, and in producing, you feel that you deserve to be here. You stop, and an anguish appears, the anguish of someone who has stopped justifying her existence, of someone who, for a moment, isn't paying the rent on her place.

That's why you can't truly stop. Even resting, there's a restlessness, a sense that you should be producing, doing, justifying. As if your right to exist had to be renewed, each day, with more work, more production, more proof that you're worth it.

And notice that this restlessness isn't about the concrete work, about a specific task left undone. It's deeper, more diffuse, it's a background anguish that's there even when there's nothing urgent, even when objectively you could rest in peace. Because what unsettles you isn't a pending matter, it's existence itself without production, being without doing, which sets off, deep down, the doubt about whether you have the right to be. Other people rest and feel peace; you rest and feel that something is wrong, that you should be justifying yourself. And that difference, which you may never have noticed, is the sign of the wound: for you, existing without producing isn't repose, it's a threat.

I want to tell you right now, before anything else, that this is the deepest wound of work, and the hardest to see, because it's disguised as ethics, as ambition, as devotion. But it's none of that. It's existential. It isn't about career. It's about your right to exist, which you feel you have to deserve, and which you deserve, without knowing it, with work.

There's a difference between working out of pleasure, for sustenance, for meaning, and working to prove you deserve to exist. The first is healthy. The second is a prison. Because it turns work, which should be an expression of who you are, into the condition of your very existence, and makes you someone who has to produce, without end, in order to have the right to be alive.

This book is the deepest of this collection, and perhaps of all. Because it touches the root of everything: your right to exist, simply, without deserving it. I've come to look at the belief that your worth depends on what you produce. At the confusion between what you do and the right to exist. And at the possibility, perhaps the greatest of all, of existing knowing that you're worth it, before any production, without having to prove it.

And let me give you the direction in advance, so you can read in peace. Your worth comes before what you produce. You exist before you do anything at all, and that existing already has worth, already suffices, already deserves. You don't have to work to have the right to exist. That right is yours, from your origin, and no production gives it to you, because it was never in question, except in the wound we're going to look at.

I want you to understand, from the start, that this book hasn't come to ask you to stop working, nor that your devotion is worth nothing, nor that what you produce doesn't matter. What you create has worth, your work matters, and it's good to love what you do. It comes to ask you something else, deeper: that you separate your work from your right to exist. That you keep doing, if you want, but from a ground of worth that's already yours, and not in order to earn it. The difference isn't in how much you work, it's in the why: working because you exist, and not in order to have the right to exist. The first is life. The second is a rent without end.

And I want you to know that this is, perhaps, the hardest wound to see, precisely because it's the deepest, and because the world applauds it. No one will tell you there's a problem in your working so much, in producing always, in never stopping; on the contrary, they'll praise you for it. That's why this wound hides better than all the others, dressed up as virtue, rewarded everywhere. And only you, on the inside, know the anguish that feeds it, the terror of the emptiness that sustains it, the doubt about the right to exist that no one sees. Seeing this, naming it, is the beginning of freeing yourself from a prison that everyone confuses with merit.

Perhaps you find yourself in the small ledgers of an ordinary day.

In feeling that a day in which you didn't produce was a wasted day, empty, in which you didn't justify your existence, in which you didn't pay for your place.

In not being able to simply be, to stay, to exist, without doing, without producing, without justifying, as if simply standing still, without purpose, were something to avoid at all costs.

In measuring your worth by what you produce, and in feeling that, without production, you're not worth anything, you have no place, you don't deserve to be, as if your person, without what she does, weren't enough.

In having, deep down, the sense that your right to exist is conditional, that it has to be earned, and that you earn it by working.

In feeling guilt or anguish when you're ill, or idle, or simply resting, as if you were failing at something essential, ceasing to pay the rent on your own life.

They are all one rent, paid in different coins. Work as proof of the right to exist. Production as the condition of an existence you feel you have to deserve.

I want to show you how this appears day to day, in things you may already take for who you are. A Sunday with no plans, with nothing to do, instead of rest, brings you an anguish, a discomfort, a need to find something to produce. You're on holiday, and instead of simply being, you feel you should make the most of it to do, to get ahead, not to waste it. You receive affection, care, attention, and inside there's a voice that says you haven't yet done enough to deserve it, that you have to make up for it, give it back, earn it.

And there's a detail that reveals the root: the way you feel when you're ill, or low, unable to produce. Instead of allowing yourself to rest, a guilt rises, almost a panic, a sense of failing, of ceasing to deserve, of losing the right to your place. Because for you falling ill isn't only being ill, it's ceasing to be able to pay the rent on existence, and that terrifies you in a way that doesn't match the real size of the situation. It's there, in those moments when you can't produce, that the wound most shows itself: in the inability to simply be cared for, without having earned it.

And there's something no one sees, beneath your devotion. People see someone hardworking, tireless, with a purpose, and they admire you. They don't see the existential anguish underneath, the terror of the emptiness when you stop, the sense that your whole existence is at stake each day. They see the ambition, not the wound. And you, who are perhaps even praised for never stopping, become even more trapped, because now producing is also being admired, and stopping seems to risk not only your right to exist, but the image others have of you.

## 2. The right to exist that became conditional

Go back. To the root of everything. To when your right to exist became conditional.

Because no one works to deserve to exist without reason. This wound is born of a time when your right to be, to be loved, to take up space, was not unconditional, but depended on something. On being good, on producing, on measuring up, on doing for it.

Perhaps you grew up where love, attention, approval, depended on what you did. Where you were valued when you brought results, when you were useful, when you produced, and went unnoticed, or were undervalued, when you simply were. And you learned that your existence, in itself, wasn't enough, that you had to justify it with what you did.

Perhaps you felt, early, that you were a burden, that your existence had to be deserved, that you had no right to be just for being. And you began to produce, to strive, to do, in order to justify your place, in order to deserve the existence you felt hadn't been given to you for free.

Perhaps you come from a place where a person's worth was measured by what she did, produced, achieved. Where whoever didn't produce wasn't worth anything. And you engraved that equation at the root: I exist insofar as I produce, and without producing, I have no right to exist.

Or perhaps your existence arrived in difficult circumstances, at a moment when it wasn't received with the simple joy with which a child should be received. Perhaps you were one more burden in an already overloaded home, or you arrived when you weren't expected, or you grew up feeling, without anyone saying it, that your presence complicated things, cost something, weighed. And a child who feels this does something touching and terrible: she tries to make up for her own existence, to make herself useful, to do for the space she occupies, as if she had to pay for being there. And she carries, into adult life, that impossible mission, that of justifying, with work, an existence she felt, from early on, she had no right to have for free.

Notice what a child learns under a roof where her place must be earned. She learns that existence isn't a right, it's a condition. That she has to deserve her place. That she's only worth something when she produces, when she does, when she justifies her presence. And that learning is engraved at the deepest level, because it touches the most basic thing: the right to exist, which should be unconditional, and which became, for her, something to deserve.

And that child did what she had to do. She began to work in order to exist. To produce in order to deserve. To justify, with what she did, her right to be. And she carried that equation into adult life, where she keeps working, without end, to prove a right to exist that should never have been in question.

Perhaps the message wasn't even said in words. Perhaps no one told you, explicitly, that you had to deserve to exist. But you felt it, in the air, in the way you were seen, or not seen, in the attention that arrived with results and disappeared without them, in the affection that seemed conditional. Children don't need words to learn these things, they learn them in the body, in the skin, in the way they're treated. And what you learned, without anyone saying it, was that your presence, in itself, wasn't enough, that you had to justify it, to make it useful, to deserve it for it to be welcome.

I want you to see this with clarity, and with tenderness. Your need to produce in order to deserve isn't ambition, nor ethics, nor devotion. It's a wound at the root, that of someone who learned that her right to exist was conditional, and that she has to earn it, without end, with what she does. You aren't unable to stop by temperament. You're someone who wasn't given the unconditional right to exist, and who tries to deserve it, her whole life, by producing.

And I want you to see where this is kept, because it's the deepest of everything. This belief isn't only in your head, in what you think about yourself. It's at the root, in a place before words, engraved in a time when you didn't yet have reason to defend yourself from it. So it isn't enough to tell yourself, today, that you have the right to exist, in order to feel it. Your reason may know, and your reason perhaps already knows, and even so the anguish continues, because it doesn't live in reason, it lives in an older layer, deeper, that formed before you knew how to think. And a wound so old isn't cured only with an argument. It's cured slowly, with experience, with time, and often with prepared hands that know how to reach where reason, alone, can't.

## 3. I don't know how to exist without producing

There's a debt you keep paying without ever seeing it. You don't know, simply, how to exist. Without producing, without doing, without justifying, you don't know how to be.

Notice what happens when you try, simply, to be. To stay still, doing nothing, without producing, without purpose. An anguish appears, a discomfort, a restlessness that pushes you to do, to produce, to justify your presence. You can't bear pure existence, without the activity that justifies it. As if existing, without producing, were unbearable, illegitimate, wrong.

Notice, too, the quantity of things you fill your life with so as never to reach that emptiness. Always a task, always a project, always something to do, even in rest, even in leisure, which you turn, without realizing it, into one more way of producing. There are not, in your life, spaces of pure existing, because you avoid them, instinctively, like someone avoiding a pain. And that permanent flight, that filling everything so as not to reach the nothing, is, in itself, exhausting, because living in flight from yourself is as tiring as the work that feeds it. There's a rest that comes only from stopping fleeing, from letting yourself reach the emptiness you so fear, and from discovering, there, not the abyss you imagined, but the firm ground that you exist, and that's enough.

And the price of this rent is enormous. Because a person who doesn't know how to exist without producing never rests, never stays, never simply is. She lives doing, without end, to justify an existence she never feels she has the right to have. And that exhausts, not only the body, but the soul, because it's an existential exhaustion, that of someone who has to prove, each day, that she deserves to be alive.

And it costs you the present. Because to be present, to live the moment, to simply be, you have to stop producing, and stopping producing opens the anguish of not deserving. So you're never truly anywhere. You're always doing, producing, justifying, and life, which happens in the present, in the simple existing, passes you by, because you're too busy deserving the right to live it.

And it costs you, deep down, the peace of existing. The peace of someone who knows she has the right to be, without having to deserve it. The peace of someone who can, simply, be, without justification. That peace, the most basic of all, that of existing without condition, is denied you, because for you existence always has a bill, and the bill is paid with production, without end.

And it costs you the body, which pays in silence an equation that doesn't respect its limits. Because a body forced to produce without end, in order to justify existence, never truly reposes, lives on alert, in effort, in debt. And the body collects that bill, sooner or later, in exhaustion, in illness, in signs that it can't bear the weight of sustaining, with work, a right to exist that should have been given for free. And there's a cruelty in this: the wound that makes you produce in order to deserve to live ends, at the limit, by taking away your very health, and even your very life, which you strive so hard to justify.

And there's a cost in your relationships, too. Because whoever works to deserve to exist is rarely present for others, busy as she is justifying herself. And because you bring, into relationships, the same equation: you have to deserve love, to earn it, to pay for it, and you never manage to simply receive it, nor to simply be with those you love, without feeling that you should be doing something useful. So even love becomes one more area where you have to produce in order to have the right, and the gratuitousness of affection, which should rest you, becomes one more rent to pay.

Notice this tragedy. You work to have the right to a life that, busy working to deserve it, you never get to live. You produce to justify an existence that production doesn't let you inhabit. It's an absurd and painful circle: you spend your life deserving the right to exist, and in that deserving without end, you don't exist, you only produce.

And there's something even deeper in this tragedy: however much you produce, the sense of having, at last, deserved it, never arrives. Because the right to exist, in your equation, is a rent that falls due every day, and today's payment doesn't cover tomorrow's. There's no production that suffices, no achievement that closes the account, no milestone that gives you, once and for all, the peace of having the right to be. So you run without ever arriving, you pay without ever settling the debt, and the peace you seek in doing always escapes you, because it isn't in doing, it's in a place that doing can't reach: in the recognition that the right to exist was never a debt, and never needed to be paid.

I want you to see, too, how this wound robs you of the capacity to receive life as a gift. Everything that's given for free, love, beauty, rest, the simple pleasure of being alive, you can't receive without wanting to deserve it, without turning it into something to pay for. And so life, which is, in large part, gift and not merit, escapes you, because you only know how to earn, and not how to receive. Learning to receive, without deserving, is a deep part of this cure: letting life be given to you, as it was given to you in the beginning, without a bill, without a condition, simply because you exist.

And it costs you the love you receive without feeling it deserved. Because even when you're loved, cared for, valued, there's a part of you that can't receive, that feels she hasn't yet done enough to deserve it, that has to give it back, to pay for it, to earn it. And so love doesn't reach your depths, because the filter of the wound doesn't let it in without a bill. You live, perhaps, surrounded by affection, and inside always in debt, always thinking you have to deserve what's given to you for free, and so the love you receive never fills you, because you don't let yourself, simply, receive it.

I want you to feel the weight of this rent on your shoulders. The weight of having to justify your existence, each day, with work. Of never being able to, simply, be. Of living a whole life deserving a right that was yours from your origin. It's the deepest exhaustion there is, and perhaps you already take it for who you are, when it's, deep down, the wound of someone who never knew she had the right to exist, just so. And you deserve, after a life of paying, to rest at last from that rent you should never have been obliged to pay.

## 4. Who am I if I don't produce

I'm going to leave here the deepest question of this book, and perhaps of this whole collection. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you if you don't produce? If you do nothing? If you simply exist, without justifying, without deserving, without proving?

Stay with it. Notice the abyss it opens.

For you, this question touches the most basic fear there is. Because your whole existence was conditioned to what you produce. Take production away from you, imagine yourself simply existing, without doing, without deserving, and a terrifying emptiness appears. Do I have the right to exist, then? Am I worth anything, if I don't produce? Is there someone there, beneath what I do, with a right to be?

And the shadow that hides here is the deepest of all: the doubt about your own right to exist. The fear that, without production, there's no one there with a right to be, that your worth is all production, and that beneath it there's no person, or no person with a right to exist without deserving it.

It's a doubt that few dare to name, because it touches the most intimate and the most fragile. Perhaps you never said it to anyone, perhaps not even to yourself, in these words. But if it resonates, if as you read this something in you recognizes, with a tightening, this doubt about the right to be, then know that you aren't alone in it, that it has a name, has an origin, and has a cure. And that naming it, at last, doesn't make you more fragile, it makes you freer, because what's named can begin to be cared for, and this wound, however deep it is, can, slowly, and with the right hands, heal.

Notice how this imprisons you, at the root. You can't stop, because stopping confronts you with the doubt about your right to exist. You produce without end, not out of love for what you do, but out of terror of the emptiness that opens when you stop, the emptiness of not knowing whether you have the right to be. Production is, deep down, a flight from that question, the most terrifying of all.

And there's a total fragility in this. Because an existence conditioned to production is an existence always at risk. You need only stop producing, through illness, through age, through circumstance, and your right to exist, in your equation, is called into question. You live, therefore, with a background terror, that of not being able to produce, because not producing is, for you, losing the right to be. And that terror, at the root, contaminates everything, because it makes your very existence a fragile thing, that you have to hold, each day, with more work.

Think of what this means for your future, for old age, for any moment when you can't produce as you do now. If your worth is in production, then your equation condemns you to see yourself lose worth as the capacity to produce diminishes, which is a cruel sentence and, above all, false. Because the worth of an old person, of an ill person, of a person who no longer produces, is no less than that of a young person at the peak of her productivity. A life is worth something for being life, not for yielding. And if your equation tells you otherwise, it isn't describing the truth, it's projecting, onto your future, the same wound that governs your present, and that needs to be cured precisely so that you can, one day, be worth something in peace even when you no longer produce.

I want to tell you something about the doubt that's at the bottom of everything, because it's the most important. That doubt, about your right to exist, feels so true that it seems a fact, an observation about you. But it isn't. It's a wound, a mark, a lie you were taught in a time when you had no way to contest it. The intensity with which you feel it doesn't make it true, it only makes it deep. And there's an enormous difference between something being felt as truth and being, in fact, truth. Your lack of right to exist is the first kind: felt with all its force, and false from beginning to end.

I want to tell you something about this doubt, because it's at the bottom of everything, and it's the most important in this book. Your right to exist isn't conditional. It never was. You have the right to exist, simply, by existing, as a newborn child has the right to exist who hasn't yet produced anything, who only receives, who only is. That right is unconditional, from your origin, and no production gives it to you, because it never depended on what you do. You were taught that it was conditional, but that was a mistake, a wound, and not a truth. You exist, and that's enough, and it always was enough.

I want you to look at the newborn, or at any very small child, because she is the living proof of the truth your wound denies. A baby hasn't produced anything. Hasn't done anything. Doesn't deserve, in the sense of having earned, anything at all. And nevertheless she has every right to exist, to be loved, to take up space, to be cared for. No one looks at a baby and thinks she has to justify her existence. Her right to be is evident, unconditional, prior to anything she might come to do. Well, that right was, one day, yours, and it still is, because it isn't lost, doesn't expire, doesn't depend on production. You were that baby, with that right intact, and it remains yours, beneath all the wound that taught you to doubt it.

Don't answer the question of who you are if you don't produce just yet. It isn't answered with the head, and sometimes it needs more than a book, and time. But leave it open. Because that's where the deepest door of all is. The day you feel, deep down, that you have the right to exist without deserving it, you stop having to work to justify your life. And then, only then, can you begin, at last, to exist, and not only to produce.

## 5. When producing to exist exhausts

I have to stop the book a moment, with seriousness. Because working to deserve to exist leads, frequently, to the limit, and from that limit to exhaustion.

Whoever produces to justify her existence has no brake. There's no point of arrival, because the right to exist has to be renewed, without end, with more work. And that race without end leads, very often, to burnout, to exhaustion, to the collapse of someone who exhausted herself deserving a right she never felt was guaranteed.

If the exhaustion you feel no longer passes with rest. If there's a background anxiety, tied to having to produce, that won't let go of you. If you've lost pleasure in everything, and life has become an obligation to justify your existence. If your body, or your mind, begin to give way under the weight of a production that never stops.

These are signs of exhaustion, and they aren't solved by producing more, deserving more, justifying more. They're solved by stopping, and with support. And I want to tell you that, in the case of this wound, support is particularly important. Because the doubt about the right to exist is deep, old, and touches layers that understanding alone can't reach. A professional, a psychologist, a therapist, can help you reach where a book can't, to the root of a wound that comes from the beginning.

I want you to see why, in this wound, the helping relationship is, itself, part of the cure. Because the doubt about the right to exist was born in a relationship, in a time when your presence wasn't received as unconditionally welcome. And what was wounded in a relationship heals, very often, in another: in a helping relationship where you're received without having to produce, where your presence is enough, where you don't have to deserve the attention given to you. To experience that, to be taken in without a bill, can repair, slowly, the wound in the very layer where it was made, teaching your body, and not only your reason, that there are places where existing is enough.

And I want to be very clear with you, because it matters: asking for that help isn't one more thing you have to do in order to deserve, it isn't one more task, one more production. It's precisely the opposite. It's the first gesture of letting yourself be cared for without having earned it, of receiving without paying, of admitting that you need without having to justify it. For someone who works to deserve to exist, seeking help can be, in itself, the most curative and most difficult act: that of receiving, at last, something for free, as living proof that your worth never depended on your having deserved it.

And I want to tell you something, with all tenderness. If the doubt about your right to exist is so deep that it brings you an emptiness, an anguish, a sadness that doesn't pass, or that leads you to think you perhaps shouldn't be here, please, don't stay alone with that. Seek help, and seek it right away. A professional, a support line, someone you trust. Because your right to exist is not in question, however much the wound tells you otherwise, and there are those who can help you feel, at last, that right that was always yours.

If you recognize, in yourself, this exhaustion, or this deep doubt, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Seeking help isn't one more thing to do in order to deserve. It's letting yourself, at last, be cared for, without having to deserve it, as proof that your worth didn't depend, after all, on your producing anything.

## 6. My worth comes before what I do

If you work to deserve to exist, then there's a new truth waiting for you, and it's the deepest of all. Your worth comes before what you do.

Read this slowly, very slowly, because it contradicts the belief that governs you from the root. For you, worth comes from production. First you produce, then you're worth something, then you deserve to exist. And I propose you invert the whole order, at the root. First you exist. First you're worth something, simply for being. And from that worth, which is already yours, you do, produce, create, not in order to deserve to exist, but because you exist, and because creating is an expression of a life that already has worth.

Let me say it another way, the deepest I know. A newborn child has worth. Has the right to exist, to be loved, to take up space. And she hasn't yet produced anything. Hasn't done anything. Only is. Her worth doesn't come from what she does, it comes from existing. That was your worth from your origin, your unconditional right to be. And if you learned that you had to deserve it, that was a mistake you were taught, not a truth about you. Your worth, like that of any being, comes before everything you do, and nothing you do increases it, and nothing you cease to do diminishes it.

And there's a total liberation in this, the deepest there is. When your worth stops depending on what you produce, you can, at last, exist. You can stop, rest, simply be, without the anguish of not deserving. You can work out of pleasure, for meaning, for sustenance, and not to justify your life. You can live, and not only produce. Because your right to exist is no longer at stake, no longer has to be earned, is already yours, once and for all.

I want you to see that this truth doesn't take away your ambition, nor your pleasure in creating, nor your pride in what you do. It only takes away the terror that was underneath. You can still want to do things, achieve, build, but now from desire, and not from fear; from the delight of creating, and not from the obligation to deserve. And what's done from delight is freer, more whole, and very often better, than what's wrenched from the terror of having no right to exist. Freeing work from the function of justifying your life doesn't diminish it, it gives it back the lightness of being an expression of you, and not a sentence.

I want to speak to you about the verb of this crossing, the deepest of all. Existing. Simply existing. Allowing yourself to be, to stay, to take up space, without justifying it, without deserving it, without paying for it. It's the most basic verb there is, and, for you, the most difficult, because you were taught that existing had a price. Relearning to exist without a price, to stay without having to do, to be without having to prove, is the deepest crossing of this collection, and perhaps the most liberating, because it gives you back not a better way of working, but the most basic right of all: that of simply being.

I want to speak to you about what's, deep down, at stake. It isn't work. It's your relationship with your own existence. Working to deserve is living like someone who is always paying the rent on her own life, without ever owning it. To recognize that your worth comes before what you do is to discover that life isn't a rent to pay, it's a right that's already yours, and that you can, at last, inhabit it, instead of deserving it without end.

The awakening, when it comes, is the deepest of all. It's the moment when you feel, for an instant, that you have the right to exist. That you're worth something, simply for being. That you don't have to deserve your life, because it's already yours. It's an instant that can bring tears, because it undoes the oldest wound, that of someone who never felt she had the right to be. And it's the instant when, perhaps for the first time, you rest, truly, not only from work, but from the very obligation of deserving to exist.

And don't be frightened if, instead of relief, that instant brings, first, a deep sadness, or a crying you don't quite know where it comes from. It's common, and it's part of the cure. Because by touching, at last, the truth that you had the right to exist all along, you also touch the pain of not having felt it for a whole life, the grief of all the years in which you demanded of yourself to deserve what was yours for free. That sadness isn't a setback, it's the recognition of the wound, and crying it is a way of letting it, slowly, heal. Let it come, with tenderness, because on the other side of it is the peace you sought in work and that work never gave you.

## 7. Existing before producing

There's a destination for this crossing, and it's the deepest of this whole collection. Existing before producing. Knowing that you're worth something, that you have the right to be, before doing anything at all.

And I want to show you what this means, at the root.

Existing before producing is inverting the order that governs you. Stopping producing in order to deserve to exist, and beginning to exist, with full right, and to produce, if you want, from that ground. It's recognizing that your life doesn't have to be justified, deserved, earned. That it's yours, from your origin, and that you can inhabit it, simply, without paying for it with work.

It's, above all, giving yourself the right that was denied you: the unconditional right to exist. To take up space, to breathe, to be, without deserving it. To be, and for that to be enough. Not as an achievement, but as a recognition of something that was always true, but that a wound hid from you.

And I want you to understand that giving yourself that right isn't a thought, it's a practice, repeated, over time. It isn't enough to decide, on one day, that you have the right to exist. You must, time after time, choose existing against the old equation: stopping when it orders you to produce, resting when it accuses you of laziness, receiving when it demands that you pay, and staying with the anguish that brings without obeying it. Each of these small choices is a brick of a new ground, the ground of someone who knows she's worth something for existing. And it's from repeating them, and not from a sudden revelation, that the peace of existing without deserving is, slowly, built.

I want, too, to free you from a subtle trap: that of turning this very crossing into one more thing to do well, to deserve, to produce. You'll be tempted to treat your cure as a project, to want to exist perfectly, to demand progress of yourself. Notice that this is the old wound infiltrating the very path of curing it. Existing without deserving isn't conquered with effort, it's received with surrender. There's nothing here to achieve, there's something to stop doing: to stop demanding of yourself to deserve what's already yours. And perhaps the hardest thing, and the most liberating, is precisely this: allowing yourself to fail at this crossing, to fall back, not to do it well, and discovering that even so, even failing at existing without deserving, you keep having the right to exist. Because that right never depended, not even, on your doing your own cure well.

It will cost you, perhaps more than anything. Because you'll feel, each time you try simply to exist, without producing, the old anguish, the doubt about your right to be, the urge to rush to do something, to justify yourself, to deserve. And you'll want to go back to producing, to working, to justifying, so the anguish passes.

But it's precisely there, in that existing without producing, endured despite the anguish, that your deepest cure lies. Because each time you stop, and simply are, and the world doesn't take away your right to be, and you don't disappear, you learn, slowly, at the root, that you had the right to exist, all this time, without deserving it. That your worth didn't depend on production. That existing was already enough.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that says that without producing you have no right to be, is engraved at the root, and it will appear each time you stop, with its anguish of always. It will tell you that you're failing, losing your place, ceasing to deserve. The work isn't to silence it at once, it's to stop obeying it. To hear it say you have no right to stop, and to answer it, slowly, I exist, and that's enough. And each time you exist despite it, and the world doesn't take away your right to be, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows that your worth comes before everything you do.

I want you to see what opens when you exist before producing. There opens the deepest rest there is, that of no longer having to deserve your life. There opens presence, because you can, at last, simply be. There opens a new relationship with work, which stops being the proof of your existence and goes back to being an expression of it, free, chosen, and not an obligation to justify your right to be. And there opens peace, the most basic of all, that of someone who knows she has the right to exist, without condition.

And I want to leave you something, about whoever sees you, especially if there are children. Because this wound is passed on, without words, as it was passed on to you. If yours see that you only value yourself when you produce, that you never stop, that your existence seems to depend on what you do, they learn, in the body, the same equation: that love and worth are earned, that existing has a price. The most liberating thing you can do for them isn't to give them the example of someone who never stops. It's to show them, and yourself, that one can exist without deserving, that worth is unconditional, that there's a right to be simply for being. Sometimes a woman cures this wound not only for herself, but so as not to pass it to those she loves, so that hers grow up knowing, from the beginning, what it cost her a lifetime to discover: that they have the right to exist, without having to pay for it with anything.

And I want to give you a way to begin, a small one, because even the deepest is done from the simple. Set aside, now and then, a few minutes in which you do nothing, and in which you notice, on purpose, that you keep existing, breathing, being, even without producing. Not in order to rest so as to yield more afterward, which would be putting rest in the service of production, but in order to train the simple existing, without end, without purpose, only for existing. It will seem strange to you, perhaps anguishing, at first. But it's in that training of gratuitous existing, repeated, that your body, slowly, begins to learn that it has the right to be, even when it isn't doing.

I won't give you a method, because this isn't solved with techniques, and sometimes it's done with help, and with time. But I leave you the principle, the deepest I know. Your worth comes before what you do. You exist before you produce, and that existing already has worth, already suffices, already deserves. You don't have to work to have the right to live. That life is already yours. Inhabit it.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book, the deepest of this collection, out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. And, in this wound, understanding is the beginning of giving yourself back a right that was always yours.

It doesn't tear up the whole rent contract overnight. It won't make you, tomorrow, feel, without doubt, that you have the right to exist. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your production with your worth, and your work with your right to be, and to begin to suspect that the doubt that governs you is a wound, and not a truth.

Now you know that you work to deserve to exist, and that this is the deepest wound of work, and it's existential, not professional.

You know that your right to exist became conditional, in a time when your existence, instead of being received for free, had to be deserved, justified, paid for.

You know that you don't know how to exist without producing, and that this robs you of the present, of peace, and of the very life you work to deserve and never get to inhabit.

You know that life is, in large part, gift, and not merit, and that learning to receive, without paying, is a deep part of this cure.

You know that the deepest question, who you are if you don't produce, opens the doubt about your right to exist, and that that doubt, however old, doesn't tell you the truth.

You know that producing to exist exhausts, and that this wound, particularly, deserves help, and that asking for that help is, in itself, an act of receiving without deserving, and therefore part of the cure.

You know that your right to exist is not, never was, truly in question, and that the doubt, however deep it feels, is a wound, and not a truth about you.

You know that your worth comes before what you do, and that the destination is existing before producing, inhabiting your life instead of deserving it without end.

I want to leave you with an image, the last of this collection, and I want you to stay with it.

Imagine someone who believes she has to pay, every day, a very high rent in order to have the right to live. And who works, without stopping, to pay that rent, afraid that, if one day she doesn't pay, she'll be evicted from her own life. And who never rests, never inhabits the house, never lives, because she's always working to have the right to be in it.

The crossing is discovering that the house is yours. That there's no rent to pay. That no one is going to evict you from your own life for not producing. That you have the right to inhabit it, to rest in it, to live it, simply, because it's yours, from your origin, and always was. And that you can, at last, set down the work of deserving, and begin, for the first time, to live.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more thing to deserve. It will be slow, and it will have setbacks, because it's the deepest wound, the oldest, and those don't heal quickly. Days when you feel, for an instant, that you have the right to exist, and days when the old anguish comes back full force, when you rush again to produce in order to feel you have the right to be. That isn't failing. It's how a wound so old heals, slowly, in back-and-forth, with small advances and expected setbacks. Don't demand of yourself, here too, a perfect and quick cure, which would be putting the very wound in the service of curing it. Ask of yourself only that you don't give up, and that you treat each setback with the gentleness of someone who knows she's undoing something engraved at the beginning of life.

And I want to tell you one last thing, the deepest I know. You never needed to deserve to exist. From the first instant, before doing anything at all, you had the right to be, to be loved, to take up your place in the world. The wound that taught you otherwise was a mistake of those who didn't know how to give you that right for free, but it was never a truth about you. And the most important thing this book, and perhaps this whole collection, can leave you, is this, said with all tenderness, so that one day you feel it, and not only know it: you already suffice. You exist, and that's enough. And it always was enough.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this book, and that perhaps holds this whole collection. My worth comes before what I do. I exist, and that's enough. I don't have to pay the rent on my own life, because the house was always mine.

Existing was the first step. But there's a second, and that one isn't done only by reading. It's done by existing, slowly, without producing, despite the anguish. By inhabiting your life, instead of deserving it. And, in this wound more than in any other, it's done very often with help, with someone to accompany you in reaching the root, because the doubt about the right to exist comes from the beginning, and rarely heals alone.

And before you close these pages, and close, perhaps, this whole crossing, I ask you one thing only. The most difficult, and the most simple.

Today, exist without producing. Stop, for a moment, without doing anything, without justifying, without deserving. Sit down, and simply be. And notice the anguish that rises, the voice that says you should be doing something, the doubt about your right to be there, still, without producing. And stay. Just stay. Because it's in that existing, endured despite the anguish, that you begin to feel, perhaps for the first time, that you have the right to be here, without deserving it, and that your life, after all, was always yours, waiting for you to live it, instead of spending it deserving it.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you're wasting time, squandering, not deserving, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you're, perhaps for the first time, exercising your most basic right, that of existing without paying for it. And that this isn't a waste, it's the beginning of a life that's lived, instead of deserved.

Because you already suffice. You exist, and that always was, and always will be, enough.

Because you already suffice, and always sufficed, and your life was always yours, waiting for you to live it.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
