# The woman who doesn't take her seat

**For you, who won the place but still ask permission to occupy it. Because you don't need to ask authorization to occupy what is already yours.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the woman who won a place and doesn't occupy it fully, always feeling like an impostor, and there is a point at which that doubt stops being modesty and becomes an anxiety that makes you ill. If you recognize in yourself a persistent sense of not deserving, an anguish of being found out, or a suffering that doesn't pass, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your place is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I won the place but I don't occupy it

Notice something strange. You won the place. The role, the position, the recognition, the competence. They are there, they are yours, you earned them. And yet, you don't occupy them fully.

You sit at the table, but like someone asking permission. You have the role, but you feel you don't belong there. You won the position, but you live with the sense that, at any moment, they will discover that you shouldn't be there, that it was a mistake, that you are not, after all, enough.

It is a strange experience, and perhaps a lonely one, because from the outside nobody can guess. People see someone who arrived, who has the role, who copes, and you, inside, feel like a child dressed in someone else's adult clothes, waiting for someone to notice and send you out. There are those who never say this to anyone, afraid that, by confessing it, it might become true. But it is more common than you imagine, and giving it a name helps: it is called feeling like an impostor, and it is not a diagnosis about your worth, it is a wound in the way you see yourself.

It is as if there were a chair that is yours, that you earned, and that you refuse to truly occupy. You sit on the edge. You shrink yourself. You apologize for your presence. You wait for permission to speak, to decide, to occupy the space that is already yours by right. As if the chair were borrowed, and you had to give it back the moment someone noticed it wasn't meant to be yours.

Notice the amount of energy you spend, every day, on this doubt. On preparing twice as hard for a meeting, afraid they'll discover you don't know. On reviewing everything a thousand times, looking for the flaw that would prove your imposture. On reading, in others' eyes, signs that they have finally realized you don't deserve it. It is invisible work, parallel to your real work, that nobody sees and that exhausts you: the work of keeping upright a fraud that doesn't exist, of not being caught in a crime you didn't commit. And while you spend so much on that, less is left for what matters, and ironically you work worse than you would if you simply trusted what you already know.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not a lack of competence. You are competent, you won your place, you deserve to be there. People perhaps see you as capable, and you are. But inside, there is a voice telling you no, that you don't belong, that you are an impostor about to be discovered. And it is that voice, and not reality, that governs you.

There is a difference between winning a place and feeling entitled to it. You won it. But you don't feel entitled. And that lack of entitlement, that sense of imposture, makes you occupy your chair asking permission, like someone who is there by mistake and fears being sent away at any moment.

Notice that this is one of the greatest injustices you live: you worked to get there, more than many, perhaps more than those who occupy the place without hesitation, and it is precisely you who doesn't enjoy it. Those who deserved it least sometimes occupy their place with complete ease, and you, who did so much, occupy it trembling. There is something profoundly unjust in this, and I want you to see it, because seeing the injustice is the beginning of correcting it: it is not right that the one who tried hardest should be the one who allows herself least to savor the arrival.

This book is not about career, nor about professional confidence, nor about techniques to assert yourself. I've come to look at something else, something deeper. At the reason why, having won your place, you don't feel entitled to it. At the wound, existential and not professional, that makes you ask permission to occupy what is already yours.

And I'll tell you the direction up front, so you can read in peace. You don't need to ask authorization to occupy what you have already won. The place is yours, you earned it, and you have the right to occupy it fully, without asking permission, without diminishing yourself, without waiting for someone to confirm that you can be there. And the sense of imposture, which shrinks you, comes from a wound we are going to look at, and which is not healed by winning even more.

I want you to understand, from the start, the difference between what it looks like and what it is. What it looks like, from the outside, is a humble, modest woman, who doesn't boast, who doesn't impose herself. What it is, inside, is a woman who doesn't feel entitled to her own place, and who confuses, without knowing it, respect for others with the erasure of herself. True humility doesn't demand that you diminish yourself, nor that you deny what you are worth. On the contrary: it knows its worth with serenity, without inflating it or hiding it. What you have is not humility, it is a wound dressed as humility, and there is an enormous difference between the two, which this book has come to help you see.

This book hasn't come to ask you to become arrogant, to boast, to diminish others in order to raise yourself. It asks you the opposite of arrogance and the opposite of erasure: to place yourself in your rightful place, neither above nor below what you are. Recognizing your real worth is not vanity, it is truth. And occupying your place fully doesn't turn you into a person you don't want to be. It makes you, at last, the whole woman who won that place and who deserves to inhabit it in peace.

Maybe you recognize yourself in small things.

In feeling that you don't deserve your role, your position, your recognition, even having won them through your own effort.

In being afraid of being discovered, that they'll realize you're not as good as you seem, that it was all a mistake that sooner or later will come undone.

In diminishing yourself in meetings, in not giving your opinion with the firmness of someone who has the right, in asking permission, through words or posture, to occupy the space that is yours.

In attributing your successes to luck, to chance, to others, and never to your own merit, as if recognizing it were presumption.

In feeling that you are always one step away from being discovered, as if there were, hidden, a truth about your incapacity that nobody has seen yet, but which one day will surface.

They are all the same thing. The chair won and not occupied. The sense of not having a right to a place that you did, in fact, earn.

I want to show you how this shows up day to day, in things you perhaps already take for humility. In a meeting, you have a clear idea, a right one, and before saying it you precede it with maybe I'm wrong, I don't know if this makes sense, it's just an idea, shrinking it in case you don't have a right to it. You receive praise, recognition, and instead of taking it in, you deflect it: it was luck, it was the team, anyone would have done it. An opportunity you deserve arises, a promotion, a project, and you don't put yourself forward, because a part of you thinks you're not enough, that it's for others more legitimate than you.

And there is a detail that reveals everything: the way you react when someone treats you as the authority you are. When they ask for your opinion as if it carried weight, when they treat you as the expert, when they expect the decision from you, a discomfort rises in you, almost a fright, a desire to say but I'm not really that good. Instead of inhabiting the authority you won, you want to give it back, to correct the impression, to lower the bar that others, rightly, set for you. Because receiving recognition of your worth confronts you with the imposture you feel, and you flee from it like someone fleeing being caught.

And there is something the world doesn't see, beneath your apparent modesty. It sees a competent woman, perhaps even too humble, and sometimes it takes advantage of that, gives your credit to others, pays you less than you deserve, passes you over, because you don't claim, you don't impose yourself, you don't occupy. It doesn't see the anguish underneath, the constant fear of being discovered, the energy spent hiding a fraud that doesn't exist. It sees the humility, not the wound. And you, who are perhaps even praised for not standing out, become even more bound to the edge of the chair, because now being small is also being well regarded.

## 2. The place I don't feel entitled to occupy

Go back. Beneath the professional surface, there is a question of legitimacy. Do I feel entitled to occupy this place?

And your answer, carved deep, is no. However much you have won it, you don't feel legitimate. There is a difference between the place you have and the place you feel you deserve, and that difference is your suffering.

Where does this lack of legitimacy come from? Almost always, from far away. From a time when your right to occupy space, to stand out, to have worth, was not confirmed, or was questioned, or was denied.

Maybe you grew up where your merits were not recognized, or were minimized, or where standing out was frowned upon. And you learned not to feel entitled to recognition, to doubt your worth, to think that standing out was presumption.

Maybe you are a woman in a world that, for a long time, told women that certain places were not for them. That the places of power, of decision, of prominence, belonged to others. And even though you have won one of those places, you carry the old message that you don't belong there, that you are on ground that was not meant to be yours.

Maybe you are the first, in your family, to reach where you reached. The first to study, to have that role, to enter that world. And you feel a stranger in it, an impostor, someone who doesn't have the pedigree, the origin, the right of those who always belonged to those places. There is a particular loneliness in being the first: you have, behind you, no one who has trodden that ground, who tells you that you belong, who confirms that it is your place. And that lack of references from within makes you feel, always, like someone who entered through a door that was not for you, even having opened it with your own effort.

But notice: being the first is not proof that you don't belong. It is proof of the opposite, that you had to be more, to do more, to open a path that no one had opened for you. You didn't arrive there by luck or by mistake, you arrived because you broke through a ceiling that others, before you, never even touched. Your strangeness in that world doesn't say that you don't deserve to be in it. It says you were a pioneer, and pioneers always feel strange, because they tread ground where no one of their origin has trodden. That is not imposture. It is courage, and it deserves to be recognized as such, above all by you.

Notice what this does to you. You win the place, but you don't inhabit it. You have the role, but you feel like an outsider. And you live with a sense of imposture, that they will discover you shouldn't be there, that makes you occupy your position fearfully, asking permission, instead of occupying it with the right of someone who earned it.

Maybe you grew up in a house where your worth was only recognized when it came accompanied by modesty, and where showing pride in what you did was seen as vanity, presumption, a flaw. Where you were taught that boasting was ugly, that you had to wait for others to recognize, never to claim. And you learned to hide your worth even from yourself, to devalue it by reflex, to fear that recognizing it would turn you into a person you shouldn't be. And you carried that lesson into work, where you continue unable to give yourself the credit you deserve, because giving it to yourself feels like a transgression.

I want you to see this without guilt. Your lack of legitimacy is not a lack of merit, nor false modesty. It is an old wound, that of someone who learned, somewhere, that she had no right to occupy certain places, and who carries that message even after having won them. You are not an impostor. You are someone who was taught to doubt her right to be there, and who won the place but not the inner permission to occupy it.

And I want you to see where this is stored, because it matters. This doubt is not only in your head, in what you think about yourself. It is deeper, in a feeling that doesn't obey reason. You know, rationally, that you won your place, that you are competent, that you deserve it. And even so you don't feel it. Because legitimacy is not decided with arguments, it is felt, or it is not felt, and yours, marked by a time when your right to be there was denied, is not felt, however much your reason confirms it. That is why it isn't enough to list your merits to feel legitimate: the wound is not in the list, it is in a place older than words, and it heals slowly, with experience and, very often, with help.

## 3. The impostor who doesn't exist

There is a figure who inhabits you, and I want to name her. The impostor. The sense that you are a fraud, about to be discovered, that you are not as good as you seem, that your place is a mistake that will one day come undone.

Notice how this figure governs you. You attribute your successes to luck, to chance, to the help of others, to everything but your own merit. You live with the fear of being discovered, that they'll perceive the fraud you feel yourself to be. You diminish yourself, so that nobody notices you, so that the imposture is not exposed. And the more you win, the stronger the impostor becomes, because each new success is one more thing you feel you don't deserve.

And there is a particular exhaustion in living like this, that perhaps you no longer even notice, so used to it are you. That of someone who always works twice as hard, not out of enjoyment, but to compensate for a flaw she imagines she has. That of someone who can never relax in what she knows, because relaxing would be letting her guard down and risking the discovery. That of someone who lives a success not with joy, but with more fear, because each step she climbs is higher from which she fears to fall. It is a tiredness that comes not from the work itself, but from doing it always under the imaginary threat of being unmasked, and few kinds of tiredness are as deep as that of living pretending a security one doesn't feel.

I want to tell you a truth about this impostor. She doesn't exist. You are not a fraud. You won your place with real merit, with real competence, with real work. The fraud you feel yourself to be is a feeling, not a fact. And the fear of being discovered is the fear of a truth that doesn't exist, because there is nothing to discover, except a competent person who doesn't believe in her own competence.

I want you to understand the difference, because it frees you: one thing is what you feel, another is what is. You feel like an impostor. But you are competent. The two things coexist in you, and the first doesn't cancel out the second. The mistake is to take the feeling for truth, to let the sense of fraud define what you are, when it is only an old echo, and not a faithful report on your worth. Learning to say I feel like an impostor, but I know I'm not, to separate the feeling from the fact, is one of the steps that frees you most, because it lifts from you the obligation to believe a feeling that lies.

Notice the injustice of this figure. You apply to yourself a severity, a distrust, a judgment that you would never apply to another person in your circumstances. If a friend of yours had won what you won, would you say she is an impostor? Or would you recognize her merit? Almost certainly the latter. But to yourself, you deny what you would recognize in anyone else, because the impostor is not a fair assessment of your worth, it is a wound that distorts the way you see yourself.

Notice, too, the twisted logic with which the impostor feeds herself. When you fail, it is proof that you are no good, that you really are the fraud you fear. When you succeed, it was luck, it was easy, anyone could have done it. Did you notice? There is no way out: failure condemns you, and success doesn't save you. It is a court in which you are always guilty, in which no proof of worth is accepted and every proof of failure is decisive. And a court like that is not justice, it is a wound pretending to be a judge. When you realize that the impostor will never acquit you, however much you win, perhaps you will stop asking her for the verdict, and start judging yourself with the justice you would apply to any other person.

And there is an enormous cost in living with the impostor. It costs you your peace, because you live on alert, afraid of being discovered. It costs you opportunities, because you diminish yourself, you don't put yourself forward, you don't claim what you deserve, because you don't feel entitled. It costs you the enjoyment of what you won, because you can't take pleasure in successes you feel you don't deserve. And it costs you an immense energy, spent hiding a fraud that doesn't exist, proving a worth you already have, justifying a place that is already yours.

And there is a cruel trap in the impostor: she is never satisfied, however much you win. Each new success, instead of calming her, gives her more fuel, because it is one more thing you feel you don't deserve, one more height from which you can fall, one more reason to fear being discovered. That is why someone who lives with the impostor is not healed by winning more, and you have perhaps already tried it, thinking that the next victory, the next role, the next recognition would bring you, at last, the sense of deserving. It didn't, and it won't, because the problem was never one of merit, it was a wound that no success fills, because it was not a success that opened it.

Notice, too, the injustice the impostor does to the truth of the facts. Your successes are real. Your competences are real. The work you did to get where you got was real. None of it was luck, nor mistake, nor favor. It was you. And when you attribute everything to chance, to the help of others, to circumstance, you are not being humble, you are erasing yourself from your own feats, robbing yourself of the credit that is yours by right. Recognizing what you did is not vanity. It is honesty with the truth of your journey.

It costs you, too, the joy of what you won. There were milestones in your life, real achievements, worthy of celebration, and you didn't celebrate them, or you celebrated them with a reserve, with a tightness, with the sense that it was too soon, that it wasn't that big a deal, that perhaps you didn't deserve them. And so you passed by the joy of reaching where you reached, always devaluing, always relativizing, always not letting yourself savor what you won. There is a silent mourning in this: that of all the victories you had and did not live, because the impostor wouldn't let you believe they were yours.

I want you to feel the weight of this. The weight of living as an impostor, in a place you won. Of never enjoying what you earned, because you feel you don't deserve it. Of living with the fear of being discovered, when there is nothing to discover. It is a deep exhaustion, and perhaps you already take it for humility, when it is, deep down, a wound that prevents you from occupying, in peace, what is yours. And you deserve, after so much work, to stop carrying it.

## 4. Who am I if I occupy my place

I'll leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who would you be if you occupied, fully, your place? If you sat in your chair with the right of someone who won it? If you stopped asking permission to be where you already are?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Perhaps a vertigo, perhaps a fear.

For you, this question touches a deep fear. Because occupying your place fully, without diminishing yourself, without asking permission, seems to you presumption, arrogance, exposure. As if claiming your place were risking being caught in the fraud, being looked at as someone who thinks too much of herself, being punished for daring to occupy what you feel you don't deserve.

And the shadow that hides here is the fear of fully asserting yourself. Of occupying your place with right, of recognizing your worth, of affirming yourself without asking permission. Because that confronts you with the imposture you fear, and with the fear that, by occupying the place, you would be exposed, judged, sent away.

Notice how this imprisons you. You don't occupy your place, even having won it, because occupying it awakens the fear of imposture. You diminish yourself, you ask permission, you stay on the edge of the chair, not because you don't deserve it, but because you lack the inner permission to occupy what is yours. And you live, this way, always short of your place, always asking authorization to be where you already have the right to be.

And there is an enormous fragility in this. Because a person who doesn't occupy her place, who diminishes herself, who asks permission, is treated, very often, as someone who doesn't deserve the place. The world, in part, treats us by the way we place ourselves. Whoever occupies her place with right is respected in it. Whoever occupies it asking permission invites, without meaning to, being questioned. Your lack of inner right becomes, at times, a lack of outer recognition, in a circle that confirms the imposture you feel.

Notice this circle, because it is important to break it. You diminish yourself because you feel like an impostor. Others, seeing you diminished, treat you as less. That treatment confirms the imposture to you, and you diminish yourself even more. It is a self-fulfilling prophecy: your doubt produces, in the world, exactly the signs that you then use as proof of the doubt. And the only way to break the circle is to enter it from another point: to occupy your place despite the doubt, and to discover that, when you place yourself with right, others treat you with the respect that your posture, now, invites. It is not manipulation. It is letting your real worth appear, instead of hiding it behind an apology.

And I want to tell you something about the terror this question raises. What terrifies you is not, truly, occupying your place. It is being caught, exposed, unmasked, by occupying it. For the impostor, occupying the place and being discovered are the same thing, as if by sitting fully in the chair you turned a spotlight on your supposed fraud. But there is no fraud to expose. When you occupy your place, what appears is not an unmasked impostor, it is a competent professional doing, at last, justice to what she is. The spotlight you fear illuminates, after all, not your shame, but your worth.

I want to tell you something about this fear, because it begins to disarm it. Occupying your place is not presumption. It is justice. You won it, you deserve to occupy it, and to do so fully is not arrogance, it is simply taking what is yours by right. Arrogance would be occupying a place you didn't deserve. You deserved yours. Occupying it is the minimum of justice you owe yourself, and there is nothing presumptuous about it, however much your wound tells you otherwise.

And I want you to see the irony of the impostor, which perhaps will relieve you. The people who suffer most from feeling like impostors are, almost always, the most competent and the most demanding of themselves. The true frauds, those who genuinely don't deserve the place, rarely doubt themselves, they occupy it without hesitation, convinced of their inflated worth. The fact that you doubt so much, that you are so afraid of not deserving, is, paradoxically, a sign that you do deserve: because only someone who takes her work seriously, who has high standards, who genuinely cares, questions herself this way. Your doubt doesn't prove that you are an impostor. It proves exactly the opposite.

Don't answer the question of who you would be if you occupied your place just yet. But leave it open. Because it is there that the door lies. On the day you feel entitled to the place you won, you stop having to occupy it asking permission. And then, only then, can you sit, at last, in your chair, fully.

## 5. When doubt becomes suffering

I have to stop the book for a moment, with care. Because there is a point at which the doubt about your right to be there stops being a pattern and becomes a suffering that calls for more.

If the sense of imposture is so strong that it causes you constant anxiety, a permanent fear of being discovered, an anguish that won't let you go at work. If that doubt extends, and you already feel, in many places, that you have no right to be there, that you are worth less, that they will realize you are no good. If there is a deep sadness, a fierce self-criticism, a persistent sense of not being enough, that takes away your peace and your sleep.

This may be more than a pattern. It may be an anxiety, a wound of self-esteem, that calls for serious help, from a professional. Impostor syndrome, taken to the extreme, and the persistent devaluation of oneself, are not merely modesty. They are real suffering, and there are those who know how to walk alongside it.

I want to distinguish two things for you, because it matters for knowing what you need. A mild doubt about one's own worth, which many people have and which experience gradually calms, is one thing. A sense of imposture so deep that it causes you constant anguish, that takes away your sleep, that makes you genuinely suffer, is another. The first eases with the understanding of this book and with the experience of occupying your place. The second, very often, calls for more: the company of someone prepared to work the root of your lack of legitimacy, which comes from far away, and which rarely undoes itself just by telling yourself that you are competent.

And I want to take away a fear of yours: seeking help doesn't confirm that you are incapable, nor that you can't bear your place. It is the opposite. Recognizing that you suffer, and caring for it, is an act of someone who takes herself seriously, who respects herself enough not to live in permanent anguish. The impostor will want to convince you that asking for help is one more proof of your fragility. It is not. It is proof that you are beginning, at last, to treat yourself with the care you would give to anyone who suffered as you suffer.

And I want to tell you something. Seeking help for this doesn't confirm that you are a fraud. It confirms the opposite: that you take care of yourself, that you take your suffering seriously, that you deserve to feel at peace in the place you won. A psychologist, a therapist, can help you undo the impostor, rebuild your sense of legitimacy, occupy, at last, your place without the anguish that today accompanies it.

If you recognize, in yourself, this suffering, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Seeking help is not weakness, nor proof that you don't deserve. It is the care you deserve, and which you perhaps deny yourself because you don't feel, even, entitled to it. But you are entitled. To your place, and to the care of occupying it in peace.

## 6. I don't need to ask permission

If you won the place but don't feel entitled to it, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You don't need to ask permission to occupy what is already yours.

Read this slowly. You live waiting for an authorization. For someone to confirm to you that you can be there, that you deserve your place, that you have the right to occupy it. And that authorization, which you wait for from the outside, never comes enough, because the lack of right is not from the outside, it is from within. However much they confirm it to you, you keep feeling that you don't belong, because the permission you lack is your own.

And notice how this waiting keeps you trapped in a position of permanent subordinate, even when your role is one of authority. Because whoever waits for authorization places herself always below the one who would give it, at the mercy of others' approval, dependent on a sign confirming that she may. And you do that even when you are the boss, the expert, the one in charge: you keep waiting, inside, for someone more legitimate to validate you. But there is no such someone. The most legitimate person to authorize you to be in your place is you, and as long as you delegate that authorization to others, you will live small in a big place, subordinate in a post of authority, waiting for a permission that only you can sign.

And I propose something to you. That you give yourself the authorization you wait for from others. That you recognize, yourself, your right to the place you won. That you stop waiting for someone to confirm that you can be there, and occupy your place by the authority of someone who earned it, without needing anyone's permission.

Notice why the authorization from outside never reaches you, however much they praise you, promote you, recognize you. It is that each piece of praise passes through a filter, that of the impostor, who undoes it before it reaches the bottom: they tell you that you are good, and the impostor translates it's just kindness, or they're mistaken, or they haven't realized yet. That is why you can collect recognitions and keep feeling like a fraud, because the wound is not in the lack of external recognition, it is in an internal filter that lets none in. And an internal filter is not fixed by accumulating more praise from outside. It is fixed from within, by changing the way you see yourself, and that, very often, is done with help.

That is why I tell you, with all firmness, that the authorization you seek is yours to give. Not because others don't value you, but because, until it is you who believes in your right, no external valuing reaches the place where it hurts. The path is not to convince others, they are already convinced, it is to convince yourself, slowly, of a truth they already see and you don't yet: that you do, in fact, deserve the place you occupy.

Because the place is yours. You won it. And nobody needs to authorize you to occupy what you have already earned. The authorization you seek is yours, and you can give it to yourself, today, by recognizing that you do, in fact, deserve to be where you are.

I want to be honest with you about one thing: giving yourself that authorization is not an instant, it is a path. You won't wake up tomorrow, determined to feel legitimate, and feel it. Inner legitimacy is rebuilt slowly, gesture by gesture, each time you occupy your place despite the doubt and discover that it remains yours. Don't wait for the sense of deserving in order to begin occupying. It is the other way around: occupy first, even doubting, and the sense of deserving comes afterward, as the fruit of the repeated experience of being there and belonging. Confidence doesn't come before the action, it comes from it.

And I want to give you an image that may help you. Think of your journey as a résumé, not the one you write for others, but the real one, with everything you did, learned, overcame, to get where you got. The years of work, the difficulties conquered, the competences gained, the problems solved. When the impostor tells you that you don't deserve it, go back to that real résumé, not to boast, but to remind yourself of the facts. Because imposture lives on forgetting the journey, and legitimacy lives on recognizing it. You didn't arrive here in a leap of luck. You arrived step by step, with merit, and that journey is the proof, which the impostor makes you forget, that the place is, in fact, yours.

I want to give you a concrete gesture, a small one, to begin. The next time you catch yourself prefacing an idea with maybe I'm wrong, or I don't know if this makes sense, or it's just a silly idea, cut that apology, and say the idea straight, without the request for permission in front. Notice how it costs you, and notice too that the idea, said without the cushion, gains weight, is heard differently. Those small cuts, repeated, gradually teach your body, and others, that you have a right to speak without apologizing for doing so. It is not a technique of assertion. It is your ceasing to undermine, with words, your own right to be there.

I want to speak to you of the verb of this crossing. To occupy. To occupy your chair fully. To sit in it with the right of someone who won it. To give your opinion with the firmness of someone who belongs. To claim what you deserve without asking permission. To recognize your successes as yours, the fruit of your merit, and not of luck. To occupy your place, at last, as yours.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you occupy more of your place, the old impostor, the fear of being discovered, the sense of presumption, the urge to diminish yourself again. And you will want to go back to the edge of the chair, to asking permission, to the safety of not exposing yourself.

But it is precisely there, in that occupying your place despite the fear, that your healing lies. Because each time you occupy your place, and you are not unmasked, you are not punished, you are not sent away, you learn that you had a right to it. That you were not an impostor. That the place was yours, and that occupying it was just.

And don't confuse, along the way, the doubt that returns with proof that you don't deserve it after all. The doubt will return, even after many steps taken, above all in the moments of greatest exposure, in a new role, in a bigger challenge, before people you admire. That doesn't mean you went backward, nor that the imposture was right. It means only that the wound is old and that it reactivates when the ground is new. The work is not to never doubt again, it is to learn to occupy your place even when the doubt appears, to not hand it the reins, to go forward despite it. The difference is not in ceasing to feel the impostor, it is in ceasing to obey her.

It will cost you, because the old voice, that of the impostor, is carved deep, and it will appear each time you occupy more of your place, with its usual harshness. It will tell you who do you think you are, they'll realize you're no good, don't take on so much. The work is not to silence it all at once, it is to stop obeying it. To hear it say you have no right, and to answer it, slowly, I won this place, and it is mine. And each time you occupy your place despite it, and the sky doesn't fall, you weaken the old voice, and you strengthen a new one, more your own, more just, which knows how to recognize what you are worth.

The awakening, when it comes, is a new sense of belonging. It is the day you sit at the table and feel, for an instant, that you belong there. That you deserve to be there. That you are not there by mistake, but by right. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves a lifetime of imposture, and gives you back the place that was always yours, but which you had never given yourself permission to occupy.

## 7. To occupy what I won

There is a destination for this crossing. To occupy, fully, what you won.

And I want to show you what this means, in practice.

To occupy what you won is to stop diminishing yourself. It is to give your opinion without apologizing, without prefacing it with maybe I'm wrong, without swallowing it for fear of not having a right to it. It is to claim the recognition, the pay, the opportunities you deserve, without waiting for someone to offer them to you out of kindness. It is to recognize your successes as yours, and not as luck.

It is also learning to receive a compliment without undoing it. When someone recognizes your work, instead of saying it was nothing, or it was the team, or I got lucky, try saying only thank you, and let the compliment in, let it stay, let it be yours. It seems a small gesture, but it is enormous, because each compliment you undo is a proof of your worth that you refuse, and each compliment you take in is a proof you let, at last, reach the place where it hurts. Taking in recognition is not vanity. It is your ceasing to rob yourself, time after time, of the truth of what you are worth.

And it is claiming what is just without guilt. Pay that matches what you give. Credit that is yours and that they keep giving to others. An opportunity you deserve and for which you don't put yourself forward because you feel you are less. Claiming what is just is not greed, it is justice, and your wound confuses the two, making you accept less than you deserve so as not to seem demanding. But asking for what is just doesn't make you difficult. It makes you someone who respects herself, and who teaches others to respect her too.

It is, above all, sitting in your chair as someone who belongs. Not on the edge, not asking permission, not waiting to be sent away. But with the calm of someone who won her place and has the right to occupy it. With the dignity of someone who knows she deserves to be there.

I want to undo a fear, because it will appear. The fear that, by occupying your place, you will become arrogant, presumptuous, one of those who thinks too much of herself. There is an enormous distance between occupying your place and being arrogant. Arrogance is thinking oneself more than one is, occupying a place one doesn't deserve, diminishing others to raise oneself. Occupying your place is simply taking what is yours, without diminishing yourself or diminishing anyone. It is not thinking yourself superior. It is ceasing to think yourself inferior. And that, far from arrogance, is only justice toward yourself.

And there is an enormous liberation in this. When you occupy your place fully, you stop spending energy hiding a fraud that doesn't exist, proving a worth you already have, justifying a presence that is already legitimate. That energy, freed, becomes available for what matters: for your work, for your life, for enjoying, at last, what you won. Occupying your place doesn't exhaust you. It frees you from the exhaustion of borrowing it.

I want you to see, too, that occupying your place is, paradoxically, a service to others, and not only to yourself. When you diminish yourself, when you hide your worth, when you ask permission to be there, you deprive others of what you have to give. Your ideas go unsaid, your experience unshared, your leadership unexercised. And those who lose with that are not only you, they are the teams, the projects, the people who would benefit from your full contribution. Occupying your place is not selfishness. It is ceasing to hide, out of fear, that which you could contribute, and which the world needs precisely from whoever has it.

And I want you to see that there are those who learn to occupy their place by looking at you. Younger women, people you guide, perhaps daughters, who see how you place yourself in the world of work. If they see you always asking permission, diminishing yourself, attributing your successes to luck, they learn that this is how a woman ought to place herself, and it is likely they will shrink too, that they will live the same imposture. But if they see you occupy your place with dignity, recognize your worth without vanity, sit in your chair fully, they learn that they can do the same. Sometimes a woman learns to occupy her place, at last, not only for herself, but to open a path for those who come after.

And I want you to see that occupying your place doesn't make you less competent, less careful, less dedicated. All of that stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops coming from the fear of being discovered, and comes instead from the security of someone who knows she deserves. And a professional who works from security is better, more creative, more courageous, than one who works from the terror of not being enough. Fear shrinks and paralyzes; legitimacy frees and expands. What you will do when you stop doubting your right to be there will be greater than everything you did trembling on the edge of the chair.

I won't give you a method, because this book is not about techniques of assertion. But I leave you the principle. The place is yours. You won it. Occupy it, fully, without asking permission. And discover, slowly, that the permission you waited for from outside was yours to give, and that, by giving it, you sit, at last, in your chair, with the right of someone who always deserved to be there.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, feel fully legitimate in a place you always occupied fearfully. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your wound with humility, and your imposture with a truth.

Now you know that you won the place but don't occupy it, and that this is not a lack of competence, it is an old wound dressed as humility.

You know that underneath there is a lack of inner legitimacy, a sense of not having a right to be there, which comes from far away and is not measured by your real merit.

You know that the impostor who inhabits you doesn't exist, that she is a feeling and not a fact, and that you can feel like an impostor and be, at the same time, fully competent.

You know whom you fear being if you occupy your place, and that underneath lies the fear of fully asserting yourself, of being exposed by occupying what is, after all, yours.

You know that doubt can become suffering, and that seeking help doesn't confirm the fraud, it is, on the contrary, an act of someone who respects herself enough not to live in permanent anguish.

You know that you don't need to ask permission, and that the verb is to occupy, what you have already won, with the serenity of someone who stopped waiting for others' authorization and authorized, at last, herself.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to stay with it.

Imagine someone who won, with real merit, a seat at the table of an important room. And who enters the room, but stays standing, leaning against the wall, without sitting in the chair that is hers, waiting for someone to tell her she may sit. And nobody tells her, because nobody imagines that she needs authorization to occupy the chair she won. And she stays standing, the whole meeting, looking at her empty chair, without daring to occupy it.

The crossing is to sit down. To recognize that the chair is yours, that you won it, that you don't need permission to occupy it. And to discover that, by sitting down, nobody tells you to get up, because the place was always yours, and the only person who denied it to you was you.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more thing in which you have to be perfect. There will be setbacks. Days when you occupy your place with firmness, and days when you go back to the edge of the chair, to asking permission, to diminishing yourself, as always. And the impostor will use each setback to tell you you see, you don't deserve it after all. Don't believe her. Setbacks are part of it. Nobody undoes all at once a lifetime of imposture. It is undone little by little, and each time you occupy your place and nothing bad happens, a new proof is left, and it is from those proofs, added together, that your inner legitimacy, slowly, is built.

And I want to tell you one last thing, simple, to close. You deserve your place. Not for occupying it perfectly, not for never doubting, not for being infallible. You deserve it because you won it, with your work, your competence, your story. The girl, or the young woman, who was taught to doubt her right to be there, was not wrong to doubt, she learned to doubt in a place that denied her legitimacy. But today, as an adult, the place is yours, you won it, and there is nothing left to prove to anyone. Only one thing is missing: that you give yourself the permission you always waited for from outside.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this book together. I don't need to ask permission to occupy what I have already won. The chair is mine, I earned it, and I have the right to sit in it fully.

To occupy was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not done by reading. It is done by sitting down, slowly, in your chair. By occupying your place, without diminishing yourself, without asking permission. And there is a deeper work, with someone who walks alongside you, to undo the impostor and rebuild your sense of legitimacy, because it comes from far away, and rarely rebuilds itself alone.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. The chair is yours. Not borrowed, not by mistake, not waiting to be claimed by someone more legitimate. Yours, won, deserved. And the only person in the world who still needs to be convinced of that is you. Others already know, that is why they gave you the place, that is why they count on you, that is why they treat you as the professional you are. Only you are left to convince, and that is the most important, and most just, work you can do: to give yourself, at last, the permission to occupy, in peace, what was always yours.

And before you close these pages, I ask one thing of you only. A hard one, for you.

This week, occupy your place in a moment when you usually ask permission. Give your opinion without prefacing it with apologies. Claim something you deserve. Recognize a success of yours as yours, without attributing it to luck. And notice the fear of the impostor, the sense of presumption, the urge to diminish yourself. And occupy your place anyway. Because it is in that occupying, endured in spite of the fear, that you begin to feel, at last, that the place is yours, and that you never needed anyone's permission to occupy what, with merit, you won.

And if, in doing it, you feel presumptuous, excessive, like someone who thinks too much of herself, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you are only occupying your rightful place, neither more nor less, and that what you feel as presumption is, almost always, only the normal size of someone who stops shrinking. Your ruler is miscalibrated by imposture, and that is why what is just seems too much to you. Trust, for now, not in what you feel, but in what you know: that the place is yours, and that occupying it is only justice.

Because you earned every inch of the road that brought you here, and you deserve, at last, to sit down.

Because that chair always waited for you, and you deserve, at last, to occupy it.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
