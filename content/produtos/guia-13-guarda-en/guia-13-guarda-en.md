# Lowering your guard in safety

**Small gestures for the woman who made herself strong, flawless, or self-sufficient in order to be safe.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a self-awareness and reflection resource. It does not replace psychological or psychotherapeutic support. If you recognize in yourself a state of vigilance that never stops, a persistent difficulty in resting or in letting someone take care of you, please seek professional help. Lowering your guard is a process. Doing it safely is what I am offering you here.*

---

## 1. The armor you no longer need but cannot take off

I will start by naming something you have perhaps never put into words: you are strong. Everyone knows you are strong. You handle things. You endure. You do not fall apart. The people around you lean on you and almost never ask whether you need support too, because your posture says, without saying it, that you need nothing.

And maybe a part of you is proud of this. Another part is exhausted.

I want you to look at this strength with new eyes. Because there is a question at the heart of this guide, and I want to set it down here gently: what did you have to become in order to survive?

Think about this seriously. Not strength as a natural gift, as a cheerful and robust temperament. Strength as a response. The strength that gets built when a child realizes, early on, that no one is going to hold her, and that therefore she will have to hold herself. The perfection that gets refined when a little girl learns that she is only loved when she causes no trouble, when she does not fail, when she does everything right. The self-sufficiency that rises up when someone discovers that asking is risky, that needing is dangerous, that depending is the shortest path to disappointment.

This is not weakness in disguise. It is intelligence. It was the most intelligent thing you could do with what you had. When the world was not safe, you built armor. And the armor worked. It kept you standing. It kept you safe. It brought you here.

The problem is not the armor. The problem is that it no longer comes off.

Imagine a woman who lived through a war and who, years after the war ended, still sleeps with her armor on. The bombs no longer fall. The streets are calm. But her body never got the message that it can rest. She still sleeps with her boots on, one ear listening, ready to leap to her feet. To someone watching from the outside, it looks like an overreaction. To her, it is the only way she knows how to be alive.

You are perhaps that woman. The war is over (or it was never really a war, it was just a childhood where no one held you), but you are still in your armor. You still solve everything alone even when there are hands held out to you. You still do everything flawlessly even when no one would notice if you failed. You still do not ask even when there are people who want to give.

And it costs you. It costs you in a way that few people see, because the armor is silent. On the outside, you function beautifully. On the inside, you carry a weight of metal that you never set down. You are always on alert. Always one step ahead. Always calculating what might go wrong so you can get ahead of it. And that tires you in a way that sleep does not resolve.

There is one thing I want you to hear clearly: your strength is real and it is yours, and I am not asking you to throw it away. What I am asking is more subtle. That you recognize there is a difference between being strong and being permanently on guard. Strength is a capacity. Being on guard is a prison. And you deserve to be able to choose when you put the armor on, instead of having it glued to your skin with no way to take it off.

There is a sign I tend to look for in the women I work with, and you may recognize it in yourself. When I ask them how they are, they always answer with the state of other people. "My son is better." "Work is under control." "At home everything is going well." Did you notice they said nothing about themselves? The armor taught you to deflect attention away from yourself toward what you manage, what you handle, the others who depend on you. And when someone insists, when they look you in the eyes and ask again, "but you, how are you?", there is a moment of panic. Because you do not know. You are so used to watching the outside that you have lost contact with your own inside. The sentinel always looks outward. Never inward.

Stay with me. We are not going to rip anything off by force. We are going to learn, slowly, to lower our guard one millimeter at a time, and only where it is safe.

## 2. Why lowering your guard feels so dangerous

Before I give you practical gestures, I need to explain something, because without it you will try to lower your guard by force and you will hurt yourself.

For you, lowering your guard does not feel like relaxation. It feels like danger.

Notice the difference. For many people, resting is simply pleasant. Asking for help is natural. Showing that they are tired or sad costs them nothing. But for someone who built survival armor, each of these gestures triggers an internal alarm. The body does not register "now I can rest." It registers "now I am exposed." And exposed, in the language of your nervous system, means at risk.

This is why you, who endure so much, struggle with gestures that seem trivial to others. Accepting someone's help. Saying "I am not okay." Leaving a task unfinished. Leaning back and doing nothing. Each of these small acts asks you to set down the armor, and your body reacts as though you were undressing in the middle of a battle.

There is a mechanism here worth knowing, because knowing it is already halfway to disarming it. Your nervous system learned, a long time ago, an equation: lowering your guard equals being caught off guard. Perhaps you were caught off guard. Perhaps, in the rare times you trusted, when you asked, when you showed the soft part, something went wrong. Someone used it against you. Someone did not show up. Someone hurt you in exactly the spot where you opened up. And your body recorded: never again. Do not open up. Do not trust. Handle it alone, it is safer.

This lesson was not foolish. It was protective. But it was recorded by a version of you who lived in a context that perhaps no longer exists. And the body, which does not distinguish the past from the present very well, keeps applying the old rule to new situations where it no longer serves. It is like having a fire alarm that was calibrated during a real fire and that, years later, keeps going off at the smoke from a piece of toast. The alarm is not broken. It is doing exactly what it was trained to do. It is just that the danger has passed, and no one has lowered its sensitivity.

There is something else that makes all of this harder, and I want to name it gently. For you, your identity has fused with the armor. You are not just someone who is strong. You became "the strong one." The one who endures. The one who does not complain. The flawless one. The one who needs no one. And when your identity is fused with the armor, taking it off does not feel like rest. It feels like ceasing to be you. It feels like losing your place. It feels like the terrifying question: if I am not the strong one, then who am I? And if I stop enduring, will they still want me here?

So listen to me carefully: I am not going to ask you to take off the armor. Never. To ask that would be to ask you to stand naked on a street that your body still reads as dangerous, and you would do very well to refuse.

What I am going to propose is completely different. Not taking off the armor. Lowering your guard. And lowering is not taking off. Lowering is loosening one piece, in a safe place, for a moment, with the possibility of tightening it again at any time. It is keeping the armor nearby while the body learns, slowly and through its own experience, that not every place is a battlefield.

The sentinel who lives inside you, the one who never sleeps, the one who keeps watch twenty-four hours a day, does not need to be dismissed. She needs to discover that she can rest a little without the world falling apart. And that discovery is not made with arguments. It is made with small, repeated, safe experiences. That is what the next chapter is about.

## 3. Small, safe gestures to begin setting down the armor

Here is the heart of this guide. Small gestures. Deliberately small. Because your sentinel is not going to accept big leaps, and she is right not to accept them. Big leaps confirm to her that lowering your guard is dangerous. Tiny gestures teach her the opposite, one at a time.

Read these gestures without rushing. You do not have to do them all. Choose one. The one that feels least threatening, not the most courageous. The courage here is not in doing the difficult gesture. It is in doing the small gesture and letting it go well.

**Start by loosening one piece where you are already safe.** Do not practice lowering your guard in the most dangerous place. Practice it in the safest one. Is there, in your life, a place or a person where the risk is minimal? A room where you are alone. An animal that does not judge you. A friend you already know, for certain, will not hurt you. Choose that place for the first gestures. The sentinel learns through contrast: she needs to live the experience of lowering your guard and the world not falling apart. Give her the easiest experience first.

**Leave one single thing undone, on purpose.** Not the whole house. Not the important work. One small thing with no real consequences. The bed unmade one morning. The breakfast dishes in the sink until the afternoon. An email answered with less perfection than usual. Leave it undone and notice what happens inside you. There will be discomfort, perhaps quite a lot. Stay with it. Do not rush to fix it. You are teaching your body that imperfection brings no punishment. That the world keeps turning even with the bed unmade.

**Accept help you would normally refuse.** The next time someone offers you help (carrying something, running an errand, taking on a task), and your mouth is already forming the "no, thank you, I have got it," stop. And say yes. Just this once. Just for this small thing. Not because you cannot do it alone. Precisely because you can and you still choose to let it in. Self-sufficiency is unlearned by letting someone do something for you that you would do better. The point is not the task. It is the experience of receiving.

**Say a true sentence instead of the armored one.** When people ask you "is everything okay?", you have an automatic answer. "Everything is great." "I am fine." "All under control." It is the armor speaking for you. Try, once, with a safe person, swapping that answer for something a millimeter more true. You do not have to bare yourself entirely. "Today I am tired" is enough, "it has been a hard week," "this is not my best day." One sentence. Small. True. And notice that saying it did not destroy you.

**Sit still doing nothing, for two minutes.** For the woman who made herself strong, rest feels like a threat, because stopping is when the thoughts arrive and when the body feels the tiredness it was ignoring. So start tiny. Two minutes. Sit down and do nothing. Not the phone, which is a guard disguised as rest. Nothing. Just being. It will be strange, perhaps distressing. The sentinel will want to get you up. Tell her quietly: "It is only two minutes. I am keeping watch. You can rest for this little while." You are showing her that stopping is not the same as being unprotected.

**Let yourself be seen in a small thing you did not control.** Perfection is a form of guard: if I never fail, no one can catch me. So let someone, once, see you in a moment that is not flawless. The house not tidy when a friend turns up unannounced. A small mistake you would normally hide and that this time you own out loud: "I was wrong, I am sorry." It is not humiliation. It is the slow discovery that the people who are worth it do not love you for your flawlessness. And that being seen failing does not take your place from you.

**Put your hand on your own chest and stay there.** This is the most intimate gesture, and perhaps the most difficult for the woman who was never held. Once a day, put a hand over your chest or over your belly, and let it stay there. Doing nothing. Fixing nothing. Just the warmth of your hand upon you. For the woman who held herself her whole life, this gesture says something new: I can be, at the same time, the one who protects and the one who is protected. I can take care of myself without being on guard. The same hand that was always raised with a sword can also rest and soothe.

I want to add something about how to do this, because the how matters as much as the what.

Go slowly. Truly slowly. If a gesture feels like too much, it is too much, and there is no medal here for forcing it. Step back to a smaller gesture. The sentinel only relaxes when the experience confirms that it is safe, and an experience in which you forced yourself and felt awful only confirms to her that she was right not to trust.

Notice what happens afterward, not just in the moment. Often, the discomfort comes later. You accept the help in the moment and it seems fine, and an hour later the shame arrives, or the urge to make up for it by doing twice as much. This is normal. It is the armor trying to tighten itself again. Do not obey right away. Notice, breathe, and let the experience settle.

And be patient with the part of you that does not want to do any of this. That part is not the enemy. It is the sentinel who kept you alive. Do not fight her. Thank her and invite her, gently, to try a small rest. She has worked a long time. She deserves to know that she can, now and then, set down the sword.

## 4. Força bridge

These gestures are real and you can begin today. They work. Each time you lower your guard one millimeter in a safe place and the world does not fall apart, your body learns something no argument could ever teach it. And that learning accumulates. Slowly, the armor stops being glued to your skin and becomes a piece you can put on and take off as you choose.

But I want to be transparent with you, as I always am. These gestures work on the surface, and many times the surface is enough. A woman who learns to accept help, to leave one thing undone, to say a true sentence, already lives with less weight and more lightness.

There is, however, a deeper layer. Because the question that opened this guide (what did I have to become in order to survive?) leads us to a place that gestures alone do not reach. The armor you built has a history. It comes from somewhere. From a home where you had to be an adult far too soon. From a figure who leaned on you instead of holding you. From an inheritance, passed down from generation to generation, in which the women of your line made themselves strong because they had no other choice, and passed on to you, without meaning to, the same armor. These roots are silent, but it is from them that the sentinel receives her orders.

For that deeper work, I created a collection of ebooks called Força, where we look calmly at the woman who made herself flawless, self-sufficient, indestructible, and at the path of relieving her of that weight without leaving her unprotected. You will find it at [viviannedossantos.com](https://viviannedossantos.com).

And if what these gestures show you is that the armor does not come only from you, that it comes from an older history, from patterns you inherited and did not choose, then it is in FreeMe that the deeper work begins. It is the space where we stop managing the symptoms of strength and look at its origin, so that you can return to its rightful place what was not yours to carry. You will find it at [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

But for now, stay with what you already hold in your hands. You do not need to change everything. You do not need to become a different person. You only need to discover, gesture by gesture, that there are places where you can already set down the sword. That not every place is a battlefield. That the sentinel can sleep a little while you rest, and that you will wake up whole.

You made yourself strong in order to survive. And you survived. You did it.

Now you can begin, slowly and safely, to learn something else. Not to survive. To live.

And for that, every now and then, it is enough to lower your guard one millimeter.
