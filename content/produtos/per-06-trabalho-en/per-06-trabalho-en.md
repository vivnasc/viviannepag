# The person who is never any trouble

**For you, who ask for nothing and need nothing, so as not to bother anyone. Because needing doesn't make you a burden, and you can need without bothering.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who erases herself so as not to weigh, who asks for nothing and needs nothing, and there is a point at which that self-erasure stops being discretion and becomes a self-neglect that hurts. If you recognize in yourself an inability to ask even when you're sinking, a sadness you hide from everyone, or a habit of never telling anyone anything, beginning with yourself, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your self-erasure is a beginning. It is not the whole path.*

---

## 1. I'm no trouble to anyone

Notice something you may say with pride, and that says more about you than you imagine: I'm no trouble to anyone. It's almost a motto, a way of presenting yourself to the world, and it looks like a virtue, but it hides a wound.

You're the easy one. The one who asks for nothing. The one who needs nothing. The one who makes do with what there is, who doesn't create problems, who doesn't weigh, who doesn't bother. The one who is always available for others, and who never asks anything back for herself.

You don't ask for help. You don't ask for attention. You don't ask anyone to take care of you, to notice you, to attend to your needs. You sort everything out alone, in silence, so as not to be any trouble to anyone whatsoever. And you even take pride in it, in being the one who manages, the one who doesn't need, the one who's never a nuisance.

And when you need something, you swallow it. When you're unwell, you hide it. When something hurts you, you say everything's fine. Because saying that you need, that you're unwell, that something hurt you, would be giving trouble, would be weighing, would be bothering, and that, you don't do.

And perhaps you can no longer even remember a time when you asked for something without rehearsing the request three times in your head, when you needed without apologizing, when you took up your place without asking permission for it. For someone who grew used to being no trouble, this way of living, always shrinking, always making herself small, has been engraved so deep that it already seems to be your temperament, almost a good quality. But it isn't. It isn't normal to live swallowing everything you need, suffering in silence, disappearing in order to be tolerated. It's only a wound so old that you stopped seeing it as a wound, and came to take it for kindness, for temperament, or for good manners.

Notice how you become small, without realizing it. You take up little space. You ask for little attention. You make yourself discreet, light, almost invisible, so as not to bother. You're the houseguest who is no trouble, the friend who doesn't weigh, the person no one has to take care of. And there is, in this, a strange pride, almost an identity: you take pride in being no trouble, in being easy, in needing nothing, without seeing that this pride is the disguise of a wound, and that what you take pride in is, deep down, your own absence.

I want to tell you right now, before anything else, that this isn't just good manners, nor consideration for others, even though it looks like that. People say you're easy, that you're no trouble, that you're impeccable. And there is kindness in you, yes. But beneath the kindness there's something else, older and sadder, that needs to be seen.

There's a belief that was engraved in you, very early. The belief that you're more lovable when you disappear. That your place, your right to be loved, depends on not weighing, not asking, not giving trouble. That needing makes you a burden, and that a burden, sooner or later, is set aside.

This book won't teach you to become demanding, to weigh on purpose, to overload others. I've come to look at something else. At the self-erasure you grew used to. At the needs you've been swallowing your whole life. At the fact that, from giving no trouble for so long, you've stopped existing, even to yourself.

And I'll tell you right away where this leads, so you can read without the fear of being pushed into weighing more. You can need without bothering. You can ask, take up space, have needs, and still be loved, because true love doesn't want you erased, it wants you present, whole, with your needs and all. And what taught you to disappear was not love. It was a wound, which we're going to look at.

While we're at it, separate with me what's seen from the outside from what's happening on the inside, because between the two there's an abyss. What's seen, from the outside, is a kind person, easy, considerate, who doesn't mind anything. What it is, on the inside, is a person who learned that she only has a place if she doesn't weigh, and who erases herself not out of free generosity, but out of fear that needing will cost her love. There's an enormous difference between not asking because, in fact, you don't need, and swallowing what you need out of terror of bothering. The first is lightness. The second is a wound dressed up as kindness.

This book hasn't come to tell you that you have to become demanding, to weigh on purpose, to put your needs above those of others. It comes to tell apart true consideration from self-erasure, the not asking because you don't need from the swallowing of what you need out of fear. And it comes to give you back the most basic right: that of having needs, of showing them, of asking for them to be met, without that taking away your place or your love. Because your place never depended on making yourself invisible. You deserve to be loved whole, with everything you need.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In sinking in silence, without telling anyone, while you smile on the outside and assure everyone that everything's fine, even when nothing is.

In feeling an enormous discomfort, almost physical, just at the thought of asking a favor, of bothering someone, of giving trouble, however small.

In minimizing your needs, your problems, your pains, as if you had no right to let them take up space, and in saying it's nothing whenever someone notices that something is hard for you.

In feeling, even when loved, the sense that you'd be left if, one day, you weighed too much, needed too much, gave too much trouble.

In apologizing, time and again, for things that are no one's fault, as if your simple presence, when it requires something, were an imposition to be apologized for.

In neglecting your health, your rest, your most basic needs, because you think attending to yourself would be giving trouble even to yourself.

They are all the same. The self-erasure. The person who is never any trouble, not even to herself.

See with me how this self-erasure works its way into the most ordinary hours of your day, in gestures you may already mistake for your temperament. You're ill, and instead of saying so, you drag yourself through everything anyway, so as not to worry anyone. You need something, and instead of asking for it, you wait for them to guess, and when they don't guess, you tell yourself it wasn't important. Someone asks how you are, and you answer fine, always, even when you're falling apart inside, because telling the truth would be giving trouble. You receive a request, and you accept, even when you're drowning, because refusing seems to weigh more than accepting.

And there's a detail that reveals everything: the way you feel when, by chance, you take up space without meaning to. When someone has to wait for you, to step aside for you, to do something for you, a shame rises in you, an apology, a wish to shrink yourself again, to make up for the inconvenience. As if your simple presence, when it requires something of others, were an imposition you have to minimize. Apologizing for existing has become almost a reflex, and you don't even notice how many times a day you apologize for things that are no one's fault.

And there's something no one sees, beneath your easiness. People find you impeccable, easy, without problems, and they grow used to your never needing anything, and they even value you for it, without imagining what you're hiding. They don't see the swallowed needs, the sinking in silence, the hunger for care that you never ask for. They see the one who is no trouble, not the person who erases herself at an enormous cost. And you, valued for being easy, become even more trapped in the self-erasure, because now not weighing is also being loved, and appearing seems to risk the little place you have.

## 2. More lovable when I disappear

Go back. To the beginning. To when you learned to disappear.

Almost always, it was very early. Before memory, perhaps. In a time when you understood, without words, that you were more loved, or at least more tolerated, when you didn't weigh.

Perhaps you grew up in a house where there was little to give. Tired parents, overloaded, with no room for one more need. And you, a child, understood that the way not to be one more weight was not to weigh. The way to be loved was not to give trouble. The way to have a place was to take up little.

Perhaps there was a sibling who gave a lot of trouble, who took up all the attention, and you became the opposite, the one who gave no trouble at all, the easy one, the good one, to compensate, to relieve, to be the one your parents could, at least, not have to manage.

Perhaps you felt, somehow, that your needs were too much. That asking brought a bad reaction, a sigh, an irritation, a coldness. And you learned not to ask, not to need, to erase your needs before they bothered.

Or perhaps you were the easy child beside fragile adults, who couldn't take any more. A mother on the verge of breaking, a father who could barely hold himself together, a house where one more need could be the drop that made it overflow. And you, sensitive, understood that you had to hold yourself in so as not to sink those who were already at their limit. You became the one who didn't weigh, not because you didn't need, but to protect those who had no way to bear you. A child taking care of the adults, swallowing herself so as not to overload them, and learning, in this way, that her role was to spare others her own existence.

See what gets engraved in a child, in any of these houses where needing was too much. That her need is a problem. That asking is a risk. That the way to have some place, some affection, is not to weigh, not to need, not to exist too much. And that lesson, engraved early, becomes her way of being in the world, making her erase herself everywhere, her whole life, to secure a place she learned to think she only had as long as she gave no trouble.

And in a house like this a child memorizes a sum by heart, without ever hearing it spoken out loud. A terrible equation. I'm more lovable when I disappear. The less I weigh, the more they want me. The less I need, the more place I have. My existence, with its needs, is a nuisance, and the way to be loved is to reduce that nuisance to the minimum, to almost not be there.

And that child erases herself. She becomes the one who gives no trouble. She swallows her needs, hides her pains, takes up the minimum of space, to secure her place. And that, at the time, works. It guarantees her tolerance, the affection that's possible, a place in a house where one more need would be too much.

But the equation doesn't undo itself when she grows up. It stays. It becomes her way of loving and of being loved. And today, as an adult, you keep erasing yourself, not asking, not weighing, disappearing, because you learned that this is how one is loved, and you're afraid that, if you appeared, if you asked, if you weighed, they would stop wanting you.

And I want you to see how this equation chooses, without your realizing it, relationships that confirm it. Because a person who offers herself as the one who gives no trouble, who never asks, who makes do with little, often attracts whoever needs to receive without giving, whoever settles into being cared for without caring. And so you repeat, as an adult, the dynamic of childhood: you go back to being the one who gives and doesn't receive, the one who erases herself so the other can shine, the one who serves without being served. And then you feel the emptiness, the loneliness, the tiredness, without seeing that your own way of offering yourself attracts whoever treats you as you treat yourself. Healing this wound changes this too: as you begin to need, to ask, to tell, you change what you accept, and you become able to be in relationships where you receive as much as you give.

Look at this without guilt, and without having to make a defendant out of whoever raised you. Whoever had no room for your needs was, very often, themselves without room, sunk, overloaded. They didn't mean to teach you to disappear. But they taught you, without meaning to, by having no way to take in the whole child you were, with everything she needed. And seeing this isn't holding on to resentment. It's, finally, understanding why you learned not to exist.

And it matters that you see in which layer of you this lodged itself. The belief that you're more lovable when you disappear isn't an idea you have, it's a way of feeling engraved deep, in a time before words. So today you don't decide to erase yourself, you erase yourself before thinking, automatically, you swallow your need before even feeling it fully. And that is why it isn't enough to understand that you have the right to need, in order to be able to do it. The belief lives below reason, in a very old place, and it undoes itself slowly, with the repeated experience of asking and not being set aside, and often with help.

There's one more thing this self-erasure left you, and that you may recognize: you lost contact with what you feel. From swallowing your needs for so long, from saying everything's fine for so long, from sorting everything out inside without showing it, you drifted away from your own emotions, until you no longer quite know what you feel. They ask how you are, and the automatic answer is fine, not because you are, but because you lost access to the truth. Relearning to feel yourself, to know what's going on with you, is part of this crossing, because you can't ask for what you need without, first, knowing what you need.

## 3. The needs I hide

There's something you need to see, and that may be hard for you, because you've hidden it so well that you can barely see it anymore. You have needs. Many. Like everyone. Only you learned to hide them, even from yourself.

Notice. Everyone needs. Attention, care, help, to be seen, to be supported, to rest, to receive. That isn't weakness, it's humanity. No one is enough on their own, no one lives without needing others. The strongest, the most capable, the ones you most admire, they all need, they all ask, they all let themselves, now and then, be supported. Needing isn't the opposite of being strong, it's part of being human, and the only person you deny that basic humanity to is yourself.

But you, from erasing yourself for so long, lost contact with your own needs. You no longer quite know what you need, because you grew used to not needing, to swallowing, to sorting things out alone, to giving no trouble. Your needs didn't disappear. They stayed hidden, swallowed, in a place in you that you learned not to look at.

And notice how this became so deep that it affects your own choices. When they ask you what you want to eat, where you want to go, what you prefer, you hesitate, you say whatever, you let others decide, not because you're indifferent, but because you lost access to your own wanting. From putting others' preferences ahead of yours for so long, from not wanting to give trouble with your own wishes for so long, you stopped knowing what you want. And there's a sadness in this: that of a person who serves everyone's desire and no longer knows what hers is, who erased herself so well that she erased herself even from herself.

And there's an enormous cost in this. Because a need that's swallowed doesn't cease to exist. It stays there, unmet, growing, weighing. You need, but you don't ask, and so you don't receive, and the need stays, a hunger to be fed, an emptiness to be filled. And it accumulates, year after year, into a muffled weight of someone who never received what, in silence, she needed.

Notice the particular loneliness of this. You're always available for others' needs, and you never ask anything for yours. You're everyone's shoulder, and you have no shoulder of your own. And when it's you who needs, there's no one to turn to, because no one knows that you need, because you never say, because you erased yourself so well that everyone sees you as the one who needs nothing.

And there's a painful irony in this. You, who would be there for anyone who needed, who would never find a burden whoever asked you for help, who take care of everyone without hesitating, are precisely the one who refuses herself what she offers others without thinking. You find it natural for others to need, and you support them without judging them. But to yourself, that same right is denied. To you, needing is weighing. You deny yourself the care you'd give to anyone, and you apply to yourself a harshness you'd never apply to those you love. You deserve from yourself the same generosity you give others, and that is, perhaps, the first thing this crossing comes to teach you: to take care of yourself as you would take care of someone you love.

And there's something even more painful. By never asking, by never needing, you deprive the people who love you of the possibility of taking care of you. They would like, perhaps, to give to you, to help you, to support you. But you don't let them. You erase yourself, you sort everything out alone, you give no trouble, and so no one can love you back, because you give them no way in. Your self-erasure, which seems like consideration, is also a door closed to the love they would want to give you.

And there's a cost in the body, too, which pays in silence what you swallow. Because a person who never asks, who never rests, who never attends to herself, who always postpones her own needs, wears away without replenishing. You neglect your health, your rest, your care, because attending to yourself seems to you like giving trouble to yourself, and you don't even allow yourself that. And the body collects, sooner or later, the bill for a whole life of attending to everyone's needs except your own. Many people who are never any trouble fall ill in silence, because their self-erasure extended to the point of not even caring for themselves.

I want you to feel the weight of this. Imagine living your whole life swallowing what you need. Never asking. Never receiving, because you never ask. Accumulating unspoken needs until they become a deep emptiness. It's a place of immense loneliness, disguised as giving no trouble, and perhaps you already take it for normal, so used are you to disappearing.

And there's a particular sadness in realizing that, from not weighing for so long, you became, in a way, invisible. People grew used to your needing nothing, and so they offer you nothing, they don't notice you, they don't attend to you. Not out of malice, but because you taught the world to see you as the one who needs nothing. And then, in the bad moments, you feel a loneliness you don't understand, without seeing that it was you, by erasing yourself so well, who became the person no one thinks to take care of, because no one imagines that you, too, need.

## 4. Who am I if I need

I'm going to leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you, if you need? If you ask? If you take up space, and give, for once, trouble?

Stay with it. Notice the fear it brings.

For you, this question touches a deep terror. Because your whole identity was built on not needing. You're the easy one, the one who gives no trouble, the one who doesn't weigh. Take that away from you, imagine yourself needing, asking, weighing, and a panic appears. Will they stop wanting me, then? If I give trouble, do they still have a place for me?

And the shadow that hides here is the fear of being a burden. The belief, deep and old, that your needs are a nuisance, that needing makes you heavy, and that a heavy person is, sooner or later, set aside. That's why you erase yourself. Not because you don't need, but because you're afraid that needing will cost you love.

Notice how this imprisons you. You can't ask, even when you're sinking, because asking seems to risk your place. You can't need, even when you need desperately, because needing seems to turn you into a burden. You live smothering your needs, sorting everything out alone, even when it costs you dearly, because the fear of weighing is greater than the need itself.

And there's an enormous fragility in this. Because a person who can't ask is alone in the face of everything. In the face of illness, of tiredness, of despair, of pain. She's always alone, because she doesn't ask, doesn't receive, doesn't let herself be supported. And there are moments in life when no one should be alone, when needing others isn't weakness, it's survival. And you, in those moments, erase yourself even more, you suffer alone in silence, because asking seems to you a luxury you have no right to.

There's something you need to know about the terror this question opens beneath your feet. What terrifies you isn't, truly, needing. It's the rejection you learned that needing brought. To your body, asking and being set aside are one and the same thing, fused in a time when your need was, in fact, received as weight. But they're separable. Needing, in itself, doesn't make you a burden, it's only human. The rejection belonged to that house with no room, to that time, to those people who had no way to take you in. Today, with whoever is trustworthy, needing and being rejected can, at last, stop going together.

Notice, too, the injustice this belief does you, and that you see at once if you apply it to others. You take care of everyone's needs, without ever finding them a burden. When someone you care about needs you, you don't think they weigh, you feel trusted, close, useful. Now, if that's how you feel when others need you, why would it be different when it's you who needs? You apply to yourself a cruel rule that you'd never apply to anyone else: for others, needing is human; for you, needing is weighing. And that double rule isn't justice, it's your wound, which treats you with a harshness you'd never use with those you love.

Let me touch this fear from the inside, because that's how it begins to lose its force. Needing doesn't make you a burden. This is the lie that erases you, and it's time to call it into question. The burden you fear being isn't a fact about what needing is, it's an old sentence that was read to you as a child and that you keep signing every day. Notice that when you carry the need of someone you love, you don't feel crushed by a weight, you feel close. Only with you, and only with yours, does the sum give a different result, and it's that crooked sum we're going to set straight.

And I want you to see the cruelty of this belief, which you may not notice. What you learned in your house, that needing is weighing, you keep doing to yourself, today, without anyone needing to do it. It's you, now, who treats yourself as a burden, who swallows your needs, who denies yourself care, who tells you, inside, that you have no right to weigh. The voice that, in childhood, taught you to disappear, became your own voice, and you repeat it against yourself, without respite. To stop erasing yourself is, deep down, to stop continuing, against yourself, what others began: it's to stop being the one who treats you as the nuisance you learned to think you were.

Don't answer the question of who you are if you need just yet. But leave it open. Because that's where the door is. The day you discover that needing doesn't make you less lovable, but more human, more close, more real, you stop having to erase yourself in order to be loved. And then, only then, can you begin to appear.

## 5. Needing isn't bothering

I need to lower my voice here, and set everything else down for a moment. Because there's a point at which this self-erasure stops being a trait and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding.

If you've erased yourself so well that you no longer know what you feel, what you need, what you want. If you sink in silence, without telling anyone, and you carry alone weights that no one should carry alone. If there's a deep sadness you hide from everyone, behind the smile of someone who gives no trouble. If you neglect your health, your wellbeing, your most basic needs, because you think you have no right to attend to yourself.

This is no longer just a habit of making yourself small. It may be a self-neglect, a sadness that has thickened, a wound of self-esteem that asks for real help, from a professional.

And I say this to you with all the firmness and all the tenderness that fit in a single sentence. You, who are never any trouble, are precisely the person who asks for help latest, or who never asks for it. Because asking for help is giving trouble, is weighing, is admitting that you need, and that goes against everything you learned. And that is why your suffering can pass unnoticed by everyone, including you, until you sink in silence, without anyone knowing.

So I want to ask you, with all clarity. If you recognize yourself in this, don't wait until you sink further. Seek help. A professional, someone you trust. Asking for help isn't giving trouble. It's the most legitimate and most lucid thing you can do for yourself. And your pain deserves to be heard, exactly as you would hear that of anyone you love, even if she, like you, thought she shouldn't bother.

And I want you to hear this with all seriousness: self-erasure, taken to the extreme, can thicken into a deep sadness, into an emptiness, and even into very dark thoughts, of someone who feels the world would be better without her weight. If you ever reached, or reach, that point, if you feel it isn't worth it, that you'd be one less burden if you disappeared for good, please, don't stay alone with that. Talk to someone, seek help immediately, a doctor, a support line, someone you trust. Your life matters, and your weight, which you learned to think excessive, is precious to those who love you more than you imagine. You aren't a burden to be eliminated. You're a person to be cared for, and there are those who know how to help you feel it.

Understanding where your self-erasure comes from is precious. But it doesn't replace the care you deserve. And you, more than anyone, need to learn that your need counts too, beginning with the need to be, at last, cared for.

See with me why, in a wound like yours, the helping relationship is itself already half the remedy. Because what erased you was a relationship in which your need had no room, in which needing was weighing. And what was wounded in a relationship heals, very often, in another: in a helping relationship where, perhaps for the first time, your need is welcome, where needing is what's expected of you, where you can ask, show yourself, take up space, without bothering anyone. For someone who learned that she only had a place by not weighing, discovering a place where weighing is allowed, and even expected, can repair the wound in the very layer where it was made.

And I want to take away the fear that, more than anyone, will hold you back: that seeking help is giving trouble, taking up someone's time, weighing with your pain. Notice that this fear is the wound itself speaking, preventing you from taking care of yourself with the argument that taking care of yourself bothers. But asking for help isn't giving more trouble to anyone. It's letting yourself, at last, attend to your own care, and that is your right, as legitimate as anyone's. You, who never weighed, have more right than anyone to be, now, attended to.

## 6. Asking

If you learned not to need so as not to bother, then there's a verb waiting for you. Asking.

Asking is the opposite of erasing yourself. It's saying what you need. It's no longer sorting everything out alone and allowing others to give to you, to help you, to support you. It's taking up space, having needs, and giving, yes, some trouble, as everyone does.

And it will cost you immensely, because it goes against your deepest learning. Asking, for you, is risking everything. It's weighing. It's bothering. It's becoming the burden you always feared being. And the first time you ask, you'll feel your whole body resist, the guilt rising, the urge to withdraw the request, to say that you didn't really need it after all, to erase yourself again.

And I want you to know how to tell apart what you feel from what is. You'll feel that you're weighing, bothering, giving enormous trouble. But what you feel isn't what is. What you feel is the old belief firing, treating a small request as if it were an enormous imposition. What is, in reality, is just one person asking something normal of another, as happens a thousand times a day among people who care for each other. The distance between what you feel, an enormous weight, and what is, a banal request, is the measure of your wound. And learning to trust not what you feel, but what is, is part of freeing yourself: you ask, you feel that you weighed immensely, and you discover, from the other's reaction, that you weighed almost nothing.

But it's precisely there, in that asking endured despite the guilt, that your cure lies. Because each time you ask, and the person doesn't treat you as a burden, but helps you gladly, you learn a new truth. That needing doesn't make you heavy. That asking doesn't cost you love. That there are those who want, even, to be able to give to you, and that by asking you give them that joy.

And there's something I want to leave you thinking about, regarding the eyes that watch you, especially if you have children. Because self-erasure is passed on, from generation to generation. If they see you never ask, never need, erase yourself so as not to weigh, they learn that this is how one should be, and they risk becoming, too, the ones who erase themselves in order to be loved. The most liberating thing you can teach whoever sees you is not the example of the one who is never any trouble. It's that of a person who asks, who needs, who lets herself be cared for, and who is still loved. Sometimes a person learns to ask, at last, not only for herself, but to give whoever she loves permission to exist whole, with needs and all.

And I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more thing to do perfectly. It will be slow, and it will have setbacks. Days when you ask, when you appear, when you let yourself need, and days when you go back to erasing yourself, to swallowing everything, to giving no trouble, as always. And the old belief will use each setback to tell you you see, it's better not to weigh. Don't believe it. The setbacks are part of it. No one undoes at once a self-erasure of a whole life. It undoes itself little by little, and each time you ask and aren't set aside, a new piece of proof is laid, and it's from those proofs, added up, that the courage to exist, slowly, is built.

Begin with small things. A favor you'd do alone, but that you ask for. A help you'd do without, but that you accept. A small need, that you'd normally swallow, and that, for once, you say out loud. And watch what happens. Almost certainly, not the rejection you fear, but a response, a care, a new closeness.

And choose well who you ask, in this phase, because it matters. Don't begin with the person who always made you feel a weight, the one who might confirm your fear. Begin with whoever truly wishes you well, with whoever you can be almost certain will respond gladly. It isn't cheating. It's choosing, for your first request, the ground where it has the best chance of going well, instead of throwing it straight against the wall that best confirms your fear. There's time, afterward, for the rest. First, the body needs proof that asking can go well, that needing doesn't take away your place.

And prepare yourself to feel the fear anyway, even with the right person, even with a small request. The guilt will rise, the shame, the urge to withdraw the request, to say that you didn't really need it after all. That shiver doesn't mean you were wrong to ask. It's the old belief firing, treating this small request as if it were the imposition you learned to fear. Don't obey it. Leave the request made, let the help arrive, and give your body the chance to discover that, this time, needing didn't weigh, didn't drive anyone away, didn't cost you anything you feared.

And I want you to see, too, that learning to ask doesn't make you selfish, demanding, someone who overloads others. That's your fear, and it's the opposite of the truth. You're so used to not asking for anything that the fair balance, the normal asking everyone does, will seem to you, at first, an enormous excess. It isn't. It's only that your ruler is off, thrown out by self-erasure, calibrated to nothing. What you'll feel as asking too much will, almost always, be just asking enough, what anyone asks without thinking. There's no risk of your becoming demanding: you come from so far away, from the side of nothing, that the whole way is only up to what's fair.

I want you to see that asking is, also, a gift to whoever loves you. Because by asking, you give the other the place of the one who gives. You let them take care of you, be useful, give back the much that you give. And there's an intimacy that opens only this way, when you let yourself need, when you show that you too have needs, when you let yourself be seen not only strong, but human. Asking doesn't drive people away. It draws them closer, because it gives them, at last, a way to love you.

Think of how you feel when someone you care about asks you for something, confides a need to you, lets you take care of them. You don't feel heavy, you feel close, trusted, loved by their trust. Well, that's exactly what you deny whoever loves you, each time you erase yourself, don't ask, sort everything out alone. You deprive whoever wishes you well of the joy of taking care of you, of the closeness of being let in, of the trust of being chosen as the one to whom you show your need. Your self-erasure, which you judge to be consideration, is also a way of keeping at a distance whoever would want you close.

And I want you to see that receiving doesn't put you in debt, as you may fear. There is, in you, probably, the sense that if you ask, you're left owing, and that owing is one more weight. But in a healthy relationship there's no accounting: one gives and receives in a flow, without keeping the tally of who gave more. You, who give nonstop, have credit to spare in that flow, and receiving doesn't put you in debt, it only balances what was running all to one side. Let yourself receive without the guilt of debt, because love doesn't charge, and whoever loves you wants to give to you as much as you want to give, and is happy to, at last, be able to.

And it will appear, with each request, the old voice, the one that tells you you're weighing, bothering, becoming a burden. It was engraved early, in that house with no room, and it doesn't go quiet at the first order. Your task isn't to silence it, it's to stop doing what it commands. To hear it say don't ask, don't weigh, and to answer it, slowly, needing doesn't make me a burden, I have a right to be cared for. And each time you ask despite it, and aren't set aside, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, more tender, that knows your need counts, as much as anyone's.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Ask. In small things, first. Let yourself need, take up space, give some trouble. And discover, slowly, that the love you feared losing by weighing was, after all, a love that only wanted you present, and that your self-erasure, however well-intentioned, was a way of keeping it always at a distance.

## 7. Needing without bothering

There's a destination for this crossing. And it fits in the sentence that holds it. Needing without bothering.

Notice the two parts. Needing. And without bothering.

The first is the right to have needs, to ask, to take up space, like anyone, without having to earn it or justify it. The second dismantles the belief that erases you: the belief that needing is, necessarily, bothering.

Because needing isn't bothering. They're different things, which you fused into one. Bothering would be demanding, overloading, putting your needs above others' in an abusive way. Needing is only being human, having needs, and asking for them to be met, in a normal exchange among people who care for each other.

You confused the two because, in your childhood, your need was, in fact, treated as a nuisance. You asked, and it was seen as weight. You needed, and it was received as more trouble. And you learned that needing and bothering were the same thing. But they were the same thing there, in that place with no room, and they aren't the same thing everywhere, forever.

I want to show you what changes when you separate the two. You can need, and ask, knowing that you aren't necessarily bothering. You can have needs, and meet them, without feeling a burden. You can take up your space, the fair space, the one everyone takes up, without erasing yourself out of fear of weighing. Your need becomes legitimate, like anyone's, and you stop having to hide it like a shame.

And there's a reciprocity that opens. Because in a healthy relationship, both need, both ask, both give and receive. When you only give and never ask, the relationship is unequal, and you always stay on the outside of the care. When you begin to need, to ask, to receive, the relationship balances, becomes a true exchange, and you, at last, enter it, not as the one who gives no trouble, but as a whole person, with needs, who is cared for as she cares.

Notice, in fact, that by never asking you may be, without meaning to, creating an inequality that pushes people away. Because whoever only gives and never receives places the other in a permanent debt, or at a distance, because the other can never give back, can never take care in turn, can never enter your life through the door of the one who gives to you. True intimacy is made from two sides: giving and receiving. And by always refusing to receive, you keep, without knowing it, an asymmetry that prevents deep closeness. Asking isn't only your receiving, it's also giving the other the place of the one who takes care of you, and it's in that back-and-forth, and not in your one-sided giving, that intimacy grows.

And I want you to see that showing your needs makes you more real, and not less lovable. A person who never needs anything, who is always fine, who never weighs, is, deep down, hard to reach, to truly know, to love up close. Because what draws people closer isn't the perfection of the one who gives no trouble, it's the humanity of the one who shows herself whole, with strengths and fragilities, with what she gives and with what she needs. By showing your needs, you stop being the impeccable and unreachable one, and you become a real person, with whom one can have a true relationship. And it's that real person, and not the erased one, who can, in fact, be loved up close.

I want, before that, to tell you something about relearning to feel yourself. Because, in order to ask for what you need, you first have to know what you need, and that, from erasing yourself for so long, you may no longer know. Begin, then, with a simple question, asked of yourself, several times a day: what do I need, now? Not to answer it right away, perhaps you don't know at first, but to reopen the channel you closed. To notice the tiredness, the hunger, the wish, the hurt, what you feel like and what you don't feel like, is to relearn the language of your needs, forgotten from not using it for so long. And only by knowing what you need can you, afterward, begin to ask for it.

On the other side of this fear, there's a joy with your name on it, waiting for you. The joy of being cared for. Of asking, and receiving. Of needing, and not being alone. Of taking up your space, of telling, of existing with your needs and all. Of discovering that love didn't want you erased, it wanted you present, and that your place never depended on not weighing, it depended on being you, whole. That joy is your right, and it's on the other side of fear, on the day you need, at last, without erasing yourself.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It won't make you, tomorrow, ask for everything you swallowed your whole life. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your self-erasure with kindness, and your invisibility with giving no trouble.

Now you know that you're never any trouble, and that this isn't just good manners nor kindness, it's an old belief that you're more lovable when you disappear, and more loved when you don't weigh.

You know that you learned, very early, to erase yourself in order to have a place, in a house where your need was, or seemed, too much, and where not weighing was the way to be tolerated.

You know that you have needs, many, like everyone, and that you hid them so well that you lost contact with them, and even with what you feel and what you want.

You know who you are, or fear being, if you need, and that beneath it is the fear of being a burden, and the rejection you learned that needing brought.

You know that needing isn't bothering, that they're different things that your childhood fused.

You know that needing isn't bothering, and that this belief, engraved in childhood, is a lie that erases you, and not a truth about what needing is, nor about what you're worth.

And you know that the verb is asking, and that asking, far from driving away, draws people closer, because it gives others a way to love you, and makes you, at last, a real person, and not the impeccable and unreachable one who gives no trouble.

I want to leave you with an image, and I ask you to carry it with you, held against your chest.

Imagine someone who walks into a house full of people, and who leans against a wall, in the most discreet corner, asking for nothing, taking up no space, making herself almost invisible, afraid of bothering. And who stays there, the whole evening, hungry, thirsty, tired, without asking for a glass of water, without sitting down, without saying that she needs, so as not to give trouble. And everyone finds her impeccable, easy, and no one notices that she's hungry, because she never says.

The crossing is leaving the corner. It's asking for the glass of water. It's sitting down. It's saying that you need. And discovering that no one throws you out for it, that there's a place for you at the table, that your glass of water takes nothing from anyone, and that, after all, you could have asked, all this time, and been served, like anyone.

And I have one simple thing left to tell you, before I let go of your hand. You were never a burden. The child who learned to erase herself so as not to weigh did what she could to have a place in a house with no room, and that is worthy of tenderness, not of reproach. But her needs were never the nuisance she learned to think they were. They were only the needs of a child, legitimate, human, worthy of being met. And today, as an adult, you can give yourself what was then missing: you can let yourself need, ask, take up space, and discover, at last, that your place at the table never depended on not weighing, it was always yours, for being.

Because you deserve to be cared for, and not only to be the one who cares; to be attended to, and not only the one who attends; to exist whole, with your needs and all, without having to disappear in order to be loved.

Deep down, you already know the sentence this whole book leans on. I can need without bothering. My needs are legitimate, like anyone's, and I don't have to disappear in order to be loved.

Asking was the first step. But there's a second, and no page can give you that one. It's done by asking, slowly, in small things first. By letting yourself need, take up space, give some trouble. And there's a deeper work, with someone to accompany you, to relearn that your need counts, because a whole life of self-erasure rarely undoes itself alone.

And if there's a single thought you keep from all of this, let it be this. You don't have to disappear in order to be loved. That was the lesson of a time with no room, but it isn't a truth about love. True love doesn't want you erased, light, invisible, without needs. It wants you present, whole, real, with everything you need. And whoever only wants you as long as you don't weigh doesn't want you, they want your absence. You deserve to be loved in your presence, and not in your absence, and there are those who want you that way, on the day you let yourself, at last, appear.

And before you close the cover of this book, let me ask you one thing only. Difficult, for you, precisely because it's so small.

This week, ask for one thing. However small. A favor you'd do alone, a help you'd do without, a need you'd swallow. Ask for it, out loud, of someone who wishes you well. And notice the fear of bothering, the guilt of weighing, the urge to withdraw the request. And ask anyway. Because it's in that asking, endured despite the fear, that you begin to leave the corner, and that you discover, at last, that needing never made you a burden, and that there is, at the table, a place that was always yours, waiting for you to take it.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you're bothering, weighing, giving too much trouble, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you're only doing what everyone does, asking something normal of whoever wishes you well, and that this takes away neither your place nor your love, but rather draws you closer to whoever would want, at last, to be able to take care of you.

Because there is, at the table of life, a place that was always yours, and you deserve, at last, to take it, with your needs and all.

Because you were never the burden you learned to think you were, and you deserve, at last, to be cared for as you always cared for others.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
