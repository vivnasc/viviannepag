# The woman who inherited a life

**For the woman who lives out a script she did not write. Because not every dream you carry was born in you.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This book touches the relationship with your family and with what you received from it, a territory that can stir things deep. If, as you read, you feel old wounds open that are hard for you to bear, a sadness that does not pass, or an inner conflict that paralyzes you, please seek the accompaniment of a professional. Looking at our origin is delicate work, and it deserves, at times, hands to accompany us. It is not weakness to ask for help. It is care.*

## 1. Is this life really mine?

There is a question that perhaps appears to you at strange moments, and that frightens you a little each time it arises. It appeared, perhaps, in the middle of an ordinary day, or on a night when you could not sleep, or while looking at the life you have built, with everything in its place, everything right, everything as it should be. The question is simple and disturbing: is this life really mine?

It is not that something is wrong. On the contrary. You have, perhaps, a good life, a life many people would approve of, a life that fulfills everything that was expected of you. You followed the right path, you made the sensible choices, you became what you were supposed to become. And it is precisely that which, at certain moments, unsettles you: the feeling that you fulfilled a script, instead of having lived a life. That you advanced along rails that were already laid, toward a station that was already marked, without ever having asked yourself whether that was where you, in truth, wanted to go.

And I want you to know, before we go on, that recognizing this does not make you ungrateful nor disloyal, however much the fear whispers it. There is an enormous difference between not valuing what you received and realizing that part of what you live is not yours. You can value your family deeply, what they gave you, the sacrifices they made, and at the same time realize that, in the midst of all of that, you also inherited scripts that do not serve you. The two things coexist. Looking at what you inherited with lucidity does not erase the gratitude for the good you received. On the contrary: it is precisely because there is so much that is valuable in your inheritance that it is worth separating, carefully, the gold from the weight, so that you keep the gold and set down the weight, instead of carrying both forever, confused together, without ever being able to enjoy the first because of the second.

There is a difference between choosing a life and fulfilling one. To fulfill a life is to follow what is expected, what makes sense, what is right, what others would approve of, without ever stopping to ask whether it is what you want. And you, who are capable, dedicated, responsible, fulfilled it well, fulfilled it with merit, perhaps even with success. But somewhere, beneath the fulfilling, there is a feeling that does not let go of you: that this life, so well fulfilled, was not entirely chosen. That you wear a garment that fits but that you did not choose. That you live a life that is good, but that is perhaps not, deep down, yours.

And there is a particular loneliness in this feeling, that few understand. Because from the outside, your life looks enviable, right, well resolved, and when you try to tell someone that you feel it is not quite yours, either they do not understand you, or they think you are being ungrateful, or they tell you that you have everything you need to be happy and that you should be grateful. And so you go quiet, and you keep the unease, and you are left alone with a question that no one around you seems to take seriously, because no one sees what you feel inside, they only see the life well assembled on the outside. That loneliness, of carrying an unease that others do not validate because your life looks perfect, is one of the most silent there is. I want you to know, then, that I take it seriously, and that it deserves to be taken seriously, because living a life you do not feel is yours, however perfect it may seem, is one of the most subtle and most real forms of suffering there is.

I want to tell you, right from the start, that this feeling is not ingratitude, nor is it a whim, nor is it the unease of someone who does not know how to value what she has. It is something much more serious and more common: it is the perception, still confused, that part of the life you live may not have been born in you. That there are dreams you carry that you inherited, as you inherited the color of your eyes, without having chosen them. That there is a script written by others, by your family, by your lineage, by what was expected of a woman like you, that you took on as yours without ever having read it with the eyes of someone who can refuse.

And perhaps you recognize this feeling in concrete moments. It is the strangeness of, having reached what you were supposed to, feeling a hollowness instead of pride, as if the victory were someone else's. It is looking at your resume, at your house, at your family, at the life well assembled, and feeling, for instants, a stranger inside it, like someone visiting a very tidy house that is not theirs. It is noticing that you make the important decisions thinking, first, about what others will think, about what is expected, about what is right, and rarely about what you, in truth, want, to the point that you no longer even quite know what you would want if no one were watching. It is the strange envy you feel, sometimes, for people who seem to live smaller lives but more their own, more free, more chosen. These signs, that you may have dismissed, are clues that there is, in your life, more fulfilling than choosing.

And I did not come, in this book, to tell you that you should throw away your life, break with your family, or reject everything you received. That is not what this is about, and it would be, in fact, only another way of not choosing, now out of reaction instead of out of loyalty. What I came to do is to help you distinguish, within the life you live, what is yours from what you inherited. Not to reject the inherited, but to see it, to recognize it, and to be able, perhaps for the first time, to choose: what you want to keep because it is really yours, and what you can give back, with love, to the one who gave it to you, because it was never yours to carry. Because there is a way to honor your origin that does not oblige you to live its life. And to discover it is the path of this book.

## 2. Whose dream is this

To answer the question "is this life really mine?", we have to ask another, more precise, that comes from the way I learned to look at families, in my training in family constellation. The question is: whose dream is this?

Because there is something that happens in all families, and that is almost never seen, that is called invisible loyalty. We, without knowing it, are profoundly loyal to our family of origin, and that loyalty leads us to carry, to continue, to accomplish things that come from before, from those who came before us, without ever having decided to do so. We carry the unrealized dreams of our parents. We continue the ambitions of our lineage. We fulfill the expectations that were placed on us before we had a voice. And we do it out of love, out of belonging, out of a deep fidelity we did not choose, and that binds us to those who gave us life.

Notice how this may be in your life. Perhaps you pursue a success that, deep down, was your father's dream, that he could not realize, and that you, without knowing, are realizing for him. Perhaps you follow a profession, a path, a way of life, that was what your family expected, what made them proud, what confirmed that you had turned out well. Perhaps you carry the ambition of a mother who renounced her dreams for you, and that you feel you have to honor by becoming everything she could not be. Perhaps you live a life that is, in truth, the answer to a question that was not yours, the realization of a script written in a previous generation, that reached you already made and that you took on as destiny.

And there is a particular form of inheritance that deserves to be named, because it is one of the heaviest and most invisible: the inheritance of gender. For many women, the inherited script did not come only from their particular parents, it came from generations of women and from what was expected of them. The idea of what a successful woman is, or a woman of worth, or a respectable one, was transmitted to you by a long line, and you may be fulfilling not only your father's or your mother's dream, but the script of a whole culture about what a woman should be, want, achieve, look like. That script is so omnipresent that it becomes almost impossible to see, because it is everywhere, it is the water you swim in. And distinguishing, within it, what is really yours from what is a mold of gender you took on without choosing, is one of the deepest parts of this crossing, and one of the most liberating, because it gives you back the possibility of defining, yourself, what you want to be, instead of fulfilling a definition that was given to you before you had a voice.

And the most subtle thing of all is that these inherited dreams do not feel inherited. They feel like yours. You really do want that success, that life, that path, and so it never occurs to you to question where that wanting came from. But there is a way to notice it: the inherited wanting has a quality of obligation, of weight, of "I have to," even when disguised as desire. And, above all, it does not satisfy. Because when you realize a dream that is yours, there is a satisfaction that comes from the alignment between what you did and what you are. When you realize an inherited dream, there is, on arriving, a strange hollowness, an "I made it, but why do I not feel anything?", because you fulfilled someone else's dream, and yours, the true one, remains unlived.

There are concrete ways in which these scripts are transmitted, and recognizing them helps to see them. They are transmitted by phrases said a thousand times: "in our family we are all like this," "what is expected of you is this," "after all we have done for you." They are transmitted through pride and disappointment: you noticed, as a child, what made your parents' eyes shine, and what saddened them, and you learned to orient your life toward the shine and away from the sadness, without ever deciding it. They are transmitted through silences: the dream a father did not realize and never spoke of, but that hovered in the house, and that you, without knowing, went and gathered and realized for him. They are transmitted even through loyalty to pain: if someone in your lineage suffered a lack, a loss, an injustice, you may feel, without knowing it, that you have to repair it with your life, to compensate with your success for what was missing for those who came before. In all these cases, the script entered you so early and so deep that it became invisible, indistinguishable from your own wanting.

I want you to see this without alarm, and without hurry to conclude. I am not saying that everything in your life is inherited, nor that you have to discover now what is yours and what is not. I am opening a question, only: whose dream is this? And inviting you to look, with curiosity and without judgment, at your life, and to notice where there is alignment and where there is obligation, where there is true desire and where there is an "I have to" that you do not quite know where it comes from. Because seeing whose dream it is is the first step to being able, one day, to choose your own.

## 3. To honor is not to repeat

When a woman begins to realize that part of her life was inherited, a fear almost always arises, and it is important to look at it early, because it can stop everything. The fear is this: if I recognize that this life is not mine, if I choose something else, I am betraying my family, rejecting those who gave me everything, dishonoring my origin. And that fear is so strong that many women prefer to keep living a life that is not theirs rather than risk the terrible guilt of seeming ungrateful, disloyal, traitors.

So I want to tell you, clearly, something that frees: to honor is not to repeat. There is a deep confusion between these two things, and undoing it changes everything. To honor your origin, your family, those who came before you, is to recognize what you received, to thank, to respect, to carry with you the good they gave you. To repeat is to live their life, to fulfill their dreams, to become a copy of what they were or of what they wanted to be. And they are completely different things. You can profoundly honor your origin and, at the same time, live a life that is yours, different from theirs. In fact, it is that, and not repetition, that honors them most.

And there is an important subtlety: sometimes what we inherit is not a dream to realize, it is a fear to obey. A family that went through hardships can transmit, without words, a "do not risk," "stay safe," "do not dream high, for you fall hard from high up," and you may have built a small and safe life not because you wanted it, but out of loyalty to a fear that is not yours, that comes from what your family lived. Just as there are those who inherit a family's ambition, there are those who inherit a family's restraint, and both are scripts, both may not be yours. That is why the question "whose dream is this" has a sister: "whose fear is this?" And it is worth asking both, because you may be living either a large life that is not yours, or a small life that is not yours, and in both cases the freedom lies in distinguishing what you inherited from what you would choose, and in being able, at last, to decide the size of your life from yourself, and not from the dream nor the fear of those who came before.

Think about this: what is it that a father, a mother, who truly love you, most want for you? They want you to be happy, to live a full life, to flourish in your own way. They did not give you life so that you would spend it realizing their dreams, they gave it to you so that you would live yours. When you fulfill a script that is not yours out of loyalty to them, you are, without meaning to, wasting precisely what they wanted to give you: a life of your own. And when you live your life, even if it is different from theirs, even if it surprises them, you are realizing the deepest wish of those who love you: that the life they gave you bear fruit, your fruit, and not a repetition of theirs.

And I want to show you a way of honoring that you may never have considered, and that comes from the systemic reading. The best way to honor those who came before you is to take what they gave you and make it grow, take it farther, give it your fruit, and not simply repeat it. Your parents, your grandparents, gave you a base, made sacrifices, opened paths, precisely so that you could go where they did not go, be what they could not be. When you repeat their life, instead of making it grow in your direction, it is as if their sacrifice had stopped in you, had not borne fruit. When you live your own life, full, chosen, you take what they gave you farther than they themselves could, and it is that, that carrying forward, and not the repetition, that truly honors what you received. The life that continues, that grows, that bears new fruit, is the greatest tribute you can make to those who gave it to you.

There are, it is true, families where loyalty is demanded rigidly, where following one's own path is experienced as betrayal, where children are expected to continue what their parents began. And if you come from a family like that, the fear of betraying is even greater, because there is, in fact, a real pressure. But even there, the truth remains: living a life that is not yours to please is not love, it is submission, and submission, however much it looks like loyalty, breeds resentment, and resentment poisons the relationship it meant to protect. To honor seriously, even in a demanding family, is to have the courage to live your truth, and to continue to love your origin from that truth, instead of erasing yourself to fit its mold.

And I want you to understand that choosing your life does not annul what you received. What your family gave you that was good, the values, the love, the base, the capacities, stays with you, it is yours, you took it and it is your true inheritance. To give back what is not yours, the borrowed dreams, the expectations you did not choose, is not to give back the love nor the good you received. It is to give back only the weight that was not yours to carry, keeping everything that actually belongs to you. To honor and to follow your path do not exclude each other. On the contrary: only those who follow their path truly honor the life they received, because only that way do they make it bear the fruit for which it was given.

## 4. Who am I without this story

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you to stay with it, even if it opens an emptiness, because it is precisely in that emptiness that your freedom dwells.

Who am I without this story?

Stay with it a moment.

Because there is a moment, in this crossing, that is at the same time the most frightening and the most liberating: the moment when you begin to separate yourself from the inherited dreams, from the expectations, from the script, and you are left, for an instant, not knowing who you are. If you took away the life you fulfilled, and it was not entirely yours, then what remains? Who are you, when you are not the realization of your parents' dreams, when you are not the answer to your family's expectations, when you are not the script you were given? Beneath everything you inherited, there is a person you may never have known well, because there was never space for her to appear, always busy as she was fulfilling what she did not choose.

And that emptiness, of not knowing who one is without the inherited story, frightens. It frightens because we are used to having a ready-made identity, given, and the idea of being left without it, even if it is a borrowed identity, feels like being left without ground. It is more comfortable to live a life that is not ours but that we know what it is, than to face the emptiness of not knowing what ours is. That is why so many women retreat at this point, and put the inherited script back on, because the emptiness of freedom seems to them worse than the known prison. But the emptiness, here, is not absence, it is space. It is the space where your true life, the one that never had a place, can finally begin to appear.

And I want to tell you why this emptiness appears, so that you do not read it as a failure. Throughout your whole life, your identity was given to you from outside: you were the daughter who measured up, the one who fulfilled, the one who accomplished what was expected, and that identity, borrowed but solid, told you who you were at each moment. As you begin to give back the inherited scripts, you are left, naturally, without that ready-made identity, and what remains, at first, seems an emptiness, because your own identity, the true one, never had space to form, always busy as it was fulfilling the borrowed one. But that emptiness is not an absence of you, it is an absence of the mold, and it is precisely in the space the mold leaves, in falling away, that you, the true one, can begin to appear. Whoever never gave back the script never knows this emptiness, and so never finds herself: she lives a whole life inside a mold, comfortable and alien. You, in feeling the emptiness, are paying the price, frightening but necessary, of being able, at last, to find yourself.

I want you to see this not knowing not as a problem, but as an opening. Because an inherited life comes with all the answers already given: you know what you want, what you are going to do, who you are, because all of that was already written for you. An own life begins, on the contrary, with questions, with a not knowing, with an open space. Not knowing who you are without the inherited story is not proof that you got lost. It is proof that you are, finally, free to find yourself, instead of continuing to be an answer to questions that were not yours. Freedom has, at first, the shape of an emptiness, because freedom is, precisely, space to be filled, and that space, which frightens, is the most valuable gift this crossing can give you.

I have to stop here and tell you, carefully, that this emptiness must be an opening, not an abyss, and that there is a difference. If not knowing who you are becomes an anguish that does not pass, a loss of ground that paralyzes you, a deep sadness that settles in, then that is no longer the healthy opening I speak of, and it asks for accompaniment. Looking at one's own origin and separating from inherited scripts is, at times, work that stirs too much to be done alone, and seeking the help of a professional, in that case, is not giving up, it is giving yourself the conditions to make this crossing safely. You deserve to cross this emptiness supported, if it becomes too great.

## 5. To give back without betraying

We have arrived at the central gesture of this crossing, and it is a delicate gesture, that comes from the heart of the systemic reading: to give back. To give back what is not yours, the inherited dreams, the borrowed ambitions, the expectations you did not choose, to those they belong to. And to do it without betraying, that is, without rejecting, without resentment, without cutting the bond, but with love and with respect.

To give back is not an outer gesture, it is not confronting your family, it is not announcing to anyone that you reject what they gave you. It is an inner, symbolic movement, that happens inside you. It is recognizing, on the inside, that that dream you carried was, in truth, your father's, and being able to tell him, internally, with love: this dream is yours, I see it, I respect it, but it is not mine to realize, and I give it back to you with tenderness. It is recognizing that that expectation you fulfilled was your mother's, your family's, and being able, on the inside, to set it down, to leave it with those who created it, instead of continuing to carry it as if it were yours. To give back is to take from your shoulders a weight that belonged to another, leaving intact the love that binds you to that other.

And I want to distinguish giving back from cutting off, because it is a confusion that frightens many people. To give back is not to cut off the relationship, it is not to stop speaking with your family, it is not to distance yourself from those you love. It is an inner gesture that, most of the time, does not even need to be said to anyone. You can give back, on the inside, the inherited dream to your father, and continue to have lunch with him on Sundays, to love him, to be part of the family, exactly as before, only now without carrying the weight that was his. The relationship on the outside may not change at all; what changes is what you carry on the inside. That is why giving back is not an act of rupture, it is an act of relief, and a silent one. You do not have to announce to your family that you gave back the scripts, you do not have to confront them, you do not have to explain. You give back inside you, you set down the weight inside you, and you follow your life, lighter, continuing to love your family exactly as you always loved them, perhaps even more, because now without the resentment that the weight brought.

And it is important to understand that giving back does not hurt those you love, contrary to what the fear says. When you give back, on the inside, an inherited dream to your father, you are not rejecting him, you are only giving back to him what was his, freeing yourself to live what is yours. Your father loses nothing by it, his dream continues to be his, and you gain your life. On the contrary, it is carrying his dream forever that prevents you from living yours, and that, yes, is a sad way of dishonoring him, because it turns the gift of the life he gave you into a repetition of his life. To give back with love is what allows each one to keep what is theirs: him with his dream, you with your life.

And I want to give you an image of what it is to give back, because it may seem abstract. Imagine that you carry, for years, a heavy suitcase that is not yours, that was handed to you by someone you love, perhaps without even being asked, and that you took up out of love and went on carrying through life, convinced that it was yours. To give back is to stop, to set down the suitcase, to look at it, to recognize "this suitcase is not mine, it is my father's, it is my mother's, it is my lineage's," and to hand it back, carefully, with love, to those it belongs to. It is not to throw it on the ground with rage, it is not to flee from it, it is to set it down with respect into the hands of those who created it. And on setting it down, you feel the enormous relief of shoulders that carried for too long a weight that was not theirs to carry, and you discover that, without the suitcase that is not yours, you finally have your hands free to take up what is yours. Whoever never sets down the suitcase that is not theirs never gets to take up their own, because they do not have free hands. To give back is to free the hands.

There is a sentence that holds the heart of this gesture, and that you can say on the inside, slowly, to the people of your origin: I can honor you without becoming you. Notice what it contains. To honor you: I recognize what you gave me, I respect you, I love you, I carry you with me. Without becoming you: but I do not have to live your life, to realize your dreams, to be your continuation, in order to honor you. I can be something else, my own thing, and continue to be your daughter, to love you, to belong. To honor and to differentiate myself do not exclude each other. And in this sentence, that recognizes and frees at the same time, lies the whole possibility of living your life without losing your origin.

And I want to add something about the generations, because it gives a greater meaning to this gesture. When you give back what is not yours and choose your life, you do not free only yourself. You interrupt a chain. Because inherited scripts pass from generation to generation: your parents carried those of your grandparents, you carried those of your parents, and, if nothing changes, your children will carry yours. Each woman who has the courage to distinguish what is hers, to give back what is not, and to choose her own life, interrupts that transmission, and leaves to those who come after an immense gift: the permission to be themselves, the example that one can honor the origin without repeating it. In choosing your life, you are not being selfish, you are doing work that benefits backward and forward: it frees you, it eases the resentment with those who came before, and it opens a path for those who come after. It is one of the most generous acts there is, even if the fear paints it as betrayal.

To give back without betraying is, deep down, an act of double love: love for those who gave you life, to whom you give back what was theirs with respect, and love for yourself, to whom you give back your own life, that was lent out to scripts you did not write. It is not a cutting off, it is a reorganization: each one in their place, with what is theirs, bound by love and no longer by the burden. And when this gesture happens, on the inside, slowly, a relief is felt that few things give, the relief of setting down a weight one carried without knowing one carried it, and of discovering that, beneath it, there was a life waiting to be, finally, lived.

## 6. Choosing for the first time

If you have given back what was not yours, then the possibility opens of the verb of this crossing, perhaps the most important of all: to choose. To choose, for the first time, your life. And I want you to feel the weight and the beauty of this word, because most people never truly choose their life, they only react to it or inherit it.

There are three ways to live a life, and only one is to choose. The first is to obey: to follow the inherited script, to fulfill the expectations, to do what is expected, out of loyalty, out of fear, out of habit. It is what you have been doing, in part. The second is to react: to do the opposite of what is expected, to reject everything that comes from the origin, to rebel, and this looks like freedom but it is not, because whoever reacts continues determined by the thing they react against, continues bound to the origin, now by negation instead of obedience. The third is to choose: and to choose is different from the two, because it is neither to obey nor to react, it is to decide from yourself, from what is truly yours, regardless of what the origin expected or its opposite.

And I want you to know that choosing for the first time, after a life of obeying or reacting, is a capacity that is trained, not a switch that is flipped. At first, you will hesitate, you will confuse what you want with what you were taught to want, you will choose and then doubt, you will perhaps fall back into the old script without noticing. This is normal, it is the learning of someone who never had to choose because everything came already decided. Do not demand of yourself perfect choices nor immediate certainties. Begin with the small: with the choices of daily life, where you can ask yourself "do I want this, or is it what is expected?", and keep practicing, in low-risk things, the muscle of deciding from yourself. Each small choice of yours, even in a tiny thing, strengthens that muscle, and prepares you for the larger choices. The freedom to choose your own life does not arrive all at once, it is built decision by decision, and the first steps, however trembling, are the most important, because they are the first you take from yourself.

Notice how rare and how difficult it is to truly choose. To choose requires that you know what is yours, and for that you had to give back what was not. It requires that you bear the emptiness of not knowing, instead of filling it in a hurry with the inherited script or with rebellion. It requires that you decide without the compass of others, from an inner compass that you may still be learning to read. And it requires courage, because a choice that is truly yours may not please your origin, may surprise it, may even disappoint it, and you have to make it all the same, because it is yours. To choose for the first time is, therefore, an act of birth: the moment when you stop being the continuation of others and become, at last, yourself.

And there is a sign that helps you know whether you are truly choosing, or merely obeying or reacting. The true choice has a quality of peace, even when it is difficult: it comes accompanied by a "yes, this is it," by an inner alignment, by a feeling that, whatever it costs, you are being faithful to yourself. Obedience has another quality: it comes with a weight, an "I have to," a resignation, and underneath, often, a resentment. And reaction has yet another: it comes with rage, with an "I'll show them," with the energy of someone who opposes, and who, by opposing, continues bound to the thing they oppose. Learn to feel the difference in the body: the true choice settles, calms, aligns; obedience weighs; reaction agitates. With time, you will learn to recognize the particular peace of the true choice, and to use it as a compass, choosing what brings you that serene alignment, and not what brings you the weight of obedience nor the agitation of revolt.

And I want you to understand the meaning of this, that goes far beyond a simple practical decision. To choose for the first time is to reclaim your own existence. It is to tell the universe, and yourself, that you are here not to repeat nor to react, but to live a life that is yours, unique, unrepeatable, that no one lived before you nor will live after. It is to honor the gift of life in the highest possible way: by living it for real, and not by fulfilling it by proxy. There is, in this, an enormous dignity, and a meaning that no obedience and no rebellion can give, because only those who choose their life truly live it, and only a life truly lived realizes that for which life was given to you.

And I want to warn you of a common confusion, because it makes many women stumble here. To choose for the first time does not mean turning your life inside out, throwing away everything you have, changing your profession, your city, your relationships, in a dramatic gesture of rupture. Sometimes the true choice leads to large changes, yes, but often it does not: often it leads you to keep exactly the life you have, but to live it in another way, now because you chose it and not because you inherited it. The same life, lived by choice instead of by obligation, is a completely different life on the inside, even if it is the same on the outside. What changes in the choice is not necessarily the what, it is the why: you stop doing things out of loyalty, out of fear, out of duty, and you begin to do them, or to leave them, out of truth. So do not frighten yourself thinking that to choose requires destroying what you built. Sometimes it requires only embracing it again, now as yours.

I am not going to tell you what to choose, of course, because that would be giving you one more script, and what this book wants is precisely the opposite: to give you back the power to choose for yourself. Nor am I going to tell you that choosing is easy or that it is done all at once, because it is a learning, and the first steps of someone who never chose are hesitant, like those of someone learning to walk. But I want to tell you that each small choice that is truly yours, however small, is a step out of the inherited life and into yours, and that, with time, those steps build a life that, when you look at it and ask "is this life really mine?", will be able to answer you, finally, yes.

## 7. To honor and to follow

If you have come this far, you have seen a lot: that you fulfilled a script you did not write, that part of your dreams were inherited, that to honor is not to repeat, that there is an emptiness to cross, that one can give back without betraying, and that to choose is different from obeying and from reacting. Now I want to show you the destination of this crossing, in broad strokes: to honor and to follow. To keep what is yours of your origin, and to follow your own path. The two things, together, without contradiction.

And I want to tell you that to honor and to follow is not only the destination of this crossing, it is also what makes it safe, what keeps it from becoming a bitter rupture. There are those who, on discovering that they lived an inherited life, react with rage, cut off everything, reject the origin as a whole, and set off toward a new life built against the family. But that rupture, however liberating it may seem in the moment, tends to leave a wound that does not heal, because to cut off the root is a form of self-mutilation, and whoever cuts off the origin carries, in truth, the wound of the origin everywhere. The path of honoring and following is different, and more mature: it does not cut off, it differentiates. It keeps the bond and the gratitude, and at the same time follows its own path. It is harder than cutting off, because it requires holding two things at the same time, the love for the origin and fidelity to oneself, but it is the only one that truly heals, because it does not trade a prison for a wound, it trades the prison for a freedom that does not cost the love.

Because the goal was never to choose between your origin and your life, as if you had to reject one in order to have the other. That is the false dilemma that traps so many women: either I am loyal to my family and I live the life I inherited, or I am myself and I betray my origin. It is false. The crossing leads to a third place, where you honor and you follow at the same time. Where you carry with you, with gratitude, everything your origin gave you that was good, and at the same time you live a life that is yours, chosen by you. Where you continue to be a daughter, a granddaughter, part of your lineage, and at the same time a whole person, with a path of your own. To honor and to follow do not compete. They complete each other.

And I want to speak to you about what changes in your relationship with your family when you make this crossing, because it is, perhaps, the most beautiful fruit of all. While you live an inherited life out of loyalty, there is, beneath the relationship with your family, a silent resentment, even if you never admit it, because a part of you knows that it gave up its own life for them, and that weighs, and sours. When you give back what was not yours and choose your life, that resentment dissolves, because you are no longer sacrificing yourself, there is no longer a debt, there is no longer an account to settle. And then you can love your family freely, for real, without the weight of a renunciation, without the silent bill of a life not lived because of them. Many women discover, with surprise, that it is after differentiating from the family that they manage, finally, to love it in peace, because the love that was trapped beneath the obligation is only freed when the obligation is set down. To differentiate yourself does not distance you from your family. It frees the love that the forced loyalty held hostage.

And there is a way of seeing this that can reconcile you with your whole story. Everything you received from your origin, even the dreams that were not yours, even the expectations that weighed on you, is part of who you are and of what brought you this far. It is not to reject, it is to integrate: to see clearly what is yours and what is not, to keep what is yours, to give back with love the rest, and to follow, carrying your origin with you not as a burden, but as a root. Because a root does not bind you, it sustains you. And your origin, seen this way, ceases to be the script you have to fulfill and becomes the ground from which you grow, in your own way, in your own direction, bearing your own fruit.

And I want to speak to you about a particular choice that this crossing often brings, and that is one of the most liberating: that of being able to keep contact with your family without having to agree with it, without having to live like it, without having to be approved by it. Many women believe there are only two options, to submit to the family in order to keep the relationship, or to break with the family in order to be free. But there is a third, more mature and more difficult: to remain bound and different at the same time. To continue to love, to visit, to be part, and at the same time to live your life, to have your ideas, to make your choices, even if the family neither understands nor approves them. This requires a particular inner strength, that of not needing their approval to feel good about your choices, and it is, perhaps, the highest form of maturity in a family relationship: to belong without submitting, to love without erasing yourself, to be different without cutting off. It is not easy, but it is possible, and it is where this crossing takes you.

Think of a tree. It has deep roots, that come from before it, that nourish it and sustain it, and that it honors simply by growing from them. But the tree does not repeat the roots, it does not become a root: it grows upward, into its own form, gives its own branches, its own leaves, its own fruit, unique, that no root determined. And that is how it honors the roots: not by copying them, but by growing from them to become fully what it is. You are that tree. Your origin is your roots, and to honor them is not to become them, it is to grow, from them, into your own form, and to bear the fruit that only you can bear.

I do not promise you that this crossing will be simple, because to distinguish what is yours from what you inherited, to give back with love, and to choose for the first time, is deep work, done slowly, and often with someone to accompany you, because it touches the oldest bonds you have. But I promise you that on the other side there is a life that is yours, and a peace that comes only from living your own truth, instead of fulfilling the truth of others. And I promise you that, contrary to what the fear says, that life of your own does not distance you from your origin. It reconciles you with it, because only when you stop carrying what is not yours can you love those who gave it to you without the weight of resentment, freely, as a root loves the tree that grew from it toward the sky.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not change everything at once, and it does not undo in a day bonds of a whole lifetime. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling trapped in a life that is good but that you do not feel is yours, and so you can begin to see that there is a path back to yourself.

And I want to leave you with a truth that gives meaning to the whole crossing. The life you received is a gift, and gifts are made to be used, not kept intact. When someone gives you a gift, the way to honor it is not to leave it on a shelf, exactly as they gave it to you, without touching it. It is to use it, to make it yours, to let it bear fruit in your life, in your own way. The life your family gave you is like that: they did not give it to you to keep it the same as theirs, they gave it to you to live it, and to live is to transform, is to choose, is to follow a path that is yours. To refuse to live your life in the name of loyalty is, deep down, to leave the gift of life unused, and there is no greater waste of a gift than that. To honor the gift of life is to live it, fully, in your own way. And it is that, and only that, that makes the gift you received, life, bear the fruit for which it was given.

Now you know how to recognize the difference between fulfilling a life and choosing it. You know how to ask the question that changes everything: whose dream is this? You know that to honor is not to repeat, and that you can love your origin without living its life. You know that there is an emptiness to cross, that of not knowing who you are without the inherited story, and that this emptiness is opening, not abyss. You know that one can give back without betraying, with love. And you know that to choose, for the first time, is different from obeying and from reacting, and is an act of birth.

And you know, perhaps, that the question "is this life really mine?", that unsettled you so much, was not a sign of ingratitude nor of madness, it was your own life calling for you. It was the part of you that never had space knocking at the door, saying "I exist, and I would like to be lived." Many women silence that question their whole life, afraid of what it might open, and die without ever having lived their own life, only fulfilled the life of others. You asked the question, you let it in, you read this book to the end. That is already the beginning of the answer, it is already your life beginning to claim its place. And that courage, of asking whose is the life one lives, is the first step, and the hardest, to making it, at last, yours.

And you know that distinguishing what is yours from what you inherited is not an act of coldness, it is an act of love, for you and for your family. For you, because it gives you back your own life. And for your family, because only when you stop carrying what was theirs can you love them freely, without the weight of a renunciation, without the silent account of a life not lived. The separation that looks like betrayal is, after all, what makes possible the cleaner love, the love that does not weigh, the love of someone who belongs to herself and therefore can, finally, give herself to others without losing herself.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can honor you without becoming you.

Seeing whose your life is was the first step, and it is not a small step. But there is a second, and that one is not read, it is done: it is done by giving back, slowly, with love, what is not yours, and by choosing, piece by piece, day by day, the life that is truly yours.
