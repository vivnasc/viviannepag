# The woman who turns value into work

**For the woman who distrusts everything that comes to her easily. Because you learned that value is paid for in sweat, and your value comes before your effort.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks about turns everything into effort, and there is a point where endless effort stops being dedication and becomes an exhaustion that the body and the mind collect on. If you recognize in yourself a tiredness that no longer lifts with rest, persistent anxiety, an inability to stop, or a loss of pleasure in everything, this may be more than tiredness, and it deserves the care of a professional. Seeking help is not giving up on being capable. It is the most lucid thing you can do for yourself.*

## 1. If it came easy, it doesn't count

There is a distrust you carry and that you have perhaps never named, because you take it for good sense: the distrust of everything that comes to you easily. When something reaches you without costing, without sweat, without struggle, instead of joy you feel a strangeness, almost a discomfort, as if there were something there that was wrong, suspicious, too good to be true or to be yours. What comes easily, for you, doesn't count, or doesn't count as much. And what cost a great deal, that one, you feel you deserved, that one you can accept, because you paid for it, because it hurt, because you sweated for it.

Notice how this governs you, how many silent decisions it makes for you. You receive a compliment for something that came to you naturally, and you play it down, you say it was nothing, because it didn't cost you. There is an opportunity that reaches you without your having to fight for it, and you distrust it, you look for the catch, or you complicate it unnecessarily, until it costs enough to seem legitimate to you. You earn money in a way that was light for you, and you feel almost guilty, as if it were stolen, as if only money that hurts to earn were honest. And when you have to choose between an easy path and a hard one to the same destination, a part of you, without knowing why, prefers the hard one, because the hard one, in your world, is what is dignified.

And there is a larger pattern beneath all this, which is the heart of this book: you turn value into work. You take everything that has value, everything that could be received, enjoyed, accepted with lightness, and you convert it into effort, into task, into struggle, into suffering. As if nothing could be simply good, simply yours, simply received. Everything has to pass through the sieve of effort to earn legitimacy. And so your whole life turns into work, because even what did not need to be work, you work it, you sweat it, you complicate it, until it has cost enough for you to feel entitled to it.

I want to make a distinction here right at the start, because it is important and few people make it. This is not, in your case, a matter of not feeling valuable as a person. There are women whose problem is not believing that they are worth anything, who need to constantly prove that they have value, and that is another book, another wound. Your wound is different, and more subtle: you may even feel that you are worth something, but you don't believe that you have permission to receive without suffering. For you, value exists, but it is only legitimate when paid for in pain. It is not the question "do I have value?", it is the question "do I have the right to receive without sweating for it?". And your answer, engraved deep, is no. What comes without pain does not fully belong to me.

I want you to know, right at the start, that this book does not come to tell you that dedication is bad, that effort has no value, that you should start wanting everything handed to you on a plate. Your work, your devotion, your capacity to dedicate yourself are real qualities, and I am not going to take them from you. I came to look at something else: at the invisible equation that tells you that only what hurts has value, and that has turned your life into a permanent test of effort, where nothing can simply be received, where even rest costs you, where even joy has to be earned with sacrifice before you give yourself permission to feel it.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read with a tight chest: your value does not come from your effort. It comes before it. You are worth something before you do anything, and not because of what you do nor of the pain it costs you. This idea, which seems simple, goes against the deepest lesson of your life, and so it will cost you to accept it. But it is the one that opens the door to something you never allowed yourself: to receive without paying in suffering, to prosper without sweating for it, to let life be, for once, light for you, without that making it less yours or less legitimate.

There is a way of feeling that you may never have connected to this, but that comes from the same place: the guilt of pleasure. When something gives you enjoyment without costing, when you enjoy something for free, when you receive a pleasure that was not earned with prior sacrifice, a shadow appears, a subtle guilt, a sense that you shouldn't, that you are being frivolous, that pleasure without effort is almost immoral. And so, without realizing it, you sabotage your own enjoyment, you cut it short, you interrupt it, or you pay for it afterward with double the work, like someone making up for the crime of having enjoyed. It is one of the cruelest forms of the equation, because it takes from you not only the lightness of receiving, but the very capacity to feel joy without staining it with debt. You live distrusting your own pleasure, as if it always had to be earned, and never simply lived.

And notice a contradiction that says everything about the size of this belief. You want, deeply, a lighter life. You dream of rest, of slack, of being able to receive without killing yourself, of a prosperity that does not cost you your skin. And at the same time, whenever lightness draws near, you distrust it, you complicate it, you flee it, you go back to picking up the weight, because lightness frightens you more than effort. You desire the destination and you refuse the arrival. That is why extraordinarily capable women like you, who could have a much lighter life than they have, keep choosing, time after time, the path that hurts, not because it is necessary, but because what does not hurt seems to them, deep down, not to be for them. It is not that lightness is missing from your life. It is that you, when it arrives, do not let it stay.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in concrete scenes. It's not being able to enjoy a holiday without feeling you have to "make up for it" with work before or after. It's looking at someone who earns well doing what they love, with lightness, and feeling a mixture of envy and distrust, a "that isn't real work." It's putting obstacles in your own way, complicating processes that could be simple, because the simple seems to you not serious enough. It's measuring the value of your day by the tiredness you end it with, and going to sleep satisfied only when you are exhausted, as if exhaustion were the receipt of a day well lived. They are all the same: the equation that tells you that without pain there is no value, and that therefore converts everything you receive into one more thing to sweat over.

## 2. Value equals suffering

There is something that needs to be understood, because it was not born in you, it was handed to you: the equation that equates value with suffering has a history, and almost always a history that comes from before you, from those who raised you and from those who raised them. You come, in all likelihood, from a lineage where bread cost real sweat, where hard work was the only known way to survive, where resting was a luxury of others and effort was, literally, what kept the family alive. And in that lineage a truth was engraved that was, in its time and its context, exact: to have, you have to toil. What does not cost, does not arrive. Whoever rests, loses.

Notice where this may have come from, without judging anyone, because whoever passed this to you did not want to harm you, they wanted to prepare you for a world that was hard. Perhaps you had grandparents or great-grandparents who worked the land from sunup to sundown, for whom stopping meant going hungry, and for whom sweat was sacred because it was the border between eating and not eating. Perhaps you had parents who rose up at the cost of much sacrifice, who repeated that in life nothing comes for free, that whoever wants something goes and fights for it, that rest is paid for afterward. Perhaps you grew up hearing that work dignifies, that idle hands are dangerous, that there is something morally suspect about those who have things easy. Each of these phrases carried a wisdom of survival, and you absorbed them not as ideas, but as truths of the body, as the very way of being a serious, dignified, decent person.

And here is what happened, that no one noticed: you inherited the equation, but you did not inherit the context. Your ancestors sweated because they truly had to sweat, because the alternative was hunger. You, most probably, no longer live in that world. You can already receive without that taking the bread from anyone, you can already have lightness without that meaning death, you can already prosper without sweating for every cent. But the equation stayed, intact, working inside you as if it were still the time of hunger, and so you keep sweating what no longer needed to be sweated, complicating what could already be simple, distrusting an ease that, in your time, is no longer dangerous. You carry the law of a world that is no longer yours, and that law, which saved your people, is today keeping you from receiving what they so wanted you, one day, to be able to have.

In the way I learned to look at these stories, there is a silent loyalty here that is worth seeing. Because to keep sweating as they sweated is, without your knowing it, a way of being loyal to them, of not betraying them, of not becoming "the one who had everything easy" and who therefore separated herself from the lineage of effort. To receive without suffering can sound, deep down, like dishonor, like forgetting those who toiled, as if accepting lightness were spitting on the sweat of those who came before. And so you keep yourself in effort, faithful to an equation that no longer serves, bound to a way of honoring your people that turned against you, because the true honor would not be to keep toiling as they did, it would be to receive, at last, the fruit of what they toiled so that you could have.

There is a movement that frees, and I want to show it to you without making it a recipe, because it is the opposite of going on toiling. First, to see: that you turn value into work, and where, and how. Then, to recognize where the equation comes from, and that it was the wisdom of a lineage that needed to sweat in order to live, and not a defect of yours nor of theirs. Then, and this is the hard step, to give back with respect the law that is no longer yours: to look, inside, at those who toiled before you, and to say something like "you sweated because you had to sweat, and I honor that sweat, but the time of hunger has passed, and to keep toiling as you toiled is of no use to you anymore, and it consumes me; I give you back your struggle with gratitude, and I receive, at last, the fruit you wanted me one day to be able to have." And finally, to occupy the lightness that is owed to you, not as a betrayal, but as the fulfillment of the deepest wish of those who preceded you. It is not a step you make in a page. It is a crossing. But the direction is this, and it is the opposite of sweating more.

Notice that there is a moving inversion here: what seemed like loyalty, to keep toiling, is after all a way of leaving their sacrifice in vain, because they did not sweat so that you would sweat too, they sweated so that you would not have to. And what seemed like betrayal, to receive with lightness, is after all the greatest of tributes, because it is the living proof that their effort produced, at last, a lighter life. When you see this, the guilt of prospering reverses: letting life be light for you becomes the way of giving meaning to all the weight that came before you, and to keep carrying it unnecessarily becomes, instead, the true waste of what they wanted to give you.

I want you to see this without judging yourself and without judging those who raised you. The equation value equals suffering was not a mistake, it was a dated truth, exact in its time and false in yours. And your attachment to it is not stubbornness, it is love, it is loyalty, it is the respect of a child for the pain of those who sustained her. Except that that love, in order to be complete, now needs to do the opposite of what you learned: it needs to give you permission to receive without pain, not to betray your people, but so that their sacrifice, at last, was worth it in you.

## 3. Who am I when I'm not producing

I am going to let a question fall here, on its own, and I ask you not to dispatch it quickly, even if it gives you vertigo, because it is the question that lies beneath your whole race.

Who am I when I'm not producing?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness it opens, the restlessness that appears just from imagining it answered.

For a woman who turned her whole life into work, this question touches a deep fear, and it is worth understanding. It is that you, without deciding to, gradually built your identity and your right to exist on top of what you produce. You are the one who does, the one who dedicates herself, the one who strives, the one who delivers, and that doing is not only what you do, it has become who you are, your way of occupying a legitimate place in the world. Take production away from you, give you a whole day with nothing to do, with nothing to sweat over, and what appears is not rest, but an anguish, the sense that like this, idle, unproductive, you stop having a reason to be, you stop justifying the space you occupy, you become somehow surplus.

And the shadow hiding here has a name, and it is uselessness. Your greatest terror, though you rarely name it, is being useless. It is existing without producing, occupying space without paying for it with work, being a mouth that eats without hands that sweat. There is something, in this image, that is unbearable to you, and that something is ancient, it comes from the same lineage where whoever did not produce was a burden, where usefulness was the condition of belonging, where to be worth something and to serve were the same thing. That is why you cannot stop, even exhausted: because stopping is not only resting, it is confronting the ghost of your own uselessness, and you prefer exhaustion to looking at that ghost head on.

Notice how this way of existing makes you strangely fragile, despite all your dedication and capacity. Because if your right to exist depends on what you produce, then any pause, any illness, any phase in which you cannot perform is a threat to your very legitimacy. You cannot afford not to produce, because not producing, in your inner world, is to stop deserving the place. You live, therefore, in a permanent tension, that of someone who has to keep justifying her existence, day after day, with one more proof of effort, and can never simply be, simply exist, simply be there without deserving it first. It is an exhausting way of life, and it explains why rest, for you, never rests: because even lying down, even on holiday, even idle, there is a part of you that goes on feeling that you should be producing, and that charges you for the idleness as if it were a crime.

And there is a cost you may not yet have connected to this: your inability to be present. Because to be present, to enjoy a moment, a meal, a conversation, a child, a sunset, you have to stop producing, and stopping producing opens the emptiness of uselessness. So, even in the good moments, there is always a part of you in the next task, in what is left to do, in what you should be performing. Life happens and you are not there, because you are busy deserving the right to be there, and when you finally would deserve it, the moment has already passed. It is the silent tragedy of someone who turns everything into work: she never gets to live what she works so hard to have.

And there is a family root worth looking at, because it sheds a great deal of light. Notice how, in your family, people looked at those who did not produce. There was, probably, a judgment, spoken or unspoken, about the "lazy ones," those who "do nothing," those who "live off others," and there was a corresponding pride in those who worked a great deal, who "never stopped," who "worked themselves to the bone." You grew up inside that morality, where work was a virtue and idleness was almost a sin, and where your place in the family's affection and esteem depended, in part, on placing yourself on the right side of that line, on the side of those who produce. You became productive, in part, so as not to fall into the despised group, so as not to be "one of those," so as to deserve the pride instead of the judgment. And that vigilance, set up in childhood, goes on working today, making you produce not out of necessity, but out of terror of falling, in your own eyes, into the group of those who are worth nothing because they do nothing.

I want to tell you something about this fear of uselessness, because it begins to disarm it. Your usefulness is not your dignity. You are not valuable because you produce, you are valuable because you exist, and those are two completely different things that your lineage fused into one. A machine is worth what it produces, and when it stops producing, it is thrown away. A human being is not. A human being is worth something for existing, and that worth does not increase when she produces more nor diminish when she produces less, because it is not a worth of function, it is a worth of presence. You confused yourself with a machine, you inherited the idea that your value was one of output, and so you live with the terror of becoming obsolete. But you are not a tool. And the day you feel, even if only for an instant, that you exist with value even without producing anything, something very old and very heavy begins, at last, to set itself down.

## 4. Effort as proof of existing

We come to the deepest point, and I want you to read it slowly, because it is here that everything is decided. Beneath the distrust of the easy, beneath the equation value equals suffering, beneath the fear of uselessness, there is one thing only, and it is this: you use effort to earn the right to exist. Sweat is not, for you, merely the price of things. It is the price of your own place in the world. You work, you strive, you suffer, not only to have what you want, but to feel legitimate in being alive, in occupying space, in breathing. Effort has become your proof of existence, the permanent receipt you pay to have permission to be here.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in a detail that reveals everything: the difficulty in saying "I did nothing today" without that weighing on you like a confession. For most people, a day of doing nothing is a luxury, a rest, a rare joy. For you, it is almost a shame, a failing, a lost day that you have to justify, if only to yourself. Have you noticed how you measure your days? A day "well spent" is, in your head, a day full of production, and a day in which you rested, in which you were there, in which you simply lived without delivering anything, is recorded as a day in deficit, a day that didn't count, a day for which you feel vaguely indebted. That silent accounting, in which only what you produced counts as life lived, is the equation operating at the most intimate level: not even your own time belongs to you without your paying for it, not even your own day do you have the right to live without justifying it.

Notice what this means, because it is enormous and it is painful. It means that you do not feel entitled to exist just by existing. That you need to justify it, to deserve it, to pay for it, every day, with one more proof of effort. Other people wake up and exist, just like that. You wake up and begin, immediately, to earn the right to the day, to justify your presence, to prove that you are not a burden, that you deserve the place you occupy, that you did not come for free. And that proof is never complete, because a right that has to be deserved is never deserved once and for all, it has to be deserved again every morning, and so you run without end, sweating for a legitimacy that, whatever you did, was already yours from the start, but that you never learned to feel as yours without paying for it.

And here I want to let fall, with weight, the hardest truth of this book: the right to exist is not deserved. It is not something you earn with effort, with merit, with sacrifice. It precedes all of that. You have the right to exist by the simple fact of existing, just as a newborn child has the right to exist who has not yet produced anything, who only receives, who only weighs, and who is, even so, fully legitimate, fully welcome, fully with a place. That was your birthright, and at some moment, for some reason, someone taught you or let you learn that no, that your place had to be paid for, that your existence had to be deserved. And ever since then you pay, without end, a debt that was never yours to pay.

Let me tell you where this usually comes from, because seeing the origin helps to let go. Sometimes it is a child who felt, early, that she was trouble, that she weighed on an already overloaded house, and who learned to make up for it by being useful, hardworking, productive, the one who is no trouble and who justifies her place by becoming valuable. Sometimes it is a child who only received attention and approval when she made an effort, when she brought results, and who realized that being, simply, was not enough, that she had to do in order to be loved. Sometimes it comes from further back, from a family where survival itself depended on everyone working, where an idle child was a real danger, and where effort became, over generations, the tacit condition of having the right to eat, to stay, to exist. In any of these cases, the conclusion was engraved the same: to exist is expensive, and it is paid for in effort.

And I want to show you how this shows itself in money, because that is where the whole collection comes to a head, and in you it is particularly clear. You can only receive money that hurt to earn. Money that comes to you easily leaves you uncomfortable, almost dirty, as if it were not clean, as if there were something illegitimate in it. So, even when you could earn well with lightness, you complicate, you work too much, you charge too little, or you sabotage the opportunity, because money has, for you, to come accompanied by suffering in order to be honest. And there is an invisible ceiling in this: since your capacity to suffer is finite, and just as well, your capacity to receive is limited to the measure of what you can toil for. You can never receive more than that for which you are willing to suffer, and since there is a human limit to suffering, there is, without your seeing it, a limit to everything you allow yourself to have. Abundance lives precisely on the other side of that equation: of value that multiplies beyond effort, of what yields while you rest, of what comes just because. And that, your system refuses, because it seems to it that it was not paid for.

I want you to feel the weight of this, because it is the weight you have been carrying without knowing you carried it. Imagine the tiredness of someone who never, not for a single day, felt simply entitled to be alive. Of someone who has to justify every hour, deserve every rest, pay for every joy. It is not only physical tiredness, it is an existential tiredness, that of someone who treats her own life as an open debt that she has to keep paying off with sweat. And perhaps, as you read this, you feel a sadness, or a relief, or both: the sadness of recognizing the weight, and the relief of realizing that perhaps, just perhaps, it was never necessary, that perhaps you have been paying, your whole life, for something that was already given to you for free at birth, and that no one, ever, should have made you feel you had to buy.

## 5. Exhaustion is not a virtue

I have to stop this book for a moment, because there is something I need to tell you with all seriousness, and that is too important to be diluted among the rest. The woman who turns everything into effort, who measures her value by her tiredness, who does not allow herself to stop, runs a real risk, and there is a border between dedication and illness that it is vital to know how to recognize, because on the other side of it we are no longer talking about an equation to revise, we are talking about a health to protect.

On one side of the border is what we have been talking about: you turn value into work, you distrust the easy, you suffer to receive, you tire from a race that never gives you permission to stop. This is painful, and it has a path, the path of this book. On the other side of the border is something else, more serious, that is not only an equation to undo, it is an exhaustion that asks for care, and that has clinical names: burnout, anxiety, depression. And I want to name the signs, plainly, because knowing them can be decisive. If the exhaustion you feel no longer lifts with rest, if you wake up tired however much you sleep, if the weekend or the holiday no longer restores you. If there is a background anxiety that tightens your chest and won't let you stop or sleep. If you have lost the pleasure in things, even in those that used to give you joy, and everything has become gray, mechanical, empty. If you feel yourself functioning on autopilot, disconnected from yourself. If your body has started to fail, to fall ill, to give signs that it can no longer take the pace. These are not signs of weakness nor of a lack of capacity. They are signs that you have crossed a human limit.

And I want to be very clear, with all firmness and all tenderness, about something that your equation will not want to hear: this is not resolved with more effort. It is not resolved by enduring more, by gritting your teeth, by being stronger. Exhaustion is, precisely, the result of having endured beyond what was human, and more effort only deepens it. And there is a specific trap in you, which is dangerous: for a woman who believes that everything of value is won through suffering, even recovery becomes one more thing to sweat over, and there are those who try to "work hard at their rest," to "strive to recover," turning the cure itself into one more sacrifice. That is not how this is treated. A clinical exhaustion, a depression, an installed anxiety, are treated with professional support, with a doctor, a psychologist, someone to help you out of the pit, and to say it clearly is the most honest way of taking care of you. The understanding in this book is precious, but it does not treat an installed burnout, and it would be irresponsible to make you believe that it was enough.

There is a doubled danger here, and I want to name it, because it concerns you in a particular way. Your equation not only pushes you toward exhaustion, it also keeps you from taking it seriously, because, in your inner world, exhaustion is a virtue. To end up destroyed is the proof that you dedicated yourself, tiredness is your receipt of honor, and so, when the body starts to give way, instead of listening to it, you feel pride, or at least legitimacy, "I am exhausted because I gave myself fully." This confusion between exhaustion and merit is perhaps the most dangerous of all, because it turns the very alarm signals into a reason for pride, and makes you endure precisely when you should stop. I want to ask you to see this clearly: your exhaustion is not a medal. It is a warning. And confusing it with virtue can cost you very dearly.

If you recognize yourself in these signs, I ask you not to turn this page the way you turn so many others, rushing to the next task. Stop. Your value, your dedication, your capacity, are not worth more than your health, than your life, than your right to exist without destroying yourself to prove it. And seeking help when you are exhausted is not admitting defeat nor weakness. It is the most lucid and most courageous way of protecting yourself, and of being able, one day, to go back to living in a way that does not cost you yourself. You deserve that care. Not when you reach the end of your strength. Now, before that.

## 6. Value before effort

If there is one sentence I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it carefully, because it dismantles the equation that governs you: your value comes before your effort. It comes first. It is not produced by sweat, it is not earned through pain, it does not depend on what you do nor on how much it costs you. It is there, whole, before you lift a finger, and nothing you do increases it, and nothing you fail to do diminishes it. You are worth something, and effort is not the source of that worth, it is only, at most, one of its expressions.

Notice what is revolutionary in this idea for you. Your whole life rested on the opposite: first the effort, then the value; first the sweat, then the right; first the proof, then the permission to exist. And now I propose to invert the whole order: first you are worth something, first you exist with full right, and from that ground, if you want, you do, you strive, you dedicate yourself, not to deserve your place, which is already yours, but because you feel like it, because you love what you do, because creating is good. Effort stops being the payment for your existence and goes back to being what it should be: a free choice, an expression of life, and not a debt to pay off endlessly.

Notice that there is a difference between effort and suffering that your equation never let you see, and that changes everything. Effort is natural and even good: applying yourself, dedicating yourself, doing with commitment, creating with devotion. There is a good tiredness at the end of a day of beloved work, a tiredness that tastes of a full life. Suffering is something else: it is effort run through with pain, with the obligation to deserve, with the guilt of stopping, with the terror of not being enough. Your equation fused the two, made you believe that without suffering there is no legitimate effort, and so you cannot make an effort with lightness, you always have to do it with weight, with anguish, with the debt on your back. To separate effort from suffering is one of the most freeing steps of this crossing: to discover that you can dedicate yourself immensely without suffering, create with diligence and with joy at the same time, work a great deal without that costing you your soul. The good tiredness fills you. Suffering empties you. And you learned to confuse them because, in your lineage, you only knew the second.

I'll say it another way, more concrete. Imagine two people who do exactly the same work, with the same dedication, and deliver it the same. One does it out of the hunger to deserve to exist, with the weight of someone paying a debt, exhausting herself to prove that she has the right to her place. The other does it out of a value she already feels is hers, with the lightness of someone who creates because she likes to, and who would set the task down without guilt if she needed to rest. From the outside, the work is the same. On the inside, they are opposite lives: one is a condemnation, the other is a freedom. The cure I propose to you is not for you to do less. It is for you to do it from the second place, and not from the first. It is to work, if you work, like someone who expresses, not like someone who pays.

And I want to speak to you about what happens with ease, because it is where your equation most robs you. There are things that are easy for you precisely because you have talent, because it is your field, because there is a gift there. And your equation teaches you to devalue exactly those things, to distrust what comes naturally to you, to only give value to what costs you. But this is madness, if you think about it well: you are despising your own gifts for being gifts, fleeing from what you do best because you do it with lightness, and forcing yourself toward what costs you because the cost reassures you. Imagine the waste. Your greatest talents, that in which you could prosper with joy, go unexplored because they seem to you "too easy to count," while you exhaust yourself in what costs you because suffering gives you the illusion of legitimacy. To receive for what is easy for you, to prosper from your gifts, is not stealing. It is, at last, letting your value circulate without sabotaging it with the toll of suffering.

The awakening, when it comes, does not come like an idea, it comes like a permission. It is the moment when you receive something that did not cost you, and instead of the usual discomfort, the guilt, the distrust, you feel, for once, that you can simply accept it. That it is all right. That you did not steal anything, did not betray anyone, did nothing wrong by receiving without suffering. It is a small and enormous instant, because it disproves, with the force of experience, a law that your lineage handed you and that you had never questioned. And from that instant on, even if the old equation goes back to whispering, you already know, inside, that there was another way: that life can be given to you, and not only charged to you, and that accepting that gift does not make you less worthy, it makes you, at last, free.

## 7. Receiving without suffering

If your value comes before your effort, then there is an experience you are missing, and that changes everything: that of receiving without suffering. Of letting life be light for you without complicating it, of prospering without paying for it in pain, of accepting the good without converting it into one more task. And I want to speak to you about it carefully, because I know that for you it sounds almost like transgression, and it is not, it is only your part of the inheritance, finally claimed.

I want to warn you of a resistance that is going to appear, because it is subtle: your equation is going to try to convince you that receiving without suffering is a way of becoming a worse person, more selfish, less worthy, one of those who "want everything on a silver platter." It is going to use your own morality against you, the morality of effort you inherited, to make you feel that accepting lightness is a flaw of character. Notice this voice when it appears, and answer it calmly: to receive what life gives, with gratitude and without guilt, is not selfishness, it is health; and to refuse the gift out of pride in only accepting what hurts is not virtue, it is a wound disguised as morality. The most generous people I know are not the ones who suffer most, they are the ones who receive most, because only those who let themselves be filled have somewhere to give from. Your equation made you believe that suffering made you a better person. It did not. It only made you more tired, and with less to give.

Receiving without suffering begins with a small and difficult gesture: to let a good thing simply be good. When something goes well for you with ease, when you receive without having sweated, resist the impulse to devalue it, to look for the catch, to make up for it with effort to legitimize it. Stay, for once, with the good, light, without paying for it. Notice the discomfort, the itch to complicate, to retroactively deserve what you received, and do not give in. Because it is in that staying with the easy, without sweating it, that you learn something new: that what comes light is also yours, also legitimate, also counts. That you did not have to pay for it. That life, sometimes, simply gives, and that refusing that gift because it didn't hurt is a way of impoverishing yourself unnecessarily.

And there is a deeper test, which is that of rest. For you, resting is one of the hardest things there is, because resting is receiving without producing, it is being there without deserving it, it is existing without paying. Learning to rest without guilt, to be idle without feeling that you are failing, to do nothing without charging yourself for the idleness, is perhaps the purest way of undoing the equation. Not because rest is a technique, but because each time you rest without guilt you are telling your body and your history a new truth: that you have the right to exist without paying for it at this very moment, that your legitimacy is not suspended just because you stopped sweating. Rest, for you, is not laziness. It is an act of healing, it is the practical affirmation that you are worth something even when you don't produce.

I want to warn you of a trap, because you are going to fall into it: turning all of this into one more project to do well. You are going to want to "work on your relationship with effort," to "strive to receive better," to "deserve rest," and suddenly you are sweating over the cure itself, turning learning not to suffer into one more suffering. Notice this when it happens, and laugh a little at yourself, with tenderness, because it is your old equation trying to survive dressed up as something new. Receiving without suffering is not one more thing to do well. It is, precisely, the place where there is nothing to do well, where you can accept without merit, rest without guilt, have without paying. It is not conquered with effort. It is allowed, in the instant when you give yourself permission for the easy.

I want to leave you with a question that can serve as a compass, because it cuts straight to the root: is this suffering I am putting into this really necessary, or am I adding it to feel entitled to what I am going to receive? Ask yourself this question each time you catch yourself complicating, overloading yourself, refusing a help or an ease. Often you are going to discover that the suffering you add does not serve the task, does not improve the result, is not demanded by anything except your need to legitimize it. It is decorative suffering, paid only to soothe the equation, and it adds no value at all, it only exhausts you. Learning to distinguish the effort that the task asks for from the suffering that only your guilt demands is learning to spare yourself an enormous amount of useless pain, and to discover that much of what you toiled at, over your life, was not needed for anything except to give yourself permission to receive.

And I want to show you what this opens, because it concerns the whole collection and your concrete prosperity. A woman who can only receive in the exact measure of what she suffered puts, without knowing it, a ceiling on everything she can have. Because abundance, by definition, is receiving more than you sweated for, it is value multiplying, coming with lightness, yielding beyond direct effort. If you only accept what hurts in the exact measure, you refuse precisely the surplus, the easy, what comes in excess, which is what prosperity is made of. To undo the equation value equals suffering is not only an existential relief. It is opening the door so that your life can, at last, give you more than what you bled for it, without your, at the entrance, giving back the surplus because it seems to you that you did not deserve it.

There is a fear you may have, by this point, and I want to go to meet it, because it is important: the fear that, if you stop suffering for what you receive, you will become lazy, soft, a person who no longer does anything, who loses her drive, who lets herself be. As if the only thing that keeps you functioning were the whip of the equation, and without it you would collapse into inertia. It will not happen, and I want to tell you why. Your energy, your will to create, your capacity to dedicate yourself, do not come from the equation value equals suffering, they come from you, from who you are, from the pleasure you take in doing things well. The equation is not the engine, it is the toll, it is the tax of pain you charge yourself at the entrance of everything. Take away the tax, and the engine is still there, intact, and even better, because it stops spending half its energy on suffering and can put it all into creating. Women who free themselves from this equation do not do less. They do with lightness what they used to do with weight, and often they do more and better, because joy, unlike suffering, does not exhaust, it nourishes.

I am not going to give you a method for receiving without suffering, because it would be one more task, and we would be feeding precisely what we came to heal. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because unlearning the law of generations is not done in a page. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: you do not need to suffer in order to deserve. Receive what comes light. Accept what did not cost. Let life give to you, for once, without demanding from it the bill of pain, and discover, slowly, that accepting lightness does not dishonor those who toiled before you, rather it honors them, because it was precisely for this, so that you could, at last, receive without bleeding, that they bled so much.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in a simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything at once, and it doesn't undo the equation of a lifetime in a day. But it changes enough for you to stop feeling suspect for enjoying, ungrateful for receiving, or in deficit for resting.

Now you know that you turn value into work, and that this is not too much dedication, it is an inherited equation that equates value with suffering. You know that this equation came from a lineage where sweat was survival, and that you inherited the law but not the context, and you keep sweating what no longer needs to be sweated. You know that the shadow that pushes you is the fear of uselessness, and that you confused your worth of presence with a worth of output, as if you were a machine. You know, and this was the hardest, that you use effort as proof of your right to exist, and that that right is not deserved, it precedes everything. You know how to distinguish dedication from exhaustion, and to seek help if that is the case, because exhaustion is not a virtue, it is a warning. And you know that your value comes before your effort, and that receiving without suffering does not dishonor you, it frees you.

And I want to tell you something about your descendants, or about whoever sees you live, because it may move you more than anything else. Children learn the value of effort by seeing how the adults relate to it. If they grew up seeing you turn everything into sacrifice, distrust pleasure, not give yourself permission to rest, only feel legitimate when exhausted, they are absorbing the same equation along with you, and it is likely that one day they will wear it, and that they will suffer the same race without arrival that you suffer. The most freeing thing you can do for those who come after you is not to give them the example of sacrifice, it is to give them the example of a woman who works and rests, who dedicates herself and enjoys, who receives without guilt and exists without always paying for it. Sometimes a woman who never gave herself permission for lightness gives it, at last, to herself, so as not to deny it to those who learn to live by looking at her.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it sums everything up. For years you lived like someone who received a house as an inheritance, a good house, solid, paid for by the sweat of those who came before, and who refuses to live in it, who sleeps on the ground at the door, who insists on building, brick by brick, with her own bleeding hands, a worse house alongside it, just because the inherited one seems to her not to be deserved, not to have been sweated for, to have come too easily. And whoever left you the house did not want it for you to suffer beside it. They wanted it for you to live in it, in peace, sheltered, receiving at last the fruit of what they toiled for. The crossing I propose to you is to enter the house. It is to accept what was given to you, what came easily because someone already sweated for it on your behalf, and to live, without guilt, what was precisely the meaning of all that ancient effort: that you could, one day, have without toiling.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I do not need to suffer in order to deserve.

Seeing this was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not made by reading, it is made by undoing, slowly, an equation of generations, and by giving yourself permission, time after time, to receive without pain.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about money, nor is it going to teach you to earn more. It is a space for you to look at the way you keep value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the deepest ways: that of only accepting what hurts, of converting everything into effort, of putting a toll of suffering at the entrance of everything that could be yours with lightness. There, the crossing is to undo the inherited equation, to give back with respect the law of a time that is no longer yours, and to relearn to receive without paying for it in pain, to prosper without destroying yourself to deserve it. Not so that you stop dedicating yourself, but so that your dedication stops being the receipt of a right that was already yours from the start.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There, the crossing is to set down the weight that is not ours to carry. Here, the crossing is to set down the law that tells us that everything has to be paid for in sweat. One sets down a weight, the other sets down an equation. But deep down it is the same journey: to discover that we had the right to a lightness we never gave ourselves, and to stop paying for something that was already given to us for free.

If you feel that it is the moment, you can find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is all right, and you do not have to deserve this reading with any effort at all. What you have read is already yours, without your having sweated for it, and it stays with you, light, waiting, with no hurry, for the day you give yourself permission to receive it.

And before you close these pages, I ask you one thing only, and it will be, for you, one of the hardest there is, and that is why I ask it of you. Today, let yourself receive a good thing without paying for it. A rest, a joy, a compliment, something that comes to you easily. And instead of devaluing it, of complicating it, of making up for it with effort, stay with it. Accept it whole. Say, inside, "this is also mine, even though it didn't hurt." Notice the discomfort, the old voice saying that without sweat it doesn't count, and do not obey it. Because it is in that accepting of the easy, without guilt, that the equation of a whole lifetime begins to come undone, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that your value was there long before any sweat, waiting for you, for free, as it always should have been.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
