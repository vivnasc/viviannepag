# Your place at the table

**Taking what is already yours without asking permission to exist.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a self-knowledge and personal reflection resource. It does not replace psychological or psychotherapeutic support. The woman who recognizes herself in these pages has a place, and it is rarely a lack of place that wounds her, it is the habit of not inhabiting it. If you feel that the erasure has moved beyond discomfort and into suffering (anxiety that will not let go of you, the sense of disappearing inside your own life, constant shame at existing), seek professional support. Asking for help is taking your place, not weakness.*

---

## 1. The chair you never sit in fully

There is a chair that is yours. It is there, at the family table, in the meeting room, in the group of friends, in the car on the way somewhere. No one took it from you. No one told you that you could not sit down. And yet, notice how you sit in it: on the edge. With half your body. Ready to stand up the moment someone needs something. Your feet already turned toward the exit, like someone passing through a place where, in fact, she lives.

I know this way of sitting. I know it from the outside, in my work, and I know it from the inside, because I sat like that for years without realizing I was doing it. It is an almost invisible detail. It draws no one's attention, and that is precisely the problem: no one notices, not even you, that you are occupying a fraction of the space that belongs to you.

See if you recognize this. In a group conversation, you have an opinion, but you wait for everyone else to speak first. And when you finally open your mouth, either someone has already said what you wanted to say, or the subject has changed, or you start to speak and immediately rush yourself: "but this is just my opinion," "I don't know, I could be wrong," "never mind, it's not important." You shrink the sentence while you say it. You apologize for the idea before you finish it.

At the table, you serve everyone before serving yourself. And when you finally sit down, it is the smallest plate, the least good part, the corner of the bench. At work, you do what is yours to do and a little more, but when the time for recognition comes, you step back. "It was a team effort." "I didn't do anything special." You are glad it went well and, at the same time, there is a part of you that dissolves, that does not want to be seen receiving. As if deserving attention meant stealing it from someone.

At home, in the group, in the family, you are often the one who holds things together. The one who organizes, the one who remembers the birthdays, the one who connects everyone. You are present in everything. And, curiously, you are not truly anywhere, because being there for others is not the same as taking your place among them. Being useful is one thing. Belonging is another. And there are women who are immensely useful and who, deep down, never sat at the table with their whole body.

There is one gesture that sums all of this up: shrinking. The body shrinks on public transport, draws in the elbows, takes up the minimum. The voice shrinks, lowers its volume when the subject matters. The presence shrinks, apologizes for being where it is. You learned, somewhere, that being small is safer. That whoever does not disturb is not sent away. That whoever takes up little does not run the risk of hearing "who are you to be here?".

And I want to tell you something right away, before we go deeper: this is not shyness. It is not temperament. It is not "just the way you are." It is an old strategy, built in a time when belonging seemed to depend on making yourself small. It worked, back then, perhaps. It kept you safe. It kept you inside. But the price was high: you came to live on the edge of your place, without ever inhabiting it.

Notice what you feel at the end of a family dinner, a long meeting, an afternoon with the group. Probably tiredness. But not the good tiredness of someone who truly shared. It is the tiredness of someone who was on alert the whole time, watching not to weigh too much, not to be in the way, not to take up too much space. That tiredness has a name: it is called living on permission. Always waiting for an authorization no one is going to give you, because no one took it from you.

The chair is there. It is still yours, even when you sit on the edge. And the work of this guide is not to find you a new chair. It is to help you sit fully in the one that is already yours.


## 2. Asking permission to exist

There is a sentence many women say without saying it. It is not in the words. It is in the posture, in the tone, in the retreat. The sentence is this: "Sorry for existing."

You apologize when you walk into a room. You apologize when you take a place in the line. You apologize when someone bumps into you (was it you who bumped? it does not matter, you apologize anyway). You apologize before asking a question, before requesting what you need, before disagreeing. "Sorry" has become your greeting. The password you pay for the right to be where you are.

I want you to look at this carefully, because it is deeper than it seems. Apologizing for being a bother is different from being polite. Politeness acknowledges the other. This "sorry" acknowledges that you should not be there. It is a request for permission to exist. And no one needs permission to exist. You already exist. You are already here. Your place is not under evaluation.

Where does this come from? It rarely comes from a decision of yours. It comes from far back. There are women who grew up in homes where there was a child who took up all the space, and what was left for them was the role of not being any trouble. There are women who learned, in silence, that love came more easily when they made themselves small, when they did not ask, when they did not need. There are women who inherited, from a mother or a grandmother, the conviction that a good woman is the one who erases herself, who serves, who does not take up space, who is always last on her own list.

In my study of Systemic Family Constellations, there is a principle that changed the way I see this: everyone who belongs to a system has a right to a place. It is not a prize you win. It is not earned through merit, through good behavior, through usefulness. It is a right that comes from the simple fact that you belong. You were born into that family, you entered that group, you were hired for that team. From that moment on, the place is yours. Intact. It does not need to be deserved every day.

What happens to those who do not take their place? The system feels it. When a person makes herself small, a void remains where a presence should be. And that void does not stay empty for long. Either someone takes it for you (and decides for you, and speaks for you), or you hover at the margin, present without being present, inside without truly belonging. The woman who does not take her place does not disappear cleanly. She stays halfway. And that halfway tires everyone, beginning with her.

There is an important confusion to clear up here. Many women do not take their place because they believe that taking up space means taking space from someone. That if I speak, I silence another person. That if I receive, I take from another. That if I shine, I cast a shadow. As if belonging were a small cake and each slice I took were a slice missing from someone else.

It does not work like that. At a table where everyone takes their place, no one is left without a place. On the contrary: when you sit fully, you give permission to the person beside you to sit fully too. Your smallness does not create space for others. It creates discomfort. Because the empty place you leave always forces someone to stretch to cover it. When you take what is yours, you free others from carrying you.

Notice too the difference between humility and erasure, because they are easily confused. Humility knows who she is and does not need to show off. Erasure does not know who she is and hides. Humility takes the place with lightness. Erasure flees the place in fear. A humble woman sits fully without making a fuss. An erased woman does not even sit down. And we often call humility what is, in truth, fear of existing.

There is also an emotional root worth naming, with care. Beneath the habit of asking permission there is usually an old belief: "I am not enough." "I am not enough to be here by full right." This belief was not born with you. It was taught to you, drop by drop, through experiences in which you felt like too much, or too little, or tolerated instead of welcome. And here I pause to tell you clearly: if that belief runs very deep, if it comes from experiences of rejection, neglect, or trauma, this guide is a starting point and not a substitute for support. There are wounds that ask for the presence of a psychologist or psychotherapist, and seeking that help is, in itself, taking your place.

But there is much you can begin to do today. Because however old the root may be, the place is still yours. And the body learns, with practice, what the head does not yet believe: that you can exist without apologizing.


## 3. The exercise: sitting fully

We have reached the most important part of this guide. So far we have talked. Now we are going to practice. Because taking your place is not an idea to understand. It is a gesture to repeat, until the body recognizes it as its own.

I am going to ask you the same thing I would ask if you were in front of me: go slowly and be honest. None of this is done with force. It is done with presence. If at any moment you feel you have touched a wound too big, stop and, if needed, seek support. Taking your place never means hurting yourself.

This exercise has four parts. You do not need to do them all on the same day. In fact, it is better that you do not. Do one, let it settle, move on to the next when you are ready.

### Part 1: The real chair

This part is done with a real chair. Choose one, at home, at a moment when you are alone. Sit the way you usually sit when you are with others. Notice: are you on the edge? Is your torso leaning forward, ready to jump up? Are your shoulders hunched? Are your feet pointing toward the exit?

Now, slowly, change. Rest your back against the backrest. Plant both feet on the floor, firmly grounded. Take up the whole seat, not just the edge. Let your shoulders drop. Let your hands rest, instead of keeping them at the ready. And stay like that. One minute. Two. Notice what comes up.

It is very likely that discomfort will come up. That you will feel the urge to go back to the edge. That a voice will say "this is too much," "I'm taking up too much space," "what if someone comes in?". Do not obey the voice. Stay. What you are feeling is not danger. It is the strangeness of occupying what has always been yours and that you never inhabited. The body needs time to believe it can be whole in a place.

Do this for a few days in a row, even just a minute. Sitting fully in an empty chair teaches the body, in safety, what you will then carry into the chairs full of people.

### Part 2: Catching the "sorrys"

For one whole day, you are going to be a detective of yourself. Your mission is simple: count how many times you apologize, or shrink, or undo what you say. Not to judge yourself. To see.

Pay attention to these moments:
- How many times do you say "sorry" without having done anything?
- How many times do you begin a sentence with "this is just my opinion," "maybe I'm wrong," "I don't know if this makes sense"?
- How many times do you end a request with "but if it can't be done, never mind," emptying it before the other person responds?
- How many times do you refuse something offered to you (the better seat, the last bite, the help) out of reflex, not out of choice?
- How many times do you stay silent when you had something to say?

Write it down, if it helps. By the end of the day, you will have a portrait. And the portrait usually surprises. Most women have no idea how many times, in a single day, they ask permission to exist. Seeing it is the first step to changing it. You cannot dismantle a habit you cannot see.

### Part 3: Swapping the gesture

Now that you see it, we are going to replace it. Not all at once, not everywhere. Choose one situation per week. Just one.

For example: this week, when someone offers me something I like, I will accept without refusing first. A simple "yes, thank you," without the automatic "no, there's no need." Or: this week, when I have an opinion in a conversation, I will say it without wrapping it in apologies. Direct. "I think that..." and that's it, without the "but I could be wrong" afterward. Or: this week, I will sit in the good spot, and not in the corner. I will serve myself too, and not only others.

Choose a small swap, concrete, possible. So small that it seems almost ridiculous. That is on purpose. The place is not taken in a heroic gesture. It is taken in tiny gestures, repeated, until they stop costing anything. Accepting the good seat at the table seems like nothing. But if you do it ten times, the body learns a new truth: I can receive without taking from anyone.

When you make the swap, guilt will show up. You will probably feel that you are being selfish, excessive, too much. I want you to know, from the start, that this is going to happen, so that you do not confuse the guilt with a sign that you did something wrong. The guilt of taking your place is not proof that you are doing harm. It is proof that you are doing something new. Like the discomfort in Part 1, it passes with repetition. Not because you became cold. Because you became your own.

### Part 4: The sentence that gives you back to yourself

Finally, a sentence. An anchor, for the moments when you feel you are disappearing, retreating, asking permission. Choose the one that makes the most sense to you, or create your own:

*"I belong in this place. I do not need to deserve it again today."*

*"My place is mine. Taking it takes nothing from anyone."*

*"I can be here, whole. I am already here."*

Say the sentence out loud, alone, first. Let your body hear it in your own voice. Then carry it with you. When you walk into a room and feel the impulse to make yourself small, repeat it inwardly. When "sorry" rises in your throat for no reason, swap it for the sentence. It is not magic. It is training. And training, repeated, changes what seemed unchangeable.

Go slowly with all of this. You do not have to do the four parts this week. You do not have to transform all at once. Every small gesture in which you take what is yours is already a victory. And many small victories, over time, make a woman who sits fully without even noticing.


## 4. Pertença bridge

What you did here was begin to answer a question many women carry their whole life without ever formulating it: what is my place among others? You looked at the chair you never sat in fully. You noticed the habit of asking permission to exist. And you began to practice, in small gestures, the act of taking what is already yours.

This is not a small thing. It is, in truth, the beginning of a deep change. Because belonging is not a feeling you wait for. It is a place you take. And you have begun to take it.

But I want to be honest with you, as I would be if you were in front of me. The gestures you trained here work on the surface, and often the surface is enough: a woman who sits fully, who accepts without stepping back, who speaks without apologizing, already lives differently. However, if the sense of not belonging runs very deep, if it has been with you forever, if it wakes you before dawn with the question "what is my place?", then the roots are further down. And no exercise, however good, reaches the roots on its own.

The feeling of not belonging rarely is born with us. It comes from somewhere. From an old exclusion, from a place left unoccupied in the family, from a silent loyalty to someone who also could never take their own. These inheritances are discreet, but they govern us from within as long as they are not seen. And seeing them, with care and company, is work worth a lifetime.

For now, stay with what you already have. Return to the chair. Sit fully. And the next time you feel the impulse to shrink, to apologize for existing, to sit on the edge, remember one thing only: the place is yours. No one gave it to you, and so no one can take it from you. You are already here. All that is missing is for you to sit with your whole body.

The chair waited for you all this time. It is time to take it.
