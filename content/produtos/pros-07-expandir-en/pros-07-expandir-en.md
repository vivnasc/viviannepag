# The woman who can't have more than the others

**For the woman who brakes when she starts to stand out. Because growing isn't abandoning anyone, and you can go further without leaving your own behind.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks of pulls back whenever she begins to go further than her own people, and there is a point where that brake stops being modesty and becomes an anguish that tightens whenever life wants to make her grow. If you recognize in yourself a persistent anxiety tied to standing out, a deep fear of being rejected, or a sadness that doesn't pass, that may have roots that deserve the care of a professional. Growing doesn't separate you from those who love you. And asking for help is also a way of letting yourself grow.*

---

## 1. Braking at the threshold of more

There is a movement you make and that perhaps you have never seen clearly, because it happens quickly, almost in silence: you brake. At the exact moment when you begin to stand out, to go further, to have more than your own people, to rise above the group you belong to, something in you pulls the brake. It isn't a lack of ability, it isn't a lack of opportunity, it isn't bad luck. It is an internal recoil, automatic, that makes you stop precisely at the threshold of more, as if there were an invisible line there that you cannot cross without something terrible happening.

And notice a detail that reveals the wound in its pure state: what you feel when someone from your group falls behind, and you went on. Instead of simple joy at your own path, a guilt appears, a wish to go back, to relevel yourself, almost as if your success were, somehow, at their expense. As if there were a fixed amount of luck in the group, and what you have extra were what they lacked. This feeling, that growing is taking from others, is one of the deepest roots of the brake, and it is false: your expansion takes nothing from anyone, life is not a fixed cake where your bigger slice means smaller slices for the others. You can grow without that taking anything at all from your own people, and your guilt, by treating success as theft, binds you to an imagined scarcity that only exists in your loyalty, not in reality.

Notice how this happens, in the concrete forms it takes. You are growing, doing well, approaching a new level, and suddenly you sabotage, you hesitate, you find reasons not to advance, or you simply lose the momentum, as if a part of you didn't want to get there. When you begin to have more than your friends, your siblings, the people you grew up with, you feel a discomfort, a guilt, a wish to minimize, to hide, not to stand out. You refuse opportunities that would put you far ahead of your own people. Or you advance, and you feel so bad, so isolated, so guilty, that you end up finding a way to return to the group's level, to relevel yourself downward, so the discomfort passes.

And there is a pattern beneath all of this, which is the heart of this book: you don't let yourself have more than the others. Not others in general, but your own, your group, your equals, those to whom you feel you belong. With them, there is an invisible measure you cannot cross, a ceiling that isn't the ceiling of your abilities, it is the ceiling of your loyalty. You can go as far as they go. Above that, the alarm goes off. As if rising higher than the group were dangerous. As if there were, in standing out, a betrayal, an abandonment, a breaking of something sacred that binds you to your own.

I want to be precise right from the start, because this is distinct from other things. It isn't the guilt of having more than your parents, more than your origin, more than those who came before you, that is a vertical loyalty, to the lineage, and it is another wound. Yours is horizontal: it is loyalty to your equals, to those at your side, to your peers, to your group, to your generation. It isn't "how can I have what my ancestors didn't have," it is "how can I have more than my own, than those who are like me, without ceasing to be one of them." It is a lateral loyalty, to the group of belonging, and its fear isn't betraying the origin, it is separating from the pack.

I want you to know, right from the start, that this book has not come to tell you that you should stop caring about your own, become competitive, trample others to rise, stop belonging to any group at all. Your bond with your own people is precious, and I am not going to take it from you. I have come to look at something else: at the brake that prevents you from growing, and that is, without your seeing it, keeping you small out of a misunderstood loyalty, making you refuse your own expansion out of fear that growing will cost you belonging. At the invisible line that confuses rising with abandoning, and that binds you to the level of the group as if going further were betraying those who stayed.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read with tightness: growing isn't abandoning. Going further doesn't separate you from those you love, doesn't take away your place in the group, doesn't make you a stranger, one who "thinks she's better." This idea, which seems simple, goes against a very deep fear, the fear of ceasing to belong, and so it will cost you to accept it. But it is the one that opens the door to something you never allowed yourself: to expand without guilt, to go as far as you can go, without leaving anyone behind, because your belonging, the real kind, never depended on keeping yourself small, it depended on loving, and love you can do just the same, or more, from whatever height you reach.

There is a way you live this that perhaps you have never connected to loyalty, but that comes from it: the discomfort with success, even when nobody is watching you. It isn't only in front of others that you brake, it is inside yourself. You approach something big and an unease appears, a feeling that it isn't for you, that you are stepping out of your place, that something bad will happen if you dare so much. It is as if you carried an inner sentinel, inherited from the group, that keeps controlling your size even when you are alone, that tightens whenever you exceed the measure, that brings you back to the known level. Loyalty to the group doesn't live only in the reactions of others, it settled inside you, and became a voice of your own that brakes you even before anyone can react. That is why it isn't enough to change groups or to move away from those who contained you: the ceiling travels with you, because you internalized it, and it is on the inside that it has to be released.

And I want you to notice a contradiction that says everything about the size of this belief. You want, profoundly, to go further. You have ability, you have will, you have dreams that ask for space. And at the same time, whenever you approach realizing them, you pull back, you sabotage, you brake, because going further frightens you more than staying where you are. You desire the flight and you hold back the wings. That is why so many talented and capable women like you live far below what they could, not for lack of potential or of opportunities, but for an internal brake that goes off whenever they begin to stand out. It isn't the world that limits you. It is you who pull the brake at the threshold of more, faithful to a measure that nobody, after all, obliges you today to respect.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in concrete scenes. It's having good news and feeling uncomfortable sharing it with someone who is down, almost apologizing for it. It's minimizing your achievements in front of your own, making yourself smaller, so as not to make them feel bad. It's feeling that you grow apart from your friends or your family as you prosper, and a dull guilt about it. It's pulling back at the edge of a leap that would put you far ahead of your group, without quite knowing why. They are all the same: horizontal loyalty, the brake at the threshold of more, the fear that rising will cost you belonging.

## 2. Loyalty to equals

There is something that needs to be understood, because it explains the brake, and it is one of the oldest and most powerful forces there is in the human being: belonging to the group. For almost all of our species's history, belonging to the group was a matter of life or death. Alone, the human being didn't survive. In the group, yes. And so we developed a deep need, engraved in the deepest part of us, to belong, to be accepted by our own, not to be expelled from the pack. And one of the tacit conditions of belonging to a group is, since always, equality: being like the others, sharing their level, not raising yourself too far above nor falling too far below, because both rising and falling threaten the cohesion of the group and your place in it.

Notice what this does. There is, in all human groups, a silent surveillance over whoever stands out. Whoever rises too much, whoever distinguishes himself too far, whoever comes to have far more than the others, almost always awakens a reaction: envy, resentment, distancing, comments, a subtle pressure to "not get above yourself," "not think you're better," "keep being one of us." And that reaction is not, most of the time, individual malice, it is the very mechanism of the group trying to restore the equality that keeps it cohesive. You felt this, you saw this, you learned this, and you drew, without deciding it, a conclusion of survival: standing out is risking being expelled, rising too much is losing my own, it is safer to stay at the group's level, to be equal, not to raise myself.

In the way I learned to look at these things, there is here an invisible message that governs everything, and it is worth bringing it to light: if I rise too much, I cease to belong. Notice the sentence. It isn't "I'm not capable of rising," because capable you are. It is "if I rise, I lose my own." And that sentence, engraved deep, works like a ceiling over your whole expansion: you can grow up to the level where you remain equal to your own, and from there the fear of ceasing to belong pulls the brake. It isn't the fear of failing that brakes you. It is the fear of having too much success, of rising above the group, of becoming different from your own to the point of no longer fitting among them.

And I want to show you how this loyalty is particularly strong among siblings, among lifelong friends, among those who grew up together. Perhaps you grew up with siblings and there is, without anyone saying so, a measure of equality among you that can't be broken without cost: if one rises far more than the others, a tension is generated, a comparison, an imbalance that disturbs the family's peace. Perhaps you have friends with whom you share a history, a level, a way of being, and you feel that prospering far more than them would push you away, would make you strange to the group, would cost you those bonds. And out of love for those bonds, out of fear of losing them, you keep yourself at their level, you brake your expansion, you level yourself downward, so as to keep belonging to the group you fear losing if you grow too much.

There is a movement that frees, and I want to show it to you without making a recipe of it, because it is the opposite of braking. First, to see: that you brake at the threshold of more, and where, and how you contain yourself. Then, to recognize where loyalty to equals comes from, and that it was your way of securing belonging, not a defect. Then, and this is the difficult step, to give back the rule you don't have to keep: to look, on the inside, at the group that contained you, and to tell it that you love it and that you belong, but that you don't have to keep yourself small to prove it, that you refuse to fold your flight out of a loyalty that nobody demands of you today. Then, to release: to let yourself go further, to occupy your full height, without the brake. And finally, to connect: to keep your heart connected to your own from whatever height, going ahead without cutting the bond. It isn't a step taken in one page. It is a crossing. But the direction is this, and it is the opposite of containing yourself.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because the tendency will be to think yourself foolish, or to feel anger at the group that brakes you. It is no foolishness at all, and the group doesn't wish you ill, it only obeys, as you do, an old mechanism of cohesion. Loyalty to equals is not a defect of yours. It is the mark of how much you need to belong, and that need is human, deep, legitimate. Only that loyalty, carried this way, turned against you: it makes you sacrifice your own life, your expansion, your growth, on the altar of a belonging that, as we are going to see, never truly depended on keeping yourself small.

## 3. Who am I if I'm the one who went further

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you not to dismiss it quickly, even if it gives you vertigo, because it is the question that lies beneath the brake.

Who am I if I'm the one who went further?

Stay with it. Notice the discomfort it brings, the loneliness it seems to announce.

For a woman whose identity is rooted in the group, this question touches a deep fear, and it is worth understanding. It is that you define yourself, in great part, by your belonging: you are one of your friends, one of your family, one of your generation, one of your milieu. And being one of them implies, without your thinking it, being like them, sharing their level, their world, their measure. Becoming the one who went further, the one who rose, the one who has far more, threatens that identity of belonging: if I'm no longer like them, then who am I? If I rose above the group, to what group do I belong now? Am I left alone, up high, without my own, a stranger to all? That image of loneliness at the top is so terrifying that you'd rather stay down below, with them, than risk the loneliness of having gone further.

And the shadow that hides here has a name, and it is separation. Your greatest fear, beneath everything, is to separate, to be left out, to be symbolically expelled from the group you belong to. And growing, in your imagination, brings that separation: by rising above your own, you cease to be one of them, you become something else, someone who no longer fits, who no longer shares, who became different. And that separation, for someone who needs so much to belong, is a kind of social death, of exile, of the loss of the warmth of the pack. That is why you brake: not because you don't want to grow, but because growing seems to demand that separation, and no achievement seems to you worth the loss of your own.

And there is a way of seeing families and groups that illuminates this, and that is worth knowing. Among equals, among siblings, among peers, there exists a kind of invisible scale that watches over equality, and that scale registers any imbalance as a threat to cohesion, both upward and downward. Those who fall into disgrace and those who rise too much are, both, seen by the group as having broken the pact of equality, and both awaken the same reaction of rebalancing. That is why prospering can set off in you the same guilt a fall would set off: because in both cases you cease to be equal to your own. Understanding this mechanism helps, because it shows you that your guilt at growing isn't a sign that you are doing wrong, it is only the working of an old scale that confuses imbalance with betrayal. And a scale, once it is seen, can be questioned: rigid equality is not love, it is only a fragile form of cohesion, and mature love bears, and even celebrates, that each one go as far as his own path takes him.

Notice how this fear makes you strangely hostage to the level of others. Because if you can't have more than your own, then your ceiling isn't that of your abilities, it is that of the group, and it moves with it. You don't grow to what you could, you grow only as far as your own reach, and you stay there, waiting, leveled, with all your potential unused, not out of incapacity, but out of loyalty. It is a moving form of waste: a woman full of ability keeping herself small so as not to clash, holding back her flight so as not to cast a shadow on those who don't fly, living far below what she could so the group feels comfortable beside her. And the saddest part is that this sacrifice helps no one: your smallness doesn't enlarge your own, it only multiplies the smallness by one more person, you.

And there is a cost you pay, that perhaps you haven't yet connected to this: the frustration of a life unlived. Because within you there is more, there is a flight, there is an expansion that asks to happen, and you brake it, time after time, out of loyalty, and that repressed expansion doesn't disappear, it stays, like a dull frustration, a feeling of a life postponed, of potential unfulfilled, of "I could have gone so much further." And that frustration, with time, can sour, turn into resentment against the very group you feel brakes you, or into a sadness about yourself, the one who didn't dare. The price of staying small out of loyalty is not saved, it is paid by you, in life unlived, in flight unflown, in all the more you had to be and that you sacrificed in order to keep fitting.

I want to tell you something about this fear of separation, because it begins to disarm it. The separation you fear isn't caused by growing. It is caused by a way of growing that abandons, that moves away, that looks down from above, that forgets its own. But that isn't the only way of growing. There is another, and it is the one this book wants to show you: going further while staying connected, rising without moving away, growing carrying your own in your heart. The fear tells you there are only two options: either you stay small and belong, or you grow and separate. It is a lie. There is a third, and it is the only one that frees: growing and belonging at the same time, because your belonging, the real kind, never depended on your size, it depended on your love.

## 4. The fear of ceasing to belong

We arrive at the deepest point, and I want you to read it slowly, because it is here that everything is decided. Beneath the brake, beneath loyalty to equals, beneath the fear of separation, there is one thing, and it is this: the fear of ceasing to belong. Rising, for you, is equivalent to losing the group. And since belonging is one of your deepest, oldest needs, most tied to your sense of security and of existence, you'd rather, without consciously deciding it, stay small and belong than grow and risk exile.

Notice what this means. It means you traded, without knowing it, your expansion for your security. That you chose belonging over growth, the warmth of the group over the solitary flight. And that choice, made in the dark, is understandable, because belonging answers a fear older and deeper than the desire to grow: the primitive fear of being abandoned, expelled, left alone. For your deepest system, being expelled from the group is a threat to survival, and growing too much seems to invite that expulsion. That is why you brake: not out of laziness, not out of a lack of ambition, but out of a choice of survival that privileges the security of belonging over the risk of expansion.

And here I want to drop, with weight, the hardest truth of this book: your smallness doesn't guarantee your belonging. This is the illusion the fear hides. You keep yourself small so as not to lose your own, but notice: true belonging never depended on your size. Those who truly love you don't love you for being small, they love you for being who you are, and they would keep loving you if you grew. And those who would move away from you if you grew, those didn't love you, they loved your equality, the confirmation that you were like them, and that isn't belonging, it is a kind of pact of mutual mediocrity. By staying small, you aren't guaranteeing the love of those who love you, you already have that, you are buying the tolerance of those who only want you as long as you don't surpass them, and that tolerance isn't worth your flight.

And I want to show you a concrete dynamic that perhaps you recognize, because it is one of the most common forms of this fear: the discomfort before envy. When you begin to have more, to stand out, you feel, or imagine, the envy of others, and that envy is so unbearable to you that you do everything to avoid it, minimizing yourself, hiding what you have, oversharing, devaluing your achievements. You'd rather shrink yourself than bear the envy of those who stayed. And there is something important to see here: the envy of others is not your responsibility, nor is it a reason to keep yourself small. If someone envies you for growing, that is that person's work with their own containment, not a sign that you did wrong to grow. By shrinking yourself so as not to awaken envy, you are managing the emotions of others at the cost of your life, sacrificing your flight to spare others a discomfort that is theirs to resolve. And that doesn't help them, and it consumes you. You don't have to make yourself small so that no one envies you. You have to let yourself grow, and let each one walk their path with whatever it awakens in them.

I want you to feel the weight of this, because it is the weight you have been carrying without knowing it. Imagine what it is to have within you a greatness, a potential, a flight, and to keep it folded, hidden, contained, your whole life, out of fear that by opening it you would be left alone. Imagine the dull sadness of someone who makes herself small on purpose, who keeps her shine so as not to outshine, who lives below herself so as not to disturb. It isn't humility, it is self-amputation, it is cutting off a part of yourself to fit into a space that, after all, was never made to contain you. And that amputation, so habitual, you perhaps already take for modesty, for being "a simple person," for not liking to stand out. But beneath the modesty there is a contained woman, and containment, unlike humility, is not a virtue, it is a wound.

I want to stop here a moment, because there is a point where this fear stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If the fear of standing out causes you an anguish that doesn't pass, if there is a persistent anxiety whenever you approach growing, if you recognize a deep terror of being rejected or abandoned that paralyzes you, or if there are old wounds of real exclusion, of having been put out, mocked, left behind, that still tighten your chest today, then this deserves to be cared for with support. There are fears of separation that were engraved by painful experiences of rejection, and that aren't resolved only by reading nor by willpower. Saying so is the most honest way of caring for you: the understanding of this book opens the door, but there are wounds of belonging, especially when they touch the primitive fear of being abandoned, that heal better when accompanied, with someone to hold you while you learn that growing no longer costs you love.

Let me tell you where this usually comes from with most force. Sometimes it is a child who realized that, in the family, standing out generated problems: jealousy among siblings, comparisons, a father or a mother who needed nobody to shine too much, or who felt threatened by the shine of their children. Sometimes it is someone who saw what happened to whoever in the family or in the group dared to rise: they were talked about, envied, pushed away, treated as a traitor. Sometimes it is the experience of a milieu where "getting above yourself" was the worst thing you could do, where equality was the sacred rule, where whoever rose was cut down. In any of these cases, the lesson engraved itself the same way: standing out costs me my own, and my own are worth more than any height I could reach. And out of love, out of fear, you stayed on the ground, with them, with your wings folded.

## 5. Growing isn't abandoning

If there is one phrase I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it with care, because it dismantles the fear that binds you: growing isn't abandoning. Going further doesn't mean leaving anyone behind. Rising doesn't separate you from those you love, unless you choose that it does. You can grow immensely, expand, go far beyond the level of your group, and remain deeply connected to your own, present, one of them. The separation you feared was never a necessary consequence of growing. It was a confusion between two different things: going further, and going away.

Notice that there is a difference between the separation you fear and the differentiation that life asks for, and seeing it frees you. To separate would be to cut the bond, to move away in heart, to stop loving, and that, yes, would be a loss. But growing doesn't require that, it requires differentiation: becoming yourself, with your path, your size, your height, distinct from that of your equals, without ceasing to belong nor to love. To differentiate yourself is not to separate yourself. It is to stop being a copy of the group and to become an individual, and that is, in fact, the sign of a maturity, not of a betrayal. You confused differentiation with separation, being-true-to-yourself with being-true-to-them, and so you stayed undifferentiated, equal, contained, so as not to separate. But you can be very different from your own and deeply one of them at the same time, because mature belonging doesn't ask that you be equal, it asks that you love. To differentiate yourself is, deep down, the gift you give the group: showing that one can be fully who one is without ceasing to belong, opening to each one the same freedom.

Notice the distinction, because it frees you. Going further is a matter of height: you grow, you prosper, you expand, you reach places your own people didn't reach. Going away is a matter of heart: you move away, you forget, you look down from above, you cut the bond. And they are completely independent things. You can go very far without going away: rising and remaining close, prospering and remaining present, growing and continuing to love your own exactly as before. And you can go away without going far at all: there are those who move away from their own without having grown at all, out of pride, out of hurt, out of distance of heart. What hurts your own isn't your height, it is your abandonment. And you can have all the height in the world without abandoning anyone, if you keep your heart connected.

And I want to show you how this connects to money and to prosperity, which is the theme of this collection. Your brake isn't only about status or accomplishment, it is also, very concretely, about money. You don't let yourself earn far more than your own, have far more than your group, prosper above the level of those around you. And so, without knowing it, you regulate your income, your material success, your financial expansion, by the measure of the group, and you brake precisely when you would begin to stand out. You can have all the talent and all the opportunity to prosper greatly, and keep yourself, year after year, at your own people's level, not out of incapacity, but out of a loyalty that puts an invisible ceiling on your bank account, on your business, on your career. Prosperity lives precisely above that ceiling, on the other side of the line where you would begin to have more than your own. And as long as horizontal loyalty keeps that line, you can be capable of anything and remain leveled downward, faithful to a group measure that costs you, in silence, a whole lifetime of unlived expansion.

I'll say it another way, deeper. What binds a person to their own was never equality of level. It was love, shared history, presence, care. Two siblings who take very different paths in life, one who rises greatly and another who doesn't, can remain deeply siblings, if the love between them doesn't depend on their being equal. Two friends who prosper in different ways can remain friends, if the friendship isn't a pact to keep at the same level. Mature belonging doesn't require equality, it requires love. And love isn't measured in levels, in achievements, in who has more or less. It is measured in presence, in care, in continuing to be there. You can rise and continue to be there. That is what growing without abandoning means.

And here is the inversion that frees, and I want you to feel it: staying small doesn't serve your own. You keep yourself at the group's level thinking that this way you honor them, respect them, don't abandon them. But notice: your smallness enlarges no one. It doesn't give more to your own, doesn't make them rise, doesn't add anything to them. It only diminishes you, and deprives the group of something that could be precious: someone who went further and who, from the height, can extend a hand, open a path, show that it is possible. Your expansion, far from abandoning your own, can be precisely what inspires them, pulls them, shows them a path. Staying small out of loyalty doesn't help the group. Growing with your heart connected, that, yes, can be the greatest gift you give them.

The awakening, when it comes, doesn't come as an idea, it comes as a deep permission. It is the moment in which you feel, on the inside, even if only for an instant, that you can go further without losing your own. That growing isn't going to expel you from the group of those who truly love you. That your expansion and your belonging aren't enemies. It is an instant that can bring relief and tears, because it undoes a lifetime's fear, and because you feel, perhaps for the first time, that you don't have to choose between flying and belonging, that you can have both things, that you always could, and that the ceiling that contained you was you yourself holding it, out of a fear that, after all, didn't correspond to the truth of your own people's love.

## 6. Expanding without guilt

There is a confusion at the center of everything, and I want to undo it with care, because it is from it that the brake is born: you confused expanding with betraying. As if going further than your own were, in itself, an act of disloyalty, a way of leaving them behind, of raising yourself at their expense, of breaking the pact of equality. And it isn't. There is an enormous difference between expanding and betraying, and it is in that difference that your freedom lives.

Notice the difference. To betray your own would be to turn your back on them, to despise them for having stayed, to forget where you came from, to use your height to diminish them. That, yes, would be disloyalty. But to expand, simply to grow, to go as far as you can go, that is no betrayal at all, it is the most natural thing in the world, it is what life asks of those who have more to give. A tree that grows taller doesn't betray the trees beside it. A bird that flies higher doesn't abandon the flock if it keeps flying with it. To grow is in the nature of things, and there is nothing disloyal in fulfilling your potential, as long as the heart stays connected to those you love. The guilt you feel on expanding isn't the proof that you are betraying someone, it is only the mark of an old fear confusing height with abandonment.

And I want to speak to you of what it means, in practice, to expand without guilt. It means letting yourself grow as far as life takes you, without pulling the brake at the threshold of more. It means stopping minimizing your achievements in front of your own, stopping making yourself smaller so as not to disturb them, stopping hiding your shine. It means being able to share good news without apologizing for it. It means not refusing opportunities only because they would put you ahead of the group. It is to stop managing your height according to the comfort of others, and to begin to grow freely, trusting that those who truly love you rejoice in your flight, and that those who don't rejoice in it perhaps never loved you, but your smallness.

I want to warn you of a resistance that will appear, because it is subtle: you will feel that expanding without guilt is becoming arrogant, conceited, one of those people who think they are better than others. And here there is an important distinction. There are, in fact, those who grow and use their height to look down on others, to feel superior, to despise those who stayed, and that is ugly, and you, precisely for fearing it so much, will never be like that. But the opposite of thinking yourself superior isn't making yourself small, it is growing with your heart connected, without contempt for anyone. You can go very far and not think yourself better than anyone at all, because the height you reach doesn't make you superior, it is only your path, as worthy as anyone's, neither more nor less. Expanding without guilt isn't thinking yourself better. It is letting yourself be who you can be, without that meaning anything about the value of others.

I am not going to give you a method for expanding without guilt, because it would be one more task, and we would be feeding your tendency to complicate what asks only for permission. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because undoing a lifetime's loyalty isn't done in a page. But the principle fits in a sentence, and it is this: your height diminishes no one, and your smallness enlarges no one. Grow, go further, expand, keeping your heart connected to those you love, and discover, slowly, that the fear that growing would cost you your own was only that, a fear, and that on the other side of it there is a much larger life, lived without the brake, and with your own still there, the ones who truly were yours.

## 7. Going ahead and staying connected

There is an image I want to leave you, because it is the destination of this crossing: going ahead and staying connected. Not going ahead by abandoning the group, which is what you feared. Not staying in the group out of fear of going ahead, which is what you did. But a third thing, the only one that frees: advancing, growing, going further, keeping alive the connection with your own, carrying them in your heart, present for them from whatever height you reach.

Notice what this resolves. Your fear told you that you had to choose between expanding and belonging, between your flight and your own, between going further and remaining one of them. And that choice was impossible, because you wanted both things, and any renunciation cost you too much. Going ahead and staying connected dissolves the choice: you no longer have to abandon anyone to grow, nor to stay small to belong. You go ahead, and you stay connected. Your height doesn't cut the bond, if you don't cut it. And you discover that the belonging you feared losing, the real kind, travels with you to any height, because it never depended on your level, it depended on your love, and that you can keep intact while you rise.

And I want to show you a way of taking your own with you that doesn't cost you the flight: the generosity of someone who reached higher. When you go ahead with your heart connected, you gain something you didn't have when you were leveled downward: the possibility of extending a hand. Of opening doors, of sharing what you learned, of creating opportunities for your own, of being, in the group, the one who went ahead and came back to pull the others. Notice the inversion: while you keep yourself small out of loyalty, you have nothing more to give the group, you are just one more at the same level. When you grow, you gain the ability to serve your own in a way your smallness would never allow. Your height, far from abandoning them, can become a resource for them, a hand extended from the height. Staying small gives nothing to anyone. Growing with your heart connected can give a great deal, precisely because from the height you reach what from the ground you couldn't reach.

And I want to speak to you of a beautiful way of going ahead, that perhaps you have never considered: going ahead to open a path. When a person from a group grows, prospers, goes further, and does it with their heart connected, without despising those who stayed, they become, unwittingly, a beacon, a living proof that it is possible, an open door for the others. The first in the family to study, the first in the neighborhood to start a business, the first in the group to dare more, opens a breach through which the others can then pass. Your expansion, far from abandoning your own, can be what shows them that they too can, what pulls them upward instead of leaving them behind. Going ahead, in this sense, isn't separating from the group, it is serving it in a way that only those who dare to rise can: by opening a path.

I want to leave you a question that can serve as a compass, because it cuts straight to the root: if I knew, with all certainty, that growing wouldn't cost me the love of those who truly love me, how far would I go? Ask yourself this question when the brake tightens. The answer that appears, almost always much further than the place where you usually stop, is the measure of your true flight, what you would do if the fear of separation didn't speak. And the distance between that place and the place where you brake is, exactly, what your horizontal loyalty costs you, in life unlived, in flight unflown. I don't ask you to go all the way there right away, if it gives you vertigo. I ask you to know where it is, so that at least you stop confusing the group's ceiling with the ceiling of your abilities. One thing is how far you can go. Another is how far the fear of separating lets you go. And distinguishing the two is beginning to release the brake.

There is a fear you may have, and I want to meet it: the fear that, if you grow, your own will move away, envy you, treat you as a traitor, and that you will be, in fact, alone at the top. And I want to be honest: it can happen, with some. There are those who can't bear your expansion, who feel diminished by it, who move away. But notice what that reveals: not that you should have stayed small, but that that bond depended on your smallness, and a bond that depends on keeping yourself small isn't love, it is a pact of mutual containment. It hurts to lose those bonds, but it is a liberation, because it shows you where your belonging was conditional. And at the same time, you will discover who stays: those who genuinely rejoice in your flight, those who loved you and not your equality, and with those the bond deepens, becomes truer, because it withstood the test of your growth.

I want you to think about what you teach those who watch you live, because perhaps it will move you more than anything. If you have children, or people who grow up looking at you, notice what you show them about growing. If they see you contain yourself, brake, make yourself small so as not to clash, refuse your own flight out of loyalty, they are learning from you that standing out is dangerous, that growing costs belonging, that the safe thing is to keep to the group's measure. And it is likely that they will fold their own wings, as you fold yours. The most liberating thing you can do for those who come after you isn't to give them one more example of containment. It is to show them a woman who dares to be the size she is, who flies without guilt, who grows without ceasing to love. Sometimes a woman releases her brake, at last, not only for herself, but to authorize those she loves to release theirs too, so that the flight, instead of the fear, is what is passed on.

And I want to tell you something about the group you fear losing, because perhaps it will surprise you. A healthy group doesn't need anyone to keep themselves small. On the contrary, it rejoices in the growth of its own, takes pride in whoever goes further, celebrates each one's flight as a victory for all. The groups that demand that nobody stand out, that cut down whoever rises, that level everything downward, are wounded groups, bound to an insecurity that confuses equality with love. And if your group is like that, that is a real pain, but it isn't a reason to keep yourself small, it is information about the limitations of that group. You can love them and grow just the same, and you can, by growing, either inspire them to release themselves too, or discover that your true belonging is elsewhere, with people who rejoice in your flight instead of fearing it.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that perhaps you don't even remember wanting, so used are you to containing yourself: the joy of flying without a brake. Of growing as far as you can, without the ceiling of loyalty. Of using all your potential, without folding it to fit. Of sharing your achievements without guilt, with those who rejoice in them. Of being, at last, the size you are, without shrinking for anyone's comfort. That joy is your right, as much yours as anyone's, and it is also, notice, a gift to the world, because the world needs women who dare to be the size they are, who don't fold their flight, who show those who come behind that it is possible to go further without ceasing to love.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything all at once, and it doesn't release the brake overnight. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling like a traitor, or arrogant, for desiring and for daring to go further.

Now you know that you brake at the threshold of more, and that this brake isn't a lack of ability, it is a horizontal loyalty, to your equals. You know that this loyalty is born of the deep need to belong to the group, and of the invisible message that rising too much costs belonging. You know that the shadow that governs you is separation, the fear of being left alone at the top, expelled from the pack. You know, and this was the hardest, that beneath everything is the fear of ceasing to belong, and that your smallness, after all, doesn't guarantee your belonging, it only diminishes you. You know that growing isn't abandoning, that going further and going away are different things, and that what binds your own to you was never equality, it was love. And you know that you can go ahead and stay connected, expand without guilt, fly without a brake, carrying your own in your heart.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it sums up everything. Imagine a group of people walking together through a valley, all at the same pace, and you, who have legs to run, holding back your pace so as not to get ahead, so as not to leave them behind, so as to stay among them. And there is a mountain ahead, and you could climb it, see what is on the other side, and come back to tell them, or extend them a hand to climb too. But you stay in the valley, at the group's pace, with the mountain unclimbed, because climbing seemed to abandon them. The crossing I propose to you isn't to leave the group in the valley and disappear up the mountain. It is to climb, with your heart connected to them, and from the height to call out, to show the path, to extend a hand. Because going ahead, when the bond is kept, isn't abandoning the group, it is opening for it a path that no one would open if everyone stayed, out of loyalty, standing in the same place.

And I want you to take one last distinction, because it protects everything else. To release the brake isn't to become indifferent to your own, competitive, someone who tramples others to rise. It is the opposite: it is to grow with your heart so connected that your flight never needs to hurt anyone. You continue to love your own, to be present, to belong. You only stop paying for that belonging with your smallness. And a woman who grows with her heart connected isn't a traitor to the group, she is, often, the best thing that happened to the group: the living proof that one can fly without ceasing to love, the door that opens so that others dare too.

You know, deep down, the phrase that holds this whole book together: I can go further without leaving anyone behind.

Seeing this was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't made by reading, it is made by releasing, slowly, a lifetime's brake, and by daring, time after time, to go further without cutting the bond. Not all at once, not without fear. But a little further each time, until the group's ceiling stops being yours, and your only limit becomes, at last, the one life wants to give you.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about money, nor will it teach you to compete. It is a space to look at the way you prevent value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the most subtle: braking at the threshold of more, keeping yourself small out of loyalty to your equals, confusing growing with abandoning. There, the crossing is to undo horizontal loyalty, to release the fear of separation, and to relearn to expand without guilt, to go ahead without cutting the bond, to fly without the group's ceiling. Not to stop belonging, but so that your belonging stops demanding that you stay small.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There is a mother there who levels her children downward, who doesn't let any of them stand out so as not to hurt the others, who keeps them all equal to keep the peace. You do the same thing, but with yourself: you are the daughter who levels herself downward, who doesn't let herself stand out from the group, who contains herself so as not to break the equality. One levels the others, the other levels herself. But deep down it is the same journey: to discover that true love doesn't need anyone to stay small, that one can grow without hurting, stand out without betraying, and that containing your own flight was never love, it was fear.

If you feel this is the moment, you'll find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't yet the moment, that's okay, and there is no group here to whom you must answer for your reading. What you have read is already yours, and stays with you, waiting, without hurry, for the day you decide to release the brake and fly as far as you can.

And before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing, and it will be, for you, among the hardest there is, and that is why I ask it of you. Today, or in these days, let yourself go a little further in a place where you usually brake. Share an achievement without minimizing it. Accept an opportunity that will put you ahead. Don't shrink, for once, for the comfort of the group. Notice the fear of separating, the guilt of standing out, and advance despite it. Because it is in that going further, with your heart connected, that a lifetime's brake begins to release, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that growing was never abandoning anyone, it was, after all, your way of flying and of, from the height, continuing to love.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
