# The mother who did not feel

**For the mother to whom love did not arrive the way they promised, and who carries in silence the shame of not feeling what she should.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This is a book written without any judgment, about one of the most silenced experiences of motherhood: that of not feeling what they promised us we would feel. I want to tell you, before anything else, something I will repeat throughout these pages: there is an enormous difference between not feeling what you expected and suffering in a way that asks for help. If you are living a deep sadness that does not pass, anxiety that incapacitates you, intrusive thoughts that frighten you, or thoughts of harming yourself or harming your baby, this is not matter for a book, it is matter for clinical support, with urgency and without shame. Seek a doctor, a psychologist, a helpline. You are not alone, and there is help, and you deserve to receive it today.*

---

## 1. The thing you never told anyone

There is a thing you may never have told anyone. A thing you keep in the deepest and most closed-off place in you, afraid that, if you said it out loud, someone would see in you what you yourself are afraid of being. The thing is this: you looked at your child, and you did not feel what they told you you would feel.

It may have been right at the birth, in that instant they had sold you as the most sublime of your life, and that for you was confusion, exhaustion, or a strange emptiness. It may have been in the days that followed, looking at the cradle and waiting to feel what wasn't coming. It may have been later, weeks or months afterward, realizing that you went on doing everything right on the outside and feeling hollow inside. It may even be, if you have more than one child, the painful realization that with one you felt and with another you didn't, and the guilt of a difference you didn't choose. Whenever it happened, and whatever your story, what matters is that there was, or there is, a mismatch between what they told you you would feel and what you really felt, and that this mismatch left you alone with a question that haunts you.

They told you it would be the greatest emotion of your life. That love would arrive like a wave, in the instant they placed him in your arms. That you would look at that face and feel, all at once, that everything made sense, that you would have given your life for him without hesitating, that you had never loved like this. They prepared you for an overwhelming, instant, total love. And when the moment came, you felt, perhaps, something else. Tiredness. Strangeness. Fear. A distance you couldn't name. An emptiness where the explosion of love was supposed to be. Or simply nothing, a confused and frightening nothing, as if you were looking at a baby who was yours but whom you felt to be a stranger.

And from then on, a silent conclusion settled in, one that may have been with you for months, or for years: there is something wrong with me. What kind of mother doesn't feel love for her own child? What kind of woman looks at her baby and feels distance instead of devotion? There must be, you think, something broken in you, some deep flaw, some incapacity to love that makes you different from all the other mothers, who seem to have felt at once what you didn't feel. And that conclusion is so painful, so shameful, that you hide it from everyone, and in hiding it, it grows, and becomes a secret that isolates you in the middle of your own motherhood.

And I want to name the concrete forms this not-feeling takes, because seeing them described eases the sense that you are a unique and unspeakable case. Perhaps you look at your baby sleeping and feel that you should be melting, and what there is, is tiredness and a strange indifference. Perhaps you hold him and he still seems to you a little foreign, someone who was handed to you but whom you don't feel entirely yours. Perhaps you take care of everything with perfection, and inside you feel that you are playing the role of mother instead of being it. Perhaps you see other mothers talk about their child with shining eyes and feel a tightness, because your shine doesn't come, or doesn't come yet. Perhaps you love one child and another costs you more, and that seems to you an unforgivable betrayal. None of this, hear me well, none of this makes you a bad mother. They are real experiences, lived by many women, and the fact that they cause you so much suffering is, in itself, proof that you care deeply.

I want to tell you, before any other thing, and I want you to read it slowly: you are not a monster, you are not broken, and you are not alone in this. Far more mothers than you imagine have lived exactly what you live, this not-feeling what they expected, this distance at the start, this strangeness before their own child. Almost none of them speak of it, because the taboo is enormous, because the shame is crushing, because it seems the most terrible confession a mother can make. And so each one suffers alone, thinking herself unique, thinking herself defective, when in truth she is living a human experience far more common than the silence around it allows one to guess.

I want also to tell you why this silence is so dangerous, beyond being painful. When a mother hides that she doesn't feel what's expected, she hides with it everything else: the doubts, the difficulties, and, in the worst cases, the suffering that could be cared for. The secret of not-feeling becomes a cloak that also covers the signs that something more serious may be happening, because admitting any part of it seems to open the door to the whole confession. And so, mothers who needed help keep quiet, out of shame, for months, sometimes years, suffering alone what could have been eased. That is why breaking the silence is so important, and it is one of the things this book most wants to do for you: to give you permission to say, to name, not to hide, because it is in the hiding that the pain grows and the help doesn't come, and it is in the saying that everything begins to be able to change.

This book was written for that thing you never said. To look at it with you, without judgment, without horror, without telling you everything is fine when perhaps it isn't, but also without condemning you for something that is no crime at all. We are going to look, together, at what happens when love doesn't arrive the way they promised, at where the shame you carry comes from, and at the most important distinction of all, the one that can change everything: the difference between not feeling what you expected, which is common and has a path, and suffering in a way that asks, with urgency, for another kind of help. But it begins here, with this thing few mothers hear: what you feel, or what you don't feel, does not make you a bad mother. And you are not, at all, alone.

## 2. What they promised you you would feel

To understand your shame, we have to look at the promise that created it. Because your pain isn't born only of what you feel or don't feel. It is born, in large part, of the distance between your real experience and a promise that was made to you, and that almost no one questions.

The promise is this: that a mother's love is instant, total, and automatic. That in the moment you see your child, an ancestral instinct activates and fills you with a love that needs no time, no knowing, no construction. That it is enough to give birth in order to love, that the bond comes for free, with the milk, with the hormones, with the instinct. This promise is everywhere: in films, in stories, in the carefully edited testimonies of other mothers, in the images of births where the woman weeps with happiness in the exact second the baby arrives. You grew up hearing this promise, and you believed it, as everyone believes it.

And when your experience was different, you didn't call the promise into question. You called yourself into question. Because the promise is so universal, so repeated, so taken for granted, that the possibility of its being false, or at least incomplete, doesn't even occur to you. What occurs to you is that you failed, that something in you didn't work the way it should, that you are the defective exception to a rule everyone else fulfills. The promise stays intact, and you stay carrying the guilt of not having fulfilled it, without ever questioning whether the promise, after all, was true.

And there is an added cruelty in this promise, which is the way it is reinforced every day by the images that surround you. You open social media and you see radiant mothers, perfect babies, captions about a love they never imagined possible. You see the first seconds of life shared as moments of pure ecstasy. And you compare that, which is the edited, chosen, filtered version of other people's experience, with your raw experience, real, from within, with all the doubts and tirednesses and absences. It is a comparison you always lose, because you compare the polished outside of others with your inside in raw flesh. No one posts the photo of the moment they looked at the baby and felt distance. No one captions the night they wondered what they had done. And so each mother who doesn't feel what's expected thinks herself the only one, surrounded by mothers who seem to have felt everything, when many of those mothers hide, behind the posted smile, the same doubt you hide.

Well, I want to tell you that the promise is, at best, incomplete, and at worst, a lie that does enormous harm. The truth is that a mother's love, for very many women, does not arrive instant. It is built. It grows with time, with knowing, with caring, with the days and the nights, with bonding slowly to a being who, at the start, was a stranger. For some mothers love arrives like the promised wave, and good for them. But for many others, perhaps most, it comes more slowly, in stages, sometimes only weeks or months later, and that is not a failure, it is simply another form, equally valid, in which love happens. The problem was never that your love took time. The problem was that they promised you it shouldn't take time.

It is worth understanding where this promise comes from, because knowing that it has a history helps to take away its force as absolute truth. The idea of instant and total maternal love is, in large part, a relatively recent and cultural construction, not a law of nature. There were times and places where it was known that the bond is built, where a woman was not expected to love wildly a baby she barely knew, where there was a whole village sharing the care and no one demanded of a single mother the totality of love from the first second. The promise of automatic love serves many things, it sells products, it sustains ideals, it relieves society of caring for mothers by placing the whole weight of the bond on them. What it does not serve is the truth of the experience of very many real women. Knowing this does not oblige you to stop desiring the overwhelming love. But it takes away the guilt of not feeling it at the appointed hour, because the appointed hour was invented, it is not yours.

And there is a specific harm in this false promise, which is precisely what brought you here: it robs you of the possibility of speaking. Because if everyone feels immediate love, how do you say that you didn't? To whom? To say it would be to confess the unspeakable, to expose yourself to the horror of others, to confirm that you are different, defective. And so you keep quiet, and in keeping quiet the shame settles in, and the shame grows in the dark, and makes everything worse, because now you carry not only the not-feeling, you carry also the secret, the isolation, the certainty that no one can know. The false promise didn't only make you feel wanting. It condemned you to feel that wanting alone.

## 3. Loving can be an act before it is a feeling

I want to offer you now a distinction that, for many mothers, is one of the greatest liberations there is. Read it slowly, because it can change the way you see yourself. The distinction is this: love and feeling are not exactly the same thing. And it is possible, more than possible, common, to already be loving long before you feel it.

Think of what you've been doing since your child arrived. You get up at night, exhausted, to feed him. You watch his breathing while he sleeps. You protect him, you worry, you attend to the smallest sign that something isn't right. You put his needs ahead of yours, day after day, night after night. You care, even without feeling the wave of love you expected. And here is the question that changes everything: what is this, if not love? What is getting up exhausted to care for someone, protecting that someone, devoting yourself to that someone, if not love in action, love in the form of an act, even before love arrives in the form of emotion?

And there is something here that changes the perspective: your child, above all while small, doesn't feel your inner emotion, he feels your care. He doesn't know whether, inside, you feel a wave or a distance. What he receives, what builds security in him, is the fact of being attended to, fed, rocked, protected, answered. It is the consistent care, and not the emotional temperature of your heart, that tells him he is loved and that the world is safe. This means that, even if you don't yet feel the wave, you are, with your care, giving your child exactly what he most needs. You are not depriving him of anything because your feeling hasn't yet arrived, as long as you care for him, and you do care. This truth frees you from one of the heaviest guilts: the guilt of thinking that your child is being harmed by your lack of feeling. He isn't, if he is cared for. And he is.

Because love is not only what you feel. It is also, and perhaps above all, what you do. There is a love that is feeling, the warm emotion, the wave, the tenderness that tightens the chest. And there is a love that is act, that is care, that is presence, that is devotion, that is being there and doing and protecting, even when the emotion isn't there yet. And the second is not a lesser love waiting for the first. It is love, whole, true, and very often it is where one begins. The feeling, that one, comes afterward, very often, precisely from the act. One loves by caring, and by caring, slowly, one begins to feel.

It may help you to think of something you know from another part of your life. Think of two people who meet and who, over time, become friends, or fall in love. The deep love between them does not exist in the first instant, in the first handshake. It is built, encounter by encounter, in the knowing, in the sharing, in the being together. And no one thinks there was a failure because love didn't explode in the first second, because we know that, between adults, love takes time to grow. Why, with a baby, would it be different? You and your child are also getting to know each other. He is, at the start, literally a stranger, a new being you never saw before that day. That love for him should take time to grow, that it should be built in the mutual knowing of the days and the nights, is no less natural than in any other human relationship. The difference is that no one told you that with a child it was also like this, and made you believe it should be instant, and blamed you for it not being so.

I want you to receive this with care, because it can ease a shame of years: if you have cared for your child, if you have devoted yourself to him, if you get up for him and protect him and attend to him, then you are already loving him, even if the emotion hasn't yet arrived, even if you still feel distance, even if you look at him and don't feel the wave. Your love is not absent. It is happening, every day, in the most concrete and most real form there is, the form of care. And the fact that you worry so much about not feeling enough is, itself, a proof of love, because to someone who didn't love, this would simply be a matter of indifference.

There is a word psychology uses that may give you ground: the bond is not a switch, it is a process. It doesn't turn on all at once, at the birth, in an instant. It is woven, thread by thread, in each gesture of care, in each time you answer the crying, in each look, in each night. And there are factors that can slow that process without it being your fault nor a lack of love: a difficult or traumatic birth, pain, extreme exhaustion, lack of support, health difficulties of yours or of the baby, a pregnancy lived under stress or fear, a life of yours that never had room to feel. When the bond takes time, there are almost always reasons, and none of them is "you don't love your child." The process is simply taking the time it needs, in the conditions it has. And processes, unlike switches, go on happening, above all when we give them care instead of pressure.

And I want to add a word about the gestures of care you may undervalue precisely because you don't feel them accompanied by emotion. When you get up at night without wanting to, when you care on autopilot, when you do everything you have to do but out of duty and not out of rapture, you are tempted to discredit those gestures, to say "but I do it without feeling, it doesn't count." It counts, and it counts a great deal. Perhaps it counts even more than the easy gestures of someone in ecstasy, because to care when you don't feel the wave, to care out of choice and out of duty and out of commitment, is a harder form of love and, in a way, a deeper one. Anyone cares well when they feel themselves melting. To care well when you feel nothing, or when you feel the opposite, that is fidelity, that is character, that is love in its barest form. Don't undervalue your care because it doesn't come wrapped in emotion. That faithful care is, perhaps, the most loving thing there is in you.

But I have to add, carefully, something important, so you don't turn this truth into a trap. Knowing that you love in act, even without feeling, is meant to ease your shame, not to silence you if you are suffering. There is a difference between a mother who cares with devotion while the feeling hasn't yet arrived, and who is, apart from that, functioning, living, and a mother who cares in the midst of deep suffering, of an emptiness that swallows her, of an anguish that doesn't pass. For the first, this truth about love in act is a liberation. For the second, it is also true, but it isn't enough, because her suffering asks for more than understanding, it asks for support. We will return to this distinction, because it is the heart of the safety of this book. But keep, for now, the relief: to care is already to love, and you, if you care, already love.

And I want to add something you may never have considered: this same principle, that love is first an act and only afterward a feeling, is true in almost all lasting human relationships. Couples who have loved each other for decades don't live in a permanent ecstasy; they love each other above all in the acts, in the mutual care, in the fidelity of the everyday, and the feeling comes and goes, on top of that foundation of acts. Parents love adolescent children whom, in phases, they can barely stand, and they love them in the act of going on being there, even when the warm feeling is buried under irritation. Mature love, in any relationship, rests far more on commitment and on the act than on emotion, which is volatile by nature. Knowing this takes motherhood out of an impossible demand and gives it back to the condition of any human love: that of being, above all, a choice to care, repeated every day, within which the feeling flowers, when it flowers, but which does not depend on it in order to be real.

## 4. The shame of the absence

We arrive at the heart of this book, and I want to ask you to breathe, because we are going to touch exactly the place that hurts most. And what I'm going to say may surprise you: the problem, most of the time, is not what you feel or don't feel for your child. The problem is the shame. The shadow you carry is not the absence of love. It is the shame of that supposed absence.

Notice this distinction carefully, because it changes everything. You think your problem is not feeling. But thousands of mothers don't feel immediate love, and many cross through it without its becoming a wound, because they don't turn it into a verdict about who they are. What makes not-feeling a deep wound is not the not-feeling in itself. It is the shame you glue to it, the interpretation that this proves you are a bad mother, monstrous, defective, unworthy. It is that shame, and not the absent feeling, that corrodes you, that isolates you, that makes everything unbearable. The feeling might arrive, with time. The shame is what won't let it, because it traps you in a judgment about yourself that poisons everything.

And shame has a cruel characteristic: it feeds on silence and grows in the dark. The more you hide it, the more it convinces you that there is something in you so terrible it cannot be said. The more you keep quiet about it, the more alone you feel, and the more certain you become that you are the only one, the exception, the aberration. Shame lives on not being said. And it is exactly for this reason that saying it, even if only to one trusted person, even if only to a professional, even if only to these pages, is the beginning of its dissolution. Because when shame is said, and from the other side comes not the horror it promised, but understanding, it begins to lose its power. What frees you is not first to feel the love. It is first to be able to say, without dying of shame, that you haven't yet felt it.

I want to help you see where this shame comes from, because it has roots that are not yours. It comes from a culture that made maternal love an untouchable sacred thing, and that therefore turned any failure in that love into the worst of sins. A mother can admit tiredness, can admit difficulty, but she cannot admit that she doesn't feel, because that touches the most protected myth of all, the myth of unconditional and automatic maternal love. And so the woman who doesn't feel what's expected is left with no place for her truth, condemned to a silence that isolates her. Your shame is not, then, only yours. It is the shame that a whole culture pours onto whoever dares not to correspond to one of its most cherished myths. Knowing this doesn't take away the pain, but it takes away the guilt of feeling it, because the shame you carry was imposed on you, it was not deserved, and what is imposed can, slowly, be given back to whoever imposed it.

I want you to hear this clearly: being able to say "I'm not feeling what I should" is not a confession of monstrousness. It is an act of courage and of honesty, and it is, paradoxically, an act of love, because only someone who cares about the relationship torments herself so much over its absence. An indifferent mother feels no shame at not feeling. Your shame is, itself, the proof that you care deeply, that you wanted to feel, that that connection matters to you. See what this means: your shame, which seemed to you the proof that you don't love, is in truth the proof of the opposite. Only someone who deeply desires the presence of the feeling suffers so much over its absence.

I want to give you a first place where the shame can be said in safety, because saying it at random, to the wrong person, can hurt more. Not everyone will know how to receive this truth without horror, because the taboo is strong, and so choosing whom you say it to matters. It could be a professional, a psychologist, someone trained to listen without judging. It could be a single friend whom you trust deeply, preferably one who perhaps has also felt the same and whom you, in opening up, discover you are not alone with. It could be, at first, only these pages, or a diary where you write what you don't dare say out loud. What matters is that the truth come out from inside you, where it rots, into a place where it can be looked at with compassion. You don't have to shout it to the world nor expose it to whoever would judge you. A safe place is enough, just one, where it stops being a secret and begins to be merely a truth, said and held.

To let this truth exist, instead of hiding it, is the turning point of this book. Not the truth that you don't feel, but the truth that you don't have to be ashamed to admit it. In the moment you stop condemning yourself for not feeling, and begin to look at it with the same compassion with which you would look at a friend in the same situation, the shame begins to loosen. And with it loosened, there is room, finally, for what was underneath: either a love that can grow in its own time, or a suffering that can, at last, be cared for. Both things only gain room when the shame is set down.

## 5. When not feeling is a sign

This is the most important chapter of this book, and I want you to read it with all your attention, because it is here that your safety lives, and your child's. Until now we have spoken of not feeling the expected love as a common experience, one that has a path, that very often resolves with time and with the setting down of shame. But there is a boundary, and it is vital to know how to recognize it, because on the other side of it we are no longer talking about the same thing, and the answer is completely different.

On one side of the boundary is what we have been speaking of: I didn't feel immediate love, I feel distant, I feel little, I find my child strange, the bond hasn't arrived yet. This, while painful, is common, and in most cases has a path, with time, with support, with the easing of shame. On the other side of the boundary is another thing, more serious, which is not a lack of feeling, it is suffering, and which asks, with urgency, for clinical support. And I want to name the signs with all clarity, without circling around them, because knowing them can be decisive.

I want to be very clear about why, here, clarity matters more than delicacy. In almost all of this book, my tone is one of welcome, of taking away weight, of showing that what you feel is common and has a path. But at this point, I change register on purpose, because there are situations in which welcome without referral can be dangerous. If I, to comfort you, told you that everything passes with time, even when the signs are of clinical suffering, I would be failing you in the worst way, keeping you far from the help you need. So, in this chapter, I prefer to risk seeming alarmist rather than leave you without the information that may be decisive. If the signs I'm going to describe are not yours, good, read them and move on, relieved. But if they are, I want this to be the chapter that makes you seek help, and I would a thousand times rather have been too clear than have comforted you too little.

Pay attention, and take seriously, if there is a persistent suffering that doesn't pass, a deep sadness that settled in and won't lift. If you can no longer function, do what the day asks, take care of yourself or your child. If you've lost the pleasure in everything, if nothing reaches you, if there is an anesthesia or a despair that won't let go. If you feel an anxiety that incapacitates you, that takes your breath, that won't let you stop. If you have intrusive thoughts, images or ideas that frighten you and that appear without your wanting them. And above all, above all, if there are thoughts of harming yourself, or of harming your baby, or that it would be better if you weren't here, or that your child would be better off without you. If any of these signs is present, then what you're living is not the common experience we spoke of. It is a suffering that needs evaluation and clinical support, and it needs it now.

I want to help you make this distinction, which from inside is difficult, with a simple question: it is not so much what you feel, it is how you are functioning and suffering. A mother can not feel the expected love and, apart from that, be functioning, sleeping when the baby lets her, finding pleasure in other things, having hope, living. That mother is living the common experience, and time and care tend to help her. Another mother may be sinking: she doesn't sleep even when she can, she feels pleasure in nothing, she lives in anguish or in despair, she can't function, she has thoughts that frighten her. That one is not living the common experience, she is in clinical suffering, and the boundary is not the sentence "I don't feel," it is the general state, the functioning, the depth and the persistence of the suffering. If, in reading this, you recognize that it is not only the feeling that is missing, it is that you are suffering in a way that takes over everything, then that is the most important information this book can give you: what you're living asks for support, and deserves it.

And here the book stops interpreting and begins to refer, without circling around, because there are moments when the most loving thing one can do is to say clearly: this is not solved with a book, nor with time, nor with willpower, nor alone. Postpartum depression is real, it is common, it is treatable, and it is not your fault nor your weakness, it is a health condition like any other, that happens to many women and that has treatment. Postpartum anxiety, intrusive thoughts, severe difficulties with bonding, all of this has a name, has an explanation, and has help. What you need, if you recognize yourself in these signs, is to seek a doctor, a psychologist, a psychiatrist, a motherhood helpline, today, and not to stay alone with this even one more day.

And I want to speak directly of the fear that perhaps keeps you from seeking help, because it is real and deserves an answer. It is the fear that, if you tell a professional you don't feel love, or that you have thoughts that frighten you, they will judge you a bad mother, or worse, that they will take your child away. I want to tell you that intrusive thoughts, those terrible images or ideas that appear without your wanting them and that horrify you, are, themselves, very often a known and treatable symptom, and they do not mean you will act on them, nor that you are dangerous, nor that you don't love, on the contrary, the horror you feel before those thoughts is the proof that they go against everything you are. Mental health professionals know these symptoms, know how to tell them apart, and are there to help, not to punish. Hiding out of fear is what truly prolongs the suffering. Saying it, to someone who knows how to listen, is what opens the door to relief. Your fear is understandable, but don't let it rob you of the help you deserve and that exists.

I want to tell you one last thing in this chapter, and I want you to keep it above everything else: seeking help does not take your child from you, does not prove you are a bad mother, is not a confession of incapacity. It is exactly the opposite. It is the act of a mother who loves enough to recognize that she needs support in order to be able to care, and to take care of herself. The professionals who help mothers in suffering are not there to judge or to take away, they are there to hold you up and to give you back, to yourself and to your child, the possibility of a healthy connection. If there is a single thing this book asks of you, it is this: if you recognize yourself in the signs of suffering, don't wait, don't keep quiet, don't endure alone. Seek help. You deserve to be cared for, and your child deserves a cared-for mother. Asking is the path, and it is an act of love, the greatest of all.

## 6. The mourning for the mother you imagined you would be

There is a hidden loss beneath all of this, and it is almost never named, and when we name it, it relieves. It is not the loss of your child, who is here, who is yours. It is the loss of an image: the image of the mother you imagined you would be.

During the pregnancy, and perhaps long before, you built a mother in your head. A mother who would look at the baby and melt, who would instinctively know what to do, who would feel that overwhelming love, who would be naturally patient, warm, in love with motherhood. That imagined mother was beautiful, and she was also a fantasy, assembled out of the promises that were made to you, out of the images you saw, out of the edited mothers you were shown. And when you became a mother in earnest, the real mother, you, was not that one. She felt other things, or didn't feel, doubted, grew tired, found things strange. And between the mother you imagined being and the mother you are, a distance opened that hurts.

There is an aspect of this mourning that is particularly cruel for those who didn't feel what was expected: the mourning for a certain innocence about yourself. Before you were a mother, perhaps you imagined yourself as someone capable of a total, instinctive love, without reservation. And the experience of not feeling what you expected confronted you not only with a mother different from the imagined one, but with a version of yourself you didn't know and that frightened you, one who could feel distance, ambivalence, even relief when she moved away from the baby. To know that part of you, which motherhood revealed, is also a loss, the loss of the simplest and most luminous image you had of yourself. And it is, at the same time, a door to a more truthful, more whole, less naive self-knowledge. You are not worse than you imagined. You are more complex, more human, more real. And a real mother, who knows herself in her shadows and not only in her lights, has far more to give a child than a fantasy that never existed.

That pain is a mourning, the mourning for the idealized mother you didn't get to be. And it is a legitimate mourning, that deserves to be felt, because to say goodbye to an image of ourselves, to a fantasy in which we invested so much, is a real loss. But, just like the mourning for the imagined child, this mourning is not a failure of yours nor a betrayal. It is a necessary stage, because while you cling to the mother you imagined being, and compare yourself with her, and condemn yourself for not being her, you can't see nor accept the mother you truly are. The idealized image casts a shadow over you, and keeps you from finding yourself.

There is, very often, beneath this mourning, an older story, and I want to touch it with the utmost care, because it helps to understand without ever explaining away the lack of love. Some women who don't feel what they expected for their children were, themselves, daughters who didn't feel fully received, loved, seen. A woman who didn't receive the holding she needed may find it harder, at the start, to know how to give a holding she never knew from within. Not because she doesn't love, but because the gesture of receiving and being received is, in part, strange to her. I say this with great care, and I want to be clear: this does not explain that you don't love your child, and it would be wrong and cruel to use your story to tell you that you don't love. Your story may help to understand why receiving this new being costs you more, why the bond asks more time of you, but the lack of immediate feeling is never the fault of a dynamic of your family nor proof that you can't love. It is only a clue, with tenderness, that perhaps you need to receive too, to be cared for too, so that the caring may flow from you with more ease.

And I want to tell you that to let go of the perfect mother you imagined is not to lower the bar nor to give up being a good mother. It is the opposite. It is to trade an impossible fantasy, with which you could never compete, for a real mother, possible, true, who can grow, learn, bond at her own pace, err and repair. The imagined mother was perfect and didn't exist. The real mother is you, with your doubts and your limits and your love that perhaps arrives slowly, and that one, unlike the imagined one, can actually love and be loved, because she exists. To mourn the idealized one is, at bottom, to stop competing with a ghost so as to be able, finally, to be present as who you are.

To do this mourning has, very often, an unexpected effect: as you let go of the idealized mother, there is room to like the real mother you are. While you compare yourself with the perfect one, you see only your defects, your shortcomings, what you don't feel. When the perfect one leaves the scene, you begin to be able to see what is good in the true mother: your devotion, your honesty, your faithful care, the courage of being here looking at this instead of fleeing. The real mother, who at the start seemed to you a disappointment compared to the imagined one, reveals herself, seen without the comparison, a mother of great worth, perhaps precisely because she is real, fallible, striving, true. To let go of the fantasy is not only a loss. It is also the possibility of, finally, being able to appreciate yourself as the mother you in fact are, instead of measuring yourself eternally against one who never existed.

And it was not you who invented that fantasy, so don't blame yourself for having had it. It was sold to you. The image of the perfect mother, naturally in love, instinctively wise, is a cultural construction that serves many things, least of all the good of real mothers. You grew up surrounded by it, you absorbed it without choice, and it would be strange if you hadn't dreamed of being her. The mourning, then, is not only for an image of yourself, it is for a lie they told you about what motherhood should be. And to let go of it is also an act of liberation, yours and, if one day you have daughters, theirs, because a mother who freed herself from the fantasy of the perfect mother does not pass it on.

## 7. To allow, without forcing

If you've come this far, and if what you're living is the common experience we spoke of, and not the clinical suffering that asks for support, then there is a path, and it has a name, which is the verb of this crossing: to allow. I'm going to show you its contours, in broad strokes. Not the step by step, which doesn't fit in a book and is done with time. But the direction.

To allow is the opposite of to force. And here is one of the most important things I have to tell you: you cannot force yourself to feel. Love doesn't come through effort, doesn't come through guilt, doesn't come through punishing yourself for not feeling it. The more you force yourself to feel, the more you demand of yourself the wave that doesn't come, the more you tense up, and the tensing is precisely what keeps the feeling from being born. Love, like much that is deep, doesn't obey orders. It comes when there is room, when there is rest, when there is permission, and not when there is demand. So the path is not to force yourself to love. It is to allow love to come, at its own pace, creating the conditions for it instead of demanding it.

To allow is to let yourself not feel what you expected, without condemning yourself for it. It is to stop demanding of yourself the right emotion at the right moment, and to let your experience be the one it is, without correcting it, without falsifying it, without pretending. There is an enormous relief in stopping pretending, in ceasing to play the ecstatic mother you don't feel yourself to be, in being able to be, with yourself at least, honest about what you feel and what you don't feel. That honesty, far from pushing love away, opens the door to it, because love doesn't grow on top of a lie about what one feels, it grows in the truth, even when the truth is "I don't feel it yet."

To allow is also to stop watching over yourself. Many mothers, after discovering that they don't feel what's expected, enter an anxious vigilance over their own heart: at all hours they examine themselves, ask themselves "do I feel it yet? not yet? did I maybe feel a little today?", measuring love like someone measuring a fever. And that vigilance is, itself, an obstacle, because it keeps you tense, waiting, demanding a feeling of yourself, and the feeling is not born under examination, it is born in relaxation, in being, in forgetting to measure. To allow is to be able to be with your child without constantly evaluating what you feel for him, it is to let the relationship simply happen, in the gestures, in the company, without the permanent thermometer. When you let go of the vigilance, you open room, and it is very often in that room, when you're no longer forcing nor measuring, that the feeling, stealthily, begins to appear.

To allow is also to let the real experience replace the fantasy. Instead of clinging to the mother you imagined and the love they promised you, letting yourself meet the real child, this one, concrete, and the real relationship, this one, possible, that is built in the everyday, in the care, in the mutual knowing. And there is a sentence that holds the heart of this crossing, and that is perhaps the deepest of this book: you can meet your child before you meet the mother you imagined you would be. You don't have to become the perfect mother first in order to love him afterward. You can go to meet him, just as you are, with what you feel and what you don't feel, and let it be that real meeting, and not the fantasy, that builds the bond, in its own time.

To allow is also to forgive yourself the time that has already passed. Perhaps you carry, besides the shame of not feeling, the guilt of the months or the years in which you didn't feel, as if you had robbed your child of a love to which he had a right. I want to tell you that you robbed nothing, if you cared for him, and that the time in which the bond took long is not a deficit to compensate with interest, it is only the time your story and your circumstances needed. You don't have to punish yourself for the past nor push yourself twice as hard now to recover a delay that, at bottom, doesn't exist in your child's account, only in your guilt. Love doesn't work by accumulated debts. It works in the present, in the now, in each gesture of today. And today, and each day, is a new opportunity to be there, to care, to allow, without the weight of a past that after all didn't fail as much as your shame tells you.

And to allow is, above all, to give time time, without a deadline. I am not going to promise you that love arrives on a date, because that would be to replace one false promise with another. For many mothers, the feeling grows, slowly, with the caring and the company, and one day they find themselves loving in a way they didn't notice arrive. For others, the path is different, and sometimes it needs support. I don't promise you a deadline nor a guaranteed ending. I promise you that to allow, instead of to force, and to care, instead of to condemn yourself, and to seek help if there is suffering, is the path that gives the bond the best chance of happening, at its own pace, which is not the one they promised you, but which can be, all the same, profoundly yours.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything all at once, and it doesn't manufacture a feeling that cannot be forced. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling a monster, defective, or alone in an experience so many mothers live in silence.

And I want you to take with you an image of what this crossing asks of you, and of what it does not ask. It does not ask you to produce a feeling, like someone pressing a button. It asks you to prepare the ground and let it grow, like someone caring for a seed. You don't see the seed germinate, you can't hurry it by pulling at the shoot, you can only water, give light, give time, and trust the process. The love you don't yet feel may be a seed like that, already planted in the care you give every day, growing beneath the earth where you don't yet see it. Your task is not to force it to sprout. It is to care for the ground, to set down the shame that hardens it, to seek help if what there is is not a seed but an illness, and to trust that, given the conditions, what has to grow grows, in its own time, which is not the one they promised you, but which can be, all the same, yours.

Now you know that not feeling immediate love is far more common than the taboo lets one guess. You know that the promise of instant love is incomplete, and that your love taking time is not a failure. You know that loving can be an act before it is a feeling, and that if you care, you already love. You know that your true wound is not the absence of love, it is the shame of it, and that telling the truth dissolves the shame. You know, and this is the most important of all, how to tell apart not feeling, which has a path, from suffering, which asks for clinical support, without hesitation. And you know that there is a mourning to do, the mourning for the mother you imagined you would be, and a verb to live, to allow.

And you know, perhaps, that the silence in which you suffered for so long was the heaviest part of everything, and that it is already breaking, only by your having read this book this far, only by your having let in the possibility that you are not a monster nor are you alone. There are more mothers like you than you would ever imagine, in silence, each one thinking herself unique in her supposed failure. If this book does one single thing, may it be this: that you know that your experience has a name, has company, has a path, and that what you carried as your most shameful secret is, after all, one of the most human and most shared pains of motherhood, even though few dare to say it.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I do not need to feel everything in order to love.

And perhaps, deep down, the secret door of this book is beginning to open: I can meet my child before I meet the mother I imagined I would be.

To understand was the first step. But there is a second, and the second is not done only by reading. And here, more than in almost all the other books, I want to be very honest about what that second step is for you.

If what you're living is the common experience of not feeling what's expected, with the shame and the mourning that accompany it, I created a guided crossing, **FreeMe**, that can accompany you in setting down the shame, in doing the mourning for the idealized mother, and in allowing the bond to happen in its own time. You'll find it at [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

But if what you're living is suffering, if you recognize yourself in the signs I spoke of in the fifth chapter, then the second step is not a crossing of self-awareness, it is clinical support, and I want to tell you this with all clarity, because it would be irresponsible not to. In that case, the most important path, the first and the most urgent, is to seek a health professional, and FreeMe, if it comes, will come much later, as part of rebuilding yourself, never in place of the support you need now.

I want to leave you with a scene that many mothers describe to me, and that may give you hope. There was no dramatic moment in which love arrived all at once, as in the films. There was, rather, some ordinary, banal day, on which the mother realized, all of a sudden, that she already loved. That somewhere, in the midst of the days and the nights, of the caring and the company, the bond had been woven without her noticing, and that that being, who at the start was a stranger to her, had become the person most hers in all the world. It didn't come like an announced wave. It came slowly, from within, and it revealed itself in a small gesture, in a laugh of the child, in a night of fever, in a fear of losing him. For many mothers who didn't feel at the start, love did not fail, it only arrived by the back door, without warning, after so much care. I don't promise you the date. But I tell you that this back door exists, and that it opens, very often, precisely when one stops forcing it.

Whatever your case, I want to leave you with one last thing, and I want you to take it with you above any other. Not feeling what they promised you says nothing about your worth as a mother, nor about your love. A mother's love has many forms and many times, and yours, whatever its pace, is no less true for not having arrived like a wave. And if what you carry is heavier than that, if it is suffering, then you deserve, more than any understanding, to be cared for, and there are those who know how to do it, and to seek it out is the most loving and most courageous gesture there is. You are not alone. You never were, however much the silence made you believe so.

With tenderness, and without a single judgment about what you feel or don't feel,
*Vivianne*
