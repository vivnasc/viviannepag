# The woman who arrived and felt little

**For the woman who reached what she wanted and was left with an emptiness she did not expect. Because no goal could deliver what you were asking of it.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. There is an important difference, and one this book respects carefully, between the anticlimax of an arrival, which is human and passing, and a persistent emptiness, a loss of pleasure in everything, a sadness that does not lift, which are another conversation. If what you feel after arriving is not a disappointment that passes, but an emptiness that has settled in, that takes away your energy, your sleep, your sense of meaning, please seek the support of a health professional. It is not weakness. It is recognizing that you deserve care, and that there are pains that are not crossed alone.*

---

## 1. I made it, and now what?

First of all, I want to tell you that what you feel is more common, and more serious, than the lightness with which it is spoken of would suggest. We live in a culture that promises, everywhere, that happiness is in reaching: reach this, conquer that, get there, and you will be happy. It is the underlying promise of almost everything, from advertising to the stories we are told. And so, when you reach something and you are not happy, you feel you failed at an equation that everyone else seems to solve, and you go quiet, because saying "I made it and I feel empty" sounds like heresy in a world that loves to celebrate achievements. But the truth is that the promise is false, or at least very incomplete, and you, in feeling it in the flesh, did not fail, you discovered something the culture hides: that reaching, in itself, does not bring the happiness it promises, and that many people who seem to have arrived live, in secret, the same emptiness as you.

You imagined this moment so many times. The moment when you were finally going to make it. And you always imagined it with a certain glow: you were going to feel full, fulfilled, at peace, you were going to look at what you had reached and feel that it was all worth it, that you had arrived, that you were finally who you wanted to be. You worked for it for months, or years. And the moment came. You made it.

And you felt little.

Perhaps there was an instant of joy, and perhaps it was real. But it was short, much shorter than you expected, and beneath it, or right after, came something else that was not in the script: a strange feeling of emptiness, of anticlimax, of "I made it, and now what?" As if you had climbed an enormous staircase to reach a door that, on opening, gave onto an empty room. You expected to feel a lot, and you felt little, and that difference between what you expected to feel and what you felt left you confused, and perhaps ashamed, and perhaps afraid that there was something wrong with you.

And perhaps you recognize the variations of this scene. The promotion you wanted so much, and that, a week later, was already just your new routine, without the glow you imagined. The goal of years reached, and the strange sadness of the following night, instead of the celebration it should have been. The public recognition you received, the praise, the congratulations, and the feeling, inside, that none of it truly touched you, that you were watching your own victory from the outside, as if it were someone else's. The purchase, the trip, the milestone you expected would change something, and life, the next day, exactly the same, with the same underlying weight. Each of these scenes has the same signature: the distance between what you expected to feel and what you felt, and the confusion of not understanding why.

Because it is not just that you felt little. It is that you do not understand why. You got exactly what you wanted. You did not fail, it was not a frustrated dream, there was no objective disappointment. On the contrary, it was a success, the kind that deserves to be celebrated, and that the others around you celebrate, without understanding why you, the person who should be happiest, walk around with that shadow in your eyes. And you go quiet, and you smile, and you thank them for the congratulations, and inside you carry a question you do not dare say out loud: if this was what I wanted, why do I not feel the way I should?

And there is an extra layer of suffering, that is perhaps the worst: the guilt of feeling the emptiness. Because there are so many people who do not get what you got, so many people who would give everything to be in your place, that feeling empty before your success seems to you an unforgivable ingratitude, almost an offense to those who have less. And so, on top of the emptiness, you place the guilt of feeling it, and you go quieter still, because how can you complain about a thing that is, objectively, good fortune. I want to take that weight off you right away: the emptiness you feel is not ingratitude, and feeling it disrespects no one. It is a real human experience, that does not choose who has more or less, and that deserves to be looked at, not hidden under a guilt that only makes it more lonely.

I want to tell you, right from the start, something that may ease part of the confusion: there is nothing wrong with you, and it was not the wrong goal. The emptiness you felt on arriving is not a sign that you chose badly, nor that you are ungrateful, nor that nothing satisfies you. It is a sign of something else, much deeper and much more common than you imagine, and that is what this book is about. The reason why one can arrive exactly where one wanted, and feel, on arriving, an emptiness instead of the promised fullness.

And I want you to know that you are not alone in this, however much you may feel it. This experience, of arriving and feeling little, even has a name in certain circles, people speak of the disappointment of success, of the emptiness of the top, and it is strangely common precisely among the most capable, most successful, most admired people. There are athletes who win what they dreamed of and fall into a deep depression afterward. There are people who reach the peak of their career and ask themselves, in the corner office, "is this all?" It is not a defect of an ungrateful woman, it is a documented human experience, that touches whoever reaches the top of the staircase and discovers that the staircase did not lead to the place it promised. Knowing that so many go through this does not take away your pain, but it takes away its shame and its loneliness, and it shows you that what you feel is not madness of yours, it is one of the least spoken truths about success.

And I will tell you the direction at once, so you do not read this in anguish: the problem was not the goal, it was what you asked of it. Without knowing it, you asked that achievement to do a thing no achievement can do: to complete you, to give you, all at once, an inner thing that had been missing for a long time. And the goal, however great, had no way of delivering that, because that is not what it contained. That is why you arrived, and felt little: not because the goal was small, but because the thing you were looking for in it was never, nor could it ever be, there. Let us look at this together, calmly, so that you can, at last, reconcile yourself with what you conquered, and discover what, in truth, you were looking for.

## 2. It was not the wrong goal

The first thing your mind is going to try to do, faced with the emptiness of arrival, is to find someone to blame. And the most convenient culprit is the goal. "Maybe this is not really what I wanted." "Maybe I chose the wrong path." "Maybe the right thing is something else, and that is why I do not feel anything." And from there you begin to devalue what you conquered, to doubt all your choices, perhaps to plan a radical change, convinced that the emptiness is resolved by swapping the goal reached for another. I want to ask you not to rush to do that, because it is almost always a mistake.

Because, in most cases, the goal was not wrong. You chose well. What you reached was, in fact, something you wanted, something that made sense to you, something of value. The emptiness that came afterward is not proof that you chose badly. If you change goals, you will probably feel, with the new achievement, exactly the same emptiness, because the emptiness does not come from the particular goal, it comes from an expectation you place on any goal. Changing the goal would be like changing dishes thinking the hunger does not pass because of the dish, when the hunger is for something else that no dish contains.

And here is an important distinction, that frees you from a cruel doubt: one thing is for the goal to have value, another is for the goal to have the power to complete you. Your achievement can have, and does have, real value. It was a good thing to reach it, it brings real benefits to your life, and there is nothing illusory in that. What is illusory is not the value of the goal, it is the power you attributed to it: the power to give you fullness, to make you feel enough, to fill an inner emptiness. That power, no goal has. And confusing the real value of the goal with a power it does not have is the root of the anticlimax.

And there is a way to test this, in your own story, that tends to be revealing. Think of the previous achievements, those of five, ten years ago. Each of them, at the time, seemed like it was going to be the one that would finally change everything, the one that would bring peace, the sense of arrival. And each one, once reached, revealed the same emptiness, and so you set off toward the next. Notice the pattern: if it were the wrong goal, one right goal would be enough for the emptiness not to appear. But the emptiness appeared in all of them, in the right ones and the wrong ones, in the small ones and the large ones. An emptiness that appears in all arrivals, regardless of the goal, cannot be caused by the goal. It has to come from somewhere else, from something you carry into all your arrivals, which is the expectation that they will complete you. That is the constant. And it is in it, not in the goals, that the answer lies.

Notice that this completely changes what there is to do. If the problem were the wrong goal, the solution would be to look for the right goal, and you would spend your life changing goals, always disappointed. But if the problem is not the goal, it is the impossible expectation you place on it, then the solution is not to change the goal, it is to change the expectation. It is to stop asking your achievements to do for you a thing they cannot do, and to discover where that thing you are looking for is truly found, which is not at the end of any achievement.

And I want to warn you of a dangerous trap, into which many women fall at this point: that of concluding that, since achievements do not bring fullness, then it is not worth conquering anything, that it is all empty, that the best thing is to give up wanting. This is as wrong as the opposite illusion. Achievements have real value, they bring good things to life, and giving them up does not bring you fullness, it only brings you a smaller life with the same emptiness. The discovery is not "achievements are worth nothing," it is "achievements are worth what they are worth, and no more than that." It is not to devalue success, it is to put it in its just place: a good thing among others, that enriches life, but that does not complete it, because completeness comes from somewhere else. Keep conquering, if you want, for the real value of achievements. Just stop asking of them what they do not have. The way out is not to want less, it is to expect the right thing from achievements.

So, first of all, I want to take off you the weight of thinking you failed in the choice. You did not fail. You chose well, you conquered well, and the emptiness you felt has nothing to do with the quality of your choice. It has to do with something we are going to see in the next chapter: what you, without knowing, were asking of that achievement, and that it could never give. And when you see that, your view of everything you reached changes, and you can, finally, stop looking for the perfect goal that does not exist, and begin to look in the right place for the thing you really lack.

## 3. What you asked of the goal that it could not give

Let us go to the heart. What did you, without knowing it, ask of that achievement, that it could never give you? Because it was that request, and not the goal itself, that created the emptiness. The goal fulfilled what it promised: it gave you what a goal gives. The emptiness came from what you asked of it in addition, in secret, without saying it, and that was far beyond what it contained.

Look at what you expected to feel on arriving, and you will see that it was almost never about the achievement itself. You expected to feel, finally, enough. You expected to feel that you were worth something, that you were worthy, that you could at last rest from an inner trial you have carried for a long time. You expected, perhaps, to feel seen, recognized, loved, belonging. You expected that achievement to resolve an old doubt about your own worth, to give you an inner peace you did not have, to make you feel, once and for all, at peace with yourself. Those were the true expectations. The goal was only the vehicle that was supposed to bring them.

And here is the problem: none of those things comes from an achievement. The feeling of being enough, of being worth something, of being worthy, of being at peace with yourself, is an inner thing, that is built in the relationship you have with yourself, and that no external success can install. An achievement can give you recognition from others, but it does not give you the inner feeling of worth, if it is not there. It can give you status, but not belonging. It can give you results, but not inner peace. You asked the goal to do the work that only a relationship with yourself can do, and it, however great, did not have that capacity, because that is not what goals are made of.

There is a reason why we do this, and it is not stupidity nor naivety, it is profoundly human. From early on, we learn to associate achievement with feeling. The child who gets a good grade receives love, attention, pride, and learns, without deciding it, that love comes with the result, that being enough depends on reaching. She grows up chasing results, not for the results, but for the love, the approval, the feeling of being enough that came with them. And she continues to do it as an adult, only now the results are larger and the love that was supposed to come with them no longer comes, because the parents who applauded are no longer there, or because the external applause, even when it comes, no longer reaches the inner place that was hungry. So she conquers, and conquers, and the promised feeling does not appear, because the link between achievement and feeling, that existed in childhood, was a construction, not a truth, and in adulthood that construction comes undone, leaving you with the achievement in one hand and the emptiness in the other.

It is like having a deep thirst and having asked a meal to quench it. The meal was good, nourishing, real, it did what a meal does. But you were thirsty, not hungry, and so you ate and remained thirsty, and your confusion was thinking that the problem was the meal, when the problem was that you were asking it for water, which it did not have. Your achievement was the meal. The fullness you were looking for was water. And the water was not on the plate, however delicious it was.

And notice a cruel thing about this mechanism: the more you conquer without quenching the thirst, the more convinced you become that you need to conquer still more, because the logic of the plate says that, if one meal was not enough, the solution is to eat more. And so you increase the doses, you chase larger and larger goals, in the hope that the quantity will resolve what is, in truth, a problem of nature: you are giving food to a thirst. That is why so many successful women become more and more ambitious and more and more empty at the same time, in a crescendo that has no end, because each achievement that does not satisfy reinforces the belief that more remains to be conquered, when what is missing is to stop looking for water on the plate. Seeing this interrupts the crescendo: you do not need a larger achievement, you need to look elsewhere for the thing you are, in truth, hungry for.

I want you to receive this, because it relieves a confusion of years: the emptiness you felt does not devalue your achievement, and your achievement should not have been able to take away that emptiness. The two things are true at the same time. You conquered something of value, and you remain with an inner thirst, and that is not a contradiction: the meal was full and your thirst stayed intact, because they were, from the start, different hungers. And it is a liberating discovery, because it means you can stop asking of your achievements a thing they will never have, and begin to look for the water where it actually is, which is not outside, it is inside you, in the way you see yourself and treat yourself. But to get there, there is an emptiness you have to cross first, and that is what we speak of next.

## 4. The emptiness on arrival

I am going to drop a sentence here, on its own, and I ask you to stay with it, even if it opens an emptiness, because it is precisely that emptiness that has something to tell you.

This was not what I was, in truth, looking for.

Stay with this a moment.

The emptiness you felt on arriving is not an error to correct, nor a defect to hide. It is a message. It is your own being telling you, in the clearest way it found, that what you were looking for was not what you reached. For years, you managed to keep that truth at a distance, because the goal was ahead, and while there is a goal ahead, we can believe it will bring what is missing. But the moment you arrive, the moment the goal stops being ahead and comes into your hands, the illusion comes undone, and the emptiness that was covered by the expectation appears, bare. The arrival did not create the emptiness. It only took away what was covering it.

There is a reason why this hurts so much, and it deserves to be said with tenderness: because, with the emptiness of arrival, a hope is also lost. For years, even on the bad days, there was a consolation: "when I arrive, it will be worth it." That promise of the future held you, gave meaning to the effort, the sacrifice, the renunciations. And on arriving, when the emptiness appears, you lose not only the fullness you expected, you also lose that hope that sustained you, the belief that there was a place where everything was resolved. It is a small grief, the grief of the illusion that arrival saves. And like all griefs, it hurts, and needs to be felt, not denied. But, like the best griefs, on the other side of it there is a freedom: that of no longer living hostage to a promise that would never be fulfilled, and of being able, at last, to look for fullness where it actually is found.

And that is why the anticlimax of arrival, however painful, is a precious moment, perhaps the most honest of your life. Because it is the moment when the truth becomes impossible to ignore. While you ran, you could tell yourself you were on the right path, that little was left, that it would be worth it. Now you cannot, because you arrived, and the fullness did not come, and there is no longer anywhere to point the hope. That moment, when you are left face to face with the emptiness without the excuse of the next goal, is uncomfortable, but it is also a door: the door to finally looking at what you truly seek, instead of continuing to chase it in the wrong place.

And I want you to see the courage it is not to flee, because you, who are brave in so many things, have here the opportunity for a different courage, rarer. It is easy to be brave at chasing, at reaching, at facing external challenges. It is much harder to have the courage to stay with the emptiness, not to cover it, to let it speak. Most people, faced with the anticlimax, flee immediately, either to another goal, or to a distraction, or to denial ("it's nothing, it will pass"). To stay with the emptiness, to look at it head on, to ask it what it has to say, is an act of inner courage that few do, precisely because it hurts and has nothing heroic in the eyes of others. But it is that quiet courage, and not the loud courage of conquering, that is going to take you where you need to go. And you have it, even if you have never used it, because whoever had the courage to come this far surely has the courage to stop and look.

The temptation, at this point, is to flee the emptiness as fast as possible, and the most common way to flee is to immediately find another goal. A new achievement to pursue, a new summit, a new project, so that the hope has somewhere to point again and the emptiness goes back to being covered. I ask you to resist that flight, or at least to see it for what it is. Each time you cover the emptiness of arrival with a new goal, you postpone the encounter with the truth it wants to show you, and you condemn yourself to repeat the cycle: run, arrive, feel the emptiness, flee to another race, forever. The emptiness is only crossed by stopping in it, not by fleeing from it.

There is an image that can help you understand the emptiness of arrival. Imagine someone who spends years saving money to buy a house in a distant land, convinced that, when they live there, they will finally be happy. They buy the house, they move, and they carry with them, in their luggage, the same sadness they had before, because the sadness was not in the lack of a house, it was inside them, and it traveled with them. The house was real, good, valuable. But it did not contain the happiness, because happiness was not a question of a house. Your emptiness is like that: it travels with you from achievement to achievement, because it is not in the lack of the achievement, it is in an inner hunger that no external change resolves. And the good news hidden in this is that, if the emptiness is not in the circumstances, then changing them infinitely is not the solution, and you can stop trying, and turn, at last, to the only place where it can actually be cared for: inside you.

And I want to tell you that crossing this emptiness is not falling into it forever. It is looking at it, listening to it, letting it tell you what it has to say, which is always, deep down, the same thing: that what you are looking for is not where you have been looking for it. And that message, however much it hurts to hear it, is a liberation, because it takes you out of an impossible search and points you, finally, in the right direction. The emptiness on arrival is not the end of your path. It is the first time your path points inward, instead of toward the next place out there.

## 5. Anticlimax or depression

I have to stop here and make a distinction with all seriousness, because it is important for your safety, and because it would be irresponsible of me to speak of emptiness without making it. There is a difference between the anticlimax of an arrival and a depression, and it is vital to know how to recognize it, because they are different things and they ask for different responses.

The anticlimax is the disappointment that comes after an achievement, that feeling of "I made it, and now what?", the emptiness that appears when the goal reached did not bring what it promised. It is uncomfortable, but it has certain characteristics: it is linked to a concrete event, the arrival; it comes in waves, it leaves room for other things, for some pleasure, for some hope; and, above all, it lets you function, live, continue. The anticlimax is human, it is common, and it is, deep down, a door to a deeper understanding, as we have seen.

Depression is something else. It is not a disappointment linked to an event, it is a weight that settles in and does not lift. And I want to name the signs, plainly, because knowing them can be decisive. If the emptiness you feel does not pass, if it persists for weeks, if it is no longer linked only to the arrival but has taken over everything. If you have lost pleasure in everything, not only in the achievement, but in things that used to give you joy, and nothing reaches you. If there is a deep sadness that does not lift, a tiredness that rest does not take away, a feeling that nothing makes sense. If you isolate yourself, if it is hard for you to function, to sleep, to get up. If there are thoughts that it is not worth it, that it would be better not to be here. These are not signs of anticlimax. They are signs of depression, and depression is not crossed alone with understanding nor with willpower.

And here I tell you, with all firmness and all care: if you recognize yourself in these signs, what you need is not this book, nor a new understanding about your goals. It is professional support, a doctor, a psychologist, someone who can help you climb out of the emptiness you have fallen into. Depression is real, it is common, it is treatable, and it is not your fault nor your weakness. Seeking help is not admitting defeat, it is the most lucid and most courageous act you can take, and it can change everything. Please, if any of these lines spoke of you, do not stay alone with that, and do not wait for it to pass with the next achievement, because it will not pass that way.

And there is a particular reason why the woman who arrives and feels little is at risk of not seeking help when she should: she tends to read her depression as lucidity. "I am not depressed, I just saw the truth, that none of this is worth anything." Depression is a liar, and one of its favorite lies, for someone who is intelligent and reflective, is to disguise itself as philosophical clear-sightedness, as a bitter discovery about the futility of everything. If there is in you a voice that says "now I finally see that nothing has meaning, that all achievements are empty, that life is not worth it," distrust that voice, especially if it comes with the signs I spoke of, the loss of pleasure, the tiredness that does not pass, the isolation. That voice may not be wisdom, it may be the illness painting the world gray and convincing you that the color never existed. When in doubt, seek help. A professional knows how to distinguish lucid anticlimax from depression disguised as lucidity, and that distinction can change, or save, your life.

I want to be very clear about why I insist on this, changing the tone. In nearly all of the book I accompany you in looking, calmly, at the anticlimax and its message. But if I told you that it is enough to understand your search for the emptiness to pass, when what you have is a depression, I would be failing you, keeping you away from the help you need. The understanding of this book is precious for the human anticlimax. It does not treat a depression. Knowing how to distinguish the two things, and having the courage to seek help when it is the second, is perhaps the most important thing this book can give you.

## 6. What you were really looking for

If what you are looking for was not in the goal, but in an inner need, then the way forward passes through something you have avoided: naming that need. Looking at what, in truth, you were looking for behind all the achievements, and that none of them gave you, because that is not what they contained.

Go back to that list of things you expected to feel on arriving, and go deeper. What was underneath? For many women, it was feeling enough, finally, after a life of feeling that she was not enough as she was, that she had to prove her worth with results. For others, it was recognition, to be seen, to count, to matter, because at some point they learned that they were only noticed when they stood out. For others, it was security, the feeling of being finally safe, at peace, because they grew up on unstable ground. For others, it was belonging, to feel that they were part of something, because somewhere they felt left out. Whatever yours is, notice one thing: it is an inner need, old, linked to who you are and to your story, and not to the achievement you were chasing.

And notice an important subtlety: these needs, in themselves, have nothing wrong about them. Wanting to feel enough, seen, safe, belonging, are legitimate human needs, healthy, universal. The problem was never having them, it was trying to satisfy them in the wrong place. There is nothing here to correct in you, no shameful need to eliminate. There is only a redirection to be made: to take those true needs from the place where they are never quenched, the exterior, the achievements, the future, to the place where they can finally be cared for, the interior, the relationship with yourself, the present. You do not have to stop wanting to feel enough. You have to stop looking for that enoughness where it is not, and begin to build it where it can actually grow. The need is good. Only the address was wrong.

And there is a way to discover which is yours, among these, that is simple and revealing. Notice what you felt, concretely, in the moment of arrival, or in the lack of it. If you felt "I made it, but I still think it is not enough," your thirst is probably to feel enough. If you felt "I made it, but no one truly saw it, no one understood what this means," your thirst is for recognition. If you felt "I made it, but I am still afraid, still without ground," your thirst is for security. If you felt "I made it, and I am still alone," your thirst is for belonging, for connection. The exact form of the disappointment points to the exact form of the hunger, because the disappointment is the hunger not being satisfied, and it tells you, by the negative, what you expected and did not come. Listen to your disappointment attentively, because it is the most precise map you have for the true need.

And notice another thing, that moves and that frees: these needs, almost always, belong to the past, to a younger version of you, perhaps to a child, who needed to feel enough, seen, safe, belonging, and who, for one reason or another, did not receive that in the measure she needed. And that part of you learned a strategy: if I make it, if I reach, if I am the best, then finally I will feel what was missing. Your search for achievements is, deep down, the attempt of an old part of you to obtain, through success, a thing that was missing long before any success existed. And that is why you arrive and it is not enough: because what is missing is not in the future nor in the achievements, it is in an old wound that asks to be cared for, not compensated.

And there is often, beneath all this, a story that comes from further back, and that is worth looking at carefully. The way you learned to look for your worth on the outside was not born from nothing. Perhaps you grew up in a home where love seemed to come with performance, where you were noticed for your grades, rewarded for your results, and where your worth at rest, with no deliverables, was not mirrored back. Perhaps you absorbed, without words, the idea that you had to deserve your place, that being loved was something to be conquered, not something given. If that was the case, your tireless search for achievements is the continuation of an old strategy, that of a child who learned that only that way would she be seen and loved. This does not explain everything, and it is not to blame anyone, but it helps to understand, with compassion, why you place on the exterior a weight it cannot bear: because it was there, on the outside, in the gaze of others, that you learned to look for the worth that should have been given to you as a ground, without conditions.

Naming this is the first step, and it is enormous, because it takes your search out of the dark, where it governed you without your seeing it, and brings it into the light, where you can finally understand it. The moment you know that what you are looking for is to feel enough, and not the promotion; to be seen, and not the prize; to be at peace with yourself, and not the result, your relationship with achievements changes. You may continue, perhaps, to want to reach things, but no longer as salvation, no longer waiting for them to complete you. And that difference changes everything, because it frees each achievement from the impossible weight of giving you what only you can give yourself.

I want to tell you something about that old part of you, because it changes the way you treat her. She does not need one more achievement. You have already given her so many, and none reached the place where she is hungry, because she is not hungry for deeds, she is hungry to be seen, welcomed, recognized as enough, exactly as she is, without having to do anything to deserve it. You have spent your life trying to buy her love with results, and she is still waiting for a thing that is not bought: that someone, and that someone can only be you, look at her and say, without conditions, "you are enough, just as you are, you do not have to prove anything." That is what she has been waiting for, for years, and that no arrival brought her. And that is what you, and only you, can finally give her, on the day you stop demanding achievements of her and begin to give her presence.

Because here is the truth that reorients the whole search: the thing you are looking for, only you can give to yourself. The feeling of being enough, of being worth something, of belonging to yourself, does not come from outside, does not come from any achievement, it comes from the relationship you build with yourself, from the way you see yourself, treat yourself, recognize yourself. It is an inner work, not an outer achievement. And that is why no goal can give it, and why all the goals left you with thirst. The water you are looking for is inside you, waiting for you to stop looking for it out there and begin, finally, to give it to yourself.

## 7. Recognizing without asking it to complete

The verb of this crossing is to recognize, and it has two senses that come together, both important. To recognize what you conquered, giving it its just value. And to recognize yourself, in the true need that was beneath the search. I am not going to give you one more staircase to climb, because it is from staircases that you come tired; I only want to point you to where the gaze should turn.

To recognize what you conquered means, first, to stop devaluing it. Because the temptation, faced with the emptiness, is to throw away the achievement, to think it was worth nothing, that it was a mistake, that it does not count. It is not true. What you reached has value, real value, and deserves to be recognized as such, with gratitude, with pride even. The problem was never the achievement being worth little. It was you asking too much of it. When you stop asking it to complete you, you can finally see it for what it is: a good thing, valuable, yours, that enriches your life, without having to be the thing that saves you. Freeing the achievement from the weight of completing you allows you, at last, to enjoy it.

To recognize what you conquered also means recovering the capacity to celebrate, that you may have lost. Because a person who lives disappointed with each arrival ceases, over time, to celebrate anything at all, anticipating the emptiness already, receiving each victory with a reserve, with a "yes, but." And that inability to celebrate impoverishes life in a cruel way, because it takes away your joy even in the good things. Learning to recognize without demanding is also relearning to celebrate: to let an achievement be a joy, whole, in the moment it happens, without spoiling it with the demand that it complete you nor with the anticipation of the disappointment. To celebrate is not naivety, it is the capacity to receive the good that life brings, and it is one of the things this crossing can give you back.

And here is the heart of this book's sentence: you can recognize what you conquered without asking it to complete you. The two things separate, and in that separation is the peace. You honor what you reached, you thank it for what it actually gives you, and at the same time you no longer expect from it a fullness that is not in it. You let the achievement be a good part of your life, instead of forcing it to be the whole meaning of it. And so, paradoxically, you come to enjoy much more of what you have, because you stopped being disappointed with it for not being what it could never be.

There is here a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, for mothers, FreeMe. There, the crossing is to set down what one carries, the weight that is not ours. Here, in Infonte, the crossing is to distinguish what one seeks, the thing we ask of goals and that they cannot give. One sets down, the other recognizes. But deep down it is the same journey: the return to what is truly ours, and that was somewhere else, not in the extra weight nor in the extra achievement, but inside, in the relationship with ourselves. The mother returns to her place; you return to your own source of worth, that inner place from which the feeling of being enough comes, and that you have looked for, in vain, on the outside. It is the same cure: to see, to differentiate what is yours from what is not, and to come back home, which in this case is the home of yourself.

And I want to tell you that recognizing what you conquered has yet another effect that few expect: it makes you see that you already have much of what you were chasing, only you did not recognize it because you were always turned forward. There is, in your life, value already built, relationships, capacities, experiences, moments, that you never stopped to recognize, because your gaze was always on the next goal. To recognize is to turn the gaze backward and to the side, and to see what is already there, what you already are, what you already have, and to give it, finally, its value. Many women, on doing it, discover with astonishment that the life they were chasing so much, in large part, they already had, and that what was missing was not one more achievement, it was the act of recognizing and receiving what was already done. The emptiness did not come only from what was missing, it came from never looking at what you already had.

The second sense of recognizing is deeper, and it is to recognize yourself. The true need that was beneath the search, that old child who needed to feel enough, seen, safe, and who learned to chase it in achievements. To recognize her is to see her, finally, and to begin to give her, yourself, what she has spent her whole life asking of the world, of the future, of goals. It is a new relationship with yourself: treating yourself as enough without having to prove it, seeing yourself as worthy without having to conquer it, belonging to yourself before any external belonging. That is the work no goal could do, and that only you can do, and it is the only one that truly quenches the thirst.

And I want to give you a hint of what it is, in practice, to give yourself what you asked of goals, because it may seem abstract. It is to begin to treat yourself, day to day, as enough before any result. It is to notice the inner voice that only approves of you when you produce, and to begin, slowly, to approve of yourself too when you simply are. It is to stop postponing the right to rest, to feel good, to like yourself, to after the next achievement, and to begin to give it now, without conditions. It is to look at a child, or at someone you love, and to notice that you do not need her to conquer anything to find her worthy of love, and to ask yourself why you apply to yourself a different rule, a crueler one. To rebuild the relationship with yourself is not a great dramatic project, it is this small and daily change in the way you speak to yourself and treat yourself, until, slowly, feeling enough stops depending on what you reach and becomes a ground that is there, even when you conquer nothing.

I am not going to give you a step by step for this, because it would be one more goal, and the last thing you need is to turn your reunion with yourself into one more achievement to be reached with excellence. And I want to tell you what happens, often, to those who make this crossing, because it is good news worth your knowing. When you begin to give yourself what you asked of goals, you do not lose the ambition nor the taste for accomplishing, contrary to what you may fear. What changes is the root: you stop conquering to fill yourself and you begin to conquer because you feel like it, because you overflow, because it is a way of expressing a life that is already yours. And, freed from the weight of completing you, the achievements become, paradoxically, more savory, because they no longer arrive with an impossible demand nor depart with a guaranteed disappointment. You come to be able to celebrate for real, to savor the path and not only the arrival, to live your life instead of chasing it. The crossing does not take away the life you built. It gives it back to you, now inhabitable, because you no longer ask of it a thing it could never give you, and you can, at last, receive it for what it is.

The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because recognizing and caring for an old need is not a thing done alone on a page. But the principle fits in one sentence: what you are looking for is not at the end of any arrival, it is in the way you treat yourself, now, before any arrival. And when you begin to give yourself what you asked of goals, goals stop disappointing you, because you no longer ask of them the impossible, and you stop arriving and feeling little, because you no longer arrive with a thirst they could never quench.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not change everything at once, and it does not fill all at once an emptiness that comes from far. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling crazy, ungrateful, or defective for arriving where you wanted and feeling so little.

And I want to leave you with an image of what changes when you make this crossing. Today, you live like someone who carries a leaking cup to the fountain, time after time, and fills it, and it empties, and you go back to the fountain, more and more tired, convinced that you need a larger fountain. The crossing is not to find a larger fountain. It is to repair the hole in the cup. Because the hole is the relationship with yourself, it is feeling that you are not enough, that you are not worth anything, that you have to prove it, and while it is open, no fountain, however large, fills you, because what enters drains away. To repair the hole, to build inside the feeling of being enough, is what allows what you conquer to finally stay, to fill you, to reach you. And you discover, then, that perhaps you did not even need such large fountains, that the problem was never the quantity that entered, it was the quantity that drained through a hole nobody taught you to repair.

Now you know that the emptiness of arrival does not mean you chose badly. You know that it was not the wrong goal, it was what you asked of it. You know that you asked the achievement to complete you, and that no achievement can do that. You know that the emptiness on arrival is a message, not a defect, and that it points inward. You know how to distinguish the human anticlimax from a depression that asks for help. You know how to name what, in truth, you were looking for. And you know that the verb of all this is to recognize: what you conquered, and yourself.

And you know, perhaps, that the emptiness that brought you to this book, the one that frightened and shamed you so much, was, after all, your best teacher. It was it that took off the blindfold, that kept you from continuing to run blind from arrival to arrival, that forced you to ask the question that changes everything. Without the emptiness, you would have continued to chase the next goal, convinced that it was the one that was missing, forever. It was the emptiness, and not the achievements, that brought you to the possibility of finding yourself. So, instead of seeing it as an enemy, you can begin to see it as an ally, the severe ally that tells you the truth no one else tells you: that the thing you are looking for is closer and more inside than you ever looked, and that it was always waiting for you, on this side of all the arrivals.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can recognize what I conquered without asking it to complete me.

Seeing what you were looking for was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not conquered, it is not one more goal to be reached. It is done the opposite way: it is done by stopping looking out there, and beginning to give yourself, on the inside, what you asked of arrivals.

It was for that crossing that I created **Infonte**.

Infonte is not one more goal to be reached, nor a method for you to conquer better. It is a space for you to ask the question that the emptiness of arrival opened: what is it that I really seek, and that no achievement gave me? And to begin to build, on the inside, the relationship with yourself that is the only one that can quench that thirst, the only one that makes you feel enough without having to prove it. Not so you stop reaching, but so that your achievements stop carrying an impossible weight, and can, at last, be enjoyed for what they are.

There, the emptiness of arrival ceases to be an abyss from which one flees to the next goal and becomes a door to what you truly seek. The chronic dissatisfaction ceases to be a defect to hide and becomes the messenger that points you to the real hunger. And to recognize ceases to be an idea and becomes a daily practice, that of honoring what you have without asking it for the impossible, and that of giving, at last, to yourself, what you asked of the world.

If you feel this is the moment, I am there. At [infonte.viviannedossantos.com](https://infonte.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is okay, and there is no goal here to fulfill. What you have read is already yours, and it stays with you, serene, until the day you decide to stop asking of arrivals what only you can give yourself.

And I want to leave you with a distinction that may accompany you. There is a difference between the happiness one pursues and the peace one receives. The happiness one pursues is always ahead, in the next achievement, in the next place, and so it is never reached, because the moment one touches it, it flees forward. The peace one receives is something else: it is not ahead, it is here, in the present, in the moment you stop pursuing and allow yourself to simply be, enough, without proof, without arrival. You are an expert in the first and barely know the second. And the crossing of this book is, deep down, to learn to swap the pursuit of happiness for the reception of peace, to discover that the thing you ran your whole life trying to reach is not reached, it is received, and that it is received not at the end, but at the beginning, before any arrival, in the moment you decide that you are already enough.

But before you close these pages, take with you a small gesture, the size of an arrival that does not need to be celebrated with fireworks. Today, look at a thing you have already conquered, and that you may have devalued because it did not complete you. And, instead of demanding of it what it did not give you, thank it for what it actually gave you, and let it be just that: a good thing, yours, valuable, that did not have to save you. And notice how, when you stop asking it for the impossible, it can finally give you what it always had to give. Because it is there, in that recognizing without demanding, that the peace begins, and that you stop, at last, arriving and feeling little.

With all my tenderness,
