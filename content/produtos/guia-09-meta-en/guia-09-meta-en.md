# Whose goal is this?

**Stop the never-is-enough race and see what you've been chasing that was never yours.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a self-knowledge and well-being resource. It does not replace therapeutic support. If the race you live in exhausts you to the point where you no longer recognize yourself, if you feel constant anxiety, deep exhaustion, or an emptiness that no achievement fills, seek professional support. Looking at what you chase is an act of courage. Taking care of yourself is the first goal.*

---

## 1. The race with no finish line

There's one thing I want you to do before you keep reading. Stop for a moment and ask yourself: when is it going to be enough?

Don't answer quickly. Stay with the question. When will the moment come when you say "I did it, this is good, I can rest"? When your child gets into that school? When the house is finished? When the number in the bank account reaches that amount? When you lose those kilos? When the promotion comes through? When other people finally see that you're worth something?

And now the more honest question: didn't you already think this very thing five years ago, ten years ago? Didn't you already say "when this happens, I'll feel at peace"? And it happened. And how long did you feel at peace? A day? An afternoon? And then the finish line moved again, further ahead, always further ahead, like the horizon that recedes as you walk toward it.

This has a name. Psychology calls it the hedonic treadmill, or the adaptation wheel. The idea is simple and cruel: you chase after something, convinced that, once you reach it, you'll be happy. And you reach it. And there's a brief peak of satisfaction. And then the nervous system adapts, the new becomes normal, and the level of satisfaction returns exactly to where it was before. The new house becomes just the house. The new car becomes just the car. The body you wanted, the moment you have it, you already see the next flaw. And off you go, running again, after the next thing that this time, you swear, will be the final one.

I call this the race with no finish line. Because that's exactly what it is. You run, and you run well, with effort, with discipline, with sacrifice. But the goal isn't fixed in the ground ahead of you. It's strapped to your back, and it moves with you. No matter how fast you run, it always stays the same distance away. Unreachable by design. Not because you're slow. Because the race was built to never end.

And there's a bodily symptom of this that you might recognize. It's that restlessness that won't let you sit down. You sit on the sofa and after two minutes you've already stood up to do something. You have a free afternoon and instead of rest you feel guilt, as if resting were a waste, as if stopping were a betrayal of something. You go on holiday and you take work with you, or you take the list of goals, or you take the anxiety of someone who feels they should be making the most of it more. The body never lets go. It's always in race mode, always tense, always waiting for the next effort. And at night, when you finally lie down, the mind keeps running in the dark, planning the next day, tallying up what's missing, pushing you toward the next lap.

I know this race from the inside. There was a phase of my life when I would wake up already tired, not from sleep, but from anticipation. Before I'd put my feet on the floor, the list was already assembled in my head, and the feeling wasn't of a day beginning, but of a test starting over. Everything I did, I did well. And nothing I did was ever enough for me. There was always more. There was always the next step. And I climbed it, breathless, only to discover, at the top, another flight of stairs I swore I hadn't seen before.

The image that best describes this, for me, is that of a hunger that eating never fills. Imagine someone hungry who sits at the table and eats, and eats, and the plate empties, but the hunger stays the same. Not because the food is bad. Not because the amount is too little. But because the hunger doesn't come from the stomach. It comes from somewhere else. And no amount of food is going to fill a hunger that isn't of the body. You can eat your whole life long and die starving, if the hunger is for something else.

Most of the goals we chase are food for a hunger that isn't of food. We work more to feel enough, but the hunger isn't for work, it's for worth. We buy more to feel safe, but the hunger isn't for things, it's for peace. We achieve more so that we'll be seen, but the hunger isn't for achievement, it's for belonging. And until we understand this, we'll keep throwing food into a bottomless well, amazed that we never see it fill.

If you recognize yourself in this race, I want you to know: the problem is not your lack of ability. You have ability to spare. The problem isn't your lack of effort either. You try harder than almost anyone. The problem is the direction. You're running with all your strength in a race that gives you nothing, after a finish line that was never designed to be crossed. And the first thing to do is not to run faster. It's to stop and look at the goal. Because there's a question that changes everything, and it's this: whose goal is this?


## 2. The goal you inherited without knowing

Here's the truth that almost no one has told you: most of the goals you chase, you didn't choose them.

It sounds like a strong claim. But think it through with me. When did you, sitting with yourself, in silence, with no one else's opinion, decide that you wanted exactly this life, this body, this career, this level of success, this pace? When did that happen? For most people, it never happened. The goals weren't chosen. They were absorbed. They entered you so early, and so silently, that you grew up thinking they were yours. Like the accent of the place where you were born. You didn't choose it. It settled into you before you had a voice to say yes or no.

There are three main sources for a goal that isn't yours, and I want you to look at each one honestly.

The first is the goal inherited from your parents. There are families where ambition is breathed like air. Where love came measured in grades, in prizes, in results. Where "I'm proud of you" only appeared after an achievement, never before, never just for existing. If you grew up like that, you learned a poisonous equation: I'm worth something if I achieve. And that equation doesn't stay in childhood. It comes with you into adult life, and it turns you into a person who needs to achieve in order to feel worthy of existing. You don't chase the goal because you want it. You chase it because, deep down, you're still trying to hear that "I'm proud of you" that never came, or that always came tied to a condition.

There's also the inheritance in reverse, which is more subtle and equally heavy. It's the goal you carry for what your parents couldn't do. The father who never studied and projected onto you the degree he never had. The mother who gave up her career and now lives hers through you. The unrealized dream of one generation that lands, without asking permission, on the shoulders of the next. In family constellations I see this time and again: exhausted people chasing a life that, when they look closely, they realize they're living for someone else. They're paying a debt they didn't incur. They're keeping a promise they never made.

The second source is the goal of the tribe. Of the circle you move in. What, within your group, defines who's worth something and who isn't. It can be the size of the house, the holiday destination, the brand of car, the children's school, the body on display, the number of followers. Each group has its currency, and you, without realizing it, spend your life trying to accumulate your group's currency, convinced it's worth something. But ask yourself: if you changed groups tomorrow, would this goal still make sense? If no one knew you'd reached it, would you still want it? If the answer is no, then the goal was never about the thing. It was about being seen having it. And a goal that only exists for other people's eyes is a borrowed goal. You carry the weight, they get the show.

The third source, and perhaps the most painful, is the goal you chase to prove something. To prove you're not what someone said you were. To prove to whoever underestimated you that they were wrong. To prove to an absent father, a critical mother, an ex who hurt you, a teacher who humiliated you, that they were mistaken about you. This goal has tremendous force, because it's driven by pain. And pain is a potent fuel. But it's a fuel that burns you. Because while you chase a goal to prove something to someone, that person keeps commanding your life, even if they're far away, even if they're dead, even if they don't even remember you. You handed them the steering wheel. And you run, and you run, waiting for a recognition that, even if it came, would no longer arrive in time to heal you.

Notice something important. An inherited goal isn't necessarily a bad goal. Studying can be good. Working can be good. Taking care of your body can be good. The problem isn't in the content of the goal. It's in the root. A goal born of you gives energy, even when it costs you. A goal born of a wound takes energy, even when you reach it. The first, when you fulfil it, leaves you more whole. The second, when you fulfil it, leaves you empty and already looking at the next one, because the hole it was supposed to fill is still open.

And there's one sign that never fails to tell one from the other. The goal that is yours, when you imagine it fulfilled, gives you peace. The goal that isn't yours, when you imagine it fulfilled, gives you relief. And peace and relief are very different things. Peace is positive, it's fullness, it's a yes. Relief is the absence of a threat, the temporary end of a fear, a "thank goodness I didn't fail." Those who run after borrowed goals rarely feel peace. They feel, at most, the brief relief of someone who escaped judgment for one more day. And the next day, the judgment comes back. And they start running again.


## 3. The test: whose goal is this?

Now let's get concrete. This is an exercise to do with pen and paper, calmly, at a time when no one will interrupt you. It takes maybe twenty minutes. It can be the best use of twenty minutes you'll make all year, because what you're about to do is to separate, from within yourself, what is yours from what you carry for others.

### First: write the goal

Choose a concrete goal that consumes you. Just one, to start. The one that keeps you awake, that makes you feel you're falling short, that's always there at the bottom of your chest, pushing. It can be a number in the bank account. A body. A position at work. A house. A recognition. Write it in a clear sentence: "I want..." And write next to it, with brutal honesty, how long you've been chasing it.

### Second: ask it four questions

For each question, write the whole answer, don't just think it in your head. The act of writing forces an honesty that thought, on its own, manages to get around.

**Question one: who would be proud if I reached this?** And notice who comes to mind before you yourself do. If the first face that appears is your father's, your mother's, someone who judged you, someone you want to prove something to, pay attention. A goal of yours appears, in the imagination, with your own satisfied face. A borrowed goal appears with someone else's face approving of you.

**Question two: if no one knew I'd done it, would I still want it?** Imagine you reach the goal and you have to keep it absolutely secret. No one knows. No one praises you. No one sees you. Do you still want it with the same force? If the desire collapses the moment you take away the audience, the goal wasn't about the thing. It was about the audience.

**Question three: what do I imagine I'll feel when I reach it, and what am I afraid of feeling if I don't?** This question is a key. Because often what you chase isn't the pleasure of the goal, it's the escape from the fear of failing it. If the answer to "what will I feel if I don't reach it" is shame, humiliation, feeling like a failure, feeling I let someone down, then you're not running forward, drawn by a dream. You're fleeing forward, chased by a fear. And those who flee never arrive, because fear runs faster.

**Question four: from whom did I first hear that this was important?** Go back in time. Try to remember when this goal entered you. Was there a sentence? Was there a look of disapproval when you brought home a lower grade? Was there a comment about your body at twelve years old? Was there a comparison with a sibling, a cousin, a neighbor? Many goals have a date of birth, and rarely is it our own date. When you find the origin, you find the owner. And often you discover, with a mix of astonishment and sadness, that you've spent twenty years running after a sentence someone said to you at a moment when you were small and defenseless.

### Third: make the separation

Now look at your answers and sort the goal into one of three boxes. This is the heart of the whole exercise.

**The "it's mine" box.** The imagination showed you yourself, not others. You'd want it even in secret. What you expect to feel is peace, not relief. The origin is you, not an old wound. If the goal falls here, wonderful. Keep it, honor it, chase it without guilt, because running after what is yours is one of the most beautiful things a human being can do. I'm not asking you to stop having goals. I'm asking you to have your own.

**The "it's borrowed" box.** The goal has someone else's face. It collapses without an audience. It's driven by fear or by the need to prove something. It bears the fingerprint of a wound. If it falls here, don't throw it away yet. Just acknowledge, out loud if you can: "This goal wasn't born of me. I inherited it. And I can give it back." Giving back, in the symbolic sense, is a powerful gesture. There's a sentence from family constellations that I use a lot and that you can say, looking at the goal written on the paper: "This is yours, not mine. I honor where it comes from, but I set it down. I leave it with you, with respect." It sounds small. It isn't. Setting down a weight that isn't yours is the beginning of being able to breathe again.

**The "it's mixed" box.** And this is the most common one, so don't be alarmed if most of your goals fall here. There's a part that is yours and a part that is inherited, tangled up in each other. You genuinely want to work at what you do, and you're also trying to prove something to your father. You genuinely want to take care of your body, and you also carry the voice of a mother who criticized you in the mirror. When a goal is mixed, the work isn't to throw it away. It's to separate the threads. To keep the part that gives you life and set down the part that takes it. To keep doing the work, but let go of the need to prove. To keep taking care of the body, but let go of the voice that criticizes it. The goal stays. The borrowed weight leaves.

Do this with one goal. Then, another day, with another. Little by little, you build a map of who you are beneath everything you inherited. And you'll notice something strange and beautiful: as you set down the goals that aren't yours, you don't end up with less life. You end up with more. Because all the energy you spent running other people's races comes back to you. And that energy, finally free, goes looking for what is truly yours. Often something simpler, quieter, older than everything you were chasing. A desire that was there beneath the noise of borrowed ambition, waiting, patiently, for you to stop long enough to hear it.


## 4. Infonte bridge

If this exercise touched you, if in separating the goals you felt something ease in your chest, or if you felt fear, which is also a good sign, I want to talk to you about what comes after setting down what isn't yours.

Because there's a delicate moment that happens next. When you let go of the borrowed goals, a space opens. And that space, at first, can make you dizzy. "If I don't chase that, then what do I chase? Who am I without the race?" This question frightens many people, and that's why many people prefer to go back to the familiar race, however much it exhausts them, rather than face the emptiness of not-knowing. But that emptiness isn't a hole. It's a womb. It's the space where something of yours, finally, can be born.

**Infonte** is the space I created for exactly that moment. For what emerges when you stop chasing what belongs to others and stay, in silence, listening for what is yours. Infonte is the inner source, the spring that only reveals itself when the race stops and the noise of borrowed ambition falls quiet. It isn't a place of more goals. It's a place of more truth. A space to discover, out of silence, what you, and only you, came here to do. Not what they told you you should do. What is born from within when you stop proving and begin to be.

You'll find Infonte at [infonte.viviannedossantos.com](https://infonte.viviannedossantos.com).

But before any space, stay with the gesture you've just learned. Take a goal that consumes you and ask it the question that gives this guide its name: whose goal is this? And have the courage to hear the true answer, even if it shows you that you've spent years running a race that was never yours. Because that discovery, however much it hurts, is a liberation. The moment you know the goal is borrowed, it loses the power to command you. And you, for the first time, are free to choose whether to keep it, to set it down, or to give it back to whom it always belonged.

The race with no finish line ends in only one way. Not by reaching the goal. By stopping and realizing you didn't want to be on that track. And turning around, calmly, in the direction of your own life.
