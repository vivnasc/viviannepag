# The woman who does not bother anyone

**For you, who learned to make yourself small because taking up space had consequences. Because shrinking yourself no longer protects you, it only erases you, and you can take up space without that bringing danger.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who learned to shrink herself because existing, in her home, brought danger, and that is, very often, a trauma response, not shyness. If you recognize in yourself a fear of being seen, a constant vigilance about not bothering anyone, or old wounds in which taking up space had consequences, perhaps you need not only understanding, but support. Seeking out a professional is not weakness. It is the care that wound deserves.*

---

## 1. I make myself small

Notice the space you take up, in the world, in rooms, in people's lives. It is little. You make yourself small.

You speak softly, or little. You take the corner. You don't stand out. You don't impose your opinion, you don't draw attention, you don't make yourself noticed. You move through the world like someone apologizing for being here, like someone trying not to bother, not to weigh, not to be too noticed. Like someone who, deep down, would prefer to be able to pass without anyone noticing her passing.

And there is a constant vigilance in this. You are always attentive to not bothering, to not being any trouble, to not taking up too much space. You measure yourself, contain yourself, shrink yourself, in a permanent effort to make yourself discreet, light, almost invisible.

Notice that this vigilance never stops. Even when you're well, even among people who love you, there's a part of you monitoring the space you take up, calculating whether you aren't talking too much, laughing too loudly, drawing too much attention, weighing too much. It is a background work, constant, exhausting, that no one sees, and that keeps you from being truly present, because half of you is always busy controlling the size of the other half. This is not how most people live. Most exist without measuring themselves, without this alarm lit. And you live measuring yourself, always, without knowing that this permanent measuring is not normal, it is the price of an armor.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only shyness, nor a reserved temperament, even if it looks like it. People may think you're shy, discreet, reserved. But what makes you shrink is not temperament. It is a fear, old and deep. The fear of what happens if you take up space.

Because you learned, somewhere, that existing brought consequences. That taking up space, being seen, standing out, in your home, was dangerous. And you made yourself small, not out of modesty, but to protect yourself. Shrinking yourself was your way of being safe, of not lighting the danger that existing, there, could bring.

This book is different. Because what makes you shrink is not only a trait to correct. It is, very often, a response to something you lived through. An adaptation to a place where being seen was risky. And a wound that taught you to take up no space asks for more than understanding alone. So, throughout these pages, I will repeat to you something that is hard for someone who learned not to weigh: understanding why you make yourself small may not be enough, and perhaps you need someone to help you take up, at last, your place.

And I'll give you the horizon now, so you can read without the old tightness of not knowing where this is going. You can take up space without that bringing danger. You are no longer in that house where existing was risky. Shrinking yourself protected you back then, but today it only erases you, and you can, slowly, and perhaps with help, learn to exist, at last, at your full size.

I want you to see, right away, the gulf between the woman others think they see and the one who in fact shrinks inside. What it seems, from the outside, is a shy, discreet, modest woman, who prefers the background. What it is, inside, is a woman who learned, in a time when being seen hurt, to make herself invisible in order to survive, and who keeps doing it long after the danger has passed. Your discretion is not, at its origin, a preference. It is a defense. And there is an enormous difference between choosing reticence because that is where you feel good, and shrinking yourself because being seen terrifies you. The first is a temperament. The second is a prison, even if it looks like humility.

This book does not come to tell you that you have to become exuberant, talkative, the center of all attention. It is not about changing your temperament. It is about giving you back a choice that fear took from you: that of being able to be seen when you want, of taking up your place when it is yours, of existing at your full size without an alarm forcing you to shrink. Whether you are, by nature, quieter or more expansive, you deserve for that to be a choice of yours, and not an imposition of fear. And it is that freedom, and not a new temperament, that these pages come to offer you.

Perhaps you find yourself in the smallest of gestures, the ones you barely notice taking up.

In apologizing for things that are not your fault, as if your mere presence were, in itself, a bother.

In shrinking, physically and emotionally, when there is tension, conflict, danger, trying to make yourself invisible.

In having difficulty giving your opinion, taking up your place, being seen, even when you would have every right.

In feeling a physical discomfort, almost an alarm, when you draw attention, when you're the center of attention, when you take up too much space.

They all shrink down to one thing. The making yourself small. The old fear of the consequence of existing. The certainty, engraved early, that your presence is, somehow, a bother you have to minimize.

I want to show you how the fear of bothering disguises itself as ordinary gestures, which you take for simply your way of being. In a meeting, you have an idea, and you keep quiet, because speaking would mean drawing attention, and drawing attention sets off a discomfort the idea isn't worth. Someone treats you badly, and instead of defending yourself, you shrink, you think maybe you overreacted, you apologize for something that was done to you. You enter a room, and you immediately look for the corner, the discreet spot, the place from which you won't be noticed. You receive a compliment, and instead of taking it in, you deflect it, minimize it, you're almost ashamed of it, because being seen, even for good, bothers you.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way your body reacts when, without meaning to, you become the center of attention. Someone looks at you, talks about you, asks you a question in front of everyone, and a heat rises in you, a tightness, an urgent desire to disappear, to make yourself invisible, to quickly hand the attention back to somewhere else. It isn't just shyness. It's an alarm. Your body treats visibility as a threat, and what rises is not shyness of temperament, it is the memory, engraved deep, of a time when being seen put you in danger.

Notice, too, the way you diminish yourself in your very language. You apologize before speaking. You say it's probably silly, but. You add a "maybe," a "I don't know," a "if it's not a bother," to everything you ask or assert. You make your voice small so it doesn't take up too much space, so no one is bothered by hearing it. And that way of speaking, shrunken, apologizing, is not humility, it is the echo, in your speech, of the smallness you learned to be in order to survive.

And there's something the world doesn't see, beneath your discretion. It sees a reserved, calm person, easy to have around, who is no trouble, who demands nothing. And it even likes that, and praises you for it, without knowing what it costs you. It doesn't see the permanent effort of containing yourself, the alarm you watch over, the whole person who lives compressed inside you, waiting to be able to exist at her full size. It sees the lightness, not the price. And you, who receive approval for not bothering anyone, become even more bound to smallness, because now there are also others used to you never weighing, never demanding, never taking up space.

## 2. Making myself small kept me safe

Go back. To the beginning. To when taking up space became dangerous.

Because no one shrinks this way without a reason. Your making yourself small was born from a real experience, in which existing, being seen, taking up space, brought a consequence that taught you to disappear.

Perhaps you grew up in a house where drawing attention brought danger. Where being noticed meant being a target. Where the child who stood out, who made noise, who took up space, drew rage, punishment, violence. And you learned that the way to be safe was not to be noticed, to shrink, to make yourself invisible.

Perhaps you lived with someone unpredictable, dangerous, and learned that safety lay in not lighting the storm. In giving no cause. In going unnoticed, staying in the corner, not drawing attention, so the danger wouldn't turn toward you.

Perhaps your very existence was treated as a bother. Perhaps they made you feel that you weighed, that you were in the way, that it was better when you didn't draw attention. And you learned to take up the least space possible, to apologize for being here, to make yourself small so as not to weigh in a place where your presence was not welcome.

Notice what a girl learns when taking up space, in any of these places, costs her dearly. She learns that existing is dangerous. That being seen draws danger. That taking up space has consequences. That the only way to be safe is to shrink, to disappear, to make herself invisible. And that equation, between being noticed and being hurt, is engraved deep, in the body, in the very place where the instinct to survive lives.

And that girl did the only thing she could do to fit, without danger, into that place. She made herself small. She learned to go unnoticed, not to draw attention, to take up the least. And that protected her, in that time, from the danger that existing, there, brought. It was an intelligent adaptation, perhaps a life-saving one, to a place where being seen was risky.

But the adaptation did not come undone when the danger passed. It stayed. And today, as an adult, in a world where taking up space no longer brings the consequence it once did, you continue to shrink, to make yourself small, to apologize for existing, defended against a danger that is no longer there, but that your body continues to fear.

I want you to see where this reflex of shrinking lives, because knowing where it lives explains why it isn't enough to want to stop having it. The fear of taking up space is not only in your memory, in what you remember. It is in your body, in a place deeper than words. When a girl learns, early, that being seen is dangerous, her body engraves that warning in the nervous system, below consciousness, in a register that does not erase simply by being understood. That's why, today, your shrinking in the face of visibility does not pass through a decision. It happens before you think, in a tightness, in a recoil, in a desire to disappear, because it is your body reacting, and not your reason choosing.

That's why you so often tell yourself that it's absurd, that no one is going to harm you for taking up space, and even so the fear does not obey. It does not obey because it is not born from reasoning. It is born from a body memory, ancient, that continues to treat visibility as a threat to survival. And that memory, the one that makes your chest tighten when someone looks at you, does not yield to arguments. It is learned again, slowly, through new experiences, repeated, in which the body discovers, time after time, that this time it was seen and the danger did not come. I tell you this not to frighten you with the difficulty, but to lift off you the guilt of not being able, simply by wishing it, to stop being afraid. The corner you keep returning to is not chosen by your will. It is the nature of a wound that lives in the body, and that therefore asks for a path that passes through the body, and often through hands that know how to accompany it.

I want you to see this without shrinking from it. Your making yourself small is not a defect, nor a lack of confidence that can be resolved with a few self-esteem tips. It was the most intelligent way you found to not be a target, in a place where being noticed put you at risk. The corner was never your nature, it was your shelter. You are a survivor, who learned to disappear in order to be safe. And recognizing this is honoring the child who found, in smallness, a way to survive.

I want to add a root you may recognize, because smallness is not always born from physical danger. Sometimes it is born from having been eclipsed. From having grown up in the shadow of someone who took up all the space, a father, a mother, a sibling, whose presence left no room for yours. Where, for there to be peace, you had to erase yourself, not to compete for attention, not to shine so as not to offend someone who couldn't bear to be outshone. And you learned to make yourself small not because existing brought violence, but because it brought jealousy, resentment, the withdrawal of affection, from someone who needed always to be the greatest.

A child who grows up in the shadow learns a cruel equation: my brightness threatens those I love, and so I have to hide it in order not to lose love. And she carries, into adult life, an automatic brake whenever she begins to stand out, a diffuse guilt for taking up space, a feeling that being seen is, somehow, taking a place from another. So you erase yourself even when no one asks you to erase yourself: because you internalized that your place is the shadow, and that leaving it is a betrayal.

And sometimes there is yet another root: that of having been too much for the place where you were born. A lively, intense, brilliant child, in an environment too small to contain her, with adults who didn't know what to do with so much light, and who, instead of celebrating it, told her to dim it. Don't make so much noise. Don't show off. Who do you think you are. And you learned to shrink your own greatness, to be ashamed of your size, to apologize for being too much, in a world that wanted you smaller.

In any of these roots, the lesson is similar: your size is a problem, your presence bothers, and the way to be accepted is to take up less. And it is that lesson, engraved early, that governs you today, and that turns each gesture of presence, each time you take up your place, into a transgression your body fears.

## 3. Shrinking myself no longer protects me

There is something about your smallness you have not yet let yourself see. The smallness that saved you, in the past, no longer protects you today. It only erases you.

Notice. In the time of danger, shrinking made sense. It made you invisible to what could hurt you. It was a real, effective, intelligent protection. But today, in your adult life, in most situations, there is no longer that danger. And shrinking no longer protects you from anything. It only keeps you from living, from being seen, from taking up your place.

And the space you refuse to take up is paid for at a steep price. It costs you your presence. Because to live shrunken is to live at half speed, without showing yourself, without asserting yourself, without letting the world see who you are. You pass through life like a shadow, present but not seen, there but almost absent, and everything you could be, show, give the world, stays contained, shrunken, unlived.

It costs you opportunities. Because what is not seen is not chosen, not invited, not considered. By making yourself invisible, you pass beside things that would be yours if you showed yourself, if you took up your space, if you let yourself be seen. Your smallness doesn't only protect you from danger, it also deprives you of everything that would require being seen.

And it costs you yourself. Because there is, in you, a whole person, with a voice, with an opinion, with presence, with size. And that person lives compressed, folded, hidden, by dint of your shrinking. You don't let her exist, at her real size, and so you live always smaller than you are, always short of yourself, always apologizing for a presence you would have every right to take up.

I want you to see the difference between healthy discretion and your smallness. Healthy discretion is a choice: sometimes I prefer the background, sometimes I take the foreground, according to how I feel and what the situation asks. It has both doors. Yours doesn't. Yours only knows smallness, the corner, the erasing, and being seen is not, truly, available to you, however much you might wish it. And a way of being that cannot be set down is not a choice, it is a shell. I don't come to tear from you the corner you like, if the taste for the corner and the silence is truly yours. I come to unlock the other door, that of being able to exist in plain sight, so that your way of being stops being a prison and becomes a choice.

And I want you to notice something, about how the world sometimes rewards your smallness. There are people who like you precisely because you don't bother, because you don't demand, because you take up little, because you're easy. And that approval tightens the armor even further, because now shrinking is not only safety, it is also being good, being convenient, being welcome. But notice the hidden price beneath that approval: your silenced voice, your ceded place, your life lived at half. Not everything that pleases others is good for you, and your smallness is, very often, among the things that most please those around you and most cost you.

Notice this loneliness. The loneliness of someone always present and never seen. Of someone who shrinks so much that no one knows her truly, because she never shows herself whole. You can be surrounded by people and feel that no one sees you, because you yourself hide, erase yourself, make yourself too small to be seen.

I want to go deeper into what smallness costs you, because the price is greater than it seems, and it is paid in silence.

It costs you your voice. You have things to say, opinions, ideas, truths, and you swallow them, because saying them would mean taking up space, and taking up space sets off the alarm. And so the world loses what you had to give, and you lose the experience of being heard, of mattering, of making a difference with what you think. Your voice, which could add, stays silent, and you grow used to a silence you confuse with who you are, when it is only the gag that fear put on you.

It costs you recognition. Because what is not shown is not recognized. You do, and you do well, but in the corner, without drawing attention, and so your work, your worth, your effort, go unnoticed, are attributed to others, or simply not seen. It isn't that you don't deserve recognition. It's that you hide too much to receive it, and then you feel the injustice of not being seen, without realizing that it was you who, without meaning to, erased yourself from others' eyes.

It costs you the love you could receive whole. Because someone who hides is never loved completely, only for the part she lets be seen, which is small. People love what they know, and you don't make yourself known, you shrink, you hide your size, and so you end up with a love that never reaches all of you, because all of you you never show. And there is a deep sadness in this: being loved for a reduced version of yourself, and never knowing whether you would be loved whole, because whole you never risked being.

I want you to feel the weight of taking up so little. The tiredness of the permanent vigilance of not bothering. The effort of always shrinking. The weight of apologizing for existing, day after day. It is a deep exhaustion, that of someone who can never, simply, take up her place in the world, and perhaps you already take it for normal, so used are you to disappearing.

## 4. Who am I when I stop shrinking myself

I'll set a question down here, in the open, where you cannot make it small. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you when you stop shrinking yourself? When you take up your space? When you are seen, at your real size?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Perhaps a terror.

For you, this question touches a trapdoor, because beneath it lies the terror of being seen. Because your entire identity was built on being small, discreet, invisible. You are the one who doesn't bother, the one who doesn't draw attention. Take that away, imagine yourself taking up space, being seen, standing out, and a panic appears. Then am I exposed? Do I draw the danger? Do I become a target?

And the shadow hiding here is visibility. Being seen. Taking up space. Existing at your size, in plain sight of everyone. That terrifies you, because it was by being seen that you were, one day, the target of danger. And you'd rather have invisibility, even if it erases you, than expose yourself again to being noticed, and perhaps, hurt.

Notice how this confines you to the corner. You can't show yourself, assert yourself, stand out, even when you wish to, even when you would have every right, because being seen sets off the old alarm of danger. You live containing yourself, shrinking yourself, erasing yourself, not because you have nothing to show, but because you're afraid of what happens when you show yourself.

And there is a sadness only of someone who makes herself invisible, and I want to name it. It is that of knowing, deep down, that there is more in you than what you let be seen. That you have a voice, ideas, a light, a presence, that the world never came to know, because you hid it out of fear. And sometimes, in rare moments, you feel that larger person wanting to come out, asking to exist, and you push her back inside, by reflex, by terror. To live this way, knowing that you are more than you show and not letting yourself be it, is a silent form of mourning, the mourning of a life at your full size that you postpone every day, afraid to live it.

And making yourself this small leaves you strangely fragile. Because a person who cannot be seen cannot, truly, live. She cannot assert herself, take up her place, leave her mark on the world. She is always at half, always hidden, always smaller than she is. And all her potential, her voice, her presence, stays unused, compressed, waiting for a safety she does not allow herself.

Notice how this invisibility, which you learned in order to protect yourself, also leaves you defenseless. Someone who erases herself does not assert herself, and someone who does not assert herself is more easily run over, ignored, passed over. You learned to disappear so as not to be a target, but your invisibility makes you, in another way, an easy target: the one who always gives way, who never complains, who accepts the worst place because she doesn't dare take another. And so the shrinking, which promised safety, ends up exposing you to a different kind of harm, that of being treated as if you counted for less, precisely because you make yourself count for less. To exist, to take up your place, to have a voice, is also a way of protecting yourself, healthier than disappearing.

And I want to tell you something about the terror this question raises, because it begins to disarm it from within. What terrifies you is not, truly, being seen. It is the consequence you learned that being seen brought. For your body, the two things are one, fused since early on. But they are separable. Being seen, in itself, is neutral, it is only existing in others' view, as everyone exists. The dangerous consequence belonged to that place, those people, that time. Today, in your life, being seen and being hurt no longer go together. And it is that separation, engraved in the body over time and with evidence, that gives you back the freedom to exist without fear.

I want to hand you the sentence that takes apart, from within, the fear of taking up space. Existing, today, does not put you in danger. This is what your body, even far from that house now, still does not believe. In the time when you learned to shrink yourself, being seen brought danger, and your fear was justified. But today, in your adult life, taking up space is, in most situations, simply human, and safe. People exist, take up their place, are seen, and are not, for that, attacked. You can too. The danger of that time has passed, even though your body continues to sound the alarm.

And there is a hidden cruelty in making yourself small, that perhaps you have never seen. What the old danger did to you, you continue to do to yourself. In that time, you were told, in words or in fear, that you took up too much space, that it was better when you didn't draw attention, that your presence bothered. And today, as an adult, it is you who tell yourself that, each time you shrink, keep quiet, apologize for existing. You internalized the voice that told you to disappear, and you repeat it against yourself, without anyone needing to. The danger from outside became a danger from within, and you continue to erase yourself as if you were still forced to.

And notice how this belief makes you strangely absent from your own life. You are not the protagonist of anything, not even of your own story. You are always on the margin, watching, letting others take the stage that was also yours. The decisions, the places, the opportunities, pass beside you, and you let them pass, because to grab them would mean taking up space, and taking up space terrifies you. You live, this way, a smaller life than the one you would have, not from a lack of capacity, but from a fear that confines you to the shadow, and that robs you of the lead role in your own existence.

Don't answer the question of who you are when you stop shrinking yourself just yet. It isn't deciphered by thinking, and there are those who can only live it with someone beside them holding their hand. But leave it open. Because that open question is the very space you are afraid to take up. On the day your body learns that being seen, today, is no longer danger, you stop having to shrink yourself in order to be safe. And there, only there, can you begin to take up, at last, your space.

## 5. Perhaps you need more than to understand

I have to make this book go still, here, and take up your full attention. With all seriousness. Because what makes you shrink is not, most of the time, only shyness. It is, very probably, a response to something you lived through, and that changes what you need.

If you learned to make yourself small because, in your home, existing brought real danger, then your shrinking is not a trait. Your smallness is a trauma response. An adaptation to the threat. And a trauma response is not treated only by understanding, nor by gaining confidence with a few techniques. It lives in the body, in the nervous system, in the alarm that goes off when you are seen.

I want to name, plainly, what may live beneath this habit of yours of disappearing. If there was, in your story, violence, abuse, unpredictability, an environment where drawing attention made you a target, then your making yourself small is a serious wound, and it deserves to be cared for with support.

And I need to tell you this plainly, with firmness and with tenderness, because you, of all people, are the one who least allows herself to hear it. This is not your fault, and it is not resolved by effort alone. If your fear of taking up space is a trauma response, you deserve the support of a professional, a psychologist, a therapist, someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, the alarm that goes off when you show yourself.

Seeking that help does not expose you to the danger you fear. It is, on the contrary, the safest care you can have, in a protected space, with someone trained to accompany you in learning, slowly, that being seen, today, no longer brings the consequence of back then.

And I want to tell you something with all firmness and all tenderness: if your shrinking comes from violence, from abuse, from a place where existing put you in real danger, this is not your fault, and it is not resolved by effort alone, nor by reading a book, however much it accompanies you. You deserve the support of someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, the alarm that goes off when you show yourself, the memory of danger you carry engraved deep. And seeking that support is not giving yourself an importance you don't deserve, nor bothering anyone with your pain. It is the most lucid care you can have for a wound you deserve, at last, to see heal.

I want to separate two things for you, because knowing which is yours decides the path you need. A shyness of temperament is worked through by understanding: you see the pattern, you understand the origin, and that, in time, helps to change it. A fear of existing born of trauma is something else. It does not live only in the head, it lives in the body, in the nervous system, in the alarm that goes off, below consciousness, when you are seen. And a wound like that, of someone who had to disappear, asks for more than understanding. It needs time, the body, a safe relationship where the nervous system relearns, slowly, that existing is no longer the danger it was. Alone, in the corner where you learned to hide, that is rarely achieved. It is done with someone beside you, accompanying the slow taking up of your space.

And there is a beauty kept in this, for someone who learned that being seen hurts, and I want you to see it. The helping relationship can be, for you, one of the first places where you experience being seen safely. Where you take up space, talk about yourself, are the center of someone's attention, and nothing bad happens. Where your presence does not bother, it is welcomed, it is expected. For someone who learned that being seen is danger, a space where being seen is safe, and even cared for, can be, in itself, deeply reparative, because it undoes, in practice, the old equation between existing and being at risk.

If you recognize, in your story, real danger linked to your existing, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Don't skip it. The understanding of this book accompanies you, but there are wounds that ask for more, and yours, perhaps, is one of them. And a wound made of having had to disappear deserves, at last, all the care you never let yourself ask for.

## 6. Existing without justifying myself

If your smallness is a defense against an old danger, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can exist without justifying yourself.

Notice how you exist today. Apologizing. Not always with words, but with your whole posture. Shrunken, contained, justifying your presence, as if taking up space were something you have to apologize for, to explain, to make up for by not weighing.

And I propose the very thing your body learned to fear. Existing without justification. Taking up your space like someone who has a right to it, simply, without apologizing, without explaining yourself, without shrinking. As any person takes up their place in the world, by existing, and not by earning it or excusing it.

Notice how other people, the ones who don't carry this wound, simply take up their place. They speak when they have something to say, they sit where they please, they ask for what they need, without ceremony, without apology, without alarm. It isn't arrogance, it's the normal thing: existing at your full size, without having to justify it. And that normal thing, which costs them nothing, seems to you a presumption, because your measuring stick is calibrated for invisibility. What you have to learn is not to be larger than others. It is only to allow yourself the same thing they allow themselves without thinking: existing, in plain sight, without asking forgiveness for it.

Because you have a right to exist, to be seen, to take up space, exactly like any person. That right does not have to be earned, nor justified, nor compensated for. It is yours, for being. And an entire life apologizing for it comes from a time when, in fact, your existence was treated as a bother, but that was the error of that place, not a truth about your right to be here.

I want to take up space with the truth, plainly. If yours is a trauma wound, this change is not made simply by convincing yourself of it. Your body will keep sounding the alarm when you take up space, however much your head knows there's no danger. So this path, very often, is made with help, with someone to accompany you in teaching your system, slowly, that being seen, today, is safe.

But there are gestures of presence you can already risk, in a small way and with care. You can begin by taking up a little more space, in a small way. Giving your opinion, once. Letting pass, without your customary apology, something that wasn't your fault. Letting yourself be seen, in a small moment, instead of shrinking by reflex. And noticing what happens, outside and inside your chest.

Outside, almost certainly, nothing of the danger you fear. Inside, the alarm, the desire to shrink again, to apologize, to disappear. And to stay with that, without shrinking, is to give your body a new piece of evidence. That you took up space, and remained safe. That being seen, this time, did not bring the danger of before.

I want to warn you of a reaction, so you don't take it for a sign that you erred. The first time you take up more space, even in a small thing, the alarm will likely rise strong, out of proportion, as if you had done something serious. You will feel an almost physical desire to retreat, to apologize, to shrink again, to repair the presumption. That is not a sign that you erred. It is your old system going off, treating this small gesture as if it were the danger of long ago. Don't shrink back to obey it. Leave the space taken up, stay with the discomfort, and give your body the chance to discover that, this time, no danger came.

And I want you to choose well where you begin, because it matters. Don't begin with the most hostile place, with the most dangerous person, with the situation that frightens you most. Begin where it's safe, with those who wish you well, in small gestures of presence that would hardly bring any bad reaction. It is not cheating. It is giving your body the best conditions to learn the new truth, instead of exposing it, right from the start, to the confirmation of the old fear. There is time, afterward, for the harder places. First, the body needs evidence that taking up space can go well.

Each time you take up space, in a small way, and nothing catastrophic happens, you give your system a new piece of evidence. That existing, today, does not bring the danger of back then. And it is with evidence, repeated, in the body, and not only with ideas, in the head, that the smallness, slowly, loosens. The shrinking is not undone by convincing yourself you can be seen. It is undone by taking up space, in a small way, and surviving, time after time, until the body believes it.

I want to speak to you of a fear that perhaps holds you back, and that is only your wound speaking: that asking for help, or taking up space, is an exaggeration, is giving too much importance to yourself, is, at bottom, bothering people. Notice how even to care for yourself you hesitate, afraid of weighing, of being too much, of taking a place you don't deserve. That very hesitating is the symptom of this book. You don't have to be in collapse to deserve care, nor to justify yourself to have a right to a place. Taking up space to care for yourself, to listen to yourself, to seek support, is exactly the kind of presence this crossing invites you to allow.

The turn, when it comes, is felt in the body as a physical relief, like air coming in. It is the time you take up your space, when you are seen, and instead of the usual panic, you feel, for an instant, that it's all right. That nothing terrible happened. That you exist, in plain sight, and remain safe. It is a small instant, but it is enormous, because it is your body learning, perhaps for the first time, that being seen is no longer danger.

I want you to see what opens up when existing stops being danger. Your voice opens up, which was swallowed, and you discover that you have things to say, and that saying them brings no catastrophe, it brings, very often, connection. Your place opens up, which you were ceding, and you discover that taking it up takes nothing from anyone, and that there is room for you as there is for everyone. The possibility of being loved whole opens up, and not only for the small part you let be seen, because now you show yourself entire, and you discover, with astonishment, that whole you are more lovable, and not less. And above all, life at your full size opens up, the life you had been living shrunken, and that, after all, fit you whole, if you had let yourself take it up.

## 7. Taking up my space

There is one verb that names this whole crossing, and it is the very thing you learned not to do. To take up.

To take up your space. Your place in the world. The space that is yours for existing, and that you grew used to ceding, to shrinking, to excusing.

And I want to show you, with care, what taking up is, when you have spent a lifetime ceding.

To take up your space is to stop apologizing for existing. It is to assert your presence, your voice, your opinion, your place, without shrinking, without justifying yourself, without making yourself small so as not to bother. It is to exist at your real size, and not at the reduced size to which fear confined you.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you take up more space, the old alarm, the terror of being seen, the desire to shrink again, to disappear, to make yourself invisible. And you will want to return to smallness, to the corner, to the safety of not drawing attention.

But it is precisely there, in that taking up your space despite the alarm, that your healing lies. Because each time you show yourself, when you take up your place, and nothing catastrophic happens, you teach your body something new. That existing, today, is safe. That being seen does not bring the danger of before. That you have a right to your space, and that taking it up does not make you a target.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that told you to erase yourself, is engraved deep, and it will appear with each gesture of presence, with its usual harshness. It will tell you that you're exaggerating, drawing attention, bothering, being too much. You don't have to silence it all at once, the voice that tells you to shrink, it's enough to stop obeying it. To hear it say don't show off, and to answer it, slowly, I have a right to my place like everyone else. And each time you take up your space despite it, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more your own, firmer, that perhaps you never had: the one that knows that existing is not a crime, it is a right.

I want you to see that taking up your space is not becoming arrogant, domineering, someone who runs over others. It is something else. It is taking up your place, the rightful one, the one everyone takes up, and that you refused yourself. It is existing at your size, no more, and, at last, no less. It is ceasing to live shrunken and beginning to live whole.

Notice this fear, because it is typical of those who shrank their whole life: the fear that, if you stop erasing yourself, you'll become excessive, exhibitionist, someone people pull away from. But look at the distance between where you are and the rightful point. You are so used to taking up almost nothing that the normal size, the one everyone takes up without thinking, will seem to you, at first, an enormous exaggeration. It isn't. It's only that your measuring stick is out of calibration, set for invisibility. What you will feel as too much will be, almost always, only enough, your legitimate place, the space that was always yours and that you learned to cede.

And I want to undo the confusion between taking up space and taking space from others. You learned, perhaps, that if you exist, someone loses, that your presence robs others of their place. But space is not like that. In a healthy relationship, there is room for everyone, and your presence diminishes no one's, it adds to it. When you take up your space, you are not taking anything from anyone, you are bringing yourself to the table, and a table with everyone present is richer, not poorer. The fear that your existing harms others is an inheritance from that time when someone needed to be the only one to shine, but it is not a truth about how relationships work when they are healthy.

And I want you to see that taking up your space does not make you less good, less kind, less attentive to others. Your delicacy stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops coming from fear, and starts coming from choice. You keep caring for others, giving them room, being attentive, but because you want to, and not because you're afraid of being noticed. And a kindness that comes from choice, and not from terror, is more whole, freer, more your own, than the one that came from erasing yourself so as not to bother.

And there is a joy waiting for you, on the other side of this fear of bothering. The joy of being seen, and not erasing yourself. Of taking up your place, without apologizing. Of existing at your real size, with your voice, your presence, your space. Of letting the world see who you are, instead of living like a shadow of yourself. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day you take up, at last, the space that was always yours.

And I want to leave you a thought, to take from these pages. You do not have to deserve your place by being small, agreeable, invisible. Your place is not a prize you earn by taking up little. It is a right you have for existing, today, now, at your full size, with all your voice and all your presence, simply for being, and for being no longer in that place where existing cost dearly.

I don't leave you a recipe for ceasing to shrink yourself, and this path is, very often, made with hands to accompany you. But I leave you the principle to take up. Take up your space, in a small way, first. Let yourself be seen. Stop apologizing for existing. And discover, slowly, and perhaps with hands to accompany you, that existing, today, is no longer danger, and that you have a right to your place in the world, exactly like any person.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. But, for someone who learned to disappear, understanding can stay halfway, and to keep that from you would be to leave you alone again with your smallness.

It doesn't unfold a lifetime of smallness all at once. It does not make you, tomorrow, take up a space you ceded your whole life. But it changes enough so that you stop judging yourself for shyness, and begin to understand that your making yourself small is not a defect, it is a wound.

Now you know that you make yourself small, and that this is not only shyness, it is a defense.

You know that you learned to shrink yourself in a place where existing brought real danger.

You know that smallness saved you back then, but today it only erases you.

You know whom you fear to be when you stop shrinking yourself, and that beneath it lies the terror of visibility.

You know that perhaps you need more than to understand, and that seeking help does not expose you to danger, it protects you from it.

You know that you can exist without justifying yourself, and that the verb is to take up, your space.

And you know, now, that your size was never the problem, that being seen is not a crime, and that you have a right to your place in the world exactly like any person, for existing, without having to earn it or excuse it. And that on the other side of this fear is a life at your full size, with your voice and your presence whole, waiting to be, at last, lived.

I want to leave you with an image to hold, one that takes up room in you.

Imagine someone who grew up in a place where any movement could draw danger, and who learned to stay still, shrunken, in the corner, not to breathe loudly, to make herself invisible, because that was how she survived. And who grew up, and left that place, but stays still in the corner, not breathing loudly, because her body never knew it could already move.

The crossing is your body learning that it can already take up space. That it can already move, speak loudly, be seen, exist at its full size. That the dangerous place is behind you, and that the world, today, does not attack you for existing. Relearning to move at your full size is not easy, and there are bodies that manage it only with someone beside them. But on the other side of smallness is a whole life, at your real size, waiting for you.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so you don't live it as one more test you have to be perfect at. There will be setbacks. Days when you take up your space, and days when you shrink yourself completely again, apologizing for existing, as always. And your old voice will tell you it isn't worth it, that being seen really is dangerous, that it's safer the way you always were, in the corner. Don't shrink back into believing it. The times when you shrink again do not erase the road traveled, they are part of it. No one undoes all at once a smallness built over a whole lifetime. It is undone back and forth, one gesture of presence at a time, and each time you take up space and nothing bad happens, a new piece of evidence stays in the body, and it is from that evidence, accumulated, that safety, slowly, is reborn.

And I want to speak to you of those who watch you, if there are eyes that learn to exist by seeing you exist, children, people close to you. If they see you live erased, apologizing for existing, always ceding your place, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it's likely they'll shrink too, that they'll live the same fear of taking up space. The most freeing thing you can do for those who watch you is not to give them the example of the discreet woman who never bothers anyone. It is to show them a woman who takes up her place, who lets herself be seen, who exists at her full size without apologizing. Sometimes a woman learns to take up her space, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn those she loves to the same smallness.

And there is one last thing, simple, that I need to leave you before I close. You were not too much. The girl who made herself small in order to survive did what she had to do, and she did it well. There was nothing wrong with her size, her light, her presence. The error was in the place that didn't know how to contain her, that told her to erase herself instead of celebrating her. Her size was never too much to take up; there is nothing in her to make smaller. There is only one thing to say to her, slowly, time after time, until the body believes it: it has passed, you can exist now, your size was never the problem.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds up everything I have told you here. I can take up space without creating danger.

Understanding yourself was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not fulfilled by reading. It is fulfilled by taking up, slowly, your space. And, very often, it is made with help, with someone prepared to accompany your body in learning that being seen, today, is safe. Because a wound of someone who had to disappear in order to survive deserves hands that know how to hold her, and seeking them is not bothering anyone, nor giving yourself too much importance. It is caring, at last, for someone who learned she had to disappear.

And if there is a single idea that stays with you after you close the book, let it be this. The world does not become better with less of you. It becomes better with your voice, your presence, your full size. What you learned to hide because it was dangerous is, very often, precisely what you have most valuable to give. And taking up your space takes nothing from anyone: it gives back to the world a person it had been losing, hidden in the corner, waiting to be able, at last, to exist.

And before you close the book, I leave you one request only. I know that, for you, it is among the hardest.

This week, take up a little more space, in something small. Give your opinion without swallowing it. When something goes wrong that wasn't your doing, hold the apology that rises in you automatically. Let yourself be seen, in a moment when you usually disappear. And notice the alarm that rises, the terror of being noticed, the desire to shrink. And take up your space anyway. Because it is in that existing, endured despite the fear, that your body begins, slowly, to learn that you can already be seen, and that the dangerous place, after all, is behind you.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are bothering someone, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you have as much right to exist as anyone around you.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
