# The one who belongs nowhere

**For you, who always feel a little on the outside, everywhere. Because the home you seek out there is perhaps built first within you.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who never feels she is on the inside, anywhere, and there is a point at which this wandering stops being a trait and becomes an isolation that hurts. If you recognize in yourself a deep loneliness that never lifts, a sadness tied to never belonging, or a sense of having no place in the world, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your wandering is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. On the outside, everywhere

Notice a sensation that accompanies you, perhaps from always, and that you know all too well. You're in a group, in a family, in a city, in a place where, in theory, you belong. And you feel, even so, a little on the outside.

It's not that they exclude you, necessarily. It's that you, inside, never feel truly on the inside. There is always a distance, a film, a sense that you're observing from outside a belonging that is the others', and not quite yours. As though there were, between you and the world, a thin pane of glass, transparent, but that separates you, always, from a belonging you see but don't touch.

You're with your family, and you feel a little a stranger to it. You're with your friends, and there's a corner of you that never feels part. You live in a place, and it's not quite your home. You belong to several places, and to none of them wholly. You're of many worlds, and of none completely, a kind of foreigner everywhere, even in the places where, on paper, you're on the inside.

And you search. You change city, group, relationship, looking for the place where, finally, you'll feel at home. And with each new place, at first, it seems it's there. But after a while, the old sensation returns. On the outside. Again. This isn't quite my place either.

And perhaps you don't even remember ever having felt, without a doubt, on the inside. Having had a place that was yours without question, a ground where you settled without thinking. For many uprooted people, this sensation of being outside is so old, so constant, that it seems the natural way of existing, it seems to be who they are. But it isn't. It is not normal to live always on the margin, always passing through, always searching. It's only a wound so old that you stopped seeing it as a wound, and came to take it for identity, for temperament, or for fate.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not a defect of yours, nor coldness, nor an inability to connect. People may find you a little distant, hard to reach, always with one foot outside. But it's not that you don't know how to belong. It's that you learned, very early, not to belong, and you carry that learning everywhere.

There is, beneath this wandering, an uprooting. Not always geographical, even though sometimes it is. But almost always emotional. You grew up without a firm ground to settle on, without a place where you felt, without a doubt, on the inside. And that lack of root became your way of being in the world: on the outside, everywhere.

And I want you to understand that the belonging we're speaking of is not only belonging to a spot on the map. It's something deeper: it's the sense of having a place in the world that is yours, of being received, of being part, of not being alone in the universe. That belonging, the most basic of all, should have been given to you early, in the arms of those who received you, in the home that took you in, in feeling that there was a place made for you. And if it was missing, what was left was this wandering, this search for a place that is, deep down, the search for that first welcome that wasn't there, or wasn't there enough.

This book is not going to promise you a place where, finally, you'll fit perfectly. I have come to look at something else. At the root that you lack, and where it can, at last, be built. Which is perhaps not in a place out there, but in a ground that is built within you.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can belong without finding yourself everywhere. The home you seek, from city to city, from group to group, perhaps does not exist out there, waiting to be found. Perhaps it is built within. You can be a home for yourself. And from that inner home, belong to the places outside in another way, without the hunger for them to be the ground that only you can give yourself.

I want you to understand, right away, the difference between what it looks like and what it is. What it looks like, from the outside, is a free person, unattached, who doesn't cling to places. What it is, on the inside, is a person who never had a firm ground, and who goes searching for it, without knowing that she searches for it in the wrong place. Your wandering is not, at its root, a choice of freedom. It's the mark of a root that was missing. And there is an enormous difference between not attaching yourself because you choose freedom, and not being able to stay because you never had anywhere to feel safe. The first is lightness. The second is a wound dressed up as lightness.

This book does not come to tell you that you have to stay in one place forever, nor that traveling, moving, exploring, is wrong. If your soul is one of movement, that is yours, and it is good. It comes to distinguish moving for pleasure from fleeing because you can't stay; exploring the world from a ground from drifting in search of a ground you never had. And it comes to show you that this ground, which you seek from place to place, is in no place waiting for you, it's yet to be built within you, and that, having it, you can move or stay, but always from home.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In always feeling a bit of an impostor in the groups you belong to, as though at any moment they were going to discover that you're not quite from there.

In idealizing other places, other lives, other groups, where you imagine you would finally belong, and in being disappointed every time you arrive, because the real place is never the imagined ideal.

In having difficulty putting down roots, in staying, in committing to a place, because a part of you is always on the way out, looking for the right spot.

In feeling, even surrounded by people, a background loneliness, that of someone who never feels, truly, at home.

In hesitating when they ask you where you're from, because there is no place you feel, without a doubt, as yours, and the answer is never simple.

In leaving, or thinking of leaving, the moment a place begins to become familiar, as though belonging, instead of calming you, activated the urge to flee.

They are all the same thing. The wandering. The one who belongs, wholly, nowhere.

I want to show you how this shows up in everyday life, in things you may already take to be who you are. In a group where everyone seems at ease, sharing an intimacy that flows, you observe from outside, with the sense of being behind a pane of glass, present but not part. In a conversation where the others recall a shared past, a belonging they share, you feel a pang at not having, yourself, that place, that shared history, that ground. And when someone asks you where you're from, you hesitate, because the answer is never simple, because there is no place you feel, without a doubt, as yours.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way that, the moment you begin to feel part of a place, the impulse to leave arises at once. As though belonging, instead of calming you, set off an alarm, and the part of you that's always on the way out wanted to go before it put down too many roots. It's not that you can't enter the places. It's that you can't stay, because staying is risking putting down roots, and putting down roots is risking being, once again, torn out.

And there's something nobody sees, beneath your apparent independence. People see someone free, well-traveled, who adapts to everything, who doesn't attach herself, and perhaps they even envy that lightness. They don't see the loneliness underneath, the hunger for a ground, the weariness of someone who never arrives, the sadness of someone who is always on the outside. They see the freedom, not the uprooting. And you, seen as free and independent, become even more bound to the wandering, because even your lack of root is read as a quality, and not as the wound it is.

## 2. I'm from here, but I'm not

Go back. To the beginning. To where you learned not to belong.

Uprooting has many origins, and perhaps you recognize yours in one of them.

It may have been geographical. Moves, migrations, a childhood without a fixed place, or a family that came from somewhere else, and where you grew up feeling that you weren't quite from there, but that you also no longer belonged back there. A daughter of two worlds, and of neither wholly.

It may have been familial. An emotionally fragmented family, without a sense of belonging, of tradition, of common ground. Or a family where there were secrets, silences, things left unsaid, that left you feeling that you never knew the whole house, that there was always a closed-off part from which you were excluded.

It may have been through exclusion. From being, as a child, the different one, the one who didn't fit, the one left out, in the playground, in the family, in the group. And you learned, early, that your place was the margin, and you carried that margin with you.

Or it may have been more subtle. A sensation, without a name, that you were never seen, known, received wholly anywhere. That there was always a part of you that stayed outside, even inside the home.

Or it may have been from being, in some way, different from your own family. The one who thought differently, felt differently, wanted things different from what your family valued. And in a house where there was no room for that difference, you learned that belonging required hiding who you were, and that showing yourself wholly was risking being left out. You grew up, then, with a conditional belonging: you were accepted as long as you hid your difference, and so, even inside the home, you never felt received wholly, because the most you part of you had to stay hidden for there to be a place.

Notice what a child learns, in any of these houses. She learns that she doesn't belong, or that she only half belongs, hiding a part of herself. That her place is the margin, the in-between, the outside. That belonging wholly, being received entirely, with everything she is, belongs to the others, and not to her. And that learning, etched early, becomes the lens through which you see all places, making you feel, in any of them, the stranger of always.

Notice what a child learns in a place like this. She learns that she doesn't belong. That her place is the margin, the in-between, the outside. That belonging wholly belongs to the others, to those who have a ground, a root, a home that is, without a doubt, theirs. And she, no.

And that learning is etched deep. It becomes her identity. I'm the one who isn't quite from anywhere. And today, as an adult, you carry that identity everywhere, and so, everywhere, you feel on the outside, because that is how you learned to be.

There is a sentence that perhaps sums up your childhood. I'm from here, but I'm not. I belong, but not quite. I'm inside, but I feel outside. And that sentence, which described your origin, became the way you feel all places, forever.

And I want you to see how this sentence became a self-fulfilling prophecy. Because, feeling on the outside, you behave like someone who is on the outside: you stay more reserved, more at a distance, always half on the way out. And the others, sensing that distance, treat you, in fact, as a little on the outside, not by excluding you, but by responding to the drawing-back that you, without meaning to, show them. And that treatment confirms for you the sense of not belonging, which makes you draw back even more, in a circle that feeds itself. Your belief that you don't belong produces, in the world, exactly the signs you then use as proof of it. And breaking the circle begins within, by ceasing to start from the certainty that you're not from there.

Notice, too, that very often it's not you who is left out, it's you who put yourself out. You're in a group, and instead of entering, you stay observing, waiting for an invitation you already have, for a permission that is already yours, for a sign that you belong that will never come because belonging is not given, it's taken. You wait to be included, instead of including yourself. And you stay on the margin, not because they put you there, but because you wait, at the door, for a call that is yours to give to yourself. Entering, committing, acting like someone who already belongs, is very often what is missing, and what depends on no one but you.

I want you to see this without guilt. The lack of root you inherited, most of the time, was not created to wound you. It was the circumstance of your family, of your history, of your unstable ground. But it left in you a wound, that of someone who never belonged, and that wound deserves to be seen, so it can, at last, begin to heal.

And I want you to see where this is stored, because it matters. The sense of not belonging is not only an idea you have about yourself, it's a way of being etched deep, earlier than words. A baby, a small child, who was not received with a sense of belonging, who did not feel a safe ground supporting her, etches that lack in the body, in a place older than reason. That's why, today, you don't decide to feel on the outside, you feel on the outside before you think, automatically, even in places where, rationally, you know that you belong. And that's why it isn't enough to tell yourself that you belong, in order to feel it: the wound lives below reason, and the missing root is planted slowly, with experience, and often with help.

And I want to name something that you may never have connected to this: the sense of imposture you feel in groups. That fear that, at any moment, they'll discover that you're not quite from there, that you don't really belong, that you're there by mistake. That imposture is not about your abilities, it's about belonging: it's the uprooted child waiting, always, to be unmasked as the outsider, the stranger, the one who isn't really of the group. And that permanent expectation of expulsion, even when no one excludes you, is one of the most exhausting ways in which uprooting is lived.

## 3. The home that never serves

There is a pattern you may recognize, and that comes straight from this wound. You search, without cease, for the perfect home. The place where, finally, you'll belong wholly. And none serves.

You change city, looking for the right spot. You change group, looking for where you'll fit. You change relationship, work, life, always with the hope that, this time, you'll arrive at a place that will be, at last, home. And at first, each new place seems to be it. There's a hope, a promise, a sense that maybe it's there.

But time passes, and the old sensation returns. On the outside. Again. This place isn't quite mine either. And you leave, or you think of leaving, looking for the next one, that maybe, that one yes.

Notice what lies beneath this search. The belief that your uprooting is resolved out there. That there is, somewhere, a place made to your measure, where you'll finally feel at home, and that your task is to find it. And until you find it, you wander, errant, from place to place, always searching, always leaving.

But there's a problem with this search, and I want you to see it. The sense of not belonging doesn't come from the places. It comes from you. It's a wound you carry, and that goes with you everywhere. That's why no place cures it, because the problem is not in the place, it's in the root that you lack, and that no place out there can give you.

It's like searching, in the world, for a house for a lack that is inner. However good the house, the lack remains, because the lack was not of a house, it was of root. And you change house, and the lack changes with you, intact, into the new house.

Notice how this explains the disappointment that repeats itself. At the start of each new place, the hope and the novelty cover the lack, and so it seems you've finally found it. But the novelty passes, the place becomes familiar, and the lack, which was only covered, reappears, intact, because it was never of the place. And you, without seeing this, conclude that the place wasn't the right one after all, and you leave for the next, where the novelty will cover the lack again, until it passes, and the lack returns. It's a cycle that repeats because you pursue, in each place, the solution to something that no place resolves. Only when you stop searching outside, and look at the lack within, can the cycle be broken.

I want you to feel the injustice this does to you, without your seeing it. Each place you abandon takes with it the possibility of a belonging that might have grown there, if you'd stayed. Each departure is the end of a root that had barely begun. And so you accumulate beginnings without ends, places half inhabited, relationships half created, and no deep root, not because the places didn't serve, but because you never gave them the time to serve. Your wandering didn't only spare you from exclusion. It deprived you, too, of all the belongings that could have been, if you had stayed long enough for them to grow.

I want you to feel the weariness of this endless search. The wandering of someone who never arrives. The hope that is reborn with each new place and dies with each disappointment. The weight of never being able to rest, because you're always on the way out, always looking for the spot that, this time, will be home. It's a weariness of a whole life spent searching outside for what can only be built within.

And there is a particular loneliness in this. That of someone who is always passing through. Who doesn't put down deep roots, because a part of her is always ready to leave. Who has many places, and no home. Who knows many people, and few wholly, because truly belonging requires staying, committing, letting yourself put down roots, and you, afraid that it isn't the right spot, never stay long enough to put down root.

And there is a trap in this search, that I want you to see. Idealization. Because until you arrive at the perfect place, you can idealize it, imagine it, dream it without flaws. The place you don't yet have is always perfect, because it's imagined. And the place where you are is always imperfect, because it's real. So you live always comparing the real, with its inevitable flaws, with a flawless ideal that exists only in your head, and the real always loses. Not because it's bad, but because no real place can compete with an imagined ideal. And that unfair comparison is what makes you always leave, in search of the ideal, which, being ideal, you'll never find anywhere.

Notice, too, how your search feeds itself. Each disappointment confirms to you that that place wasn't the right one, and renews the hope that the right one is in the next. And so the search never ends, because each ending is only the beginning of the next search. It's a cycle that can last a whole life, from place to place, from group to group, always with the same hope and the same disappointment, without ever seeing that the problem is not in the places that don't serve, but in the root that's missing, and that no place, however good, can give you.

## 4. Who am I if I stop searching for a home

I'm going to leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who are you, if you stop searching for a place to belong?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs.

For you, this question touches a deep fear. Because the search became your identity. You're the one who wanders, the one who seeks, the one who hasn't yet found her place. Take away the search, imagine yourself stopping, staying, ceasing to seek the perfect home, and an emptiness appears. Then what am I, if not the one who searches? What place do I have, if I stop searching for a place?

And the shadow hiding here is wandering as identity. You became the eternal traveler, the one who doesn't belong, the one on the outside. And that identity, however lonely, is yours, and letting it go frightens, because without the search you don't know who you'd be.

Notice, too, something subtle. The search, however much it tires you, protects you. As long as you search for the perfect place, you never have to commit to any. You never have to stay, to put down roots, to risk belonging and, perhaps, being excluded again. The search keeps you always on the way out, and being on the way out is safe, because someone who is on the way out is never, truly, left out, she was already leaving.

Beneath the wandering, there is, very often, a fear of belonging. Because belonging is to risk. It's to stay, to give yourself over to a place, to a group, to people, and to risk that, after you've put down roots, they'll exclude you again, as happened, perhaps, in the beginning. The wandering spares you that risk. You don't put down roots, and so you can never be torn out. But the price is the loneliness of never truly belonging.

Notice this defense, because it's ingenious and sad. If I never truly belong, I can never be, truly, excluded. If I'm already always half outside, no one can put me outside, because I'm already there, by my own choice. You turned your wandering into a protection against the pain of being left out, anticipating it, staying always on the way out so that no one could expel you. But it's a protection that charges you the very good it was trying to protect: so as never to be excluded, you never let yourself be included; so as never to be torn out, you never put down roots. And so you stay, safe from exclusion and condemned to loneliness, which is, deep down, an exclusion that you yourself choose so as not to suffer it from others.

And I want you to see the courage that belonging requires, and that you, perhaps, never allowed yourself to have. Belonging is risky, yes. It requires staying, giving yourself over, letting yourself be seen, and accepting that you can, again, be wounded. It takes courage for that, especially for someone who has already been excluded. But it's the only way to truly belong: by risking. The wandering seems brave, of someone who needs no one, but it is, deep down, the safer option, the one that avoids the risk. True courage, for you, is not to keep leaving. It's to risk staying.

I want to tell you something about this emptiness, because it begins to transform it. The emptiness you feel when you imagine stopping the search is not proof that you have no place. It's the sign of a root yet to be built. Of a ground you never had, and that you go on searching for outside, without knowing that it's within that it's built. The emptiness is not your sentence of wandering. It's your root, yet to be planted, calling for you.

And I want to tell you, clearly, that stopping the search is not giving up on belonging, it's changing direction: ceasing to search outward and beginning to build inward. The search, which seems to you the path to belonging, is, deep down, what keeps you from reaching it, because it keeps you always on the way out, always at the door, always on the way to the next. Stopping is not resigning yourself to never belonging. It's acknowledging that belonging is not at the end of an endless search, it's at the beginning of a building, that of a ground within you, which, once planted, allows you, at last, to belong to the world.

Don't answer just yet the question of who you are if you stop searching. But leave it open. Because it is in it that the door lies. On the day there is, within you, a ground, a home, a root that you carry with you, you stop needing to search for it out there. And then, only then, can you belong to the places without the hunger for them to be the home that only you can give yourself.

## 5. Putting down roots without depending on the outside

If you search out there for a root that you lack within, then there is a new truth waiting for you. The root you seek is built within you, and not in a place in the world.

Read this slowly, because it reverses your entire search. You go searching, from place to place, for the home that you lack. And the home that you lack is not a spot. It's an inner root, a sense of belonging to yourself, a ground that you carry with you wherever you go. As long as you search for it outside, you don't find it, because it isn't outside. It's yet to be built within.

I want to distinguish two things, because it matters. There is belonging to a place, which always depends, in part, on the outside, and that can be given to you or taken away. And there is being a home for yourself, which depends on no one, which is yours, and which no one can take from you. The first is fragile, because it's in the hands of others, of places, of circumstances. The second is firm, because it's in yours.

And what has been missing for you is not the first. It's the second. You go trying to resolve the lack of an inner root by searching for outer belongings, and so it never reaches you, because a place out there, however good, cannot give you the ground within that only you can build.

Being a home for yourself means building, within, a sense of belonging to yourself. An inner place where you feel at home, wherever you are. A root that is not in the ground of a city, but in you, and that for that reason travels with you, and cannot be torn out.

How is that inner home built? Not from one day to the next, and not entirely alone, sometimes. But it begins with one thing. With your ceasing to search outside, and turning your attention inward. With asking yourself not where do I belong, but who am I, what is mine, what makes me feel, myself, at home with myself. With cultivating a relationship with yourself in which you are, at last, your own ground.

It's also built through small concrete things, that create a sense of ground in everyday life. Rituals that are yours, repeated, that you carry with you wherever you go: a way of beginning the day, an inner place you return to, objects that accompany you, habits that give you continuity. Because the inner home is not only an idea, it's made of small practices that tell you, every day, here is who I am, here is what is mine, here is my ground. And those practices, being yours, go with you, and give you, in any place, the continuity of a belonging that does not depend on the spot where you are.

And it's built, above all, by ceasing to flee from yourself. Because one of the reasons you never feel at home is that perhaps you don't feel at home with yourself, with who you are, with what you feel, with your own history. And no one feels at home fleeing from themselves. Returning to yourself, knowing yourself, accepting yourself, making peace with your history, is building the most basic ground of all: that of feeling, at last, good in your own skin. Because the first home, the one you carry everywhere, is yourself, and it's in it that one learns, first, to belong.

I want to pause here for a moment, carefully, because there is a point at which the wandering stops being a trait and becomes a wound that asks for more. If the sense of not belonging has become a deep loneliness that never lifts, a sadness that isolates you, an emptiness of having no place in the world that takes away your spirit, that deserves to be cared for with support. And if, beneath the uprooting, there are painful experiences of exclusion, of severe instability, of a childhood without ground, that still run through you today, then we are in the presence of something that does not undo itself just by reading. Building a root that never existed, planting an inner ground from scratch, is rarely done alone. It's done, very often, with hands to accompany you, and in a safe relationship that is, itself, a first ground.

The awakening, when it comes, is a strange and new sensation. It's the day you feel at home not because you're in a place, but because you're with yourself. When you notice that you carried, to some place or other, a sense of belonging that didn't come from the place, it came from you. It's a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves the belief that your home was somewhere out there, waiting to be found. Your home was in you, waiting to be built.

## 6. Being a home for myself

There is a verb for this crossing. To put down roots.

And it's a particular putting-down-of-roots. Not in a place, but in you. To put down root within, in a ground that is yourself, and that you carry wherever you go.

Being a home for yourself is the heart of this. It's ceasing to search, outside, for the home that you lack, and beginning to be, yourself, your own home. To have, within, a place where you feel at home, wherever you are, whoever you're with.

Notice what this frees. When you're a home for yourself, you stop depending on a place to feel at home. You can be in a new city, in a strange group, far from everything, and carry with you your root, your inner home, and feel, even there, at home with yourself. Belonging stops being in the hands of the places, and comes to be in yours.

And, paradoxically, it's when you're a home for yourself that you manage, at last, to belong to the places outside. Because you stop arriving at them with the desperate hunger for them to be your ground. You arrive with your ground already with you, and from it you can connect, commit, put down roots in a place, without the impossible weight of it having to be your salvation. Someone who is a home for herself belongs lightly. Someone who isn't demands of places that they be the home she lacks, and none can bear that weight.

Notice this hunger, and how it pushes away. When you arrive at a place, or a relationship, with the desperate hunger for it to be your ground, you demand of it something it cannot give, and that demand, even if you don't speak it, weighs, suffocates, pushes away. People feel when they're sought to fill a void, and they draw back. And idealized places, under the weight of having to be your salvation, always disappoint, because no real place can save anyone. The hunger that makes you search is, ironically, what keeps you from belonging, because you always arrive asking too much. When you stop having that hunger, because you've already given yourself the ground, you begin to arrive at places to inhabit them, and not for them to save you, and it's then that, without the weight, you can finally belong.

And I want you to see that being a home for yourself is not a destination reached all at once, it's a relationship that is cultivated, like any other. Just like a friendship, or a love, the relationship with yourself grows with attention, with time, with care. There will be days when you feel more at home with yourself, and days when less, as in any relationship. What matters is not abandoning it, not fleeing from yourself when it's hard, always returning, always caring. Because it's from that relationship, cultivated over time, that the firm ground you searched for everywhere is born, and that, after all, is only built in the one place that goes with you forever: within you.

I want you to see that putting down roots in yourself is not closing yourself off, becoming an island, ceasing to need others and places. It's the opposite. It's having a ground from which to connect to the world, instead of drifting in search of a ground. The people most capable of belonging, of creating deep ties, of staying, are, very often, those who have a firm inner root, because they can give themselves to a place without fear of losing themselves in it.

It's going to cost you, because the old voice, the one that says you're not from anywhere, is etched deep, and it will appear each time you try to stay, to put down roots, to belong, with its warning of always. It will tell you that this isn't your place either, that it's better to look for another, that you'll never really belong. The work is not to silence it all at once, it's to stop obeying it. To hear it say you're not from here, and to answer it, slowly, I carry my home with me, and I can belong from it. And each time you stay despite it, and put down roots a little, and aren't torn out, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows belonging didn't have to come from outside.

And I want to leave you something, about the ones who watch you, especially if you have children. Because uprooting is passed on, from generation to generation, without anyone meaning it. A person who never had ground can, without realizing it, be unable to give ground to her children, passing on to them the same wandering, the same sense of not belonging. Building your inner root is not only for yourself, it's also so you can be, yourself, the firm ground your children need, the sense of belonging that you lacked. Sometimes a person puts down roots, at last, not only for herself, but to give those she loves the home she never had.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Stop searching, outside, for the home that you lack. Build it within. Cultivate a relationship with yourself in which you are, at last, your own home. And discover, slowly, that the belonging you searched for your whole life, from place to place, it was you who could give it to yourself, and that, having it, you can at last belong to the world without the hunger of someone who never had a ground.

## 7. Belonging without finding yourself everywhere

There is a destination for this crossing. And it fits in the sentence that holds it up. To belong without finding yourself everywhere.

Notice the two parts. To belong. And without finding yourself everywhere.

To belong, yes. I don't condemn you to wandering, nor do I tell you that belonging is not for you. You can belong, and to a place, to a group, to people, in a real and deep way.

But without finding yourself everywhere. Without depending on each place being the perfect home. Without demanding from the outside what is only built within. You belong to the places outside, but from a root that is in you, and not at the mercy of each place giving you, or not, the ground that you lack.

Notice the freedom that is in this sentence. Finding yourself everywhere would be an impossible demand, it would condemn you to an endless search, because there is no place that gives you everything, that fits perfectly, that is the total home. Belonging without finding yourself everywhere is freeing yourself from that impossible demand: it's belonging to the real places, with their flaws, with their imperfect fit, without needing them to be the ideal. And that is the only possible belonging, because the other, the perfect one, exists for no one. By ceasing to demand it, you gain, at last, the real belonging, which is always imperfect, and which is the only one there is.

I want to show you the difference this makes. Before, your belonging depended entirely on the place. If the place received you, you belonged. If not, you were left out, errant, in search of the next. You were a hostage to the places. Now, with a root in you, your belonging no longer depends only on the place. You carry your ground with you, and from it you connect, or not, to each place, with the freedom of someone who already has a home.

And this changes, too, the way you live the imperfect places. Before, any flaw of a place, any sense of not fitting, any exclusion, was the proof that it wasn't there, and it pushed you to leave. Now, you can stay in an imperfect place, that isn't the perfect home, and belong to it all the same, because your home is not it, it's you. You stop needing the perfect place, and so you can, at last, stay.

And I want to show you something about belonging that you may never have considered: belonging is not fitting perfectly, it's staying despite not fitting. Every person, even those who seem to belong fully, feels, to some degree, that they don't quite fit, that they're a little different, that there are parts of them that stay outside. The difference is that they stay all the same, they commit all the same, they put down roots despite the imperfect fit. Belonging is not the absence of the sense of difference, it's the decision to stay despite it. And when you understand this, you stop waiting for the place where you'd fit perfectly, which exists for no one, and you begin to be able to belong to the real places, with their always imperfect fit.

And I want you to see that, very often, belonging is not found, it's built, and it's built by staying. The sense of belonging to a place, to a group, doesn't come on the first day, it comes from time, from shared history, from ties that are only created by staying. You, who always leave before putting down root, never give the places the time to become yours. You leave before belonging has had time to grow, and then you conclude that you didn't belong, when, in truth, you never stayed long enough for belonging to be born. Staying, even in the initial discomfort of not fitting, is what allows that, with time, a place becomes, at last, home.

I want you to see that this is not resignation, it's not giving up on searching for good places. It's something else. It's ceasing to search, out there, for the solution to a lack that is inner. You go on choosing places, groups, relationships, that do you good. But no longer with the desperate hunger for them to give you the ground that you lack, because that ground you already have, in you.

And I want you to see, as well, a hidden richness in your wandering, that you can recover when it stops being a wound. Someone who lived on the outside, in many places, gains a rare capacity: that of seeing with the eyes of someone who isn't fully inside, of perceiving what those inside don't perceive, of building bridges between worlds, of understanding difference. Your wandering, which cost you so much, also left you a broad sensitivity, an openness, a capacity to connect with very diverse people. When you have a ground in you, that breadth stops being uprooting and becomes a gift: that of someone who belongs to herself, and so can move between many worlds without losing herself in any, carrying her home with her.

And I want you to understand that the inner home, once built, doesn't bind you, it frees you. Because you stop having to flee and you stop having to search. You can stay, if you want, or leave, if you want, but always by choice, and not out of hunger or out of fear. You can live in a single place your whole life, or travel the world, and in both cases be at home, because the home goes with you. The inner root doesn't tie you to a spot, it gives you the freedom to inhabit any spot, or many, from a center that is yours. It's the deepest form of freedom: that of someone who is free because, wherever she is, she is at home with herself.

There is a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of feeling at home with yourself, in any place. Of belonging without the hunger, without the wandering, without the endless search. Of being able to stay, to put down root, to commit to a place, without the fear that it isn't the right one, because the right one, after all, you carry with you. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the wandering, on the day you are, at last, a home for yourself.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, feel at home where you never felt it. But it changes enough for you to stop searching, outside, for a root that is only planted within.

Now you know that you feel on the outside everywhere, and that this is not a defect of yours, nor coldness, it's an old uprooting that you inherited, and not a truth about your worth.

You know that the sentence of your childhood, I'm from here but I'm not, became the way you feel all places, and even a prophecy that, without your meaning it, fulfills itself.

You know that you search, endlessly, for the perfect home, and that none serves, because the lack is not of a home, it's of root, and that this search, instead of leading you to belonging, keeps you always on the way out.

You know who you are, or you're not yet, if you stop searching, and that beneath the wandering there is a fear of belonging, a protection against exclusion, and a root yet to be built.

You know that belonging is not fitting perfectly, it's staying despite not fitting, and that belonging, very often, is not found, it's built, by staying.

You know that the root is built within, and that the verb is to put down roots, in you, and to be a home for yourself, with small practices, with time, and by making peace with yourself.

You know that very often you're not put outside, it's that you put yourself outside, waiting for an invitation that is yours to give to yourself.

And you know that you can belong without finding yourself everywhere, from a ground that you carry with you, and that this inner root doesn't bind you to a spot, it gives you the freedom to inhabit any one.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to keep it.

Imagine someone who spent her life searching for a home, from city to city, from door to door, map in hand, in search of the perfect home where she would finally rest. And who carried, all this time, without knowing it, the seeds of a home inside a suitcase she never opened. She searched outside, exhausted, for what she carried with her the whole time.

The crossing is stopping, opening the suitcase, and planting the seeds. In you. Building, within, the home you were searching for outside. And discovering that, having it within, you can inhabit any place, because you carry your home with you, and you no longer depend on finding it ready-made, waiting for you, anywhere.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more search. It's going to be slow, because a root isn't planted in a day, it grows slowly, with care, with time. And it's going to have setbacks. Days when you feel at home with yourself, and days when the old sensation of being outside returns, the hunger to leave, the certainty of not belonging. Don't believe that those setbacks are the proof that you have no root. They are only the time a new root takes to take hold. Each time that, instead of leaving, you stay, and return to yourself, and give yourself a little ground, the root grows a little more, and it's from that sum, slowly, that a home is built that no one can take from you.

And I want to tell you one last thing, simple, to close. You are not a mistake, a person strange to the world, condemned to never have a place. You are someone who lacked a ground, early, and who learned to search for it where it wasn't. The child who always felt on the outside had nothing wrong with her, she only lacked the ground that every child deserves. And today, as an adult, you can give yourself that ground, you can be, at last, the home you lacked, and discover that belonging was never about finding the right place, it was about becoming, yourself, a place where you could, at last, stay.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds up this book. I can belong without finding myself everywhere. I can carry my home with me, and from it inhabit the world.

Putting down roots was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by ceasing to search outside, and building, slowly, the ground within. And there is a deeper work, with people to accompany you, to plant a root that never existed, because a ground that was missing since childhood is rarely built alone, and because the relationship of help can be, itself, one of the first places where you feel, at last, received wholly.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. There is nothing wrong with you. You are not a stranger to the world, condemned to never have a place. You are someone who lacked a ground, early, and who searched for it where it wasn't. And the home you lacked you can, at last, give to yourself, slowly, building within the home you never had. Because the place where you belong is not a point on the map waiting for you. It's a ground that is planted, and the soil where it grows is you.

And before you close these pages, I ask one thing of you only. A hard thing, for you.

This week, instead of searching for where you belong, ask yourself what makes you feel, yourself, at home with yourself. It could be a gesture, an inner place, a way of being, something that is yours. And practice it, carry it with you, repeat it, wherever you are. Because it is in that cultivating of an inner home that you begin to put down roots, and that you discover, at last, that the home you searched for your whole life, from place to place, could have been, all this time, being built by you, within you, wherever you go.

And if, in doing so, you feel that it's not enough, that what you really need is to find your place out there, remember: that is the old search, not the truth. The truth is that no place out there can give you the ground that is only planted within, and that as long as you search for it outside, you'll go on leaving. Building your inner home is not giving up on belonging to the world. It's gaining, at last, the ground from which you can, without hunger and without fear, belong to it.

Because the home you searched for your whole life, from place to place, was always you, waiting to be, at last, inhabited.

Because the home you searched for your whole life, from place to place, was always possible, in the one place that goes with you wherever you go: within you.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
