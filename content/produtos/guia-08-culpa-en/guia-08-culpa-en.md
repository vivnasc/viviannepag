# The guilt that has no origin

**How to find the guilt you feel but did not commit, and return it to whom it belongs.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a self-knowledge resource about guilt, emotional inheritance, and family patterns. It does not replace therapeutic support or any form of clinical treatment. If the guilt you carry paralyzes you, keeps you from sleeping, leads you to thoughts of self-punishment, or is connected to symptoms of persistent depression or anxiety, this guide is not enough: seek professional support. Looking at guilt is delicate work. Taking care of yourself, while you do it, comes first.*

---

## 1. The guilt you feel but did not commit

There is a guilt you know so well that you no longer even notice it. It's there when you wake up. It's there when you finally sit down, after a whole day of serving others, and instead of rest you feel a tightening, a little voice saying you should be doing more. It's there when you say "no" to someone and spend the following hours justifying yourself inside. It's there when something good happens to you and, instead of pure joy, you feel that you don't deserve it, that you'll have to pay for this later.

This guilt has a strange characteristic: you can't say what you did wrong. Ask yourself now. What is the crime? What is the concrete, dated, identifiable fact that justifies this weight you've carried for so long? Most of the time, there's no answer. There's no crime. There's only the feeling. The permanent feeling of being in debt to someone, without knowing to whom, without knowing why, without knowing how much.

And that's exactly what I want you to understand right at the start: true guilt has an origin. You did something, you hurt someone, you failed at a real responsibility, and you feel the weight of it. That guilt is useful. It's the moral compass working. It points to a concrete action you can repair, an apology you can make, a behavior you can correct. That guilt has an address. You know where to deliver it.

But there is another guilt. One that points to nothing. That has no fact, no date, no possible repair, because there's nothing to repair. It's a guilt without an origin. And that's the one we're talking about here. Because that one, no matter how much you do, never diminishes. You can be the best mother in the world and still feel that you're failing. You can give everything and still feel that you should give more. You can exhaust yourself and still feel that it wasn't enough. Because the problem isn't in what you do. The problem is that this guilt wasn't born from an action of yours. It was lent to you.

Imagine that someone, long ago, put a coat over your shoulders. A heavy coat, a winter one, made for another body, in a time that isn't yours. And you, a child, didn't know how to refuse. You put it on. And you grew so used to the weight that today you no longer even feel it as weight. You feel it as part of you. "That's just how I am, I've always felt guilty about everything." But no. You aren't like that. You are wearing a coat that isn't yours. And there's an abyssal difference between being and carrying.

I noticed this first in myself, and then in almost every woman I work with. Competent, generous women, who take care of everyone, and who even so live with the constant feeling of failing. Of owing. Of falling short. And when I ask them "what, exactly, did you do wrong?", they fall silent. Because they don't know. They feel the guilt with enormous intensity, but they can't name the crime. And it's in that silence that the truth usually begins.

The question that organizes this guide is simple and, at the same time, one of the most liberating you can ask: what do I carry that isn't mine? Not "how do I get rid of the guilt," because getting rid of it isn't the verb. The verb is to return. There's a difference. Getting rid of something is throwing it away, hiding it, pretending it doesn't exist. Returning is looking clearly at a weight, recognizing that it belongs to another person, and handing it back, with respect, to its owner. Not with anger. With dignity.

Before we go on, I want to be very clear about one thing. I'm not telling you that you've never made a mistake. I'm not inviting you into irresponsibility. There are guilts that are yours and that you need to own, to repair, to live. That work is important and it isn't the work of this guide. Here we speak only of the other kind. The kind without origin. The kind you feel without having committed. Because as long as you carry other people's guilt, you won't have the space, nor the strength, nor the clarity to look honestly at the ones that are truly yours. The borrowed coat blocks your view.

And there is a detail that changes everything. A debt that isn't yours cannot be paid. No matter how hard you try to pay it. You can struggle your whole life, you can give yourself to the point of exhaustion, and the balance never goes down, because you aren't the debtor. You are simply paying someone else's bill. And there is something profoundly unjust, and at the same time profoundly common, in women who spend decades paying a debt they never took on. This guide is, at its heart, a plea for you to stop paying. And to discover whose bill it is, after all.


## 2. Where what isn't yours comes from

If the guilt wasn't born from an action of yours, then where was it born? This is the question of this chapter. And the answer, in most cases, lies in three places: in what you inherited, in what you absorbed, and in what you were taught to feel as yours. Let's go through each one, slowly, because recognizing the origin is half the work of returning.

The first place is family inheritance. There are guilts that cross generations as if they were household furniture, passed from mother to daughter without anyone asking whether they still fit. Think about your mother. Think about her mother. What weight did they carry? A grandmother who had a child out of wedlock at a time when that was a scandal and spent her whole life feeling stained. A mother who gave up a dream to take care of her siblings and never forgave herself for having, in secret, wished for another life. A great-grandmother who lost a child and forever carried the guilt of having survived. These guilts don't die with the person. They look for a body in which to continue. And often they find it in the daughter, in the granddaughter, in the woman of the next generation who, without knowing why, feels in debt to life without ever having done anything to deserve it.

In family constellations, you see this with a clarity that still moves me. A woman arrives with a diffuse guilt, with no apparent cause, and when you look at the family system, it appears. There's always someone behind her carrying the same thing. A mother, a grandmother, a forgotten aunt. And the daughter, out of love, out of a blind and silent loyalty that children have toward those who raised them, took on the weight without anyone asking her to. Children do this. They see their mother suffer and, in their magical world, they believe that if they suffer too, if they carry it too, somehow they relieve her. They don't relieve her. They only multiply it. But they keep doing it, generation after generation, because no one ever told them they could set it down.

The second place is what you absorbed. There are guilts you didn't inherit from far away, from the family past, but that you caught up close, from people who lived with you. A mother who would repeat "look what you put me through." A father whose heavy silence made you feel responsible for his unhappiness. A partner who, whenever he was down, managed, without saying a single direct word, to make you feel that it was your fault. Children and women, above all, are emotional sponges. They absorb the state of those around them. And when you grow up in an environment where someone holds you responsible, in a subtle and constant way, for their feelings, you learn to feel as yours a guilt that is, in truth, that person's refusal to take care of their own feelings.

Notice a concrete scene. You leave the house for a dinner with friends, something rare, something just yours. And at the door, your mother (or your child, or your partner) makes that face. They say nothing. They just make the face. And your dinner is ruined, because you spend the evening with a weight on your chest, the feeling that you're doing something wrong simply by existing outside the role of caretaker. That weight isn't yours. It's someone's emotional blackmail, turned into guilt inside you. You caught what was theirs.

The third place is cultural, and perhaps it's the most invisible of all, because it's everywhere and that's why no one sees it. Women have been taught, for centuries, to feel guilty for existing for themselves. Guilty if she works too much, guilty if she works too little. Guilty if she's too much of a mother, guilty if she's not mother enough. Guilty if she wants, if she desires, if she rests, if she says no, if she takes up space, if she shines. This guilt didn't come from a person. It came from an entire culture that wanted you available, devoted, and apologizing. And you, without knowing it, put on that coat too.

How do you know that a guilt isn't yours? There are signs. Here are some, and I want you to read them slowly, checking them against your own experience. First: the disproportion. The guilt is enormous and the fact is tiny, or nonexistent. You feel terrible about something that, seen from the outside, no one would consider serious. Second: the permanence. No matter how much you repair, explain, or strive, the guilt doesn't diminish. It stays the same, because it has nothing to do with what you do. Third: the vagueness. When you try to say what you did wrong, you can't. There's only a fog, a "I don't know, I feel I should be better." Fourth, and this is the most revealing: the familiarity. There's an echo. When you look carefully, you realize that your mother felt exactly the same. And maybe her mother too. The guilt has a face, and the face isn't yours.

If you recognized yourself in any of these signs, I want you to breathe before you go on. Not with alarm. With relief. Because you've just discovered something important: what you always thought was a flaw of character, a fault of personality, a "that's just how I am," is not, after all. It's a coat. And coats can be taken off.


## 3. Returning what weighs: an exercise

Now we come to the practical part. Knowing that the guilt isn't yours is the beginning. But knowing, on its own, isn't enough. The guilt settled into the body, not only in the head, and so it needs a gesture, not only an understanding. The exercise that follows is simple to describe and demanding to do. Set aside about twenty minutes, a place where you won't be interrupted, paper and pen. Do it calmly. It isn't a task. It's an act of returning.

**First step: finding the guilt.** Sit down and close your eyes for a moment. Ask your body, not your head: where is the guilt? Where do you feel it? In the chest, in the throat, in the belly, in the shoulders (curious, the shoulders, where a coat is carried). Place your hand on that spot. Stay there for a moment. You don't have to do anything, just recognize that it exists and that it has a physical place in you. Then, with your eyes open, write a sentence that begins like this: "I feel guilty for..." And complete it. Without thinking too much. Write the first thing, and then another, and another. "For resting." "For not being able to be the mother I wanted to be." "For wanting to be alone sometimes." "For not calling my mother more." "For being well when others aren't." Write everything that comes. This is your list.

**Second step: the origin test.** Look at each sentence on your list and ask it one question, just one: what concrete fact justifies this guilt? Not the feeling. The fact. What you did, on what day, against whom, that can be named and repaired. Mark each sentence with one of two letters. Put a "Y" (for yours) next to the ones that have a real, concrete, identifiable fact involving someone you genuinely hurt. And put an "O" (for other, for an origin that isn't yours) next to the ones that, no matter how hard you look, have no crime. They only have the feeling. You'll see that most carry an "O." Almost all of them. And that, in itself, is usually a quiet shock, and a good one.

**Third step: finding the owner.** Now take only the sentences marked with "O." For each one, ask the constellations question: whose guilt is this, after all? Who felt this before me? Close your eyes and let a face appear. It might be your mother. Your grandmother. It might be a culture, a time, an old voice. Don't force it. Trust the first one that comes up. And when the face appears, don't judge it. This person also carried a coat that wasn't theirs. This person is also a link in a long chain of women paying debts they never took on. Look at them with the compassion of someone who finally recognizes the same wound.

**Fourth step: the sentence of returning.** This is the heart of the exercise. For each guilt that isn't yours, and looking at the face of the one who carried it, you're going to say, out loud if you can, a sentence. Not invented by me, but adapted from the language of constellations, because those words have a weight that others don't. Say it like this: "This guilt is yours, not mine. I saw it. I carried it out of love for you, for a long time. But it was never mine. I return it to you, with respect. And I leave it with you, who are an adult, who have your own strength, who know what to do with what is yours. I keep my life. You keep what is yours."

Say it slowly. Repeat it, if you need to. And pay attention to your body as you say it, because that's where you notice whether the return is happening. Many women describe a physical sensation at the right moment: shoulders that drop, a deeper breath that comes on its own, a shiver, tears that aren't of sadness but of relief. Don't chase the sensation. But if it comes, let it come. It's the coat coming off.

I want to warn you about two things, so you don't get frightened. The first: returning isn't cutting off. You aren't expelling your mother, nor ceasing to love her, nor breaking the bond. You're only setting down a weight that was never yours to bear. On the contrary, when you return what isn't yours, the relationship usually even improves, because it stops being contaminated by a guilt that had no place between you. You honor your mother more when you return her whole life to her, weight included, than when you relieve her by carrying what is hers. The second thing: it may not happen the first time. The guilt settled over years, perhaps over generations. It may need several returns. It isn't your fault if the coat doesn't come off right away. It's the time these things ask for. Come back to the exercise as many times as you need.

And there is a physical gesture that helps a great deal, and that you can add. When you say the sentence, make with your hands the movement of taking something off your shoulders and setting it down in front of you, on the floor, carefully, like someone laying a coat over a chair. The body understands the language of gestures better than that of words. When the shoulders and the hands make the movement of setting down, something in the nervous system receives the message: I no longer have to carry this. It isn't magic. It's the connection between the body and the mind working in your favor, as it always wanted to.

After you return it, don't stay in the emptiness. There is one last gesture, and it's the most important of all. Go back to your list and look at what remains: the sentences marked with "Y," the few guilts that are truly yours, and above all the new space, now empty, where before there were all the coats of others. That space is yours. Ask yourself: what do I want to put here now? What weight of my own, chosen by me, is worth carrying? What responsibility do I want to take on not out of fear, but out of love? Because the goal was never for you to end up light and empty. It was for you to end up light and yours. Carrying only what you chose to carry.


## 4. FreeMe bridge

If this exercise touched you, if you felt, while doing it, that something moved, that an old coat loosened even a little, I want you to know that this is only the beginning. A guide can show you the door. It can give you the first gesture of returning. But inherited guilt rarely resolves itself in a single session alone, with paper and pen. It has deep roots. And deep roots ask for more careful work, more accompanied, more unhurried.

FreeMe is the space I created exactly for this. For the women who have realized that they carry weights that aren't theirs, and who want, with method and with support, to discover what they inherited, what they absorbed, what was put on their shoulders without their being able to refuse. It's a work of liberation, hence the name. Not in the sense of fleeing, but in the sense of returning, one by one, the guilts, the loyalties, the weights that don't belong to you, until you reach what remains when everything that isn't yours comes off. And what remains, almost always, is more luminous than you expected.

The question that moves FreeMe is the same one that moved this guide: what do I carry that isn't mine? But in FreeMe you don't answer it alone, on a sheet of paper. You answer it with support, with the tools of family constellations, of transpersonal psychology, of the work with the patterns that come from behind. You discover the whole system, not just one guilt at a time. And you learn to make the return not as an isolated gesture, but as a new way of being in the world. You'll find FreeMe at [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

For now, though, stay with what you already have in your hands. You don't need to wait for anything to begin. The list, the origin test, the sentence of returning, the gesture of setting down the coat: all of that you can do today, tonight, alone in your room. And each guilt you return, even just one, even the smallest, opens space. It returns a little bit of you to yourself. And that little bit, added to another, and to another, is the path back to who you always were beneath all the coats.

Because there was a woman underneath all of that. There always is. A woman who wasn't born guilty, who owes nothing to anyone just for existing, who has the right to rest, to want, to say no, to shine, to be well even when others aren't. That woman was always there. Only covered. And each return is a layer that comes off, until her light shines through again.

I want to leave you with an image, the same one we began with, because it sums everything up. You're standing at the entrance of a corridor full of hooks. On each hook, a coat that someone put on you over the course of your life. Your grandmother's. Your mother's. The culture's. The one belonging to that person who made you feel guilty for existing. And you, for years, walked around with all of them on at the same time, barely able to move under so much weight. The work I propose to you is simple to imagine and brave to do: to walk the corridor, slowly, and hang each coat on its hook. With respect. Without anger. Recognizing that each one had its owner, and that the owner wasn't you. To the end of the corridor. Until you step out the other side feeling the air on your skin, finally without weight, finally light, finally yourself.
