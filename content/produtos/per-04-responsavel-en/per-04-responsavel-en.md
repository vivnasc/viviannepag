# The one who became responsible far too early

**For you, who were an adult before you were old enough to be. Because your place was never to be everyone's adult, and you can give back the responsibility you took on far too early.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who was forced to grow up far too early, to care for those who should have cared for her, and that, when there is neglect, abuse, or childhood trauma underneath, asks for more than a book. If you recognize in yourself old wounds that still run through you today, an exhaustion that never lifts, or a guilt that won't let you rest, seek the support of a professional. This book accompanies you in seeing. It does not treat trauma. And there are wounds that deserve hands prepared to hold them.*

---

## 1. I was never a child

There is a sentence you may carry, in silence, and that says more about you than any other: I was never, truly, a child. Perhaps you never said it out loud, perhaps not even to yourself in these words, but it explains much of the weight you carry today.

Look back, to your childhood. And notice what you were doing while other children played, carefree, protected, free of the weight of the world.

You were caring. Taking care of a younger sibling. Worrying about the bills the adults couldn't pay. Comforting a mother who cried. Managing the moods of an unpredictable father. Holding up a house that, without you, seemed to crumble. Watching the climate of the house, alert to every sign of danger, like a small sentinel who could never lower her guard.

And perhaps you don't even remember ever being, simply, a child. Having played without a worry deep down, having received without having to give, having trusted that there were adults taking care of everything, and that all that fell to you was to grow. For many people who grew up this way, this way of living, always responsible, always alert, always caring, became so habitual that it seems to be who they are. But it isn't. It's what was learned, far too early, and what was learned can, slowly, be unlearned, because beneath the adult who carries everything there is, still, a child who never got to be one, and who waits, only, for permission to exist.

You were the adult before you were old enough to be. The one who solved things. The responsible one. The one who gave no trouble because she was too busy taking care of other people's trouble. And today, as a real adult, you go on being it. Responsible for everything and everyone. The one who holds things up. The one who never allows herself not to cope. The one everyone seeks when there's a problem, and the one no one thinks to seek to ask, simply, how are you.

Notice what you carry, still today, without realizing it. A responsibility that seems to have no end. An inability to rest without guilt. A sense that, if you're not the one to take care of things, no one does, and everything collapses. And a background tiredness, old, of someone who has carried the world on her shoulders since before she had shoulders for it.

Notice how the place of the adult shows up for you in everyday life, in gestures so automatic that you already confuse them with your nature. You can't be in a group without becoming the responsible one, the one who organizes, the one who takes care of everything, even when no one asked you to. When something goes wrong, your first reaction is to feel that you should have done more, foreseen it, prevented it, as though everything were, somehow, your concern. And when, by chance, you have nothing to take care of, instead of relief, you feel a discomfort, an unease, as though you were failing at something, because you don't know how to exist without being responsible for someone.

And there's one detail that reveals the root: the enormous difficulty you have in letting others take care of you. When someone offers you help, comfort, care, you draw back, you refuse, you say it isn't necessary, that you'll handle it. Receiving is almost impossible for you, because your place was always to give, and being on the side of the one who receives gives you a discomfort, almost a shame, as though it were a forbidden reversal. You know how to take care of everyone, and you don't know how to let yourself be taken care of, because no one taught you, because no one, at the right time, took care of you.

And there's something nobody sees, beneath your responsibility. People see someone mature, capable, trustworthy, and they lean on you, and they praise you for it, without imagining what it cost you to become this way. They don't see the child who was never a child, the exhaustion of a whole life spent carrying, the hunger for a care that never came. They see the maturity, not the wound. And you, praised for being so responsible, become even more bound to the post, because now being everyone's adult is also being admired, and letting it go feels like risking who you are.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not your maturity, even though it looks like it. People praise you. They say you were always mature, responsible, ahead of your age, an example. And there is something true in that. But beneath the praise there is a truth no one saw. You weren't mature. You were robbed. They robbed you of your childhood, and you grew up too fast, not because you wanted to, but because the house needed an adult, and you were the adult it didn't have.

It has a name, what happened to you. It's called a reversal of places. You became the mother of your mother, the father of your father, the caregiver of your siblings. The place of the child, which should have been yours, was left empty, and you took up the place of the adult, which was not for you. And that reversal, which made you seem so capable, left in you a deep wound, that you carry still.

And I want you to see that this wound is silent, and for that reason hard to recognize. There is not, in your story, perhaps, a clear scene of violence, a moment of horror to point to. There was, rather, an absence: that of the adult who wasn't there, of the care that didn't come, of the protection that was missing. And the wounds of absence are the hardest to see, because there is no event, there is a void, and it's hard to weep for what didn't happen, the comfort that wasn't there, the childhood that never was. But those wounds, the ones of absence, hurt as much as the others, and they deserve to be acknowledged, even if they have no scene to show. What you lacked counts as much as what was done to you.

This book is not going to tell you to become irresponsible, to stop caring, to abandon the people you love. I have come to look at the backpack instead, and at the small back it was strapped to. At the swapped place you took up. At the responsibility you took on and that was never yours. And at the possibility of giving it back, at last, to whom it belonged.

And I'll point to where this crossing is heading now, so you can set the backpack down for a moment and read in peace. Your place was never to be everyone's adult. You can give back the responsibility you took on far too early. Not in one blow, not without care, but slowly, you can set down what was never yours to carry. And rest, play, receive, without that feeling to you like a betrayal.

I want you to understand, right away, the gap between the capable child everyone saw and the small one breaking quietly underneath. What it looks like, from the outside, is a mature, responsible person, who always knew how to take care of everything. What it is, on the inside, is a child who had to grow up far too early, and who never again managed to stop carrying. Your maturity is not, at its root, a gift that flourished, it's an adaptation to a lack, a response to a void that should have been filled by adults. And there is an enormous difference between a maturity that grows in its own time, with protection, and one that rises too early, at the cost of childhood. The first is a strength. The second is, also, a wound.

This book does not come to tell you that caring for others is wrong, nor that you have to become irresponsible. Your capacity to care is real, and it is precious. It comes to distinguish healthy caring, of an adult who chooses, from the compulsive carrying of someone who never got to stop; being responsible for what is yours from being responsible for everything and everyone. And it comes to give you back the right to something that was taken from you far too early: the right to be, yourself too, taken care of, small, light, and to stop carrying what was never yours.

I want to be honest with you right away. This is, of all the books, perhaps the most delicate. Because beneath a stolen childhood there is, very often, serious pain, neglect, and sometimes wounds that ask for more than these pages. We'll get there, with all care. But I want you to know, from the start, that if what you lived through was heavy, you're not alone, and there is help, and you deserve to seek it.

Perhaps you find yourself in the small, everyday details of a life spent holding things up. In being the first one everyone turns to, and the last to ask for anything for yourself. In being unable to rest while you feel there's someone in need, even when that person is an adult and capable. In feeling an enormous guilt at the mere thought of living your life, of putting yourself in front, of not coping with everything. In being exhausted, for so long, from carrying a weight that nobody asked you for in words, but that you feel is yours, and yours alone, to hold up. In not even knowing what you'd do with a whole day with no one to take care of, because you never knew how to exist without being responsible for someone.

All of those threads run back to one knot. The swapped place you took up far too early. The child who became everyone's adult, and who never again knew how to be, simply, a child.

## 2. The reversal of places

Go back. To the beginning. To how your place was swapped.

In a family as it should be, there is a simple order. Parents take care of their children. Adults hold up the house. Children receive, grow, play, and are protected from the weight of the world. The weight comes down, from the big ones to the little ones, never the other way around.

In your family, that order was reversed. For some reason, the adults could not be the adults. A mother sunk in a depression, in a pain, in an incapacity. An absent father, ill, dependent, or who simply couldn't cope. A family in crisis, in instability, in survival. And the place of the adult, which should have been occupied by them, was left empty.

And you, a child, took it up. Not because you were forced in words, most of the time, but because you felt the void, you felt the danger, and you did what a loving child does. You filled the missing place. You became the adult the house didn't have.

You began to take care of those who should have taken care of you. To comfort the mother, instead of being comforted by her. To worry about the father, instead of feeling protected by him. To take care of the siblings, to manage the house, to carry adult worries in the body and soul of a child.

And perhaps there was, in this, a particular form of reversal: that of becoming the emotional confidante of one of your parents. The one who heard the confessions, the fears, the adult complaints, that a child should not hear. The one who served as emotional support to the one who should have been your support. And you learned, then, that your worth lay in containing the pain of others, in being the shoulder, the harbor, the one who endured what the adults couldn't endure. A child should not be the shoulder of an adult. But you were, and that reversal left you carrying, still today, everyone's pain, as though it were your job to contain it.

And I want you to see the deep loneliness there is in this. Because a child who takes care of adults is, deep down, alone. She has no one to turn to, because those she should turn to are precisely the ones she shelters. She has nowhere to set down her own pain, her own fears, because her place is to contain those of others. You grew up, perhaps, surrounded by family, and profoundly alone, with a loneliness that few see, that of someone who holds everyone up and is held up by no one. And that loneliness of a child echoes, still today, in the difficulty of believing that there can be, for you, someone who holds you up.

Notice what this cost you. It cost you your childhood. The right to be small, carefree, protected. The right to receive, instead of give. The right to make mistakes, to play, to be responsible for nothing but being a child. All of that was taken from you, because the house needed an adult, and there was no other.

And it cost you something even deeper. It cost you the safety of having someone bigger than you holding you up. Because a child needs to feel that there's an adult taking care of everything, that she can be small because someone big protects her. You didn't have that. You were the adult. There was no one bigger to hold you up, and so you never could, truly, rest, be small, trust that someone was taking care of everything. You had to be the one to take care of everything, always.

In the way I have learned to look at these stories, there is here one of the deepest wounds there is. Because parentification, this technical name for what you lived through, doesn't wound only through the excess of responsibility. It wounds through the lack of protection. Through the child who was never held up, because she was too busy holding others up. And that child, inside you, is still waiting to be, at last, taken care of.

And it matters that you see in what part of you the swapped place became inscribed. What you lived through is not only in your memory, in what you remember. It's in your body, in your way of being in the world, etched at an age when you still had no way of defending yourself. That's why, today, responsibility is not a choice you make, it's a reflex that fires off: you take it on before you think, you care before you decide, you feel the guilt rise before any reason. And that's why it isn't enough to realize that you no longer need to be everyone's adult, in order to stop being it. The wound lives below reason, in a place that formed very early, and that heals slowly, with care, and often with prepared help.

There is one more thing this reversal left you, and that you may recognize: a difficulty in knowing what you yourself feel and need. Because a child busy feeling what the adults felt, anticipating their needs, managing the emotions of the house, tunes an antenna outward and loses access to herself. Today, you can read in an instant the state of everyone, and about what you yourself need, feel, want, you hesitate, you don't know. You grew up turned toward the needs of others, and your own inner life was left a little-explored territory, waiting for you to return to it, at last.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without necessarily accusing those who raised you. Those who let you take up the place of the adult, very often, were sunk in a pain that kept them from being the adult they should have been. There was no malice in their hands when they set the weight on your small shoulders. But the weight landed there all the same, because they could not take up their own place. And seeing this is not, necessarily, hating. But neither is it excusing everything. It's, finally, naming what happened to you, and acknowledging that it wasn't fair, and that the mistake wasn't yours.

## 3. What the system asked for and didn't fit

There is something you need to see clearly. What was asked of you, as a child, didn't fit in a child. It was too big. Not for you, specifically, but for any child.

Notice. There is no child in the world capable of being the mother of her mother. Of carrying an adult's depression. Of holding up a house in crisis. Of being the pillar of a family system. That doesn't fit in a child, because a child does not have, nor should have, the resources for it. And when a child does it, she does it at the cost of herself, breaking inside to cope with a weight designed for adult shoulders.

You coped, in a way. On the outside, you were capable, responsible, mature. But on the inside, you broke. Because coping with what doesn't fit in a child is only possible at the price of sacrificing the very child. And that's what happened. The child you were sacrificed herself so that the adult the house needed could exist in you. And no one saw that sacrifice, because on the outside it looked only like a mature child, capable, exemplary, and not a child breaking in silence to cope with the impossible.

I want you to feel the injustice of this, because seeing it matters. It wasn't you who asked for the place of the adult. The system asked it of you. The family, in its crisis, in its incapacity, placed upon you a weight that did not belong to you, and that you, out of love and out of survival, accepted. But accepting is not the same as owing. You carried what you didn't have to carry. And the fact that you managed doesn't mean it should have been you.

And there is something the reversal left you, and that you carry still. The belief that responsibility for everything is yours. Because you learned, early, that if you weren't the one to take care of things, no one did. And that lesson was etched so deep that today, as an adult, you go on feeling that everything depends on you. That you have to cope with everything. That resting, delegating, letting others take responsibility, is risking the disaster that, as a child, was real.

Notice how this governs you. You can't delegate, because you learned that no one does it like you, that everything depends on you. You can't ask for help, because it was always you who helped, and asking feels to you like an impossible reversal. You can't rest, because resting, as a child, was risking the house falling. You carry, as an adult, the total responsibility you took on as a child, and that you never learned to set down.

And there is something this total responsibility takes from you, and that you may never have had: carefreeness. That lightness of someone who trusts that someone bigger takes care of things, that the world stands without her vigilance, that she can let go without everything falling. You never knew that lightness, because from early on you were the someone bigger, the one who watched, the one who took care of everything. And so you live with a background of permanent tension, an alertness that never switches off, the sense that you can't lower your guard. That carefreeness you lack is not irresponsibility, it's the basic right of someone who was, one day, protected enough to be able to let go. And it is one of the rights this crossing comes, slowly, to give back to you.

And beneath all this, there is an exhaustion that is not of now. It's an exhaustion of a whole life of being responsible for everything, since before you were old enough to be. A tiredness so old that it already seems to you your way of being, when it is, in truth, the weight of decades of a burden that should never have been yours.

And there is a cost in your adult relationships, too, that you may recognize. Because you arrive at relationships already in the role of the one who cares, who solves, who takes responsibility, and you attract, without meaning to, those who need to be cared for, those who lean, those who delegate to you the weight of their lives. You repeat, as an adult, the reversal of childhood: you become, again, the responsible one, the strong one, the one who holds up, and not the one held up. And then you feel exhausted, overloaded, resentful, without realizing that you chose, or accepted, relationships that reproduce the only place you learned to occupy. Healing this wound changes this too: you begin to be able to be in relationships where you receive, and not only give, where you lean, and not only shelter.

I want you to see, as well, how the guilt of not coping pursues you in a disproportionate way. When something fails, when you can't do everything, when someone you care for isn't well, you feel an enormous guilt, as though it were a failing of yours, a betrayal. That guilt doesn't match your real responsibility, it's the old guilt of the child who felt that, if she didn't cope with everything, the house would fall, and that the fall would be because of her. Learning to distinguish your real responsibility, which is limited and human, from that infinite guilt of a child, is part of setting down the weight. You are not responsible for the happiness, the lives, the choices of the adults around you. You never were, even though a frightened child believed it.

## 4. Resting feels like betrayal

I'm going to leave a sentence here, and I ask you to notice whether it is yours.

For you, resting feels like betrayal. Playing feels like selfishness. Receiving feels like weakness.

Stay with this, because it is the heart of the wound. It's not only that you're too responsible. It's that everything that isn't responsibility brings you guilt. Resting, having fun, receiving care, putting yourself in front, all of that feels to you, deep down, wrong, as though you had no right to it.

And the shadow hiding here is guilt. A deep guilt, old, that appears whenever you try to exist beyond caring. The child who only had the right to be there while she was useful, while she cared, while she coped, learned that existing for herself, without serving, was not permitted. And today, whenever you rest, whenever you receive, whenever you put yourself in front, that guilt fires off, and tells you that you're failing, abandoning, being selfish.

Notice how this keeps you bent under the backpack, the way that small sentinel never lowered her guard. You can't rest without feeling guilty, as though there were always something you should be doing for someone. You can't have fun without a shadow, as though joy were a luxury you have no right to while there are people in need. You can't receive, be cared for, let others hold you up, because receiving, for you, is a forbidden reversal, and the weakness of someone who should always be the strong one.

And there is something moving, and painful, beneath this. It's that the child you were never received the care she needed. She cared, but was not cared for. She gave, but did not receive. She held things up, but was never held up. And that child, inside you, still hungers for that care that never came. But the guilt won't let her receive it, because she learned that her place was to give, not to receive, and that receiving would betray the only place she knew.

I want you to feel how heavy this backpack truly is. Imagine never being able to allow yourself, without guilt, a moment that is only yours. A rest without the sense that you should be doing something. A joy without the shadow of someone in need. A care received without feeling weak, or in debt. It's a place of an endless exhaustion, because the guilt won't let you, ever, stop caring, giving, being responsible, not even for an instant.

And the truth the guilt hides is this. Resting is not betrayal. Playing is not selfishness. Receiving is not weakness. Those are the laws of a child who had to care in order to have a place. But you are no longer that child in a house in crisis. You're an adult, with the right to rest, to play, to receive, like any person. And giving yourself that right does not betray anyone. It gives you back, at last, the childhood that was taken from you.

## 5. When there is trauma underneath

I have to set the backpack down on the ground here, and ask you to stop with me. With all seriousness. This is the most important chapter, and I ask you to take it seriously, because it may be what matters most to you.

What you lived through, an inverted childhood, a child who cared for adults, is not only a pattern to be understood. Very often, beneath it, there is trauma. And trauma is not treated with a book. It is treated with help, serious help, from a professional.

I want to name, without circling around it, what may lie beneath your stolen childhood.

There may have been neglect. A child left to take care of herself and others, without the protection, the care, the safety that every child needs. That is a deep wound, and it leaves marks that ask for care.

There may have been emotional abuse. A child used to meet the emotional needs of the adults, made responsible for feelings that were not hers, blamed, manipulated. That wounds in a way that isn't seen, but that hurts for a whole life.

There may have been severe parentification. Not only helping at home, but being, in fact, the adult responsible for incapable adults, for small siblings, for an entire family, for years, without end and without relief.

And there may be, today, the marks of this. An anxiety that never lifts. An enormous difficulty in trusting, in resting, in letting yourself be cared for. A background exhaustion. Episodes in which the past assails you, in thoughts, in nightmares, in a tightness. A deep sadness. A sense that something, in you, was broken far too early.

If you recognize yourself in this, I want to tell you something with all firmness and all tenderness. This is not healed only by understanding. However lucid this book helps you become, the wound of childhood trauma asks for prepared hands. A psychologist, a therapist, someone trained to accompany you in undoing, with safety and at your own pace, a weight that comes from very far back.

I want you to see why, in this wound, the relationship of help is, itself, part of the healing. Because what wounded you was a relationship, or the lack of one: not having had, as a child, an adult who held you up, who took care of you, on whom you could lean. And what was wounded in relationship heals, very often, in another: in a relationship of help where, perhaps for the first time, someone holds you up, takes care of you, without your having to take care of them in return. Experiencing that, being, at last, the cared-for and not the caregiver, can repair the wound in the very place where it was made, giving the child you were the good adult she never had.

And I want to take from you a fear that may hold you back: that of seeking help being one more way of being a burden, of giving trouble, of becoming a load, you who were always the one who gave no trouble. It isn't. Seeking help is not being a burden, it's letting yourself, at last, be cared for, and that is no load to anyone, it's the work of someone who dedicates themselves precisely to holding up those who were never held up. You, who carried everyone, have more right than anyone to be, now, carried. And allowing yourself that is not weakness, it's the most courageous act of someone who was strong far too early.

It is not weakness to seek that help. It's the most courageous, and most loving, thing you can do for the child you were, and who was never cared for. You took care of everyone. It's time to let, at last, someone take care of you, with the preparation that requires.

This book accompanies you in seeing the place you took up. But it does not treat trauma, and it would be irresponsible to make you believe that it was enough. If your wound is deep, this chapter is the one I want you to take with you. Don't skip it. Seek help. Because the child who carried the world deserves, finally, to be held up by hands that know how.

## 6. Giving back what was never mine

If you carried what was not yours, then there is a verb waiting for you. To give back.

To give back the responsibility you took on far too early. The weight that was not yours. The place of the adult you took up when you should have been a child. To give back, with care, to whom it belonged.

And I want to be clear about what this is, and what it is not. Giving back is not revenge. It's not going to your own people and throwing in their faces everything you carried. It's not presenting an invoice, demanding reparation, turning them into the guilty ones. That would be unstrapping one backpack only to shoulder another, this one full of resentment, and that load binds the small back as tightly as the first.

Giving back is an inner movement, and it's above all about the order of things. It's acknowledging, inwardly, that the weight you carried belonged to the adults, and not to you. That the responsibility for the house, for their pain, for the family, was theirs, not the child's you were. And it's ceasing, slowly, to go on carrying what was theirs, giving back to them, within you, the place that was theirs to occupy.

There is an image that helps to understand this order. In a healthy family, the weight comes down: from grandparents to parents, from parents to children, the big ones supporting the little ones, the care flowing from top to bottom. In yours, the weight went up: it was you, small, supporting the big ones, the care flowing the other way, from the child to the parent. Giving back is restoring the natural order, even if belatedly, even if only inwardly: it's acknowledging that it was not your task to support those who should have supported you, and giving back, within you, each one to their place, the adults as adults, and you as the daughter you were, and who deserved to have been cared for.

And I want you to understand that this giving back is done, first, within you, and not necessarily in a conversation with your family. You don't need to convince anyone, to obtain acknowledgment, to wait for your own people to admit what they put you to carry. Many won't admit it, perhaps they won't even remember, perhaps they have no way of seeing it. The giving back does not depend on them. It's done in you, in the way you understand what you lived through, in the way you stop taking on what is not yours, in the way you treat yourself, at last, as a daughter and not as everyone's mother. And only afterward, if and when it makes sense, does it translate into gestures in the world. But it begins, always, within, and that part doesn't need anyone's permission.

Imagine saying, inwardly, to those who let you carry: this was your life, your pain, your responsibility. I was a child, and I carried it for you, because I loved you and because there was no one else. But it never belonged inside a child's backpack. I give you back, now, what was yours. I keep my place, that of the daughter, that of the child I was and who deserves, at last, to let herself be cared for. There is an immense strength in this gesture. And no abandonment. Because giving back the weight is not the same as no longer loving. It is loving from the right place, that of the daughter, and not that of the mother of one's own parents.

I want you to see what is freed when you give back. Guilt is freed. Because the guilt of resting, of receiving, of existing, was born from carrying a place that was not yours. When you give back that place, the guilt loses its reason, because you're no longer abandoning any post, you're returning to your own.

And the child is freed. The child you were, who never got to be small, gains, at last, permission to exist. To rest. To play. To receive. To be cared for, even if belatedly, by you, who can now be, for that child, the caring adult she never had.

I want to be clear about one thing, because it matters: giving back does not mean, necessarily, abandoning those who need you. There are, perhaps, in your life, people who in fact depend on your care, and caring for them can be a legitimate choice, of love, of an adult. The difference is not in ceasing to care, it's in ceasing to do it from the imprisoned child, from the reflex, from the guilt, from the impossibility of stopping. You can go on caring for those you choose to care for, but from a place of an adult who chooses, and not of a child who has no alternative. And you can, at the same time, set down what is not yours, ask for help, share the weight, let yourself too be cared for. Giving back is not turning your back. It's ceasing to carry alone, and out of obligation, what can be shared, and chosen, and light.

And I want you to see the difference between caring from a place of fullness and caring from a place of emptiness. Someone who cares from a place of fullness gives from what she has to spare, and stays whole. Someone who cares from a place of emptiness, as you learned, gives from what she doesn't have, scrapes the bottom, drains herself, because caring became the condition of having a place, and not a choice of someone who is full. By letting yourself be cared for, by receiving, by filling yourself up too, your caring changes in nature: it stops being a draining of yourself to deserve to be there, and becomes an overflowing of someone who has something to give. And that caring, which comes from fullness, is lighter for you and cleaner for those who receive it.

And don't be surprised if, on this path, there comes to the surface an anger, or a hurt, that you may never have allowed yourself to feel. Anger at those who let you carry what you shouldn't have. Hurt for the childhood you didn't have. For a long time, perhaps you protected your own people from any resentment, excusing them, understanding them, because loving them and caring for them left no room for anger. But anger, when it comes, is not a setback, it's part of the healing: it's the child you were, at last, acknowledging that it wasn't fair, that she deserved more, that it hurt. Let it come, with support if necessary, because it is by crossing through that anger, and not by avoiding it, that you arrive, on the other side, at a peace that is not made of excusing everything, but of having, at last, honored the truth of what you lived through.

The awakening, when it comes, is a deep relief, almost a grief and a liberation at the same time. It's the moment when you feel, inwardly, that perhaps you don't have to go on being everyone's adult. That you can set down the weight of a whole life. That the child you were can, finally, rest. It's an instant that can bring tears, because it undoes a reversal of decades, and because you feel, perhaps for the first time, that you have the right to be, yourself too, cared for.

## 7. Ceasing to be everyone's adult

This crossing has a destination, and it is to set down the post you took up as a child and never again let go of.

And I want to show you what this means, in practice, with all care.

Ceasing to be everyone's adult is ceasing to take on, automatically, responsibility for everything and everyone. It's letting the other adults be adults, take care of their lives, carry what is theirs. It's leaving the post of universal responsible one that you took up as a child, and that you never let go of.

It's also learning to distinguish, in each situation, what is yours and what is others'. Because your wound fuses everything: an adult's problem becomes yours, someone's pain becomes yours, each person's life becomes your responsibility. And part of the crossing is, faced with each weight, to ask: is this mine to carry, or is it someone else's? Am I responsible for this, or am I taking on what falls to a capable adult? That simple question, repeated, helps you separate what is yours from what never was, and to set down, weight by weight, what was not yours.

Setting the backpack down is going to cost you. You're going to feel the old guilt, the old fear of disaster, the old sense that if you're not the one, no one takes care of it, and everything collapses. And you're going to want to go back to taking on, to taking care, to holding up, so that the anxiety will pass. That fear is real, and it is old, but it comes from a time when the danger of everything falling was true, and you were the only thing between the house and disaster. Today, as an adult, that is almost never true anymore, even though your body goes on sounding the alarm as though it were.

But it's precisely there, in that not-taking-on, endured, that your liberation lies. Because each time you let an adult be responsible for himself, and the world doesn't collapse, you learn that you weren't the only thing holding everything up. That the others are more capable than the child in you believed. That resting doesn't bring about the disaster you feared.

It's going to cost you, because the old voice, the one that says everything depends on you, is etched deep, and it will appear each time you try to set things down, with its guilt of always. It will tell you that you're abandoning, failing, being irresponsible. Don't expect to silence it in a day, that voice has the age of the child who learned it; what changes everything is to stop doing what it commands. To hear it say you have to take care of everything, and to answer it, slowly, this is not mine to carry, and I too have the right to be cared for. And each time you set things down despite it, and the world doesn't collapse, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows your place was never to be everyone's adult.

And I want to set down a word here about the small ones watching the backpack on your shoulders, especially if you have children. Because parentification is passed on, from generation to generation, without anyone meaning it. A person who was never cared for can, without realizing it, lean too much on her own children, make them her confidants, her shelters, passing on to them the weight that was passed on to her. Healing this wound is not only for yourself, it's also so as not to pass it on. By ceasing to be everyone's adult, and by letting yourself, at last, be cared for, you give your children the chance to be only children, to receive without having to give, to live the childhood that was taken from you.

And ceasing to be everyone's adult is, above all, beginning to be your own adult. To care for yourself, you who were never cared for. To give the child you were what she never had: protection, rest, joy, care. To be, for yourself, the good adult who was missing in your childhood.

I want to give you a concrete way of beginning to care for the child you were. Think of her, the child you were, at the age when you already carried what you shouldn't have. Look at her, inwardly. And ask yourself what she needed, and didn't have: comfort, rest, protection, someone to tell her that she didn't have to be the one to take care of everything. And begin, slowly, to give that to yourself, today, as an adult. When you catch yourself carrying everything, remember her, and give her what was missing: a pause, a care, a permission to be small. To reparent the child you were, to be for her the good adult she didn't have, is one of the deepest, and tenderest, works of this crossing, and it's done, very often, better with help.

I want to speak to you about what opens up when you leave the post. There opens up, for the first time, the possibility of being small. Of not coping, and everything being fine. Of asking for help, and receiving it. Of resting, without guilt. Of playing, without feeling selfish. Of receiving care, without feeling weak. Things that everyone has, and that were denied to you, and that you can, at last, give back to yourself.

And I want you to see that recovering childhood is not a whim, nor a luxury, nor something you have no right to because you're an adult. It's a reparation. There are, in you, the needs of a child that were never met, and that remain alive, beneath the responsible adult: the need to play, to be cared for, not to have to cope with everything, to receive without deserving. Those needs don't disappear by being ignored, they stay waiting. And giving yourself, as an adult, a little of what was missing, allowing yourself the game, the rest, the comfort, the care, is not returning to being a child, it's honoring the child you were, and completing, at last, what was left unlived.

And there opens up a new relationship with your family. Because as long as you're everyone's adult, you keep the others dependent on you, and the relationship rests on your sacrifice. When you leave the post, you let them grow, take responsibility, be adults, and the relationship can become, at last, between equals, and not between one who carries and the others who lean.

I want to ready you for something, so that you don't reach back for the straps when it comes. When you begin to give back, to cease being everyone's adult, it's likely the family will resist. It got used to your being the one who carried, and change is unsettling. There may be complaints, pressures, sentences that make you feel selfish, absent, a bad daughter or a bad sister. There may even be those who accuse you of having changed, of no longer being the one you were. None of that means the small one is finally setting down her backpack wrongly. It's the natural reaction of a system reorganizing itself, and that tests whether the change is really for real. If you give in, you go back to the post; if you cross through it, with firmness and without war, the family, slowly, finds other ways to stand on its own, and you, at last, rest.

And I want you to see that this, however much it costs, is also a good for them. A family that leans entirely on one person doesn't grow, doesn't develop its own resources, stays dependent. By ceasing to carry everything, you give the others the opportunity, that your devotion took from them, to become capable, to take responsibility, to grow. You don't abandon them. You give them back their own strength, that your over-responsibility, without meaning to, was confiscating from them. Sometimes, the most generous thing you can do for those you love is not to go on carrying them, it's to let them, at last, learn to walk.

Once the backpack touches the ground, there is a joy waiting for the child who straightens up. The joy of setting down a lifelong weight. Of ceasing to be responsible for everything. Of being, at last, cared for, even if belatedly, by yourself and by those who love you. Of recovering, as an adult, a little of the childhood that was taken from you. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the guilt, on the day you cease to be everyone's adult, and become, at last, the adult who takes care of you.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. And, in this case, understanding can also be the beginning of a deeper healing, with help.

Understanding does not lift the whole backpack from your shoulders in a single morning. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, set down a lifelong responsibility. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your sacrifice with your maturity, and your weight with your duty.

Now you know that you were an adult too early, and that this was not maturity, it was a stolen childhood, an adaptation to a lack, and not a gift that flourished.

You know that there was a reversal of places, that you became the mother of your mother, the father of your father, the caregiver of your siblings, and that you took up a post that was not yours, at the cost of the protection you should have received.

You know that what was asked of you didn't fit in a child, and that you coped at the price of sacrificing the child you were, and that having managed doesn't mean it should have been you.

You know that resting feels to you like betrayal, playing like selfishness, receiving like weakness, and that this guilt is born from having had a place only while you cared.

You know that you took care of everyone and were never cared for, and that the child you were still hungers for the care that never came.

You know that, underneath, there may be trauma, and that this asks for serious help, from a professional, because a book accompanies but does not treat.

You know that the verb is to give back, what was never yours, and that giving back is not abandoning, it's loving from the right place, restoring the order of things, first within yourself.

And you know that you can cease to be everyone's adult, and become, at last, the adult who takes care of you, and of the child you were and who was never cared for.

I want to hand you a picture to carry where the backpack used to sit, and I want you to keep it.

Imagine a small child, still with short legs, carrying on her shoulders an enormous backpack, heavy, full of the worries, the pains, the responsibilities of whole adults. And walking like that, bent under the weight, for years, never able to set it down, never able to play, because the backpack was too important to let go of.

That child is you. And the backpack was never yours. It was placed on your back by adults who couldn't carry it. The crossing is setting down the backpack. Giving back, with care, what was inside it to whom it belonged. And discovering, as you straighten up for the first time, that beneath the weight there was a child, who never got to be one, and who deserves, now, even if late, to be, at last, cared for.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more responsibility to be carried out to perfection. Unstrapping a backpack carried since childhood is slow work, and the straps will slip back onto your shoulders more than once. Days when you set things down, when you let yourself be cared for, when you're small, and days when you go back to carrying everything, to being everyone's adult, in a panic that something will fall. And the old guilt will use each setback to tell you, you see, you're the one who has to take care of everything. Don't believe it. The slipping of the straps belongs to the crossing too. No one sets down all at once a backpack they carried since childhood. It's set down little by little, and each time you set it down and the world doesn't collapse, a new piece of proof remains, and it's from those proofs, added together, that the permission to be, yourself too, cared for, slowly, is built.

And I want to kneel down to the height of that small sentinel and tell her one last thing, with all tenderness. That child who carried the world did nothing wrong. She did something extraordinary, with an immense love, to hold up what the adults didn't hold up. Nothing in that small one who carried the backpack needs fixing. But she deserved to have been protected, held up, allowed to be small. And today, as an adult, you can give her yourself what was missing then: you can be, for the child you were, the good adult she never had. Not to replace her, but to care for her, at last, and to tell her, slowly, what no one told her: you can set things down now, you no longer have to hold everything up, now I take care of you.

Deep down, beneath the straps, you already carry the sentence that holds up this book. I can cease to be everyone's adult. And I can be, at last, the adult who takes care of the child I was.

Giving back was the first step. But there is a second, and to that one no page takes you. And, more than in any other book, this second step is done, very often, with prepared hands, with a professional to accompany you in undoing, with safety, a wound that comes from very far back. Because the child who carried the world deserves, at last, to be held up by someone who knows how.

And before you close these pages, with the backpack at last resting on the ground beside you, I ask one thing of you only. A hard one, and a tender one.

Today, do something childlike. Something useless, joyful, carefree, just for the pleasure of it. Without aim, without responsibility, without serving anyone. And notice the guilt that rises, the voice that says you should be doing something useful, taking care of someone. And stay with the childlike thing all the same. Because it is in that small gesture, in the midst of the guilt, that you begin to give yourself back the childhood that was taken from you, and to become, at last, the good adult that child always deserved to have.

And if, in doing so, you feel that you're being irresponsible, selfish, abandoning those who need you, remember: that thought is the old guilt, of the child who only had a place while she cared, not the truth. The truth is that you have the right to be, yourself too, cared for, light, small, and that giving yourself that does not betray anyone, it repairs, at last, an injustice you carried far too early.

Because that child who took care of everyone deserves, now, to be cared for, and the adult she waited for her whole life can be, at last, you.

Because the child who carried the world deserves, at last, to set it down, and to be, herself, held up.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
