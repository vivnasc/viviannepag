# The woman who is afraid to owe

**For the woman who knows how to give to everyone and cannot bear to receive from anyone. Because needing someone was never the prison you learned to fear.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks about closed herself off to receiving so as not to be trapped, and there is a point where that armor stops protecting and starts isolating. If you recognize in yourself an inability to ask for help even when you are sinking, a loneliness that already costs you your body and your spirit, or a distrust that won't let you get close to anyone, this may have roots that deserve the care of a professional. Asking for help is not being left in debt. It is the first time you let yourself receive.*

---

## 1. I owe nothing to anyone

There is a phrase you have perhaps said many times, with pride, and that says more about you than you imagine: I owe nothing to anyone. You say this like someone stating a virtue, and there is, in fact, dignity in it. But if you look beneath the dignity, you will find something else, older and less comfortable: a fear. The fear of owing. The fear of receiving and, with that, being trapped, being in deficit, being in someone's hands.

Notice how you react when someone offers you something. Someone wants to pay for your coffee, and you insist on paying for your own. Someone offers to help you carry something, to move house, to take care of something, and you say it isn't necessary, that you'll handle it, that you can manage. Someone does you a favor, and the first thing you feel is not gratitude, it is an urgency to repay, to balance, not to be left owing, as if the favor had put you in an uncomfortable position from which you need to escape quickly. You receive badly, you refuse easily, and when you cannot refuse, you stay restless until you have given back double.

And it is curious, because at giving you are immense. You give without hesitating, you help without it weighing on you, you are there for everyone, you are the first to offer yourself. The problem was never the generosity. The problem is the direction. You know how to be in the place of someone who gives, and you don't know how, you truly cannot bear, to be in the place of someone who receives. As if giving were safe and receiving were dangerous. As if there were, in the act of receiving, a threat that in the act of giving does not exist.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in a revealing detail: the relief you feel when you manage to give back. When someone does you a favor, you stay restless, with the relationship out of balance, and you only rest on the day you repay, when you return the favor, when you settle the bill, when you erase the debt. That relief says everything. For most people, receiving is pleasant and giving back is just a natural part of the cycle. For you, receiving is the uncomfortable part and giving back is the relief, because giving back takes you out of the place you cannot bear, that of someone who owes, and puts you back in the place where you feel safe, that of someone who is even or ahead. That little cycle of restlessness and relief, repeated a thousand times, is your armor working in real time, and noticing it is the first step to realizing that it is not the world that puts you in debt, it is you who cannot bear the place of someone who, for a time, simply received.

I want to name that threat, because it is the heart of everything: for you, receiving means being trapped. There is, deep down, a silent equation that says that whoever receives is left owing, whoever is left owing becomes dependent, and whoever becomes dependent is in the other's hands, exposed, vulnerable, with no way out. So you refuse to receive: not because you don't need to, but so as not to enter that chain that ends, in your imagination, in a prison. You would rather carry everything alone, whatever the cost, than risk owing anything at all to anyone at all.

I want you to know, right at the start, that this book does not come to tell you that you have to become dependent, fragile, or that your autonomy is a defect. Your capacity to sustain yourself, to handle things yourself, to not hang on anyone is a real strength, and I am not going to take it from you. I came to look at something else: at the point where that strength became a wall, and the wall, which served to protect you, came to keep anyone from reaching you. At the solitary price of owing nothing to anyone, which is, in the end, having no one on whom you can, truly, depend.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read on the defensive: the problem is not that you need to be independent. It is that you believe that needing others imprisons you. That belief, ancient and silent, turned every gesture of receiving into a threat, and made of you a woman who gives to the whole world and lets the world give her nothing back. We are going to look at where it was born, and at what would happen if, for once, you let yourself receive without fleeing.

Perhaps you can already feel the wall in small, daily refusals that you call independence. It's drowning in work and not asking anyone for help, even those who would give it gladly. It's preferring to do badly alone than to do well accompanied, just so as not to owe. It's having difficulty delegating, trusting a task to another, because depending on someone's work leaves you tense. It's feeling uncomfortable when you are cared for, pampered, surprised, and immediately wanting to repay in order to erase the debt. It's, at the limit, being in suffering and telling no one, bearing alone what you could share, because sharing would be needing, and needing is the thing you learned not to do. They are all the same: the refusal to receive, so as not to be trapped.

I want you to notice a contradiction you may never have noticed, because it reveals the size of the wall. You want, deeply, to be loved. Like everyone. You dream of close relationships, of intimacy, of someone who truly knows you and on whom you can rest. And at the same time you do everything, without knowing it, so that that closeness never happens, because closeness requires needing, and needing is what you do not allow. You want to be known without owing, loved without handing yourself over to the imbalance of receiving, and those two things do not fit together, because no one truly knows you until you let yourself owe them something. That is why so many strong and capable women like you live surrounded by people and secretly alone, admired and not known, sought out to solve things and never sought out simply to be there. It is not that love does not want you. It is that you do not let it come to the distance where it would have to see you need.

And there is a detail that says everything about the size of this refusal: you probably prefer to suffer alone than to be relieved accompanied. Has it happened to you to be down, in crisis, truly needing someone, and to say nothing to anyone? To smile on the outside, to answer that everything is fine, to carry alone what was crushing you, while around you there were people who loved you and who would have come in an instant if they had known? That is the sharpest point of the armor: it reaches a place where it no longer protects you from any danger, it only keeps you from being rescued. And it is worth looking at it head on, because that is where independence stops being dignity and starts being a silent way of abandoning yourself.

I am not going to ask you to stop being autonomous. I am going to ask you for something much harder: that you begin to ask yourself whether needing others is really the prison you learned to fear. Because perhaps, and this is what we are going to see, needing does not diminish you, and receiving does not imprison you, and the true prison is, after all, the wall you built so as not to owe anything, and that has left you outside of all exchange, alone on the inside of a fortress that no one can cross.

## 2. If I receive, I get trapped

There is something that needs to be understood about giving and receiving, because it explains the tension you feel. Between two people, giving and receiving create a movement, a circulation. When you receive something from someone, a small imbalance is left between you, a slight pressure asking you to give back, and when you give back, the balance is restored, and the relationship breathes, and grows deeper, because each exchange weaves one more thread between you. That is how relationships are built, in this back-and-forth of giving and receiving that, far from being a debt, is the very fabric of love between equals.

But for you that imbalance, instead of being a gentle pressure that weaves, is an alarm. The instant you receive, you feel the scale tip, and instead of living it as the beginning of an exchange, you live it as a debt that tightens around you, a deficit you have to settle urgently. You cannot bear to be on the side of someone who owes. So you give back quickly, double, or you refuse from the start so as not to even enter the game. And in doing so, without knowing it, you also refuse what giving and receiving build: the intimacy, the interdependence, the fabric that is born only when two people allow themselves to owe each other over time.

Notice the strange effect this has: by always giving back double, by always repaying more than you received, you think you are being generous, and you are, in truth, refusing the gift. Because someone who receives a favor and immediately returns a bigger favor is saying, without words, "I don't want to be left owing you, here, now it's you who owes me." You reversed the debt instead of accepting it. And the person who wanted to give you something is left with the gesture undone, turned into a commercial exchange where she only wanted to offer. There is a subtle violence in this generosity-as-response, however involuntary: it does not let the other give. It refuses them the place of someone who offers. And with time, people learn not to give you anything, because they know that everything they give you will come back bigger, turned into a debt of theirs, and it tires you to love someone to whom you can never manage to give anything.

Notice what this does to you in relationships. A healthy relationship between adults lives off an exchange that is never perfectly balanced, and it is good that it should be so: today I receive, tomorrow you give, and it is that imperfect dance, that little balance of credit and debit that never closes, that keeps the two connected, always with a small thread pulling toward the next meeting. You, by wanting to settle everything immediately, by not being able to bear being left owing, cut that thread. You leave the accounts always at zero, and a relationship with the accounts always at zero is a relationship with no woven future, because nothing is left pending to connect you. Without knowing it, by refusing to owe, you refuse the very mechanism by which people bind themselves to one another, in the good sense of the word.

And there is a particular way this shows itself, which you may recognize: you always give more than you receive, on purpose, even if you don't admit it. You always keep yourself on the creditor side of the relationship, always with the balance in your favor, because being on the side of someone who gave more is being safe, and being on the side of someone who received more is being exposed. But notice what this creates: unbalanced relationships, in which you are always the strong one, the one who gives, the one who doesn't need, and the other is always the one who receives, the one who is left owing, the one who can't repay you because you don't let them. And those relationships tire you, and sometimes you resent them, without seeing that it was you who built them that way, by always refusing the place of someone who receives.

In the way I learned to look at these things, there is an imbalance here that asks to be seen: you want the whole exchange in one direction. You want to give and not receive, to sustain and not be sustained, to take care and not be taken care of. And an exchange in one direction is not an exchange, it is a wall disguised as generosity. Because while you give, you keep control, you keep the distance, you keep the position of someone who doesn't need. Giving, for you, has become a subtle way of not getting too close, of not letting anyone into the place where you could, in fact, need. You give a great deal, precisely so as not to have to receive, and so your generosity, which looks like openness, is after all your most sophisticated way of closing yourself off.

Look inside a home, at the exchange between parents and children, and you suddenly understand where your discomfort comes from. Between parents and children, the exchange is, by nature, unequal: the parents give and the children receive, and the child has no way of giving back, nor does he need to, because his place is to receive and later to pass on. But between adults, between equals, the exchange has to balance itself over time, in a back-and-forth of giving and receiving that keeps the two on the same plane, neither above, neither below. Now, when someone only knows how to give and never to receive, she places herself, without realizing it, always in the position of someone who is above, in the position of the source, of the parent, of the one who has and provides, and leaves the other eternally below, in the position of someone who receives and cannot repay. Your refusal to receive, which you feel as humility, is sometimes, seen from the outside, a way of keeping yourself always above, always in the strong position, and that, however unconscious it may be, prevents the equality from which true intimacy is born. One is not intimate with someone who is always above. One is grateful, one is dependent, but one is not intimate.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because the tendency will be to turn it into one more failing. It is no failing. The refusal to receive was, somewhere, an intelligent way of protecting yourself from a dependency that, in the past, hurt you or frightened you. You learned that needing was dangerous, and your response, to close yourself off to receiving, was the best you found. Except that that protection, kept over a lifetime, stopped protecting you from an ancient danger and came to deprive you of a present richness: that of being able, at last, to let someone take care of you.

## 3. The armor of independence

I am going to tell you something that may bother you, because it will touch on what you are most proud of. Your independence, the one you speak of with such pride, is not always the strength it seems. Often, it is an armor. And an armor, however gleaming it may be, is distinguished from a strength by one thing: strength chooses, armor cannot be taken off. You don't choose to be independent in each situation, as it suits you. You can't be anything else. And that is what makes it a shell, and not a freedom.

Notice the difference. A woman who has inner strength can be autonomous when it makes sense and can ask for help when she needs to, can give and can receive, can be in command and can hand herself over to another's care. She has both positions available, and she chooses between them. You, no. You only have one. You know how to be the one who gives, the one who solves, the one who doesn't need, and when life pushes you toward the other position, that of someone who receives, who needs, who depends, you enter panic, discomfort, flight. You are missing half of the human movement. And the half you are missing is, precisely, the one that would truly connect you to others.

This armor has become your identity. You are "the strong one," "the one who endures everything," "the one who never complains," "the one who is no trouble to anyone," "the one who solves it alone." And there is an enormous pride in this, and an enormous loneliness too, because an identity built on not needing anyone is an identity that has to keep everyone at a certain distance. You cannot let them get too close, because closeness would bring the possibility of needing, and needing would destroy your whole identity. So you live surrounded by people who admire you and none who truly knows you, because to truly know you would require letting them see you need, and that, the armor does not allow.

And I want to name what the armor hides, because it is moving: beneath the woman who needs no one there is, almost always, a child who needed immensely and was not attended to. Who asked and did not receive, who leaned and the support gave way, who depended and the dependence let her fall. And that child drew from it a logical and painful conclusion: if I can't count on anyone, then I have to be enough for myself, I have to not need, because needing and not being attended to hurts too much. The adult independence that gives you such pride was born, often, from that early disillusionment. It is not strength that came from nowhere. It is the scar of a needing that was left without an answer, hardened to the point of no longer looking like a scar, but like muscle.

There is another possible root, and it is worth looking at: perhaps you saw, at home, what happened to those who depended. Perhaps you saw a mother trapped by depending financially on a man, or a woman in the family erased by needing, or someone who became a hostage to whoever sustained them. And you drew, as a child or as a young woman, a silent vow: with me, never. I will never depend on anyone, I will never be trapped, I will never be in someone's hands as they were. That vow saved you from repeating a prison you saw, and in that it did good. But taken to the extreme, it turned into another prison, that of isolation, and perhaps you have not yet noticed that fleeing one cell locked you in another, cleaner, more dignified, more yours, but equally a cell.

I want to stop here a moment, because there is a point at which this armor stops being a character trait and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If your inability to receive and to ask leaves you, in fact, alone before things too heavy to face alone, if there is a loneliness that already robs you of sleep, spirit, will, if beneath the shell you recognize ancient experiences of abandonment or of betrayal that taught you that no one can be trusted, then this deserves to be cared for with support. There are stories in which the refusal to depend was engraved by real and deep wounds, and those are not resolved by reading alone nor by force of will. To say it is the most honest way of taking care of you: there are those who need, before learning to receive from others in life, to learn to receive first in a safe relationship of help, with someone trained to hold you while you lower, slowly, a guard you had good reasons to raise.

I want you to see your armor with gratitude and with lucidity at the same time. With gratitude, because it kept you standing, made you capable, spared you dependencies that would in fact have hurt you. And with lucidity, because it is no longer only protection, it is also the wall that separates you from what you most need: from a relationship in which you can, at last, not have to be strong. Where you can need without losing your place, depend without being trapped, receive without being left owing. That relationship exists, and it is possible, but only on the other side of the armor, and to reach it you are going to have to do the thing it was built to prevent: to risk needing.

## 4. Who am I when I need

I am going to let a question fall here, on its own, and I ask you not to dispatch it quickly with your habitual answer that you are fine, that you don't need anything, because it is precisely that answer that we are going to call into question.

Who am I when I need?

Stay with it. Notice the discomfort it brings, the immediate urge to refuse it.

For a woman who built herself on not needing anyone, this question touches the deepest fear, and it is worth understanding why. It is that you don't know who you are in the place of someone who needs, because you forbade yourself from being there. Your whole identity was built in the opposite position, in that of someone who gives, sustains, endures, solves. Take that position away from you, put you in the place of someone who needs help, who depends on another, who can't manage alone, and a terrifying emptiness appears, the sense that there, in that place, you are no one, or worse, you are someone diminished, exposed, in danger.

And the fear at the bottom of all this has two faces. One is the fear of being trapped, of owing and therefore being in the other's hands, of losing the freedom that cost you so much to win. The other, deeper and less confessable, is the fear of needing and not being attended to again. Because that is what happened, somewhere: you needed and the support failed. And the part of you that still remembers that abandonment would rather not risk it again, would rather be enough for herself forever, than expose herself once more to the pain of asking and seeing that no one comes. Your refusal to need is not only pride. It is also a child protecting herself from reliving the greatest of her sorrows.

Notice how this refusal, which looks like fortitude, makes you strangely fragile. Because a woman who cannot need is alone before everything that life brings. Before illness, loss, exhaustion, fear, despair, she is always alone, because she cannot lean on anyone, cannot receive, cannot let herself be held. And there are moments in life when no one should be alone, moments when needing others is not weakness, it is survival, it is humanity. And you, in those moments, close yourself off even more, endure even more alone, and what seems to you like dignity is, sometimes, a loneliness that harms you, a refusal of rescue that no true strength would demand of you.

Notice too that this refusal robs you of something you say you want: to be known. For someone to truly know you, they have to see you whole, and you are whole on both sides, in what you give and in what you need, in the strength and in the fragility, in what you solve and in what frightens you. When you only show the strong side, when you only let yourself be seen giving, solving, enduring, you give people half of yourself, and then you suffer from feeling alone even when accompanied, without realizing that you fabricated that loneliness yourself, by hiding the half that needed. No one truly knows you because no one has seen you need, and no one has seen you need because you never allowed it. The intimacy you are missing is not the intimacy of someone who gives a great deal. It is the intimacy of someone who lets herself be seen whole, and that requires showing precisely what your armor hides.

And there is a cost you pay without seeing, and that those who love you pay too. The people who care about you want, naturally, to be able to give to you, to take care of you, to be there when you need. That is how love expresses itself, in both directions. By never letting them give you anything, by always refusing, by never needing, you deprive those who love you of the joy of taking care of you, and you keep those relationships eternally unbalanced, with them always receiving and never able to repay. There are those who pull away from you because of this, suffocated by never managing to reach you, by feeling that you are not needed and that they therefore are not needed. Your self-sufficiency, which you think a gift to others, is sometimes a door you shut in their faces.

I want to tell you something about this fear, because it begins to disarm it. Needing does not make you smaller. This is the lie your armor tells you, and it is time to question it. Where did you learn that whoever needs is worth less? That depending is humiliating? That receiving is being in a position of inferiority? You were not born thinking this. It was taught to you, by an experience or by an example, and it engraved itself so deep that it became invisible, the very way you feel. But look at the relationships you admire, the true connections you see in the world: they are not made of two people who don't need each other. They are made, precisely, of two people who allow themselves to need, depend, lean on, receive. Interdependence is not the opposite of strength. It is what strength permits, when it stops being afraid.

I am not asking you to answer right away who you are when you need. It is not answered with the head, it is answered with experience, and that is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because lowering a lifetime's armor is not something you do alone in a page. I ask you only to leave the question open, not to close it with one more proof that you are fine alone. Because it is in that question, borne without fleeing, that the possibility of something else begins to open: that of discovering that, in the place of someone who needs, you do not disappear, you do not diminish, you are not trapped. You simply, at last, let yourself be accompanied.

## 5. Needing does not make me smaller

If there is one sentence I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it slowly, because it dismantles the belief that governs you: needing does not make you smaller. It does not diminish you, does not imprison you, does not put you below anyone. Needing is, simply, what it means to be human and to be in relationship. And life, contrary to what your armor taught you, is not a debt to avoid. It is an exchange to trust.

Notice how this single idea loosens the knot you have carried for years. Your whole life rested on the premise that the ideal would be to need no one, to be enough for yourself, to be a self-sufficient island that gives to the world and owes it nothing. And that premise, which seems noble, is in truth impossible and lonely, because no human being is enough for herself, we all came from others, were sustained by others, and live tangled up in others, whether we want to or not. Total independence is an illusion, and an illusion that costs dearly, because to maintain it you have to deny your own need, and to deny your own need is to deny a part of you that does not disappear by being denied, it only stays alone.

Notice that your very existence began in an absolute receiving that you could not refuse. You received life. You received a body, a name, milk, an embrace, care, without having done anything to deserve it, without any way of repaying, in the purest imbalance there is. If your rule of owing nothing to anyone were true, you would have to apply it to life itself, and life is, by definition, an unpayable debt, because no one asked to be born and no one can give back to their parents what they received from them. To accept living is already to accept owing. And perhaps here lies the deepest root of your difficulty: a part of you never accepted this first receiving well, this entry into life in total imbalance, and spent its life trying to compensate for it, giving more than it receives, never owing, like someone trying to settle an original debt that has no way of being settled. But it is not meant to be settled. It is meant to be received, with gratitude, and passed on. Life does not ask you to pay for what you received. It asks you to let it, at last, have been worth it.

I'll say it another way. Human life is a current of giving and receiving that comes from long before you and continues long after. You received life without having asked for it nor being able to repay it. You received care when you were small and had no way of giving it back. And later you will give, to your own, to the world, what you received, and they will pass it on, and so the current continues, without anyone ever settling the accounts, because they are not meant to be settled, they are meant to be let to flow. Whoever tries to settle everything, whoever cannot bear to owe, whoever wants to always be even, leaves the current. She stays outside the very flow of life, alone with her accounts at zero, while around her life circulates, generous and unequal and alive, among those who allow themselves to owe one another.

And here is the verb of this crossing, the one that costs you most and that you most need: to trust. Because receiving without being left in debt requires trust. Trust that the other will not use against you the fact that you received. Trust that needing will not be taken advantage of to trap you. Trust that the exchange, over time, balances itself, without your having to control it at every instant. Your armor is, deep down, a distrust made muscle: you don't receive because you don't trust that receiving is safe. And the cure is not to receive by force, it is to rebuild, slowly, the trust that somewhere was broken, until receiving stops being a threat and goes back to being what it always should have been, a gesture of connection.

The awakening, when it comes, does not come like a surrender, it comes like a relief. It is the moment when you let, for once, someone help you, take care of you, give you something, and you stay there, in the place of someone who receives, and you notice, with amazement, that nothing terrible happened. That you were not trapped. That you did not lose your freedom. That the other did not use you nor trap you. That, on the contrary, that moment of receiving brought you closer, relieved you, reminded you that you don't have to carry everything alone. It is a small and enormous instant at the same time, because it disproves, with the force of experience, a belief that your head had defended for decades. And from that instant on, even if the armor closes again, you already know, inside, that there is another way, and that it does not kill you.

I want to show you something about trust, because it clarifies what happened to you. Trust is not a decision of the head, it is an experience of the body, and it is built, or destroyed, in repetition. If as a child you needed many times and were attended to, your body learned that needing is safe, that after asking comes the rescue, and you grew up trusting. If you needed and were left waiting, if you asked and the silence came, if you leaned and the support gave way, your body learned the opposite, that needing is followed by disillusionment, and it shut off the tap for protection. You did not decide to distrust. Your nervous system learned, with proof, that receiving was not worth the risk. So the cure is not to convince yourself to trust, it is to give your body new proof, small, repeated, that this time, with these people, needing is safe. Trust is rebuilt as it was broken: slowly, in experience, one time at a time, until the body learns that it is no longer the time of that ancient sorrow.

I want to be honest about one thing, because it matters: there will be people who do, in fact, use what they give to trap, who offer in order to control, who help in order to later collect with interest of submission. That danger is real, and your armor was born, in part, to protect you from it. To trust is not to become naive, it is not to receive from just anyone, it is not to lower your guard before those who want to trap you. It is to learn to distinguish. To recognize those who give out of love from those who give out of control, and to let yourself receive from the first without the fear of the second closing you off to all. The armor did not distinguish, it shut the door to everyone. Mature trust opens it to those who can be trusted, and that capacity to distinguish is precisely what gives you back the freedom the shell was taking from you.

## 6. Receiving without debt

If needing does not make you smaller, then there is an experience you are missing, and that changes everything: that of receiving without it turning into a debt. Of accepting what they give you without the tightness of having to give back, without the urgency to balance, without the restlessness of being left owing. And I want to speak to you about it carefully, because I know that for you it is one of the hardest things there is, and it is also one of the most healing.

Receiving without debt begins with a tiny and almost impossible gesture for you: to let time pass between receiving and giving back. When someone gives you something, instead of giving back right away, instead of paying immediately, stay, for once, in the position of someone who has received and has not yet given back. Not so as to never give back, but so as to bear the interval, the imbalance, the small debit left in the air. Notice the discomfort, the itch to balance, and do not give in at once. Because it is in that borne interval that you learn something new: that being left owing, for a time, does not destroy you, does not trap you, does not put you in anyone's hands. It is just the normal fabric of a relationship making itself, that back-and-forth that you always cut at birth.

There is a small phrase that can be your greatest practice, and that will cost you more than any task: to learn to say "thank you" and stop there. Without adding "but it wasn't necessary," without "I'll repay you later," without "oh, I feel so bad," without undoing the gesture with your hurry to balance it. Just "thank you," and stay. Notice how it is almost impossible for you to receive a compliment without giving it back or minimizing it, to receive help without promising to repay, to receive a gift without staying there calculating what you are going to give in return. Each of those reactions is the armor refusing what they give you. To train the dry, complete "thank you," that receives and rests, is to train a musculature you never used, that of someone who accepts. And though it seems a tiny thing, it is in those tiny things, repeated, that a whole life of refusal slowly comes undone.

And there is a way of receiving that is the hardest of all for you, and the most important: receiving without doing anything to deserve it. You can bear, with effort, to receive when you earned it, when you worked, when there is a justification that turns the received into a payment and not a gift. What you cannot bear is to receive freely, to be given something just because they love you, without your having done anything for it. Because there there is no way to balance, there is no merit to invoke, you are left exposed in the pure place of someone who is loved without having paid. And it is exactly that place, the most terrifying for you, that you most need to learn to inhabit, because it is the place of true love, which is neither earned nor paid for, only received.

Notice something important about what will happen when you start receiving. Some of your relationships were built on your not-needing, on your being always the strong one, the one who gives. When you start needing, asking, receiving, some people will find it strange, and some, those who needed you weak in order to feel strong, or who only knew how to be the side that receives, may pull away. I want to prepare you for that without fear: it is not the proof that you should stay closed. It is the revelation that that relationship depended on your imbalance, and does not withstand reciprocity. But you will also discover, and this is the luminous side, that there are people who are relieved when you finally let them give you something, who always wanted to be able to take care of you and never knew how, because you wouldn't let them. With those, the relationship deepens, becomes mutual, becomes real, because it stops being a one-way road and becomes a true exchange.

I want to speak to you about what receiving opens, because it is precious and you never knew it. When you receive, you give the other the place of someone who gives, and there is in that a generosity that you, from so much giving, never practiced: the generosity of letting the other be generous with you. There is an enormous dignity in knowing how to receive well, in accepting with grace, in letting them give to you without ruining the gesture with your hurry to repay. Whoever receives well honors whoever gives. And you, who always wanted to honor others by giving them everything, perhaps never realized that there is a way of honoring them that you are missing completely: that of letting them give to you, and of receiving what they give you like someone receiving a gift, whole, without throwing it back in the face of whoever offered it.

There is a place where this difficulty is seen with a clarity that may bother you: money. Notice how it costs you to receive money you did not "earn" in the strict sense. A gift of money leaves you uncomfortable. Financial help, even from someone who gives it to you from the heart, makes you feel in unbearable debt. A discount, an offer, something that reaches you without your having sweated for it, seems to you almost not to belong to you. And at work, perhaps you take it badly to be paid well for something that was easy for you, as if the payment were only legitimate in proportion to your suffering. This is not only about personal relationships: it is the same wall applied to material value, and it has a concrete cost, because a woman who can only receive money in the exact measure of what she suffered for it is putting an invisible ceiling on everything she can receive. The value that reaches you easily, freely, generously, is precisely what your armor refuses, and it is also, notice, what abundance is made of.

I am not going to give you a method for receiving, because it would be one more task to carry out well, and we would be feeding precisely your way of turning everything into merit. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because unlearning the distrust of a lifetime is not done in a page. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: let yourself owe, for a time, to those who love you. Not everything, not to just anyone, but to those who can be trusted, let yourself be left owing, let the scale tip, and discover that that tipping, far from trapping you, is precisely the thread that connects you, at last, to another human being.

---

## 7. Giving and receiving, and life circulating

There is a balance I want to show you, because it is the destination of this crossing, and it is the opposite both of your armor and of its opposite. I am not proposing that you trade not-receiving for not-giving, that you start to only receive and stop giving, that you become dependent instead of independent. That would be falling into the opposite cell. What I am proposing is the whole movement, both directions, the complete circulation: to give and to receive, to sustain and to be sustained, to take care and to be taken care of, as life asks, without any of the positions frightening you.

Notice what happens in a relationship where the exchange circulates in both directions. There are days when you give, there are days when you receive. There are phases when you are the strong one, there are phases when you lean. There are matters in which you help, there are matters in which you ask for help. And none of these positions defines you nor threatens you, because both are available, and you move between them with freedom, according to the moment. That is mature interdependence, and it is the opposite of your armor: not the absence of need, but need lived without fear, in the back-and-forth of two people who allow themselves, alternately, to need each other.

And I want to show you something about what circulates in that exchange, because it concerns the theme of this whole collection. When you refuse to receive, you are not only refusing favors and help. You are refusing value. You are preventing the value that exists in your life, in the form of care, of support, of affection, of goods, of money even, from circulating to you. You are a woman who knows how to make value go out, give, distribute, and does not know how to let it in, stay, be received. And the value that cannot come in ends up being scarce, not because you deserve it less, but because you shut the door through which it would arrive. Learning to receive is not only a matter of relationships. It is opening the tap through which all the abundance of your life, in all its forms, can finally flow back inward, and not only outward.

There is a fear you may have, by this point, and I want to go to meet it: the fear that, if you learn to receive, you will lose your strength, become dependent, weak, trapped, everything you always fled from. It will not happen. Your capacity to sustain yourself does not disappear by your learning to receive, it stays there, intact, available for when it is needed. What changes is that it stops being your only option, your prison. A woman who knows how to give and to receive is stronger, not less, than one who only knows how to give, because she has the whole movement, and not half. Interdependence does not weaken you. It frees you from the exhausting work of being strong all the time, alone, without ever being able to set anything down.

I want you to notice something about your children, if you have them, or about whoever sees you live, because it may move you more than anything else. Children learn to receive by seeing receiving. They learn that needing is not shameful by seeing someone need without shame. If they grew up seeing you give to everyone and never accept anything, endure everything alone, never ask, never let yourself be taken care of, they are learning from you the same armor, and it is likely that they will wear it one day, and that they will suffer the same dignified loneliness that you suffer. The most generous thing you can do for those who love you and see you is not to give them even more. It is to let yourself, in front of them, need and receive, so that they learn that this is possible, that this is human, that this diminishes no one. Sometimes a woman who never learned to receive for herself learns it, at last, so as not to deny it to those who come after her.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that you may not even remember wanting, from having so learned not to need it: the joy of being taken care of. Of falling ill and letting someone tend to you. Of being tired and letting someone carry, for once, a weight you swore you would never hand over. Of being down and letting someone hold you. Of receiving a gift and smiling without immediately thinking about what you are going to offer in return. Of belonging to a web of people in which you give and receive, sustain and are sustained, and in which, for the first time in your life, you are not alone enduring everything. That joy is your right, as much yours as anyone's, and it is waiting for you on the other side of the armor, in the moment when you trust enough to lower it.

I am not going to give you a step-by-step to reach that balance, because it would be one more thing for you to do well. The deep path is made slowly. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: life is not a debt to avoid, it is an exchange to trust. Enter the exchange. Leave the accounts unclosed. Owe, and let them owe you, and let life circulate between you and others without anyone needing to settle it, because it is precisely in that imperfect and never-even flowing that everything dwells that you, from so much not wanting to owe, never let yourself have.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in a simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything at once, and it doesn't make you lower the armor from one day to the next. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your loneliness with strength, and to begin to suspect that needing others was perhaps not the prison you learned to fear.

Now you know that owing nothing to anyone is not only a virtue, it is also a fear, the fear of owing and with that being trapped. You know that refusing to receive cuts the fabric that connects people, and that always giving more is, sometimes, your most sophisticated way of closing yourself off. You know that your independence has become an armor, and that beneath it there is a child who needed and was not attended to, and a vow to never depend again. You know, and this was the hardest, who you are when you need, and that the fear that appears there has two faces, that of being trapped and that of not being attended to again. You know that needing does not make you smaller, and that the verb of this crossing is to trust. And you know that on the other side there is not dependency, there is circulation, the whole movement of giving and receiving in which life, at last, flows in both directions.

And I want to leave you with the one image that holds your whole story of owing and refusing. For years you lived like a well that only knows how to give water, that distributes without stopping, generous, abundant, and that refuses any rain, any spring, anything that would fill it back up, because receiving water from outside seems to it a threat to its independence as a well. And a well that only gives and never receives, however deep it may be, runs dry. The crossing I propose to you is not to stop giving your water. It is to open yourself to the rain. It is to let the springs around you, the people who love you, fill you back up, so that you have, at last, somewhere to give from without exhausting yourself, and so that you discover that receiving does not make you less of a well, it makes you a living well, connected to the whole earth, instead of a closed cistern that prides itself on owing the rain to no one, and that therefore, slowly, empties itself.

And I want you to take a last distinction, because it protects everything else. Learning to receive is not becoming a person who hangs on, who demands, who expects others to sustain her. That would be falling into the opposite cell, and that is not what I am proposing to you. It is recovering the movement you are missing, without losing the one you already have. You go on knowing how to give, how to solve, how to sustain yourself, and that is your richness, you are not going to lose it. You only add the other side: the capacity to receive, to need, to lean, when it makes sense, on those who can be trusted. You stop having half of the human movement and come to have the whole movement. And a woman with the whole movement is not more dependent than one who only gives. She is freer, because she is no longer condemned to a single position, the strong one, forever, alone.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can receive without being left in debt.

Letting this debt-fear be named was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not made by reading, it is made by lowering, slowly, the armor of a whole lifetime, and by risking, time after time, letting someone reach you.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about money, nor is it going to teach you to earn more. It is a space for you to look at the way you keep value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the most stubborn ways: that of only letting it go out and never come in, that of giving to the world and not letting the world give you back. There, the crossing is to disarm the ancient distrust, to rebuild the trust that was broken, and to relearn to receive, to need, to depend, without that taking from you the freedom that cost you so much. Not so that you stop being strong, but so that your strength stops being a cell.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There, the crossing is to let in those who always kept their distance so as not to weigh. Here, the crossing is to let receive those who closed themselves off so as not to owe. One lowers the guard of weighing, the other lowers the guard of owing. But deep down it is the same journey: to discover that needing others does not diminish us, and that true strength includes the courage to, for once, not be strong.

If you feel that it is the moment, you can find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is all right, and you are not left owing me anything for having read. What you have read is already yours, with no bill, with no counterpart, waiting, with no hurry, for the day you decide to trust enough to let yourself receive.

And before you close these pages, I ask you for the one small debt you have spent a lifetime refusing to let yourself owe, and I ask it of you precisely because it will cost you more than anything. Today, or in these days, ask for help with something you would do alone. Something small, that you wouldn't really need to ask for, but ask. And when the help comes, receive it, and do not give back right away, do not balance in a hurry, do not erase the debt. Stay, for a moment, in the position of someone who needed and received. Notice the tightness, the urge to give back, and do not give in. Because it is in that staying, owing a little to someone who wishes you well, that the armor of a whole lifetime begins to lower, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that needing did not make you smaller, it made you, at last, accompanied.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
