# The woman who loves absence

**For you, who light up with distance and grow cold with presence. Because the love that is here is also love.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a way of loving that chases what runs away, and there is a point at which chasing stops being a pattern and becomes suffering: anxiety that won't let go, obsession with someone who isn't there, dependency that pulls the ground out from under you. If you recognize yourself in this, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I only want what runs from me

Notice something, honestly.

The one who wants you, who stays, who answers, who remains, leaves you cold. And the one who takes his time to answer, who pulls back, who doesn't give himself, lights you up.

You know this. You've lived it so many times that you've stopped finding it strange.

The available man becomes, almost immediately, less interesting. As if his availability stripped him of his shine. As if your wanting him were, in itself, a disadvantage.

And the man who runs, who won't decide, who leaves you in doubt, that one stays in your head. That one doesn't leave. That one lights in you an intensity you mistake for love.

Look back at your stories. How many began with a pursuit. How many died the instant the person finally stayed.

And notice what you feel when someone wants you truly, fully, without reservation. It isn't joy. It's a strange urge to run. A boredom. A distrust. Almost a tightening.

As if the love that is available could not be the real love. As if the real one had to cost, had to hurt, had to run, in order to be worth anything.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not a flaw in you. You're not shallow, you're not incapable of love, you're not broken beyond repair.

You know how to love. The problem is not your capacity to love. It's the direction you point it in.

You learned to point it at absence. At what runs away. At what doesn't arrive. And the love that is right in front of you, present, settled, that one you don't recognize, because it doesn't look like what you learned to call love.

This book will not teach you how to catch the man who runs. I didn't come to give you strategies to hold him, to make him stay, to win the game.

I came to show you something else. Why the game attracts you. Why absence tastes to you like love, and presence tastes like danger.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. The love you chase is not the love you want. It's the love you learned to chase, long before this man, long before any man.

Maybe you'll recognize yourself in small things.

In staying awake staring at your phone, waiting for a message that doesn't come, and feeling, in that waiting, a living intensity, almost sweet.

In losing interest on the exact day he shows himself sure of you.

In finding the steady, present, good men "without chemistry," and in calling "passion" the unrest of the one who treats you badly.

They are all the same. Absence lights you up. Presence puts you out. And until today you called it your type, your luck, your bad luck in love.

It's none of that. It's a pattern. And patterns, when they are seen, can change.

I want you to notice something more subtle. It's not only with men. It's with everything.

You want the job you can't get, and you lose interest in the one that accepts you. You want the invitation that doesn't come, and you devalue the one you're given. Your attention runs, always, toward what is out of reach.

Absence has, for you, a shine that presence never had. What runs away lights up. What stays goes dim.

And there is a cost in this that perhaps you've never tallied. The life you have, the people who want you, the love that is available, all of it passes you by without your seeing it, because your eyes are fixed on what isn't there.

You live in the room, but you always look out the window. And life happens in the room.

There's one more thing that confuses you. You call this way of loving "depth." You think you feel more than others, that you love with more intensity, that you're one of the ones who love for real.

But notice. The intensity you feel is not the depth of love. It's the pain of lack. It's the nervous system on alert because what you want is not guaranteed.

When it finally becomes guaranteed, the intensity drops. And you, instead of resting in the love, feel that it's over.

It isn't over. It just stopped hurting. And you learned to mistake love for the pain of not having it.

## 2. The love you learned to chase

Go back. Further back than any boyfriend.

Your first love was not a man. It was whoever raised you.

And it's there, in that first love, that you learn what it is to love. Not in theory. In the body. In the way love arrived, or didn't arrive, when you were little.

Ask yourself one thing, slowly. Was love, in your home, available? Or did it have to be earned, chased, won?

For many women who love absence, the answer is the same. There was love. But it wasn't available.

Maybe you had a father present in the house and absent from you. Who was there, but didn't reach you. Who looked at the newspaper, the work, the television, and at you only now and then, when you deserved it, when you earned it.

Maybe you had a mother who gave her love in drops, conditional, unpredictable. One day affectionate, the next day cold. And you, a child, studying her sky, trying to figure out what to do so the sun would come back.

Or maybe love was always busy. With a sick sibling, with the pain of the adults, with survival. Present in the house, unavailable to you.

Notice what a child learns in a place like this.

She learns that love does not come simply because she exists. It comes when she chases it, deserves it, wins it.

She learns that to love is to want what cannot be reached. It's to stretch toward an affection that is right there, in sight, but out of reach.

She learns that the natural shape of love is lack. Effort. Waiting.

And that learning doesn't stay in childhood. It stays in you. It becomes your map of love.

That's why, when you grow up and look for love, you look for it in the shape you know. In the shape of lack.

An available man doesn't match your map. Your map says love is chased. If this one doesn't run, something is wrong, either with him, or with the love, or with you.

A man who runs, that one fits. That one rekindles the child who learned to love at a distance. With that one, you feel, without knowing why, at home.

That's why you fall in love with the unavailable. Not because you're drawn to suffering. But because the unavailable reproduces the love of origin, and your system mistakes the familiar for the right.

I want you to see this without guilt. And without accusing whoever raised you.

Whoever gave you an unavailable love, most of the time, gave what they had. They gave the love that was also given to them that way. They didn't mean to wound you. They wished you well, in the incomplete way they knew.

And there is an order in families that is worth knowing, because it explains a great deal about your love.

Between parents and children, the parents are meant to give, and the children to receive. Love flows downward. The child doesn't have to do anything to deserve it. She receives it for existing.

When that order is reversed, when it's the child who has to tend to the adult's mood, win his attention, deserve his affection, something falls out of balance. The child begins to work for the love she should simply receive.

If you were that child, you learned to stand on the wrong side of the order. You learned to earn love instead of receiving it. And you never again knew how to stand on the side of the one who receives, without effort, what is given.

That's why, in adult love, you put yourself to work again. To deserve. To win. Even when love is offered to you for free, you don't know how to receive it for free, and you find a way to turn it into something to be deserved.

But the truth is this. You learned to love where love was difficult. And you carried that lesson everywhere.

There's one thing worth looking at calmly. Children don't ask for the love they need. They adapt to the love that's there.

If the love that was there was unavailable, the child doesn't conclude "this love is insufficient." She concludes "love is like this, and I have to try harder."

She doesn't blame the love. She blames herself. She thinks that, if she were better, prettier, kinder, more deserving, love would become available.

And so she learns two things at the same time. That love is chased. And that, if she doesn't reach it, the failing is hers.

See if this doesn't still follow you today. When a man pulls back, what's your first thought? It's almost never "he's not available." It's "what did I do wrong."

You turn against yourself. You look for your defect. You try harder. Because you learned, as a child, that the other person's unavailability was a task of yours to solve.

Notice too a figure you might recognize. The love you chased first may not have been cold. It may have been intermittent.

Sometimes present, warm, whole. Other times absent, distant, closed. And that intermittence is the one that binds the most.

Because love that never comes, we give up on. But love that comes sometimes, that one we chase forever, waiting for the next moment when it returns.

Maybe that's what you learned. Not total absence. Intermittence. The sun that appears and disappears, and leaves you a whole lifetime studying the sky.

And today, when a man gives you attention in drops, hot and cold, near and far, you don't run. You stay. Because that is exactly the climate of your childhood, and you know how to live in it.

There's another piece in this inheritance, and it's one of the hardest to see.

Maybe you were the child who had to win the love of an adult who was wounded, absent, or turned toward something else. And maybe you managed, sometimes, to wrench from him a moment of presence.

That moment, rare, was engraved as the peak of love. The reward after great effort. The sun after great cold.

And you learned the most dangerous equation of all. The love that's worth something is the one wrenched from the one who doesn't give it easily.

The love that comes easily, given, without effort, that one doesn't have the taste of victory. It lacks the sweetness of having overcome the other's unavailability.

That's why, as an adult, the given love doesn't satisfy you. It lacks the conquest. It lacks the effort that, for you, is what makes love real.

And you look, without knowing it, for men who force you to win them over again. Who pull back, so you'll chase. Who close up, so you'll open them. Who are unavailable, so you can relive the only victory of love you know.

Notice how this condemns you. If only conquered love tastes to you like love, then you can only love the one who doesn't want you. Because in the one who wants you, there's nothing to conquer.

It's a perfect trap. You want what runs away, and the instant it stops running, it loses the taste you were looking for. You are, then, condemned to always love what you can't have, and to not want what you can.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, and without sinking into childhood.

Looking at the origin is not opening a case against whoever raised you. It's not making a list of the failings, feeding the hurt, staying to live in what you didn't have.

It's only seeing. Understanding where your map comes from, so you can redraw it. Whoever stays trapped in accusing her parents continues, deep down, to chase their love, now by way of complaint.

The crossing is not that. It's recognizing what was missing, without dramatizing and without denying, and then turning to face forward. Toward the love you can, today, give yourself and receive, and that no longer depends on those who, long ago, couldn't give it to you.

Whoever raised you gave what they had. You can grieve for what was missing and feel gratitude for what there was, at the same time. And you can, above all, stop continuing to ask old addresses for a love they will never have to send you.

The child waited for the love of origin to change. The adult knows it won't change, and that's all right, because the love she seeks is now in another place. In herself, first. And then in those who, today, are capable of staying.

## 3. Presence frightens, absence soothes

There's something you'll want to deny, but I ask you to let it settle.

Presence, for you, is not comfortable. It's threatening.

When someone is really there, available, loving you without running, you don't feel peace. You feel an alarm.

And when someone runs, pulls back, leaves you in uncertainty, you don't feel only pain. You feel, beneath the pain, a strange relief. A return to the known.

This seems backwards. It should be backwards. Presence should soothe and absence should hurt.

But your system wasn't made that way. It was made in childhood, and in your childhood the familiar was lack. That's why lack soothes you, even when it wounds you. And presence frightens you, even when it does you good.

Notice what happens in your body when an available man comes close for real.

It's not desire that rises. It's defense. Up comes the urge to push him away, to test him, to find in him a flaw that justifies running.

You sabotage. Without deciding to. You create distance where there was closeness. You go cold where there was warmth. Because closeness, for you, is foreign territory, and the foreign frightens.

And when he, tired of being pushed away, pulls back, notice what you feel. It's not only loss. It's also familiarity. You've returned to the ground you know, the ground of pursuit, and there, however much it hurts, you know how to move.

There's a name for this. It's called mistaking the familiar for the safe.

Your nervous system doesn't look for what does you good. It looks for what it knows. And what it knows is absence.

That's why, when you finally find yourself in a present love, you feel something is missing. There's no pursuit, no anxiety, no roller coaster that you learned to call love. And in the absence of that intensity, you decide it isn't love.

But maybe it is. Maybe it is, for the first time, real love, and you don't recognize it because it doesn't hurt.

There's a sentence that might catch you by surprise. You're not afraid of not being loved. You're afraid of being loved.

Think about it. Not being loved you already know. It's the ground of origin, it's lack, it's pursuit. Painful, but familiar.

Being loved, truly, fully, by someone who stays, that is foreign territory. That is what you don't know how to inhabit. And what we don't know how to inhabit, we avoid.

That's why, without meaning to, you sabotage present love. Not because you don't want it. Because you don't know how to receive it, and what we don't know how to receive, we push away.

Notice the forms of your sabotage. You find flaws where there are none. You create problems where there was peace. You test, you provoke, you pull back, looking for a reason to run before the love gets so close that it could wound you.

And when the man, tired, moves away, you tell yourself "I knew it, it wasn't for real." But it wasn't he who left. It was you who pushed him, slowly, until he couldn't bear being pushed anymore.

Present love doesn't fail you because it's too little. It fails because you, without knowing it, make it unbearable, by testing it to see whether it can withstand your distrust.

I want you to think about this carefully.

Maybe you've never rejected a man for being too little. Maybe you rejected them for being too much. Too present. Too available. Too yours.

And maybe, without knowing it, you always chose the one who kept you in lack, because lack was the only place where you knew how to love.

I want to show you how this shows in the body, because the body doesn't lie.

When you receive a message from a man who wants you, present, clear, without games, what's the sensation? For many, it's a slight drop. An "oh." A disappointment they can't explain.

And when the phone lights up with the name of one who treats you with distance, what's the sensation? A leap. The heart speeds up. The day lights up.

Notice what your body is doing. It's rewarding absence and punishing presence. It's giving you adrenaline with the one who wounds you and boredom with the one who cares for you.

It's not you, consciously, choosing badly. It's your body, trained in childhood, lighting up with what it knows.

And what it knows is unrest. Waiting. Uncertainty. Your body learned that this is love, and it responds to it with everything it has.

There's a hard word for this, but a fair one. You don't fall in love with men. You fall in love with states of anxiety.

The man who gives you anxiety you call passion. The man who gives you peace you call a friend, or boring, or "no chemistry."

And the chemistry you feel, the one you value so much, isn't compatibility. It's recognition. Your system recognizes, in the unavailable man, the climate of origin, and it fires.

Chemistry, for you, has been the name of familiar danger. And that's why your chemistry has led you, time and again, exactly to the places where you suffer most.

I want to give you an image for this, because it helps you see.

Imagine someone who grew up in a house where there was a lot of shouting. The noise was normal. Silence was rare, and almost always announced a storm.

That person, as an adult, walks into a silent house and feels bad. The silence, which should soothe, leaves her on alert. She misses the noise her body got used to.

You're like that, but with love. You grew up in the noise of lack, of waiting, of uncertainty. And today, in a silent, calm, safe love, you feel something is missing.

Nothing is missing. What's missing is the noise. And the noise was never love. It was only what you knew.

Presence frightens you for the same reason that silence frightens the one who grew up in shouting. Not because it's bad. Because it's unknown.

And the unknown, to the body, is always danger, even when it is, in fact, the safest thing that ever happened to you.

Notice a test you may have already run without knowing it. When a present man tells you, clearly, that he wants you, what's your inner reaction?

For many, it's distrust. "If he wants me this easily, there's something wrong with him." Or "if I won him over without effort, he's not worth much."

You invert the value. What costs you to have, you value. What is given to you, you suspect. And you apply this rule to love with a cruelty you'd never apply to anything else.

Imagine applying it to a friend. "This one likes me, so she must have a flaw." It sounds absurd. But it's exactly what you do in love.

There's a wound of worthiness hidden here. Deep down, you don't believe you deserve to be loved without effort. That's why easy love seems to you suspect, or too little, or false. Because a part of you feels that, if it were for real, it would have to cost, it would have to be deserved, it would have to be wrenched from someone.

And the love that is wrenched is never at peace. It's always at risk. You always have to keep deserving it, winning it, holding on to it. That's why, even when you have it, you don't rest.

Given love, that one rests. You don't have to hold on to it, because it's not running. And it's precisely that rest that is foreign to you, because you never knew it.

## 4. Who am I when I'm not chasing

I'll leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it too quickly.

Who are you when you're not chasing anyone?

Stay with it. Notice what it opens.

For you, this question may bring an emptiness. Because chasing is not just something you do. It became who you are.

You're always wanting someone. Always with a thought occupied by a man who isn't there, who doesn't answer, who won't decide. That tension, that stretched desire, has accompanied you for so long that it now seems like your personality.

You're the one who loves intensely. The one who feels everything deeply. The passionate one. The intense one.

But notice. You're almost never in a love. You're almost always in the pursuit of one.

And when, by chance, there's no one to chase, you don't feel peace. You feel an emptiness. A boredom. A restlessness that pushes you to find another right away, to rekindle an old one, to look for someone who'll give you again the tension you need.

That emptiness is your true shadow. Not the lack of love. The emptiness that appears when there's no pursuit at all to fill you.

Because pursuit fills. It gives you a focus, an adrenaline, a reason to wake up looking at your phone. It pulls you off something you don't want to feel.

And that something is the emptiness of being alone with yourself. With no one to desire. Without the tension of an absence inhabiting you.

You run from that emptiness with pursuit. You fill the silence with the desire for someone who isn't there. And so you never truly get to be with anyone, because you're always running after someone.

Notice how this robs you of the present.

You're at dinner with a good man, present, and your head is on another, who didn't answer. You're being loved and desiring the one who ignores you. You're here and living there.

Pursuit doesn't give you love. It gives you the illusion of love, while it keeps you from living the real love that may be, at this very moment, in front of you, waiting to be seen.

Notice something almost cruel that pursuit does to you. The more you chase, the less attractive you become to the one you were chasing.

Because pursuit smells of lack. And lack pushes away. Whoever runs feels your running and speeds up, because your need weighs, suffocates, takes the air away.

And so, without meaning to, your pursuit produces exactly what you most fear: distance. You run after, he pulls back more, you run more, he pulls back still further. And you call this bad luck in love, when it's actually a mechanism, and you are on one of its sides.

Presence, calm, not chasing, have the opposite effect. Whoever doesn't run after doesn't weigh. Whoever is whole, with her own life, not depending on the other's answer, is, paradoxically, more attractive, because she doesn't suffocate.

But notice: this is not a new strategy to hold him. I'm not telling you "don't chase, so that he'll come." That would be chasing in another way, with the same hunger underneath.

It's something else. It's no longer needing his answer so much that your life depends on it. And when that happens, for real, you stop chasing not out of tactics, but because you no longer need to. And then, yes, the one who is capable of staying stays.

And there's a fragility in this, beneath all your intensity.

If you're the one who chases, then you always need someone who runs. You depend on the unavailable in order to exist as a lover. Without a distant man, you don't know who you are.

You handed your identity over to the absence of others. And it's a terrible place to live, always dependent on those who don't stay.

Notice something that may be hard for you. Pursuit also spares you a piece of work.

While your head is occupied with a man who doesn't answer, it's not available for something else, harder. For looking at yourself. For feeling what you feel when there's no one to fill you.

The absent man is, without your knowing it, a distraction. A place to put your attention, so you don't have to put it on yourself.

And while you chase, you postpone the meeting with yourself. You postpone the questions that only appear in the silence. Who am I? What do I really want? What makes me happy, beyond being chosen by someone?

They are big questions, and they frighten. It's easier to think about him. Easier to chew over the last message, decode the last silence, plan the next approach. Anything but stopping and being with yourself.

That's why letting go of pursuit isn't only letting go of a man. It's accepting the meeting with yourself that pursuit was sparing you. And that meeting, at first, frightens, because you don't know who you'll find.

But it's there, in that postponed meeting, that your life is. Not in his next message. In you, waiting for yourself, for so long now, in the silence you always filled with absence.

Don't answer right away the question of who you are when you don't chase. It isn't answered with the head.

But leave it open. Because it's in that question that your freedom begins. The day you know how to exist without chasing anyone, you stop needing absence to feel alive. And then, only then, can you begin to recognize the love that is there.

I want to stay a little longer in this emptiness, because it's there that everything is decided.

The emptiness frightens you. That's why you fill it. With a man, with the pursuit of a man, with the pain of a man who doesn't answer. Anything will do, as long as it fills.

But notice what the emptiness really is. It's not proof that you're alone, that nobody wants you, that there's something wrong with you.

The emptiness is just the space that appears when you stop running. It's the silence after the noise. And in that silence there's a person waiting for you. You.

The child who learned to chase was never, herself, chased by anyone. No one ran after her. No one chose her first. And she waited, her whole life, for someone to do it.

And there's a way to end that waiting. It's not finally finding the man who will chase you. It's you returning to yourself. Choosing yourself. Staying with yourself in the emptiness you always filled with others.

I know it sounds like little, put this way. But it's everything. Because as long as you need an absent man to feel alive, you're in the hands of those who don't stay. And whoever is in the hands of those who don't stay never has peace.

Returning to yourself is not giving up on love. It's stopping begging it from those who don't give it, so that you can, at last, receive it from those who do.

I want you to look at something that may be hard to admit. Pursuit gives you power.

While you chase, you're in motion, you're doing something, you're in control of your pain. It's active. It's a way of not feeling powerless before absence.

Receiving, on the other hand, is passive. It demands that you stop, that you open your hands, that you let the other come to you. And that, for someone who learned to control lack through effort, is terrifying, because it takes the helm from you.

That's why you prefer chasing to receiving. Chasing keeps you doing. Receiving forces you to trust. And trusting, in someone who can stay or leave without your controlling it, is precisely what you could never do as a child.

Notice the irony. You call yourself romantic, devoted, capable of anything for love. But deep down you're defending yourself. Pursuit is your armor. While you run after, you never truly give yourself, because the other is never close enough to wound you from within.

It's present love that truly frightens you. Because present love demands the surrender that pursuit spares you. It demands that you lower your guard before someone who is really there, and who could, for that very reason, see you, and wound you, and stay.

To love absence is, deep down, a way of never risking yourself. You chase what runs away because what runs away never reaches you. And what never reaches you can never break your heart from up close.

## 5. When chasing stops being a pattern

I have to stop the book here. With seriousness.

There is a border between a pattern and a wound that truly wounds. And it's important to know which side of it you're on.

On one side is what we've been talking about. You light up with absence, you grow cold with presence, you chase what runs away. It hurts, but there's a path, and the path is this book.

On the other side is something more serious. And I want to name it, without circling around it.

If your thinking about a man who isn't there has become an obsession you can't stop. If you spend hours, days, chewing it over, watching, waiting, and you can't live in the meantime.

If someone's lack of an answer takes away your sleep, your appetite, your ability to function. If you feel that without that person you can't breathe.

If you've gone back, time and again, to someone who treats you badly, not out of love, but because you can't let go, because the idea of losing him gives you a physical panic.

If your entire life revolves around a bond that consumes you, and everything else, work, friends, you yourself, has disappeared.

This is no longer only a pattern to understand. It may be attachment anxiety, emotional dependency, a relational obsession that asks for help. And serious help, from a professional.

I want to be clear, with firmness and with tenderness. Understanding where your pattern comes from is precious. But it doesn't treat an established dependency.

If you see yourself in these signs, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Don't skip it. Seek support. A psychologist, a therapist, someone trained to hold you while you come out of the pit.

It's not weakness. It's the most lucid thing you can do.

Because there is a difference between loving absence and being trapped in it to the point of losing yourself. The first, this book accompanies. The second asks for hands closer than mine.

And seeking those hands is not giving up on love. It's beginning to treat yourself with the care you've been giving, all of it, to those who don't stay.

I want to add a border, because it's important and few people draw it.

Chasing the love that runs away is one thing. Staying clung to someone who treats you badly is another.

There are unavailable men who are only that, unavailable. They don't hurt you on purpose. They simply aren't ready, don't want the same thing, don't stay.

And there are men who use your pursuit. Who give you enough to bind you and little enough to keep you anxious. Who know that the more they pull back, the more you advance, and who play with that.

If you recognize this second type, pay redoubled attention. Because your pattern, the one of lighting up with absence, makes you an easy target for those who manipulate through distance.

Don't mistake the calculated coldness of someone who controls you for the innocent unavailability of someone who isn't ready. The first is a danger. And no understanding from this book should keep you near someone who uses your wound against you.

If there is, in the relationship you live in, control, humiliation, manipulation, or a pattern in which you always end up worse, this stops being a matter of self-awareness and becomes a matter of protection. Get away, and seek help. Understanding is not excusing, and loving is not enduring everything.

## 6. Love does not have to be conquered

If the love you chase is the love you learned to chase, then there's a new truth waiting for you. Read it slowly.

Love does not have to be conquered.

It can be received. It can be available. It can arrive without your having to deserve it, chase it, or wrench it from someone who doesn't want to give it.

I know this sounds strange. Your map says the opposite. Your map says easy love isn't worth anything, that real love costs, that if there's no pursuit there's no passion.

But your map was drawn by a child, in a place where love was, in fact, unavailable. It was accurate then. It's not accurate now.

Today there is, in the world, available love. There are men who stay, who answer, who give themselves without games. Your map doesn't mark them, because it was made in a territory where they didn't exist. But they exist. And recognizing that is redrawing the map.

Let me tell you this another way.

A newborn child doesn't chase her mother's love. She receives it. She does nothing to deserve it, doesn't run after it, doesn't win it. Love arrives because she exists.

That was your birthright. Available love, given, present. If you didn't have it to the measure you needed, it wasn't that love didn't exist. It was that the availability was missing.

And what we're going to do is not teach you to chase better. It's to give you back the possibility of receiving. Of letting love arrive without your hunting it.

The awakening, when it comes, doesn't arrive like a thunderclap. It arrives like a calm strangeness.

It's the day you're with a present man and, instead of the usual boredom, you feel, for an instant, peace. And you notice that peace, after all, is not the absence of love. It's what love feels when it stops running.

It's a small instant. But it changes everything. Because it disproves, with the force of what you felt, a law you'd been carrying for decades.

I want you to understand why peace tasted to you, for so long, like boredom.

The boredom you feel with a present man is not a lack of interest. It's your nervous system coming down from a state of alert it was used to.

When the body always lives on alert, on alert it feels alive, and off alert it feels strange, dimmed, lacking. It got used to the adrenaline, and without it, it thinks nothing is happening.

But it is. Peace is happening. Safety is happening. A love is happening that doesn't demand your vigilance. It's just that your body, trained in the storm, doesn't yet know how to read that as life.

That's why calm, at first, tastes like emptiness. Not because it's empty, but because your system hasn't yet learned to register calm as full.

And it learns. With time, with repetition, the body discovers that calm is not the absence of life, it's the ground where life, at last, can happen without a jolt. And on that day what seemed like boredom reveals itself as rest, and rest, for someone who never had it in love, is the most precious thing there is.

From there on, even if the old map speaks again, you already know there's another path. That love can stay. And that staying is not less than running. It's more.

I want to warn you about something, because it's going to happen.

When you begin to choose present love, you're going to feel strange. Almost in withdrawal.

You're going to miss the adrenaline. The roller coaster. The drama of waiting. And a part of you is going to whisper that this isn't love, that it's lukewarm, that you're settling for too little.

Don't believe that voice. That voice is the voice of addiction, not the voice of love.

Whoever quits an addiction, at first, doesn't feel better. They feel worse. They miss the substance they got used to. And they mistake the lack of the substance for the lack of life.

The intensity of absence was your substance. In letting go of it, you're going to feel its emptiness. You're going to want to go back to it, thinking that's where life was.

It wasn't. There was the pain. Life is on the other side, in the love that stays, and that one, at first, is too calm for your palate, addicted to the storm.

Give your palate time. The calm, which today tastes to you like boredom, is going to begin, slowly, to taste like home. And one day, without noticing, you're going to prefer the quiet of the one who stays to the frenzy of the one who runs.

That day can't be forced. It's prepared. Every time you stay, instead of running, you bring it a little closer.

And I want to tell you what the awakening is not, so you don't deceive yourself.

It's not that you stop feeling attraction for the one who runs. That attraction, trained over years, doesn't disappear overnight. You're going to keep, for some time, feeling the leap in your chest before the unavailable.

What changes is not what you feel. It's what you do with what you feel.

Before, the leap was an order. You felt the attraction to the unavailable and you followed it, without questioning, straight into suffering.

After the awakening, the leap becomes a warning. You feel the attraction to the unavailable and, instead of following it, you stop. "Here's my pattern recognizing familiar ground. Careful."

The healing is not to stop feeling the old. It's to stop obeying the old. It's to be able to feel the magnet of absence and, even so, choose presence.

At first, this demands an enormous effort, almost against nature. You're going against a lifetime of training. But every time you do it, the training weakens, and the new grows stronger.

Until the day when, without effort, the present attracts you more than the absent. That day comes. But it comes at the end of many days in which you chose presence even though you felt like reaching for absence.

## 7. Seeing the love that is here

There is a destination for this crossing. And it fits in one word. To see.

Not to chase. Not to conquer. To see. To recognize the love that is here, instead of running after what runs away.

And I want to show you what changes when you begin to see.

You begin by noticing who stays. The one who answers, who is there, who wants you without games, and whom you used to find rather uninteresting. Instead of discarding him for not giving you the tension, you look again. And you ask yourself whether what you called boredom was not, after all, peace.

You begin to notice your reflex to run. Whenever someone comes close for real, you feel the alarm, the urge to push away, to test, to sabotage. And instead of obeying the alarm, you notice it. "Here it is again. The fear of presence." And you stay, instead of running.

You begin to distrust intensity, instead of trusting it. When a man lights you up with his absence, with his games, with his pulling back, you no longer say "this is passion." You say "this is my pattern recognizing familiar ground." And you think twice.

And you begin to notice who has already been there. Sometimes present love is not a new man. It's someone who was always nearby, to whom you never gave a chance, because he seemed too right, too calm, too available to have the shine you were looking for in what ran away.

Look again at those ones. At the ones who stayed, who didn't play games, who wanted you without making you suffer, and whom you filed away as friends, or as too little, or as no chemistry. Maybe one of them was, all this time, the love you said you were looking for and refused because he didn't make you run.

To see is also to see again. To look again, with new eyes, at what you discarded with old eyes. Because sometimes the love you were looking for wasn't at a distance. It was at your side, waiting, longer than you imagine.

To see is not to stop feeling. It's to stop mistaking what you feel.

The intensity that absence gives you is real. But it isn't love. It's your wound being touched, the child reliving the pursuit of origin. Real love has another taste. Calmer. Less urgent. Safer.

And you're going to have to relearn that taste. Because your palate was educated in lack, and at first present love is going to taste to you like too little, the way food without salt tastes like too little to someone who always ate it too salty.

I want to give you a practical way to begin to see, without making this into a technique.

The next time a man lights you up, stop for an instant and ask yourself: what, exactly, is lighting me up? Is it him, who he is, how he treats me? Or is it the uncertainty, the distance, the not knowing whether he wants me?

If what lights you up is the uncertainty, notice: you're not feeling love for him. You're feeling your wound being touched. The fire is not his. It's yours, and old.

And the next time a man leaves you lukewarm, ask yourself the same question in reverse. What is it that leaves me lukewarm? Is it him, truly, who has nothing to do with me? Or is it only the fact that he's available, that there's no uncertainty, that there's nothing to conquer?

If your lukewarmness comes only from his availability, distrust it. It may be hiding from you a real love, in front of your eyes, that you're discarding because it doesn't hurt.

It's not to force yourself to anything. It's only to begin asking. Because while you don't ask, the pattern chooses for you. And each of these questions is an instant in which you regain, yourself, the choice.

It's not that present love is bland. It's that your palate needs to be re-educated. And it's re-educated by staying. By bearing the strangeness of presence until it stops being strange.

I won't give you a method. It would be one more way of turning this into a task, and love is not a task.

And there's a concrete sign to guide you, because your head is going to want to complicate things. Ask yourself, before a man: with him, do I feel calmer or more anxious?

Your pattern taught you to choose the one who leaves you anxious, and to call it passion. Invert it. Next time, notice the one who leaves you calm, safe, at peace with yourself. And distrust, instead of giving yourself, the one who leaves you trembling.

Anxiety is not the measure of love. Throughout your whole life it was the measure of danger. And you always ran toward it, thinking you were running toward love.

There's one more thing that changes when you begin to see. You stop taking the absence of others as a judgment about yourself.

When a man isn't available, that speaks about him, about his phase, about his capacity to love. It doesn't speak about your worth. You don't have to make him available, deserve him, conquer him. You only have to see that he isn't there, and not make it your mission.

Letting go of the mission of making available the one who isn't there is perhaps the greatest relief of this crossing. Because that mission was impossible, and you'd been carrying it for a lifetime.

I want to show you what appears when you begin to see the love that is here.

At first, almost nothing. The present men keep seeming uninteresting to you, and the absent ones keep shining. Don't expect the attraction to change just because you understood. Understanding opens the door, but it's time, and the practice of staying, that change what you feel.

Then, little by little, you begin to notice things you didn't see before. The way a present man listens to you. How he remembers what you said. How he is, simply there, without leaving you in doubt.

Before, this was invisible to you, or worse, it was boring. Now it begins to have a value it didn't have. You begin to see the beauty of constancy, which you always mistook for a lack of passion.

And one day you notice that you rested. That you were with someone without the usual anxiety, without your eyes on the phone, without the tension of waiting. And that this rest, far from being boredom, was something you'd never felt in love. Peace.

Peace, for someone who only knew the storm, is a learning. It doesn't come for free. It has to be tolerated, at first, the way you tolerate silence after a lifetime of noise. But it comes. And when it comes, you don't trade it back for the storm.

But I leave you the beginning, in one sentence. The love that is here is also love. Perhaps the only one you ever wanted, and the only one you never knew how to recognize, because it arrived without running.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, desire the present man and let go of the one who runs. But it changes enough for you to stop feeling crazy, or condemned, or without a solution in love.

Now you know that you light up with absence and grow cold with presence, and that this isn't your type, it's a pattern.

You know that you learned to love where love was unavailable, and that you carry that map into every relationship.

You know that presence frightens you because the familiar, for you, is lack.

You know who you are when you don't chase, and that the emptiness that appears there is not the truth about you, it's only the child who learned to fill the silence with the desire for those who don't come.

You know how to distinguish the pattern from the wound that asks for help, and to seek it if that's the case.

And you know that love does not have to be conquered. That it can stay. That staying is not less.

I want to leave you an image.

You spent your life running after trains that were leaving. Loving the empty platform, the smoke moving away, the longing for the one who left. And there was, all this time, someone sitting beside you on the station bench, looking at you, waiting to be seen.

Seeing the love that is here is turning your head. Stopping looking at the trains that are leaving and noticing the one who stayed.

It's not to stop feeling. It's to stop mistaking longing for love.

I want you to take a distinction with you, because it protects everything else.

Seeing the love that is here is not forcing yourself to love whom you don't love. It's not staying with the first present man just because he's present, nor convincing yourself of an attraction that doesn't exist.

There are present men who, in fact, are not for you. And it's right to let them go on their way. Discerning is not saying yes to everything that stays.

What seeing changes is something else. It's no longer discarding, from the start, everyone who stays, just for staying. It's giving a real chance to presence, instead of refusing it by reflex.

Before, your choice was made before it began. The present was discarded, the absent was chased. There was no true choice, there was a pattern.

Seeing gives you back the choice. You look at the present without the reflex to run, and the absent without the reflex to chase, and from there you choose, for real, calmly, whom you want to love.

It may even keep on not being this one or that one. But for the first time it's a choice of yours, and not your wound choosing for you.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this book. I can recognize the love that is present.

Seeing was the first step. But there's a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by staying, when everything in you wants to run. By bearing presence until it stops frightening. And, if there's someone at your side, by learning, together, to remain.

That is the work of SyncHim. The book names. SyncHim crosses over. Each in her own time.

There's one last thing I want you to know, about time.

This pattern doesn't undo itself in a day, nor with a book. It was engraved over years, in the body, before words. It's going to need time, and perhaps hands to accompany you, in order to loosen.

You're going to relapse. You're going to light up again with an absent one, run from a present one, mistake the storm for love. It's not failure. It's the old map still being there, while the new one is drawn.

What changes, even in the relapses, is that now you see. You no longer chase in the dark. You know what you're doing while you do it. And seeing, even in the middle of the pattern, is the beginning of being able, one day, to choose not to follow it.

Be patient with yourself. Treat your wound as you would treat a much-loved friend who learned to love in a difficult place. Not with haste, not with judgment. With tenderness, and with all the time it takes.

And before you close these pages, I ask you one thing only. A hard one, for you, because it's so calm.

The next time someone present comes close, and you feel the boredom, the alarm, the urge to run, don't run right away. Stay a little longer. Notice the fear. And ask yourself, honestly, whether what you call a lack of chemistry is not, after all, the fright of being, for the first time, truly loved.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
