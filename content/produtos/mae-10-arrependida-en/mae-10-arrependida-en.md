# The mother who regrets

**For the mother who loves her children and, in secret, wonders whether she would do it again. Without judgment.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This is a book without any moral judgment, about one of the most silenced truths there is: that of loving a child deeply and, at the same time, lamenting what motherhood cost. I want to tell you right here, with all clarity, the very thing this whole book exists to separate: regretting the experience of motherhood was never the same as rejecting your child, and your child never was, not for an instant, a mistake. If what you feel comes accompanied by a deep sadness that doesn't pass, by despair, or by thoughts of harming yourself or harming someone, this asks for clinical support, with urgency and without shame. And if there is any risk to the well-being of a child, seek help. You are not alone, and there are people who can help you.*

---

## 1. The sentence you will never say out loud

There is a sentence that lives in the deepest and most closed-off place in you, and that you will never, never say out loud. A sentence that frightens you just to think it, that you hide from everyone, and sometimes from yourself. The sentence is more or less this: if I knew what I know today, perhaps I wouldn't have chosen this path. Perhaps I wouldn't become a mother again.

Perhaps it appears in concrete moments you recognize. At the end of a day that drained you to the bone, when there is finally silence and you wonder where your life went. On seeing a woman without children living with a freedom that tightens your chest in a way that shames you. In the middle of yet another endless task, when the thought crosses, fast and forbidden, "and what if I hadn't followed this path." On looking back, at the woman you were before, and feeling that she disappeared and that you don't know whether you'd want her back or whether you no longer even remember who she was. These moments do not make you a bad mother. They are what happens when a total experience, that no one described to you with honesty before you lived it, reveals itself to be harder, costlier, more ambivalent than the promise that was sold to you.

And when this thought appears, in the dark, on a night of exhaustion, or on some ordinary day for no apparent reason, right behind it comes a wave of horror. What kind of mother thinks this? What monster looks at her own life and regrets having children? And you push the thought down, you hide it, perhaps you weep with shame for having had it, and you swear to yourself that never, ever, will anyone be able to know that it passed through your head. Because there are, in our culture, few taboos as absolute as this one: a mother can confess almost anything, except regretting being a mother.

And I want to tell you why this taboo is so violent, because understanding it helps to free you from the weight of it. Our culture built motherhood as the supreme sacred thing of a woman, her highest fulfillment, the proof of her love and of her worth. To touch that, to admit that it can cost, that it can fail to fulfill, that it can be lamented, is to touch one of the most protected pillars of all, and that is why the reaction is so fierce. But notice: that pillar was not built for the good of real mothers, it was built to serve an idea, a social order, an expectation. And real mothers, with their complex and ambivalent experiences, are crushed by that pillar every time it demands of them a love without any shadow. Your difficulty in saying your truth is not a defect of yours. It is the weight of a cultural pillar crushing a human truth that doesn't fit in it. And to begin to question that pillar, to realize that it is not the truth but a construction, is to begin to come out from under its weight.

I want to tell you, before any other thing, and I want you to read this very slowly: you love your children. The fact that you have this thought does not mean that you don't love them, does not mean that you are a monster, does not mean that something in you is rotten. And you are not alone. Far more mothers than you would ever imagine know this sentence from within, this thought that appears and horrifies, this forbidden question of whether they would turn back. Almost none of them speak of it, because the taboo is crushing, and so each one carries it alone, convinced she is the only one, the aberration, the bad mother among all the good ones.

And there is a terrible confusion beneath all of this, a confusion that makes this thought so unbearable, and that this whole book exists to undo. It is the confusion between regretting the experience of motherhood and regretting having your child. To society, and perhaps to you, these two things are the same. To say "I regret motherhood" sounds, to everyone's ears, like "I didn't want my child," like "my child was a mistake." But they are not the same thing. They are completely different things, and to separate them is the most important and most liberating task of this book.

I want you to know that I write this with a very serious awareness of the care this subject demands, because it can be misread in ways that hurt. I do not write to normalize indifference to a child, nor to validate ceasing to care, nor to suggest that any child is unwanted. I write for the woman who loves, who cares, who is present, and who carries in secret, on top of all that, a lament for the experience that tortures her and isolates her. It is to that woman this book speaks, and it is her truth, so silenced and so alone, that it wants to welcome. If you ever feel that your lament is overflowing into the way you treat your child, that is the moment to seek support, and the book will tell you so clearly. But for the overwhelming majority of mothers who recognize themselves in these pages, the problem was never the care, which exists and is faithful, it was the silent torture of a lament that no one let them name.

Because it is possible, and more than possible, it is common, to love a child deeply and, at the same time, to lament deeply what motherhood took away, cost, changed. To love the person and lament the experience. To want the child and not want to repeat everything that being a mother demanded of you. These two things fit in the same heart, at the same time, without canceling each other, and we will see, throughout these pages, how. But it begins with this, with this truth that can ease a shame of years: your child never was the mistake. And being able to think about what motherhood cost you does not make you a bad mother. It makes you an honest woman, looking at a hard truth, and that, far from being a crime, is an act of courage.

## 2. Regret is not a lack of love

I want to say early, right in this second chapter, the distinction that holds this whole book, so that you read it not with fear, but with relief. The distinction is this: regret of motherhood is not a lack of love for the child. They are two things that live on different planes, and that our culture, out of laziness and out of taboo, joined into one.

Think of what you are in fact evaluating when the thought appears. You are not evaluating your child, the person he is, his worth, your love for him. You are evaluating an experience: the experience of being a mother, with everything it demanded of you, took from you, changed in you. They are different things. A person can love a child immensely and, at the same time, recognize that the experience of motherhood cost her more than she expected, that it took from her things she missed, that it transformed her in ways she didn't choose. To lament the experience is not to reject the person who was born of it. I can love who you are and lament what it cost me to have you. The two things are true, and they fit together.

There is a simple proof that regret and love are not the same thing, and it is in your own life: you go on caring. Despite the lament, you get up every day and you care, protect, give, are there. If the regret were a lack of love, you wouldn't care, or you would care badly, with neglect, with indifference. But you care, with devotion, often better than mothers who never felt a lament in their lives. Your regret of the experience coexists with a faithful care for your child, and that care is love in action, every day, regardless of what you feel about the path that brought you here. The proof of your love is not in the absence of lament. It is in the presence of care. And the care, that, you did not fail.

We take this for granted in every other area of life, and only in motherhood does it seem impossible to us. Someone can love deeply the work they chose and, at the same time, lament what that work cost them in health, in time, in other possible lives. Someone can love the country to which they emigrated and lament everything they left behind. To love a choice and lament its price is not a contradiction, it is the normal complexity of any human life. Only in motherhood do we demand a love so absolute that it admits no lament, as if loving a child obliged one to love every minute, every loss, every renunciation it brought. It doesn't oblige that. One can love the child and lament the cost, as in everything else.

I want also to dismantle an idea that may be torturing you: the idea that, if you regret, then you were destined not to be a mother, or that you failed at a vocation that others have naturally. There is no obligatory natural vocation for motherhood that you failed. The idea that every woman is born with a full maternal instinct, that being a mother necessarily fulfills every woman, is a myth, not a biological truth. There are women for whom motherhood is the greatest fulfillment, and there are women for whom it is a far more ambivalent experience, and both are normal, both exist, both love their children. You did not fail a vocation. You live an experience that, for you, in your circumstances, with your story, was harder and costlier than they promised you, and that says nothing about your worth nor about a supposed nature you should have and don't.

And I want to be careful not to fall into the opposite, which would be easy and would be false: I am not going to tell you "it doesn't matter, everything's fine." I am not here to absolve you with a pat on the back, because that would be to treat your pain as small, and it is not. What you feel is deep, it is serious, and it deserves to be looked at with seriousness, not dispatched with a cheap consolation. To lament what motherhood cost you is a real pain, that has real roots, and that deserves room to exist. What I offer you is not an "it doesn't matter." It is something more respectful: the separation between two things that had been confused, so that you can feel what you feel without accusing yourself of a crime you didn't commit.

Notice the relief this separation can bring. While regret and lack of love were glued together, every time you felt the lament, it came accompanied by the terrible accusation that you didn't love your child, and that was unbearable, and so you pushed everything down, and in pushing it, you suffered even more. When you separate the two things, you can finally look at the lament without the horror, you can recognize that motherhood cost you without that calling your love into question. The love stays intact, on one side. The lament can be looked at, on the other. And to look at it, instead of hiding it, is the beginning of being able to make peace with it.

## 3. What motherhood took from you

To look at the truth with honesty, we have to name what motherhood took from you, and I want to do it without guilt, without the "but it was worth it" glued to every sentence, because it is precisely that automatic "but" that keeps you from feeling the real loss. For a few minutes, we are going to let the loss exist, whole, without hurrying it toward consolation.

I want to say, first, why naming the loss is so important and is not, as guilt tells you, an ingratitude. It is that a loss that is not recognized does not disappear, it only transforms. It transforms into guilt, into diffuse resentment, into nameless sadness, into irritation one doesn't understand, into a malaise that hovers without apparent cause. The denied loss doesn't vanish, it goes on seeping underneath, and it contaminates everything, including the relationship with the children, because the resentment over something that can't even be named ends up, without meaning to, splashing onto those who are nearest. To name the loss, to let it exist in the light, is precisely what keeps it from seeping in and poisoning the rest. To recognize what motherhood took from you is not to disrespect what it gave you. It is what allows the loss to be mourned and integrated, instead of staying to rot in silence and to contaminate what you have that is good.

Motherhood takes time, and it takes it in a total way. The time that was yours, the hours that belonged to you, the room to think, to create, to simply be, with no one needing you. It takes sleep, for years, in a way that changes the body and the mind. It takes freedom: the freedom to decide alone, to change your mind, to leave, to be spontaneous, to live without having to calculate the impact of everything on the life of another person who depends entirely on you. It takes body: your body changed, and perhaps you don't recognize it, and perhaps you lament what it was. It takes, very often, profession, or at least part of it, ambitions postponed or abandoned, a career that could have been. It takes spontaneity, creativity, energy, whole parts of the woman you were before.

And there is a subtler loss, that few mothers name: the loss of yourself as a priority. Before you were a mother, even if with difficulty, you were, at least sometimes, the first person in your own life. Afterward, you stopped being it, perhaps forever, or at least for so many years that you forgot what it was like. Your needs went to last, your desires to never, your rest to later. And there is a mourning in this, in ceasing to be, to yourself, someone who counts, who matters, whose needs have weight. It is not selfishness to miss this. It is human. Every person needs to count, at least a little, in their own life, and motherhood, above all as society demands it, very often asks the woman to disappear entirely behind the mother. To lament that disappearance of yourself is not to not love your children. It is to feel the lack, legitimate, of a woman who also deserved to exist.

And there is a forbidden question that lives beneath all of this, and that perhaps you never dared to ask: who would I have been? Who would the woman have been who didn't have children, or who had them later, or in another way, or who chose another path? What life would I have lived, what person would I have come to be, what parts of me, that were left undeveloped, would have flowered? This question is painful, and the culture forbids it, because it seems to wish for a world without your children. But that is not what it does. It only recognizes that every chosen path closes others, and that it is human, and legitimate, to look from time to time at the life one didn't live, without that meaning one doesn't want the life one lives.

There is a frequent root of this regret, and touching it can ease the guilt, as long as it is done with care. Many women became mothers without having, truly, chosen to be. They became mothers out of duty, out of social pressure, because it was what was expected, because it was the path that seemed to have no alternative, because of a pregnancy that happened, because they were given no room to ask themselves whether this was really what they wanted. When motherhood was not a full choice, free, consented to from within, it is more likely that the lament will appear afterward, not because the woman is defective, but because she lived something she didn't entirely choose. This doesn't explain all regret, and there are women who freely chose to be mothers and who lament all the same, simply because the real experience was different from the imagined one. But if you recognize that you entered motherhood without having truly chosen it, perhaps you'll understand your lament better, and perhaps you'll judge yourself less harshly, because there is a difference between regretting a choice of yours and lamenting a path in which, at bottom, you feel you were never truly consulted.

And I want to ask you one thing in this chapter, which is not to compare yourself with the mothers who seem to have endured everything better, who seem to have given everything without lamenting anything, who seem to float in motherhood with a joy you don't feel. First, because you don't know what they feel inside, and many who seem to float carry, in secret, exactly the same as you. And second, because the comparison only serves to add to your pain a guilt you don't deserve, the guilt of not being like the others, when the truth is that you don't know how the others are inside, you only know how they show themselves on the outside. Your experience is yours, with your circumstances, your story, your resources, and it is valid without having to compete with anyone's.

Let, then, the loss exist, without the immediate "but it was worth it." Not because it wasn't worth it, perhaps it was, and we will speak of that. But because a loss that is not let to be felt is not cured, it stays, buried, rotting in guilt. To recognize what motherhood took from you is not ingratitude nor is it ceasing to love. It is honesty, and it is the only path by which that loss, finally felt and named, can one day stop hurting in secret and become merely a part, recognized and integrated, of your story.

## 4. The shadow is the silence

We arrive at the heart of this book, and I want to ask you to breathe, because we are going to touch exactly the place where it hurts most. And what I'm going to say may surprise you: your greatest problem, probably, is not the regret you feel. It is the silence it forces on you.

Notice this distinction, because it changes everything. Regret, in itself, is a feeling, and feelings, by themselves, do no harm to anyone. You can feel what you feel, and that, kept within a heart that goes on loving and caring, does not wound your child nor make you a bad mother. What corrodes you, what isolates you, what turns a human feeling into a wound that rots, is the silence. It is not being able to say. It is having to hide from everyone, and from yourself, a part of your truth, afraid that, if it came out, it would make you a monster in the eyes of the world and in your own eyes. The shadow of this book is not the regret. It is the silence about the regret.

Notice that the silence creates a perverse cycle. You keep quiet because you are ashamed. The shame, never being said nor contradicted by any response of acceptance, grows and confirms itself, becoming the certainty that you really are monstrous. That certainty makes you keep quiet even more, with even more fear of being found out. And so, the silence feeds the shame, and the shame feeds the silence, in a cycle that tightens over you, year after year, without ever having the chance to be broken, because the one thing that would break it, to say it and be welcomed, is precisely what the silence keeps you from doing. To break this cycle requires an act of apparently small but enormous courage: to say, once, to a safe person, the truth you kept quiet. And to discover, on the other side, not the horror the shame predicted, but understanding. It is that meeting, between your truth said and a response of acceptance, that begins to undo the cycle, and that is why it matters so much.

And society has an enormous responsibility in this silence, which it is fair to name. Because it is society that makes this truth unsayable, that reacts with horror to any mother who dares to admit a lament, that confuses regret with a lack of love and condemns whoever feels it. It is that social reaction that teaches you to keep quiet, that makes you believe your truth is monstrous, that isolates you. The silence is not born of you, it is born of the fear of judgment, and that fear was taught to you by a culture that can't bear to hear that motherhood, for some women, costs more than it gives in certain dimensions. Your shame is not yours, it was imposed on you, and part of what this book wants is to give it back to whoever imposed it.

Notice what the silence makes you pay, beyond the loneliness. The silence forces you into a permanent vigilance, into a constant effort to hide, to control what you say, not to let anything show through, and that tires you in a deep way, because to live with a part of you permanently hidden is to live divided. And the silence robs you also of the possibility of discovering that you are not alone, because while you keep quiet, you don't know that the woman beside you, who seems so serene in her motherhood, perhaps carries exactly the same truth, kept quiet. The silence keeps each mother on her island, convinced she is the only one, when in truth there is a whole archipelago of women feeling the same, all silent, all thinking themselves alone. To break the silence, even if only with one person, even if only with a professional, is to discover that the island was after all part of a much larger and much more inhabited land than the shame let you see.

I want you to hear this clearly, because it is the turning point of everything: to be able to say "this cost me, I lament what motherhood took from me" is not a confession of monstrousness. It is an act of honesty and of courage. And it is not incompatible with loving, indeed, only someone who loves suffers so much with this conflict, because it is precisely the love that makes the lament so hard to admit. A woman who didn't love her children would not torment herself with this, she would leave them and that would be that. Your torment is, itself, the proof of your love, and of your decency, because it is the sign that you go on choosing your children every day, even when the experience costs you.

I want to distinguish, with care, this admitting the truth to yourself from a different thing: I am not saying that you have to announce it to the world, nor even to your children. There is a difference between breaking the inner silence, letting yourself recognize what you feel, and to whom, when, and whether you say it out loud. To break the silence that most corrodes you begins within you, in ceasing to lie to yourself, in letting the truth exist in your own consciousness without the horror. Afterward, to whom you say it out loud is a choice of yours, a careful one: to a professional, to a trusted friend, to whoever knows how to listen. You don't have to dump it on whoever would judge you nor on whoever it might wound. The essential, what frees, is first to stop hiding it from yourself, and then to find, in your own time, a safe place where you can say it and be welcomed.

To let this truth exist, instead of hiding it, is the beginning of the liberation. Not the truth that you regret, but the truth that you have the right to feel it and to say it, without that condemning you. When the shadow of the silence is broken, when the truth is said in a safe place and from the other side comes not the promised horror but understanding, something changes. The regret, said and welcomed, loses the power to isolate you, and can finally be looked at, understood, integrated, instead of being a secret that corrodes you from within. It is not by feeling less that the liberation begins. It is by being able to say.

## 5. To love and to lament at the same time

We are going now to deepen the coexistence that is the heart of this book, because it is in it that your peace dwells: the possibility of loving and lamenting at the same time, without one thing canceling the other.

We are used to thinking that feelings have to be coherent, pure, of a single direction. That if you love, you can only feel good things, and that any lament contaminates and disproves the love. But the human heart does not work like that. The human heart is perfectly capable of feeling opposite things at the same time, and does so constantly. You can love a person and, at a given moment, feel anger at them. You can be happy about a change and, at the same time, in mourning for what it ends. You can love your life and lament things about it. The coexistence of opposite feelings is not a failure nor a contradiction, it is the normal nature of human emotional experience, in everything, and also in motherhood.

So you can, yes, love your child deeply and lament deeply what you lost in order to have him. The two things are not at war, although your guilt presents them to you as enemies. The love doesn't grow smaller because of the lament, and the lament proves nothing against the love. They live in you at the same time, like so many other pairs of opposite feelings, and to see this, to accept that it is so, frees you of an impossible demand: the demand to have pure feelings, perfect, of a single direction, in one of the most complex and most total experiences there is.

This capacity to hold opposite feelings even has a name in psychology, ambivalence, and far from being a sign of illness or of immaturity, it is a sign of emotional maturity. Small children don't tolerate ambivalence, for them people are either all good or all bad, and feelings are of a single direction. To grow is, in part, to learn to tolerate that one can love and hate the same person, want and not want the same thing, be happy and sad at the same time. Motherhood is perhaps the most ambivalent terrain of all, because it joins the most intense love with the most total demands, and that is why it is absolutely natural that it generates contradictory feelings. To feel ambivalence in relation to motherhood is not a failure of your love. It is the sign that you are emotionally mature enough to bear the complex truth of one of the most complex experiences there is.

And there is a specific thing this acceptance frees you from having to choose, and I want to name it: the choice between being a good mother and being truthful. While you believed that loving obliged you not to lament, you lived divided between two impossible loyalties: either you were a good mother, and then you had to pretend you didn't feel what you felt, or you were truthful, and then you felt yourself a bad mother for feeling. It was a prison with no way out. The coexistence takes you out of that prison: you can be both things, a good mother and truthful, because being truthful about what motherhood cost you doesn't make you a worse mother, and loving your child doesn't oblige you to lie about what you feel. You stop having to choose between your heart and your honesty.

There is a concrete liberation in this coexistence, and I want you to see it: it frees your child from an impossible responsibility. When the love has to be absolute, without any lament, the child becomes, without knowing it, responsible for justifying the whole sacrifice, for being worth the whole renunciation, for compensating for everything it cost. It is a crushing weight for someone to carry, the weight of having to make worthwhile the entire life of another person. When you accept that you can love and lament at the same time, you take that weight off your child's shoulders. He no longer has to justify your sacrifice, no longer has to be perfect to compensate for what you cost, he can simply be loved for who he is, and live his own life. Your honesty about what motherhood cost you, far from wounding your child, can in truth free him from a debt he never should have had to pay.

And I want, once again, to guard you from an easy flight, because it always appears here: the flight to "but deep down it was worth it, deep down I would do everything the same again." Perhaps that is true for you, and if it is, good. But don't hurry to say it just to make the discomfort stop, because that hurry to reach the "it was worth it" is, very often, one more way of not feeling the loss. You can stay, for some time, without that reassuring conclusion. You can love and lament at the same time, without needing to quickly resolve the tension into an "it was worth it" that spares you from feeling. The whole truth, with its tension, is harder to bear than a comforting conclusion, but it is the only one that frees, because it is the only one that is real.

## 6. Regret is not depression

This is the safety chapter of this book, and I want you to read it with all your attention, because it makes a distinction that can be decisive. Until now we have spoken of regret as a human feeling, lucid, that one can feel without it being an illness. But there is a boundary that matters greatly to recognize, and it is not in the sentence you think, it is in the way you are functioning and suffering.

On one side of the boundary, there is the woman who thinks, with lucidity, "if I knew, perhaps I wouldn't choose this path again," and who, apart from that thought, is functioning, living, caring for her children, finding pleasure in things, having good days and bad like everyone. That woman is not ill. She is having an honest thought, perhaps a painful one, about a difficult experience, and that thought, by itself, is not a symptom of anything, it is only lucidity, and the book does not pathologize her nor should it frighten her. She can feel what she feels and go on with her life.

On the other side of the boundary, there is another thing, more serious, and the boundary is not the sentence, it is the functioning. There is the woman who thinks herself regretful but who, in truth, is sinking: who feels pleasure in nothing, who lives in a sadness or a despair that doesn't pass, who can't function, who isolates herself, who lost hope, who has difficulty caring for herself or her children, who has thoughts that it would be better not to be here, or thoughts of harming herself, or of harming someone. For that woman, what is happening may not be regret, in the lucid sense, it may be depression, it may be deep burnout, it may be a health condition that needs treatment. And the "regret" she feels may be, in part, the way depression paints her life gray, making her read everything, including motherhood, through the dark lens of the illness.

I want to add something important, because it protects your child and protects you. To feel the lament is not the same as to act badly upon it. One thing is your inner truth, what you feel, what you think, and that, kept within a care that goes on, wounds no one. Another thing would be to let that lament turn into neglect, into coldness directed at the child, into making him feel that he is unwanted. The book never validates this, and I want to be clear: if you feel that your lament is turning into something that affects your child's well-being, that makes you treat him badly or neglect him, then that is a sign that you need support, with urgency, not to judge you, but to protect you both. To feel is human and wounds no one. What asks for help is when the suffering begins to overflow into the care, and to recognize that in time, and to seek help, is one of the greatest proofs of love you can give.

That is why the distinction matters so much, and that is why I want to be very clear: it is not the sentence "perhaps I wouldn't choose again" that indicates a problem, it is the general state, the functioning, the depth and the persistence of the suffering. If you recognize that it is not only a lucid thought, that there is a sadness that has taken over everything, that you can't function, that you've lost the pleasure and the hope, that there are thoughts that frighten you, then what you're living asks for evaluation and clinical support, and it asks for it now. Depression, including the kind that appears at any phase of motherhood, is real, is common, is treatable, and is not your fault nor weakness. And if there are thoughts of harming yourself or harming someone, that is an emergency, and you deserve to seek help immediately, without shame.

I want still to name a dangerous trap, in the opposite direction: that of a woman in depression using "I only regret it" so as not to seek help, thinking that what she feels is merely a lucid truth about motherhood, when in truth she is ill. Depression is a liar, it convinces whoever has it that life is really like that, without color, without meaning, and it disguises itself easily as "regret," as "lucidity," as "I see things as they are." If there is in you a voice that says "it's not worth seeking help, because I'm not ill, I really just regret it," distrust it, above all if it comes accompanied by the signs I spoke of, the sadness that doesn't pass, the loss of pleasure, the despair. That voice may be the depression itself protecting itself, keeping you from seeking what would help you. When in doubt, seek. A professional will know how to distinguish, and if it's only lucidity, good, and if it's depression, you will have taken the step that can change everything.

To seek help, here, is not to admit that you are a bad mother nor that you truly regret your children. It is to recognize that you are suffering in a way that needs care, and that you deserve that care, as much as any person who suffers. Mental health professionals know how to distinguish lucid lament from depression, know how to help with both, and are not there to judge your motherhood. If there is one thing I want you to take from this chapter, it is the capacity to ask yourself, with honesty, not "what sentence do I think," but "how am I functioning and suffering," and to seek help if the answer is that you are sinking. That question can save you, and the courage to ask it is, itself, an act of love for yourself and for your children.

## 7. To distinguish the love from the loss

If you've come this far, and if what you're living is the lucid lament and not the clinical suffering that asks for support, then there is a path, and it has a name, which is the verb of this book: to distinguish. Almost all your suffering is born of different things that fused into one, and the whole work is to separate them, with care, one by one. I'm going to show you the contours, in broad strokes, not the step by step, which is done slowly and sometimes with company.

Before I show you what distinguishing separates, I want to give you the image that best explains it. Imagine two things that were glued together so long that they seem one, like two damp sheets of paper that dried together. To separate them, you don't pull hard, you tear everything. You separate them slowly, with care, with patience, peeling a little at a time. So it is to distinguish the love for your child from the lament for the experience: they were glued together so long, in your head and in the culture's, that they seem the same thing, and to separate them is not done from one day to the next nor by force. It is done slowly, with care, peeling little by little, until one day you can look at the love without the lament staining it, and look at the lament without it threatening the love. It is a work of patience and of tenderness, not of force, and that is why it needs time, and sometimes hands to help you peel apart what has been stuck together for so long.

To distinguish is to separate the child from the experience. Your child, the person he is, your love for him, on one side. The experience of motherhood, with everything it cost you, on the other. They are different things, and when you separate them, you can lament the second without that touching the first. To distinguish is to separate the love from the choice: you can love entirely who your child is and, at the same time, recognize that, if you could go back with what you know today, perhaps you would choose differently. To love a reality doesn't oblige you to wish you had chosen it, nor to want to repeat it. And to distinguish is to separate motherhood from your whole identity: you are a mother, but you are far more than a mother, and motherhood, however central, is not the totality of who you are.

To distinguish is also to separate the gratitude from the renunciation, and to let both exist. You can be grateful for things motherhood brought you, the love of your child, certain joys, parts of you that it revealed, and at the same time lament what it made you renounce, the freedom, the life you didn't live, the parts of you that were left undeveloped. The gratitude doesn't cancel the renunciation, and the renunciation doesn't cancel the gratitude. We are always wanting to make a final accounting, a balance, to decide whether on the whole it was positive or negative, but life doesn't let itself be reduced to that balance. You can carry, at the same time, the gratitude for what you received and the mourning for what you lost, without needing to add them up and see which wins. To distinguish is to stop trying to make the balance, and to let each part exist, whole, in its place.

And there is a sentence that holds the heart of all this, and that is, perhaps, the most important thing of this whole book: you never were the mistake. This sentence is for your child, of course, because what this book most protects is the truth that he never was a mistake, never was unwanted, never was the problem. But the sentence is also, and perhaps above all, for you. Because beneath the shame of regret there is, very often, a mother who feels that she herself is the mistake, that she failed, that she is defective for feeling what she feels. And I want to tell you, with all clarity: you too never were the mistake. Neither you, nor your child. What there was, was a difficult experience, lived by a real woman, who loves and who laments, and nothing in that is a mistake to be atoned for.

And I want you to see that distinguishing is not an analytical coldness, it is, in truth, a gesture of love for yourself and for your child. When you confuse everything into a single mass, in which the lament seems to stain the love and the love seems to oblige you to silence the lament, you live in a painful chaos that serves no one. To distinguish is to put order in that chaos, with tenderness, giving each feeling its name and its place, so that you can love with more clarity and lament with less guilt. It is like tidying a house where everything was mixed up and heavy: after each thing is in its place, the house breathes, and you breathe in it. It is not to make the heart cold. It is to make the heart habitable, organizing a disorder that was suffocating you and that, tidied, stops hurting in the same way.

To distinguish is, at bottom, to return each thing to its place, so that they stop contaminating one another. The love for the child to the place of love, intact, safe. The lament for the experience to the place of lament, where it can be felt without wounding the love. The gratitude for what motherhood brought on one side, the renunciation for what it cost on the other, both true. When each thing is in its place, you stop living in that confused and painful mixture in which loving seemed to oblige you not to lament, and lamenting seemed to disprove the love. Everything breathes better when each truth has its space, and none is obliged to silence the other so that you can bear yourself.

There is a last relief that distinguishing brings you, and that touches your whole identity. When you separate motherhood from your identity, you realize that being a mother, however central, is not all that you are. You are a mother, and you are also a woman with a story prior to motherhood, with desires of your own, with an inner life that doesn't fit entirely in the role of mother, with parts of you that exist independently of your children. The culture tends to swallow the whole woman inside the mother, as if, on becoming a mother, everything you were before should disappear or submit itself. But it is not so, or it doesn't have to be. You can be entirely a mother and go on being entirely yourself, and to recover the awareness that you are more than a mother doesn't make you a worse mother, it makes you a whole person who, among other things, is a mother. And a whole woman has more to give, including to her children, than a woman who reduced herself to a function.

And when you distinguish, a thing happens: the love, freed from the weight of guilt and of confusion, can usually flow better. Many mothers discover that, on ceasing to torture themselves for feeling what they feel, on letting the lament exist in peace in its place, the love for the children becomes, in truth, freer, more present, less contaminated by the guilt that before hovered over everything. It is not that distinguishing makes you love more, it is that it takes from the love the weight that was smothering it. And a love that no longer has to carry the guilt of existing beside a lament is a love that can, finally, be lived with more lightness and more truth.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change the past, doesn't undo what motherhood cost you. But it changes the way you carry your truth, and that, in a truth as heavy and as silenced as this one, changes almost everything.

Now you know that the sentence you will never say out loud does not make you a monster, and that you are not alone in thinking it. You know that regret is not a lack of love, and that you can love the person and lament the experience. You know how to name what motherhood took from you, without the automatic "but it was worth it" that keeps you from feeling. You know that your true shadow is not the regret, it is the silence. You know that one can love and lament at the same time, without choosing between being a good mother and being truthful. You know how to distinguish regret from depression, by the functioning and not by the sentence, and to seek help if needed. And you know that the verb of all this is to distinguish, to return each thing to its place.

And you know, perhaps, that what you carried as your most shameful secret is, after all, one of the most human and most shared truths of motherhood, even though few dare to say it. That there are more mothers like you than you would ever imagine, each one alone on her island, each one thinking herself the only one, the aberration, when in truth what they live is a common experience that the culture denies the right to exist. If this book does one single thing, may it be to free you from the conviction that you are a monster, and to give you back to the company of all the women who love and who also lamented, and who go on, every day, loving and caring, despite everything it cost them.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can love you without having loved everything that motherhood asked of me.

And you know, above all, the sentence this book exists to give you, and that is yours and your child's at the same time: you never were the mistake.

To understand was the first step. But there is a second, and the second is not done only by reading. And here I want to be honest about what it is, for you.

If what you're living is the lucid lament, with the shame and the silence that accompany it, I created a guided crossing, **FreeMe**, that can accompany you in setting down the shame, in distinguishing the love from the loss, and in making peace with a truth you carried alone for too long. You'll find it at [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

But if what you're living is clinical suffering, if you recognize yourself in the signs I spoke of in the sixth chapter, then the second step is not a crossing of self-awareness, it is professional support, and I tell you this with all clarity, because it would be irresponsible not to. In that case, the most important and most urgent path is to seek a health professional, and FreeMe, if it comes, will come afterward, never in place of the support you need now.

And I want to tell you a word about your child, and about the fear that he might one day discover what you feel. Perhaps you live in terror that he reads it in you, or comes to know, that there was regret, and that it destroys him. I want you to think about this: what wounds a child is not the complex truth of a mother who loves and who also lamented. What wounds is coldness, rejection, indifference, feeling unwanted. And none of that is what you live, because you love and you care. A mother who made peace with her own truth, who loves her child and integrated the lament without it contaminating the care, transmits love to the child, not rejection. It is the mother who hides, who tortures herself, who lives divided and tense, who risks transmitting a diffuse malaise that the child feels without understanding. To make peace with your truth is not a danger for your child. It is, on the contrary, what allows you to be with him in a whole way, present, without the shadow of a secret weighing over the relationship.

I want to leave you with one last thing, and I want you to take it with you above any other. The fact that you managed to look at this truth, that you let it exist, that you read this book to the end instead of fleeing from it, is already an act of enormous courage, and of enormous love. Because it was out of love for your children, and out of respect for yourself, that you were willing to look at what is hardest to look at. A mother who does this is not a bad mother. She is a profoundly honest and profoundly human mother, who loves truly, with everything that truly means, including the part that costs. And you, and your children, never, never were the mistake.

And I want to tell you something about the future, because regret always looks backward, at the choice you wouldn't make again, and there is a liberation in turning your gaze also forward. You can't change the past, can't undo the choice, can't recover the life you didn't live. But you can, from today on, live the life you have with more truth and less guilt, you can recover pieces of yourself that were left unlived, you can give yourself, at last, some room, some freedom, some life that is yours. Regret, when it stops being a torture and becomes an integrated truth, can even become an engine: the engine of taking better care of yourself from here on, of not losing yourself any further, of recovering the woman that motherhood buried. Not to stop being a mother, but to go back to being, also, yourself. And that, unlike the past, is a story you can still write.

With all my tenderness, and without any judgment,
*Vivianne*
