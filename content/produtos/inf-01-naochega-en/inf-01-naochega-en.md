# The woman who never arrives

**For the woman who reaches everything and feels little. Because the goal you chase was never, after all, what you were looking for.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. I want to tell you something right here: the woman this book speaks about does not do too little, she does too much, and running without ever stopping has a limit that the body and the mind eventually charge for. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion that no longer lifts with rest, persistent anxiety, an inability to stop, or a loss of pleasure in everything, this may be more than tiredness, and it deserves the care of a professional. Seeking help is not weakness, nor is it ceasing to be capable. It is the most lucid thing you can do for yourself.*

---

## 1. The finish line that keeps moving away

You know this scene, because you have lived it so many times that you stopped noticing it. You reach the thing you fought for. The promotion, the diploma, the house, the project, the number, the recognition. The thing that, for months, or years, you told yourself was going to change everything, that was going to be the moment when you could finally breathe, rest, feel that you had arrived. And the day comes. And you reach it. And there is, at most, an instant of relief, short as a sigh. And then, almost without a break, your mind is already sketching the next goal.

The finish line, which seemed so definitive from a distance, dissolves the moment you step on it. And ahead of it, always, another appears. Higher, farther, harder. As if the goal you reached were, after all, merely the first step of a staircase with no top. You run your whole life toward a place called "there," and every time you get to "there," "there" has moved, and it is in front of you again, calling you, promising that this time, this time for sure, you will feel what you came to feel.

And you don't feel it. That is the secret you carry and almost never say. That you reach, and you feel little. That from the outside your life looks like a path of achievements, and from the inside there is a strange sense of emptiness, of insufficiency, of something always missing that you cannot name. You have a full calendar and an empty chest. You receive praise and you still feel you are not enough. You change jobs, cities, projects, and the discomfort packs its bag and goes with you, intact, to the new place.

And notice one thing, because it reveals the whole mechanism: the relief you feel on arriving always lasts less than you expected. You imagined, during the race, that arriving would bring a long happiness, a deserved rest, a sense of arrival that would stay. And what comes is an instant, sometimes not even that, and then the restlessness returns, faster each time. The more you achieve, the shorter the relief becomes, until it almost disappears, until you reach great things and barely feel anything, only the immediate urge to set off toward the next one. This is not ingratitude on your part, nor an inability to enjoy. It is the sign that you are drinking from a source that does not quench your thirst, and your body knows it, which is why the relief doesn't take hold, why it pushes you straight to the next thing, searching, again, in the wrong place.

And there is a particular tiredness in this, different from the tiredness of someone who works hard and then rests. It is the tiredness of someone who can never arrive, who runs with no real finish line, because every goal reached reveals itself as false the instant it is touched. It is a tiredness of the soul, not only of the body, the tiredness of someone who strains immensely and does not feel that she is truly moving forward anywhere, because the place she wants to reach lies at the end of no race. And nobody around you understands it, because from the outside they see a successful woman, fulfilled, with everything, and they do not see the emptiness she lives with, nor the exhaustion of a race that has no possible arrival. That loneliness, the loneliness of having everything in other people's eyes and feeling that you have nothing inside, is one of the most silent there is.

I want you to know, right from the start, that this book is not about productivity. I did not come to teach you to reach things faster, to plan better, to optimize anything. You already do all of that, and you do it well, probably better than most of the people around you. Your problem was never a lack of capacity. I came to look at something else, much deeper and almost never spoken about: at the reason why, doing so much, reaching so much, you always arrive and never arrive. At the reason why the finish line moves away every time you touch it.

And I will tell you the direction at once, so you do not read this with anxiety: the problem is not your goal, nor the next one, nor the way you chase it. The problem is that the goal you chase, whatever it may be, was never, deep down, what you have been looking for. You are chasing, in the future, in the form of an achievement, a thing you failed to receive much earlier, and that no achievement, however great, can give you. That is why you arrive and it is not enough. Because you are looking in the wrong place for something that is not, and never was, at the end of any race.

And perhaps you recognize your own race in concrete scenes, the kind you live without noticing. It is finishing a project and, instead of celebrating, feeling a strange emptiness, almost a sadness, and throwing yourself straight at the next one so as not to feel it. It is looking back at everything you have already done, and instead of pride feeling a "yes, but," an immediate dismissal, as if nothing you achieved truly counted. It is being unable to be on holiday without working a little, without planning, without feeling guilty for resting. It is measuring your days by what you produced, and going to sleep with a sense of failure whenever a day was merely lived, with no deliverables. It is comparing yourself constantly to those who are further ahead, and the comparison never leaving you in peace, because there is always someone further ahead. They are all symptoms of the same race, and they all point to the same thing: you are trying to fill, with doing, a place that doing does not fill.

I will not ask you, in this book, to stop wanting, to lose your ambition, to settle for less. That is not what this is about, and it would be, in fact, just another way of telling yourself that there is something wrong with you. What I am going to do is help you see what you are truly looking for behind everything you chase. Because on the day you see that, something shifts. The race stops being a flight and can become a choice. And you may discover, perhaps for the first time, that you exist even before you arrive.

## 2. It is not laziness, it is the opposite

There is something that needs to be said early, because you have probably already accused yourself of the opposite: your problem is not laziness, it is not a lack of discipline, it is not procrastination. It is the exact opposite of that. Your problem is excess. You do too much.

We live surrounded by a culture that assumes people's suffering around their goals comes from not doing enough: you don't try hard enough, you don't organize yourself, you don't have discipline, you don't want it enough. And there are people for whom that is true. But you are not that person, and so all the advice you receive lands beside the point, and it even makes you feel worse, because you already do everything they prescribe, and more. You get up early, you deliver, you exceed yourself, you do more than is asked of you, and you still feel it is not enough. The problem, in your case, was never doing too little. It was being unable to stop doing.

Look at your days. They are full to the last hour. You find it hard to sit still without feeling you are wasting time, falling behind, failing. Rest, for you, is not rest, it is a guilty pause between two efforts, lived with the clock running inside you. You have a list that never ends, and for each thing you cross off, you add two. And if, by chance, there is a moment of emptiness, a gap with no task, you feel a restlessness that pushes you at once to fill it, to do, to produce, to move forward, because sitting still stirs in you a discomfort you can barely bear.

And here is something important: you have too much talent and too little clarity. Capacity, you have to spare. What you lack is not the strength to run, it is knowing where to, and why, and for whom. You run with an enormous force in a direction you never truly chose, and so all that force, instead of carrying you to a place that is yours, carries you deeper and deeper into a race you don't quite know whose it is. It is a silent tragedy, this one, of a woman full of talent spending it chasing a thing that, deep down, she suspects she doesn't even want.

And there is a clear sign that your problem is excess and not lack: what is hard for you is not starting, it is stopping. People who suffer from procrastination stall at the start, they delay, they avoid. Not you. You set off easily, you dive in, you give everything, and what is almost impossible for you is the opposite: stopping, letting go, saying "enough for today," leaving something unfinished, resting without guilt. If you observe where your difficulty lies, you see that it is all on the side of the brake, not the accelerator. And this matters, because all the advice the world gives assumes that people's problem is in the accelerator, in getting started, in doing, and what you lack is exactly the opposite, a brake you never learned to use, because nobody taught you that stopping is also needed, that resting is also work, that existing without producing is also living.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because the tendency will be that, to turn this realization too into one more failing of yours. It is not a failing. The excess was not born from a character defect. It was born from a real, old need, that learned to express itself in this way, running, doing, reaching, because that is how you learned that you had worth, that you were seen, that you deserved your place. Doing too much was, for a long time, your way of surviving, of belonging, of being enough in someone's eyes. It is no laziness at all. It is the opposite: it is a devotion taken to the extreme, in the service of something we have not yet seen, but that we are going to see.

There is also a trap that makes all of this harder to see: the world applauds you for it. It rewards you, promotes you, admires you, holds you up as an example, precisely for the excess that is consuming you. You receive recognition for running, and so the race disguises itself as a virtue, and stopping seems, in the light of that applause, a failure, a giving up, a betrayal of what everyone expects of you. It is very hard to question something for which we are constantly rewarded. But the applause from outside does not cure the hunger inside, and you know it, you feel it on every night when, despite all the praise of the day, you lie down with the same sense of emptiness. The recognition you receive for what you do never reaches the place that needed to be reached, because that place does not ask you to do, it asks you to be seen for who you are, and that, applause for your performance cannot give.

For now, it is enough for you to recognize this, and to stop beating yourself up over a laziness you do not have. The tiredness you feel is not a lack of effort. It is the exhaustion of someone who has been running too long without ever being able to arrive. And the way forward, contrary to what everyone tells you, is not through running better. It is through discovering what sets you running.

## 3. What you are looking for is not the goal

We come to the heart of this book, and to the idea that may change the way you see yourself. Read it slowly. The goal you chase was never, in itself, what you wanted. The goal is merely a vehicle. Behind it, and without your seeing it clearly, is something else, a need that is not about achievements, and it is that, and not the achievement, that you are truly looking for.

Look at what you chase, and ask yourself what you hope, deep down, that it will give you. Because it is almost never the thing itself. You don't want success, you want the recognition you imagine it brings. You don't want money, you want the security, the feeling that you are finally safe. You don't want productivity, you want to feel that you have worth, that you justify your space in the world. You don't want approval, you want belonging, to feel that you finally are part of something. You don't want status, you want to feel, at last, enough. Do you see what is happening? In each case, the goal is only the packaging. What you want is inside, and it is a deep human need, that has nothing to do with accomplishments.

And here is the knot, the thing that explains why you arrive and it is not enough: you are trying to obtain in the future a thing you failed to receive in the past. The recognition you did not have when you were small, the security you did not feel, the sense of being enough that nobody gave you, the belonging you lacked. And those things, because they belong to the past and to an inner need, are not found at the end of any future race. However much you reach, however high you climb, the achievement cannot go back in time to give you what was missing then. That is why with each arrival the emptiness returns: because you conquered the wrong thing for a hunger that came from somewhere else.

And there is a simple proof that the goal was never what you were looking for: if it were, reaching it would have satisfied you. When we truly want a thing and we obtain it, we feel satisfaction, fullness, the desire is fulfilled and rests. You reach things and you do not rest, the desire is not fulfilled, it leaps at once to something else. Now, a desire that is not satisfied when it is met is proof that it was not the true desire, that beneath it there was another, hidden, that the achievement did not touch. Your chronic dissatisfaction, far from being a defect of yours or an ingratitude, is one of the most valuable pieces of information you have: it is your own being telling you, time after time, that you are looking in the wrong place. If you learn to listen to it instead of silencing it with one more race, it will lead you, slowly, to the true hunger.

Imagine someone who is thirsty eating more and more, convinced that the next meal will finally satisfy them. They eat, and eat, and remain thirsty, because thirst is not quenched with food, however much they eat. You have a thirst, old, real, legitimate, for recognition, for security, for belonging, for feeling enough. And you learned to try to quench it with achievements, which are food, not water. So you achieve and you remain thirsty, and your response, for lack of another, is to achieve still more, to eat still more, when what you needed was a water that achievements do not contain.

In the way I have learned to look at these things, there is a word for what is happening with you: substitution. You placed one thing in the place of another. You put achievement in the place of recognition, money in the place of security, status in the place of belonging. And you did it without knowing, very early, because the true thing was not available and the substitute was, or seemed to be. A child who does not receive the recognition she needs, but who discovers that she receives applause when she gets good grades, learns to substitute the first with the second, and grows up chasing grades, prizes, results, in the hope that one day the applause will turn into true recognition. It does not turn into it, because they are different things. But the substitution settles so deep that the person spends a lifetime chasing the substitute, convinced she is chasing the real thing.

And the most subtle part of substitution is that it works just enough to keep you trapped, but never enough to satisfy you. Each achievement gives you a crumb of what you are looking for, an instant in which you almost feel the recognition, the security, the belonging, and that crumb is enough to convince you that you are on the right path, that a little more, one more goal, and this time you will get the whole meal. But the meal never comes, because the food you chase does not contain the nutrient you are hungry for. And so substitution keeps you running forever, always with the promise almost fulfilled, always with the satisfaction postponed to the next one, in a race that could last your whole life without ever giving you what you came for.

I do not tell you this to diminish what you have achieved, which is real and is yours and has merit. I tell you to free you from an impossible race. Because as long as you believe that the next goal will finally give you what you are looking for, you will keep running forever, more and more tired, further and further from yourself, after a promise that no goal can keep. But the moment you see that what you are looking for is not in the goal, that it is in a need of yours that has not yet been seen nor cared for, another possibility opens. The possibility of stopping running after the wrong thing. And the possibility of beginning to look, at last, at the true hunger.

## 4. Who am I when I stop

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you not to flee from it quickly, even if it makes you dizzy, because it is the question that changes everything.

Who am I when I am not running?

Stay with it a moment. Notice what it stirs.

For many women like you, this question opens a hole, and for a reason worth understanding carefully. It is that, over the years, without deciding it, you went on building your entire identity on top of movement. You are the one who does, the one who manages, the one who delivers, the one who is always moving forward. When people ask who you are, you answer with what you do, with what you have achieved, with what you are building. Your worth, your sense of existing, of mattering, of having a place, became glued to movement, to achievement, to the next objective. And so stopping is not, for you, simply resting. It is something that threatens your very existence, as if, in stopping running, you ceased to exist.

Notice where this gluing of what you do to who you are comes from, because seeing it born helps to loosen it. Almost always, it was learned early. You were, perhaps, the child who received attention and love when she brought results, and who went unnoticed when she simply was. Or the one who realized that, in a difficult home, being useful, being good, being the one who caused no problems and brought pride, was the way to secure her place. You learned, without deciding it, that your worth was not in your existing, it was in your producing, your measuring up, your achieving, and that equation engraved itself so deep that it became invisible, the very way you see yourself. It is not a truth about you, it is an old lesson, and old lessons, however early they were learned, can be revised. You were not born believing you are only worth something when you produce. It was taught to you, and what was taught can, slowly, be unlearned.

That is why you cannot stop, even exhausted. It is not just habit, nor is it just anxiety, although it is that too. It is that, deep down, stopping confronts you with a terrifying question: if I am not doing anything, if I am not reaching anything, if I am not moving forward, then who am I? Do I have any worth when I am not producing? Do I exist, when I am not achieving? And that question is so frightening that you prefer to keep running, doing, filling, rather than risk facing it. The race has become, without your noticing, a flight. A flight from the emptiness that appears when you stop.

And it is that emptiness that is your true shadow. Not the lack of goals, not the lack of capacity, but the emptiness that opens in the silence, when there is nothing to do and you are, finally, alone with yourself. It is from that emptiness that you flee your whole life, filling every hour, every gap, every silence, with one more task, one more objective, one more race. Because while you run, you do not have to feel it. And the price of that flight is that you never stop, and never arrive, and never rest, and never find yourself, because finding yourself would require precisely stopping and descending into that emptiness you have spent your life avoiding.

Notice how this identity built on movement makes you strangely fragile, despite all your strength. Because if you are what you do, then each failure, each project that does not go well, each goal that slips away, is not merely a setback, it is a threat to your very worth, to your very existence. That is why you are so afraid of failing, that is why mistakes cost you so much, that is why you cannot bear not being excellent: because, in your equation, ceasing to succeed is ceasing to have worth, and ceasing to have worth is, in some way, ceasing to have the right to exist. It is a terrible place to live, always one failure away from no longer being worth anything, and it explains the permanent tension you live with, the impossibility of relaxing, the subtle panic whenever something does not go as it should. A person whose worth did not depend on achievements could fail and remain whole. You do not give yourself that right, because you built your entire worth on something you have to prove, time after time, without end.

Notice how this flight from the emptiness robs you, without your seeing it, of the very things you say you want most. You cannot be truly present with the people you love, because a part of you is always on the next task, the next objective, the clock. You cannot enjoy what you have, because enjoying requires stopping, and stopping opens the emptiness. You cannot even celebrate your own victories, because celebrating is a rest, and you do not pause long enough to do it. The race that seemed to serve your life is, in truth, eating it from within, taking the present from you, the only thing that is actually lived, in exchange for a future that never comes. And the most painful part is that you know this, you feel the moments passing without living them, and even so you cannot stop, because stopping hurts more, in the immediate, than continuing. That is the prison: you prefer the exhausted race to the emptiness it hides, and so you keep postponing, indefinitely, the meeting with yourself.

I want to tell you something about that emptiness, because it changes the way you look at it. The emptiness you feel when you stop is not proof that you are worth nothing without your achievements. It is the sign of an old need that was never met, and that only appears, and can only finally be seen, when you stop smothering it with the noise of movement. The emptiness is not your enemy. It is your messenger. It is trying to show you what you truly lack, the thing no race ever gave you, and that can only be received from the moment you stop running long enough to hear it. People flee the emptiness their whole life, when what it asks, deep down, is only to be, at last, heard.

## 5. When running is a symptom

I have to stop this book for a moment, because there is something I need to tell you with all seriousness, and that is too important to stay hidden among the rest. The woman this book speaks about does too much, and doing too much, for too long, has a real cost for the body and the mind. There is a border between running a great deal and being ill, and it is vital to know how to recognize it, because on the other side of it we are no longer talking about the same thing.

On one side of the border is what we have been speaking about: you run a lot, you reach a lot, you feel an emptiness, a dissatisfaction, a thirst that achievements do not quench. This is painful, and it has a way forward, the way of this book. On the other side of the border is something else, more serious, that is not just an unsatisfied race, it is an exhaustion that asks for care, and that has clinical names: burnout, anxiety, depression. And I want to name the signs, plainly, because knowing them can be decisive.

Pay attention, and take it seriously, if the exhaustion you feel no longer lifts with rest, if you wake up tired however much you sleep, if the weekend or the holidays no longer restore you. If there is an anxiety that accompanies you in the background, that tightens your chest, that lets you neither stop nor sleep. If you have lost pleasure in things, even in the ones you reach, even in the ones that used to give you joy, and everything has become gray, mechanical, empty of meaning. If you feel you are running on automatic, disconnected from yourself, as if you were living your life from the outside. If, beneath each new goal, a muffled voice has settled in that says "however much I arrive, it is never enough," a disbelief that not even the next objective can lift. If your body, the one that endured years of an inhuman pace, has begun to fail, to fall ill, to refuse to keep running. None of this speaks of weakness on your part, nor of a capacity you have lacked. They are signs that you have crossed a human limit, and that what you need is not to run better, it is care.

And here I tell you, with all firmness and all tenderness, something that perhaps your identity as a capable woman does not want to hear: this is not resolved with more effort. It is not resolved by enduring more, by being stronger, by gritting your teeth and continuing. Exhaustion is, precisely, the result of having endured beyond what was humanly bearable, and more effort only deepens it. What this asks for is the opposite of the race: it asks that you stop, and that you seek support, a doctor, a psychologist, someone who can help you climb out of the pit. It is not an exaggeration, it is not drama, it is not weakness. It is the most lucid and most urgent care you can have for yourself.

And I want to name a trap specific to the capable woman, because it is dangerous: you are, precisely, the person who endures the most, and therefore the one who asks for help latest. You got used to solving everything alone, to not failing, to being the strong one, and admitting that you are sinking seems to you a contradiction with everything you are. So you hide it, you continue, you smile, you deliver, even when inside there is no longer any ground. And the exhaustion of someone who is very capable is treacherous, because the capacity disguises it: you keep functioning at a high level even while already ill, and that deceives everyone, including you, until the day when the body or the mind simply stop, without warning. Do not wait for that day. Your capacity to endure is not a reason to endure more, it is precisely what makes it more urgent that you learn to stop before the limit, instead of only seeing it after you have already crossed it.

And I want to be very clear about why I change tone here, in this chapter, and become more direct. In nearly all of the book I accompany you in seeing, slowly, your race and the hunger beneath it. But there is a point where accompanying without directing would be irresponsible, and this is it. If I told you, in the face of signs of clinical exhaustion, that it is enough to understand your substitution for everything to get better, I would be failing you, keeping you away from the help you need. The understanding of this book is precious, but it does not treat an established burnout, it does not treat a depression, it does not treat a clinical anxiety. That is treated with professional support, and saying so clearly is the most honest way of caring for you. So, if the signs I described are yours, let this be the chapter you remember, the one that leads you to seek help. The others can wait for your calm reading. This one cannot.

If you recognize yourself in these signs, I ask you not to turn this page as you turn so many others, in a hurry. Stop. Your capacity, your talent, your achievements, are not worth more than your health, than your life, than your right to exist without destroying yourself. And seeking help when you are exhausted is not admitting defeat. It is the most intelligent and most courageous way of protecting yourself, and of being able, one day, to live again in a way that does not cost you yourself. You deserve that care. Not someday, when you arrive. Now.

## 6. What you failed to receive, and it was not in the future

If what you are looking for is not in the goal, but in an old need, then the way forward passes through something you have avoided your whole life: looking at that need head on, and naming it. Not to sink into the past, but to stop chasing it, disguised, in the future.

Go back to that list from the third chapter, and this time go deeper. What is it that you are, in truth, trying to obtain, behind everything you chase? For many women, it is recognition, because they grew up feeling that they were only seen when they stood out, when they brought good grades, good results, reasons for pride, and that their worth, at rest, with no deliverables, was neither noticed nor loved. For others, it is security, because they grew up on unstable ground, and learned that only much effort, much achievement, much accumulation, could protect them from a danger they felt early. For others, it is belonging, the feeling of being part of something, because somewhere, at some time, they felt left out, and achievement became the ticket in. For others still, it is simply to feel enough, because an old voice told them, with words or without them, that they were not enough as they were.

Notice that all these needs have one thing in common: they belong to the past, and to a child. The child you were, who needed to be seen, to be safe, to belong, to be enough, and who, for one reason or another, did not receive that in the measure she needed. And that child continues, inside you, with the same hunger, and it was she who learned the strategy that still governs you today: if I do, if I achieve, if I am the best, then finally I will be seen, safe, enough, belonging. The race you live today is the strategy of an old child still trying to get what she lacked. And it is moving, and it is heartbreaking, because she is trying with everything she has, and she is looking in the one place where it cannot be found: in the future, and through things.

And here enters a part that comes from the way I have learned to read people's stories, and that may surprise you: very often, the race you live did not even begin with you. There are dreams you carry that were not born in you. Ambitions you inherited, expectations placed on your shoulders before you were old enough to choose them, scripts of success that came already written in your family, spoken or unspoken. Perhaps you run after a recognition your mother never had and that you, without knowing, are trying to win for her. Perhaps you chase a security that was lacking for those who came before you, and that became a silent mission passed from generation to generation. Perhaps you are living out a life that was dreamed for you by others, and that you took on as your own without ever asking yourself whether you wanted it. It is worth asking the question, from time to time, in the middle of the race: whose is this goal, after all? Who decided that this was success? Whose is this voice that tells me it is still not enough?

Because there is an enormous difference between running after what is yours and running after what you inherited, and the second can never satisfy you, for a simple reason: even if you reach it, it was not your hunger you were feeding. You conquer someone else's dream and you remain empty, because your dream, the true one, went unlived while you chased the borrowed one. Distinguishing what is yours from what you inherited, separating your voice from the voices that decided for you what counted, is one of the most liberating steps of this crossing, because it gives you back the possibility of, for the first time, choosing. Not out of loyalty to those who came before, not out of compensation for an old lack, not out of fear, but out of truth. And a goal chosen out of truth, that one, however much it costs, does not leave you with the emptiness of the others, because it is, finally, yours.

Because here is the difficult and liberating truth: what you failed to receive in the past is not obtained in the future. No future achievement can go back and give the child you were the recognition, the security, the belonging she lacked. That path is closed, and that is why running down it is running forever without arriving. And I want you to notice that this is not a sentence of helplessness, it is the opposite, it is where your power begins. As long as you believed the solution was in the future, in a next achievement, you were handing your peace over to a thing that never came, and so you never had peace. The moment you see that what you are looking for is not out ahead, but here inside, in a need you can finally care for, your peace stops depending on an endless race and starts depending on something within your reach: the relationship you have with yourself. This is not bad news, it is the best of all, because the race you did not control, it controlled you, and the relationship with yourself, that one, you can begin to change today. You leave the position of hostage to a goal that flees and you enter the position of someone who can, at last, give herself what she needs.

But there is another way, that goes neither to the future nor to the past, it goes to the present, to you, now. The recognition, the security, the sense of being enough, you can begin to give them to yourself, today, to that child who still waits inside. Not through one more achievement, but through a new relationship with yourself, in which you finally see yourself, hold yourself, recognize yourself as enough, without having to prove it first.

And I want to tell you something about that old child who lives in you, because it changes the way you treat her. She does not need you to keep running for her. What she needs is for you to finally see her, to stop long enough to notice that she is there, with her old hunger, and to begin to give her yourself what she has spent her whole life asking of others, of the world, of the future. When a childhood need was not met, it does not disappear with age, it waits, and there are two ways of dealing with it: continuing to try to have someone or some achievement satisfy it out there, which never works, or beginning to satisfy it from within, with a new relationship with yourself. Recognizing yourself. Giving yourself the security that you will not abandon yourself. Belonging, first, to yourself. It is not that others stop mattering, they matter. But the hunger that sets you running is not theirs to feed, it is yours, and the water it asks for is closer than any goal: it is in the way you begin to treat yourself.

Naming the real need is the first step, and it is already enormous, because it takes the hunger out of the dark, where it governed you without your seeing it, and brings it into the light, where you can finally care for it. The moment you know that what you are looking for is recognition, and not the promotion; security, and not the money; belonging, and not the status, the race loses its blind power over you. You may continue, perhaps, to want the promotion, the money, the place, but no longer as salvation, no longer as the thing that will finally complete you. And that difference changes everything, because a goal that no longer has to save you is a goal you can, at last, choose freely, or let go of, without your existence depending on it.

## 7. Resting without ceasing to exist

If your emptiness is a messenger, and your race a flight, then the verb of this crossing, the one that most frightens you and that you most need, is to rest. And I want to present it with care, because I know that for you the word sounds almost like a threat, and that is not what this is about.

Resting, for you, looks like giving up. It looks like falling behind, like ceasing to be who you are, like risking ceasing to exist. And that is why, even exhausted, you cannot, because a part of you believes that, if you stop, you disappear. So I need to tell you, with all clarity: resting is not ceasing to exist. It is exactly the opposite. It is discovering that you exist even when you are not producing, that your worth does not depend on your movement, that you are not your achievements, you are the person who exists beneath them, and who goes on existing, whole, when they stop. Resting is not the end of you. It is the meeting with yourself, the one the race did not allow to happen.

There is an enormous difference between stopping running and disappearing, and you have confused the two your whole life. Disappearing would be ceasing to have desires, to have direction, to have will, becoming nobody. That is not it. Resting is stopping running in order to flee, and being able, from the stillness, to choose where you want to go, and why. It is keeping your energy, your talent, your will to live and to do, but putting them in the service of a life that is yours, chosen by you, and not of a flight from an emptiness that, after all, only asked to be seen. Whoever rests does not cease to exist. She begins, perhaps for the first time, to truly exist, instead of merely functioning.

I want to show you, without making this a recipe, what the movement that frees you looks like, because it is the opposite of the race. The race says: do more, reach more, get farther. The movement that heals says something else, in six steps. First, to see: what am I, in truth, looking for, behind this goal? Then, to differentiate: what is mine and what did I inherit, whose is this voice, who decided that this was success. Then, to recognize: what I am looking for is not in the objective, it is in an inner need I have not yet seen. Then, to give back: the expectations that are not mine, the borrowed ambitions, the family's dreams, the comparisons, the invisible scripts that set me running. Then, to choose: for the first time, not out of loyalty, not out of compensation, not out of fear, but out of truth. And finally, to occupy: my own path, the one I perhaps never got around to walking because I was too busy running other people's path. It is not a step by step that you do on a page. It is a crossing. But this is the direction, and it is the opposite of doing more.

And there is a rest deeper than that of the body, which is the one you most lack: the rest of no longer having to prove anything to deserve to exist. Imagine what it would be like to set down, for one moment even, the permanent demand to justify your space in the world, to validate yourself with one more deliverable, to prove your worth with one more achievement. Imagine existing, simply, without having to deserve it first. That is the rest your soul thirsts for, even more than your body does. The rest of someone who is no longer in debt to her own existence, who allows herself to be worth something, without conditions, without proof, without arriving. That rest is not conquered. It is received, the moment you stop postponing it to after the next goal.

And I want to warn you of a trap that catches almost every woman like you when they discover this: turning the rest itself, the self-knowledge itself, into one more goal to be reached with excellence. You begin to "work on your rest," to "optimize your presence," to make of stopping one more project to be carried out well, with objectives and progress and self-assessment, and suddenly you are running after rest with the same hunger with which you ran after everything else. It is the race disguising itself as the cure. Notice this when it happens, and laugh a little at yourself, with tenderness, because it is your old pattern trying to survive dressed up as something new. Resting is not one more thing to do well. It is, precisely, the place where there is nothing to do well, where you can be imperfect, unproductive, with no goal, and be, even so, whole. Rest is not conquered with effort. It is allowed.

I am not going to give you, in this book, a step by step for resting, because that would be one more task, one more goal, one more thing to do well, and we would be feeding precisely what we came to cure. The deep path, that one, is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because unlearning a race of a whole lifetime is not something done alone on a page. But the principle fits in one sentence, and it is this: you can exist before you arrive. You do not have to wait for the next achievement to have the right to stop, to breathe, to be. That right is already yours, now, exactly as you are, before any arrival. And perhaps the most courageous thing you can do, you who were always courageous at running, is, for the first time, to stop.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not change everything at once, and it does not stop the race overnight. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling crazy, defective, or ungrateful for reaching so much and feeling so little.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it sums up everything. For years you ran like someone running on a treadmill, with all your strength, sweating, exhausting yourself, convinced you were moving forward to a place. And the treadmill goes nowhere: however fast you run, you stay in the same spot, only more tired. The goal moved at the same speed as you, always in front, always at the same distance, because it was the treadmill itself creating the illusion of arrival. Stepping off the treadmill is not giving up walking. It is stopping running to stay in the same spot, and being able, at last, to choose a real path, on firm ground, that truly takes you somewhere, to your own side. And you discover, as you step off, that you did not need to run so much, that most of that effort was taking you nowhere, and that existing, simply existing, on solid ground, without the treadmill forcing you, is a rest you had forgotten was possible.

Now you know that the finish line always moves away, and why. You know that your problem is not laziness, it is excess, and that you have too much talent and too little clarity. You know that what you are looking for is not the goal, it is an old need that the goal can never give. You know, and this was the hardest, who you are when you stop, and that the emptiness that appears there is not an enemy, it is a messenger. You know how to distinguish running a lot from falling ill, and to seek help if that is the case. And you know that resting is not ceasing to exist, it is beginning, at last, to exist.

And you know, perhaps, that the question this book opens is bigger than your race. Who am I when I am not running is not just a question about goals, it is the question about who you are, deep down, beneath everything you do. And the answer, which you will discover slowly, is not conquered, it is discovered: you are someone, whole, with worth, before any deed, beneath any achievement, a person who exists and who deserves to exist without having to prove it. You came to this question through exhaustion, through emptiness, through the race that was never enough, and that, which seemed your failure, is after all your door. Few people get to ask it, because few stop long enough. You, in reading this, have already stopped enough to hear it. And that, which counts on no resume, is perhaps the most important thing you have done in a long time.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can exist before I arrive.

Seeing what you chase was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not done by reading, it is done by undoing, slowly, a race of a whole lifetime, and by choosing, for the first time, a path that is truly yours.

It was for that crossing that I created **Infonte**.

Infonte is not a productivity method, it is not one more way for you to reach things better. It is a space for you to ask the question the race never let you ask: what is it that I am, in truth, looking for? And to distinguish, calmly and from within, what is yours from what you inherited, your voice from the voices that decided for you what counted as success, the path you chose from the path you merely set off running. Not so you stop wanting, but so you start wanting from within yourself, and not from an old hunger that governed you in the dark.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There, the crossing is to set down what one carries, the weight that is not ours. Here, in Infonte, the crossing is to distinguish what one seeks, the goal that is not ours. One sets down, the other chooses. But deep down it is the same journey: the return to what is truly yours. The mother returns to her place in the family; you, here, return to your place in your own life, the one you abandoned to go running after a place that, after all, was never yours. It is the same cure, seen from another angle: to see, to differentiate, to give back what does not belong to you, and to choose, at last, what is yours.

There, the question "who am I when I stop" ceases to be an abyss and becomes a door. The emptiness ceases to be something to flee from and becomes something to listen to. And rest ceases to look like disappearance and begins to taste like a return, to yourself, to your own life.

If you feel this is the moment, you will find Infonte at [infonte.viviannedossantos.com](https://infonte.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is okay, and there is no further goal here to fulfill. What you have read is already yours, and it stays with you, waiting, without hurry, for the day you decide to stop long enough to hear it.

And I want you to take one last distinction with you, because it protects everything else. Existing before you arrive is not ceasing to arrive. I am not asking you to abandon your objectives, to lose your ambition, to become a person with no direction. I am asking you to invert the order. Today, your existence depends on your arrival: you only feel you have the right to exist after you achieve. I propose the opposite to you: that you exist first, whole, with worth, without conditions, and that from that ground, and not from a hunger for it, you choose what you want to build. You go on reaching, perhaps as much as before, perhaps more. But no longer in order to exist, rather because you exist. No longer to fill an emptiness, rather to express a life that is already yours. It is the same action, the achievement, but with the root swapped: it ceases to be flight and becomes creation. And that one, unlike the other, does not empty you. It fills you.

But before you close these pages, I ask you for just one thing, and it will be, for you, one of the hardest there is, and that is why I ask it of you. Today, stop. Five minutes even, doing nothing, producing nothing, moving forward to nothing. Sit with yourself and notice what appears: the restlessness, the urge to get up, the discomfort, perhaps the emptiness. And, instead of fleeing, stay. Just stay. Because it is in that staying, borne without fleeing, that the race of a whole lifetime begins to undo itself, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that you exist even when you are not arriving anywhere.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
