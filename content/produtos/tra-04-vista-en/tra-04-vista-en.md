# The woman who is afraid of being seen

**For you, who do work that deserves to be seen and hide from it. Because you can be seen without losing yourself, and what you create deserves to reach the world.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of the fear of being seen, and there is a point at which that fear stops being shyness and becomes an anxiety that limits your life. If you recognize in yourself an intense anguish about exposing yourself, panic attacks in the face of visibility, or a fear that prevents you from living the life you wanted, this may be social anxiety, and it deserves the support of a professional. Understanding your fear is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I do, but I hide

Notice a contradiction you perhaps live. You do work that deserves to be seen. You have worth, competence, something to show the world. And yet, you hide.

You don't show yourself. You don't expose yourself. You avoid the stage, the camera, the spotlight, visibility. You do your work in the shadow, and when the moment comes to show it, to show yourself, you pull back, you shrink, you let others, often less capable, occupy the light that could be yours.

And maybe you no longer even remember a time when the light didn't burn you. A time, perhaps in childhood, when you showed your things with pride, when you sang, danced, spoke without fear, when being in view was simply being alive. That time existed, in most people, before something closed it. The child you were was not born afraid of being seen, she learned it. And what was learned can, slowly, be unlearned, because beneath the fear there is, still, that part of you that knew how to show herself without terror, and that waits only for safety to appear again.

Maybe you have something to say, to create, to share, that would be better in the world than hidden in you. But the fear of being seen is greater than the desire to show. And so you keep, you hide, you postpone, and your work, your voice, your worth, go unshown, out of fear of exposing yourself.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only shyness, nor a lack of ease. It is a fear, at times intense, of being seen. Of exposing yourself. Of being under the gaze of others. A fear that tightens, that paralyzes, that makes you prefer to hide rather than risk exposure.

And there is, underneath, a question of legitimacy, and sometimes something deeper. The fear that, if you show yourself, they will judge you, expose you, discover that you are not enough. The fear that being seen is, somehow, dangerous, shameful, or that it takes something from you.

Notice the contradiction this leaves you in, because it is cruel. On one hand, you desire to be seen, recognized, for your work to reach, for your voice to count. That desire is legitimate and it is healthy, we all want, deep down, to be seen at our best. On the other hand, that is exactly what terrifies you. And you live between these two forces, wanting and fearing, drawing closer and pulling back, in a conflict that exhausts and paralyzes you. It is not that you don't want the light. It is that you want it and fear it at the same time, and fear, being older and stronger, almost always wins, leaving the desire unfulfilled and the frustration growing.

This book is not about personal marketing, nor about how to gain confidence on stage. I've come to stand where the light frightens you and to look at what keeps you in the shadow. At the fear of being seen. At the reason why you hide from a visibility your work deserves. And at the possibility of showing yourself without the terror that today paralyzes you, and of letting reach the world what you have, at last, to give.

And I'll leave you the direction right at the entrance, so you can read without the tightness of someone who fears what is coming. You can be seen without losing yourself. Showing your work, your voice, your worth, doesn't take anything from you, doesn't expose you to the danger you fear. And the fear of being seen, which hides you, comes from a wound we are going to look at, and which, if it is intense, also deserves the support of someone who knows how to walk alongside it.

I want you to distinguish, from the start, the woman others see from the woman who hides inside. What is seen, from the outside, is a reserved, discreet woman, who prefers the wings. What she is, inside, is a woman who has much to show and who hides out of fear, not out of preference. There is an enormous difference between choosing discretion because that is how you feel good, and hiding because being seen terrifies you. The first is a temperament, and it is legitimate. The second is a prison, even if dressed as modesty. And it is only the second that this book wants to free you from.

This book hasn't come to tell you that you have to become extroverted, that you have to love stages, that you have to live exposed. It is not about changing who you are. It is about giving you back a choice that fear took from you: that of being able to show your work when you want to, of occupying the light when it is yours, of being seen without a terror forcing you to hide. Whether you are, by nature, more reserved or more expansive, you deserve for that to be a choice of yours, and not an imposition of fear.

Maybe you catch sight of yourself in the small ways you step back from the light.

In having something valuable to show, and in hiding it, postponing it, keeping it, out of fear of exposing yourself, telling yourself that it isn't ready yet when, in truth, what holds you back is fear.

In feeling an intense anguish in the face of the possibility of speaking in public, of showing yourself, of being under the gaze of others, to the point that the body reacts before you think.

In letting others, less capable, occupy the visibility, the recognition, the stage that could be yours, and in feeling, afterward, the frustration of someone who watched without entering.

In preferring anonymity, the shadow, the hidden work, even when it costs you opportunities, recognition, the life you could have, and even when, deep down, you wished to be seen.

Underneath each one, the same shadow waits. The fear of being seen. The hiding from a visibility your worth deserves.

I want to show you how this shows up day to day, in things you perhaps already take for temperament. The possibility arises of presenting your work, of speaking at an event, of showing yourself, and you find a thousand reasons to refuse, to pass it to someone else, to stay in the wings. You have something created, ready, good, and you postpone it, keep it, you say it isn't perfect yet, when the truth is that you are afraid to show it. You see an opportunity that would require exposing yourself, and you let it pass, with a relief mixed with a frustration you don't quite know how to name.

And there is a detail that reveals everything: what you feel in your body just from imagining being under the gaze of others. The tightness in the chest, the heat, the heart racing, the urge to disappear. Even before exposing yourself, your body already reacts as if there were danger, and it is that reaction, more than any reasoning, that makes you pull back. It is not that you don't want to show yourself. Often you want to, you desire it, you dream of it. It is that your body brakes, and fear decides for you.

And there is something nobody sees, beneath your hiding place. People see someone discreet, reserved, who prefers the wings, and perhaps don't even notice what is going on, or think it is just your temperament. They don't see the worth you keep hidden, the voice you stifle, the frustration of someone who has so much to give and locks it away out of fear. They see the shadow you choose, not the light you deny yourself. And you, taken for modest or for reserved, become even more bound to the hiding place, because it even seems that this is how you ought to be.

## 2. Being seen was dangerous, or shameful

Go back. To when being seen became threatening.

Because no one fears visibility like this without reason. Your fear of being seen was born from an experience in which being seen brought something bad: judgment, humiliation, exposure, danger.

Maybe, as a child, you were exposed, ridiculed, humiliated in front of others. Maybe you showed yourself, took a risk, shone, and were mocked, criticized, called into question. And you learned that being seen brings shame, that it is safer to hide, not to stand out, not to expose yourself to judgment.

Maybe you grew up where standing out was frowned upon, where humility was valued to the point that brilliance was punished, where whoever showed herself was accused of thinking too much of herself. And you learned to hide, not to stand out, to stay in the shadow, so as not to be judged for daring to be seen.

Maybe you are a woman in a world that, for a long time, taught women not to expose themselves, to be discreet, not to occupy the stage. And you carry that old message, that visibility is not for you, that showing yourself is improper, risky, shameful. Maybe you saw, at home, women who erased themselves, who served in the wings while others occupied the light, and learned, without words, that this was a woman's place. And even though your reason knows that this is not true, the body learned early, and continues to treat the light as a place that doesn't belong to you.

Or maybe your wound doesn't come from a public humiliation, but from a home where the brilliance of a child was seen as a threat or an affront. Where standing out brought jealousy, or resentment, or the accusation of thinking too much of yourself. Where you learned that being noticed, being praised, shining, took away love instead of bringing it. And so you learned to erase yourself, not to stand out, to hide your best, because showing it seemed to cost you the affection you needed. And today, as an adult, you keep hiding your brilliance, out of an old fear that it will push people away, instead of drawing them closer.

Notice what you learned. That being seen is dangerous, or shameful. That exposure brings judgment. That it is safer to hide. And that learning carved itself in, and it governs you, and it makes you hide from a visibility that, today, might be not only safe, but good for you, for your work, for your life.

Maybe the humiliation was a defining moment, the kind that stays: a time when you exposed yourself, when you showed something of yourself with pride or with enthusiasm, and you were cut down, mocked, ridiculed, in front of others. And the body kept that shame so deeply that, ever since, being seen and being humiliated got stuck together, and the mere possibility of showing yourself makes the body relive, in anticipation, that old shame. You don't, perhaps, consciously remember how much that marked you. But your fear remembers for you, and protects you, by hiding you, from a humiliation that has already passed.

Look at this under a gentle light, without guilt. Your fear of being seen is not weakness, nor a lack of confidence that is solved with a few tips. It is a wound, that of someone who was judged, exposed, or humiliated, on showing herself, and who learned to hide to protect herself. You did not draw back from the light because that is your nature. You are someone who learned that being seen hurt, and who hides so as not to hurt again.

And I want to show you in what place of the body this fear lodges, because it is there that everything is decided. This fear is not only in your head, in what you think. It is in the body, in a reaction that fires before thought. That is why it isn't enough to tell yourself that there is no danger, that nobody will mock you, that your work is good. The fear doesn't obey reason, because it isn't born from it, it is born from a body memory, an old one, that treats visibility as a threat. And a body memory isn't undone with arguments, it is relearned slowly, with new experiences, in which the body discovers that it showed itself and was not humiliated, time after time, until it believes it.

## 3. Hiding costs me the life I could have

Let me bring one thing into the light, the one your hiding keeps in the dark. Hiding protects you from judgment, yes. But it costs you the life you could have.

Notice the price. What isn't shown isn't known, isn't chosen, isn't recognized. By hiding your work, your voice, your worth, you pass by opportunities, recognition, impact, that would be yours if you showed yourself. Your avoided visibility is a closed door to everything that would require being seen.

And it costs the world, too. Because what you have to create, to say, to show, might be needed by someone. Your voice, hidden, is a voice that doesn't reach the one who would need it. Your work, in the shadow, is a work that doesn't touch the one it would touch. By hiding, you deprive not only yourself, you deprive the world of what you had to give.

Think of the people who marked you, who helped you, who inspired you, through what they created, wrote, shared. Imagine if they had hidden, if they had let fear win, if they had kept for themselves what, after all, reached you and did you good. You would have lost that. Well, there are, somewhere, people who would lose what you have to give, if you kept hiding. You are not less than those who marked you, and what you have to offer can mark someone, in the same way, if you dare to let it reach. Hiding is not only a loss of yours, it is a lack you create for those who would need you without even knowing you exist.

And there is a cost in your relationships, too, that you perhaps don't see. Whoever hides never shows herself whole, not even to the close people. There is always a part of you, the one that has worth, that shines, that creates, that you keep covered, out of fear of judgment. And so the people who love you know only a reduced version of you, and you never come to be seen, nor loved, fully. The same armor that protects you from the judgment of strangers also keeps those close at a distance from your light, and there is a particular loneliness in being much loved for a small version of yourself, and never for the whole one.

And there is a cost that accumulates without making a sound, year after year. Each time you refuse to show, you pass it to someone else, you stay in the wings, it seems a small gesture, almost weightless. But those gestures add up, and after many years they form a whole life lived off to the side, beside the light and never inside it. It is not a great renunciation that costs you the possible life, it is a thousand tiny renunciations, each justified in the moment, that nobody sees, not even you, until you look back and realize all that went unshown.

And it costs you growth. Because growing, in what you do, very often demands being seen. Showing, exposing, risking the gaze of others. And by avoiding visibility, you stay stuck at one level, unable to grow beyond what is done in the shadow. Your fear of being seen is a ceiling, and you live beneath it, always short of what you could reach.

And it costs you, above all, the sense of coherence with yourself. Because deep down you know that you have more to give, you know that there is in you a voice, a worth, a light that you are not letting out. And living like this, feeling yourself bigger than what you allow yourself to be, is a particular form of suffering, that of someone who betrays herself every day, not out of malice, but out of fear. There is a mismatch between who you are and who you let yourself show, and that mismatch weighs, even if you don't quite know how to name it, because it is the weight of a life lived below your size.

I want you to see the difference between healthy discretion and your hiding place. Healthy discretion is a choice: sometimes I show myself, sometimes I withdraw, depending on how I feel and on what makes sense. It has both doors. Yours doesn't. Yours only knows how to hide, and showing yourself is not, truly, available, however much you wished for it. And a way of being that can't be set down is not a choice, it is a prison. The work of this crossing is not to take from you the enjoyment of seclusion, if it is yours. It is to unlock the other door, that of being able to show yourself, so that your discretion stops being a cell and becomes an option.

Notice this silent tragedy. There is, in you, something valuable to show. And that worth stays hidden, out of fear. You see others, less capable, occupying the light, having the recognition, living the life that could be yours, not because they are better, but because they don't have your fear of being seen. And you stay in the shadow, with your worth unshown, watching. And the most unjust thing is that the world, very often, rewards not the greatest worth, but the greatest visibility, and so your hiding place doesn't only protect you from being judged, it also leaves you systematically behind those who, with less to give, dare to show.

And it costs you, perhaps, the most painful thing of all: watching others live what could be yours. Seeing them recognized for work that you would do equally or better. Occupying stages, projects, opportunities, not for having more worth, but for not having your fear. And there is a particular pain in this, that of knowing that what separates you from them is not talent, it is terror, and that you could be there, were it not for the fear that binds you to the shadow. That pain, of seeing your possible life being lived by others, is among the hardest to carry in silence.

I want you to see, too, that hiding doesn't spare you, after all, from suffering. You traded the fear of being seen for the frustration of not being. You thought hiding protected you, but the hiding place has its own suffering: that of the life unlived, of the worth unshown, of the frustration of someone who knows she is capable and sees herself stopped. There is, after all, no option without pain. There is the pain of risking being seen, which passes and teaches, and the pain of hiding, which stays and corrodes. And between the two, perhaps it is worth choosing the one that, at least, takes you somewhere.

Feel for a moment how heavy the shadow becomes. The weight of having something to give and hiding it. Of living short of yourself, in the shadow, out of fear of the light. Of watching pass the life you could have, for not daring to be seen. It is a deep frustration, and perhaps you already take it for modesty, or for temperament, when it is, deep down, a wound that robs you of what you could be.

## 4. Who am I if I show myself

I'll set a question here, in plain view, where you can't look away from it. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who would you be if you showed yourself? If you occupied the light, instead of the shadow? If you let the world see your work, your voice, your worth?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Perhaps a terror, perhaps a desire quickly stifled.

For you, this question lights up the old alarm at once. Because showing yourself, exposing yourself, being seen, awakens the old terror of judgment, of humiliation, of exposure. As if being seen were risking what hurt you before: the mockery, the criticism, the shame of having dared to show yourself.

And the shadow that hides here is the fear of exposing yourself and being judged. That, by showing yourself, they will see you, and criticize you, and discover something in you that makes you deserve the shame you fear. The fear is not only of being seen. It is of what you imagine they will see, and of the judgment you imagine will come.

See how this keeps you behind the curtain. You don't show yourself, even having worth, because showing yourself awakens the terror of judgment. You hide, you shrink, you leave the light to others, not because you don't deserve the light, but because you are afraid of what it brings. And you live, this way, short of yourself, in the shadow, bound to a fear that robs you of the life your worth would deserve.

And I want to stop here for a moment, with care. Because if the fear of being seen is very intense, if it causes you an anguish that tightens, panic attacks, an avoidance that seriously limits your life, this may be more than shyness. It may be social anxiety, and that has a name, and it has help. It is not temperament, it is not weakness, it is not something that is solved only by effort. A professional can help you, and seeking one is not an exaggeration, it is taking care of something that deserves to be cared for.

I want to bring to light a truth that makes this fear begin to shrink. Being seen, today, doesn't bring, most of the time, the judgment you fear. People are too busy with themselves to judge you as you imagine. And even if someone judges, that speaks of them, not of you, and it doesn't take your worth. The terror of judgment is, in large part, a projection of what you lived, and not a prediction of what will happen. The world, today, is not the place where you were, one day, exposed.

And I want to distinguish for you something that frees. What frightens you is not, in fact, being seen. It is being judged. Do you notice? If you knew, for certain, that on showing yourself you would be welcomed, recognized, well received, you would have no fear at all of showing yourself. The fear is not of visibility itself, it is of the judgment you imagine it brings. And that judgment, most of the time, is much greater in your imagination than in reality. People don't look at you with the cruel magnifying glass with which you imagine yourself observed. They look distracted, busy with themselves, and most don't even notice, and of those who notice, most don't judge, and of those who judge, the judgment speaks more of them than of you.

Don't answer the question of who you would be if you showed yourself just yet. But leave it open. Because that is the door out of the shadow. On the day your fear of being seen stops governing you, you stop hiding from a light you deserve. And then, only then, can you show, at last, what you have to give.

## 5. When the fear is social anxiety

I have to step out of the shadow of the book and speak plainly here. With seriousness. Because there is a border between the fear of being seen and an anxiety that calls for help, and it is important for you to know which side you are on.

On one side, there is a dread of exposing yourself, a discomfort with visibility, a shyness that shrinks you. It hurts, but it has a path, and the path passes through understanding, and through risking, slowly, showing yourself.

On the other side, there is something more serious. And I want to name it. If the idea of being seen causes you an intense anguish, a panic, physical symptoms, the heart racing, the breath failing, a terror that paralyzes you. If you avoid, at all costs, situations of exposure, and that avoidance seriously limits your life, your work, your relationships. If the fear of the judgment of others dominates you, takes away your sleep, prevents you from living the life you wanted.

This may be social anxiety. And social anxiety is not shyness, it is not temperament, it is not a lack of effort. It is a real condition, that causes real suffering, and that has treatment. There are ways to help someone who lives with social anxiety, and they work, but they pass through professional support, from a psychologist, at times from a doctor, and not through forcing yourself, alone, to face what terrifies you.

And I want to tell you something with firmness and care. If what you feel is this intense anguish, seeking help is not an exaggeration, it is not weakness. It is recognizing that your suffering is real and deserves to be cared for. Social anxiety, treated, can free you from a fear that has been robbing you of life, and give you back the possibility of showing yourself, of living, of being seen without the terror that today accompanies it.

I want to take away a fear that may be holding you back from seeking help. Maybe you think that asking for support for a fear of exposing yourself is, in itself, an exposure, an admission of fragility that costs you to make. It is understandable, but notice: the relationship with a professional is, precisely, one of the safest places there is to begin to be seen. There, nobody judges you, nobody mocks you, and you can show yourself, slowly, in a space made for that. For someone who fears visibility, the helping relationship can be the first place where one experiences being seen without danger, and that, in itself, is already part of the healing.

And I want you to know that social anxiety, when that is the case, is not won by willpower, however much you try. Forcing yourself to face what terrifies you, alone and suddenly, often makes it worse, because it confirms to the body that the situation is indeed dangerous. The path that works is another: gradual, accompanied, with small and safe approaches, at the pace your system can bear. So it is not a matter of courage that you lack. It is a matter of method, and the right method is done with someone who knows, not by pushing yourself alone.

If you recognize, in yourself, this anguish, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Don't skip it. The understanding of this book walks alongside you, but if your fear is serious anxiety, you deserve more than a book. You deserve prepared help, and it exists, and it works.

And I want you to know, so that you have hope, that social anxiety is among the conditions that respond best to support. It is not a sentence, it is not for life, it is not an immutable trait of who you are. With the right support, many people who lived paralyzed by the fear of being seen reached a place where they show themselves, speak, share, with an ease they thought impossible. I don't tell you this to rush you, I tell it to you so that you know it is worth it: that on the other side of this fear, with the right help, there is a real freedom, and it is within your reach.

## 6. To be seen without losing myself

If your fear of being seen hides you, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can be seen without losing yourself.

Read this slowly. For you, being seen seems to risk losing yourself, exposing yourself, being at the mercy of judgment. As if visibility took something from you, made you vulnerable in a dangerous way, undressed you before the gaze of others.

And I turn the light the other way for you. Being seen takes nothing from you, if it is by your choice, of what you choose to show. You can show your work, your voice, your worth, without exposing yourself entirely, without being at the mercy, without losing yourself. Being seen, in the sense that matters, is letting reach the world what you have to give, and that doesn't diminish you, it multiplies you.

Notice the word I chose: multiplies you. Because what you hide stays trapped in you, limited to you, and what you show reaches many, repeats itself, echoes, lives beyond you. To show is not to spend yourself, it is to multiply yourself, it is to make your worth, instead of staying closed, spread and touch. Fear tells you that by showing yourself you expose yourself, you spend yourself, you risk losing. But the truth is the opposite: by showing yourself, what you have grows, reaches, stays, and you, far from losing yourself, find yourself enlarged in what you managed to share.

And I want you to understand that control, here, is more in your hands than the fear lets you see. You choose what you show and what you keep, where you show yourself and to whom, when you go forward and when you pull back. To show yourself is not to hand yourself over, defenseless, to the gaze of everyone. It is to share, with intention, what you decide to share, always keeping the helm. And knowing this changes the experience: it is no longer a leap in the dark, at the mercy of judgment, it is a gesture of yours, controlled, in which you decide each step. The visibility you fear is the one you don't control; the one I propose to you is the one you steer.

Let me hold it to a different light. There is a difference between being seen and being exposed. To be exposed is to be at the mercy of the gaze of others, without control, undressed, vulnerable, as you perhaps were, one day. To be seen, by choice, is to show what you choose to show, your work, your worth, with the control of someone who decides what she shares. They are different things, and your fear confuses them. Exposure hurt you. But being seen, by choice, is something else, and it can be safe, and good.

Notice this distinction, because it gives you back the power. When you were humiliated, you didn't choose to expose yourself, or you exposed yourself trusting and were betrayed in that trust. You were without control, at the mercy. But showing your work today, by your decision, is the opposite of that: it is you who decides what, when, to whom, and how. You hold the reins. And that difference, between being exposed without control and showing yourself by choice, changes everything, because what terrifies you is the first, and what I propose to you is the second. You can be seen while keeping the helm in your hands, and that is not the danger you lived, it is a new way, yours, of showing yourself.

And I want you to see that showing your work is not showing yourself, whole, undressed. You can share what you create while keeping what is intimate, you can occupy the light in what concerns your professional worth without exposing your private life, your fragilities, what you don't want to show. Being seen in what matters, your work, your voice, doesn't oblige you to open everything. You choose the border of what you share, and that border is yours, and respecting it is what makes visibility safe, instead of an exposure without limits.

And I want to be honest. If your fear is serious social anxiety, this truth is not enough to heal it. Your body will keep panicking in the face of visibility, however much your head knows that it is safe. So, in that case, this path is done with help, with someone prepared to walk alongside you as you face, slowly and in safety, what terrifies you.

But there are things you can begin, with care. You can begin by showing yourself in small ways, in safe contexts. Sharing an idea, showing a piece of work, occupying a little light, in a place where you feel relatively safe. And observing what happens. Almost certainly, not the humiliation you fear, but a normal response, or even a good one, to what you show. And each time you show yourself, and you are not judged as you feared, your fear loses a little of its force.

Each time you show yourself, in small ways, and nothing terrible happens, you give your system a new proof. That being seen, today, doesn't bring the danger of back then. And it is with proofs, repeated, in the body, and not only with ideas, in the head, that the fear, slowly, loosens. The terror is not undone by convincing yourself that you can show yourself. It is undone by showing yourself, in small ways, and surviving, time after time, until the body believes it. It is slow work, of many small proofs, and so don't demand of yourself to lose the fear suddenly: the body learns at its own pace, and your task is only to give it, repeatedly, the experience of showing yourself and being fine.

And pay attention to what happens after you show yourself, because your mind will want to twist the story. Even if it goes well, you will look for the small sign of judgment, the face you thought noticed, the comment you misinterpreted, and you will use that as proof that it was bad after all. Don't believe that reading, which is the fear defending itself. Train yourself to notice also what went well, the recognition there was, the fact that nothing terrible happened. Because the fear collects the proofs in its favor, and your task is to collect, on purpose, the proofs in favor of the new truth: that you showed yourself, and you were fine.

I want you to choose well where you begin, because it matters. Don't begin with the biggest stage, with the riskiest exposure, with the hardest audience. Begin where it is safe, with those who wish you well, in small gestures of showing yourself that would hardly bring judgment. It is not fleeing the challenge, it is giving your body the best conditions to learn the new truth, instead of throwing it, right from the start, into what confirms the fear. There is time, afterward, for the bigger stages. First, the body needs many small proofs that showing itself can go well.

And prepare yourself to feel the fear anyway, even when the context is safe. The alarm will rise, the heart will race, the urge to pull back will appear. That is not a sign that you shouldn't show yourself. It is only your old system firing. The work is not to wait for the fear to disappear in order to show yourself, it is to show yourself with the fear present, and to discover that, despite it, nothing terrible happens. Courage is not the absence of fear, it is showing yourself despite it, and it is this way, and only this way, that the fear, slowly, keeps losing force.

The awakening, when it comes, is a surprise. It is the time you show yourself, when you occupy the light, and instead of the judgment you expected, comes recognition, or interest, or simply nothing bad. And you feel, with astonishment, that being seen took nothing from you, didn't hurt you, and that, after all, your work, your voice, did, in fact, deserve to reach the world. It is a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves a lifetime of terror, and opens the door to showing, at last, what you have to give.

## 7. To show what I have to give

This crossing has a place it is leading you, out of the shadow and into the light. To show what you have to give.

And I want to show you what this means, with care.

To show what you have to give is to let reach the world your work, your voice, your worth, instead of hiding them in the shadow. It is to occupy the light you deserve, to share what you create, to let yourself be seen by those who would benefit from seeing you. It is to stop depriving the world, and yourself, of what you have to offer.

And it is, too, separating your worth from the judgment of others. Because one of the things that binds you is making what you are worth depend on the reaction of those who see you: if they like it, I'm worth something; if they criticize, I'm worth nothing. But your worth is not in the opinion of others, it is in what you do, in who you are, regardless of who applauds or criticizes. When your worth stops depending on others' judgment, visibility stops being so frightening, because no longer is your own worth at stake, each time you show yourself. You show your work, some like it, others don't, and you keep being worth the same, because your worth was never in their hands.

And I want you to see that you will be able to show yourself without pleasing everyone, and that this is not only inevitable but healthy. Anything that is shown is judged, and never unanimously: there will be those who like it, those who are indifferent, those who criticize. To expect the approval of everyone is an impossible condition, that condemns you to silence forever. To show yourself is to accept, beforehand, that not everyone will like it, and to do it anyway, because the worth of what you have to give doesn't depend on pleasing everybody, it depends on reaching those who need it. And to reach those, you have to risk not pleasing the others.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you show yourself, the old terror of judgment, the anguish of exposure, the urge to go back to the shadow. And you will want to hide again, to keep your work, to leave the light to others, so the fear will pass. But the fear doesn't pass by your fleeing it, it strengthens; it passes by your crossing through it, slowly, in safety, and discovering that you survive it.

But it is precisely there, in that showing yourself despite the fear, that your liberation lies. Because each time you show yourself, and you are not humiliated, you are not judged as you feared, you learn that being seen, today, doesn't bring the danger of back then. That your work deserves to be seen. That you have a right to the light, as much as anyone.

And I want you to see that showing yourself doesn't make you less deep, less genuine, less yourself. There is an idea, in someone who hides, that visibility is for superficial people, and that staying in the shadow is more authentic, more serious. It is not true. You can be seen while keeping all your depth, your truth, your substance. The difference is not becoming superficial, it is letting your depth reach those who would benefit from it, instead of keeping it only for yourself. The world needs, precisely, more deep, genuine, serious people showing themselves, and fewer hiding while the superficial ones occupy all the light.

I want you to see that showing what you have to give is not vanity, nor exhibitionism. It is generosity, and it is justice. Generosity, because you share with the world what you have of value, which would be needed by someone. Justice, because your worth deserves to be seen, recognized, and to occupy the light you earned. Hiding is not humility. It is depriving the world, and yourself, of what you had to give.

Think of who would benefit from what you hide. There are people, somewhere, who would need your work, your voice, what you know, and who don't receive it because you hide. Your fear doesn't harm only you, it leaves an emptiness where your contribution could be. And when you see this, the equation changes: showing yourself stops being a risk you run for yourself, and becomes also a service you render to those who would need what you have. Sometimes, what we don't do for ourselves, out of fear, we manage to do for others, out of generosity. And thinking of who needs what you hide can give you the courage that the fear of exposing yourself takes from you.

I want also to undo the confusion between hiding and being humble. True humility doesn't hide worth, it recognizes it with simplicity and shares it with generosity. To hide what you have to give is not humility, it is fear disguised as virtue. And the world, which applauds discretion, helps you, without meaning to, keep the disguise, making you feel that hiding is a quality. It is not. It is a wound. And confusing it with humility only makes it harder to heal, because it makes you defend, as virtue, that which robs you of life.

And stepping into the light frees something enormous in you. When you show yourself, you stop living in the frustration of someone who has something to give and hides it. You stop seeing others occupy the light that could be yours. You stop living short of yourself, in the shadow. And you begin to live the life your worth deserves, with your work reaching the world, your voice being heard, your worth being seen.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that orders you to hide, is carved deep, and it will appear each time you dare to show yourself, with its usual warning. It will tell you who are you to expose yourself, they'll mock you, better not to risk it. The task is not to silence that voice in a single gesture, it is to stop doing what it orders. To hear it say hide, and to answer it, slowly, what I have deserves to be seen, I'll show it. And each time you show yourself despite it, and you are not humiliated, you weaken the old voice, and you strengthen a new one, more your own, which knows that you have a right to the light.

And I want to leave you with something, about who sees you, above all if there are daughters, or younger people you guide. If they see you hide your worth, refuse the light, live short of yourself out of fear, they learn that this is how one ought to do, and it is likely they will hide, too, what they have of best. But if they see you show your work with dignity, occupy the light you deserve, risk being seen, they learn that they can do the same. Sometimes a woman learns to show herself, at last, not only for herself, but to give those who watch her the permission not to hide.

And I want you to see what opens up when you stop hiding. Your work opens up, which had been kept, and you discover that it reaches people, that it touches, that it matters. Your voice opens up, which had been stifled, and you discover that it has weight, that it is heard, that it makes a difference. Opportunities open up that were closed to whoever doesn't show herself. And, above all, the coherence with yourself opens up: the joy of being, on the outside, the size you always were on the inside, of ceasing to betray yourself out of fear, of living, at last, up to what you have to give.

I won't give you a method of exposure, because this book is not about techniques. And if your fear is serious anxiety, remember that the path passes through help. But I leave you the one light to steer by. What you have to give deserves to be seen. Show it, in small ways first, in safety, and discover, slowly, that being seen takes nothing from you, and that the world, and you, gain when you let reach what you have to offer.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. And, when the fear is intense, understanding opens the door to seeking the help that can treat it.

The shadow doesn't lift all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, lose a lifetime's fear. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your hiding place with modesty, and your fear with temperament.

Now you know that you hide from a visibility your worth deserves, and that this is not only shyness, it is a wound dressed, at times, as modesty.

You know that being seen, somewhere, was dangerous, or shameful, and that you learned to hide to protect yourself from a humiliation that has already passed.

You know that hiding costs you the life you could have, and deprives the world, and those who would need you, of what you have to give.

You know that what frightens you is not, in fact, being seen, it is being judged, and that that judgment is almost always greater in your imagination than in reality.

You know whom you fear being if you show yourself, and that underneath lies the fear of being judged, and the conflict of wanting and fearing the light at the same time.

You know that, if the fear is intense, it may be social anxiety, and that that has a name, has help, and responds well to support.

You know that the visibility you steer is not the exposure you suffered, and that the verb is to show, what you have to give, by your choice, keeping the helm in your hands.

Before closing, I want to set before your eyes a scene, and ask you not to let go of it.

Imagine someone with a beautiful voice, a rare one, capable of moving others, who sings alone, hidden, in a closed room, afraid that, if she sang for someone, they would mock her, as once they mocked her. And who spends her life singing to the walls, while, out there, there are those who would need to hear that voice, and those who would applaud it, if she dared to open the door.

The crossing is to open the door. Slowly, in safety, and perhaps with someone who helps you do it. To sing, first, for one trusted person. Then for more. And to discover that your voice doesn't bring the mockery you feared, but the recognition you deserve, and that the world, after all, needed to hear it. Because what you have to give deserves to leave the closed room.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more test. There will be setbacks. Days when you show yourself, and days when you go back to hiding completely, in panic, as always. And the old voice will use each setback to tell you you see, it's not for you. Don't believe it. Slipping back into the shadow is part of the way out of it. Nobody undoes all at once a lifetime's fear. It is undone little by little, and each time you show yourself and nothing bad happens, a new proof is left, and it is from those proofs, added together, that the courage to be seen, slowly, is built.

And there is one thing I want to leave said before everything else, without going around it. What you have to give is precious, and the world is poorer without it. The woman who hid, for having been humiliated one day, did what she could to protect herself, and that is worthy of tenderness. But her worth was never the problem, the problem was the humiliation that taught her to hide it. There is nothing to correct in what she has to give. There is only one thing to remind her, slowly, until she believes it: you are no longer in that place where showing yourself hurt, and what you keep deserves, at last, to reach the world.

And you already know, now, the sentence that holds these whole pages together. I can be seen without losing myself. And you know that showing what you have is not vanity, it is generosity and justice, and that the world, and those who need you, gain when you let reach what you have to give.

Understanding was the first step, and you took it by reaching this far. But the second doesn't fit on a page read. It is done by showing yourself, slowly, in safety. And, if the fear is serious anxiety, it is done with help, with someone prepared to walk alongside you as you face it. Because a fear that robs you of life deserves to be cared for, and seeking that care is not weakness, it is giving yourself the chance to be, at last, seen.

If you take a single idea from here, take this, and guard it against fear. You were not born for the shadow. You were born to show what you have, like any person, and the shadow in which you live is not your natural place, it is the hiding place where an old fear put you. Coming out of it, slowly, is not becoming another person. It is going back to being who you were before the fear taught you to hide, the child who showed her things with pride, and who still lives in you, waiting for light.

And if there is a second idea worth taking, it is this, and perhaps it is the one that moves you most. What you have to give doesn't belong only to you. It was given to you to be shared, to reach those who need it, to do in the world the good that only it can do. Keeping it hidden, out of fear, is not only a loss of yours, it is a lack you create for those who would receive it. And perhaps the way to overcome the fear is not to think of yourself, of the risk you run, but of who waits, without knowing it, for that which only you have to show.

And before you leave the closed room of this book, I ask one gesture of you only. A small one, but hard for you.

This week, show one thing, in small ways, in safety. Share a piece of your work with someone you trust. Say an idea you usually keep. Occupy a little light, in a place where you feel safe. And notice the terror that rises, the fear of judgment, the urge to go back to the shadow. And show it anyway. Because it is in that showing, in small ways, endured in spite of the fear, that you begin to discover that being seen takes nothing from you, and that what you have to give deserved, all this time, to reach the world.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are showing off, thinking too much of yourself, risking ridicule, remember: that thought is the wound, not the truth. The truth is that you are only letting reach the world what you have of value, and that this is not vanity, it is generosity and it is justice. What you feel as showing off is, almost always, only the normal size of someone who stops hiding.

Because what you keep, hidden, deserved to be seen, and you deserve, at last, to let it shine.

Because the world needs to see you, and you deserve, at last, to be seen.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
