# The one who is never chosen first

**For you, who learned to be the second option and to always give up your place. Because you can stop waiting for a choice that comes from outside and take first place in your own life.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who learned to always come second, to give up her place, to wait for a choice that never comes, and there is a point at which this stops being modesty and becomes a self-devaluation that makes you ill. If you recognize in yourself a sadness that never lifts, a persistent sense that you have no worth, or a dependence on the approval of others to feel like someone, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your place is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. The second option

Notice, honestly, the place you occupy, time and again, in people's lives and in your own.

You are the second option. The one left for last. The one who gives up her place. The one who makes do with what's left after the others have chosen. The one who is always available, and rarely the one chosen.

In the group of friends, you're the one who is always available, but rarely the first to be invited. In love, you're the one who waits to be chosen, and who is often chosen second, after someone else didn't work out. In the family, you're the one who gives up her turn, who relinquishes what she wanted, who puts her need at the end of the line, behind everyone else's. At work, you're the one who does what nobody wants, who's left with the bad hours, who accepts the worst place without complaining.

And you've grown so used to this that you no longer even see it as a problem. It seems natural to you, almost fair. It's your place. There's always someone more important, more urgent, more deserving of coming first, and you, patiently, are left for later, as though that were, simply, the spot where it falls to you to be.

But notice what grows, in silence, beneath that patience. A sadness grows. A sense that you don't count as much as the others. A weariness of someone who waits, always, to be chosen, and who watches the others be chosen first, time after time.

And perhaps you don't even remember it ever being any different. There having been a time when you expected, naturally, to be chosen, when you thought, without thinking about it, that your place counted as much as anyone else's. For many people who live in second place, this place became so habitual that it seems the only way of existing, it seems to be who they are. But it isn't. It is not normal to live always behind, always waiting, always making do with what's left. It's only a belief so old and so often repeated that you stopped seeing it as a wound, and came to take it for humility, or for temperament, or for fate.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not humility, even though it looks like it. People say you're easy, that you don't cause problems, that you make do with little. And there is generosity in you, yes. But beneath the generosity there is something else, older and sadder, that needs to be seen.

There is a belief that was etched in you, somewhere, very early. The belief that there is always someone more important than you. That your place is second, never first. That you don't deserve to be chosen before others, but only afterward, if anything is left. A belief so deep that you no longer even question it, that you take for a truth about who you are, when it is, only, a wound you learned early.

This book is not going to teach you to become selfish, to push ahead of everyone, to stop caring about others. I have come to look at something else. At the way you grew used to coming second, and at what that cost you. At the place you gave up so many times that you stopped believing you had a right to it.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can stop waiting for a choice that comes from outside. You can take first place in your own life, not by pushing ahead of others, but by ceasing to put yourself, always, behind them. And what keeps you in second place is not your true place. It's an old belief, which we are going to look at.

I want you to understand, right away, the difference between what it looks like and what it is. What it looks like, from the outside, is a humble, generous person, who doesn't need to be in the spotlight, who gives way gladly. What it is, on the inside, is a person who does not believe she deserves to come first, and who gives way not out of free choice, but out of an old belief that her place is behind. There is an enormous difference between giving way because you want to, from a place of abundance, and giving way because you don't dare to take up space, from a place of scarcity. The first is generosity. The second is self-erasing, even when it dresses itself as virtue.

This book does not come to make you selfish, nor to teach you to push ahead of everyone, nor to tell you that caring for others is wrong. It comes to distinguish true generosity from self-erasing, giving way by choice from giving way out of habit, putting others first out of love from putting yourself behind because you don't believe you deserve more. It comes to give you back a place in your own life, without taking away your goodness, but placing you, at last, yourself too, on the list of those who deserve priority.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In always offering to give way, to relinquish, to let the other go first, and in feeling, at the same time, a pang of hurt, quickly stifled, when no one offers you the same in return.

In waiting, in love, to be chosen, and in discovering, very often, that you were the choice of someone who couldn't get anyone else, the backup, and not the preferred one.

In always putting your needs at the end of the list, after everyone else's, and in thinking that normal, until the day you notice that the list never reaches your name, because there's always someone else ahead.

In feeling a muffled resentment, which you don't quite know where to direct, of someone who always gives priority to others and never, or almost never, receives it back.

In becoming suspicious, or overly moved, when, by chance, someone chooses you first, so strange is that to the place you've grown used to.

They are all the same thing. The place of the second option. The one who is never chosen first, not even by herself.

I want to show you how this shows up in everyday life, in things you may already take to be who you are. When a plan is being decided, they ask everyone what they prefer, and you say I don't mind, whatever, even when you're not indifferent, because putting your wishes first feels to you like an impertinence. When there's something good to share out, you serve the others first, you're left with what's over, and you don't even notice you did it. When someone treats you as a second choice, you feel a pang, but you swallow it, you tell yourself you have no reason to complain, that this is just how it is.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way you react when, by chance, someone chooses you first. Instead of receiving it naturally, you become suspicious, or overly moved, or you wait for the catch, because being someone's first choice is so strange to your usual place that you almost don't know what to do with it. You're not used to being the preferred one, the priority, the first. You're used to being the one who stays, the reliable one, the one who's always there, and rarely the one who is, in fact, chosen with desire.

And there's something nobody sees, beneath your availability. People see someone easy, generous, who doesn't cause problems, who makes do with little, and they get used to it, and they even value you for it, without imagining the sadness that grows beneath. They don't see the hurt of someone who always gives way and never receives, the hunger for a choice that doesn't come, the devaluation you carry in silence. They see the generosity, not the wound. And you, valued for being easy, become even more bound to second place, because now giving way is also being loved, and complaining feels like risking the little you have.

## 2. There's always someone more important

Go back. To the beginning. To when you learned that there was always someone more important.

It wasn't always rejection. Sometimes, it was only invisibility. Not that they told you that you had no worth, but that, simply, there was always something else, or someone else, that came first.

Perhaps there was, at home, a sibling who needed more. A sick person who took up all the attention. A father or a mother whose needs, whose dramas, whose urgencies, filled the house, leaving no room for yours. And you, a child, learned not to be a burden, not to ask, to wait for your turn, a turn that never came.

Perhaps you were the good child, the one who gave no trouble, and who for that reason received less attention, because the attention all went to the one who gave trouble. You learned that being easy made you invisible, and that to be noticed you'd have to be a problem, which you, the good child, never wanted to be.

Or perhaps, simply, there was little to share out. Little time, little attention, little affection available, and you, in a child's silent calculation, decided to give up your share, so that there would be enough for the others, so as not to be a burden in a house already overloaded.

Notice what a child learns in a place like this. She learns that her place is second. That there's always someone with more right to come first. That the way to have any place at all is not to claim the first one, is to make do with what's left, is to give way before asking.

And that child adapts. She becomes an expert at giving way. At waiting. At taking up little space, asking for little attention, making do with crumbs, to ensure that at least she isn't cast out, that at least she has a place, even if the last one.

That adaptation saved her, in that time. It guaranteed her a place in a house where the first place was taken. But the adaptation didn't undo itself when she grew up. It became her way of being in the world. And today, as an adult, you go on giving up your place, waiting for your turn, putting yourself second, in a world that perhaps already had room for you in first place, if you claimed it.

And I want you to see the sad intelligence of this adaptation. A child who realizes she can't compete for first place makes a clever move to survive: she gives up competing, and becomes the good one, the one who gives no trouble, the one who gives way, because that way, at least, she isn't one more burden, one more problem, and she secures the place that's available, even if the last one. It was a way of being loved with the little there was, of not risking losing even that little by demanding more. It wasn't weakness, it was a strategy of emotional survival. But what saved you then imprisons you now, because you go on giving up competing for your place long after the danger of losing it has passed.

Notice, too, how this adaptation made you skilled at anticipating and serving the desires of others, and blind to your own. From concentrating so much on figuring out what others wanted, in order to give way to them, so as not to be a burden, you tuned an antenna for the desires of others and lost your own. Today you know, in an instant, what each person prefers, needs, wants, and about what you yourself want, you hesitate, you don't know, you have to guess. That blindness to your own desire is not a lack of personality, it's the result of a life spent looking outward, and it's recovered, slowly, by turning, at last, the attention inward.

Or perhaps there was, yes, a comparison that marked you. A sibling more brilliant, easier to love, more celebrated, beside whom you always felt less. A sentence heard early, that placed you below another. A look that lit up for another and not for you. And you learned, from those comparisons, that there was in you something that made you less choosable, less preferable, less worthy of coming first. It wasn't a decision of yours, it was a conclusion you arrived at, as a child, seeing yourself always in second place in the eyes of the ones you most wanted to be chosen by.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing those who raised you. Those who left you in second place, most of the time, didn't do it out of not loving you. They were grappling with their own urgencies, with their own sinkings, and had no way of giving you the first place you deserved. They didn't mean to make you invisible. But they did, without meaning to, by having no room to see you. And acknowledging this is not holding on to hurt. It's, finally, seeing why you learned to always stay behind.

And I want you to see where this is stored, because it matters. This belief, that your place is second, is not just an idea that can be changed at will. It's etched deep, in a layer earlier than words, in a time when you had no way of contesting it. That's why, today, you don't decide to give up your place, you give it up before you think, automatically, like someone answering a truth that was never called into question. And that's why it isn't enough to tell yourself that you deserve to come first, in order to feel it. The belief doesn't live in reason, it lives deeper, and it undoes itself slowly, with the repeated experience of taking up your place and not being cast out, and often with help.

## 3. The silent resentment

There is something that grows, in silence, in someone who always gives up her place, and I want to name it, because it's hard to admit. Resentment.

You give way. You give priority to others. You're left for last. And you do this, on the surface, willingly, with a smile, like someone who doesn't mind. But beneath, in a place you may not want to look at, a hurt grows. A muffled resentment of someone who always gives priority and never receives it. Of someone who always gives up her place and never sees anyone give up theirs for her.

That resentment is hard because it doesn't match the image you have of yourself. You're the generous one, the one who doesn't mind, the one who gives way gladly. And the resentment contradicts that, and so you hide it, even from yourself, and it stays simmering, silent, with no way out. And the more you hide it, the more it grows, because what isn't acknowledged isn't resolved, it only accumulates, and one day it overflows in ways you don't understand, or it sours within into a bitterness that slowly robs you of your sweetness.

And it comes out, sometimes, where it shouldn't. In a disproportionate irritation. In a coldness you don't understand. In a bitterness that surprises you. Because the resentment that isn't acknowledged doesn't disappear, it comes out through cracks, it contaminates, it sours, without your knowing where it comes from.

Notice the origin of this resentment, because it is just. You resent because there is a real injustice in your place. You give others the priority that no one gives you. You give up a place that no one gives up for you. You put your needs last, and no one comes to fetch them from the back. The resentment is not malice on your part. It's the sign that there is, in fact, an imbalance, and that a part of you knows it deserved more than what it receives.

But there is a truth here that's hard to take. That imbalance, in large part, you created it. Not the others. You. Because you were the one who always gave up her place, who always put herself second, who taught the world to treat you as a second option by treating yourself, yourself, as such. The others learned to put you second because you put yourself second. And the resentment you feel toward them is, deep down, directed at the wrong place. The person who most put you second, your whole life, was you.

I want you to hear this without blaming yourself even more, because that's going to be the tendency. It's not one more failing of yours. It's the logic of the child who gave way to have a place, continued in the adult who gives way still. But seeing that it was you, and not only others, who put you second, is also seeing where your power lies. Because if it was you who gave up the place, it's you who can, from now on, begin to take it up.

And I want to show you what to do with the resentment, because it has an important message for you. The resentment you feel is not to be swallowed, nor poured out onto others. It's to be heard. It's telling you something that you, on the surface, refuse to admit: that you're not well with your place, that you wanted more, that you have needs and desires you've been silencing. The resentment is the muffled voice of your own right, protesting beneath the smile. Instead of hiding it, listen to it: it's showing you exactly where you've been betraying yourself, where you give up what you didn't want to give up, where you put yourself behind against your own will. And that information is precious, because it points you the way back to yourself.

And when you begin to take up your place, to say what you want, to put yourself too on the list, you'll notice that the resentment lessens. Because the resentment is born from the imbalance between what you give and what you receive, and when you begin to give to yourself, that imbalance corrects itself. The bitterness you felt was not a defect of your character, it was a symptom of an injustice, and as you repair the injustice, by doing justice to yourself, the bitterness dissolves, and in its place comes something new: the peace of someone who, at last, counts too.

## 4. I stopped believing that I deserve it

I'm going to leave a sentence here, more than a question. Read it slowly, and notice whether it is yours.

At some point, you stopped believing that you deserved to be chosen first. You stopped even thinking it, expecting it, considering it possible.

Stay with this. Because here is where the knot lies. It's not only that others put you second. It's that you, from being in second place for so long, stopped believing that you had a right to the first. The devaluation, which began outside, became yours. You internalized it. You no longer need anyone to put you behind. You put yourself there, automatically, because you stopped thinking you deserved to be in front.

And the shadow hiding here is that internalized devaluation. The belief, deep and silent, that you are worth less. That there is, in others, something that makes them more deserving of priority, of attention, of love, than you. That your place is second, not by circumstance, but by a kind of truth about your worth. And the cruelest part is that this belief confirms itself: because you put yourself second, you are treated as second, and that treatment proves to you, once more, that your place is indeed behind. It's a circle that only you can break, and it's broken from within, by changing the belief, and not by waiting for the world, on its own, to put you in front.

Notice how this belief became invisible, so much yours that it is. You no longer think it. You feel it. You feel, without words, that you're not supposed to come first, that asking for priority would be presumptuous, that claiming your place would be arrogant. And you give way, before you even think, because the belief has already decided for you that your place is behind.

And notice the cruelty of this belief: it applies to you a measuring stick you'd never apply to anyone else. If a friend of yours told you that she always comes second, that she always gives up her place, that she thinks she doesn't deserve to be chosen first, what would you say to her? Almost certainly: but you're worth as much as anyone, you deserve to be chosen, don't put yourself behind. You'd acknowledge, without hesitation, her worth. But to yourself, you deny what you'd grant to any other. The belief that you're worth less is not a fair assessment of who you are, it's a distortion that treats you with a harshness you'd never use with someone you love. And one of the ways to disarm it is precisely that: looking at yourself with the eyes with which you'd look at a beloved friend who devalues herself.

And there is an enormous fragility in this. Because if you don't believe you deserve it, then you depend entirely on others choosing you, validating you, putting you in front, in order to feel any worth at all. You handed your esteem over to the decision of others. And since others, taught by you, put you second, you rarely receive the validation you depend on, and you live in a permanent hunger for a choice that doesn't come.

And there's a way of sabotaging yourself, without realizing it, that comes from this belief. When the possibility arises of being chosen, of coming first, of taking up a good place, there is in you a force that draws back, that gives way, that devalues itself, as though first place were not for you. Perhaps you diminish yourself before they diminish you, perhaps you offer yourself right away as the one who gives way, perhaps you turn down an opportunity before you lose it. It's the belief protecting itself: if I already put myself second, no one can put me there, and that way I avoid the pain of being passed over. But the price is high, because that way you guarantee, with your own hands, the second place that so hurts you, anticipating the rejection so as not to suffer it from others.

I want you to see, too, how this belief makes you distrust those who value you. When someone treats you as first, chooses you, gives you priority, instead of receiving it, you become suspicious: what does this person want, could they be mistaken, when are they going to realize that after all I'm not worth that much. The belief doesn't let in the validation that contradicts its sentence, and so you push away, without meaning to, those who would treat you well, and you feel strangely more comfortable with those who treat you as second, because it confirms what you already believed. Relearning to let in those who value you, instead of pushing them away, is a deep part of this crossing.

I want you to feel the weight of this. Imagine living always waiting for someone to choose you first, in order to finally feel that you're worth something. And watching others be chosen, and you not, or only afterward, time after time. And concluding, each time, that the fault is yours, that there's something in you that makes you second. It's a place of a deep sadness, and it's also a dangerous place, because a whole life of internalized devaluation can thicken into something darker.

I want to pause here for a moment, seriously. If this devaluation has become a sadness that never lifts, a constant sense of being worth nothing, a total dependence on the approval of others to feel like someone, or if there is in you a dark weight that won't lift, this may be more than a pattern. It may be a depression, a self-esteem wound that asks for serious help, from a professional. It is not weakness to seek it. It's the care you deserve, and that perhaps you never gave yourself, because you thought you always came second, even to yourself.

Don't answer just yet whether you deserve first place. The belief doesn't undo itself with a sentence, nor from one day to the next. But leave the question open. Because it is in it that the door lies. On the day you begin to believe, however slowly, that your place doesn't have to be second, you stop waiting for others to choose you in order to feel like someone. And then, only then, can you begin to choose yourself.

## 5. Prioritizing yourself

If you stopped believing that you deserved first place, then there is a verb waiting for you. To prioritize yourself.

And I want to be careful with this verb, because your fear is going to distort it. Prioritizing yourself is not becoming selfish, pushing ahead of everyone, trampling others to come first. It's something else, simpler and harder. It's ceasing to put yourself, always, last. It's beginning to count, you too, on the list. It's taking up first place in your own life, not in the lives of others.

Notice this distinction, because it matters. I'm not asking you to be first in the lives of others, to demand to be everyone's choice. That doesn't depend on you, and it's not the point. I'm asking you to be first in your own life. That your needs, your desires, your well-being, stop being left at the end of your own list. That you treat yourself as someone who deserves priority, at least from your own self.

Because notice. You wait for others to choose you first, but you yourself never choose yourself. You always put yourself behind, in your own life. And as long as you treat yourself as a second option, you teach the world to treat you that way, and you expect from others a priority that not even you give yourself. The first place you expect from others is, above all, the one you refuse yourself.

Prioritizing yourself begins there. With asking yourself what you want, and with that counting. With putting, sometimes, your need ahead of another's, without guilt. With ceasing to give up your place automatically, and seeing what happens when, for once, you take it up. With treating yourself, at last, as someone who deserves to come first, at least for herself.

And I want to warn you about something: at first, when you ask yourself what you want, it's likely you won't know how to answer. From getting so used to wanting what others want, to giving way before you think, you've lost access to your own desire. The question what do I want may bring a blank, an uncertainty, almost a fright. Don't be frightened by that. It's only the effect of a whole life of putting yourself second: your desire rusted from disuse. It comes back, slowly, as you insist on asking, on stopping to listen to yourself, on giving attention to what's going on with you. Relearning to want is like relearning a forgotten language, and each small time you discover what you want, and respect it, gives you back a little of yourself.

And I want you to begin with the small, because the big, at first, is too much. You don't have to claim first place right away in the big things. Begin by saying your preference in a simple plan, by choosing what you want to eat, by not giving up your turn in something unimportant. Small gestures of putting yourself in front, in situations where the risk is minimal, and where your body can learn, slowly, that taking up your place doesn't bring the casting-out it fears. It's from those small victories, added together, that the courage for the bigger ones is born.

It's going to cost you. You're going to feel the old belief, the guilt of taking up first place, the fear of seeming arrogant, of being a burden, of being cast out for claiming what you always gave up. And you're going to want to go back to giving way, to putting yourself behind, so that the guilt will pass.

But it's precisely there, in that taking up your place despite the guilt, that your healing begins. Because each time you prioritize yourself, and the world doesn't cast you out, you learn that you had a right to the place. That giving way wasn't the only way to belong. That you can take up first place in your life without ceasing to be loved.

It's going to cost you, because the old voice, the one that says your place is second, is etched deep, and it will appear each time you dare to put yourself in front, with its guilt of always. It will tell you that you're being selfish, presumptuous, that there are others more deserving. The work is not to silence it all at once, it's to stop obeying it. To hear it say your place is behind, and to answer it, slowly, I count too, I deserve too. And each time you prioritize yourself despite it, and no one casts you out, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows your place doesn't have to be always the last.

And I want to leave you something, about the ones who watch you, especially if you have children, sons or daughters. Because this place is passed on, from generation to generation. If they see you always give way, always put yourself last, make do with what's left, they learn that this is how a person, or a woman, should be, and they risk becoming, themselves too, everyone's second option. The most freeing thing you can teach those who watch you is not the example of the one who always gives way. It's that of a person who values herself, who takes up her place, who counts for herself too. Sometimes a person learns to prioritize herself, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to leave to those she loves the inheritance of living always behind.

I want to give you a sign to guide you by. Notice, over the course of a day, how many times you put yourself second automatically. How many times you give way without thinking, relinquish without being a burden, put your need last. And in each of those times, ask yourself: am I giving way by choice, or out of habit? Out of true generosity, or out of the old belief that my place is behind? Distinguishing the two is the beginning of recovering your place.

And don't confuse prioritizing yourself with ceasing to be generous. You'll go on giving way, giving, putting others first, very often, and that is good, when it comes from a choice of yours. The difference is that it stops being automatic, stops being the only thing you know how to do, stops erasing you. Giving way by choice, now and then, knowing that you can also take up your place, is generosity. Always giving way, by reflex, without ever putting yourself in front, is self-erasing. Prioritizing yourself doesn't take away your generosity, it gives you back the possibility of choosing, instead of always giving way because you don't know how to do otherwise.

## 6. Taking first place in my life

There is a destination for this crossing. Taking first place in your own life.

And I want to show you what this means, because it is freeing and it is the opposite of what you feared.

It doesn't mean others stop mattering. It means that you, too, begin to matter. That you enter the list. That you stop being the only person, in your life, whose needs don't count. Notice this carefully, because it's revealing: in every list of priorities you make, your own self is the only one who is, always, left out. You take care of everyone, and not of yourself. And prioritizing yourself is, simply, adding your name to a list you always excluded yourself from.

Notice the injustice you'd been living. You took care of everyone, and not of yourself. You gave priority to everyone, and not to yourself. You were important to the life of everyone, and not to your own. Taking first place in your life is correcting that injustice. It's treating yourself with the same care, the same priority, the same importance you give to others. No more. But, at last, no less.

And there's something that changes, in the world, when you begin to take up your place. Others begin to treat you differently. Because the world learns to treat us, in large part, by the way we treat ourselves. Someone who always puts herself second teaches others to put her second. Someone who treats herself with priority teaches others to respect her. Not always, not with everyone, but it changes. And you discover that part of your place as a second option came, after all, from how you yourself positioned yourself.

I want to speak to you about what opens up when you prioritize yourself. There opens up the possibility of being chosen, at last, for real. Because as long as you always offer yourself as a second option, as the one who gives way, as the one who makes do, you attract those who treat you as such. When you begin to value yourself, to take up your place, to treat yourself as someone who deserves priority, you attract those who see you that way, and you push away those who only wanted you in second place. The relationship with your own worth changes the quality of those who seek you.

I want to give you a concrete gesture, to begin. The next time they ask you what you prefer, and your automatic answer is I don't mind, whatever, stop. Ask yourself, for real, what you want, and say it, even if it costs, even if it seems small. Not to impose your wishes, but to bring them to the table, so that they count, so as to train yourself to exist too in the decisions. It seems a detail, but it's enormous: each time you say what you want, instead of erasing yourself behind a whatever, you're teaching yourself, and others, that your wishes count too. And it's from that sum of small gestures that your right to your place is, slowly, rebuilt.

And I want you to see the difference between being needed and being chosen, because your whole life you confused the two. You have been much needed: you're the one who's always there, who fixes things, who gives way, the reliable one. But being needed is not the same as being chosen, being desired, being preferred for who you are. You can be very useful to someone, and never be, in fact, chosen by that person. And that distinction, painful to see, is also the beginning of wanting something else: no more usefulness, no more being the one who serves, but being, at last, chosen for who you are, and not for what you give.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of counting, in your own life. Of your needs mattering. Of not waiting, always, for a choice that comes from outside, because you've already chosen yourself. Of ceasing to live in the hunger for validation, because the first to validate you became you. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the old belief, on the day you take up, at last, your own first place.

And I want you to see that prioritizing yourself does not make you less generous, less good, less loving with others. Your generosity stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops coming from scarcity, from the fear of having no place, and begins to come from the abundance of someone who already knows herself to be valuable. And the generosity that comes from abundance is cleaner, freer, truer, than the one that comes from scarcity, because it doesn't keep accounts, doesn't resent, doesn't expect anything in return. By prioritizing yourself, you don't cease to give to others. You begin to give from a more whole place, in which you give because you want to, and not to buy a place you thought you didn't deserve.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Be, in your life, the first to choose yourself. Not ahead of others. But not, ever again, systematically behind. And discover, slowly, that the place you waited your whole life for someone to give you, it was you who could give it to yourself, and that only you, after all, could do it.

## 7. Ceasing to wait for someone to choose me

There is a waiting that organized your life, and I want to name it so you can let it go. The waiting for someone to choose you.

You waited, perhaps your whole life, for someone to come and choose you first. To take you out of the line. To tell you: it's you, you're my priority, you come in front. And that choice, coming from outside, is what you've been waiting for in order to finally feel that you're worth something, that you have a place, that you matter.

But there's a problem with this waiting. It puts your worth in the hands of others. As long as you wait to be chosen in order to feel choosable, you're dependent on a decision you don't control. And if it doesn't come, or comes late, or comes in second place, you're left, once more, without the worth that only from outside could arrive.

Ceasing to wait for someone to choose you is bringing that choice inward. It's ceasing to put your worth on hold waiting for the validation of others, and beginning to give it to yourself. Not because others stop mattering, but because your worth stops depending exclusively on them.

Notice this reversal. As long as you wait to be chosen, you are the object of the choice of others. When you choose yourself, you become the subject of your own life. And there is an enormous difference between the two. One is passive, dependent, at the mercy of others. The other is active, sovereign, yours.

And when you choose yourself, something curious happens. You stop needing so desperately for others to choose you. And that absence of desperation makes you, paradoxically, more choosable, because you no longer offer yourself like someone begging for a place, but like someone who already has hers, and who shares it by choice, and not out of scarcity.

Notice this truth, which seems a paradox but isn't: the less you desperately need to be chosen, the more you are chosen. Because desperation pushes away, and the serenity of someone who already values herself attracts. Someone who always offers herself as a second option, available for everything, content with any crumb, teaches others to treat her as dispensable. Someone who values herself, who has her place, who doesn't give everything up, is treated with the respect her bearing invites. It's not manipulation, it's simply the reflection of the fact that the world learns to treat us by the way we treat ourselves. And by ceasing to offer yourself as second, you stop attracting those who only wanted you that way.

And I want you to see what this means for love, because that's where it most hurts to be the second choice. As long as you offer yourself as the one who is always available, the one who accepts crumbs, the one who waits to be chosen after someone else fails, you attract precisely those who want you in that small place. When you begin to value yourself, to know that you deserve to be someone's first choice, and not the backup, you change the kind of love you accept, and the kind of person who approaches. It's not that you start demanding to be idolized. It's that you stop accepting being treated as second, and that difference, in love, changes everything: you stop attracting those who want you in second place, and you open space for those who want you, at last, in first.

And I want to show you something about the hunger for validation, because it is the engine of this waiting. The more you depend on others choosing you in order to feel worthy, the more that choice, when it comes, never reaches you. Because a hunger that comes from within is not filled from outside: however much they choose you, validate you, prefer you, the inner belief that you have no worth filters everything, undoes everything, and the hunger comes back the next day. That's why the validation of others, which you so pursue, never satisfies you: because the hole is not in the lack of external validation, it's in an inner belief that no validation fills. And the way is not to manage, at last, to get everyone to choose you, it's to begin to undo, from within, the belief that you don't deserve it.

I want you to see that this doesn't isolate you. Choosing yourself is not ceasing to want others, to connect, to belong. It's belonging from another place. From a place where you no longer give everything up to have the right to be there, where you no longer put yourself second to be tolerated. It's belonging as someone who is worth something, and not as the one who makes do with what's left.

And I want to distinguish for you the belonging that begs from the belonging that chooses, because your whole life you knew the first. The belonging that begs gives everything up, accepts any place, makes do with crumbs, for fear of being left out. The belonging that chooses is in a relationship because it wants to be, gives because it desires to give, and stays where it is valued, not where it is merely tolerated. You belonged, perhaps always, in the first way, clinging to any place so as not to be left with none. And there is a more dignified belonging waiting for you: that of someone who knows she is worth something, and who for that reason doesn't need to buy her place with her own devaluation.

And I want you to see, even if it costs, that some of the relationships in which you always came second were perhaps not relationships worth staying in. There are people who treated you as a second option because it suited them, because you accepted it, because you never demanded more. And when you begin to value yourself, it's possible that some of those relationships will drift away, because you no longer serve them in the small place they got used to. That loss, however much it hurts, is a liberation: it opens space for relationships where you are chosen for real, and not tolerated in second place. Not every relationship you lose by valuing yourself was a relationship that deserved you.

There is a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of not waiting anymore. Of ceasing to live in the line, waiting to be called. Of knowing that you've already chosen yourself, and that for that reason, whether or not the choice of others comes, you already have a place, your own, in your own life. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the waiting, on the day you stop waiting for someone to choose you, and choose yourself.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, take up a place you gave up your whole life. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your self-erasing with humility, and your waiting with patience.

Now you know that you occupy the place of the second option, and that this is not humility, it's an old belief, etched early, that your place is behind.

You know that you learned, early, that there was always someone more important, and that you put yourself second to have some place, in a strategy of survival that saved you then and imprisons you now.

You know that there grows, in you, a silent resentment, and that this resentment, however just, points to the wrong place, because the person who most put you second was you, and that it has a message for you to hear, and not to hide.

You know that you stopped believing you deserved first place, and that this devaluation, internalized, made you dependent on the choice of others, and makes you push away, without meaning to, those who value you.

You know that the verb is to prioritize yourself, to take up first place in your own life, not ahead of others, but not, ever again, systematically behind.

And you know that the less you desperately need to be chosen, the more you will be chosen, because the serenity of someone who values herself attracts what desperation pushes away.

And you know that you can stop waiting for someone to choose you, and choose yourself, and that by doing so you become, paradoxically, more choosable, because you stop begging for a place and begin to have your own.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to keep it.

Imagine a room full of people, and a line to go in. And you, always, giving up your place in the line to whoever arrives after you. Out of politeness, you say. And you fall behind, and behind, and the line moves, and you never go in, because you gave up your place so many times that you reached the end without ever passing through the door.

The crossing is not pushing the others aside to go in first. It's ceasing to give up, automatically, your place. It's staying in the spot that was yours in the line. It's acknowledging that you have as much right to go in as anyone, and that always giving way wasn't goodness, it was the belief that your place was after everyone else's. And that you have the right, like any person, to pass through the door, and to go into the room where your own life happens.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more demand in which you have to be perfect. It's going to be slow, and it's going to have setbacks. Days when you prioritize yourself, and days when you go back to giving everything up, to putting yourself last, as always. And the old belief will use each setback to tell you, you see, your place is behind. Don't believe it. The setbacks are part of it. No one undoes all at once a belief etched in childhood. It undoes itself little by little, and each time you take up your place and the world doesn't cast you out, a new piece of proof remains, and it's from those proofs, added together, that the certainty that you deserve it, slowly, is built.

And I want to tell you one last thing, simple, to close. You always deserved to come first. Not ahead of others, but alongside them, with the same right, with the same place. The child who learned to give way, to wait, to make do with what was left, did what she could to have some place in a house where the first was taken. There is nothing wrong with her. But she deserved to be chosen, seen, put in front, as much as any other. And that right is still yours, intact, waiting for you to be, at last, the first to acknowledge it.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds up this book. I can stop waiting for someone to choose me. I can be, at last, the one to choose myself first.

Prioritizing yourself was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by taking up, slowly, your place. By ceasing to give way out of habit. By choosing yourself, time after time, until you stop needing others to do it for you. And there is a deeper work, with people to accompany you, to undo a devaluation that comes from far back, because esteem isn't rebuilt alone, from one day to the next.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. You are worth as much as anyone. Not less, not in second place, not after others. As much. And the place of first that you waited your whole life for someone to give you is not in anyone's hands to give it to you, it's in yours. Begin by choosing yourself, and the rest, slowly, follows.

And before you close these pages, I ask one thing of you only. A hard thing, for you.

This week, in a situation where you'd automatically give up your place, don't give it up. Take it. Say what you want, and let it count. Put, for once, your need in front, without guilt. Notice the fear of seeming selfish, the old belief that your place is behind, and take up your place all the same. Because it is in that taking up, endured despite the guilt, that you begin to stop being the second option, and that you discover, at last, that the first place in your life was always yours, waiting for you to be the one to take it up.

And if, in doing so, you feel that you're being selfish, arrogant, taking a place from someone who deserves more, remember: that thought is the old belief, not the truth. The truth is that you're only taking up your fair place, alongside others, with the same right. What you feel as selfishness is, almost always, only the normal size of someone who stops erasing herself. Your measuring stick is miscalibrated by a life in second place, and so the fair seems too much to you.

Because you deserve to be chosen, and the first to choose you can be, at last, you.

Because the place you waited your whole life for someone to give you, it was you who could give it to yourself, and it is, at last, waiting for you.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
