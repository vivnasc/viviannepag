# The woman who can't charge

**For the woman who lowers her price, gives things away, and discounts out of guilt. Because money does not corrupt the value you deliver, and to charge is to let it be valued.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and does not replace therapeutic support. The woman this book speaks of gives her best and falls apart when it comes time to charge, and there is a point where that difficulty stops being modesty and begins to compromise her livelihood, her health, her peace. If you recognize in yourself an anguish that tightens whenever you have to put a price on something, an exhaustion of someone who works a great deal and receives little, or a guilt that takes away your sleep, that may have roots that deserve the care of a professional. To charge what you are worth is not selfishness. It is letting your work support you.*

---

## 1. The voice that tightens when it's time to charge

There is a moment you know well, and that is perhaps among the hardest in your working day: the moment of saying the price. You have before you someone who wants what you do, you did your best, you know it's worth it, and the moment arrives to name how much it costs, and something tightens in your throat. Your voice hesitates. The number that comes out is lower than it should be. Or you add, right away, a discount nobody asked for. Or you find a way to offer, to make it easier, to not charge for everything, because charging the whole value seems to you, deep down, something ugly, exaggerated, almost indecent.

Notice how many forms this takes in you. You lower your prices to levels that barely sustain you, and you justify it, "the person can't pay more," "I don't want to seem expensive," "I'd rather have the work." You offer your time, your attention, your knowledge, for free, time after time, because asking to be paid costs you more than working for nothing. You do "just this one more thing" without charging, you offer extras, you stretch the service, and you never count what that is worth. And when, at last, you send an invoice, or say a figure, you do it with your heart pounding, waiting for a bad reaction, almost apologizing for having the audacity to want to be paid for what you delivered.

And there is a pattern beneath all of this, which is the heart of this book: you can't charge. It isn't that you don't know how to do the math, nor that you don't need the money, nor that your work isn't worth it. It is that, when it comes time to put a price on what you do, an old voice tightens, and that voice tells you that charging is ugly, that asking for money for what you give is somehow dirty, selfish, that it strips the purity from what you deliver. And so you lower, you offer, you discount, you bargain against yourself, and you always come out losing, not for lack of value, but for an inability to turn it into the livelihood you deserve.

And notice a detail that reveals the wound in its pure state: what you feel when someone pays without haggling, when they accept your price immediately, without hesitating. Instead of thinking "great, they valued my work," the opposite often appears: "if they accepted so quickly, I should have charged more," or else "did I overcharge, did I take advantage?" Notice the trap: if they haggle, you feel you charge too much; if they accept right away, you feel you should have charged more or that you took advantage. There is no price that leaves you in peace, because the problem isn't in the price, it's in the guilt, which always finds a way to tighten its grip, no matter what you charge. Recognizing this is important, because it shows you that you won't resolve the tightness by adjusting the number, up or down. You only resolve it by going to the root, to the guilt that turns any price into a source of discomfort.

I want to tell you right away, because it matters, that this affects above all women like you: the ones who care, the ones who teach, the ones who create, the ones who heal. Therapists, teachers, consultants, artists, caregivers, creators of every kind. Those who work with something they feel comes from the heart, from vocation, from a gift, and to whom charging for it seems almost a betrayal of the nature of what they do, as if there were things that shouldn't be sold, and the best of us were one of them. If you devote yourself to something you love, to something you feel serves others, it is very likely that charging costs you even more, because you mixed up, without knowing it, the value of what you give with the impossibility of putting a price on it.

I want you to know, right from the start, that this book has not come to tell you to become cold, mercenary, to stop caring about those you serve, to put money above love for what you do. On the contrary. The love you put into your work is precious, and I am not going to take it from you. I have come to look at something else: at the voice that prevents you from charging what you are worth, and that is, without your seeing it, sabotaging your livelihood, exhausting you, and preventing the value you deliver from returning to you in the form of money, of security, of a life your work should be able to sustain.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you don't read with your defenses up: money doesn't dirty the value you deliver. Charging doesn't corrupt what you do, doesn't make it less true, less generous, less yours. Money is only the way value circulates in the world, and letting your work be paid for is letting your value be, at last, valued. This idea, which seems simple, goes against a very old association, and so it will cost you. But it is the one that opens the door to something you never allowed yourself: receiving, in money, what you deliver in value, without guilt, without discount, without apologizing for wanting to live off what you do well.

There is a way of sabotaging yourself that perhaps you have never recognized as such, because you dress it up as professionalism: over-delivering. You promise one thing and deliver three. You agree on a service and add extras nobody asked for. You work double the hours you charged for, because "you want it to come out well," "it's no trouble," "the person deserves it." And from the outside it looks like dedication, and there is dedication in it, but underneath it is the same mechanism: you always give more than you charge to compensate for the discomfort of charging, to make sure nobody feels cheated, to earn what you receive by giving far more than what you receive. The result is that you work enormously, you deliver a great deal of value, and you receive a fraction of what it's worth, and you call that being a good professional, when it is, deep down, your difficulty in charging multiplying your work and dividing your livelihood.

And I want you to notice a contradiction that says everything about the size of this belief. You want, profoundly, to live well off what you do. You dream of stability, of breathing room, of being able to do what you love without the anguish of the bills. And at the same time, whenever you approach charging what would give you that life, you pull back, you lower, you offer, you sabotage, because charging enough to live well seems to you, deep down, too much, greedy, wrong. You desire the destination and you block the only road that leads there. That is why so many talented and dedicated women like you spend their lives working a great deal and earning little, not for lack of value or of demand, but for an inability to charge in proportion to what they deliver. It isn't the market that pays you badly. It is you who won't let yourself charge well.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in concrete scenes. It's looking at what others charge for the same thing you do, often worse, and realizing that you charge half. It's feeling relief when a client accepts your price, instead of feeling that it's fair. It's giving discounts to those who didn't even ask for them, because you anticipate the discomfort of charging. It's working enormously and having little, and not realizing that the problem isn't the amount of work, it's what you refuse to charge for it. They are all the same: the voice that tightens when it's time to set a price, and that makes you give away, almost for free, what should sustain your life.

## 2. To charge is to take

There is an association that needs to be brought to light, because it is from it that the tightness is born, and it is almost always older than your working life: the association between charging and taking. Somewhere, you learned that putting a price on something is taking from the one who pays, that asking for money is a form of selfishness, that keeping something for oneself is less noble than giving. And that association engraved itself so deeply that it became invisible, a kind of silent morality that operates in you whenever you have to charge, and that turns a legitimate act, asking to be paid, into a vaguely shameful one, for which you have to do penance with discounts and offerings.

Notice where this may have come from, without judging anyone. Perhaps you grew up hearing that money is dirty, that those who charge a lot are greedy, that good people don't care about money, that talking about money is ugly. Perhaps you saw, at home, money associated with conflict, with shame, with strain, and you learned to keep your distance from it, as from something dangerous or impure. Perhaps you were taught, explicitly or implicitly, that a good woman gives, cares, serves, and doesn't ask, doesn't demand, doesn't charge, because charging is a thing for those who have no heart, and you wanted, above all, to have heart, to be good, to be loved for it. In any of these cases, the lesson engraved itself the same way: to charge is to take, and to take is bad, and I don't want to be bad.

Notice that there is here an old division, almost centuries old, that you may have inherited without knowing it: the idea that there are noble kinds of work, which shouldn't be paid for, and base kinds of work, which are paid for without scruple. To care, to heal, to teach, to create beauty, to console, were for a long time on the side of vocations, of missions, of things done out of love, out of duty, out of calling, and not for money, and they were, not by chance, for a long time, mostly the work of women, and mostly unpaid or badly paid. The nurse, the teacher, the caregiver, the midwife, the one who helped, did it out of vocation, and to charge well for it seemed to betray the nobleness of the gesture. If your work belongs to this family, you may have inherited this old morality that separates the noble from the paid, and that condemns precisely the trades of care to living on crumbs, as if the value they deliver were too sacred to be justly rewarded. This morality protects neither you nor your trade, it impoverishes you both, and it is worth seeing it for what it is: an old way of keeping badly paid the work of those who give the most.

And I want to show you how this association is particularly strong in those who do work of vocation. When what you do comes from the heart, when you heal, teach, create, care, there is a romantic idea, very widespread, that these things shouldn't have a price, that they are too noble to be sold, that charging for them lowers them. "How am I going to charge for helping someone?" "What I do has no price, and so I feel bad putting one on it." And this idea, which seems lofty, is a terrible trap, because it condemns precisely the most dedicated people, those who deliver the most value, to being unable to live off what they do, while those who deliver less, but don't have the tightness, charge without a problem and prosper. The purity you attribute to your work is preventing you from sustaining it, and impoverishing you for being, precisely, too generous.

In the way I learned to look at these things, there is here an underlying confusion that is worth undoing: you confused the value of what you give with the impossibility of charging for it. As if something very valuable, because it is valuable, couldn't have a price. But it is the opposite: precisely because what you do has value is why it deserves to be paid for. The price doesn't lower the value, it recognizes it. When something is free, it is frequently treated as if it were worth nothing, and when it has a fair price, it is valued, respected, taken seriously. By offering, by discounting, by not charging, you are not honoring the value of what you do, you are, unwittingly, devaluing it, teaching the world to treat as free what cost you a lifetime to learn to do well.

I want you to see this without judging yourself, because the tendency will be to think yourself foolish, or to feel anger at whoever taught you that charging was ugly. It is no foolishness at all, and whoever taught you did so out of their own wounded relationship with money. The association between charging and taking is not a defect of yours, it is an inheritance, an old morality about money that you absorbed before you were old enough to question it. Only that morality, which may have made sense in some context, is today costing you your livelihood, your health, the possibility of living off what you love. And undoing it is not becoming a worse person. It is recognizing, at last, that charging for what is worth it takes nothing from anyone, it gives to you what is yours by right.

## 3. Who am I when what I do has a price

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you not to dismiss it quickly with a mental discount, because it touches the bottom of the tightness.

Who am I when what I do has a price?

Stay with it. Notice the discomfort it brings, the feeling that putting a price on what you do turns you into something you didn't want to be.

For a woman who delivers value from the heart, this question touches a deep fear, and it is worth understanding. It is that, in your mind, there is an incompatibility between being a good, generous, dedicated person, and being someone who charges, who puts a price, who wants to be well paid. As if the two things couldn't coexist. As if, in the moment you charge for your value, you stopped being the generous caregiver and became a saleswoman, a self-interested one, one of those who "only think about money." And that transformation, in your imagination, is unbearable to you, because your whole identity was built on being the one who gives, who serves, who loves what she does, and charging seems to betray all of that.

And the shadow that hides here has a name, and it is guilt. The guilt of wanting to be paid, the guilt of putting your interest first, the guilt of taking money from those you serve, the guilt of not giving everything for free. That guilt appears whenever you charge, and it is so uncomfortable that you do everything to avoid it: you discount, you offer, you lower the price, so as not to feel it. You'd rather lose money than feel the guilt of asking for it. And so guilt governs your business, your career, your livelihood, making you give away, almost for free, what should sustain you, only to spare yourself the discomfort of seeming, in your own eyes, self-interested.

Notice that there is a difference between what you feel and reality, and seeing it helps. You feel that charging turns you into someone self-interested. But look at the professionals you admire, whom you respect, to whom you entrust what is precious to you: the doctor you trust, the therapist who helped you, the teacher who left a mark on your children. They charge, and they charge well, and does that diminish them in your eyes? Do you think they are self-interested for living off what they do? Almost certainly not, you respect them precisely for being good at what they do, and the fact that they charge seems to you the most natural thing in the world. Now, if that is how you see others, why would you be the only one whom charging makes unworthy? The rule you apply to yourself, and only to yourself, is not a truth about charging, it is an old sentence about you, and the fact that you don't apply it to anyone else should tell you that it doesn't describe reality, it describes your wound.

Notice how this guilt makes you strangely hostage to those who pay you. Because if charging gives you guilt, then any sign of discomfort from the client, any hesitation, any "it's a little expensive," confirms your guilt and makes you lower the price right away, offer, give in. You are left at the mercy of others' reactions, unable to defend your value, because your own guilt had already condemned you before they said anything at all. And there are those who, sensing this, exploit you, haggle with you, ask you for more for less, not necessarily because they are bad people, but because you yourself teach them, with your guilt, that your value is negotiable, that you can always give more, charge less, give in. Your difficulty in charging doesn't protect you from seeming self-interested, it exposes you to being devalued.

And there is a cost you pay, that perhaps you haven't yet connected to this: resentment. Because to give and give and not be paid, or to be badly paid, generates, sooner or later, a dull resentment. You begin to feel used, exploited, devalued, and that resentment contaminates the very work you love, contaminates the relationship with those you serve, contaminates the pleasure you used to have in what you do. And here is the cruel irony: you don't charge so as not to seem self-interested, to preserve the purity of the relationship, and the result is precisely the opposite, it is a relationship poisoned by a resentment that is born of always feeling on the losing side. To charge fairly, far from dirtying the relationship, is what would keep it clean, because a fair exchange leaves no resentment, and an exchange in which one of the parties always gives too much, does.

I want to tell you something about this guilt, because it begins to disarm itself. Wanting to be paid for your work doesn't make you self-interested, nor bad, nor less dedicated. It makes you someone who recognizes the value of what she does and who needs, like everyone, to live. There is no contradiction between loving what you do and charging for it. The best professionals in the world, the most dedicated, those who deliver the most value, charge, and charge well, and that diminishes in no way the love they put into what they do. The idea that you have to choose between being good and being well paid is false, and it is one of the lies that most impoverishes dedicated women. You can be both. In fact, you should: because a well-paid professional can continue to do what she loves in a sustainable way, and one who exhausts herself giving away for free ends, sooner or later, by being unable to give at all.

## 4. Giving away for free in order to be loved

Beneath all the discounts you give there is a single reason, and it is the deepest one in this book, so read this chapter slowly, like someone descending to the place where the price is decided long before you say it out loud. Beneath the voice that tightens, beneath the association between charging and taking, beneath the guilt of wanting to be paid, there is one thing, and almost always it is this: you give away for free in order to be loved. You don't charge, or you charge little, because, deep down, you associated being paid with the risk of ceasing to be wanted, and giving generously with the guarantee of continuing to be loved, accepted, well regarded. The price you don't put in money, you pay in another currency: in the hope of being loved for being so generous.

Notice what happens. When you offer, when you discount, when you give more than you charge, there is an immediate relief, and that relief is not only about avoiding the guilt. It is also because the generosity buys you something you value more than money: the approval, the affection, the image of a good person, the gratitude of those you serve. You traded, without knowing it, money for love. You'd rather be the beloved and badly paid professional than the well-paid and perhaps less loved one. And that trade, which seems noble, is in truth a way of buying affection with your work, of using your generosity to make sure you are wanted, exactly like a child who learned that by being good, by giving, by not asking, she secured the love she otherwise feared to lose.

And here I want to drop, with weight, the real price you pay for not charging. Because there is a price, and it is high. You pay in money, of course, in the livelihood you lack, in the security you don't build, in the strained life you have despite working so much. But you pay in things even more costly: you pay in exhaustion, because to compensate for what you don't charge you have to work more and more. You pay in resentment, because you feel used. You pay in devaluation, because the world treats as cheap what you offer cheaply. And you pay, above all, in your own relationship with yourself, because every time you don't charge what you are worth you are telling yourself that your value doesn't deserve to be defended, that it is you, before any client, who least values your work. The price of not charging is not saved, it is only paid by you, in silence, in every currency that matters most.

And there is an even more subtle way you pay the price of not charging, which I want to name because it is very common: you pay with your credibility. Strange as it may seem, those who charge little are often taken less seriously than those who charge well. The client associates, without thinking it, price with value, and a price that is too low raises doubts, makes you seem amateur, unconfident, dispensable. By lowering your prices so as not to seem expensive, you are, unwittingly, communicating that you aren't very sure of your value, and that insecurity infects those who seek you out. Have you noticed how hard it is to fully believe in the value of something that is very cheap or free? Your clients feel the same about you. To charge with confidence doesn't drive people away, it attracts precisely those who value what you do and want to commit seriously, and it drives away those who only wanted to take advantage of your difficulty in charging. The price is not only money: it is also a message about how much you yourself believe in what you deliver.

And there is a way your difficulty affects not only your livelihood, but your whole growth, and I want to name it. Those who don't charge well have no margin to invest, to rest, to grow. You live always at the edge, working too much to compensate for what you don't charge, without financial breathing room to train further, to hire help, to slow down, to create with calm. Your difficulty in charging doesn't bind you only to a low income, it binds you to a way of life without margin, in which you are always running to plug holes, without ever being able to lift your head and build something bigger. To charge well is not only to earn more, it is to gain the breathing room, the space, the margin that would let you, at last, grow instead of merely surviving. And that margin, notice, also benefits those we serve, because a professional with breathing room serves better than one who is always exhausted and struggling to make ends meet.

Do the math on this weight once, honestly, because it is an invoice you have been paying without ever opening it. Imagine what it is to dedicate your life to something you do well, with love, with devotion, and never to be able to live off it in peace. To work a great deal and have little. To see others, with less talent and less dedication, prosper where you exhaust yourself. To feel, at the end of the day, the satisfaction of having served and, at the same time, the dull anguish of one more month where the accounts don't balance. It isn't only a financial matter, it is a wound to dignity, the dignity of someone who gives her best to the world and can't get the world to give it back in what she needs to live. And that wound, so chronic, you perhaps already take for normal, for inevitable, for the price of doing what you love. It isn't. It is the price of not charging, and that price you can stop paying.

I want to stop here a moment, because there is a point where this difficulty stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more than understanding. If the inability to charge has left you in a financial situation that truly distresses you, that takes away your sleep, that threatens your livelihood and your security, if there is an anxiety that tightens whenever you have to talk about money, or if you recognize, beneath the guilt of charging, old experiences in which asking or wanting for yourself cost you affection in a painful way, then this deserves to be cared for with support. There are wounds around money and deservingness that were engraved by real suffering, and that aren't resolved only by reading nor by willpower. Saying so is the most honest way of caring for you: the understanding of this book opens the door, but there are old knots, especially when they touch survival and the fear of losing love, that come undone better when accompanied, with someone to help you do it safely.

This equation, the one of giving away for free in order to be loved, usually comes from far back, and almost always from before you had a single client. Sometimes it is a little girl who learned that she was loved when she gave, when she helped, when she asked nothing for herself, and that she became a nuisance, or lost affection, when she complained, demanded, wanted. Sometimes it is a woman who saw, in her family, that asking was dangerous, that wanting for oneself was selfish, that the safe way to be loved was to serve without charging. Sometimes it is the inheritance of generations of women who gave everything and never asked for anything, and who passed on, without words, the lesson that that is how one is a good woman. In any of these cases, the equation engraved itself the same way: if I give without charging, I am loved; if I charge, I risk ceasing to be. And out of love, out of fear of losing affection, you go on giving away for free what should sustain your life.

## 5. Money doesn't dirty the value

If there is one phrase I want you to take from this book, it is this, and I want to present it with care, because it dismantles the association that tightens its grip on you: money doesn't dirty the value you deliver. It doesn't corrupt it, doesn't lower it, doesn't make it less true nor less generous. Money is only the way value circulates in the world, a means of exchange, neutral, with no morality of its own, and receiving money for what you do stains in no way the love you put into doing it.

Notice what comes loose inside you when you stop putting money and value on opposite sides of the same table. You live as if money and true value were enemies, as if putting a price on something noble made it impure, as if your dedication and your livelihood were on opposite sides. And I propose that you see the truth: money and value are not enemies, they are partners. Money is what allows the value you deliver to return to you, to sustain you, to give you the conditions to keep delivering it. Without it, your value goes out of you and never comes back, and you dry up, and you end up unable to give at all. With it, your value circulates, it goes and comes back, it sustains you while you serve, and it lets you keep doing what you love for a whole lifetime, instead of exhausting yourself and giving up. Money is not the opposite of your vocation. It is what makes it sustainable.

There is a movement that frees, and I want to show it to you without making a recipe of it, because it is the opposite of lowering the price. First, to see: that you don't charge what you are worth, and where, and how you sabotage yourself. Then, to recognize where the association between charging and taking comes from, and that it was an inheritance, not a defect. Then, and this is the difficult step, to give back the morality that isn't yours: to look, on the inside, at those who taught you that money was dirty and that charging was ugly, and to tell them that you respect their relationship with money, but that it doesn't have to be yours, that you refuse to keep impoverishing yourself out of loyalty to a shame you didn't choose. Then, to separate: the value you deliver, which is yours, from the money that pays for it, which is only the means by which it circulates. And finally, to value: to put a price with dignity, to defend what you are worth, to let yourself receive. It isn't a step taken in one page. It is a crossing. But the direction is this, and it is the opposite of continuing to give yourself away.

Turn the scene around, to the side of the one who pays you, because from there another truth can be seen. When someone pays you for your work, they are not dirtying your work, they are recognizing it. They are saying "this thing you do has value, and I value it to the point of giving you, in exchange, something that cost me to earn." Payment is a form of respect, of recognition, of valuing. And when you refuse, discount, offer, you are, unwittingly, refusing that recognition, saying "no, what I do isn't worth that much," devaluing yourself in front of someone who was precisely valuing you. To accept being well paid is to accept being valued. And there is a dignity in that, not a shame: the dignity of someone who recognizes her own value and lets the world recognize it too.

And I want to show you something about what happens to those who don't charge, because perhaps it will free you from an illusion. You think that by offering, by charging little, you are doing a good thing for those we serve, being more accessible, more generous. But notice: what is cheap or free is frequently devalued, not taken seriously, little made use of. Those who pay little for something commit little to it. And those who pay a fair price value it, engage with it, make use of it. By charging what you are worth, you are not only taking care of yourself, you are also serving better those we serve, because a fair price creates a serious, committed relationship, in which what you deliver is taken seriously precisely because it has recognized value. Your misunderstood generosity doesn't help as much as you think. Sometimes, the greatest service you can render is to charge what it's worth, so that what you give is, at last, valued by those who receive it.

When the turn happens, it doesn't arrive as a reasoning you understood, it arrives as a permission you finally gave yourself. It is the moment in which you say your price, the fair price, without discount, without apology, and instead of the usual guilt, you feel, for once, that it is right. That it is fair. That you took nothing from anyone, you only recognized the value of what you give. It is a moment small and enormous, because it contradicts, with the force of experience, a lifetime's association, and it opens the door to a way of working in which you no longer have to choose between loving what you do and living off it. From that instant on, even if the old voice tightens again, you already know, on the inside, that charging doesn't dirty you, it values you, and that money, far from corrupting your gift, is what can, at last, sustain it.

## 6. To set a price is to honor the value

There is an inversion I want to show you, because it changes everything: putting a price on what you do isn't lowering it, it is honoring it. To charge fairly doesn't devalue your work, it values it. And learning to do it isn't becoming self-interested, it is beginning, at last, to respect what you deliver with the same respect with which you would like others to treat it.

And I want to show you a truth about abundance that links this book to the whole collection. The value you deliver in the world is enormous, but the value delivered only turns into prosperity if it is valued, that is, if it returns to you in the form of money, of livelihood, of means. You are like a spring that delivers a great deal of value and watches it all run off, because the door through which it would return, the act of charging, is closed by guilt. That is why you generate a great deal and have little, you give a great deal and receive little, and abundance never settles in, not for lack of value on your part, but for lack of valuing, which it is you yourself who refuses. To charge is, literally, the act of letting the value return. It is the inlet tap of your prosperity. And as long as guilt keeps it closed, you can be the most valuable professional in the world and remain strained, because prosperity is not born of value delivered, it is born of value that is allowed, at last, to be valued and to return.

Notice the logic, because it is clear once it is seen. You want your work to be valued, respected, taken seriously. And price is one of the languages in which the world expresses value. When you put a fair price on what you do, you are communicating, to yourself and to others, that it is worth it, that it is serious, that it is professional, that it deserves respect. When you offer or discount as a matter of course, you communicate the opposite: that it isn't worth that much, that it is dispensable, that it can be treated as free. It isn't that you have to charge a lot out of vanity. It is that a fair price is a form of honor, of recognition, of dignity, and by refusing it you are denying your own work the valuing it deserves, and that you, more than anyone, should defend.

And I want to speak to you of what it means, in practice, to value your work. It means knowing what it is worth and defending that value, even when it tightens, even when the client hesitates, even when the old guilt whispers. It means stopping apologizing for your prices, stopping justifying them as if they were a wrong, stopping anticipating refusal with discounts. It means treating your work as you would treat that of a colleague you admire: with respect, with the certainty that it is worth it, with the firmness of someone who doesn't haggle over what is fair. To value yourself is not arrogance. It is the most basic form of self-respect, and it is, notice, what teaches the world to value you too, because the world learns to treat us, in great part, by the way we treat ourselves.

I want to warn you of a resistance that will appear, because it is subtle: your guilt will try to convince you that charging your value is taking from those who can't pay, that you are excluding, being elitist, closing the door to those who need it. And there is here a partial truth that it matters to honor: there may, in fact, be those who can't pay, and you can, if you wish, choose to give, to offer, to make exceptions. But notice the difference: one thing is choosing to give, from a place of abundance, to those you decide to help; another is not knowing how to charge anyone, from a place of guilt, exhausting yourself. The first is true generosity, free, chosen. The second is your wound disguised as kindness. You can be generous, very, but from a sustainable business, from a value charged fairly, and not from your own exhaustion. The generosity that destroys you serves no one in the long run, because an exhausted and bankrupt professional cannot help anyone at all.

I want, further, to undo a confusion between price and the deservingness of those who seek you out, because it feeds your guilt. You feel that, by charging, you decide who can or cannot have access to what you do, and that weighs on you, because you wished everyone could. But notice: your price is not a judgment about who deserves it, it is a condition of the sustainability of what you do. You don't charge to exclude anyone, you charge so you can continue to exist as a professional. And there are always ways to serve those who can't pay, if you wish: a percentage of free work chosen by you, prices adjusted to specific cases, other channels. The difference is that those exceptions are born of your conscious choice, of your abundance, and not of the guilt that prevents you from charging anyone at all. A sustainable professional can afford the luxury of being generous with whom she chooses. An exhausted and badly paid professional cannot be generous with anyone, because she herself is in deficit. To charge well, far from making you less accessible, is what gives you, at last, the breathing room to be able to offer to whom you decide to offer.

I am not going to give you a method for putting a price on your value, because it would be one more task, and we would be feeding your tendency to turn everything into something to do well. The deep path is made slowly, and sometimes with someone to accompany you, because undoing a lifetime's association isn't done in a page. But the principle fits in a sentence, and it is this: your value deserves to be valued, and the price is one of the ways of honoring it. Put a price on it with the firmness of someone who respects what she does. Defend it without apology. And discover, slowly, that charging what you are worth doesn't make you less generous, it makes you, at last, sustainable, and that a sustainable generosity serves the world far more than a sacrifice that consumes you.

## 7. Charging without guilt

There is a destination for this crossing, and it fits in a sentence: charging without guilt. Putting your price and staying with it, without the automatic discount, without the apology, without your heart pounding waiting for the refusal. Receiving in money what you deliver in value, with the naturalness of someone who knows it is fair, that she took nothing from anyone, that she only let her value be, at last, valued. And I want to show you what this is, because it is the opposite of your whole working life, and it is liberating.

Notice how you charge today. You apologize. Not with those words, perhaps, but with your whole bearing: you hesitate, you justify, you offer a discount right away, you anticipate the refusal, and when the client accepts, you feel relief instead of naturalness, as if you had escaped a transgression. To charge without guilt is the opposite. It is saying your price with calm, with firmness, like someone stating a fact, and staying with it. It is not offering discounts nobody asked for. It is not justifying your figures as if they were a wrong. It is letting the silence happen after you say the price, without filling it right away with a concession. It is treating your invoice as a colleague of yours would treat it: with naturalness, with firmness, without drama, because it is fair, and the fair doesn't apologize.

I want to leave you a question that can serve as a compass, because it cuts straight to the root: if I fully believed in the value of what I do, and had no fear at all of losing anyone's affection, how much would I charge? Ask yourself this question before saying a price. The answer that appears, almost always higher than the one you usually say, is your fair price, the one you would say if guilt didn't speak. And the difference between that number and what you usually charge is, exactly, the measure of what your wound costs you, in every service, in every month, in every year. I don't ask you to charge that value right away, if it gives you vertigo. I ask you to know it, to know what it is, so that at least you stop confusing the price of your guilt with the value of your work. One thing is what you are worth. Another is what fear lets you ask. And beginning to distinguish the two is beginning to leave the prison.

And I want to speak to you of what this requires, because it requires something that frightens you: tolerating the discomfort of charging without giving in to it. To charge without guilt doesn't mean the guilt disappears immediately, it means you learn to feel it without obeying it. The voice will tighten just the same, at first. The difference is that you no longer lower the price because of it. You say your value, you feel the tightness, and you stay. And each time you stay, each time you charge what is fair despite the discomfort, you teach your body and your history a new truth: that charging doesn't kill you, doesn't make you bad, doesn't make you lose the love of anyone worth keeping. The guilt, without the obedience that feeds it, slowly fades, as experience proves to you, time after time, that charging what you are worth is fair and safe.

I want you to think about what you teach those who watch you work, because perhaps it will move you. If you have daughters, or younger women who learn by looking at you, notice what you show them about the value of a woman's work. If they see you give for free, lower yourself, apologize for charging, exhaust yourself without return, they are learning from you that a woman's work is worth little, that asking is ugly, that being good is not charging. And it is likely that they will repeat the same, and that they will exhaust themselves as you exhaust yourself. The most liberating thing you can do for those who come after you is not to give them one more example of generous sacrifice. It is to show them a woman who loves what she does, who does it well, and who charges for it with dignity, without guilt, living with breathing room off her value. Sometimes a woman learns to charge, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to pass on to those who come next the same devaluation she inherited.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that perhaps you don't even remember wanting, so used are you to giving for free: the joy of living off what you love. Of doing what gives you meaning and being well paid for it. Of looking at the accounts and seeing that your work sustains you, with breathing room, with peace. Of no longer exhausting yourself compensating for what you don't charge, and being able to work less and better, because each hour is, at last, justly paid. Of feeling, on receiving, not guilt, but the satisfaction of someone who was recognized, valued, respected in her worth. That joy is your right, as much yours as any professional's, and it is also, notice, what makes it possible for you to keep doing what you love for a long time, because what doesn't sustain us, sooner or later, we abandon, and what sustains us we can cultivate a whole lifetime.

And I want to leave you a distinction that protects everything else. To charge without guilt is not to stop caring about those we serve, it is not to put money above value, it is not to become a cold professional who only thinks of profit. It is to keep all your love for what you do and add to it respect for yourself. You continue to deliver value, with the same dedication, with the same heart. You only stop giving it away for free, devaluing it, exhausting yourself offering it. The love for what you do and charging fairly can not only coexist, they sustain each other, because it is precisely because you love what you do that you want to be able to keep doing it, and for that you need it to sustain you. To charge well is, deep down, a way of protecting the love you have for your work, ensuring that you will be able to keep having it.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything all at once, and it doesn't make you charge without the tightness overnight. But it changes enough so you can stop feeling bad, or self-interested, for wanting to live off what you do well.

Now you know that you can't charge, and that this isn't a lack of skill with numbers, it is an old voice that tightens when it's time to set a price. You know that this voice is born of the association between charging and taking, and that it is particularly strong in those who do work of vocation. You know that the shadow that governs you is guilt, and that the guilt of charging makes you hostage to those who pay you and fills you with resentment. You know, and this was the hardest, that you give away for free in order to be loved, and that the price of not charging is not saved, it is paid by you, in exhaustion, in devaluation, in the livelihood you lack. You know that money doesn't dirty the value you deliver, that to set a price is to honor it, and that valuing yourself teaches the world to value you. And you know that you can charge without guilt, keeping all your love for what you do and adding to it respect for yourself.

And I want to leave you with an image, because it sums up everything. For years you were like a spring of the purest water that, thinking the water was too noble to be sold, let it run for free to everyone, until the day when, exhausted from giving without ever receiving, it began to dry up. And a dry spring gives water to no one. The crossing I propose to you is not to stop giving your water, nor to stop finding it precious. It is to recognize that precisely because it is precious it deserves to be valued, and that receiving in exchange for what you give doesn't dirty your water, it allows it to keep running, fresh and abundant, for a whole lifetime, instead of drying up from a generosity that was consuming you. To charge what you are worth is not to betray the purity of what you do. It is to ensure that you will be able to keep doing it.

And there is an effect that may surprise you when you begin: by charging fairly, you will like what you do more. Because the resentment of feeling badly paid contaminated the pleasure of the work, and when that resentment goes, the pleasure returns, cleaner. You will notice that you care better, teach better, create better, when you do it from a fair exchange and not from a sacrifice. Fairness in charging doesn't cool your vocation, it warms it, because it removes the poison of "I give so much and receive so little." To charge well is, in the end, also a way of taking care of the love you have for what you do.

You know, deep down, the phrase that holds this whole book together: the value I deliver can be valued.

Seeing this was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't made by reading, it is made by charging, slowly, what you are worth, and by staying with your price, time after time, despite the tightness.

It was for that crossing that I created **Prosperity**.

Prosperity is not a course about sales techniques, nor will it teach you tricks to bill more. It is a space to look at the way you prevent value from circulating in your life, and this book showed you one of the most concrete and most costly: not being able to charge what you are worth, giving away out of guilt what should sustain you. There, the crossing is to undo the association between charging and taking, to release the guilt that makes you hostage, and to relearn to value your work, to put a price on it with dignity, to receive in money what you deliver in value. Not to stop loving what you do, but so that what you do can, at last, sustain you.

And there is a parallel that may touch you, if you know the first collection I wrote, FreeMe, for mothers. There, there are mothers who erase themselves, who give everything and ask for nothing, who confuse loving with sacrificing themselves endlessly. You do the same, in your work: you give everything and don't charge, you confuse serving with sacrificing yourself. One erases herself at home, the other erases herself in what she does. But deep down it is the same journey: to discover that we can value ourselves without ceasing to love, that taking care of ourselves doesn't betray those we serve, and that a woman who respects herself serves the world far better than one who consumes herself.

If you feel this is the moment, you'll find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't yet the moment, that's okay, and this reading is my gift, without an invoice. What you have read is already yours, and stays with you, waiting, without hurry, for the day you say your price without apologizing.

And before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing, and it will be, for you, among the hardest there is, and that is why I ask it of you. The next time you have to say a price, say it fairly, without discount, and stay with it. Don't offer what nobody asked for, don't justify, don't hurry to lower it. Say the value, and let the silence happen. Notice the tightness, the voice telling you to give in, and don't give in. Because it is in that staying, in your fair price, without guilt, that a lifetime's voice begins to come undone, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that charging what you are worth was never taking from anyone, it was, after all, letting your value be, at last, valued.

With all my tenderness,
