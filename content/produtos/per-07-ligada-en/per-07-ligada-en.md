# The person who keeps everyone connected

**For you, who are the knot that holds the whole web, and fear that without you everything would come undone. Because the bonds don't depend on your hands alone, and you can let go of the threads without losing the people.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who sustains, alone, the bonds of a whole group, and there is a point at which that stops being devotion and becomes a weight that exhausts. If you recognize in yourself an anxiety tied to keeping everything united, an exhaustion that doesn't pass, or a fear of separation that robs you of your peace, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your place is a beginning. It is not the whole path.*

---

## 1. If I let go, everything comes undone

Notice the place you occupy, in your family, in your group of friends, in the circles you belong to. You are the knot. The one who holds the whole web.

It's you who remembers everyone's birthdays. Who organizes the gatherings, the dinners, the parties. Who calls, who texts, who asks how everyone is. Who brings people together, who keeps the contact, who lets no one fall into forgetting. You're the living calendar of the group, everyone's memory, the one who makes sure no one loses sight of anyone.

Without you, you suspect, the family would stop gathering. The friends would lose sight of one another. The bonds, which you hold with such care, would loosen, one by one, until the whole web came undone. And that image, of the web coming undone without you, terrifies you so much that you don't even let yourself test it, and you keep holding everything, exhausted, so you never have to find out whether it's true or not.

And that's why you don't stop. You're always keeping the threads. Reminding, organizing, gathering, connecting. You carry, alone, the invisible work of keeping a whole group united, and you do it with devotion, with love, but also with a weight no one sees, and with a fear underneath that you rarely name.

And perhaps you can no longer say when was the last time you walked into a room without counting who was missing. When you were ever in a group without being the one organizing it, ever belonged to a family without being the one gathering it, ever let the bonds simply happen without your hand holding them. For many people who become the knot, this way of being has become so habitual that it seems to be who they are, it even seems like a virtue, a gift. But it isn't only a gift. It's also a weight, and a fear, and a wound so old that you stopped seeing it as such, and came to take it for temperament, or for love, or for simply being this way.

The fear that, if you let go, everything will come undone, and that the blame for that scattering will be, then, yours.

I want to tell you right now, before anything else, that this isn't only generosity, nor love for people, even though it looks like that. Everyone thanks you. They say that without you the family wouldn't gather, that you're the heart of the group, that everything revolves around you. And there's true love in what you do, yes. But beneath it there's something else, older, that needs to be seen, and that costs you dearly without anyone noticing.

There's a silent loyalty. The loyalty of keeping everything united. And there's a fear, the fear of separation, that without you people will drift apart, will get lost, will stop belonging to one another. And it's that fear, more than the love, that sets you holding the web without rest, day after day, without ever being able to stop.

This book won't teach you to abandon people, to stop caring, to cut the bonds. I've come to look at something else. At the weight of being the knot that holds everything. At the fear that, without you, it comes undone. And at a possibility you may never have considered: that the bonds, after all, don't depend on your hands alone, and that you can belong to the web without having to hold it whole.

And let me tell you right away where these pages are going, so you can lower your shoulders as you read. Opening your hands isn't letting fall those you love. The bonds have a life of their own, and they don't depend on you alone to exist. And letting go isn't abandoning. It's trusting that relationships, like plants, have roots you don't see, and that they won't die just because you stopped watering them alone, day after day, exhausted.

I want you to tell apart, from the start, the thread held out of love from the thread held out of terror of seeing it fall. What it looks like, from the outside, is a generous, devoted person, who loves her people so much that she bends over backward to keep them united. What it is, on the inside, is a person who learned that bonds are fragile and that her task is to hold them, and who does it not only out of love, but out of an old fear of separation. There's an enormous difference between bringing people together out of pleasure, from a place of lightness, and holding them out of terror that, without you, they'll be lost. The first is joy. The second is a weight, even when dressed up as devotion.

This book hasn't come to tell you that caring for relationships is wrong, nor that you have to become indifferent, distant, someone who lets the bonds fall. To bring together those we love, to care for the ties, is one of the most beautiful things there is. It comes to tell apart nurturing from carrying, gathering out of pleasure from holding out of fear, caring for the bonds from being the sole one responsible for their existence. And it comes to give you back a life that isn't only keeping others' lives united, and a place in the web that doesn't depend on your being the one to hold it whole.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In always being the one to take the first step to gather people, and in feeling that, if you don't take it, no one will, that the gathering simply won't happen.

In feeling responsible when two people in your group drift apart, as if it were your fault for not having kept them united, and in rushing to try to bring them back together.

In being exhausted from organizing everything, and at the same time unable to let go, because letting go seems to you like letting the web come undone, and the blame for it falling on you.

In feeling that your worth, in the group, is in being the knot that holds everything, and that without that function you might have no place.

In organizing, reminding, gathering, and in feeling, at the same time, a muffled hurt that no one does the same for you, that you're always the one seeking and rarely the one sought.

In not even being able to imagine a group, a family, in which you weren't the one taking care of everything, because taking care of the bonds has become so much yours that you no longer know how to belong any other way.

They are all the same. The knot that holds the whole web. The person who keeps everyone connected, and who is afraid to let go.

I want to point out to you the small gestures where this hides, the ones you make without noticing them, so much yours have they become. It's you who creates the group chat, who sets up the family lunch, who takes the first step after a falling-out between two people. You notice that a friend has gone quiet, and it's you who seeks them out, always, because if it isn't you, you know no one will. A date approaches, a Christmas, a birthday, and the weight of gathering everyone falls, naturally, on you, without anyone deciding it, because that's how it always was.

And there's a detail that reveals everything: what you feel when a gathering doesn't happen, or when two people drift apart. Instead of seeing it as something natural, between adults who make their own choices, you feel a guilt, a responsibility, as if you'd failed in your mission of keeping them united. You carry others' separations as defeats of your own, and the unity of the group as a task that falls to you to guarantee, when, in truth, the relationships between adults are theirs, and not yours to sustain.

And there's an effort that stays hidden beneath each gathering that goes well. People receive the gatherings, the closeness, the unity, and they thank you, and they say you're the heart of the group, without imagining the invisible work it costs you. They don't see the exhaustion of the one who organizes everything, the anxiety of the one who watches over every bond, the loneliness of the one who unites others and has no one to unite her. They see the heart of the group, not the weight of being that heart. And you, valued for being the knot, become even more bound to the post, because now holding everything is also being loved, and letting go seems to risk the place that function gave you.

## 2. The knot that sustains the web

Go back. To the beginning. To how you became the knot.

Almost always, it was early, and almost always in a family that, without someone holding it, would have fragmented.

Perhaps you grew up in a scattered family, distant, cold, where people didn't seek one another out, and where you, a child, felt the danger of separation and began to gather. To remind, to organize, to pull everyone toward one another, so that the family, which threatened to come undone, would stay, at least, connected through you. A child doing the work of cohesion that the adults weren't doing, holding a unity that no one else seemed to want to hold.

Perhaps you saw a family break apart. Parents who separated, branches that stopped speaking, bonds that ruptured, perhaps forever. And you swore, without words, that you wouldn't let any more of them rupture, that you would be the one to hold what was left, to keep the fragmentation from continuing, your way of not letting the pain of separation repeat itself.

Or perhaps you learned that your place, your worth, your way of being needed, was to keep others united. That as long as you were the knot of the web, you would have a place in it, you would be important, indispensable, loved for the function you served. And that, without that function, there might be, for you, no place at all.

Think about what a child engraves when she grows up being the one who holds the household together. She learns that bonds are fragile, that people drift apart if no one holds them, that the unity of the family depends on someone keeping it. And she decides, without deciding it, to be that someone. To take up the place of the knot. To carry the responsibility of keeping everyone connected.

And she learns, above all, to fear separation. Because she saw, perhaps, what happens when bonds rupture: the coldness, the distance, the pain of a family that breaks apart. And she engraved, deep, that separation is a danger to be avoided at all costs, and that avoiding it is her task. That fear of separation, engraved early, is the engine of everything: you don't hold the web only because you like to gather, you hold it because drifting apart terrifies you, because each bond that loosens reactivates, in you, the old terror of a family coming undone. And that is why letting go costs you so much, because it isn't only releasing a task, it's facing the deepest fear you carry, the fear that, without you holding it, everything will separate and be lost.

And that child becomes the adult who holds the web. Who feels that, if she lets go, everything comes undone. Who carries, alone, the weight of keeping a whole group united, because she learned, early, that without her the bonds loosen, and that her place is to be the knot that holds them.

And there is, very often, beneath this mission, a way of securing her own place. Because a child in a fragile family, who feels she might be left out, discovers that, by being the one who unites everyone, the one who is necessary to the cohesion, the one no one wants to lose, she secures a place that otherwise seemed uncertain to her. Becoming the knot was, perhaps, not only a way of protecting the family, but of protecting your own belonging: as long as you're indispensable to everyone's unity, they can't leave you out, because without you everything comes undone. And that is why letting go frightens doubly, because it threatens not only the cohesion of others, but your own place among them.

I want you to see this without judging yourself. There's nothing calculating in this. It was a child finding, with what she had, a way of belonging to a place that seemed fragile to her. But today, as an adult, it's worth seeing that perhaps you don't need to be the knot in order to have a place, that your belonging can rest on who you are, and not on the function you fulfill, and that this is, deep down, a far more secure way of belonging, because it doesn't require you to carry the whole web in order to fit into it.

Or perhaps you became the knot not in a scattered family, but in a family in conflict, where there were angry branches, people who didn't speak, old hurts. And you became the one who kept the bridges, the one who spoke with everyone, the one who didn't take sides so as to keep connecting the sides that refused to connect among themselves. You grew up doing the diplomacy of a divided family, and you learned that your function was to keep the cracks from becoming ruptures, holding, alone, a unity that no one else wanted, or managed, to hold.

There's a very particular loyalty here, and I want to name it. It's the loyalty of someone who became the center of a family that, very often, didn't take care of her. You hold everyone, and who holds you? You remember everyone, and who remembers you? You gather everyone, and who gathers you? You're the knot that sustains a web that, deep down, perhaps never attended to you the way you attend to it. And you keep holding it, out of loyalty, out of love, out of fear, even without receiving, in return, the care you give.

And I want you to see the deep loneliness in this, which you may know well. It's the loneliness of being at the center of everything and, at the same time, alone. Surrounded by people, by bonds, by gatherings that you organize, and with no one to hold you, to remember you without being reminded, to seek you out without your being the one to seek. You built, around yourself, a web in which you're always the one who gives, who gathers, who cares, and so no one imagines that you, too, need to be gathered, remembered, cared for. And in the bad hours, you feel a loneliness you don't understand, without seeing that it was you, by becoming the knot, who left yourself with no one to be a knot for you.

I want you to see this without guilt. The fragility that set you holding the web, most of the time, wasn't created to wound you. It was the circumstance of a scattered family, cold, or wounded. But it placed on you a weight, that of keeping everyone united, that should never have been yours alone, and that you carry still.

And I want to show you in what part of you this reflex lives, because that's where it comes undone. The need to hold everything isn't only a habit, it's a way of feeling engraved deep, in a time when the unity of the family seemed to depend on you, and when separation was a real danger. So today you don't rationally decide to hold the web, you do it before thinking, automatically, and the anxiety rises whenever you feel a bond loosening, as if your own safety were at stake. And that is why it isn't enough to understand that others' relationships don't depend on you, in order to stop carrying them. The wound lives below reason, and it undoes itself slowly, with the repeated experience of letting go and seeing that the web doesn't fall, and often with help.

There's a sentence that perhaps sums up what you learned early. If I don't hold it, no one holds it, and everything is lost. And that sentence, which was perhaps true in that fragile family, became the lens through which you see every group, making you take on, everywhere, the impossible task of keeping everyone and everything united, as if the cohesion of the world depended on your hands.

## 3. Keeping united as a mission

There's something you need to see. Keeping people united has become, for you, more than a habit. It has become a mission. A responsibility you feel as yours, and yours alone, and which you cannot free yourself from.

Notice how this mission occupies you. You're always attentive to who is drifting away, to who doesn't come to the gatherings, to who has stopped speaking to whom. And you feel it as a problem of yours, to be solved. As if it were your task to keep everyone close, to repair the bonds that loosen, to keep the web from coming undone. It's a radar that never switches off, always tuned to the state of everyone's bonds, and that occupies, in you, an enormous space, that could be your own life.

And there's an enormous weight in this, that no one sees, but that consumes you. Because keeping a whole web united is endless work. There's always someone drifting away, always a bond needing attention, always a gathering to organize, a message to send, a bridge to repair. And you do it all, alone, in silence, in a ceaseless effort that consumes you, and that no one recognizes as the work it is.

Notice the injustice of this. You carry the responsibility of keeping a whole group united, and the group, most of the time, doesn't even notice. It receives the fruits of your effort, the gatherings, the closeness, the unity, as if they happened on their own, without seeing that it's you, alone, making them happen. And if one day you fail, if a bond loosens, you feel guilty, as if the failure were yours, when the responsibility should never have been yours alone.

And there's something cruel in this mission: it never ends. There's no point of arrival, no moment when you can say, there, the web is united, I can rest. Because there's always someone drifting away, always a new bond needing attention, always a new gathering to organize. The task renews itself, endless, and you run after it without ever catching it, without ever being able to stop, because stopping, you feel, is letting everything loosen. It's an emotional labor of Sisyphus, in which you push the boulder of unity every day, and every day it rolls back down, and you, exhausted, push it again, without end.

And I want you to see the difference between healthy generosity and this mission. Healthy generosity gathers people out of pleasure, when it feels like it, and rests when it needs to, without guilt. Your mission doesn't. Yours has no limit, doesn't rest, doesn't choose, it feels obliged to hold everything, always, with guilt lying in wait at each failure. The first is a pleasure. The second is a prison, even when it dresses itself in devotion. And the work of this crossing isn't to take away your pleasure in gathering those you love, it's to free you from the compulsive obligation of holding everything, so that gathering can be a pleasure again, and not a weight.

And there's something deeper. This mission has become your identity, and your worth. You are, in the group, the one who keeps everything united. And a part of you fears that, without that function, you would have no place. That if you stopped being the knot, you would stop being needed, being important, having a spot in the web. So you hold on, also, out of fear of disappearing if you let go.

And it costs you your health, too, because watching over and sustaining the bonds of a whole group is mental work that doesn't stop. Your head is always monitoring who has drifted away, what's left to organize, the bond that needs attention. It's a permanent, invisible vigilance, that consumes you from within, and that adds to everything else you carry. Many people who sustain the unity of everyone burn out, not from weakness, but because carrying, alone, the cohesion of a group is a weight no shoulder was made to bear without end.

I want you to feel the weight of this. Imagine carrying, alone, the unity of a whole family. Feeling that everything depends on you, that without you it comes undone, that it's your responsibility to keep everyone close. And doing it without recognition, without rest, with no one to hold you. It's a weight of immense loneliness, the loneliness of someone who unites everyone and has no one to unite her. And you deserve, after holding so much, to let yourself, at last, be held.

## 4. Who am I when I stop uniting

I'm going to leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you, when you stop uniting everyone?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs.

For you, this question pulls a thread you didn't want to pull. Because your identity has glued itself to the function of knot. You're the one who keeps everything united, the heart of the group, the one who holds the web. Take that away from you, imagine yourself not organizing, not gathering, not holding, and an emptiness appears. What worth do I have, then? What place do I occupy, if not that of keeping everyone connected?

And the shadow that hides here is the fear of separation. The terror that, without you holding it, people will drift apart, get lost, stop belonging to one another. And, deeper still, the fear that, without your function as knot, you too will get lost, drift away, stop belonging, because your place in the web was being the one who held it.

Notice how this imprisons you. You can't let go, even exhausted, because letting go seems to you like risking the separation you most fear. You're bound to the post of knot, in permanent sentinel over everyone's bonds, because dropping the vigilance seems to you like letting everything come undone. And you live in an effort without end, holding a web that might hold up more than you think, if you, for once, dared to trust.

And there's an enormous fragility in your importance as knot. Because a knot exists to hold the threads, not for itself. It's always in the service of the web, never of its own life. And you, from being the knot for so long, forgot that you too are a person, with a right to be cared for, to be sought out, to belong not for the function you serve, but for who you are. The knot doesn't live. It holds. And you, by becoming the knot, slowly lost your own life outside of keeping others' lives united.

And I want to look head-on at the terror this question wakes in you. What terrifies you isn't, truly, letting go. It's the separation you imagine letting go would bring, and the fear that, without your function, you too will stop belonging. To you, letting go of the web and losing your place in it are one and the same thing, fused since early on. But they're separable. You can let go of the function of knot and keep belonging, now for who you are, and not for being useful. The separation you fear belonged to that fragile family, to that time when you felt you only held on to yourself by holding others. Today, you can discover that your place didn't depend on your being the knot, and that letting go doesn't exile you from the web, it gives you back to it as a person, and not as a function.

I want to tell you something about the fear of separation, because it begins to disarm it, and because it's the heart of everything. Relationships don't depend on you alone. This is the idea that holds you with your arms raised, and it's, almost always, false. You believe you're the only thing between unity and scattering, that without you everything loosens. But that's the perception of the child who felt herself the only one holding a fragile family. The reality is otherwise. Relationships have a life of their own. People, even those who depend on your organizing, have bonds that don't depend on you alone. And very often, when you finally let go, you discover that the web doesn't come undone, that others pick up the threads, that the relationships hold up in ways you didn't control.

Don't answer the question of who you are when you stop uniting just yet. But leave it open. Because that's where the door is. The day you discover that you have a place in the web not for the function you serve, but for who you are, you stop needing to hold everything in order to belong. And then, only then, can you begin to let go, without the terror that letting go will leave you out.

## 5. The bonds don't depend on me alone

If you carry the unity of everyone out of an old loyalty, then there's a truth that needs to reach you. The bonds don't depend on you alone.

Read this slowly, because it contradicts the belief that governs you. You feel yourself the only thing holding the web. Without you, everything loosens. You're the knot without which the threads come undone. But that knot, however tight you feel it, isn't what holds the web up.

The relationships between people have a life of their own. They exist between them, and not only through you. The bonds of a family, of a group of friends, are made of many hands, of many histories, of many threads, and they don't all depend on your holding them. You carry them as if you were the only one, but you aren't. You're one of the hands. Important, perhaps central, but not the only one.

I want to show you a distinction that frees. There's a difference between nurturing a relationship and being the only one responsible for its existence. You can nurture the bonds, care for them, feed them, and that is beautiful. But it isn't you, alone, who keeps them alive. They live, in large part, on their own, by the will of the people who make them, by the ties that exist between them independently of you.

And there's something important to see here. By wanting to hold everything, by being the only knot, you prevent, without meaning to, others from connecting directly to one another. They connect through you, and not among themselves, because you're always the intermediary, the organizer, the one who gathers. And so you keep a web that depends on you, instead of a web where people have their own bonds, direct, that don't need you in the middle.

Notice the paradox. You hold the web so that it doesn't come undone, but it's precisely your holding that makes it fragile, because it makes it depend on a single person, you. A healthy web has many knots, many direct connections, and so it holds up even if one fails. Yours, with a single knot, is strong while you hold up and would collapse on the day you couldn't. By wanting to guarantee the unity, you create, without knowing it, the dependence that puts it at risk. And letting go, allowing other knots to form, other direct connections, doesn't weaken the web, it strengthens it, because it makes it stop depending on you alone.

Imagine what would happen if you let go a little. Perhaps, at first, there would be fewer gatherings, less organizing, less contact. But perhaps also, over time, people would begin to seek one another out directly, to take the initiative you always took for them, to develop bonds that don't pass through you. The web wouldn't come undone, it would transform, into one in which you're no longer the single knot, but one of the people, connected to the others by your own bonds, and not only by the function of keeping them united.

And I want you to see a truth that's hard, but that frees. Not all the bonds you hold should, perhaps, be held. There are relationships that would naturally drift apart, branches of a family that would follow separate paths, distances that would be healthy, and that you, in your effort to keep everything united, prevent from happening. Not every separation is a loss to be avoided. Some are natural, fair, even liberating. And part of letting go is accepting that it isn't yours to keep united what, perhaps, should follow its course. Letting others' relationships follow their own path, even when that path is drifting apart, isn't failing in your mission, it's respecting that each bond belongs to whoever lives it, and not to you to sustain by force.

I want to give you a concrete example, of the kind you may already have lived without naming it. There's a cousin, or a sister, who fell out with someone else in the group, and the two stopped speaking. And you, without anyone asking you, start phoning one and the other, setting up the dinner where they'll have to cross paths, passing along messages disguised as conversation, sewing, thread by thread, a bridge that neither of them wanted to build. You spend weeks on this, you lose nights of sleep rehearsing what you'll say, and when they finally greet each other, no one knows what it cost you. Notice what happened there. You carried a reconciliation that was theirs, not yours. You picked up a thread that two adults had dropped, and you held it alone, with your own hands, as if the peace between them were a task of yours to fulfill. And perhaps, if you had left the thread lying where they left it, you'd discover that either they sought each other out of their own will, in their own time, or that that distance was, after all, a legitimate choice of both, and not a rupture for you to mend.

And I want you to think of the rest that's on the other side. There's, in you, a vigilance that never stops, a permanent attention to who's drifting away, to what's left to organize, to the bond that needs care. It's a background noise that never switches off, and that you may not even notice anymore, so used to it are you. Letting go is, also, switching off that noise. It's being able to be in a group without monitoring it, in a family without managing it, at a gathering without being the one responsible for it. It's discovering the lightness, that you may never have known, of simply being with people, instead of keeping them united. That lightness is one of the sweetest things letting go can give you back.

I want to stop here a moment, with care, because there's a point at which sustaining the unity of everyone stops being a trait and becomes a wound that asks for more. If this weight has exhausted you to the point of making you ill, if there's an anxiety that won't let go of you tied to keeping everything united, or a fear of separation that robs you of your peace, that deserves to be cared for with support. And if, underneath, there's a history of a family breaking apart, of instability, of losses, that still crosses through you today, then we're facing something that doesn't undo itself only by reading. Undoing a fear of separation engraved early rarely happens alone. It happens, very often, with hands that accompany you in relearning to trust that the bonds hold up without you.

The awakening, when it comes, is a relief. It's the instant when you feel your fingers open a little and the thread doesn't fall. That relationships hold up, in part, without you. That you can let go of a thread, and it doesn't loosen, because it's held by more hands than yours. It's a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves the belief that without you everything comes undone.

## 6. Letting go of the threads

There's a verb for this crossing. Letting go.

Letting go of the threads. Not cutting them, not throwing them away, not abandoning people. Letting go. Stopping holding, alone and without rest, everyone's bonds. Opening your hands, slowly, and trusting that the web holds up without you holding it at every instant.

And I want to be clear, because your fear will distort this. Letting go isn't abandoning. Letting go is no longer carrying, alone, the responsibility of keeping everyone united. It's letting others pick up the threads, take the initiative, connect directly. It's trusting in the life of relationships, instead of replacing it with your effort without end. It's the difference between releasing the hand of someone who already knows how to walk and abandoning someone who needs you: the first is trust, the second is giving up. You, by letting go, trust, you don't give up.

Letting go begins with small things. With not always being the one to organize the next gathering, and seeing whether someone else does it. With not reminding, once, of someone's birthday, and seeing whether anyone else remembers. With not repairing every bond that loosens, and letting people, themselves, seek one another out or not.

And I want to warn you of something, so it doesn't frighten you. At first, when you let go, it's likely that some things will, in fact, stop happening. A gathering that isn't set up, a contact that spaces out, a bond that cools. And your anxiety will shout: you see, without me everything comes undone. But notice well what that means, without the panic. It means that connection depended, indeed, on you alone, and that it perhaps wasn't, after all, a living bond, but a thread you held alone. The connections that matter, the ones that have root in the people, will hold up, or someone will pick them up. The ones that come undone when you let go reveal what was only your effort. And that, however much it hurts, is precious information, not a catastrophe: it shows you where there was life and where there was only your work.

And I want you to see that letting go doesn't have to be all at once, nor does it have to be cold. You can keep gathering the people you take joy in gathering, caring for the bonds that fill you, organizing what you feel like organizing. The difference is that it stops being a compulsive obligation, a weight you carry for everyone, and becomes a choice, in things you choose, with people you choose. Letting go isn't ceasing to care for relationships. It's ceasing to be the only one responsible for all of them, and beginning to care, out of pleasure and not out of fear, for the ones that truly matter to you.

It will cost you. You'll feel the old anxiety, the fear of separation, the guilt of not holding everything. You'll imagine the web coming undone because of you, the people drifting away, the family fragmenting. And you'll want to pick up the threads again, to organize, to unite, so the anxiety passes.

But it's precisely there, in that letting go endured, that your liberation lies. Because each time you let go of a thread, and the web doesn't come undone, you learn a new truth. That you weren't the only one holding it. That relationships have a life of their own. That letting go isn't abandoning, it's trusting. And that you have a right to a life that isn't only keeping others' lives united.

It will appear, with each thread you let go, the old voice, the one that says that without you everything comes undone, that it's your responsibility, that if you let go you fail everyone. That voice has the age of the child who held the household together, and it doesn't go quiet because you, once, decided that it should. The work isn't to make it go quiet, it's to let go of the thread even while it shouts. To hear it say hold everything, and to answer it, slowly, the bonds don't depend on me alone, and I too deserve to be cared for. And each time you let go in spite of it, and the web holds up, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows that belonging isn't the same as carrying.

And there are those who watch you hold the threads, and learn from you to do the same, especially if you have children. Because the place of knot is passed from hands to hands, from one generation to the next, like an inheritance no one chose. If they see you carry the unity of everyone, exhaust yourself keeping the bonds, live in service of gathering people, they learn that this is how one loves, and they risk becoming, too, the knots who exhaust themselves holding webs. The most liberating thing you can teach whoever sees you is not the example of the one who sustains everything alone. It's that of a person who cares for her bonds without losing herself in them, who belongs without carrying, who trusts that love doesn't depend on her effort alone. Sometimes a person lets go of the threads, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to pass to whoever she loves the weight of holding the whole world.

I want you to see what is let go, along with the threads. Let go is the weight of a responsibility that was never yours alone. Let go is the exhaustion of work without end. Let go is the anxiety of someone who watches, without rest, everyone's bonds. And you gain, in exchange, space. Space for yourself, for your life, to be a person in the web, and not the knot that holds it alone.

And I want to teach you to tolerate the anxiety that will rise when you let go, because that's where much of the crossing lies. When you leave a thread unheld, you'll feel an almost unbearable pressure to pick it up again, to organize, to gather, to repair. Learning to stay with that anxiety, without obeying it, is a muscle that's trained. At first, it will seem that something terrible will happen if you don't act. But, if you stay, you'll discover, slowly, that the separation you feared doesn't come, that the web holds up, that your not-acting didn't bring the catastrophe. And it's in that tolerance of the anxiety, repeated, that your body relearns that it can let go without everything coming undone.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Let go, slowly, of the threads. Let others hold their part. Trust that relationships hold up by more hands than yours. And discover, weight by weight, that the web didn't depend on you alone, and that you can, at last, belong to it without having to carry it whole.

## 7. Letting go without losing the people

There's a fear that holds you, and I want to go to meet it, because it's what keeps you the knot. The fear that, if you let go, you'll lose the people. That without you holding it, the bonds will come undone, and you'll be left alone, without the web you kept.

And I want to undo that fear with clarity. Letting go of the threads isn't losing the people. They're different things.

Notice. The bonds you hold with such effort, if they only exist because you hold them, then they aren't true bonds, they're an artificial web that you keep alive by force. And a web like that, kept only by your effort, is, deep down, fragile, because it depends entirely on you. Letting go reveals what's real. The relationships that hold up when you let go were true. The ones that come undone when you release them were perhaps never relationships, but only threads you held alone.

Let me say it another way. You confuse your function as knot with your place in the web. You think that, if you stop being the knot, you lose your place. But your place in the web doesn't have to be that of the knot. It can be that of a person, connected to the others by your own bonds, loved for who you are, and not for the function you serve. Letting go of the function of knot doesn't take away your place. It gives you back a better place, that of belonging for yourself, and not for being useful.

I want to show you what opens when you let go. There opens the possibility of being sought out, and not only of seeking. Of someone remembering you, and not only of you remembering everyone. Of receiving the initiative, the care, the connection, and not only of giving them. Because as long as you're the one holding everything, others never need to do it, and you never receive what you give. When you let go, you open space for others to seek you out too, and you discover, perhaps, who remembers you when you aren't the one to remember them.

And there's a truth, sometimes painful, but liberating, in this. You'll discover who stays and who drifts away. Some people, when you stop holding them, drift away, and that reveals that the relationship depended on your effort, not on a real bond. Others stay, seek you out, keep the bond, and those are the true relationships, the ones that were worth it. Letting go hurts, because it shows what was real and what wasn't. But it's a liberation, because it leaves you, at last, with the relationships that hold up on their own, and not with a whole web that only you kept alive, exhausted.

I want you to see, too, that by letting go you open space to discover who seeks you out. As long as you're always the one to take the first step, you never know who would take theirs if you didn't. When you let go, and wait, you discover who remembers you without being reminded, who seeks you out without your being the one to seek, who gathers you instead of your always being the one to gather. And that discovery, however much it may hurt by revealing who doesn't seek you out, is also a gift, because it shows you who wants you, and not your function, and lets you, at last, receive, and not only give.

On the other side of lowered arms, there's a joy waiting for you. The joy of belonging without having to hold everything. Of being a person in the web, and not the knot that carries it. Of being sought out, cared for, remembered, and not only of seeking, caring, remembering. Of trusting that the bonds that matter hold up, and of being able, at last, to rest from work that should never have been yours alone. That joy is your right, and it's on the other side of fear, on the day you let go, and discover that you didn't lose the people, you only lost the weight of holding them all alone.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It won't make you, tomorrow, let go of a web you've held for a lifetime. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your effort with love, and your exhaustion with belonging.

Now you know that you're the knot that holds the whole web, and that this isn't only generosity nor love, it's an old loyalty and fear, and a way, engraved early, of securing your place.

You know that you became the knot in a fragile family, or one in conflict, and that you carry, alone, the unity of everyone, also to secure your own place in it.

You know that beneath everything is a fear of separation, engraved early, and that it's that fear, more than love, that sets you holding the web without rest.

You know that keeping united became your mission and your identity, and that you carry that responsibility without recognition and without rest, in a task that never ends.

You know who you are, or fear being, when you stop uniting, and that beneath it is the fear of separation, and the dread that without your function you too will stop belonging.

You know that the bonds don't depend on you alone, that they have a life of their own, that holding everything makes the web more fragile and not stronger, and that the verb is letting go, the threads, without cutting them.

And you know that letting go isn't losing the people, it's discovering which bonds were real, and belonging for who you are, and not for the function you serve. And that, by letting go, you open space to be, at last, you too, sought out, remembered, cared for by whoever wants you.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to carry it with you, out beyond these pages.

Imagine someone who holds, in both hands, dozens of threads, each one tied to a person, and who spends her life with her arms raised, never able to lower them, holding the whole web, afraid that, if she lets go of one thread, that person will be lost. And who is exhausted, her arms trembling, never able to rest, because the whole web, she believes, depends on her hands. And no one sees the effort, because from the outside it looks only like a beautiful tapestry, and not a pair of arms trembling with tiredness beneath it.

The crossing is discovering that many of those threads are held, too, by other hands. That they don't depend on you alone. And that you can, slowly, lower your arms, let go of the threads, and see that the people who matter stay connected, by bonds that weren't only yours, and that you can, at last, rest your arms and belong to the web without having to hold it whole.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more task to fulfill perfectly. It will be slow, and it will have setbacks. Days when you let go, when you trust, when you rest, and days when you go back to picking up all the threads, in a panic that something will loosen, as always. And the old voice will use each setback to tell you you see, without you everything comes undone. Don't believe it at first. The setbacks are part of it. No one lets go at once of a web they've held a whole life. It's let go little by little, one thread at a time, and each time you let go and the web holds up, a new piece of proof is laid, and it's from those proofs, added up, that the trust that the bonds don't depend on you alone, slowly, is built.

And before I lower my voice, I leave you a simple sentence, of the kind you keep in your pocket. You deserve to be cared for, and not only to be the one who cares for the unity of everyone. You deserve to be sought out, remembered, gathered, and not only to be the one who seeks, remembers, gathers. The child who became the knot of a fragile family did something extraordinary, with an immense love, to keep united what threatened to break apart. There's nothing to reproach in her. But she deserved to have, too, someone to hold her, someone to attend to her as she attended to everyone. And today, as an adult, you can begin to seek out, and to let in, that care, discovering that your place in the web never truly depended on your being the one to hold it, and that you deserve to belong not for what you do for the bonds, but for who you are.

Beneath everything you've read, there's a sentence that holds this whole book, and it's yours to say. I can let go of the threads without losing the people. The love that matters didn't depend on me alone holding it.

Reading this was beginning to untie the first knot. But the next knot isn't untied with the eyes, it's untied with the fingers. It's done by opening, slowly, your hands. By letting others pick up their part. By trusting in the life of relationships. And there's a deeper work, with someone to accompany you, to undo a fear of separation that comes from far back, because trust in relationships rarely rebuilds itself alone.

And if from all of this you keep a single thread, let it be this. Love doesn't depend on your effort alone. The bonds that matter have roots you don't see, and they won't die just because you stopped watering them alone. You can rest, live your life, be a person in the web and not the knot that carries it, and the people who want you will keep wanting you, because they want you, and not the function you serve. Letting go of the threads isn't risking losing the love. It's discovering, at last, the love that didn't depend on your holding it, and that was there, waiting for you, beneath all your tiredness of sustaining it.

And before you put down this book and go back to your web, I ask you one single gesture. Difficult, for you.

This week, let go of a thread. Don't organize, once, what you would always organize. Don't remind, don't gather, don't hold. And observe two things. What happens, which almost certainly isn't the coming undone that you fear. And what you feel, the anxiety, the fear of separation, the guilt of not holding. Stay with that, without picking up the thread. Because it's in that letting go, endured despite the fear, that you begin to discover that the web didn't depend on you alone, and that you can, at last, belong to it without exhausting yourself carrying it whole.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you're abandoning your people, being selfish, letting everything come undone through your fault, remember: that thought is the old fear, not the truth. The truth is that the bonds that matter have roots that don't depend on you alone, and that letting go isn't abandoning, it's trusting. You're only ceasing to carry alone what can be of many, and opening space for a life that is, also, yours.

Because you deserve to be a person in the web, and not only the knot that holds it, and you deserve to be cared for, remembered, sought out, as much as you care for, remember, and seek out everyone else.

Because the bonds that matter have roots that don't depend on you alone, and you deserve, at last, to lower your arms and rest.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
