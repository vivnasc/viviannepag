# Learning to receive

**Five practices for letting in what is already yours, without giving it back by reflex.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a resource for self-knowledge and wellbeing. It does not replace therapeutic support. If you feel that your difficulty in receiving is tied to a persistent sense of not deserving, to guilt that won't pass, or to old wounds that ache every time someone comes close, seek professional support. Learning to receive can stir up what lies deepest in you. And it's completely okay to need company on that path.*

---

## 1. Why receiving is hard

There's a question I ask the women I work with, and the answer almost never comes in words. It comes with a glance away, a shrug of the shoulders, an uncomfortable smile.

The question is this: "When was the last time you received something, without doing anything in return, and simply kept it, at peace?"

Notice that I didn't ask whether you know how to give. No one doubts that. You know how to give. You give your time, your attention, your energy, your lap, your money, your care. You give even when you have nothing left. You give until it hurts. You give the way you breathe, without thinking, because giving has become your way of being in the world.

The question is about the other side. The side that many of us never learned to inhabit: the side of receiving.

And here the discomfort begins. Because receiving, for many women, is strangely harder than giving. Someone compliments you and you reply "oh, it's nothing," or "it was a mess, don't even look," or you change the subject right away. Someone offers you help and you say "don't trouble yourself, I've got this," even when you don't. Someone offers you a gift and the first thing you feel isn't joy, it's the urgency to reciprocate, to even the scales, to not be left owing. Someone says "I love you" and you answer "you too" before you even feel what it did in your chest.

You know this reflex. It's almost physical. Something comes toward you and, instead of opening your hands to hold it, you close them. You close them with a kind smile, with a polite excuse, with a rush to give back. But you close them.

There's an image that helps me explain this. Picture two hands. When you give, the hands are open, turned downward, letting what you have fall onto the other person. It's a gesture you know by heart. Now picture the opposite gesture: hands open, turned upward, waiting to receive. For many of us, this second gesture is almost impossible to hold. The hands turn back down right away. They don't know how to stay open to receive. They weren't trained for it. They were trained to give.

And there's another image I use a lot. Picture a glass turned upside down, mouth down, on top of the table. No matter how much water you pour over it, nothing gets in. The water runs down the sides, wets the table, goes to waste. Not because there's a shortage of water. Not because no one is trying to fill the glass. But because the glass is turned in such a way that nothing can fill it. Many women live like this: surrounded by people who want to love them, help them, value them, care for them, and empty all the same. Not for lack of someone to give. For lack of the capacity to receive.

Why is receiving so hard? The reasons are many, and almost all of them begin early.

Many of us grew up hearing that it was better to give than to receive. That asking was ugly. That needing was weakness. That a well-behaved girl gave no trouble, didn't demand, wasn't a burden. We learned that our worth lay in what we offered others, not in who we were. And a child who learns that she is only loved when she gives becomes a woman who is terrified of receiving. Because receiving, for her, means being exposed. It means admitting that she needs. It means running the risk of being left owing, and the debt, deep down, frightens her more than the exhaustion.

There's also a layer of deserving. Deep down, many women carry the silent conviction that they don't deserve. They don't deserve the rest, they don't deserve the compliment, they don't deserve the money, they don't deserve love they haven't first paid for with effort. When something good arrives without having been earned through sweat, it sounds like a mistake. It sounds like an error that will be found out. And so the reflex is to refuse, or to minimize, or to give it back quickly, before anyone realizes there's been a misunderstanding.

And then there's control. Whoever gives, commands. Whoever gives decides how much to give, to whom, and when. Whoever gives stays in a safe position, on top of the situation. Whoever receives, on the contrary, surrenders. Becomes vulnerable. Depends. And for a woman who learned that the world is only safe when she's in command, receiving means letting go of command. It means staying with her hands open, not knowing what's coming. And that, for someone used to holding everything, is terrifying.

I want you to know one thing before we go on. This difficulty is not a flaw of character. It's not selfishness in reverse, it's not false modesty, it's not fussiness. It's a protection you built a long time ago, probably when you were small, probably for good reasons. The reflex of not receiving protected you from something. Maybe from disappointment. Maybe from debt. Maybe from the shame of needing. It makes sense that it exists.

But today it costs what it costs. Because a woman who doesn't receive slowly empties out. She gives, gives, gives, and the glass that should fill with what others offer her stays turned upside down. And one day, without anyone noticing (sometimes not even her), she dries up inside. Not for lack of love around her. For an inability to let it in.

This guide is about turning the glass back over. Slowly. Without violence. Without forcing yourself to suddenly become someone who receives with ease, because that doesn't happen in a day. But by opening, gesture by gesture, your capacity to let in what is already yours.


## 2. The reflex of giving back

Before the practices, I need to show you up close the mechanism at the center of it all. Because until you see it, it keeps working without you noticing. I call it the reflex of giving back.

It works like this. Something comes toward you: a compliment, a favor, a gift, a gesture of affection, a sum of money, a moment of rest someone offered you. And almost in the same instant it arrives, before you've even fully felt it, an urgency lights up inside you. The urgency to balance. To reciprocate. To give back. To not be left owing.

Notice what this does. At the exact moment when you should be receiving, you're already planning what you'll give in return. Your attention, which should be resting on the good thing that just arrived, jumps right to the obligation to compensate for it. The result: you never receive anything fully. You're always halfway between receiving and giving back, and in that halfway the good thing slips through your fingers, exactly like the water on the upturned glass.

Look at a few examples, because it's in the examples that you'll recognize yourself.

Someone says: "Your work is so beautiful." And you, instead of breathing and letting the compliment settle, answer right away: "Oh, but yours is too." You gave it back. You didn't receive the compliment. You took it and threw it back, like a hot potato that burns to hold.

A friend offers to take your children for an afternoon. And you accept, but for the whole afternoon you don't rest: you spend the time thinking about what you'll do for her in return, what dinner you'll offer her, what favor you'll give back. The afternoon that was meant for you was spent calculating the debt. You didn't receive the rest. You received an obligation.

You receive money, maybe a generous payment, maybe some help, maybe a sum for your work that finally recognizes your worth. And instead of feeling joy, you feel discomfort. You spend it quickly, or give part of it away right away, or minimize it ("it wasn't that much"). Because holding it in your hands, keeping it, sounds like too much. It sounds like it doesn't belong to you.

Someone who loves you says it in plain words. And you answer with a hurried "I'm very fond of you too," or with a joke, or you change the subject. Because to stay there, with your hands open, letting those words come in and touch you where it hurts most and heals most, is unbearable. It's too much. It's more than you know how to hold.

The reflex of giving back disguises itself as virtue. It looks like generosity, it looks like good manners, it looks like humility. "I don't like to be left owing." "That's just how I am, I'd rather give." "It's no trouble to reciprocate." It sounds good. But beneath the pretty varnish is an inability. The inability to simply keep what you're given. And that inability has a cost you keep paying bit by bit, with no bill in sight.

The cost is this: the people who love you want to give to you and can't. There's a silent frustration in someone who tries to offer you something and keeps running into your "there's no need," your evasion, your rush to reciprocate. Giving to someone who doesn't receive is like hugging a statue. It's tiring. And little by little, without any ill will, people stop trying. Not because they stopped loving you. Because they grew tired of knocking on an upturned glass.

And there's an even deeper cost. Giving is also a form of receiving. When someone gives to you and you truly receive, you give that person the joy of seeing you happy. Your receiving is a gift to the one who gives. When you refuse to receive, you also refuse the other person that gift. You take away their joy of giving to you. The reflex of giving back, which looks so generous, is in the end a way of not letting the other person be generous with you.

I want to be clear about one thing. Reciprocating isn't bad. Reciprocity is beautiful, it's healthy, it's what lets relationships breathe in both directions. The problem isn't giving back. The problem is the reflex: the automatic, immediate, anxious return that happens before you've received anything at all. The difference between reciprocating and giving back by reflex is time. It's space. It's letting the thing come in first, keeping it, feeling it, and only then, if you want, without rush, without debt, giving too.

The practices that follow are, at heart, ways of creating that space. Of putting a pause between what arrives and your reflex of throwing it back. In that pause lives your capacity to receive.


## 3. The five practices

Here are the five practices. They aren't theoretical. They are concrete, small gestures you can start doing today, in the real situations of your day. None of them requires extra time. All of them require just one thing: that you notice the reflex and, instead of following it, do something slightly different.

Don't do them all at once. Choose one. The one that seems least impossible. Practice it until you no longer have to think about it. Then add another. Receiving is learned like anything else: bit by bit, with repetition, with patience toward yourself.

### Practice 1: Just say "thank you" and stay quiet

The next time someone compliments you, try an experiment. Answer only with two words: "Thank you." And then stay quiet.

Don't add anything. Don't say "but it was a mess." Don't say "oh, it's nothing." Don't give the compliment back with another compliment. Don't explain, don't minimize, don't deflect. Say "thank you" and stay silent long enough for the compliment to come in.

You'll feel an enormous discomfort. The silence will seem too long, you'll have a physical temptation to fill it with anything that takes the weight off you. Resist that temptation. That silence is exactly the space where, for the first time, you're letting a compliment settle in you instead of giving it back.

It's the smallest practice of all and, for many women, the hardest. Start here. A "thank you" followed by silence is the first gesture of open hands.

### Practice 2: Accept help the first time

Most of us refuse help the first time out of habit, even when we need it. "Don't trouble yourself." "I've got this." "It's not worth it." It's automatic. And often the person, hearing the "no," doesn't insist, and you're left alone with a weight that someone was actually willing to share.

The practice is simple and radical: the next time someone offers you help, accept the first time. Without the test of "no, there's no need" to see whether the person insists. Without the dance of someone who only accepts after refusing three times. If they offer to carry the bags, let them carry. If they offer to take the children, accept. If they offer you a ride, get in the car.

And while you receive the help, make an effort not to spend the time calculating what you'll give in return. Just let yourself be helped. Notice how the body eases when the weight is shared. That relief is yours. You don't have to pay for it.

Accepting help doesn't make you incapable. It makes you human. People need one another, and letting someone help you is trusting that person, it's honoring them, it's telling them without words "I trust you enough to need you." There are few greater gifts than that.

### Practice 3: Delay reciprocating

This practice attacks the reflex of giving back directly. The rule is this: when you receive something, don't reciprocate on the same day.

I'm not saying never to reciprocate. I'm saying to create space between receiving and giving back. If someone gives you a gift, don't rush out to buy another. If a friend does you a favor, don't hurry to do an equal one right away. Let time pass. A day, a week. Let the thing you received stay with you, without the immediate counterweight of the debt.

At first it will feel like you're being left owing, and that will bother you. Sit with that discomfort. Notice it. It's exactly the reflex squirming because it isn't being obeyed. Tell yourself: "I'm not owing anything. I received. And it's completely okay to just keep this, for now."

When, later, without rush, you feel a genuine wish to give to that person too, give. But let it be out of wish, not reflex. Let it come from a full place, not from an urgency to even the scales. The difference changes everything: it stops being the payment of a debt and becomes, at last, true generosity.

### Practice 4: Receive rest without justifying it

Rest is, for many women, the hardest thing to receive, because it seems it has to be earned. You only rest when everything is done, when the house is in order, when no one needs you, when there's no guilt left possible. And since everything is never done, you never truly rest. Or you rest with one foot out the door, your mind listing what's left, your body present but on alert.

The practice is to let yourself rest without justifying it to yourself. Sit down. Lie down. Stop. And when the guilt arrives (it will, with its voice saying "you have so much to do," "you don't deserve to be sitting still"), don't obey it and don't argue with it. Just answer, in silence: "I'm resting. I don't have to justify this to anyone, not even to myself."

Don't wait for everything to be done. Receive fifteen minutes of rest in the middle of the chaos, with the house untidy, with the list unfinished. Because rest isn't the prize that comes after you deserve it. It's a need of your body, as basic as water. And you don't justify drinking water when you're thirsty. You don't have to justify resting when you're tired.

Receiving rest is telling your body that it matters for existing, not for producing. It's one of the deepest ways of turning the glass back upward.

### Practice 5: Let love in for one more second

This is the most intimate practice, and perhaps the one that heals the most. When someone gives you love (a tender word, a hug, a look that tells you you're seen, an "I love you"), instead of giving it back right away or fleeing with a joke, stay there one more second.

Just one more second. Don't flee, don't answer in a rush, don't look away. Let the words or the gesture come in. Feel what they do in your chest, even if what they do is discomfort, or that strange tightening of someone unused to being loved with open hands. That tightening isn't a bad sign. It's the sign that you're letting in something to which you normally close the door.

When they hug you, don't be the first to let go. Stay one more second in the embrace, feeling the warmth, the weight, the presence of the other. When they tell you they love you, before you answer, breathe once and let it settle. Your answer can come afterward. But let it come after you've received, not in place of receiving.

This one extra second is the gesture of someone learning to turn the glass back over. It's small. No one around you notices it. But inside you, something shifts. Little by little, that second becomes two, then a whole moment, then a new capacity: the capacity to let yourself be loved without having to pay for it.

Five practices. Five ways of putting a pause between what arrives and your reflex of throwing it back. You don't need to master them all. It's enough to start with one and notice what happens when, instead of giving back, you receive.


## 4. Prosperity bridge

If this guide touched you, if you recognized yourself in the upturned glass, in the reflex of giving back, in the hands that don't know how to stay open to receive, I want to tell you that this is only the doorway.

Because the difficulty in receiving doesn't stay only in relationships. It spreads through your whole life, and in a very concrete way, it spreads through your relationship with abundance, with money, with everything that is yours and that you still don't let yourself have.

Notice: a woman who doesn't receive a compliment also has difficulty receiving what she's worth for her work. A woman who gives back by reflex also gives back prosperity, spending quickly, giving too much, charging too little, refusing what belongs to her because she thinks she doesn't deserve it. The upturned glass doesn't let in love, but it also doesn't let in abundance. It's the same gesture, in the same closed hand, applied to everything.

The question beneath all of this is this: what stops me from receiving what is mine? And it's a question too big for a guide. It's a question for a path.

It was for that path that I created **Prosperity**. It isn't a finance course, nor a formula for earning more. It's a space to tend to the root: your relationship with receiving, with deserving, with letting in what is already yours and that you've spent years refusing by reflex. It's where the five practices of this guide stop being small gestures and become a new way of being in the world, with open hands, with the glass turned upward.

You'll find Prosperity at [prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com](https://prosperidade.viviannedossantos.com). It's the doorway to everything that has to do with abundance in your life, and this guide was your first step into it.

But before any space, today's invitation is simple: choose one of the five practices and do it once. Say "thank you" and stay quiet. Accept help the first time. Let a hug last one more second. A small gesture, today. Because turning the glass back over isn't done with force. It's done slowly, one gesture of open hands at a time, until what was always yours can, finally, come in.

It's completely okay to not know how to receive yet. No one taught you. But it can be learned. And it begins now.
