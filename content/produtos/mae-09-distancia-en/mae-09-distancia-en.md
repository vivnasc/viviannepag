# The mother who raised from afar

**For the mother who left in order to provide for them, and carries the absence as betrayal when it was sacrifice.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This is a book about a pain that many mothers know from within and rarely hear named: the pain of having left, or having stayed far away, in order to provide for their children, and of carrying the absence as if it were a betrayal, when it was a sacrifice. It does not come from outside, with the gaze of someone who judges. It comes from within, with respect for the truth of what it is to leave the one you love in order to give him a future. If, in reading, you feel that the pain is too great to carry alone, or if there is a sadness that doesn't pass, please seek support. It is not weakness. It is care.*

---

## 1. The weight of a packed suitcase

There is a suitcase, in your story, that perhaps you never finished setting down. The suitcase you packed on the day you left. For another country, for another city, for a job far away, for where there was a future and where there weren't, right at hand, your children. You left, and inside that suitcase, beside the clothes and the documents, there went a thing that cannot be seen and that weighs more than all the rest: the guilt. The guilt traveled with you, and on many days it seems it never left the suitcase again.

You left for them. This is the first and the most important thing that needs to be said, and that you, on the bad days, forget. You didn't leave to flee, you didn't leave out of selfishness, you didn't leave to be rid of them. You left to provide for them, to give them what wasn't there, food, school, a roof, a future, a better life than the one you could have given if you had stayed. It was a decision of love, perhaps the hardest a mother can make: the decision to move away from the one she loves precisely in order to be able to care for him. And yet, although you left out of love, it is the absence that weighs on you, and it weighs very often as if it were a betrayal.

Perhaps you recognize the guilt in concrete moments. It is the video call in which your small child cries and you hang up and are left shattered, alone, in a room far away, with no one to hold you. It is the birthday you spend working while someone else sings happy birthday to him. It is the first word, the first step, the first day of school, that they tell you over the phone and that you didn't see. It is the return, on a visit, in which your child is different, has grown, and you feel that you've lost a piece of him that doesn't come back. It is, sometimes, the worst of all, the strangeness in the eyes of a child who takes a while to recognize you, or who calls "mother" the one who stayed to care for him. Each of these moments is a stab, and you have collected many, and you carry them all, kept quiet, because to whom would you say them, and who would understand that they hurt even having been done out of love?

And there is a deep reason why this guilt won't go away no matter how much you're told, and you yourself already know, that you are not to blame. It is that guilt, very often, is not an error of accounting, it is a disguised bond. Beneath it lives a sentence you never said but that your unconscious repeats: "if I go on suffering for you, I stay bound to you," "if I blame myself for the absence, perhaps I can repair what was missing." Do you see the trap? To let go of the guilt may seem, deep down, like abandoning your child again, this time in your own peace, as if having peace were a second absence, a betrayal of the time when you were far away. That is why the consolation doesn't arrive. That is why the guilt resists every argument. Because a part of you clings to it as to the last bridge over the distance, as if to suffer were the way of going on being present in what you were absent from. But your child doesn't need you to suffer in order to know that you loved him. Your guilt doesn't recover the years, doesn't fill the absence, doesn't repair anything. What binds you to him is not your pain. It is your love, and that was always there, even from afar, and it doesn't need to hurt in order to be true.

Because that is what guilt does: it reads your absence not as what it was, a sacrifice, but as abandonment. It tells you that you fell short, that you weren't there, that you lost the years that don't come back, that others saw what you didn't see, that your children grew up with your absence marked on them. And that voice has a part of truth, because there really was absence, there really were things that were missing, and this book is not going to pretend there weren't. But guilt takes that part of truth and erases the other, the part of the love that made you leave, and it keeps only the accusation, and it hammers you with it, day after day, year after year.

I want to tell you, before we go on, that this book has not come to give you the right against your children, nor to give your children the right against you. It has not come to decide who is right, because the truth of this pain, and we'll get there slowly, is that you are both right at the same time. You sacrificed yourself, and it was real. Your children felt your absence, and it was real. Both things are true, and neither cancels the other, and the suffering, very often, is born precisely from trying to make one of them silence the other, as if there could only be one truth, yours or theirs.

And I want to tell you, first of all, that I see the immense loneliness there is in this form of motherhood, one that so few see. The mother who raises from afar is doubly alone: far from her children, and very often far from everything she knew, in a strange place, working a great deal, without a network, swallowing the longing alone so as not to worry those who stayed. You sent good news and kept the tears. You worked on days of celebration. You got sick far from those who knew you. And all of that for them, always for them, without anyone often asking how you were, alone, on the other side. That loneliness deserves to be named and honored. Your sacrifice was not only the money you sent, it was the whole life you lived far away, the longing you carried every day, your own existence put between parentheses so that theirs could grow. That is immense, and it is rarely recognized, and I want you to know that I recognize it, and that you too can recognize it, in yourself, without shame.

What I've come to do is to help you set down, at last, that suitcase. To look at what you did with eyes that are not only the eyes of guilt. To separate the real absence, which there was, from the betrayal there was not. And to find a way of carrying your whole story, with what it had of sacrifice and what it had of loss, without your having to choose between defending yourself and condemning yourself. Because there is a kind of peace that does not pass through proving you were right nor through accepting that you were wrong. It passes through honoring, at the same time, everything you gave and everything that was missing. And it is that we are going to seek, together, throughout these pages.

## 2. It was not flight, it was provision

I want to begin by undoing an accusation that perhaps you make against yourself, or that perhaps was made against you, and that is profoundly unjust: the accusation that leaving was a way of fleeing, of being rid of responsibility, of choosing your life instead of your children. It was not. And it is important to see this clearly, because it changes the way your whole story is told.

In the way I have learned to look at families, there is an idea that matters here. Sometimes, a person occupies, in another's life, a place that is not theirs, leaves their post, flees from their responsibilities. That is not your case. You did not leave your place of mother. On the contrary, it was precisely to fulfill your place of mother, the place of the one who provides, who sustains, who guarantees the children's future, that you had to leave. Life put you before an impossible choice: either you stayed near and had no way to provide, or you left and provided without being able to be near. And you chose to provide. You occupied exactly the place life demanded of you, in the only way it allowed you to occupy it.

And it is important to distinguish you from an image that perhaps was thrown at you, or that you yourself fear: the image of the father or the mother who leaves and forgets, who rebuilds their life and truly abandons those who stayed. You are not that, and the proof is precisely in the pain you carry. Whoever abandons doesn't suffer from longing for years, doesn't send everything they can, doesn't count the days until the visit, doesn't carry any guilt, because they stopped caring. Your guilt, however much it weighs on you, is the living proof that you never abandoned, that you were present in everything you could be present in even in the physical absence, that your heart never left, only your body had to go. There is an abyssal difference between being physically far away and abandoning, and you, who were far away, never abandoned. To confuse the two things is one of the greatest injustices you commit against yourself.

So there is not, in your story, what could be called a flight, a betrayal of your post. There is the opposite: a fidelity to your post taken to the extreme of sacrifice, to the point of giving up your own presence, your own wish to be with your children, in order to be able to fulfill the duty of providing for them. What you did was one of the hardest things there is, and it was done out of love and out of responsibility, not out of selfishness. Whoever flees does not sacrifice themselves. And you sacrificed yourself, whole.

But then, if there was no flight, if you occupied the right place, where is the knot? Because there is a knot, there is a pain, and this book is not going to pretend there isn't. The knot is not that you failed your duty. The knot is subtler and more painful: it is that what you gave, however real and however valuable it was, was not exactly what your child most needed from you. You gave provision, and the provision was necessary and was love. But your child, being a child, needed also, and perhaps above all, presence, holding, having you there. And those two things, in those circumstances, did not fit together. You had to choose one, the one that guaranteed survival and the future, and the choice cost the other.

I want also to name the context in which many of these choices are made, because it matters and it is rarely said with respect. For many women, leaving to provide for their children was not even a choice between comfortable options, it was the only door life left open. Where there is no work that pays, where there is no network that holds, where staying would mean having no way to feed those you love, leaving is not a preference, it is a necessity imposed by circumstances greater than you, by the economy, by the lack, by the history of an entire region. You did not create those circumstances. You were obliged to respond to them, and you responded in the most courageous way, putting your children's future ahead of your own heart. There is a greater injustice behind your pain, a social and economic injustice that put so many mothers in the impossible position of having to choose between being there and providing. Part of your guilt belongs, in truth, to a world that gave you such hard options, and not to you, who only did what you could within them.

See the difference, because it frees you of half the weight. You should not blame yourself for having failed, because you didn't fail, you fulfilled, and you fulfilled in the hardest way. What there is to look at is not a guilt over betrayal, it is a pain over an impossible choice, in which to win on one side meant to lose on the other, without there being an option that cost nothing. That pain deserves to be felt. But it is not the same thing as guilt, and to treat it as guilt, to condemn yourself as if you had fled, is an injustice you commit against yourself, and one that this book has come to help repair.

## 3. What you gave and what was missing

Now we are going to look head on at the two truths, and we are going to place them side by side, without letting one silence the other. Because it is in the meeting of these two truths that all the suffering dwells, and it is in letting them coexist that all the possible peace dwells.

Before we look at the two lists, I want to ask you one thing: that you make them not to defend yourself nor to accuse yourself, but to see yourself truthfully. Guilt wants you to look only at what was missing. Wounded pride wants you to look only at what you gave. Truth asks you to look at both, with the same honesty, without inflating either or erasing either. It is not a trial, it is an inventory, made with tenderness, of your real story. And the goal is not to reach a verdict on whether you were a good or a bad mother, that verdict is a trap that leads nowhere. The goal is to see your whole story, with what it had of light and of shadow, and from there to be able to carry it with peace, instead of carrying it as a permanent accusation against yourself.

On one side is what you gave. And it is much, and it is real, and it deserves to be named without shame and without guilt devaluing it. You gave provision: food on the table, clothes on the body, a safe roof. You gave school, the possibility of learning, of having a future that you perhaps didn't have. You gave the possibility of dreaming of more than survival. Every euro, every metical you sent, every hour you worked far away, was a concrete act of love, was care in the form of sacrifice. Your children have, in their lives, things that exist only because you left and worked and deprived yourself. That is yours, that you gave, and no one can take it from you nor diminish it.

And there is something that may give you some comfort about what you gave, though it doesn't erase what was missing: the provision you sent was not only money, it was possibility. It was the possibility for your children to study instead of having to work early, to have health instead of untreated illness, to dream of a life instead of merely surviving. Many of the things your children are today, of the doors that opened for them, of the choices they were able to make, exist because you, from afar, made them possible. That is not little, and it is not abstract, it is in their concrete lives, in what they were able to be. One day, perhaps, they will understand this better than they understand it now, above all if they come to be parents and to face their own impossible choices. But, whether they understand or not, the truth remains: what you sent from afar built, in their lives, possibilities that otherwise would not exist.

On the other side is what was missing. And it too is real, and it too deserves to be named, without your sacrifice erasing it. Presence was missing: you weren't there on many days, on many nights, in many moments that don't come back. Holding was missing in the hours when they needed it and you were far away. The everyday was missing, the small things of every day that build a connection, and that are not recovered with spaced-out visits nor with calls. The present mother was missing, for them, physically there, that other children had. And that hurt them, and may still hurt, and it is legitimate that it hurt, however much you left out of love.

And I want you to see how, very often, these two truths live each on its own side of the relationship, without meeting. You carry the truth of the sacrifice, and your children carry the truth of the lack, and when they meet, instead of hearing each other, they defend themselves. You want them to recognize what you gave, and they want you to recognize what was missing, and each one, in defending their truth, feels that the other's threatens it. And so years can go by, perhaps, in a painful estrangement, you hurt because they don't value your sacrifice, they hurt because you don't recognize the lack they felt, without either being able to take the first step of saying: you're right, and so am I. Because that is what is missing, very often, and what changes everything when it appears: the capacity of each one to recognize the other's truth without feeling that they are losing their own. The two truths are not at war. They were put at war by the pain, but in reality they fit together, side by side, and the day they meet without attacking each other is the day reconciliation becomes possible.

And here is the heart of this pain: these two truths are the two of them true, at the same time. It is not that your sacrifice makes the lack less real for your children. It is not that the lack they felt makes your sacrifice less loving. Both things happened, both are true, and the suffering is born when they meet and you feel that you have to choose one. When you defend yourself with the sacrifice and erase their pain, or when you accept their pain and erase your sacrifice. But you don't have to choose. You can hold both, and it is precisely holding both, without letting either cancel the other, that is the path.

And there is something important about what was missing, that needs to be said with care: to recognize the lack your children felt is not to accept that you were a bad mother, it is only to be truthful about what a child feels when the mother is far away. A small child doesn't understand economics, doesn't understand sacrifice, doesn't understand future. He understands presence and absence. And so, however noble your reason was, what the child lived was your absence, and that experience of his is real and legitimate, even though it is not a judgment on your worth. To be able to recognize this, without feeling accused, is a sign of strength and of love, not of guilt. It means that you can look at your child's experience with his eyes, and not only with your need to defend yourself. And it is precisely that capacity to see his pain without crumbling that will allow, later, the true meeting between you.

Notice that the temptation to rank is enormous, on both sides. There are those who tell you, or there is a voice in you that says, "you did everything for them, they have no right to complain," and that erases their pain. And there are those who say, or there is a voice that says, "you were absent, you failed as a mother," and that erases your sacrifice. The two voices are unjust, because the two try to make one truth the only truth. The whole truth, the hardest to bear but the only one that frees, is that you gave much and much was missing, at the same time, and that both things can be said without your having to defend yourself from one in order to honor the other.

## 4. The identity of sacrifice

I want now to look at something that is not about what you did, but about who you became on the inside over all these years. Because sacrifice, when it lasts years, stops being only a thing we do and becomes a way of being. You became the one who sacrificed herself. The one who left. The one who endured the longing. The one who sent everything she could. The one who renounced her own life for her children.

This form of motherhood has, in our region and in so many others, a collective dimension that matters to name, because it takes you out of isolation. You are not alone in this story. There are generations of women, and of men too, who left to provide for those they loved, who raised children from afar, who sent from far away what wasn't there. It is one of the oldest and most common stories of the places from which life forces people to leave in order to survive. Your mothers, your grandmothers, your neighbors, so many know this pain from within. This doesn't diminish your suffering, but it gives it company, and it takes away the shame, because what we share with so many stops being a personal failure and becomes a wound of a people, of a time, of a greater circumstance. You belong to a long line of mothers who loved from afar because life gave them no other way of loving and providing at the same time. And there is dignity in that line, not shame.

And for a long time, that identity served you, and served you well. It gave meaning to your suffering. Because the longing, the distance, the loneliness of raising from afar, are almost unbearable, and what made them bearable was precisely knowing that it had a purpose, that it was for them, that your sacrifice was worth it. The identity of the one who sacrifices herself gave you ground in the hardest years: each deprivation of yours had a meaning, each tear had a reason, everything was justified by the good of your children. Without that identity, perhaps you would not have endured. It was your strength.

But there is a problem that appears with time, and it is subtle. When the children grow up, when the sacrifice has already been made, when the hardest phase passes, the identity of sacrifice does not disappear with it. It stays. And it goes on needing to justify itself, needing to prove it was worth it, needing the recognition of the children in order to feel confirmed. There is a particular trap in the identity of sacrifice, and it is important to see it: it can make you confuse love with suffering, to the point where it seems to you that you only loved because you suffered, and that if you hadn't suffered so much, you wouldn't have loved so much. This confusion is dangerous, because it binds you to valuing suffering as proof of love, and to distrusting any relief, any joy, any lightness, as if they betrayed the love. But love is not measured by suffering. A mother who could have given everything to her children without suffering would not love them less for it. Your suffering was not the measure of your love, it was the price that the circumstances charged your love. And now that that price has already been paid, you can stop charging it to yourself, you can allow yourself the lightness and the joy without feeling that you are betraying what you suffered. Love doesn't need suffering in order to be real. It was already real before it hurt, and it goes on being real when the pain sets itself down.

And it is here that it begins to hurt in a new way: because you need your children to recognize your sacrifice, to say that it was worth it, to thank you, and they don't always do it, or they don't do it in the way you needed, because they have their own truth, the truth of the lack they felt, and that truth doesn't always fit in the thanks you expect.

There is an added weight that this identity carries, and that touches your relationship with life itself. When someone becomes the one who sacrifices herself, she learns, without meaning to, that she has no right to live for herself, that her life is worth only while it serves others, that to enjoy, to rest, to want something only for herself, is almost a betrayal of the sacrifice. And so, even after the children grow up, even when you could already live a little for yourself, there is a voice that won't let you, that says it isn't the time, that there is still more to give, that to enjoy life would be to waste the meaning of the sacrifice. The sacrificial identity doesn't only bind you to the need to prove it was worth it. It binds you also to a life of continual renunciation, in which your turn never comes, because you learned that your turn is selfishness. To free yourself from it is also to give yourself, at last, permission to live, to receive, to enjoy what you built, without that seeming to you a betrayal of everything you sacrificed.

And I want to tell you something carefully, because it is delicate. The identity of sacrifice, however much it was born of love, can become a prison. It can bind you to the need to always be right, to always prove that what you did was correct, to be unable to bear that your children feel the lack without that seeming to you an accusation. And the more you cling to that identity, to that need for them to recognize your sacrifice, the harder the true meeting with your children becomes, because they feel they can't say their pain without wounding you, and you feel you can't hear their pain without crumbling.

There is, therefore, a work to be done, which is not to deny the sacrifice, never, but to loosen the need for it to be the only thing that defines who you are, and the need for others to validate it in order for you to be at peace. Because you are more than the woman who sacrificed herself. The sacrifice was an enormous part of your story, but it is not your whole identity, and to free yourself from having to be, forever, the one who sacrificed herself, is to free yourself also from the weight of having to prove, at all times, that it was worth it.

## 5. Who am I without proving it was worth it

I'm going to let fall here the realization that changes everything, and I ask you to keep it, even if it costs.

Your love is not measured only by what you suffered. And you are more than the woman who sacrificed her own life.

Stay with this a moment.

For years, your suffering was the proof of your love. The more you suffered, the more you loved, that was the equation that sustained you. And there is a truth in that, your sacrifice was in fact love. But, without your noticing, that equation became a trap: you came to need to prove that you suffered, that it was worth it, that the sacrifice was justified, as if your love were only real if it were measured in suffering and in results. And that need to prove became so strong that it no longer lets you exist outside of it. Your whole relationship with your children, with your own story, came to revolve around one question: was it worth it? And that question, repeated without end, doesn't leave you in peace.

There is a cruel way in which this need to prove shows itself in the everyday: you turn everything your children do or say into an answer to the question "was it worth it?". If a child prospers, you feel relief, it was worth it. If a child struggles, you feel that perhaps it wasn't worth it, that the sacrifice was in vain. If they thank you, you breathe. If they complain of the lack, you collapse. Your peace thus becomes hostage to your children's lives and to what they say, and that is a terrible place to live, because you control neither their lives nor what they feel. As long as you need your children's lives to prove that your sacrifice was worth it, you will never have peace, because their lives, like any life, will always have lights and shadows, and you will read each shadow as a verdict against your sacrifice. To free yourself from this reading, to let your children's lives be theirs and not the proof of your worth, is one of the most important liberations of this crossing.

Notice what lies beneath this need to prove. There is the fear that, if it wasn't worth it, then the sacrifice was in vain, and all the pain was useless, and you lost the years with your children for nothing. It is a terrifying fear, and it's understandable that you do everything to avoid it, that you need the results to justify the sacrifice, that your children confirm that it was worth it. But there is a prison in this: as long as the meaning of your suffering depends on proving that it was worth it, you stay hostage to results, hostage to your children's recognition, hostage to a validation that may never come in the form you need.

And here is the question that frees, the turning-point question of this book: who am I when I no longer need to prove that it was worth it? Who am I beyond the one who sacrificed herself, beyond the one who has to demonstrate that the sacrifice was justified? If you let go of that need, if you stopped measuring your worth by suffering and by results, who would you be left being? This question may open a vertigo, because for so long you've defined yourself by the sacrifice that perhaps you no longer know who you are without it. But it is precisely in that vertigo that your liberation dwells: the possibility of existing as a person, and not as living proof that a sacrifice was worth it.

I want to give you an image that may help. Imagine that your sacrifice was a bridge you built, with your own hands, over a river, so that your children could cross to the other bank, where there was a future. You built it with years of your life, with longing, with deprivation. And your children crossed, and they are on the other bank, which is where you wanted them to arrive. But you, for years, stayed looking at the bridge, asking at all hours whether it was worth having built it, waiting for those who crossed to turn back and say yes. The question "was it worth it" is looking at the bridge. There is another way of looking: at the bank, at where your children arrived, at the life they were able to have. The bridge was for them to cross, not for you to stay eternally contemplating it waiting for recognition. When you stop looking at the bridge and look at the bank where they arrived, you stop needing to prove it was worth it, because you see, there, the answer being lived: they arrived, and that was what you always wanted.

I have to stop here and tell you, carefully, that this liberation is not done in a day, because an identity built over years of sacrifice is not loosened with one sentence. And if, in looking at this question, you feel that beneath it there is a very deep sadness, an emptiness, a sense that without the sacrifice your life had no meaning, or if there is a pain that doesn't pass, then that deserves to be accompanied, not crossed alone. To seek support, someone who helps you make this crossing, is not weakness, it is giving yourself the care that for years you only gave to others. You deserve that care, as much as they deserved yours.

## 6. Honoring both sides

We arrive at the verb of this book, and it is a beautiful and difficult verb: to honor. To honor what you gave and to honor what was missing, at the same time, without obliging anyone, neither yourself nor your children, to choose a side.

For many mothers, the hardest thing to honor is not what they gave, it is to let their children's pain exist without feeling it as an accusation. When a child says "I felt your absence," or "you weren't there," your first impulse is to defend yourself, to explain, to remind them of everything you did, because you hear in that an accusation that you failed. But what if it weren't an accusation? What if your child were not saying "you are a bad mother," but only "this hurt me"? They are very different things. He can recognize and even thank you for your sacrifice, and at the same time need to say that the lack hurt him, and those two things, in him, also coexist. When you manage to hear his pain without immediately turning it into an accusation against yourself, you give him an enormous gift: the gift of being able to feel what he felt without having to hide it to protect you. And you give yourself another: the gift of being able to hear without crumbling, because you no longer need his pain not to exist in order for your love to be real.

To honor is different from to defend and is different from to confess. To defend would be to insist only on your sacrifice, to prove that you did what was right, to silence their pain in the name of your love. To confess, in the sense of blaming yourself, would be to take on only the lack, to accept the accusation of abandonment, to silence your sacrifice in the name of their pain. To honor is another thing, it is greater than the two: it is to recognize, with dignity and without shame, the two whole truths. To honor what you gave: to say, without guilt, "I left out of love, I provided for you, I sacrificed myself, and that was real and was love." And to honor what was missing: to say, without defense, "and at the same time I wasn't there, and you felt my absence, and that pain is legitimate and I recognize it."

This way of holding two truths at the same time, without letting one cancel the other, is one of the hardest things the human mind does, and it's worth knowing that it is hard, so you don't demand of yourself that it be easy. We are used to thinking in terms of either this or that: either you're right or you're guilty, either you were a good mother or you were absent, either it was worth it or it was in vain. Real life, however, is almost never like that, it is almost always both things at the same time, and maturity, the true kind, is the capacity to bear that complexity without reducing it to a single side. So, if it costs you to hold both truths, if your mind always wants to choose one and discard the other, it is not a defect of yours, it is the way we were trained to think. To hold both is a learning, it is trained, and each time you resist the temptation to choose a side and you stay with the whole truth, you become a little freer, and a little more at peace.

The sentence that holds the heart of this, and that is perhaps the most important of this book, is this: my sacrifice does not erase your pain, and your pain does not erase my love. Notice how it holds the two truths without letting either win. It doesn't say "I sacrificed myself, so you can't complain." It doesn't say "you suffered, so I failed." It says both things, side by side, and lets them coexist. And it is in that coexistence that the war ends, the war you wage inside yourself, and the war that is sometimes waged between you and your children, each defending their truth as if it could only exist by erasing the other's.

To honor has yet another dimension that many mothers forget: to honor yourself, the woman who did all this. You spent so long looking at what was missing for your children that perhaps you never stopped to honor what it cost you. The courage to leave. The strength to endure the longing. The discipline to work and to send and to deprive yourself. The loneliness borne in silence. All of this were acts of an enormous greatness, and you deserve to recognize them in yourself, with pride, without that pride erasing the pain there was. To honor yourself is not arrogance nor is it to deny what was missing. It is to do justice to a woman who gave of herself to the limit, and who for years judged herself only by what she didn't manage to give, without ever crediting herself for the great deal she gave. Look at yourself, at everything you carried, and allow yourself, at last, a little pride. It is deserved, and it takes nothing from anyone.

When you honor both sides, a freeing thing happens: you stop needing to prove that you were a heroine, and your children stop needing to prove that they were victims. You don't have to be the perfect mother who sacrificed herself without anything being missing, and they don't have to be the abandoned children who recognize nothing of what you gave. You can all be what you in fact were: a mother who gave what she could, in the hardest way, and children who received much and felt the lack of something else. No one is the villain. No one is only a victim. You are real people, in a real story, made of love and of loss at the same time.

And I want to tell you something important about reconciliation, because it may relieve you. To honor both sides does not oblige you to a reconciliation with your children, if it is not possible, or if they are not ready, or if the relationship is complicated. The reconciliation this book speaks of is, above all, a reconciliation with what was lived, with your own story, within you. Whether or not your children come to recognize your sacrifice, whether or not you come to an understanding, that is not only in your hands. But the peace with your own story, that you can make, and it does not depend on their making it with you. You can honor what you gave and what was missing within yourself, and find peace, even if the meeting with them is not yet possible.

## 7. Reconciling with your own story

If you've come this far, you've already seen much: that it was not flight but provision, that you gave much and much was missing at the same time, that the identity of sacrifice bound you, and that honoring both sides is the path. Now I want to show you the destination of this crossing, in broad strokes. Not the step by step, which is done slowly and sometimes with company. But the direction: the reconciliation with your own story.

To reconcile with your story is to find a way of looking at it whole, with what it had of good and of painful, without absolving yourself entirely nor condemning yourself forever. It is to be able to say, with truth and with peace: I did the best I could with what I had, in the circumstances I had. This sentence is not an excuse nor a victory. It is a humble and profound truth. It doesn't say you did everything perfectly, nor that there were no losses. It says that, within the real options that life gave you, which were few and hard, you chose with love, and you did what was within your reach. And that, for a human being, is all that can be asked, and it is enough, even when it wasn't perfect.

To reconcile with your story includes, very often, reconciling with a version of yourself that made that decision without knowing all you know today. The woman who packed the suitcase, on that day, did not know what you would feel, did not know what would be missing, did not have the perspective the years have given you. She decided with the information, the fear, and the hope she had in that moment, and she decided the best she could. It is unjust to judge her with the knowledge that only came afterward, to condemn her for not having foreseen what was unforeseeable. Look at that younger woman, the one who left, with the compassion with which you would look at a friend before an impossible choice. She does not deserve your tribunal. She deserves your tenderness, because she carried, alone and frightened, one of the hardest decisions a mother can make, and she carried it out of love. To forgive her, or rather, to understand her and embrace her, is part of reconciling with your own story.

And here I have to warn you of a trap, because it is very tempting and does harm. It is the temptation to give the suffering a meaning too great, to glorify it, to say that everything happened as it had to happen, that the sacrifice was a blessing, that all suffering has a purpose. This temptation seems comforting, but it is dangerous, because it erases the real pain, yours and your children's. If you say that the suffering was a blessing, you can no longer weep for what it cost. If you say that everything was as it had to be, you can no longer recognize what was missing. The glorification of suffering is a way of fleeing the pain, and the pain that is not felt is not cured, it stays.

Notice how the glorification and the guilt, which seem opposites, are in truth two flights from the same thing: from the painful complexity of what was real. The guilt flees to one side, "I did everything wrong, I abandoned them." The glorification flees to the other, "it was all perfect, a blessing, worth every tear." The two simplify, the two lie by half, the two avoid the discomfort of staying with the whole truth, which is harder: you gave much and much was missing, it was love and it was loss, you did well and it hurt all the same. To stay in this whole truth, without fleeing either to guilt or to glorification, is the most mature and most difficult work, and it is the only one that gives true peace, because the peace that comes from a lie, whether the lie of guilt or of glory, doesn't last, the truth always stays underneath pressing. Only the peace that rests on the whole truth is firm, because it has nothing underneath to contradict it.

The right point, the difficult and the true, is this: the sacrifice doesn't need to be romanticized in order to be honored, and the fact that it hurt doesn't mean that it was useless. Notice the two halves. You don't need to turn your suffering into a blessing, into a marvelous lesson, into a divine purpose, in order to respect it. You can honor it simply as what it was: an act of love that cost much. And the fact that it cost, that there were real losses, doesn't cancel its worth, doesn't make it useless. You gave a future to your children, and that was real and valuable, and at the same time it cost presence, and that was a real loss. The two things, once again, side by side, without the meaning erasing the pain nor the pain erasing the meaning.

And I want to tell you something about the time you still have, because the reconciliation is not only with the past, it is also an opening to the future. However much was missing, the relationship with your children is not closed, it did not end in what was lost. There is, almost always, still time to build what was missing in another way, not to recover the childhood that doesn't come back, but to create an adult relationship, present, new, that doesn't erase the past but that adds to it a present. Many mothers who raised from afar discover, later, a connection with their adult children that heals, in part, what the distance opened, not because the past is undone, but because the present also counts, and a true present, of listening, of being there, can be a balm over the old absence. You don't control whether your children will want or be able to do this. But, for your part, you can be available, present now, and that presence of today is, itself, a way of honoring what was missing: not with guilt, but with what you can still give.

To reconcile is to live with this complexity without needing to simplify it. It is to carry your whole story, with pride for what you gave and with mourning for what was missing, without having to choose between the two. It is to stop being at war with your past, and at war with yourself, and to find a peace that is not the peace of someone who proved she was right, but the peace, deeper, of someone who accepts her own story just as it was, complex, loving, painful, human. That peace does not depend on anyone else, neither on your children, nor on a verdict about whether it was worth it. It depends only on your reconciling with the woman who did what she could, and on your looking at her, at last, with tenderness.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change the past, doesn't undo the absences, doesn't recover the years. But it changes the way you carry your story, and that changes everything else.

And I want you to take with you a truth about your love, because it is worth more than any verdict about the past. Love is not only in what was missing nor only in what you gave. It is, above all, in the fact that you carried all of this, all these years, precisely because you love. A mother who didn't love would not have sacrificed herself, would not carry this guilt, would not be now reading a book in search of peace for a pain that comes from wanting to have done more and better for her children. Your whole pain, from beginning to end, is made of love. The guilt you feel is love. The longing you carried is love. The sacrifice you made is love. Even what was missing, was missing because love forced you to an impossible choice. Whatever the accounts you make of your story, there is one that was never in doubt: you loved, you love, and you loved the whole time, even from afar, even in the lack, even in the guilt. And that love is the deepest truth of your whole story.

Now you know that it was not flight, it was provision, and that you occupied the place life demanded of you. You know how to place side by side what you gave and what was missing, without letting one truth silence the other. You know how to recognize the identity of sacrifice, and how it bound you to the need to prove that it was worth it. You know the question that frees: who am I when I no longer need to prove? You know that honoring both sides is the path, and that the most important reconciliation is with your own story. And you know how to avoid the trap of glorifying suffering, honoring it without romanticizing it.

And you know, too, that the guilt you carried all these years was not justice, it was only love that found nowhere to set itself down. A love so great that, having been unable to be presence, it turned into guilt, because guilt was the only way that love had of going on hurting for the children from afar. Now that love can find another place to set itself down besides guilt: it can set itself down in peace, in reconciliation, in the present you can still build, in the serene pride for everything you gave. The guilt fulfilled its time, it was the form of your love suffering for the distance. You can let it go now, and let the love it hid show itself, at last, without the weight of the accusation.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: my sacrifice does not erase your pain, and your pain does not erase my love.

And perhaps, deep down, the sentence where the war ends is beginning to make sense: I can honor what I gave without denying what was missing.

To understand changed the weight you carried. But the deep reconciliation does not fit in a page read: it is made by crossing, slowly, what was lived.

It was for that crossing that I created **FreeMe**.

FreeMe is not a course to study nor one more reading. It is a crossing to do from the inside. It is a guided crossing that you do from within, at your own pace, to reconcile with your own story and to set down the weight you've carried for so long. For a mother like you, who left out of love and carries the absence as betrayal, there is a path designed for your pain: the guilt that confuses sacrifice with abandonment, the identity that binds you to proving it was worth it, and the peace of honoring, at the same time, everything you gave and everything that was missing. Not to absolve you nor to condemn you. To reconcile you with the woman who did what she could with what she had.

And I want you to know that this crossing, being yours, can also touch your children, even without their doing it with you. When a mother reconciles with her own story, when she sets down the guilt and finds peace, something changes in the way she is with her children, and the children feel it, even without knowing why. You stop being on the defensive, you stop charging for recognition, you stop turning every conversation into an accounting of whether it was worth it. And that change in you opens room for them, free of the weight of having to validate you, to be able to draw near in another way, perhaps even to recognize, of their own will, what before they couldn't say in the face of your demand. Your inner peace is not only for you. It is, very often, the key that unlocks, from your side, a meeting that the guilt and the defense kept closed.

There, the sentences you read here in silence are spoken slowly, with room for the body to set them down. "I fulfilled what was mine to fulfill, in the way life allowed me." "I did the best I could with what I had." "I can honor what I gave without denying what was missing." What here was a map, there becomes a path crossed. To honor stops being an idea and becomes a gesture. And your story stops being a tribunal and begins to be able to be, at last, simply your story, carried with peace.

If you feel this is the moment, I'm there. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't the moment now, that's all right. What you've read stays with you, and the path of reconciliation doesn't flee: it will be here when you're ready.

And I want to leave you with a word about peace, because it is what this book, at bottom, wishes for you. The peace you seek will not arrive on the day your children finally recognize your sacrifice, because that peace would depend on them, and you have already handed over too much of your life to the dependence on what comes from others. The peace that can truly arrive is another: the peace of reconciling with yourself, of looking at your whole story and embracing it, of setting down the suitcase and the guilt that traveled in it for years. That peace doesn't need anyone to confirm it. It is made within you, in the way you come to look at the woman who left out of love and carried everything she carried. And when that inner peace arrives, a curious thing often happens: you stop needing your children's recognition so much, and it is precisely then, when you no longer demand it, that it becomes more possible, because the meeting stops carrying the weight of a demand and comes to have the lightness of someone already at peace.

But before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing. Today, say to yourself, out loud if you can, the two truths together, without letting either silence the other: I gave much, and much was missing, and both things are true, and I always loved. And notice what happens inside you when you hold both at the same time, without choosing. Because it is there, in that coexistence that costs and that frees, that the war inside you begins, at last, to be able to end.

With all my tenderness,
