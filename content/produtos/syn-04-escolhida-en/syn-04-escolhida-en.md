# The woman who waits to be chosen

**For you, who wait for life and love to choose you. Because to wait is to hand your life over to the decisions of others, and you can choose, not only wait.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a way of living and of loving in which one waits to be chosen instead of choosing, and there is a point at which this passivity stops being a temperament and becomes an entire life handed over to the decisions of others. If you recognize in yourself an inability to act on behalf of your life, a deep sadness that won't pass, or a sense of powerlessness over your own destiny, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. Waiting to be wanted

Notice something, honestly.

You wait.

You wait for him to decide. To want you. To take the step. To choose you.

In love, you're rarely the one who advances. You wait for them to advance toward you. You position yourself, you show yourself, you stay available, and you wait for someone to notice, to want, to choose.

And when they don't choose, you don't think "I didn't choose." You think "I wasn't chosen." As if your love life were a line where you wait, patient, for someone to take you out of it.

Notice that this isn't only in love. It's in everything.

You wait for the opportunity to come knocking at your door, instead of going to get it. You wait for them to recognize your worth, instead of affirming it. You wait for life to happen to you, instead of making it happen.

You're good at positioning yourself. At preparing yourself. At being ready for when they choose you. But the act of choosing, you, that one you lack.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this isn't a lack of worth. You don't wait because you find yourself too little. You wait because you learned to wait.

You learned to be chosen, not to choose. To be desired, not to desire. To be the object of others' decisions, not the subject of your own.

This book will not teach you to seduce better, to position yourself better, to make yourself more choosable. That's more of the same, it's perfecting the waiting.

I came to show you something else. That while you wait to be chosen, you hand your life over to the decisions of others. And that there's another place, the place of the one who decides, of the one who chooses, of the one who desires out loud, and that this place, however much it frightens you, is yours.

I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can choose, not only wait. You can go from the one who waits to be taken out of the line to the one who decides where she's going. And what holds you in the waiting is not a lack of worth. It's fear. Fear of something we're going to look at head-on.

Maybe you'll recognize yourself in small things.

In liking someone and never taking the first step, waiting, sometimes for years, for it to be him.

In staying in relationships you didn't truly choose, only because they were the ones who chose you, and being chosen was enough.

In leaving important decisions of your life in the hands of others, and then living the result as if it had befallen you, and not as something you chose.

In dreaming of a different life, and waiting for it to happen to you, instead of going to build it.

They are all the same. The waiting. The place of the one who waits to be chosen, instead of choosing.

Notice something you may never have noticed: your relationship with the word "want." For many people, wanting is natural, it's the engine of life. For you, wanting out loud is almost unseemly. As if there were, in affirmed desire, something improper, too much, hardly worthy of a woman who learned to be chosen and not to choose.

And so you soften everything. You don't say "I want," you say "maybe I'd like," "perhaps it would be good," "I don't know, you decide." You put your desire in the conditional, in the diminutive, in the question, so as never to affirm it entirely, so as never to expose yourself to being refused in a clear want.

Notice too the strange relief you feel when the decision is taken out of your hands. When someone decides for you, when the circumstances choose, when there's nothing left to do but accept what came. There's, in that moment, a rest, because you're freed from the responsibility of having wanted. And that relief, repeated, is the sign of how much deciding weighs on you, and of how much you got used to handing your wanting over to everything except yourself.

I want you to notice something that distinguishes waiting from trusting, because your waiting disguises itself as trust and isn't.

To trust is to let happen what doesn't depend on you, while keeping your hands on what does. It's planting the seed and trusting that it grows, but planting, watering, caring. To wait, the way you do, is not to plant, and to stay waiting for the harvest to appear. One is active on the inside, calm on the outside. The other is passive on the inside and on the outside.

And there's a detail that reveals everything. You know very well what others want, and very badly what you want.

You read the desire of others with an impressive precision. You know what he would like, what would please, what would make you be chosen. But if they ask you what you want, there's a silence. A blank. A hesitation.

Because you spent your life turned toward the wanting of others, waiting to satisfy it and be, for that, chosen. And you never cultivated your own wanting, which was left undeveloped, like a muscle that was never used.

And you can't choose without knowing what you want. So the waiting isn't only fear. It's also not knowing what you'd choose, if you chose. Your own desire is yet to be discovered, buried beneath an entire life of feeling the desire of others.

Notice, too, what you do instead of choosing. You position yourself. You invest in seeming desirable, in being ready, in being the best version of yourself, for when someone looks. And that seems like effort, seems like initiative. But notice where all that effort points: outward, toward the gaze of the other, toward the moment when you'll be chosen. Never toward your own wanting.

It's an enormous effort in the service of waiting. You work hard to be a good candidate. You don't work at all to decide what you, truly, want. You perfect the way you're seen, and leave undeveloped the one thing that would take you out of the line: your own will.

And there's a sadness in this that you may already take for temperament. The sadness of the one who is always ready to be chosen, and is rarely chosen first. Of the one who sees others be desired, invited, decided upon, while she waits, impeccable and invisible, for a turn that is slow to come. That sadness isn't bad luck in love, nor in life. It's the bill for the waiting, which keeps you always available and never in command.

## 2. The place of the one who doesn't decide

There's a place where you settled, and I want to name it. It's the place of the one who doesn't decide.

In that place, life happens to you. You're not the one acting on it, you're the one receiving it, according to what others, chance, the circumstances decide.

It's a passive place. Comfortable, in a way, because it spares you something that frightens you. But it's also a place of powerlessness, because it hands the helm of your life to everyone except you.

Notice how you position yourself, instead of deciding.

You don't say "I want this man, and I'm going after him." You say "I hope he wants me." You don't say "I want this life, and I'm going to build it." You say "I hope this life happens to me." You don't desire out loud. You wait, in silence, to be desired.

And there's an enormous difference between desiring and waiting to be desired.

Whoever desires is a subject. She points, advances, risks, chooses. She's in command of her wanting.

Whoever waits to be desired is an object. She shows herself, and waits for another to point her out, to advance, to choose her. She's in the hands of others' wanting.

You live almost always as an object. Waiting to be pointed out. And you confuse that waiting with modesty, with patience, with a calm temperament, when it's, deep down, a renunciation. The renunciation of your own power to choose.

I want to show you what this costs you.

Whoever waits to be chosen gets what appears. Not what she wants, but what wants her. And those two things are rarely the same.

You end up, then, in relationships, in jobs, in lives, that you didn't choose, that only chose you. And you live with a dull sense that this isn't quite your life, that it befell you, that you're living a script that wasn't yours, without realizing that the passivity with which you waited was, itself, a choice. The choice not to choose.

Because there's a truth that's hard. Not to choose is also a decision. When you wait, and let others decide, you're deciding to hand them your life. The passivity doesn't take you out of the responsibility. It only hides it from you.

I want to show you how the waiting disguises itself as virtue, because that's how it deceives you.

You call it patience. You say you're not the type who runs after things, that you prefer to let things happen, that you trust in time. And there's wisdom in knowing how to wait, sometimes. But your waiting isn't wisdom. It's fear dressed as patience.

You call it humility. You say you don't want to impose yourself, that you don't like to force, that you prefer them to come to you. And there's delicacy in that. But your delicacy, taken this far, is a way of never wanting out loud, so as never to expose yourself to a no.

You call it loyalty to fate. You say that, if it's meant to be, it will be. That what's yours doesn't escape. And that phrase, which can comfort, became your excuse not to act, to leave your life in the hands of chance, of others, of everything except your own.

Notice what the waiting spares you, because that's where its secret is. It spares you the risk of wanting and not receiving. As long as you don't desire out loud, you can't be refused. As long as you don't choose, you can't be wrong. As long as you wait, you stay innocent of everything that doesn't happen, because it wasn't you who failed, it was life that didn't come.

And there's a place where this is seen clearly: in the loves you didn't live. How many men passed through your life who maybe wanted you, and with whom nothing ever happened, because you waited for them to take every step, and they, out of shyness, out of doubt, or because they never noticed your quiet interest, didn't take it? How many trains did you watch leave, from the platform, waiting for them to stop for you, instead of running toward them?

That's the bill for the waiting. And it's a high bill. It's not a life without love because they didn't want you. It's a life with less love than you could have had, because you were never the one to choose, to advance, to say I want you. You always waited for them to choose for you, and you got what appeared, instead of what you wanted.

And it's not only in love. It's at work, where you waited for them to recognize your worth instead of affirming it, and watched rise those who claimed it. It's in dreams, which you postponed waiting for a permission, a sign, a right moment that never came. It's in the whole of life, lived in the position of the one who waits, while the others, the ones who choose, go on making their own.

I want to show you what the place of the one who doesn't decide costs you, concretely, because the price is high and you almost never count it.

It costs you getting what appears, and not what you want. Whoever waits to be chosen doesn't choose whom she loves, she chooses among those who want her. And those who want her and those whom she would want are rarely the same people. That's why you end up, so many times, in relationships you didn't choose, that only chose you, and you live with a dull sense that this isn't quite your life.

It costs you a life that you feel is borrowed. You live a script that wasn't yours, that befell you, and however well you fulfill it, there's an undercurrent of strangeness, the strangeness of someone who is living a life that happened, and not one that she chose. That strangeness has a name: it's the mark of a life lived in the position of object.

It costs you your self-esteem, even if you don't see the connection. Because each time you wait and aren't chosen, you conclude, without deciding to, that there's something in you that isn't enough. And that conclusion, repeated, gradually convinces you that you're worth less, when what failed was not your worth, it was your passivity. You're not passed over for being worth little. You're passed over for never offering yourself to choose, and therefore staying always dependent on their choosing for you.

And it costs you resentment. Because a part of you, deep down, is angry. With those who choose, with those who advance, with those who have what you didn't dare go and get. And that resentment, which you don't know where to direct, is the anger of the one who waits against a world that seems to reward those who decide. But the world doesn't treat you badly. You're the one who stayed waiting, while the others went.

I want you to see all of this not to blame yourself more, but to feel the size of what the waiting has been costing you. Because it's by seeing the price that one gains the courage to stop paying it. And the price of the waiting, added up over a lifetime, is an entire life that you watched happen, instead of having lived.

## 3. Choosing is frightening

I'm going to tell you something that may bother you. You don't wait out of laziness, nor out of a lack of will. You wait because choosing frightens you.

And the fear has a very concrete reason. Choosing makes you responsible.

Notice. When you wait, and the thing goes badly, it's not your fault. It was life, it was him, it was what befell you. You stay protected, because you didn't decide.

When you choose, and the thing goes badly, the responsibility is yours. You're the one who decided. And then there's no one to attribute the error to except yourself.

It's this that you flee. The responsibility of choosing. The weight of being the one who decides, and therefore the one who can be wrong.

The waiting is, deep down, a way of never being able to blame yourself. If you never choose, you never make a mistake. If life happens to you, it's life that made the mistake, not you. You stay innocent, always, at the price of never being the author of anything.

I want you to feel the size of this trade. You trade the power over your life for protection against error. You give up deciding so as not to have to carry your decisions. And the result is a life that isn't yours, in exchange for never having to take responsibility for it.

There's here also an older fear, hidden. The fear of wanting and not receiving.

If you desire out loud, and what you desire is refused you, it hurts in a way that the waiting spares you. Whoever waits is never refused to her face, because she never asks. Whoever desires, and advances, and chooses, exposes herself to direct rejection, and that, for you, may be unbearable.

So you don't desire. You don't ask. You don't advance. And you spare yourself the rejection, at the price of sparing yourself also everything you could have, if you dared to want.

Let me tell you where this usually comes from. Almost always from a place where desiring, as a child, was neither safe nor welcome.

Maybe you learned that wanting caused problems. That having a will of your own was selfishness, or defiance, or a nuisance. That the good child was the one who didn't ask, didn't want, didn't demand, and waited, patient, for what they wished to give her.

Maybe you saw that whoever decided, in your home, took the consequences, and that the safe place was the place of the one who didn't get involved, didn't choose, didn't take responsibility.

You learned, then, that safety was in waiting. That wanting was risky, and waiting was safe. And you carried that into love, where you keep waiting, patient, for them to decide for you, so as never to have to risk your own wanting.

There's a figure you might recognize. A girl taught, with or without words, that a girl doesn't run after. She waits. She doesn't show herself too interested. She doesn't take the initiative. She lets them come to her.

You grew up, maybe, inside that idea. That your role, in love, was to be chosen, and not to choose. That to advance was improper, desperate, undignified. That the woman of worth is the one who is desired, not the one who desires.

And that idea, which seems just an old rule of courtship, was engraved deeper. It became your position in life. The position of the one who waits to be wanted, in every terrain, because wanting, herself, seems to her almost unseemly.

Notice how this imprisons you. If you can only be chosen, then your destiny is always in the hands of those who choose. You stay waiting for the taste of others, hostage to their decision, with no power over who wants you nor when.

And there's a particular sadness in this. The sadness of wanting someone, in silence, and watching time pass, waiting for a step he doesn't take, without daring to take it yourself. The sadness of losing possible loves, not because you didn't want them, but because you waited, patient, for them to guess what you never said.

How many trains did you watch leave, from the side of the road, by waiting for them to stop for you, instead of running toward them? That's the bill for the waiting. And it's a high bill.

I want to go deeper into the fear, because it has layers, and seeing them all helps to release it.

The first layer is the fear of responsibility. When you wait, and something goes badly, it's not your fault, it was life, it was him, it was what befell you. When you choose, and it goes badly, the responsibility is yours, you're the one who decided. It's this that you flee: the weight of being the one who can be wrong.

The second layer is the fear of wanting and not receiving. If you desire out loud, and what you desire is refused you, it hurts in a way that the waiting spares you. Whoever waits is never refused to her face, because she never asks. Whoever desires, and advances, exposes herself to direct rejection, and that, for you, may be unbearable.

And there's a third layer, deeper, which is the fear of discovering what you want and not being able to have it. As long as you don't know what you want, you live in a vague hope that everything is possible. The moment you know, exactly, what you desire, you risk seeing it denied, and the diffuse hope turns into a concrete lack. That's why, sometimes, you don't even let yourself know what you want, so as not to have to feel the pain of wanting it and perhaps not reaching it.

Notice how these three layers keep you still. The first makes you avoid deciding, so as not to carry the error. The second makes you avoid asking, so as not to hear the no. The third makes you avoid even knowing what you want, so as not to feel the lack. And between the three, you build an entire life of waiting, where you never decide, never ask, and barely know what you desire.

But there's a cost that these layers hide, and it's the greatest of all. Not to choose is also a decision. When you wait, and let others decide, you're deciding to hand them your life. The passivity doesn't take you out of the responsibility, it only hides it from you. The life that befell you was, in part, the life that you let happen. And that hurts, because it takes from you the comfort of being only a victim of the circumstances. But it's also where your power is: if you were the one who let it happen, then you're the one who can, from now on, begin to make it happen.

And I want you to see that the waiting, however safe it seems, doesn't protect you from error. It protects you from one kind of error, the error of choosing badly, and exposes you to another, perhaps worse: the error of not living. Of arriving at the end with a life that wasn't yours, that only happened to you, full of trains you watched leave. That's the silent error of the one who never decides, and it's the only one the waiting doesn't spare you.

## 4. What would happen if it were I who chose

I'll leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it.

What would happen if it were you who chose?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Maybe vertigo. Maybe a weight. Maybe a strange excitement, quickly smothered.

For you, this question opens an unknown territory. Because to choose, truly, to want out loud, to advance through your life, is something you almost never did.

And the shadow hidden here is responsibility. The weight of being the one who decides, and therefore the one who carries what you decide.

While you wait, you're innocent. The moment you choose, you become an author. And to be the author of your own life is, at the same time, the greatest freedom and the greatest weight there is.

The greatest freedom, because you stop being at the mercy of others' wanting, and come to be able to go after your own. The greatest weight, because there's no longer anyone to blame. Your life becomes yours, with everything that brings.

And you, until today, chose innocence instead of freedom. You preferred not to be an author, so as not to carry the authorship. You preferred a life that befell you to a life you chose, because the first doesn't hold you responsible, and the second does.

Notice how this makes you strangely powerless, despite perhaps being capable of so much.

You have, probably, worth, talent, capacity. But you put all of that in the service of waiting. You prepare yourself, position yourself, stay ready, and then wait for someone to make happen what you could make happen. It's like having a car and waiting, seated in it, for it to drive on its own, instead of taking the wheel.

The wheel is there. It always was. But taking it means assuming that you're going somewhere by your decision, and therefore that you're the one responsible for the destination. And it's this that you postpone, perhaps for a lifetime.

There's also a hidden grief in this question. If I could have chosen, all this time, then much of what I lived as "what befell me" was, after all, what I let happen. And that hurts. Because it takes from you the comfort of being only a victim of the circumstances, and gives you back a responsibility you refused.

But that pain is the beginning of your power. Because if you were the one who let it happen, then you're the one who can, from now on, begin to make it happen.

Don't answer the question right away with your head. Live it. Because it's in it that the door is. The day you feel, in your body, that you can be the one who chooses, you leave the place of object and enter the place of subject. And then, only then, your life begins, at last, to be yours.

Go back, to the origin, because no one learns to wait without a reason.

Almost always, it was in a place where desiring, as a child, was neither safe nor welcome. Where having a will of your own caused problems. Where the good child was the one who didn't ask, didn't demand, didn't want, and waited, patient, for what they wished to give her.

Maybe you learned that wanting brought conflict. That asking was selfishness. That having a will of your own was a nuisance in a house already full of wills bigger than yours. And you learned to silence your wanting, to wait, to content yourself with whatever came, because wanting out loud was costly.

Maybe you saw that whoever decided, in your home, took the consequences. That taking sides, choosing, asserting oneself, brought punishment, guilt, conflict. And you concluded that the safe place was the place of the one who doesn't get involved, doesn't choose, doesn't take responsibility. That waiting was safer than wanting.

Or maybe, simply, no one taught you that you had the right to desire. That your will counted. That you could want, ask, choose, go and get. You grew up being chosen, or not, by others, and never learned to be the subject of your own wanting.

Notice what a child learns in a place like this. She learns that her role is to receive, not to choose. To wait, not to desire. To be the object of others' decisions, not the subject of her own. And that learning is engraved, and governs you still, and makes you wait, your whole life, for a choice that should come from you.

And there's a figure you might recognize. The figure of the girl taught, with or without words, that a girl doesn't run after. She waits. She doesn't show herself too interested. She lets them come to her. You grew up, maybe, inside that idea, that your role, in love, was to be chosen, and not to choose. And that idea, which seems an old rule of courtship, became your position in your whole life.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing whoever raised you. Whoever didn't teach you to desire, most of the time, didn't know how to desire either. They waited, like you. They didn't mean to make you passive. But they made you, without meaning to, by not showing you that you had the right to want, to choose, to go and get your life instead of waiting for it.

There's also a hidden grief in this question, and I want to name it, because it has to be crossed. If I could have chosen, all this time, then much of what I lived as "what befell me" was, after all, what I let happen.

And that hurts. Because it takes from you the comfort of being only a victim of the circumstances, and gives you back a responsibility you refused. The relationships you didn't live, the dreams you postponed, the life that fell short, weren't only bad luck. They were, in part, waiting.

But I ask you not to stay in that grief blaming yourself. Because that same truth is where your power is. If you were the one who let it happen, then you're the one who can, from now on, begin to make it happen. The grief of not having chosen before is the door to beginning to choose now.

And I want you to see that it's never too late. The life you waited for can begin to be made today, with the next small decision. You don't need to recover the lost time all at once. You need only, from now on, to go on being, slowly, the subject, and not the object, of your own life.

## 5. From object to subject

If you waited your whole life to be chosen, then there's a passage waiting for you. From object to subject.

An object is that about whom one decides. A subject is the one who decides. And you lived as an object, waiting for others' decisions, when you could live as a subject, the author of your own.

This passage is the journey of this book. And I want to show you what changes in it.

The direction of desire changes. Instead of waiting to be desired, you begin to desire. To point. To want, actively, and not only to be available to be wanted.

Imagine the difference. At a party, the one who waits to be chosen positions herself, smiles, stays visible, and waits for someone to come. The one who chooses looks around, notices who interests her, and goes. One is an object waiting. The other is a subject in motion.

And it's not only with men. It's with the whole of life. Instead of waiting for the opportunity, you go to get it. Instead of waiting for recognition, you affirm your worth. Instead of waiting for life to happen to you, you make choices that build it.

I know this frightens you. To leave the waiting is to leave the innocence, it's to take on the responsibility you avoided. But there's a joy on the other side that you don't yet know. The joy of being an author. Of looking at your life and seeing, in it, your choices, and not only what befell you.

I want to undo a fear. To become a subject is not to become aggressive, domineering, someone who tramples others to get what she wants. To desire actively is not to force. It's to affirm your wanting, with clarity, and to let the world, and the other, respond.

You can choose a man, go after him, show your interest, and he may not respond. That's not failure. It's the life of the one who chooses. Whoever chooses risks the no. But whoever never chooses guarantees the no, because she never even gets to ask for the yes.

And there's an enormous liberation in discovering that you survive the no. That you ask, and they refuse you, and you don't die. That you desire out loud, and don't always receive, and remain whole. Each no you survive takes power from the fear that kept you silent. And little by little, choosing stops being terrifying, and becomes, simply, living.

I want to tell you about the passage that awaits you, from object to subject, because it's the entire journey of this book.

And when you choose yourself, something curious happens, almost paradoxical. You stop needing, so desperately, for others to choose you. And that lack of desperation makes you, without your doing anything for it, more choosable.

Because whoever offers herself like one who begs for a place weighs, pushes away. And whoever already has her place, who chose herself, who has her own life, offers herself another way: out of choice, and not out of need. And that attracts, because no one wants to feel like the last resort of someone who only waits to be saved from her own waiting.

Notice the inversion. You waited your whole life to be chosen, and it was that waiting, that hunger to be chosen, that made you less choosable. And on the day you choose yourself, when you stop depending on the choice of others, you'll discover that you come to be, too, more desired, not because you changed your appearance, but because you changed your position: from the one who waits in the line to the one who walks through her life.

But let this not become one more strategy. Don't choose yourself in order to be chosen. Choose yourself because your life is yours, and deserves to be lived by you, whether or not someone else's choice comes. The rest, if it comes, is an addition. The essential is to stop living in waiting, and to begin to live.

## 6. Deciding

There's a verb for this crossing. To decide.

Not to wait. To decide. To be the one who turns the helm, instead of waiting for the current to decide where you go.

And I want to distinguish this verb from another, so you don't confuse them. To decide is not the same as choosing yourself, prioritizing yourself. To decide is deeper. It's the act of sovereignty over your own life. It's assuming that you're the one in charge of it.

Sovereignty is a big word, and it's the right one. It means that you recognize that your life is a territory of yours, and that the one who decides what happens in it, to the extent possible, is you. Not others. Not chance. Not the waiting. You.

Notice what changes when you decide.

To decide transforms you from the one who waits in a courtship to the one who chooses whether or not she wants that relationship. From the one who waits for a promotion to the one who decides her career. From the one who lives what befell her to the one who builds what she wants.

You don't control everything, of course. There are things that don't depend on you, and those are accepted. But there's much more under your decision than the waiting let you see. And to decide is to begin to use that power, instead of handing it over.

But there's an enormous liberation in that responsibility. Because to be the author of your life is, at the same time, the greatest weight and the greatest freedom there is. You stop being at the mercy of others' wanting, and come to be able to go after your own. You stop living what befell you, and come to build what you chose. ## 7. Desiring out loud

There's a destination for this crossing. And it fits in one image. Desiring out loud.

Your whole life you desired in silence. You wanted, but you kept silent. You waited for them to guess, to come, to choose for you, so as never to have to say, out loud, I want this.

To desire out loud is the opposite. It's saying what you want. To yourself first, and then to the world. It's to stop hiding your wanting behind the waiting, and to affirm it, with clarity, even at the risk of not receiving it.

Notice what the silence of your desire cost you. How many things you didn't have, not because they were denied you, but because you never asked for them. How many loves you didn't live, not because they didn't want you, but because you waited for them to guess what you never said. How many lives you didn't live, waiting for a permission that only you could give.

Silent desire doesn't protect you. It only deprives you. Because the world doesn't guess, and what isn't asked for, is rarely received.

To desire out loud frightens you for a simple reason. Making the desire visible makes it refusable. As long as the desire is hidden, it can't be rejected. Shown, it can. And it's from that possible refusal that you flee, by keeping everything silent.

But there's something the silence also does, and that you may never have seen. It makes you invisible. Whoever never says what she wants becomes hard to love, to help, to choose, because no one knows what she wants. Your silence, which was supposed to protect you, leaves you alone, because it makes you illegible to the one who might want to give you precisely what you never dared to ask for.

And there's a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of wanting, and of saying it, and of sometimes receiving. Of being the author of your desires, and not only their silent guardian. Of living a life you chose, even with its no's, instead of a life you waited for, full of silences.

That joy is your right. And it's on the other side of the fear, on the day you say, for the first time, out loud, and to yourself first of all, I want this, and you're the one who goes to get it.

I want to warn you about one thing, because it's going to appear. When you begin to desire out loud, you're going to feel exposed, almost shameless.

It's going to seem like too much to you. Improper. You're going to want to withdraw the desire right away, to return to the safety of silence, of waiting, of letting them decide.

That discomfort is the old rule defending itself. The rule that told you a woman of worth doesn't desire, she waits. Notice it, and disobey it, slowly. Because that rule didn't protect you. It cost you a life of waiting.

And it's going to happen that you'll receive no's. You're going to desire, and advance, and sometimes not receive. That's not proof that you should have stayed silent. It's the price, a fair one, of the one who lives by choosing.

Whoever waits never hears a no to her face, it's true. But she also never hears a yes that she went to get. She trades all the possible yeses for protection against the no's. And it's a bad deal, because the yeses you could have are many, and the no's, after all, are survivable.

Each no you survive teaches you something precious. That you don't die from wanting and not receiving. And as you learn that, the fear that silenced you loses force, and to desire out loud stops being terrifying, and becomes, simply, your new way of living.

It doesn't change everything at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, decide everything you waited for your whole life. But it changes enough for you to stop mistaking your waiting for patience, or for temperament, or for a lack of luck.

Now you know that you wait to be chosen, and that this isn't only in love, it's in everything.

You know that choosing frightens you because it makes you responsible, and that the waiting spared you the weight of carrying your decisions.
