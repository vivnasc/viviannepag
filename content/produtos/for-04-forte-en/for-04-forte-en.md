# The woman who became too strong

**For you, who made yourself into the protection you lacked, and the armor never came off again. Because you no longer need to carry the armor all the time, and resting will not make you collapse.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a strength that is, at bottom, an armor raised because there was no one strong for you, and that, very often, asks for more than understanding. If you recognize in yourself a constant hypervigilance, a body that never relaxes, an exhaustion that does not pass, or the feeling that resting would make you collapse, perhaps you need not only to understand yourself, but to be supported. Seeking out a professional is not collapsing. It is the care that wound deserves.*

---

## 1. I am the strong one

Notice the place you occupy, everywhere. You are the strong one. The one who endures everything. The one who is not shaken. The one who holds things up, solves, sustains, without ever giving way.

People lean on you. They count on you. They know that, whatever happens, you endure. You are the rock, the pillar, the one who never falls. And there is a pride in that, but there is also an immense weight, and a loneliness no one sees, the loneliness of someone who holds everyone up and is held up by no one.

Because you don't allow yourself weakness. You don't allow yourself to collapse, to cry in front of someone, to show that you can't cope. You swallow, you endure, you hold, always. Your strength is permanent, it has no pause, no time off. You are always in armor, always ready, always enduring.

And notice that this vigilance never stops, even when there is no danger at all. Even at home, in safety, surrounded by those who love you, there's a part of you on alert, on the lookout, waiting for the next blow, unable to trust that everything is fine. It is an engine that never shuts off, a background of tension that accompanies everything you do, and that keeps you from being truly present, because half of you is always on sentry. Most people don't live this way. Most can, in safety, let go. And you can't, not because you don't want to, but because your body never learned that it's safe to do so, and so it stands guard even when there's nothing left to guard.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this strength is not, at its origin, a virtue you chose. People praise it, admire it, say you're incredible, that they don't know how you endure. But your strength was not born from a choice. It was born from a necessity.

You became strong because there was no one strong for you. Because, in a time when you needed to be protected, there was no protection, and you had to become your own protection. You made yourself into the strength you lacked. You raised the armor no one raised for you. And that armor, made out of necessity, never came off again.

This book is different. Because your strength is not only a trait to moderate. It is, very often, an armor raised over a wound. A response to a time when you had no one to hold you, and in which you learned to hold yourself, alone, whatever it cost. And that deserves more than understanding. So, throughout these pages, I will tell you that perhaps you need not only to understand yourself, but to be supported.

And I'll give you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You no longer need to carry the armor all the time. Resting will not make you collapse. The armor protected you when there was no one else, but today it crushes you, and you can, slowly, and perhaps with help, learn to set it down, at least for moments, and discover that you don't fall.

I want you to understand, right away, the difference between what it seems and what it is. What it seems, from the outside, is a very strong, autonomous, unshakable woman, who needs no one. What it is, inside, is a woman who learned, in a time when no one held her, to have to hold herself, always, and who never knew how to stop again. Your strength is not, at its origin, a choice. It is a necessity that hardened. And there is an enormous difference between being strong because you choose to be, and being strong because you don't dare be anything else. The first is freedom. The second is an armor glued to the skin, that you no longer know how to take off.

This book does not come to tell you that your strength is bad, or that you have to become fragile, dependent, incapable. Your strength is real, it is yours, and it saved you. It comes to tell you that it would grow larger, and not smaller, if you added to it the capacity to rest, to lean, to be fragile safely. That a woman who knows how to endure and knows how to set things down is more whole than one who only knows the first half. And that you deserve that wholeness, and that rest, after an entire life on sentry.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In not being able to show weakness to anyone, in always swallowing, in being always the strong one, even when you're falling apart inside.

In being permanently on alert, tense, vigilant, even when there's no danger in sight.

In not being able to rest, to relax, to lower your guard, because a part of you is always on guard, waiting for the next blow.

In feeling a deep, old exhaustion, of someone who has carried an armor for so long that she no longer remembers what it is to be without it.

They are all the same. The strength that is armor. The shell that was raised because there was no one strong for you.

I want to show you how this shows up in daily life, in things you may already take for who you are. Someone asks how you are, and you answer fine, always, even falling apart inside, because telling the truth would mean showing weakness. You feel yourself sinking, and instead of telling someone, you redouble the effort, endure more, smile more, so no one notices. You're ill, exhausted, at the limit, and you keep taking care of everything, holding everything up, because stopping seems impossible to you, as if the world depended on you not giving way.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: the way you feel when someone offers you help, or care, or comfort. Instead of relief, a discomfort rises in you, almost a shame, a desire to refuse, to say it isn't necessary, that you endure. Because receiving care means lowering your guard, and lowering your guard sets off an old alarm. You are so trained to be the one who gives, who holds, who protects, that being on the side of the one who receives seems to you a dangerous place, almost forbidden.

And there's something the world doesn't see, beneath your strength. It sees an incredible, unshakable woman, who endures everything, and it admires you, and it leans on you, and it counts on you never falling. It doesn't see the exhaustion beneath the armor, the vigilance that never stops, the tired person who carries, alone, the weight of always being the strong one. It sees the rock, not what it costs to be a rock. And you, who receive admiration for your strength, become even more bound to it, because now there are also others counting on you never faltering, and disappointing them adds to your own terror of falling.

## 2. I made myself into the protection that was lacking

Go back. To the beginning. To when you became too strong.

Because no one becomes this strong without a reason. Your shell was born from a lack. From the absence of someone to protect you, to hold you, to be strong for you, in a time when you needed that.

Perhaps you grew up without protection. With absent, fragile, incapable parents, or parents lost themselves, and no one to be for you the shelter a child needs. And you learned that, if you weren't the one to protect yourself, no one would. You became your own guard, because there was no other.

Perhaps you lived in a place of danger, of instability, of threat, with no one to keep you safe. And you raised, yourself, the shell, because it was the only protection available. You hardened, in order to survive, in a place where fragility was not welcomed, it was exposed.

Perhaps you had to be strong for others. To hold up a family, to care, to endure, before you were old enough for it, because the adults couldn't cope. And there was never, over you, a greater strength to lean on. You were the strength, for yourself and for others, without ever having your own.

Notice what a child learns in any of these places. She learns that she can't count on anyone to protect her. That she has to be strong, alone, always. That fragility is dangerous, because there's no one to welcome it, and that the only safety is the shell she herself raises. And that lesson is engraved deep, in the body, tied to survival.

And that child did what she had to do. She became strong. She raised the armor. She made herself into the protection she lacked. And that saved her, in that time, in a place where there was no one to protect her. It was an intelligent adaptation, perhaps the only one possible, to the lack of a strength that cared for her.

But the armor did not come undone when the lack passed. It stayed. And today, as an adult, in a world where perhaps there are already people who would hold you, you continue in armor, always strong, always on guard, defended against a vulnerability that, in the past, was not welcomed, and deprived of a rest you never let yourself have.

I want you to see where this gets stored, because it matters for understanding why it costs so much to set down the shell. This lesson is not only in your memory, in what you remember. It is in your body, in a place deeper than words. When a child learns, early, that there is no one to protect her, her body stays in permanent alert, in a state of readiness that engraves itself in the nervous system, below consciousness. That's why, today, your vigilance does not pass through a decision. Your body is on guard before you think, tense, ready, on the lookout, because it learned, long ago, that lowering its guard was risking its life, and no one ever told it that it could stop.

That's why you so often tell yourself that you should rest, that there's no danger, that you can relax, and even so you can't. Your body does not obey your reason. The tension does not pass simply because you wish it, sleep does not restore you, the alert does not switch off, because it is not born from an idea, it is born from a body memory, ancient, that continues to stand guard as if life depended on it. And a body memory is not disarmed with arguments. It is relearned, slowly, through new experiences, repeated, in which the body discovers, time after time, that it lowered its guard and nothing bad came. I tell you this to lift off you the guilt of not being able, simply by wishing it, to rest. It is not a lack of discipline. It is the nature of a wound that lives in the body, and that asks for a path that passes through the body, and often through hands that know how to accompany it.

I want you to see this clearly and with tenderness. Your strength is not a defect, nor an excess to correct. It is an intelligent response to a real lack. You are not hard by temperament. You are a survivor, who made herself strong because there was no one strong for her. And recognizing this is honoring the child who became her own protection, and aching, at the same time, that she had to do it.

I want you to pause, for a moment, on the pain there is in this origin, because it's easy to pass over it quickly, as you pass over everything. A child should not have to be her own protection. She should have, over her, strong adults, who held her, who were the shelter, who carried the weight that is too much for small shoulders. And that is what you lacked. You had to be, too early, the strength that should have come from outside. And there is a grief in this truth, the grief of a childhood in which you couldn't be small, fragile, cared for, because you had to be big, hard, the one who endures. That grief deserves to be felt, not skipped, because it is from it that is born the tenderness for yourself that this crossing wants to give you back.

I want to add a root you may recognize. Sometimes the shell is born not only from the absence of protection, but from having learned that your fragility bothered, or was used against you. You showed yourself fragile, once, and you were mocked, humiliated, or your vulnerability became a weapon in someone's hands. And you learned, in your body, that showing weakness was exposing yourself to danger, giving ammunition to those who would use it against you. You hardened, then, not only from a lack of shelter, but so as never again to give anyone the power that your fragility, once, gave them. And you carry, into today, the certainty that being seen fragile is handing yourself over, disarmed, to those who could hurt you.

In any of these roots, the lesson is similar: fragility is dangerous, strength is the only safety, and there is no one to entrust the weight to. And it is that lesson, engraved early, that governs you today, and that turns each moment of rest, each lowering of the guard, into a threat your body refuses, even when your reason knows it has passed.

## 3. The shell that never came off again

There is something about your strength that you need to see. It became a shell you can no longer take off.

Notice the difference. A person with inner strength can be strong when it's needed, and can be fragile, gentle, vulnerable, when it's safe to be. She has both. You don't. You only have the strength. The shell doesn't come off. And a protection that cannot be taken off is not strength, it is a prison of metal.

And there is an enormous cost to living in armor, without ever being able to take it off.

It costs you the body. Because a permanent shell is a permanent tension. Your muscles, your nervous system, your whole body, live in a state of alert, of readiness, of defense, never relaxing. And the body tires of a vigilance that does not stop. Many women who are too strong fall ill, not from weakness, but from the exhaustion of a body that could never rest.

Notice the signs, because perhaps you already take them for normal. The tension in the shoulders, in the jaw, that never lets go. The sleep that does not restore, light, guarded, of someone who sleeps with a part on watch. The background tiredness that no night cures, because it is not the tiredness of effort, it is the tiredness of alert. The aches that appear with no clear cause, the body that falls ill when, at last, you stop, as if only in illness did it allow itself the time off you never gave it. None of this is a weakness of your body. It is your body paying the toll of an armor it could never take off, warning you, in its own way, that it was not made to live in permanent war.

It costs you intimacy. Because intimacy is born from vulnerability, from letting yourself be seen fragile, from lowering your guard before someone. And you don't lower your guard. You are always in your shell, always strong, and so no one reaches you truly, no one knows you in your fragility, no one loves you in your most tender part, because that you never show.

It costs you rest. Because resting requires lowering your guard, and lowering your guard, for you, seems dangerous. So you never truly rest, not while sleeping, not on holiday, not in quiet. There's always a part of you on watch, on the lookout, ready for the next blow, and that part never lets you, at last, set yourself down.

Notice this loneliness. The loneliness of someone who is always the strong one, and never the one held. You hold everyone up, and who holds you? You are everyone's support, and on whom do you lean? And in the moments when you would most need a strength greater than yours, you discover there is none, because you grew used to being the only strength, and no one even knows that you, too, might need to be held.

And there is a painful irony in this. You, who would hold anyone you loved, who would be there at any hour, who would never let anyone who needs you fall, do not give yourself that same care. You find it natural that others need, that they rest, that they lean, and you never judge them for it. But yourself, you don't. For you, needing is weakness, resting is carelessness, leaning is falling. You apply to yourself a harshness you would never apply to those you love, and you deny yourself a care you would give anyone without thinking. You deserve from yourself the same tenderness you give others, and that is, perhaps, the first thing this crossing comes to teach you.

And there is a cost paid by those who love you, that perhaps you don't see. The people who care for you would like to be able to care for you, hold you, be strong for you, at least sometimes. And you don't let them. You're always so whole, so in armor, that no one reaches you, no one can give you anything, no one reaches the part of you that would need comfort. And so, without meaning to, you deprive those who love you of the possibility of loving you back, of returning what you always give, of entering your life not only as someone who leans on you, but as someone who also supports you. Your shell, which you believe weighs on no one, closes the door to those who would want to hold you.

It costs you, too, joy. Because joy, the true kind, requires an abandon, a letting go, a letting yourself be carried, and you don't let go, you don't let yourself be carried, you're always holding, controlling, watching. And so life is lived in survival mode, enduring, and rarely in enjoyment mode, in lightness, in pleasure. You fulfill, solve, endure, but when was the last time you truly had fun, that you laughed without reserve, that you gave yourself to a moment without a part of you on sentry? The shell doesn't only take away your rest. It takes away your capacity to live the good moments without watching over them.

I want you to see the difference between healthy strength and yours. Healthy strength is one capacity more: I know how to endure when it's needed, and I know how to set things down, to be fragile, to lean, when it's safe. It has both doors. Yours doesn't. Yours only knows how to endure, and setting things down is not, truly, available, however exhausted you are. And a strength that cannot be set down is not strength, it is a prison. The work of this crossing is not to take away your strength, which is real and is yours, and which saved you. It is to unlock the other door, that of rest, so that your strength stops being a permanent armor and becomes a choice.

I want you to feel the tiredness of this. The tiredness of an armor that never comes off. Of a vigilance that never stops. Of a strength that can never fail. It is a deep exhaustion, of an entire life in armor, and perhaps you already take it for normal, for being this way, without knowing that beneath the shell is a person exhausted from carrying it.

## 4. Who am I without the armor

I'll leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it.

Who are you without the armor? Without the permanent strength? When you lower your guard, and let yourself, for a moment, be fragile?

Stay with it. Notice what it stirs. Perhaps a terror, perhaps a vertigo.

For you, this question touches a deep fear. Because your entire identity was built on being the strong one. Take that away, imagine yourself without the shell, fragile, vulnerable, needing, and a panic appears. Then what am I? Who protects me, if I lower my guard? And if I collapse, and there's no one to hold me, as there never was?

And the shadow hiding here is fragility. The part of you that is gentle, that needs, that can be hurt, that can fail to cope. That part terrifies you, because in your past fragility was not welcomed, it was exposed, dangerous. And you armed yourself against your own fragility, as if it were a threat, when it is, at bottom, the most human part of you, and the most capable of being loved.

Notice how this imprisons you. You cannot allow yourself fragility, even exhausted, because fragility sets off the terror that there's no one to hold you. You live enduring, holding, being strong, even when you're falling apart, because lowering your guard seems to risk the fall you always feared, that of collapsing with no one underneath.

And there is a particular sadness in this that I want to name. It is that of knowing, deep down, that there is in you a tender, gentle part, that needed comfort, that never had it, and that is still waiting. Sometimes, in rare moments, you feel that part wanting to appear, asking to be cared for, and you push it quickly back inside, by reflex, by terror, and you put the shell back on. To live this way, knowing that there is in you a girl who was never held and continuing not to hold her, is a silent form of mourning, repeated every day. And this crossing is, at bottom, letting, at last, that part appear, and giving it yourself, now, the comfort that was lacking then.

And there is an enormous fragility in this strength of yours, that needs to be seen. A person who cannot be fragile is not truly strong. She is a hostage of the shell. True strength includes the capacity to be vulnerable, to ask, to lean, to rest, knowing that one can. Your strength, which cannot fail, which cannot rest, which cannot be fragile, is not freedom. It is an armor that imprisons you in an endless vigilance.

And I want you to see the courage there is, after all, in fragility, contrary to what you learned. It takes much more courage to lower your guard than to keep it up. Keeping the shell is what you know, it's the safe thing, it's the automatic thing. Lowering it, letting yourself be seen fragile, risking that someone holds you or doesn't, that does take enormous courage, the greatest there is, because it exposes you precisely to what you most fear. The woman who allows herself to be fragile is not weaker than you. She is, in many senses, more courageous, because she faces the terror you avoid by keeping the armor always on. Fragility, far from being the opposite of strength, is one of its highest forms.

And I want to tell you something about the terror this question raises, because it begins to disarm it. What terrifies you is not, truly, resting. It is the fall you learned that rest brought, in a time when there was no one underneath. For your body, lowering the guard and collapsing are one and the same thing, fused since early on. But they are separable. Resting, in itself, is not falling. It is only setting down the weight, trusting that there is ground, letting the body restore itself. The fall belonged to that time when you were alone. Today, perhaps you no longer are, and resting and collapsing can, at last, stop going together.

I want to tell you something about this fear, because it begins to disarm it. Today, there are people who can hold you. This is the truth your body has not yet learned. In the time when the shell was born, there was no one strong for you, and your strength was the only protection. But today, in your adult life, perhaps there are already people you could lean on, hands that would hold you if you lowered your guard. The danger of collapsing with no one underneath was real back then. It may no longer be real now.

And there is a cruelty in this that perhaps you don't see. What your past denied you, you continue to deny yourself. In that time, there was no one to hold you, to comfort you, to tell you rest, I'll keep guard. And today, as an adult, it is you who deny yourself that comfort, who don't let yourself be held, who don't allow yourself the rest no one gave you. The lack from outside became a lack you practice, against yourself. And to stop practicing it, to let yourself, at last, be cared for, rest, set yourself down, is perhaps the most reparative gesture of this crossing: to give yourself, now, the shelter that was lacking then, instead of continuing, alone, to stand the guard no one came to relieve.

Don't answer the question of who you are without the armor just yet. It isn't answered with the head, and sometimes it needs more than a book. But leave it open. Because that is where the door is. On the day your body learns that it can lower its guard, at least with some, without collapsing, you stop needing to carry the shell all the time. And there, only there, can you begin to rest.

## 5. Perhaps you need more than to understand

I have to stop the book here. With all seriousness. Because your strength is not, most of the time, only a personality trait. It is an armor raised over a wound, and that changes what you need.

If you became strong because there was no one strong for you, because you grew up without protection, in danger, or you had to protect yourself alone, then your shell is not just a habit. It is a trauma response. An adaptation to the lack of safety. And a trauma response is not treated only by understanding. It lives in the body, in the permanently activated nervous system, in the hypervigilance that does not stop.

I want to name, plainly, what may lie beneath. That constant tension, the body that never relaxes, the permanent vigilance, the background alert even without danger, have a name. It is called hypervigilance. It is the nervous system trapped in a state of alert, engraved by a time when danger was real and constant. And that is not temperament. It is a trauma response, and it deserves to be cared for with support.

And I want to tell you something with firmness and tenderness. You, who are everyone's strength, are precisely the one who asks for help latest, or never asks, because asking for help seems to mean lowering your guard, showing weakness, collapsing. But seeking support is not collapsing. It is the strongest and most lucid act of someone who endured alone for too long. The support of a psychologist, of a therapist, someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, your nervous system, the hypervigilance engraved deep.

I want to distinguish two things for you, because it matters for knowing what you need. A wound of temperament is worked through by understanding: you see the pattern, you understand the origin, and that, in time, helps to change it. A trauma response is something else. It does not live only in the head, it lives in the body, in the nervous system trapped on alert, in the hypervigilance that does not switch off simply by being understood. And a trauma response needs more than understanding. It needs time, the body, a safe relationship where the nervous system relearns, slowly, that it can lower its guard. That, alone, is rarely achieved. It is done with accompaniment.

And there is a beauty in this. The helping relationship can be, for you, the first place where you experience lowering your guard safely. Where you are, finally, the one held, and not only the one who holds. Where someone is strong for you, for a moment, and your body learns, slowly, that it can rest without collapsing, because there are, this time, hands underneath.

And I want to take away a fear that perhaps holds you back. You don't have to be in collapse to deserve to seek help. That is, in fact, the very shell speaking, telling you that only when you've ruined entirely is stopping justified. To seek support because you recognize, in yourself, an exhaustion that does not pass, a body that never rests, a vigilance that does not switch off, is exactly the early care, before the collapse, that makes sense. It would be, itself, a first gesture of setting down the armor: that of admitting that you, too, need to be held, and that asking for it is not falling, it is the wisest thing you can do for someone who endured alone for too long.

If you recognize, in yourself, the hypervigilance, the body that never stops, the strength that can never fail, this is the chapter I want you to remember. Don't skip it. Seeking help is not weakness. It is the care you deserve, and that you perhaps never gave yourself, for always being the one who cares for everyone.

And I want to tell you something with all firmness and all tenderness: this is not your fault, and it is not resolved by effort alone, however much you try, and you know how to try like no one else. If your shell was born from a time without protection, from danger, from a fragility that was exposed or used against you, then you deserve the support of someone trained to work not only your understanding, but your body, your nervous system, the hypervigilance engraved deep. And asking for it is not surrendering. It is the strongest, and most tender, thing you can do for someone who was strong alone for too long.

## 6. Resting without collapsing

If your strength is an armor that never comes off, then there is a new truth waiting for you. You can rest without collapsing.

Notice what you fear. For you, lowering your guard, resting, ceasing to be strong, seems to risk collapse. As if the shell were the only thing holding you upright, and that without it you would crumble, fall apart, fall with no one to catch you.

And I propose to question this. Lowering your guard is not collapsing. Resting is not crumbling. The shell is not the only thing holding you. You have, beneath it, a whole person, who holds herself up, and who can rest without falling. The fear of collapsing comes from a time when, in fact, there was no one underneath. But your ground, today, is not only the shell. It is you, and perhaps also some hands that, this time, would hold you.

Notice the confusion you live in, which is the core of this fear. You confuse the shell with your spine. You think it's the armor that keeps you upright, and that without it you would crumble. But the shell is not your structure, it's a weight you carry over it. Your structure, the true one, is your inner strength, which has been built over all these years, and which holds itself up even without the permanent vigilance. Setting down the armor does not leave you without a spine. It leaves you only without the extra weight you carried over it, and you discover, with astonishment, that you hold yourself up just the same, lighter, because what holds you was never the shell, it was always you.

And I want you to understand why this is not resolved by willpower alone, you who have so much. It isn't because you don't try that you don't rest. It's because effort, here, works against you: the more you force yourself to relax, the more you activate the alert, because forcing is, itself, a form of vigilance. Rest is not conquered by force, one surrenders to it. And surrendering is precisely what costs you most, you who learned that surrendering your guard was dangerous. So this path is not made with more effort, it is made with more safety: as your body discovers that there is ground, it begins, on its own, to allow itself to let go, without your having to force it.

I want to be honest. If yours is a trauma wound, this truth does not settle simply by convincing yourself of it. Your body will keep on alert, will keep fearing the collapse, however much your head knows there's ground. So this path, very often, is made with help, with someone to accompany you in teaching your system, slowly, that it can rest safely.

But there are things you can begin, with care. You can begin by lowering your guard in a small way, with those who are trustworthy. Showing a fragility. Admitting that you're tired. Ceasing, for a moment, to be the strong one. And observe what happens, outside and inside.

Outside, with the right person, almost certainly, not the collapse you fear, but a welcoming, a care, a hand. Inside, the alarm, the terror of crumbling, the desire to put the shell back on. And to stay with that, to let yourself, for a moment, be fragile, is to give your body a new piece of evidence. That you lowered your guard, and didn't collapse. That there was, this time, ground underneath.

Each time you set down the armor, in a small way, and don't collapse, you give your system a new piece of evidence. That resting, today, does not bring the fall of back then. And it is with evidence, repeated, in the body, and not only with ideas, in the head, that the shell, slowly, loosens. Hypervigilance is not undone by convincing yourself you can rest. It is undone by resting, in a small way, and discovering that there is ground, time after time, until the body believes it. It is slow work, of many small pieces of evidence, and so haste, which is your hallmark, does not serve here: the body learns at its own pace, and your task is only to give it, repeatedly, the experience of setting itself down and not falling.

And don't be surprised if, at first, resting tires you more, instead of less. It's common. When you finally stop, after years on alert, the body, instead of relaxing right away, sometimes releases everything it held, and the accumulated tiredness comes, the falling ill, the endless desire to sleep. It is not a sign that rest is bad for you. It is a sign of how much you needed it, and that your body, at last authorized to set itself down, is doing the maintenance it postponed for a lifetime. Cross that tiredness with gentleness, without reproaching yourself for it, because on the other side is a repose you never knew.

I want to warn you of one thing, so it doesn't frighten you. The first time you lower your guard, even just a little, the terror will likely rise strong, you'll feel a vertigo, an urgent desire to pull yourself together, to put the shell back on, to prove that you do endure after all. That is not a sign that you did wrong. It is your old system going off, treating this small rest as if it were the abandonment of long ago. Don't obey it right away. Stay, for a moment, without the shell, let yourself be held, and give your body the chance to discover that, this time, there is ground underneath.

And I want you to choose well with whom you begin, because it matters. Don't lower your guard, at first, before those who already hurt you, those who used your fragility against you, those who might confirm the fear. Begin with those who have already proven trustworthy, with those who wish you well, with those you can be almost certain will welcome you. It is neither weakness nor cheating. It is giving your body the best conditions to learn the new truth, instead of exposing it, right from the start, to the old wound. There is time, afterward, for the rest. First, the body needs evidence that setting down the shell can go well.

The awakening, when it comes, is a physical sensation of deep relief. It is the time you lower your guard, when you rest, when you allow yourself, for a moment, not to be strong, and instead of the collapse you feared, you feel a rest you didn't know. The rest of someone who can, at last, set down the armor, and who discovers that she didn't fall, that she held herself up, that there was ground. It is a small instant, but it is enormous, because it is your body learning, perhaps for the first time, that it can rest without collapsing.

## 7. Setting down the armor

There is a verb for this crossing. To rest.

To rest for real. To set down the armor, at least for moments. To stop carrying the shell all the time. To give your body, your vigilance, your strength, the time off they never had.

And I want to show you what this means, with care.

Resting is not ceasing to be capable, losing your strength, becoming fragile forever. Your strength is real, and it stays with you. Resting is ceasing to carry it permanently, without pause. It is being able to be strong when it's needed, and to set down the shell when it's safe. It is having both, the strength and the rest, instead of only the strength without end.

Setting down the armor, at least for moments, is letting yourself be fragile with those who are trustworthy. It is admitting that you're tired. It is asking, leaning, letting someone be strong for you, for a moment. It is resting, truly, without the vigilance of guard. It is giving your body, at last, permission to relax.

And I want to undo a fear, because it will appear: the fear that, if you set down the shell, you'll lose the strength that defines you, you'll become soft, incapable, someone who can no longer be relied on. It won't happen. Your strength does not live in the shell. It lives in you, in your real capacity, which has been proven over an entire lifetime. Setting down the armor takes away none of that capacity. It takes away only the obligation to exercise it at every second, without pause, to the point of exhaustion. You remain capable, strong, reliable. What changes is that you stop being so out of terror, and start being so out of choice, and what comes from choice is firmer, freer, more your own, than what came from the fear of falling.

I want you to see, too, that resting is not selfishness, nor abandonment of your loved ones. It is precisely the opposite. An exhausted woman, in permanent alert, depleted from carrying everything, has less and less to give. Rest does not take you from others, it gives you back to them, more whole, more present, less at the limit. Caring for yourself, setting down the armor, letting yourself rest, is not taking care away from those you love. It is ensuring that there is still, in you, someone who can care, instead of an exhausted sentry who consumes herself until there's nothing left for anyone, not even for you.

It will cost you. You will feel, each time you lower your guard, the old terror of collapse, the hypervigilance pulling you back to alert, the desire to put the shell back on. And you will want to go back to being the strong one, enduring, holding, because the shell is what you know, and lowering it seems dangerous.

But it is precisely there, in that setting down the armor despite the terror, that your healing lies. Because each time you rest, when you lower your guard, and don't collapse, you teach your body something new. That it can rest without crumbling. That there is ground, today, beneath you. That you no longer need to carry the shell at every instant.

It will cost you, because the old voice, the one that told you to endure, is engraved deep, and it will appear with each moment of rest, with its usual harshness. It will tell you that you're being soft, slacking off, risking everything you held with so much effort. The work is not to silence it all at once, it is to stop obeying it. To hear it say don't lower your guard, and to answer it, slowly, today I can rest, there is ground underneath. And each time you rest despite it, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more your own, more tender, that perhaps you never had: the one that knows you deserve to set things down, and that setting things down is not failing.

I want you to see what opens up when you stop carrying the shell at every instant. The body opens up, which at last relaxes, and you discover that the background tiredness, which you took for yourself, was only the weight of the armor, and that without it there is a lightness you didn't know. Intimacy opens up, because you let yourself, at last, be seen fragile, and you discover that it is in fragility, and not in the shell, that people reach you and love you up close. And the possibility opens up of being, you too, the one held, of setting the weight down in someone's hands, of not being, always and in everything, the only strength. It is a different life from the one you have, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day your body learns that it can rest.

And there is a joy waiting on the other side, that perhaps you don't even remember wanting, so used are you to being strong. The joy of resting. Of being fragile with those who love you, without falling apart. Of being held, and not only the one who holds. Of setting down, at last, a weight you've carried for a lifetime. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day you rest, for the first time for real, and discover that you didn't collapse, you were held, or you held yourself, with ground underneath.

I won't give you a method, and this path is, very often, made with help. But I'll leave you the principle. Set down the armor, in a small way, first, with those who are trustworthy. Let yourself be fragile, rest, lean. And discover, slowly, and perhaps with hands to accompany you, that you no longer need to be strong all the time, and that resting, today, does not make you collapse.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things. But, in this case, understanding may not be enough, and telling you so is the most honest way to care for you.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It does not make you, tomorrow, set down an armor of a whole lifetime. But it changes enough so that you stop confusing your exhaustion with strength, and your shell with who you are.

Now you know that you became too strong, and that this strength was not a choice, it was a necessity, raised when there was no one else to be it for you.

You know that you made yourself into the protection that was lacking, because there was no one strong for you, and that this is worthy of tenderness, not of reproach.

You know that the shell never came off again, and that it costs you the body, intimacy, rest, and the joy of living the good moments without watching over them.

You know whom you fear to be without the armor, and that beneath it lies the terror of collapsing with no one underneath.

You know that perhaps you need more than to understand, that hypervigilance is a trauma response, and that seeking help is not collapsing, it is the strongest thing there is.

You know that you can rest without collapsing, and that the verb is to rest, to set down the armor, at least for moments.

And you know, now, that your strength was never the problem, that it saved you, and that what you lack is not less strength, it is the permission to set it down every now and then, to be, also, the one held, to rest without that making you fall.

I want to leave you an image.

Imagine a soldier who was left alone defending a fortress, without reinforcements, without anyone to relieve his guard, and who stayed there, in armor, on sentry, day and night, year after year, never able to sleep, because if he slept, no one guarded the gate. And the war ended, long ago, but the soldier remains on sentry, exhausted, because no one ever came to tell him he could rest.

The crossing is someone coming to relieve your guard. Discovering that you are no longer alone defending everything. That there are, today, reinforcements, hands, ground. That you can set down the armor, sleep, rest, and that the gate doesn't stay defenseless, because it is no longer only your shell holding it. It isn't easy, and sometimes it's done with help. But on the other side of the armor is the rest of an entire lifetime, waiting for you.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so you don't live it as one more battle to win alone. There will be setbacks. Days when you rest, and days when you put the whole armor back on, enduring everything, on alert, as always. And your old voice will tell you that you failed, that you can't even rest after all. Don't believe it. The setbacks are not the end of the road, they are part of it. No one sets down all at once an armor of a whole lifetime. You set it down a little, you put it back on, you set it down again, a little longer, and it is this way, back and forth, that the body, slowly, learns that it can rest without collapsing.

And I want to leave you something, about those who watch you, if there are those who learn by looking at you, children, people close to you. If they see you always be the strong one, never set things down, never ask, endure everything alone, they learn that this is how one ought to be, and it's likely they'll raise, too, the same shell, and live the same exhaustion. The most freeing thing you can do for those who watch you is not to give them the example of the unshakable woman. It is to show them a woman who also rests, who lets herself be held, who knows how to be strong and fragile according to what the moment asks. Sometimes a woman learns to set down the armor, at last, not only for herself, but so as not to condemn those she loves to the same endless shell.

And I want to tell you one last thing, simple, to close. You did what you had to do. The girl who became her own protection, because there was no other, did something extraordinary, and she deserves to be honored, not corrected. There is nothing wrong with her strength. There is only one thing to say to her, slowly, time after time, until the body believes it: you can rest now, someone came to relieve the guard, you no longer have to endure everything alone.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this book. I no longer need to carry the armor all the time.

Resting was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not made only by reading. It is made by setting down, slowly, the armor. And, very often, it is made with help, with someone prepared to relieve your guard while your body learns that it can rest without collapsing. Because a wound of someone who endured alone for too long deserves hands that know how to, at last, hold her, and seeking them is not collapsing. It is caring, at last, for someone who was never cared for.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. You do not have to deserve rest by enduring first everything there is to endure. Rest is not a prize you earn at the end of a limitless strength. It is a human need, a right of yours, today, now, with everything there still is to do, simply for being human and for being no longer in the time when there was no one to relieve your guard.

And before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing. Difficult, for you.

This week, lower your guard, in a small way, with someone trustworthy. Show that you're tired. Admit that you can't cope with something. Cease, for a moment, to be the strong one. And notice the terror that rises, the fear of collapsing, the desire to put the shell back on. And stay, for a moment, without it. Because it is in that setting down, endured despite the fear, that your body begins, slowly, to learn that there is ground underneath, and that the war that made you strong, after all, is over.

And if, in doing it, you feel that you are failing, giving way, ceasing to be who you always were, remember: that thought is the shell speaking, not the truth. The truth is that resting does not take away your strength. It gives you back the life that strength, alone, was taking from you.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
