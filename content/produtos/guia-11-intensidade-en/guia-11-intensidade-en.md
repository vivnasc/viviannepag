# Love or intensity?

**An honest test before you give yourself, to tell apart the love that's good for you from the one that only burns.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This guide is a self-knowledge resource about relationships. It does not replace therapeutic support or any form of specialized help. And there is one thing I need to tell you right away, before anything else: if your relationship involves physical, psychological, or sexual violence, threats, coercive control, or any form of abuse, this guide is not the appropriate resource for you. What I describe here assumes two free people trying to love each other. Abuse is something else, and it isn't solved with tests or with more understanding on your part. Seek specialized help, talk to someone you trust, contact a victim support line. Telling love apart from intensity matters. Your safety comes first.*

---

## 1. When intensity disguises itself as love

I want you to start by imagining two things.

The first is a storm. The sky heavy, the wind lifting everything off the ground, your heart pounding as if it were about to leap out of your mouth. There is something hypnotic about a storm. You can't look away. You feel alive in a way that frightens you. Every flash of lightning seems to tell you that this is important, that this is big, that this is real.

The second is firm ground beneath your feet. It doesn't shake. It doesn't shine. It doesn't take your breath away. It's simply there, holding you up, day after day, in a way so constant that you almost forget it exists. Firm ground doesn't give you adrenaline. It gives you something else: the possibility of resting without falling.

Most of us were taught to confuse the storm with love. And we were taught to confuse firm ground with boredom, with a lack of passion, with "I don't feel butterflies." And this is what I want to talk to you about in this guide. Because I have seen too many women (and too many men) leave firm ground to chase the storm. And then spend years asking themselves why they are always soaked, always trembling, always exhausted.

Let me be clear about what intensity is. Intensity is the roller coaster. It's the passion that burns in the first months and then needs drama to keep itself lit. It's the cycle of distance and reconciliation that gives you, each time, a chemical rush so strong that you confuse relief with love. It's the constant anxiety of not knowing where you stand with the other person. It's checking your phone a thousand times. It's the knot in your stomach waiting for a message. It's what we call "chemistry," "connection," "soulmates," when it's often just our nervous system in a permanent state of alarm, reading danger as desire.

And here is the part no one tells you: your body doesn't tell excitement and fear apart very well. Physiologically they are almost identical. The racing heart, the cold hands, the focus narrowed onto that person, the feeling that nothing else matters. You feel this and you say "I'm in love." But, very often, what you're feeling isn't love. It's your body sounding the alarm. It's fear dressed up as passion.

There is an expression everyone uses: "to have butterflies in your stomach." And everyone treats butterflies as the supreme sign that this is true love. But I ask you to look again at those butterflies. Where do they come from? Butterflies are often the pretty way we describe anxiety. They are the cold in the belly of someone who doesn't know whether she'll be chosen. They are the nervousness of someone standing before an unpredictable person, someone who both warms you and chills you, and your body never knows which version will show up today.

Notice the difference. When you're with someone safe, with someone who treats you well consistently, what you feel isn't the cold of butterflies. It's the opposite. It's warmth. It's your shoulders relaxing. It's your breath growing deeper. It's the relief of not having to pretend, of not having to guess, of not having to brace yourself for the worst. Safety doesn't give you butterflies. It gives you roots.

And this is why so many people, when they finally find healthy love, feel that something is missing. "I like him, but I don't feel that thing." That thing you don't feel is, very often, the alarm. It's the turbulence. It's the intensity you got used to and that your body learned to recognize as love. Calm feels empty to you because you grew up confusing chaos with affection. If, in your childhood home, love always came wrapped in tension, in unpredictability, in explosions and reconciliations, then your nervous system was calibrated for that. And when peace arrives, it doesn't recognize it. It finds it strange. It finds it suspicious. It finds it dull.

I'm not telling you this to blame you. I'm telling you because the first thing you need to know is this: what you feel is not proof of what is. The intensity of what you feel does not measure the worth of a relationship. Sometimes it measures exactly the opposite. Sometimes, the more it burns, the closer it is to burning you.

And there is still another layer I want to name, because it is the most treacherous of all. There is a kind of intensity that doesn't come only from our past. It comes from the other person, and it is manufactured on purpose. There are those who create turbulence to keep you trapped. Those who give you a lot and then take it away, not out of emotional instability, but as a strategy. Those who keep you in doubt because doubt keeps you holding on. I'll talk more about this later, in the chapter on the test, because that's where intensity stops being a question of self-knowledge and becomes a question of safety.

## 2. The signs that tell them apart

So how do you tell them apart? How do you know whether what you feel is firm ground or the storm, love or the alarm?

Not by the intensity of the feeling. We've already seen that. It's by what the relationship does to you over time. Love and intensity feel similar at the start. But they behave completely differently after months, after years. So instead of looking at the flame, I want you to look at the signs. At what the relationship produces inside you and around you.

Let me give you the contrasts, side by side, so you can begin to train your eye.

Love makes you grow. Intensity consumes you. Notice yourself after being with the person. Do you come away bigger or smaller? Do you come away with more energy for your life, for your friends, for your work, for your dreams? Or do you come away drained, your head full of them, with no room for anything else? Healthy love is expansive. It gives you energy instead of stealing it. Intensity is a black hole: no matter how much you feed it, it stays hungry, and you're always left with less.

Love is stable. Intensity is a roller coaster. In healthy love there are highs and lows, of course, because it's a relationship between two humans. But there is a constant ground beneath the waves. You know where you stand. You know that tomorrow the person will still care for you, even if you argued today. In intensity, you never know. Today is heaven, tomorrow is hell, and you live in the permanent uncertainty of which of the two people will show up.

Love calms your nervous system. Intensity activates it. This is perhaps the most important one. Pay attention to your body when you're with the person and when you think of them. Does your body relax or tighten? Do you breathe more deeply or hold your breath? Do you sleep better or worse since this relationship entered your life? The body doesn't lie. It knows, before your head does, whether it's at home or in danger.

Love lets you be yourself. Intensity asks you to lose yourself. In healthy love, you keep being who you are. Your tastes, your friends, your limits, your way of seeing the world. The person loves you for that, not in spite of it. In intensity, you fade away little by little. You start molding yourself to what they want. Abandoning what you love. Pulling away from those who knew you. And one day you look at yourself in the mirror and no longer recognize yourself.

Love is clear. Intensity lives on confusion. In love, you know what you feel and you know what the person feels. There is transparency. In intensity, you're always trying to decode. Analyzing messages. Asking your friends "what do you think he meant by this?" Confusion isn't a detail of passion. It's its fuel. Where there is clarity, obsession can't grow.

Love respects your pace. Intensity is in a hurry. Be careful of anyone who wants you all, now, too fast. Who tells you you're their soulmate in the second week. Who wants commitment, exclusivity, total fusion when you barely know each other. In relationship psychology this is sometimes called "love bombing." It looks like the most sweeping love in the world. It is, in truth, frequently, a way of capturing you before you have time to think.

I want to add a note about peace, because I know it can confuse you. If you've reached this point and thought "but my relationship is calm and that bores me, does that mean I don't love?", don't draw conclusions too quickly. The calm of a healthy relationship is not the absence of feeling. It's the absence of fear. They are very different things. You can feel passion, desire, joy, deep tenderness, and still feel peace. What peace takes away isn't love. It's anxiety. And if your relationship gives you peace and you read that as boredom, maybe the problem isn't the relationship. Maybe it's that you haven't yet learned to trust the calm. That your body still seeks the storm because it's the only thing it recognizes as home.

Learning to feel good on firm ground is work. It doesn't happen overnight. But it's one of the most important pieces of work you can do for yourself.

## 3. The test

Now let's get concrete. I promised you an honest test, and here it is.

I want you to think of a specific relationship as you answer. It can be the relationship you're in. It can be someone you feel attracted to and are thinking of giving yourself to. It can be a pattern that repeats in all your relationships. Choose one, and answer honestly, not with what you'd like to be true.

Take a pen and paper, if you can. Writing changes the way we answer. It pulls us out of the romantic fog and forces us to look it in the face.

Ask yourself these questions. For each one, answer yes or no, without justifying yourself, without finding excuses for the other person.

About how you feel with yourself:

Do I feel at peace with this person, or do I feel on alert? Do I sleep better or worse since this relationship entered my life? Do I like who I am more when I'm with them, or less? Do I still have my life, my friends, my interests, or have they slowly disappeared? Do I feel I can be myself, or am I always editing myself so as not to lose them?

About how the relationship works:

Do I know where I stand with this person, or do I live in uncertainty? When there's a problem, can we talk about it, or am I afraid to bring it up? Do I feel we're a team, or do I feel I'm always trying to win them over? After being with them, do I come away with more energy or drained? Does this relationship do me good most days, or does it do me good only in the peaks, between long stretches of pain?

About what your body tells you:

When I think of spending my life like this, does my body relax or tighten? If the wisest person I know saw this relationship from the outside, what would they tell me? And if a daughter of mine, or a friend I love, were in this exact relationship, what would I feel for her?

This last question is, for me, the most revealing of all. Because with ourselves we are lenient. We always find an excuse, a justification, a "but he has a good side." But if we imagine someone we love in our place, the truth suddenly appears, naked. What we accept for ourselves, we often couldn't bear to see happen to those we love. And that tells you everything.

Look at your answers. There is no magic score. But if most of your answers point to alert, uncertainty, shrinking, and drain, your body already knows the answer. It's only your head that's still arguing with it.

And now I need to stop and change my tone, because there is a part of this test that isn't about telling love apart from passion. It's about your safety. There are signs that aren't too much intensity. They are abuse. And abuse isn't a stronger version of love. It's something else. It's the absence of it.

I want you to read very carefully. If you recognize any of these signs, you're not living an intense relationship. You're living a dangerous one:

The person controls where you go, who you talk to, what you wear, what you spend your money on. They pull you away from your friends and your family, little by little, until you're alone and dependent. They monitor your phone, demand to know where you are at all times, make jealous scenes that limit your life. They make you feel that you're crazy, that you're exaggerating, that you're imagining things (this is called manipulation, and it's a way of taking away your trust in your own judgment). They threaten, even if in subtle ways: they threaten to leave you, threaten to hurt themselves, threaten to tell things, threaten to hurt you. They humiliate you, belittle you, call you names, and then say you were the one who provoked it. There is physical or sexual violence, or you are afraid of it, even if it has never happened yet. After hurting you, they're charming, they apologize, they promise to change, and you cling to that version. And the cycle begins again.

This last point is important: the cycle of hurting and then charming is not proof that there's love underneath. It's the mechanism of control. It's what keeps you holding on to the hope of the good version, while you keep enduring the bad one.

If you recognized your relationship here, I need to tell you one thing with all the clarity I'm capable of: abuse doesn't ask for your understanding. It asks for your exit and your call for help. It's not your job to understand what hurt your abuser in childhood. It's not your job to love him enough for him to change. It's not your job to endure until he gets better. Abuse doesn't get better with more love on your part. Frequently, it gets worse.

So if this is your case, close this guide and seek specialized help. Talk to a victim support line. Talk to your family doctor. Talk to someone you trust who is outside the relationship. There are people trained to help you leave safely, because leaving an abusive relationship carries its own risks and deserves support. You don't have to do this alone, and you don't have to do this with a guide. You have to do this with real help.

Intensity asks you for self-knowledge. Abuse asks you for protection. Don't confuse the two.

## 4. SyncHim bridge

If you've reached this point and realized that your relationship isn't abusive, but is, even so, a relationship of too much intensity, of turbulence, of a roller coaster that wears you out, I want you to know there is work to be done. And that this work has two sides.

One side is the relationship. The other side, the deeper one, is you.

Because the question that gives its name to this world I created is exactly this: what makes me lose myself when I love? Why do I abandon firm ground to chase the storm? Why does calm feel empty to me? Why do I confuse the alarm with passion? The answers to these questions are not in the other person. They are in your story. In what you learned, as a child, that love was. In the patterns you inherited without knowing. In what your nervous system learned to recognize as home, even when that home was hurting you.

SyncHim is the space I created for those who want to understand and transform the way they love. For those who are tired of losing themselves in relationships and want to learn to love without abandoning themselves. For couples who want to step out of turbulence and build firm ground without losing the flame along the way. You'll find it at [synchim.viviannedossantos.com](https://synchim.viviannedossantos.com).

And if you feel that, before looking at any relationship, the work begins with you (and when it comes to losing yourself when you love, it almost always begins with you), FreeMe may be the starting point. It's where you look at your patterns, at what you inherited, at the way your body learned to confuse intensity with love. It's where you begin to re-educate your nervous system to recognize peace as home. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

But for now, stay with the test. Stay with the questions. Stay with the image of the storm and the firm ground. And the next time you feel that thing, that sweeping rush, that cold in the belly you confuse with love, stop for a moment and ask yourself honestly: is this love, or is it just intensity? Does this do me good, or does it only burn?

You don't have to answer in a hurry. You don't have to know everything today. But the simple fact of asking the question already changes something. Because from the moment you know that intensity isn't the same thing as love, you stop letting yourself be fooled by it so easily. And you begin, slowly, to look for firm ground. Not because it's less exciting. But because it's the only place where you can rest without falling.

And you deserve to rest. You deserve to love without losing yourself. You deserve a relationship that enlarges you instead of erasing you. Not as a reward for being perfect. Just for being who you are.
