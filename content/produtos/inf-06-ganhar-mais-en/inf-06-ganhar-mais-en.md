# The woman who does not allow herself to earn more

**For the woman who brakes when she could have more. Because it is not the money that frightens you, it is having more than those you are loyal to.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This book touches family loyalties and the relationship with abundance, a territory that can stir things deep. If, as you read, you feel old wounds open that are hard for you, a guilt that paralyzes you, or a suffering that does not pass, please seek the support of a professional. And an important note: this book does not speak of money management nor of finances. It speaks of the inner permission to exist in a certain size. If what you need is practical help with your economic situation, that is another conversation, equally legitimate, but it is not the one in this book.*

## 1. The invisible ceiling

There is a moment you have perhaps already lived several times, always with the same strangeness. You are about to have more. More money, more success, more recognition, more than you ever had. It is there, within your reach, you deserved it, you could have it. And something in you brakes. You retreat, you postpone, you sabotage, you find a reason not to advance, or you advance and then right after you feel a discomfort, a guilt, an urge to rid yourself of the excess. As if there were, above you, a ceiling that you do not see but that you feel, a limit up to which you can rise and from which something pulls you back.

And the most frustrating thing is that, often, you do want more. It is not that you lack the desire, or the ambition, or the will. You want, you dream, you strive, and even so, at the decisive moment, something brakes, as if there were in you two forces in conflict: one that wants to grow, and another, deeper and older, that does not let it. And it is that internal conflict, and not the lack of wanting, that leaves you exhausted and confused, because you strive in one direction and sabotage yourself in the other, without understanding why, feeling that you fail when, in truth, you are divided between a conscious desire to have more and an unconscious prohibition against having it. To understand this conflict, and where the prohibition comes from, is what can, finally, align the two forces, so you stop rowing against yourself.

That ceiling has nothing to do with your capacity. You are capable of much more than what you allow yourself to reach. The ceiling is not one of competence, it is one of authorization: there is a level beyond which you stop allowing yourself to have, even being able to, even deserving to, even wanting to. And the most curious thing is that the ceiling is always, more or less, at the level of those you belong to. You can rise up to near what your people have, what your family had, what the people of your milieu have, but to surpass that, to clearly have more than them, is where the ceiling tightens and something in you says no.

And there is a curious and revealing detail: often, the ceiling is not at an absolute number, it is at a comparison. It is not that you do not allow yourself to earn a certain amount, it is that you do not allow yourself to earn more than a specific person, your father, your mother, a sibling. The number that brakes you is, often, exactly the number they have or had, and you can rise comfortably up to near it, but to surpass it clearly is where the brake tightens. This shows that the ceiling is not about money in the abstract, it is about a concrete comparison with concrete people you are loyal to. And it is good news, in a way, because it means the ceiling is not a law about how much you can have, it is a loyalty to specific people, and loyalties, unlike laws, can be understood and reorganized, so that surpassing those you love ceases to seem a betrayal and becomes only what it is: your life taking its own dimension.

Notice how this manifests. Perhaps you earn well, but you spend or give in such a way that you never truly accumulate. Perhaps you refuse opportunities to earn more with reasons that seem good but that, looked at closely, are excuses. Perhaps you work a lot and charge little, or you have difficulty asking for what you are worth. Perhaps, whenever you are about to make a leap, something happens, often provoked by you without your noticing, that makes you retreat. Perhaps you feel a physical discomfort, a real guilt, when you have more than the people around you. Each of these things is the same invisible ceiling at work: an inner limit on how much you allow yourself to have.

And perhaps you recognize the ceiling in concrete scenes. It is the project that was going too well and that, without your noticing, you sabotaged right at the door of the leap. It is the raise you did not ask for, the opportunity you refused, the price you did not charge, always with a good reason on top, and a guilt underneath. It is the money that comes in and that, mysteriously, always goes out, without ever letting you accumulate beyond a certain level. It is the physical discomfort, real, that you feel when you buy something expensive, when you earn more than a sibling, when you have something your parents never had. It is the hurry to give, to distribute, to help, whenever you have extra, as if you had to rid yourself of the excess to return to the safe level. Each of these scenes is the same ceiling at work, keeping you at the level of your people, preventing you from surpassing a line that no one marked but that you feel with precision.

I want to tell you, right from the start, that this ceiling is not a lack of ambition, nor laziness, nor incompetence, nor bad luck. It is something else, much deeper: it is an inner prohibition, old, against having more than those you are loyal to. At some point, without deciding it, you learned that having more than your people would be dangerous, would be a betrayal, would be leaving them behind, and you built, out of love, a ceiling that keeps you at their level. The ceiling is not a defect of yours. It is a loyalty disguised as a limit, and that is what this book is about.

And there is a particular loneliness in this wound, that few understand. Because from the outside, your restraint looks like humility, generosity, a lack of greed, and it is even praised: people say you are not materialistic, that you do not let yourself be carried away by money, that you are unattached. No one sees that, beneath that apparent virtue, there is a woman who brakes herself, who does not allow herself, who lives below what she could, not by free choice but out of a guilt and a loyalty that grip her. And when you try to explain that there is something preventing you from prospering, either they do not understand you, or they think it is just a matter of trying harder, without seeing the inner ceiling that brakes you. That loneliness, of an inner struggle that others read as virtue or as a lack of effort, is one of the most silent there is, and I want you to know that I see it, and that it is real, and that braking yourself is neither virtue nor laziness, it is a wound that deserves to be cared for.

And there is something I need to clarify right away, because it changes everything: this book is not about money. It may seem so, because the most obvious "having more" is money, but the wound is not your relationship with money, it is your relationship with the authorization to exist in a certain size. The proof is simple: if instead of earning more money, you were to have more success, more visibility, more power, more than your people, the ceiling would tighten all the same. It is not the money that frightens you. It is surpassing those you love, and what that seems to cost. That is why this is a book about identity, about authorization, about the right to have more without that meaning loving less. And that is the path we are going to take.

## 2. Having more than my people

Let us go to the root, which is a very specific and very powerful loyalty: loyalty to those who had less. Because your ceiling, most of the time, is at the level of your origin, and surpassing it activates a deep fear, that of having more than those you belong to, and of, in having it, betraying them, leaving them behind, separating yourself from them.

And there is a frequent root of this loyalty worth naming, because it removes the guilt: often, it was not born in you, it was transmitted to you. You grew up, perhaps, hearing that money corrupts, that rich people are bad, that whoever has much took it from someone, that humility lies in not wanting too much. Phrases said at home, in your community, perhaps in your culture, that associated having with greed and not-having with virtue. And you absorbed that association before you were old enough to question it, and today it brakes you, making you feel that to prosper is to become one of those bad people you used to hear about. To recognize that this belief was inherited, and not chosen, is to begin to free it, because what was transmitted can be examined and given back, and you can decide, as an adult, that prospering does not make you bad, that abundance in the hands of a good person is a force for good, and that you can have much and continue to be exactly who you are.

In the way I learned to look at families, there is a principle that explains this: belonging to a group creates invisible loyalties, and one of the strongest is loyalty to the level of the group. We belong by sharing a condition, and having much more than those with whom we share the belonging can feel, deep down, like a breaking of the pact, a leaving of the group, almost a desertion. If your family had little, if your milieu is of people who struggle, having much more can bring a deep guilt, the guilt of having left, of having escaped, of having what they do not have, while they remain where they are.

Notice the ways in which this loyalty manifests. It can be the guilt you feel when you buy something your people could not buy. It can be the discomfort of earning more than your parents ever earned, as if that were a way of diminishing them. It can be the difficulty in enjoying what you have when you think of those you love who have less. It can be the impulse to give, to help, to distribute what you earn, not only out of generosity, but to relieve the guilt of having, to level yourself back, so as not to keep more than your people. It can be sabotaging yourself, without knowing it, whenever you are about to clearly surpass the level of your origin. In all of them, the same loyalty: I cannot have much more than my people.

And there is a particularly painful form of this loyalty, when "having more" makes you surpass concrete and close people. To surpass your parents, to have a larger life than theirs, can bring a strange guilt, as if you were disrespecting them by being larger. To surpass your siblings, to have more than them, can bring the fear of creating distance, envy, resentment, of breaking the equality that united you. To surpass your partner, to earn more than him, to have more success than him, can bring a specific fear of threatening the relationship, of diminishing him, of breaking a balance. In each case, to have more is not only to have, it is to surpass someone you love, and it is that surpassing, and not the having itself, that frightens you and brakes you.

And there is a form of this loyalty that deserves to be named, because it is one of the most painful: survivor's guilt. It is the name given to the guilt of someone who escaped something that caught others, of someone who had a luck that their people did not have. If you left a difficult milieu, if you prospered while siblings, cousins, childhood friends were left behind, still struggling with what you already surpassed, you may carry a deep guilt just for having made it, as if your success were a way of abandoning them, of betraying them, of putting yourself above them. And that guilt can lead you to sabotage your prosperity, to not allow yourself to enjoy what you conquered, to keep yourself bound to their difficulty through your own restraint, as if suffering a little along with them were the way not to leave them alone. It is a love, this, but a love that does not help them and that brakes you, and to recognize it is the first step to transforming it into a solidarity that actually helps, instead of a guilt that only diminishes you.

I want you to see this loyalty without condemning it, because it is born of love and of belonging. There is nothing wrong with loving your origin, with wanting to belong, with caring about those who have less. The problem is not the love, it is the way you learned to express it: by keeping yourself at the level of others, not allowing yourself to have more, braking yourself so as not to surpass them. And there is another way, that we are going to discover: a way of having more that betrays no one, of growing without leaving anyone behind, of honoring your origin without keeping yourself small within it. But first you have to see the loyalty that sets your ceiling, and recognize that your brake is not a lack of ambition, it is love mistranslated into a limit.

## 3. It is not the money, it is the consequence

There is a sibling book to this one, about the woman who is afraid of her own size, and it is worth seeing the difference between the two, because it clarifies exactly what is happening with you. That woman is afraid of being large, of shining, of occupying her space: the fear is of the size itself, "can I be large?" You, in this book, perhaps already allow yourself to be large, perhaps already occupy your space, and your fear is of something else, of the next step: of the consequence of the size, of the having, of the receiving, of accumulating what the size brings. Your question is not "can I be large?", it is "can I have more?"

You may recognize yourself, in truth, in both books, the one about size and this one, because the two wounds usually go together. But notice which of them is your main one, because that orients the path. If your bigger brake is showing yourself, shining, occupying space, being seen, your main wound is size. If you already show yourself, already shine, but you brake when that translates into having, into receiving, into accumulating, into earning more than your people, then your main wound is this one, the wound of the authorization to have. Many women manage to be admired, recognized, even successful in others' eyes, and even so they sabotage the having, because being was more permitted to them than having. To know which is your main wound helps you to focus the work: not on the shining, which you perhaps already do, but on allowing yourself to receive and to keep what the shine brings.

They are sibling wounds, but not the same. One can allow oneself to be large and still brake at the having. There are women who authorize themselves to shine, to stand out, to be recognized, but who, when that shine translates into having more, earning more, accumulating more, retreat, because the having, and not the being, is what activates the guilt of the loyalty. To be large can be admired, applauded, even by the origin. To have more is more concrete, more visible, harder to forgive, because it makes the difference tangible, measurable, undeniable. It is one thing to be brilliant; it is another to have, in the account, in the house, in the life, clearly more than your people.

And that is why I insist that the money is not the problem, it is the consequence of what the money represents: the concrete difference in relation to your people. The money is only the most obvious example, because it is the most measurable, the easiest to compare. But the same brake would appear in any form of having more that made the difference concrete. You are not afraid of the numbers. You are afraid of what the numbers mean: that you surpassed, that you have more, that you separated yourself, that you are no longer equal to your people. The money is the messenger; the message is the difference, and it is from the difference that you protect yourself, by braking the having.

And I want you to understand, clearly, why this distinction matters so much for your path. There is a whole industry that promises to teach you to have more money, to prosper, to break financial ceilings, with strategies, abundance mindsets, techniques. And much of that promise fails with women like you, not because the techniques are bad, but because they do not touch the root. You can learn all the prosperity strategies in the world and continue to sabotage yourself, because your brake is not in the lack of strategy, it is in a loyalty and a guilt that no financial technique reaches. It is like trying to resolve with gardening tricks a plant that is being held back not by the technique of whoever tends it, but by a rope tied to the stem that no one sees. Until the rope, the loyalty, the guilt, is seen and loosened, no technique lets it grow. That is why this book does not give you money strategies: it goes to the rope, to the authorization, because it is only there, and not in the techniques, that your ceiling truly lifts.

That is why this book stays in the domain of identity, and not in that of money. Because if your problem were really money, it would be resolved with more financial knowledge, better strategies, more information about how to earn and manage. And you know, deep down, that it is not that way, that you can have all the information in the world and continue to brake, because the brake is not in the head that knows how to manage money, it is in the heart that does not allow itself to have more than what it loves. The root is the authorization, not financial competence. And that is why the solution is also not financial: it is inner, it is about the permission you give yourself, or do not, to exist in a size larger than the one to which the loyalty confines you.

And there is a way to confirm, in your own experience, that it is not the money, it is the consequence. Notice what happens when you earn something that does not make you surpass anyone you are loyal to: there is no brake, there is no guilt, you earn it with naturalness. The brake only appears when the having makes you cross the line, surpass your people, become visibly different. If it were the money itself, the discomfort would appear with any gain. But it does not appear with any gain, it appears with the gain that separates you, that raises you above your origin. That proves that what brakes you is not the quantity, it is the difference, it is not the number, it is what the number means in relation to your people. And that is the best proof that your wound is one of belonging and of loyalty, and not of money, and that it is there, in belonging and in loyalty, and not in finances, that the path lies.

And I want you to see that this distinction frees you from a wrong search. As long as you think the problem is money, you are going to look for money solutions, and they will not work, because they do not touch the root. When you see that the problem is the authorization, the loyalty, the fear of surpassing those you love, you can finally look in the right place, which is inside, in your relationship with your origin and with your right to have more. And there, yes, there is a path, because the authorization, unlike the economic circumstances, depends on you, it is yours to give. And that is what we are going to discover.

## 4. Who authorizes me

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you to stay with it, because it contains the key to everything.

Who is it that you are waiting for to authorize you to have more?

Stay with it a little, without rushing to answer.

Because there is, at the bottom of your brake, a waiting. You are waiting for an authorization that never comes. Waiting for someone, your family, your parents, the world, to tell you "you can, you have permission, it is all right for you to have more." As if there were an external authorization missing, and you could not advance without it. And you live, without knowing it, waiting for that permission, braked at the door of what you could have, waiting for someone to open it for you, for someone to give you the license you feel you lack to surpass the level of your people without guilt.

And the hard and liberating truth is this: that authorization is never going to come from outside. Not because people are bad, but because it is not theirs to give. Your parents are not going to give you permission to surpass them, because often they do not even know you need it, and because the permission for your life is not theirs. The world is not going to give you a license to have more, because the world does not distribute licenses like that. The thing you wait for to come from outside, the authorization to exist in a larger size, to have more, is not in anyone's hands but yours. And as long as you wait for it out there, you are going to wait forever, braked, at the door of your own life, waiting for a key that only you have.

And there is a subtle way in which this waiting disguises itself, and that is worth recognizing: the "when." "When I am more prepared, I will authorize myself." "When I deserve it, I will advance." "When the time is right." The "when" seems responsible, seems like prudence, but often it is the waiting in disguise, it is the way you postpone indefinitely the authorization you never give yourself. Because the right time never comes, being ready never happens completely, the deserving never feels sufficient, and so the "when" keeps you in permanent waiting, always about to authorize yourself, never actually authorizing yourself. Notice your "whens," and distrust them, because they are, often, the ceiling dressing itself up as prudence. The authorization does not come after you are ready. It is the authorization that allows you to become ready, because it is by advancing that one learns, not by waiting for the perfect readiness that never comes.

Notice what this waiting does to you. It keeps you in the position of a child who needs the parents' permission, instead of an adult who disposes of her own life. It keeps you dependent on an approval you do not control, hostage to a yes that may never come. And, above all, it prevents you from seeing that the authorization you are looking for is already yours, that it was always yours, that no one has to give it to you because you already have it, only you do not recognize it, and so you look for it out there, where it is not. Your ceiling is made of this waiting: of the belief that you need an external permission that, in truth, only you can give yourself.

And there is a root of this waiting worth seeing. Often, we wait for the authorization from outside because, as children, it was from outside that it came: it was the parents who authorized or forbade, who said what we could or could not have, be, do. We grew up needing their permission, and that need can stay, even after we are adults, transferred to life in general: we continue waiting for a figure who authorizes us, who tells us "you can," as the parents used to. But there is a moment, in a person's growth, when the authority has to pass from outside to inside, when we stop needing someone to authorize us and we begin to authorize ourselves. That moment, for many women, when it comes to having more, never arrived, and they continue, as adults, waiting for a parental permission that no longer makes sense to wait for. To grow up is, in part, to take for oneself the authority one expected from outside, and to tell oneself the "you can" one was waiting to hear from others.

And I want to tell you what changes when you see this. The moment you realize that the authorization is yours to give, you leave the position of waiting and you enter the position of power. You are no longer waiting for someone to free you; you can free yourself. You no longer depend on an external yes; you can give yourself your yes. This does not make the path easy, because giving yourself the authorization requires facing the guilt, the loyalty, the fear of betraying, but it makes it possible, because it puts the key in your hands, where it always was. You stop being the woman who waits for permission to have, and you begin to be the woman who authorizes herself, and that change, from waiting to authorization, is the heart of this crossing.

## 5. The guilt over abundance

I have to speak now of the guilt, because it is the guardian of your ceiling, and until it is understood, no authorization holds. The guilt over abundance, the guilt of having more, is one of the most subtle and most powerful there is, and I want to look at it carefully, without romanticizing it nor dismissing it.

The guilt over abundance says, more or less, this: I have no right to have more when there are those who have less, especially when those are my people. It is a guilt that mixes love, loyalty, and a certain idea of justice, and that is why it is so hard to undo, because it seems noble, seems solidary, seems the opposite of selfishness. But, looked at closely, this guilt helps no one. Your deprivation does not enrich those who have less. The fact that you do not have more gives more to no one. The guilt over abundance is a form of suffering that does not relieve the suffering of others, it only adds yours to theirs, in a solidarity that does not make anyone solidary, that only subtracts.

And I want to be careful here, because there are two opposite errors to avoid. One is to romanticize poverty, lack, deprivation, as if there were virtue in not having, as if having little were more pure, more dignified, more spiritual. It is not. Lack has no nobility at all, and to romanticize it is a way of accepting injustice and of preventing yourself from prospering. The other error is the opposite: to demonize having, to treat abundance as dirty, success as betrayal, money as evil. That is also not true. To have, to prosper, to be abundant, has nothing wrong about it in itself, and it can even be a force for good, for you and for others. The just path is neither to romanticize lack nor to demonize having: it is to recognize that you have a right to abundance, and that that abundance, far from being a betrayal, can be a blessing, including for those you love.

And I want you to see something about the relationship between your abundance and that of others, because the guilt distorts it. The guilt works as if wealth were a fixed cake, in which what you have extra is taken from those who have less, as if your having were, literally, the cause of their lack. But, in most cases, it is not so: what you have was not taken from those you love, and what you do not have would not be given to them. Your deprivation fills no one's plate. If you refuse an opportunity, it does not go to your sibling who needs it, it goes to some other person. If you do not earn more, that money does not reach your mother who had less, it simply does not exist in your life. The guilt creates a false link between your having and their lack, and from that false link is born the idea that to prosper is to steal. To see that this link is false, that your having is not the cause of their lack, frees you from a guilt that rests on an error, and opens space for a real solidarity, that actually helps, instead of a guilt that only brakes you.

Because here is a truth that the guilt hides: your abundance can help those you love much more than your deprivation. If you have more, you can give more, support more, open more paths, be more useful to those around you. An abundant woman has more to share than a deprived woman. Your guilt tells you that having is taking from others, but often it is the opposite: your having can add to the life of those you love, while your not-having adds nothing to anyone. So, to prosper is not to betray your people, it can be to serve them, and abundance, used with love, does not distance you from those who have less, it gives you more with which to love them in practice.

And I want to touch on a specific and painful form of this guilt, the one that appears in relation to the partner. For many women, earning more than the partner, having more success than him, brings a particular guilt and fear: the fear of diminishing him, of threatening him, of breaking a balance, of putting the relationship at risk. And so they brake themselves, they shrink their success, they make themselves smaller so that he does not feel smaller. This is common, and it is sad, because it asks a woman to diminish herself so that a man does not feel diminished. I want to tell you that a partner who needs you to be smaller in order to feel good has a wound that is his to tend to, not yours to accommodate with your smallness. And a true love does not ask you to brake yourself to protect it, it rejoices in your success and grows with you. To brake yourself to protect your partner's place does not save the relationship, it impoverishes it, and it impoverishes you. Here too, having more does not have to mean loving less, nor being loved less.

I want to leave you with a distinction that frees: one thing is solidarity, which is good, another is guilt, which serves nothing. You can be profoundly solidary, you can care about those who have less, you can want a more just world, and at the same time allow yourself your abundance, without guilt. True solidarity does not require that you deprive yourself, it requires that you care and that you help, and one helps better from abundance than from deprivation. Guilt, on the other hand, only diminishes you without helping anyone. Let go of the guilt, keep the solidarity. Have more, and use what you have for good. There is no contradiction between prospering and being a good person, and the belief that there is is precisely what keeps you under the ceiling, poor on the inside even when you could be abundant.

## 6. The authorization is yours to give

We have arrived at the verb of this crossing, and it is a verb of power: to authorize. To authorize yourself to have more. To give yourself the permission you waited for from outside and that never came, because it was never outside to give. I am not going to give you a recipe for lifting the ceiling in seven steps, because the ceiling does not yield to recipes; I am going to point you to the direction in which it loosens, and the rest you do yourself, in your own measure.

To authorize yourself begins by recognizing, truly, that the authorization is yours. That you do not need your parents to approve, your family to understand, the world to give you a license. That you are an adult, owner of your own life, and that you have the right to decide the size of that life, including the right to make it larger than that of your people. This seems simple but it is revolutionary, because your whole life you waited for an external permission, and to realize that it is yours is like receiving a key you always had in your pocket and never knew was there. The authorization is not conquered, not deserved, not waited for. It is given. And only you can give it.

Then, to authorize yourself requires facing the guilt and the loyalty, not with force, but with understanding. It is to recognize the loyalty that binds you, "I cannot have more than my people," and to be able, on the inside, to free it, saying to those you love, internally, something like: I love you, I belong to you, and I am going to have more, not against you, but from you, and what I have does not diminish you, and my love for you does not diminish because I have more. It is to separate the having from the loving, to show yourself that you can have more and continue to love the same, that abundance does not steal the love, that to prosper is not to betray. Each time you authorize yourself to have a little more, and the world does not end, and the love of your people does not disappear, the loyalty loosens, and the ceiling rises.

And there is a systemic gesture that helps, and that comes from the way I learned to look at these things: to honor those who had less is not to have less like them, it is to take farther what they gave you. Your parents, your grandparents, those who came before and had little, often struggled and deprived themselves precisely so that you could have more than them. When you brake yourself, when you keep yourself at their level out of loyalty, it is as if their sacrifice stopped in you, did not bear fruit. When you prosper, when you have what they did not have, you realize the deepest wish of those who sacrificed themselves for you: that your life be larger than theirs. To have more, seen this way, is not to betray those who had less. It is to honor them, it is to bear fruit to what they planted, it is to make their sacrifice worthwhile. Your abundance can be the harvest of the seeds they sowed without ever seeing grow.

And I want to give you an image of what it is to authorize yourself, because it may seem abstract. Imagine a door to a larger life, that was always unlocked, and in front of which you spent years standing still, waiting for someone to come and open it for you, for someone to give you the key, without realizing that the door was never locked, that you only had to push it, that the key you were waiting for was already in the lock, on your side. To authorize yourself is to realize that the door was always open, and to cross it. It is not to force anything, it is not to conquer a difficult permission, it is simply to stop waiting and to take the step you could always take. And the most moving thing is to realize how much time you spent standing still in front of an open door, waiting for an authorization you did not need, when it was enough to know that you could, and to go. Most invisible ceilings are like that: open doors in front of which we wait, our whole life, for a permission we always had.

There is a sentence that holds the heart of this, and that you can say on the inside: I can have more without loving less. Notice what it separates. To have more: I authorize myself to abundance, to success, to growth, to what life offers me. Without loving less: and that does not diminish in the slightest my love, my loyalty, my belonging to my people. The two things do not touch. Love is not measured by leveling, loyalty does not require deprivation, and you can have everything life offers you and continue to love your people exactly as you always loved them, perhaps even more, because the love that comes from abundance is freer and more generous than the one that comes from guilty deprivation.

## 7. Having more without loving less

If the authorization is yours, and if having more does not betray those you love, then the destination of this crossing is clear: to have more without loving less. To prosper without guilt, to grow without betrayal, to be abundant without separating yourself from who you are and from those you love. Here there is no number of steps to fulfill nor a height to reach; there is a sky that was always open, and the only thing I ask of you is that you begin to fly a little higher than the cage taught you.

And I want you to know that giving yourself authorization is not a single great moment, it is a repeated practice, almost daily. There is no one day when you authorize yourself once and for all and the ceiling disappears. There are, rather, a thousand small moments when you choose to authorize yourself instead of braking yourself: to ask for what you are worth, to accept what you deserve, to keep what you receive, to allow yourself to want more, to enjoy what you have without guilt. Each of these moments is a small act of authorization, and together, repeated, they gradually raise the ceiling, slowly. Do not demand of yourself that you authorize yourself perfectly, nor all at once. Begin with the next moment when you catch yourself braking, and, in that moment, choose to authorize yourself a little more than you usually do. It is in the sum of those small yeses to yourself that the ceiling, piece by piece, lifts, and that you discover, with time, that much more life fits above it than you ever let yourself imagine.

The first step is to stop waiting for the authorization and to begin to give yourself small authorizations, in concrete things. To allow yourself to receive what you deserve without giving it back. To allow yourself to ask for what you are worth without apologizing. To allow yourself to have something your people did not have without the immediate guilt. To allow yourself to enjoy what you have without spoiling it with the thought of those who have less. Each of these small authorizations is a training, and the small ones prepare the large ones. As you authorize yourself, in low-risk things, and discover that you are not punished, that the love of your people does not disappear, that the world bears you abundant, the ceiling rises, and having more becomes, slowly, more inhabitable.

And I want to speak to you about a concrete way of prospering without betraying the loyalty, that many women discover with relief: that of taking your people with you, in some way, in your rise. I am not asking you to distribute everything to relieve the guilt, that would be to return to the leveling. But there is a difference between prospering and closing the door behind you, and prospering and leaving the door open for those who come after. You can have more and, from that more, open paths, create opportunities, be an example, help when it makes sense, show those who come from your origin that it is possible. This way, your prosperity stops being a separation and becomes a bridge: you do not distance yourself from your people by rising, you take them with you, not through your deprivation, but through your abundance shared with wisdom. This is the difference between the guilt, which brakes you and helps no one, and the generosity, which lets you prosper and still take care of those you love. Prosper, and leave the door open. It is possible to have both things.

Then, learn to receive without guilt, because that is where everything is decided. The woman who does not allow herself to have more usually has an enormous difficulty in receiving: she gives back, refuses, minimizes, feels in debt for everything she receives. And to learn to receive fully, without guilt, without the hurry to repay, without the discount of "I do not deserve it," is the concrete way of raising the ceiling. To receive is to let in the abundance, it is to recognize that you have a right to what comes to you, that you deserve it, that you fit in the larger life. Next time life offers you more, instead of refusing it out of loyalty to those who have less, try to receive, and to use what you receive, too, for the good of those you love. To receive does not distance you from them. It gives you more with which to love them.

And I want to give you a concrete inner gesture, that comes from the way I learned to look at family systems, and that many women find relieving. It is to look, on the inside, at those who had less, your parents, your grandparents, those who came before and struggled, and to say to them, in silence, something like: I see what you lacked, I respect your struggle, and I am going to have what you could not have, not against you, but for you, taking farther what you gave me. I receive the life you gave me and I make it grow, and that honors you. This gesture, of receiving the abundance as a continuation of what they began, and not as a betrayal of what they did not have, transforms the guilt into gratitude, and the ceiling into permission. Because you stop seeing your having as a distancing from them and you begin to see it as the fruit of what they planted, and no one betrays those they honor by bearing fruit to the seeds they received.

And there is the deepest work, which is to make peace with your origin from abundance, and not from deprivation. To recognize that you can love your people profoundly, honor them, belong to them, and at the same time have a larger life than theirs. That your prosperity is not an accusation against their poverty, nor an abandonment, nor a betrayal. That you can be abundant and continue to be a daughter, a sister, part of your people. And that, far from distancing you from them, your abundance, lived without guilt and shared with love, can bring you closer, because a woman who prospers without guilt is a freer woman, more generous, more capable of loving and of giving. To have more without loving less is not only possible: it is the fullest way of honoring where you come from, taking what you received farther than it ever went.

And I want to leave you with an image that may accompany you, that of flight. Think of a bird raised in a low cage, that learned to fly only up to the height of the cage's ceiling, and that, even when the door opens and it goes out, continues to fly low, at the height it got used to, because it internalized a ceiling that no longer exists. The sky is all up there, open, waiting, and the bird flies close to the ground, not because it cannot rise, but because it learned that above a certain height there is a limit, and it carries that limit with it even after it has disappeared. You are that bird. The ceiling that brakes you is no longer there, or never was where you think it is, it is a limit you internalized and that you carry, and that makes you fly low in a sky that is, in truth, all open for you. To authorize yourself is to discover that the ceiling is gone, or never truly held you, and to rise, slowly, discovering at each meter that the sky bears you, that you can go higher, that you always could, and that all that was missing was for you to stop flying at the height of a cage that no longer exists.

I do not promise you that this will be simple, because to undo an old loyalty and a deep guilt is work that is done slowly, and often with someone to accompany you, because it touches your deepest bonds. But I promise you that on the other side there is an enormous freedom: that of being able to have everything life offers you without braking it, without sabotaging it, without spoiling it with guilt. That of existing in your full size, with everything it brings. And that of discovering that having more was never, after all, betraying those you love, and that the authorization you waited for your whole life was always in your hands, waiting for you to finally give it to yourself.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not change everything at once, and it does not lift in a day a ceiling built over a lifetime. But it changes enough so you can stop blaming yourself for braking, and begin to see the loyalty and the guilt that set your ceiling, and the key you always had to lift it.

And I want you to take with you the parallel that links this book to the others of Infonte, because it gives meaning to the whole. In all of them, the crossing is the same: to distinguish what is yours from what is not, and to return to what is truly yours. In the woman who never arrives, it is to distinguish what she seeks from what she pursues. In the one who inherited a life, it is to distinguish her life from the one she was given. Here, it is to distinguish your right to have from the ceiling you inherited, and to give back the prohibition to whoever gave it to you, keeping your freedom to prosper. It is always the same movement, seen from different angles: to see what you carry or pursue without it being yours, to give it back, and to occupy, to choose, to authorize what is, in fact, your life. And in the end, all these crossings lead to the same place: the return to yourself, to your own life, free of the weights, of the scripts, of the prohibitions you did not choose, and finally yours.

Now you know that the ceiling you feel is not a lack of capacity, it is a lack of authorization. You know that the root is loyalty to those who had less, the fear of surpassing your people. You know that it is not the money, it is the consequence, the concrete difference in relation to your origin. You know that you wait for an authorization that never comes from outside, because it is not outside to give. You know that the guilt over abundance does not help those you love, it only diminishes you. And you know that the verb of all this is to authorize: to give yourself the permission.

And you know, perhaps, that this ceiling does not limit only you, it limits what you can give to the world. Imagine what you would do, what you would create, what you would change, if you authorized yourself to your full size and to the abundance it can bring. Imagine your capacity, freed from the brake, yielding everything it can yield, and the good you could do with it, for yourself, for those you love, for the world. Your ceiling robs not only you, it robs the world of everything you would do if you allowed yourself to prosper. That is why to authorize yourself is not selfishness, it is, deep down, generosity: it is to free your capacity so that it bears its whole fruit, instead of keeping it braked by a loyalty that helps no one. The world needs women who authorize themselves, who prosper without guilt, who occupy their size and use their abundance for good. To authorize yourself is, also, a service.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can have more without loving less.

Seeing your invisible ceiling was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not read, it is done: it is done by authorizing yourself, slowly, to have more, and by discovering that the love of your people does not disappear when you grow.

Not so you stop loving your origin, but so you discover that you can honor it and surpass it at the same time, that having more is not loving less, and that abundance, lived without guilt, can be a blessing for you and for those you love.

There, the question "who authorizes me" ceases to be a waiting and becomes an answer: I authorize myself. The guilt over abundance ceases to be a guardian and begins to loosen. And to have more ceases to look like betrayal and reveals itself as what it can be: the harvest of what your people sowed, taken farther by you.
