# The woman who makes herself indispensable

**For you, who make yourself necessary so that you won't be left. Because being needed is not being loved, and you can be chosen without making yourself indispensable.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of a way of loving that makes itself indispensable in order not to be abandoned, and there is a point at which this stops being devotion and begins to drain you until you disappear. If you recognize in yourself an exhaustion that won't pass, a panic of abandonment that pulls the ground out from under you, or an erasure of yourself in the name of the other, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your pattern is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I make myself needed

Notice what you do when you love.

You make yourself useful. Quickly. Before they ask you.

You solve what he doesn't solve. You anticipate what he's going to need. You take care of his things, his home, his life, his emotions.

Before long, he no longer knows how to live without you. And you breathe.

Because that's what you're looking for, without saying it. To make yourself indispensable. To make yourself so necessary that he can't leave you.

You call this love. And there is love, yes. But there's something else underneath, older.

There's a silent calculation. If he needs me a lot, he won't leave me. So, I'll make him need me.

Look at your relationships. You're always the one who gives the most, who does the most, who holds everything together. The pillar. The one who can't be absent.

And there's a pride in that. "Without me, the whole thing would have collapsed." But there's also an enormous tiredness, and a loneliness you don't understand.

The loneliness of someone who is very necessary and little known. Very useful and little loved for who she is.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that you're not pretending. Your love is real. Your devotion is true.

But devotion became your currency. You pay for your place in love with usefulness. As if without it you had no right to stay.

This book will not teach you to give less in order to hold him. I didn't come to give you games.

I came to show you something you may never have seen. That being needed is not being loved. And that you confused the two so long ago that you don't even notice anymore.

I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can be chosen without making yourself indispensable. You can be loved for who you are, and not for what you do. And the fear that sets you to doing so much, the fear of being left, is not cured by doing more. It's cured another way.

I want you to notice a contradiction. You want, deeply, to be loved for who you are. You dream of someone who sees you, who wants you, not what you do.

And at the same time you do everything, without stopping, to never give anyone the chance to love you for who you are. Because you never show yourself. You're always hidden behind what you do.

You long to be seen, and you never let yourself be seen. You want to be loved for yourself, and you never stop serving long enough for anyone to get to know you.

That's why, even surrounded by those who need you, you feel alone. Because being needed is not being known. And you built your entire life on the first thing, leaving the second, the one you most wanted, unrealized.

Maybe you'll recognize yourself in small things.

In feeling an unease when he doesn't need anything from you. In almost provoking a need, just to be useful again.

In not being able to bear that he solves it on his own, because if he can do it on his own, then what are you for.

In measuring your place in the relationship by the number of things that depend on you.

They are all the same. Usefulness as insurance against abandonment. The manufactured need to guarantee that they won't leave you.

And there's a particular exhaustion in this, which you may already take for normal. The exhaustion of someone who can never stop. Because stopping, for you, isn't resting, it's risking. It's ceasing to be necessary for a moment, and in that moment the fear opens up that, without your usefulness, they'll leave you. So you continue, even destroyed, doing, solving, anticipating, because rest costs you more than the tiredness: tiredness only takes away your body, rest threatens your place.

Notice, too, what you feel when someone praises you for being tireless, for doing so much, for being essential. You feel pride, yes, but also a weight, because that praise confirms the trap: you are loved for what you do, and the day you don't do it, you fear, you'll cease to be loved. The recognition of your usefulness doesn't calm your fear, it feeds it, because it reminds you that it's usefulness that's applauded, and not you.

There's a detail you might recognize. You don't know how to ask.

To give, you know with your eyes closed. To ask is almost impossible for you. To ask for help, to ask for attention, to ask to be cared for, gets stuck in your throat.

Because to ask is to show that you need. And to show that you need, in your world, is dangerous. It's putting yourself on the weak side, the side of the one who can be left.

So you don't ask. You give. And you wait, in silence, for the other to guess and give back. And he almost never guesses. And you resent it, silently, giving so much and receiving so little.

But notice. You're the one who set this up. You chose the role of the one who gives, and refused the one who receives. And then you complain about only giving.

There's another thing. You control, through usefulness.

As long as you're the one doing everything, you're the one in charge, the one who knows, the one who holds the helm. To let go, to let him do it, to trust that he manages without you, is to lose control. And losing control, for someone who fears abandonment, is terrifying.

That's why your devotion has, underneath, a closed fist. You give to bind. You serve to command. You make yourself necessary so as never to be at the mercy of the other's decision to stay or leave.

There's a detail that reveals everything, and I want you to notice it. What you feel when someone you take care of becomes capable, autonomous, stops needing you.

Instead of joy at his growth, there often appears a tightening. An unease. An emptiness. "And now, what am I for?" As if the autonomy of the one you love were a threat, and not good news.

Notice what this says. A part of you, without wanting to, needs others to need. It roots, without knowing it, for their dependency, because their dependency is the guarantee of your place. And that isn't malice. It's the fear of abandonment operating backstage, making you want to keep close, dependent, the one you fear losing.

But there's a deep sadness here. Because to love someone is to want him whole, capable, free, even if that means he no longer depends on you to live. And your indispensability, in needing him to need, comes into conflict with the love you say you have, because love wants the other to be large, and your wound wants the other dependent.

Seeing this is not to blame yourself. It's to understand that your way of loving has, underneath, a fear, and that this fear makes you want the opposite of what love wants. And that releasing the indispensability is, deep down, letting yourself be truly loved, wanting the other free, and risking that he'll choose you for it.

## 2. Necessary so as not to be left

Go back. Further back than any boyfriend.

The question underneath all of this is simple, and it's old. What do I have to do so that they won't leave me?

You weren't born with this question. You learned it.

You learned it in a place where your place wasn't secure. Where staying wasn't guaranteed. Where you had to do something to keep yourself in someone's affection.

Maybe you were the child who realized that, when she was useful, she received attention. And that, when she simply was, she went unnoticed.

Maybe you grew up in a house where you had to relieve the adults. Help. Care. Not be any trouble. Be the one who solved things, to deserve your place.

Maybe you felt, early on, that love could go away. That it wasn't unconditional. That you had to hold it with your hands, by doing, giving, being needed.

Notice what a child learns in a place like this.

She learns that existing is not enough to be loved. That she has to serve. That love is paid for with usefulness.

She learns that being left is a real danger, and that the way to avoid it is to make herself necessary, to the point where they cannot do without her.

And that child grows up. But the question remains. What do I have to do so that they won't leave me? And the answer she found as a little girl keeps governing her: I make myself indispensable.

That's why, when you love, you can't simply be. You have to do. To solve. To make yourself the pillar. Because stopping doing, for you, is risking being left.

There's a way of seeing families that illuminates this. A child's place should be secure simply by being a child. Unconditional. Not deserved.

When that place isn't secure, when the child feels she has to earn it, something breaks. She spends her life paying rent for a place that should have been hers for free.

And as an adult, in love, she does the same. She pays the rent. With usefulness, with devotion, with making herself necessary. She never rests in love, because she thinks love is a rented place, and that if she stops paying, she's evicted.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing whoever raised you.

Whoever didn't give you a secure place, most of the time, didn't have a secure place to give you. They gave what they had. They didn't mean to wound you.

But the truth is this. You learned that your place had to be earned. And today you keep earning it, every day, in love, with a tiredness that no longer ends.

There's a figure you might recognize from your childhood. An adult who only saw you when you were useful to him.

Who lit up when you brought good grades, when you helped, when you solved things, and who went dark, indifferent, when you simply were. Who rewarded the doing and ignored the being.

A child like that learns quickly where love is. It's in the doing. And she runs there, and settles there, and never again leaves there.

Or maybe you saw, at home, what happened to the one who wasn't useful. A sibling pushed aside, a relative scorned for not serving. And you swore, without words, never to be her. Never to be the dispensable one. Whatever it cost.

And there's also the child who became indispensable to her own parents. Who took care of them, of the house, of the siblings, before she was old enough for it. Who was the pillar when she should have been the daughter.

If you were her, you learned your entire identity in being needed. That's how you had a place in the family. And you carried that place, the place of pillar, into every love, because it's the only one you know how to occupy.

The problem is that the place of pillar is not a place of lover. The pillar holds up the house. It isn't loved inside it. And you, from pillar to pillar, held up many loves without ever, in any of them, being simply loved.

Notice something you may never have connected to this. You anticipate.

You know what the other is going to need before he knows. You see the problem coming and solve it before it knocks at the door. You're always one step ahead, preparing, foreseeing, handling.

It looks like care. And there is care. But there's also vigilance. The vigilance of someone who can't rest, because she has to keep being necessary, and being necessary demands always being alert to what might be needed.

That anticipation drains you. You live in the future, in the needs that haven't arrived yet. Never in the present, with yourself, at peace. Because the present, without a need to solve, leaves you without a function, and without a function you feel you lose your place.

There's a detail you might recognize. You know everything about his life. The passwords, the schedules, the commitments, the fears, the medications. And he knows little about yours.

It's not by chance. Knowing everything gives you a place. It makes you the one who manages, who handles, who can't be absent. And keeping your own life invisible protects you from being the one who needs, who depends, who exposes herself.

You built a relationship in which you're the headquarters of everything, and you yourself are a mystery. You know everything about him, and he barely knows you. And you call that love, when it's, deep down, a way of keeping yourself in command and out of reach at the same time.

I want you to notice the fear, because it explains everything. The fear is abandonment. Somewhere, it was engraved in you that you would be left. That love goes away. That whoever stays, stays as long as they need.

And your answer to that fear was brilliant and tragic at the same time. If I'm indispensable, they can't leave me. If everything depends on me, they stay, even if only out of dependency.

But notice what this condemns you to. To being kept by need, and not by love. To having someone bound to you, and not devoted to you. And a part of you knows the difference, and that's why, in the midst of all your usefulness, you feel so little loved.

Because being kept by dependency doesn't calm the fear of abandonment. It confirms it. It tells you, every day, that if you stopped being useful, they'd leave you. And so the fear never passes, however indispensable you make yourself, because indispensability doesn't cure the fear of not being loved, it feeds it.

## 3. Usefulness is not intimacy

There's a confusion at the center of everything. And I want to undo it carefully.

You confused being needed with being loved. They are different things. Very different.

To be needed is to be useful. It's to be missed. It's the other person needing what you do, what you solve, what you give.

To be loved is something else. It's the other person wanting you. Not what you do. You. Your presence, your way of being, who you are when you're not doing anything.

And here's the problem. You built the whole of love on the first thing, usefulness, and almost nothing on the second, intimacy.

You replaced being loved with being needed. Because being needed you control, with effort. Being loved you don't control, it depends on being seen and wanted for who you are, and that frightens you.

Notice what this costs you.

A relationship built on usefulness never gives you the certainty of love. Because deep down you know, always, that he stays for your usefulness, not for you.

And that doubt doesn't rest. "Does he want me, or does he just need me? If I stopped doing all this, would he stay?"

You live with this question gnawing at you, and your answer is to do even more, so that you never have to test it.

But there's a cruel irony in this. The more you make yourself indispensable, the less intimate the relationship becomes.

Because intimacy needs two who show themselves, who give themselves, who see each other fragile. And you never show yourself fragile. You're always in the position of the one who gives, who solves, who holds. Never in the position of the one who needs, who leans, who lets herself be cared for.

So he never truly knows you. He knows your function, not your soul. And he loves, at most, what you do, because what you are you never showed him.

Usefulness became your mask. Behind it, you hide the woman who's afraid she isn't enough just for existing. And as long as the mask is in place, no one can love the woman, because no one sees her.

There's something harder still. In making yourself indispensable, you also bind him in a way that isn't love.

A man who doesn't know how to live without you doesn't necessarily stay because he loves you. He stays out of dependency. And dependency isn't love, it's a comfortable prison, for both of you.

And a part of you knows this. That's why, even with him bound to your usefulness, you don't feel loved. You feel used. And you resent it, without realizing that you're the one who set the relationship up this way.

There's a scene you might know. He forgets something you'd do in an instant, and you feel, more than irritation, a strange relief. "Good thing I'm the one who handles this. I'm still needed."

Notice that. His failing soothes you, because it confirms that he needs you. And his competence frightens you, because it threatens your place. You're rooting, without wanting to, for the incapacity of the one you love, because his incapacity is the guarantee of your staying.

It's a sad place from which to love. Wanting, deep down, for the other not to grow, so you can keep being indispensable to him. And it's the opposite of love, which wants the other whole, capable, free, even if that means he no longer needs us to survive.

Notice a fear you may never have dared to look at head-on. Deep down, you don't believe you're enough just by being.

You think that, if you stopped doing, giving, solving, there'd be nothing there to bind anyone. That your person, stripped of usefulness, is little. Insufficient. Not enough to stay for.

That's the hole that usefulness covers. Not the fear of not helping. The fear of not being enough.

And while you do a lot, you don't have to look at that hole. The busyness covers it. You're so busy being necessary that you never stop long enough to feel the terror of, perhaps, not being enough.

But the hole is there. And it's felt in the rare times you stop. On a night when there's nothing to solve. In a moment when he's fine without you. There appears, then, an unease, an emptiness, and you run right away to find something to do, to cover it up again.

All your usefulness is, deep down, a flight from that hole. And the hole isn't covered with more doing. It only heals when you discover, at last, that you're enough. That there's a person there. That you're worth something for existing, and not for what you produce.

I want to show you the price that your family, or those you love, pay for your indispensability. Because they pay too.

When you're the piece that everything depends on, you keep the others dependent. You solve what they'd learn to solve. You take care of what they'd take care of. And, without meaning to, you keep them from growing, from becoming capable, from gaining their own ground.

There are children who never learn to manage because their mother manages everything for them. There are partners who never mature because she handles everything. Your usefulness, which looks like love, makes the ones you love small, because it leaves them no room to be large.

And there's a resentment that grows, on both sides. You resent carrying everything. And they, deep down, resent being treated as incapable, never being given room, your help always coming with the message that without you they can't manage.

Notice this, because it's hard to see. Your indispensability isn't only a burden of yours. It's also a way, involuntary, of keeping the others small, so that they keep needing you. And a part of them feels that, and suffocates, and moves away, and you don't understand why.

Letting go of indispensability isn't only freeing yourself. It's freeing them. It's letting them grow, be capable, have their ground. It's loving them enough to let them not need you, and to discover, then, whether they stay because they want you, and not because they depend on you.

## 4. Who am I if no one needs me

I'll leave a question here. Alone. Don't run from it too quickly by going to solve something for someone.

Who are you if no one needs you?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness it opens.

For you, this question may be one of the most terrifying there is. Because your entire identity was built on being needed.

You're the indispensable one. The one who holds everything together. The one who can't be absent. Take that away from you, imagine yourself in a relationship where the other needs nothing that you do, where he's whole, capable, without dependence on you, and a terror appears. Then what am I for? What place do I have, if no one needs me?

That terror is the heart of everything. You don't know how to exist without being needed. You don't know how to have a place without earning it. You don't know how to be loved without being useful.

And the shadow hidden here is the emptiness of having no worth of your own. The feeling, old and deep, that without what you do, there's nothing in you to justify their staying.

That's why you make yourself so necessary. Not out of generosity alone. Out of terror. Because without usefulness, you feel you disappear, that there's no person there worth staying for.

Notice how this makes you a hostage to those who need you.

If you only feel safe when you're needed, then you always need someone dependent. And you end up, without wanting to, choosing men who need a lot, who aren't enough for themselves, who depend on you, because with the capable ones you don't know what to do.

You attract the dependent one, the one who doesn't take care of himself, the one who needs to be saved, the one who leans on you. Not because you like to suffer. Because it's with the dependent one that you feel safe, indispensable, in your place.

And then you drain yourself holding up the one who doesn't hold himself up, and you complain that you give everything and receive nothing, without seeing that you chose, precisely, the one who only knows how to receive, because it was with that one that your place seemed guaranteed.

Notice too something you do without realizing. You make others more dependent than they'd need to be.

You solve what they'd solve on their own. You anticipate what they'd learn to do. You take care of everything, and in taking care of everything, you keep them from growing, from becoming capable, from being enough for themselves.

Not out of malice. Out of terror. Because a capable man, who manages without you, doesn't guarantee you the place. A dependent man, yes. So, without deciding it, you keep him dependent.

And then you grow tired of carrying someone who doesn't grow, without seeing that you're the one who kept him from growing, because his growth threatened your indispensability.

It's a trap for both. He stays smaller than he could be. And you stay exhausted from carrying him. And neither of you is, truly, free.

Notice too the fragility this hides, beneath all your strength as a pillar.

If your worth is in what you do, then you're always one tiredness away from losing it. You only have to fall ill, drain yourself, not be able to do, and the terror fires: now that I'm not useful, they're going to leave me.

That's why you don't allow yourself to stop, not even sick. Because stopping isn't only resting. It's risking the place. And you live in an endless tension, the tension of someone who has to keep proving her usefulness in order to keep deserving the love.

Don't answer right away the question of who you are if no one needs you. It isn't answered with the head.

But leave it open. Because it's in that question that your freedom begins. The day you discover that there's a person there, beneath everything you do, with her own worth, worthy of staying for simply by existing, you stop needing to make yourself indispensable. And then, only then, can you be loved for yourself.

There's a very particular loneliness in your life, and I want to name it, because you may never have put words to it.

You're the one who takes care of everyone, and you have no one to take care of you. You're everyone's pillar, and you lean on no one. You're surrounded by people who depend on you, and you feel, in the midst of them, profoundly alone.

Because being needed is not being accompanied. People come to you for what you solve, not for who you are. They seek you out when they need, not when they want, simply, to be with you. And that difference, which you may not name, hurts in a deep place.

Notice: when was the last time someone asked you how you were, and truly waited for the answer? Who took care of you without your having to ask, or to collapse first? For many women like you, the answer is a silence, and in that silence dwells the loneliness of the one who gives everything and receives little.

And there's a part of you that resents it. That's tired of always being the strong one, the one who handles, the one who endures. That would like, for once, to be the one who is cared for. But you don't say it, because saying it would be to need, and to need is what you don't allow yourself.

I want to show you where this place comes from, because seeing it being born helps to release it. There was a time when being useful guaranteed you what being yourself didn't guarantee.

Maybe you were the child who received attention when she helped, and who went unnoticed when she simply was. Maybe you had a mother, or a father, who needed you, who leaned on you, who made you their confidante, their helper, their little caretaker. And you learned that your place, in that house, was to be useful, and not to be a child.

A child like that learns early the equation that still governs you today. I'm not loved for existing. I'm kept for serving. And she runs, her whole life, serving, afraid that, the moment she stops, she'll discover that there was no love, only need.

And that child never knew what it was to be loved without being useful. She never experienced being wanted just for being there, without doing, without giving, without solving. And so, as an adult, she doesn't believe that's possible, and keeps buying, with usefulness, a love she's afraid she doesn't deserve for free.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing whoever raised you. Whoever needed you too soon, most of the time, was themselves lacking, without realizing it, leaning on the child they had at hand. They didn't mean to rob you of the right to be loved without serving. But they robbed you of it, without meaning to, and you carry it still.

## 5. Being loved for being, not for serving

I want to undo the confusion that's at the center of everything. You confused two things that look the same and are opposites: being needed and being loved.

To be needed is to be missed, it's the other needing what you do. To be loved is the other wanting you, whether you do or you don't. The first depends on your usefulness. The second doesn't depend on anything you do, it depends only on your being.

And you built everything on the first, because you control it. You can make yourself necessary with effort. Being loved you don't control, and that frightens you, because it exposes you to the possibility of not being enough.

But notice the price. Whoever is kept by usefulness never has the certainty of love. You always live with the doubt: does he want me, or does he just need me? And that doubt doesn't rest, however indispensable you make yourself, because indispensability guarantees that they need you, never that they love you.

There's a question that reveals it to you. If I stopped doing all this, would he stay? If the answer terrifies you, it's because the relationship rests on need, and not on love. And until you risk discovering it, you're going to serve endlessly, without ever knowing whether it was you, or only what you do, that he wanted.

If you confused being needed with being loved, then there's a new truth waiting for you. Read it slowly.

You can be loved for being, not for serving.

There's a love that doesn't want you for your usefulness. That wants you. That would stay with you even if you did nothing, even if you solved nothing, even if, for once, you were the one who needed.

I know this sounds strange. Your map says the opposite. Your map says that without usefulness there's no place, that love is paid for with service, that stopping doing is being left.

But your map was drawn by a child who had to earn her place. It was accurate then. It's not accurate everywhere, now.

There is, in the world, love that gives itself for free. That doesn't ask for an invoice. That wants the person, not the function. Your map doesn't mark it, because it was made where it didn't exist. But it exists.

Let me tell you this another way.

Think of someone you love deeply. A child, a friend, someone. Do you love that person for what they do for you? Or would you love them just the same if, suddenly, they could do nothing?

Almost certainly you'd love them just the same. Because your love for them isn't for their usefulness. It's for them.

Now, if that's how your love works, why would the love of others for you be any different? Why is it that only you, in the whole world, have to be useful in order to be loved?

The answer is that you don't. That rule isn't a truth about love. It's an old sentence about you, learned in a place where, in fact, you had to serve in order to stay. And sentences, unlike truths, can be revoked.

The awakening, when it comes, doesn't arrive like a thunderclap. It arrives like a relief.

It's the day you do nothing special, when you're tired, when for once you neither solve nor anticipate anything, and he stays just the same. He's there. He wants you there. Without your having done anything for it.

And you feel, for an instant, something you'd never felt. That maybe you can be loved even without serving. That maybe your place isn't rented. That maybe you don't have to pay the rent.

It's a small instant. But it changes everything. Because it disproves, with the force of what you felt, a law you'd been carrying for decades.

From there on, even if the old terror speaks again, you already know there's another path. That you can be wanted for who you are. And that this, far from being less secure than being needed, is the only love that truly rests.

I want you to think about what you're teaching, if there are children watching you love.

A daughter who sees you give everything and never ask, hold up everything and never rest, make yourself indispensable and never simply be loved, learns from you that this is how a woman loves. She learns to pay for her place with usefulness, the way you pay for yours.

And it's likely she'll repeat it. That she'll drain herself, one day, the way you drain yourself. That she'll confuse, as you do, being needed with being loved.

The most liberating thing you can do for the one who watches you is not to give her even more. It's to show her a woman who lets herself be loved without erasing herself. Who gives and receives. Who has a place without buying it.

Sometimes a woman learns to choose herself, at last, not only for her own sake, but so as not to pass on to those she loves the same endless rent she paid.

I want to stop here a moment, because there's a point at which this stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more.

If the fear of being left is so great that it leads you to erase yourself entirely, to disappear inside what you do for others, to lose yourself in the name of being necessary. If there's a panic of abandonment that pulls the ground out from under you, that makes you endure relationships that diminish you, just so as not to be alone. If you've drained yourself to the point of no longer knowing who you are without serving.

This may be more than a pattern. It may be a wound of attachment, a deep fear of abandonment, that deserves the support of a professional. It's not weakness to seek it. It's the care you deserve, and that you may never have given yourself, because you were always busy caring for everyone except yourself.

And I want to tell you one thing, with tenderness. The helping relationship can be, for you, the first place where you experience being cared for without having to serve. Where someone occupies themselves with you, without your having to make them dependent, without your having to pay with usefulness. It's healing, in the very place of the wound: letting yourself, at last, be the side that receives.

## 6. Choosing yourself

There's a verb for this crossing. And it's not to give less. It's to choose yourself.

Choosing yourself is not becoming selfish, cold, ceasing to love. It's stopping erasing yourself in the name of the other. It's counting again, yourself, in your own life.

Notice how you live today. All your attention is on the other. On what he needs, on what he feels, on what you can do for him. And you, in the midst of this, have disappeared.

You don't even really know anymore what you want. What you need. What would make you happy, beyond being necessary to someone.

Choosing yourself begins there. With asking yourself again what you want. With remembering that you too have needs, desires, limits, and that you're not just a function in the service of another.

And it begins, above all, with something difficult for you. With ceasing, sometimes, to do.

With not solving everything. With letting him manage on his own. With not anticipating every need. With bearing the discomfort of not being, in that moment, indispensable.

It will cost you. You'll feel the terror that, without your usefulness, he'll go. You'll want to run to do something, to solve, to make yourself necessary again.

But it's precisely there, in that borne not-doing, that you discover the truth. If he stays when you stop serving, then he loved you. If he goes when you stop serving, then he never loved you, he loved your usefulness, and that, however much it hurts, is better to know.

Because a relationship that only holds up through your usefulness isn't love. It's an endless job, badly paid, from which you can never resign. And you deserve more than a job. You deserve to be chosen.

Choosing yourself begins with something small and difficult: asking yourself what you want. Not what he needs, not what you'd do for him, but what you, in the midst of everything, want. You'll probably draw a blank, because you spent your life turned toward the needs of others, and switched off the channel that told you of your own. Reopening it is the beginning. Counting again, yourself, in your own life.

I want to warn you about one thing. Choosing yourself is not ceasing to give.

You'll keep giving, caring, being present, because that's your nature and it's beautiful. The difference is the root. Before, you gave out of terror, so as not to be left. After, you give out of choice, because you feel like it, from a full place.

And when you give out of choice, and not out of terror, you can also not give, without panic. You can set limits. You can need. You can receive. You can be, for once, the side that leans, and not only the one that holds up.

That's it, choosing yourself. Not ceasing to love. Existing again inside the love.

And there's a difference that protects everything else. Choosing yourself is not ceasing to give, to care, to be there. That's your richness, and you won't lose it. It's ceasing to give out of terror, and beginning to give out of choice. From a full place, and not out of fear. And when you give out of choice, you can also not give, you can need, you can receive, without your place being at risk. You remain generous. You just stop paying, with your generosity, a rent you should never have had to pay.

I want to add a border, because it's important.

There's a difference between a man who receives your care with gratitude and one who takes advantage of it. The first, when you stop doing everything, notices, thanks you for what you did, and begins to give too. The second gets angry, demands, makes you feel guilty for ceasing to serve.

If, when you begin to choose yourself, the other punishes you, penalizes you, makes you pay for having dared to need, pay attention. That's not love adjusting itself. It's control feeling threatened by losing its servant.

And no understanding from this book should keep you serving someone who only wants you useful. Choosing yourself includes, sometimes, discovering that the relationship wasn't love, it was an exchange in which you gave everything and received crumbs. And leaving there is not failing. It's beginning to treat yourself with the respect you gave, all of it, to the one who used you.

There's something else you're going to have to learn. To tolerate gratitude without turning it into one more service.

When someone thanks you, wants to give to you, wants to care for you, notice your impulse. You deflect. You minimize. You run to give back, to do more, to return to the position of the one who gives. Because receiving puts you in the vulnerable place you avoid.

Choosing yourself is also learning to receive. To let them take care of you without fleeing right away to take care in return. To stay, for once, on the side of the one who is loved, and not only on the side of the one who loves.

I want to tell you about what changes in the relationship when you begin to choose yourself.

At first, it will shudder. Because the relationship was organized around your indispensability, and when you stop being the piece that everything depends on, the old balance is shaken. That frightens. You'll feel that you're ruining everything.

You're not. You're giving the relationship the chance to be true. To rest on love, and not on dependency. To discover whether there is, beneath your usefulness, a real wanting, on both sides.

And you'll discover one of two things. Either he, freed from the dependency, loves you just the same, and the relationship breathes, lighter, more equal. Or he moves away when you stop serving him, and that, however much it hurts, shows you that it wasn't you he wanted, it was what you did. Both possibilities, however different, are worth more than the doubt you were living in.

I want to warn you about one thing. When you begin to need, to ask, to stop being only the one who gives, you'll feel selfish. An enormous guilt, as if you were failing, abandoning, ceasing to be who you should be.

That guilt is the old pattern defending itself. For you, always giving was the norm, and asking seems like a transgression. But needing is not selfishness. It's human. And a relationship where only one person can need is not love, it's a service, and you deserve more than to be the provider of a service you mistake for being loved.

There's something else you're going to have to relearn. To receive. When someone wants to give to you, care for you, help you, notice your impulse. You deflect. You refuse. You run to give back, to return to the position of the one who gives. Because receiving puts you in the exposed place you avoid.

Choosing yourself includes staying, for once, on the side of the one who receives. Letting them care for you without fleeing right away to care in return. Bearing the discomfort of not giving anything, and discovering that, even so, they stay. It's there, in that borne receiving, that you learn the truth you most lack: that you can be loved without serving.

## 7. Chosen without being indispensable

There's a destination for this crossing. And it fits in one image. To be chosen without being indispensable.

Don't be confused. Indispensable and chosen seem like the same thing, and they're opposites.

Indispensable is their not being able to live without you. It's dependency. It's their staying because they need to.

Chosen is their being able to live without you, and wanting to stay anyway. It's freedom. It's their staying because they want you.

Notice the difference. Whoever holds you as indispensable is bound to you. Whoever chooses you is free, and chooses you just the same. And which of the two loves gives you more peace?

The love of indispensability never gives peace, because you know it's the dependency that holds it, and that if the dependency falls, he'll go. The love of choice gives peace, because you know he doesn't need you to survive, and that therefore, if he's there, it's because he really wants you.

You always looked for the first, thinking it was safer. It isn't. It's more fragile. Because it rests on a dependency that you have to feed endlessly, and that drains you, and that never gives you the certainty of love.

The second is the one that's safe. But it demands a courage you haven't had yet. The courage to stop being indispensable, and to stand there, in sight, to see whether they choose you for yourself.

I want to tell you about what frees a relationship when you stop being indispensable.

It frees the other. Because whoever is loved for his dependency feels, deep down, that he's loved for his weakness, and that weighs, suffocates, infantilizes. When you stop wanting him dependent, you let him grow, and he can love you standing, and not clinging.

It frees you. Because you stop carrying the entire relationship on your shoulders. You stop being the only one responsible for everything working. You can, at last, lean too, rest too, receive too.

And it frees the love between you. Because a love between two whole people, who choose each other free, is deep in a way that a love between one who holds up and another who depends can never be.

There's a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of being wanted without having to do anything to deserve it. Of resting in love, instead of holding it up. Of being, for once, cared for, and not only a caregiver.

That joy is your right. As much yours as anyone's. And it's on the other side of the terror, on the day you risk ceasing to be indispensable in order to discover whether they choose you for yourself.

I want to give you a way to begin, without making this into a technique.

Notice, over the course of a day, how many things you do for him that he could do on his own. Not to stop doing them all at once. Only to see the size of your automatic devotion.

Then, choose one. Just one. And don't do it. Leave it to him. And observe two things: what happens in the relationship, and what happens inside you.

In the relationship, almost nothing terrible is going to happen. He manages. Or he doesn't, and that's information too. The world doesn't end because you didn't solve that one thing.

Inside you, the terror will rise. The guilt. The urge to run and do it. That discomfort is your wound being touched, not proof that you're failing. Stay with it. Breathe. Let it pass.

And each time you don't do something you'd do out of terror, and the relationship doesn't fall, you learn something new. That your place didn't depend on that. That maybe it doesn't depend on anything you do. That maybe, just maybe, you're wanted for being there.

They're small steps. But that's how a lifetime of usefulness is dismantled. Not in one blow. One thing not done at a time, until your body learns that you remain loved even when you don't serve.

I want to tell you about a joy you don't yet know, and that's on the other side of this crossing.

The joy of being cared for. Of falling ill and letting someone take care of you, without getting up to take care of everything. Of being tired and letting someone relieve you. Of receiving, without giving back right away, without paying, without the rush to balance things.

You don't know this joy, because you never let yourself be on the side of the one who receives. You were always the one who gives, who cares, who holds. And there's a hunger, in you, that was never satisfied: the hunger to be, for once, you, the one held.

That hunger is not weakness. It's human. We all need to receive, to be cared for, to lean. And you, who give to everyone, deserve, too, to receive. Not because you served enough. Just because you are, like anyone, someone who also needs.

And there's something that changes when you let yourself receive. Your relationships become reciprocal, and not one-way streets. People can, at last, give to you, and that draws you closer, equalizes, deepens. You stop being the spring that's never watered, and you come to be in a flow of giving and receiving, where you too are filled.

Notice one last thing, about the one who watches you. If there are children, or younger people, who learn to love by looking at you, notice what you teach them. If they see you always give and never receive, erase yourself to be necessary, buy love with usefulness, they learn that this is how a woman loves. And it's likely they'll repeat it.

The most liberating thing you can do for the one who watches you is not to give her even more. It's to show her a woman who lets herself be loved without erasing herself. Who gives and receives. Who has a place without paying for it. Sometimes a woman learns to choose herself, at last, not only for her own sake, but so as not to pass on to those she loves the same endless rent she paid.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, stop making yourself necessary. But it changes enough for you to stop mistaking your exhaustion for love.

Now you know that you make yourself indispensable, and that this isn't only generosity, it's insurance against abandonment.

You know that the question underneath is old. What do I have to do so that they won't leave me. And that you learned it in a place where your place wasn't secure.

You know that usefulness is not intimacy, and that in making yourself needed you hid the woman who could be loved for being.

You know who you are when no one needs you, and that the terror that appears there is the fear of having no worth of your own.

You know that you can be loved for being, not for serving. And you know that the verb is to choose yourself, to exist again inside the love.

And you know that being chosen is different from being indispensable. That one is a prison and the other is freedom. And that only the second gives peace.

I want to leave you an image.

You built, around those you love, a house in which you are the foundations, the walls, the roof, the heating, everything. A house that, without you, would fall. And you feel safe because, as long as it depends on you, they can't send you away from it.

But notice. In a house where you are everything, there's no place for you to live. You're the structure, not the inhabitant. You hold it up, but you don't inhabit it. And that's why, in the midst of so much love given, you feel so alone.

The crossing is to stop being the structure and to become the inhabitant. To let the house hold up without you too, so that you can, at last, live inside it, rest inside it, be loved inside it. Not as the one who holds everything together. As someone who is wanted for being there.

And I want you to take one last thing, about the fear that's underneath everything. The fear of being left is not cured by making yourself indispensable. It's cured by discovering that your worth doesn't depend on being missed. That there are those who want you, and not what you do. And that, even if someone leaves, you stay, whole, with yourself, because your place in the world never depended, after all, on being the piece that everything depends on. That fear was learned, and what is learned can, slowly, and perhaps with help, be unlearned.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this book. I can be chosen without making myself indispensable.

Choosing yourself was the first step. But there's a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by stopping serving, when everything in you wants to run and make itself useful. By bearing the terror of not being needed. And, if there's someone at your side, by learning, together, to be free and choose each other just the same.

There's a simple sign to guide you, day to day. Notice, before a person, what binds you to them. Is it how much they need you? Or is it how much you like being with them, whether they need you or not?

When you notice that you bind yourself through the other's need, distrust it. It's not love, it's your wound looking for a guaranteed place. And when you notice that you stay with someone who doesn't depend on you, who manages without you, and who wants you anyway, notice that too: it's there, in that wanting without dependency, that the love you always wanted and were afraid you didn't deserve dwells.

That is the work of SyncHim. The book names. SyncHim crosses over. Each in her own time.

And before you close these pages, I ask you one thing only. A hard one, for you.

This week, leave one thing unsolved that you'd normally solve for him. Not as punishment, not as a game. Only to see what happens, and what you feel. Notice the terror that rises, the urge to run and do it. And stay, a little longer, without doing it. Because it's there, in that borne not-doing, that you begin to discover whether they love you for yourself, and that you begin, at last, to return to yourself.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
