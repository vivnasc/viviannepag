# Loving without carrying

**For the mother of a child on the spectrum. The guilt, the fear, and the control that nobody sees, and the path to remaining in your own life.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is about you, the mother, not about your child. It does not come to teach you how to raise a child on the spectrum, it brings no techniques, it does not suggest that neurodivergence is a defect to be corrected or a flaw to be compensated for. It comes to look at what you carry: the guilt, the fear, the control, the exhaustion that nobody sees. It is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support nor the work of the professionals who accompany your child. If you recognize in yourself signs of deep exhaustion, of sadness that does not pass, of anxiety that takes the ground from under you, please seek support. Not because you are ill. Because you deserve care with the same seriousness with which you care for him.*

---

## 1. The tiredness that has no name

You know a tiredness that almost nobody talks about.

It's not the tiredness of someone who slept little, though you do sleep little. It's not the tiredness of someone who works a lot, though you work more than anyone could measure. It's a tiredness that can't be seen from the outside, that doesn't appear on any to-do list, that has no name in any conversation between mothers at the school gate. It's the tiredness of someone who stopped being only a mother a long time ago, without ever choosing to stop.

Because you are not only a mother. You are the therapist before the therapist arrives and after she leaves. You are the translator of the world to him and of him to the world. You are the mediator between what he needs and what the school can give. You are the advocate, in meetings where you have to explain, again, from the beginning, who your son is to someone who doesn't yet know him. You are the coordinator of a schedule of appointments, therapies, assessments, reports, that you hold in your head because nobody else holds the whole of it. And you are all of this without a salary, without set hours, without weekends, and almost always without anyone noticing.

There is work you do all day long that doesn't count as work. Anticipating. You live anticipating. Before you walk into a new place, you've already mapped the exits, the noises, the textures, what could go wrong. Before a party, you've already rehearsed what will happen if it becomes too much. Before a small change in the routine, you've already prepared the ground days in advance. And nobody sees that work, because work done well is invisible: when it goes well, it looks as if nothing was needed.

Think of one of your days, an ordinary day. You wake up already calculating: how the transition from bed to breakfast will go, whether there's time for the routine not to be rushed, which clothes don't have the tag that bothers him, whether the noise from the street will be too much today. You take him, and at drop-off you've already read his mood from the way he holds his backpack. During the day, a part of you never switches off, it stays listening for the phone that might ring from school. In the afternoon, you are the one who bridges what happened in there and what he can tell you, deciphering a whole day from three words and a gesture. At night, when everyone is finally asleep, instead of resting, you open the computer to answer an email from the therapist, or to read one more article, or to fill in one more form for one more support service. And when you lie down, your head keeps working, because tomorrow there's another transition, another meeting, another small battle that nobody will see. This isn't a bad day. This is Tuesday.

And there's an extra weight that almost nobody names: what all of this does to your relationship, to your friendships, to what's left of you for the rest of life. The couple that turns into a logistics team that barely sees each other. The friends who drifted away because it was always easier to say no, and after a few years they don't even invite you anymore. The extended family that doesn't understand and that, without meaning any harm, comments, judges, gives you unasked-for advice about a life they don't live. And you, in the middle of all this, comparing yourself to mothers whose days don't have half the layers of yours, and always coming out the loser in that comparison, because guilt never lets you see everything you hold up.

And there's something you do that you may never have told anyone, because you didn't even know what it was called. You live ten years ahead of real life. You look at your child playing on the floor, at five, seven, ten years old, and your head is already at twenty, at thirty, asking who will be with him, who will understand him, who will have the patience you have, what happens to him when you are no longer here. You pre-live every difficulty he hasn't even had yet. You suffer in advance pains that may never come. And you return to the present exhausted by a life that hasn't yet happened.

That is the tiredness that has no name. The tiredness of someone who carries, at the same time, the present and every possible future.

And there's the loneliness. That one almost nobody understands. Because from the outside your life may look like an ordinary life, with an ordinary child, and when you try to explain the size of what you feel, they either minimize it ("oh, but he's so sweet," "mine never sits still either"), or they get frightened and change the subject. And you learn not to say it. You learn to answer "everything's fine" because the truth doesn't fit into a five-minute conversation. And you grow lonelier and lonelier inside a life that is fuller and fuller.

There is also a loneliness within the loneliness, which is not feeling seen even by the very community that should see you. Sometimes you go to a group, you read a testimony, you listen to another mother, and even there, among the people who should understand, you feel that your version is different, that your child doesn't fit into other people's stories, that your pain has a shape that finds no echo. Every child is unique, every family is unique, and that, which is true and is beautiful, has a lonely flip side: nobody lives exactly what you live. And so you learn to translate yourself for everyone, all the time, and you grow tired of translating, and there are days when you just wish someone would understand without you having to explain. That wish is not weak in any way. It's the most human wish there is: to be seen without having to justify yourself.

I want you to know, before we go any further, just one thing: I did not come here to tell you that you're doing too much and you should do less. I didn't come to give you strategies for managing the tiredness, as if the problem were your organization. The problem is not your organization. You organize the impossible every single day. What I came to do is something else. I came to give a name to what you feel. Because what has no name can't be set down, and you've been carrying for so long a weight that nobody named that you've forgotten it is a weight. You think it's you. You think it's your life.

It's not you. It's a weight. And what is a weight can, one day, be set down.

Not today. Not with a sentence. But we'll get there, slowly, over these pages. For now, this is enough for you: the tiredness you feel is not weakness, it's not clumsiness, it's not a mother complaining. It's your body telling you the truth. It's time someone looked at you.

## 2. Was it something I did?

There is a question you may never have said out loud, but that you know perfectly. It shows up at night, it shows up when nobody can hear you, it shows up disguised as other, smaller questions. And the question is this: was it something I did?

Was it the pregnancy? That fright, that medication, that stress you couldn't avoid? Was it the food? Was it the screen time you let slide when you were exhausted? Was it the genes, and if it was, which side did they come from, and does that make you guilty for passing them on? You were slow to notice, and what if you had noticed sooner? You were late to have him assessed. You made mistakes, perhaps, in the early years, without knowing you were making them. Guilt has an infinite imagination, and none of these questions leaves you in peace, because none of them has an answer that absolves you.

And notice how clever the guilt of origin is. It doesn't accuse you all at once, head on, which would be easy to refuse. It seeps into the folds. It's in the comment of a well-meaning person who asks, "And how was your pregnancy?", and leaves you stewing for three nights. It's in the moment when you read, somewhere on the internet, an unfounded theory that points to the mother, one you know is false, and that even so sticks to your skin. It's in the way you look back and reconstruct your story searching for the exact moment when, you think, you should have noticed sooner. "If only I'd had him assessed at two instead of four." "If only I hadn't dismissed that sign." As if an earlier diagnosis made you a better mother, and a later one condemned you. It isn't like that. You did what you knew with what you had, in the time you had, the way everyone does with everything in life.

That's why I want to tell you something in plain words, and I want you to read it slowly, and if you need to, to come back to this page whenever the question returns.

Your child's autism was not caused by you.

It wasn't caused by anything you did, nor by anything you failed to do. It wasn't the pregnancy, it wasn't the food, it wasn't your tiredness, it wasn't a lapse of attention, of love, or of care on your part. It wasn't a punishment, it wasn't a consequence, it wasn't a debt you're paying off. And, above all, it wasn't any dynamic in your family, no secret, no hidden pattern, no unresolved emotion. Your child's neurodivergence is the way his brain is, from the very beginning. It's not a wound someone opened in him. It's a way of being, and you did not cause it.

I need to be very clear here, because later in this book I'm going to use a way of looking that comes from my training in Family Constellation, and I don't want that to confuse or frighten you. The systemic reading I'll bring does not serve, at any point, to explain who your child is. It will not suggest, not in the slightest, that there is a family cause behind his autism. That would be false and it would be cruel, and it would do the opposite of what this book exists to do.

Constellation work, here, does not touch your child. It touches you. It serves to relieve what you carry: your guilt, your fear, your control, your exhaustion. Those things, yes, have a history, have a weight, have layers that come from before you. Your child is not a problem to be solved. You are the one carrying more than is yours to carry. That is the only thing we'll be dealing with.

Because notice what the guilt of origin does to you. As long as you believe, even deep down, even without saying it, that something in you caused this, you stay trapped in an impossible place: you have to compensate for a flaw you did not commit. And a debt that doesn't exist is never paid off. No matter how much you do, it's never enough, because there is no end to compensation for something imaginary. That is why you deplete yourself without relief. You're not caring for a child. You're trying to repair a guilt that has no repair because it has no origin.

And there's something else the guilt of origin does to you, more hidden. It takes away your lightness with your child. When you carry the suspicion that you failed him, every interaction with him acquires an undercurrent of pain. You look at him and, beneath the love, there's a regret that shouldn't be there. And he feels it. Children always feel the undercurrent, even the ones who don't speak, even the ones who don't look you in the eye. Your child doesn't need a mother paying off a debt. He needs a mother who can, simply, be with him without the weight of a guilt that isn't true.

I want you to notice something, because it shows how unfair this guilt is to you. The same mother who accuses herself of having caused everything is, at the same time, the one who fights hardest for her child to have the best. These two things don't fit together. Someone who causes harm doesn't spend her life repairing with that kind of devotion. Your fierce dedication is the living proof that your love was never missing. The guilt of origin takes exactly that enormous love and turns it against you, convincing you that if you try so hard it's because you have a lot to make up for. You have nothing to make up for. You try so hard because you love so much. They are the same thing, and neither of them is a confession of failure.

I want to tell you one more thing that may explain why this guilt doesn't go away no matter how often you're told it isn't your fault. It's that guilt, very often, is not an error of judgment, it's a bond in disguise. Beneath it lives a sentence you never said but that your unconscious whispers: "if I suffer too, I stay connected to you," "if I blame myself, maybe I can repair the irreparable." Notice what this means: letting go of the guilt may feel, deep down, like betraying your child, like ceasing to be connected to his pain, like abandoning him in a suffering you can't take away. That's why comfort doesn't reach you, that's why hearing "it's not your fault" isn't enough: because a part of you clings to the guilt as the last way of not leaving him alone. But your child doesn't need you to suffer with him in order to feel loved. Your guilt doesn't help him, doesn't heal him, doesn't repair anything. What connects him to you is not your suffering, it's your love, and that love, unlike guilt, doesn't need to hurt in order to exist.

Letting go of this guilt is not letting go of responsibility. You keep looking for the supports, the therapies, the paths that help him, and you do well, and that is love in action. Letting go of the guilt of origin is only letting go of the lie that you broke him. You didn't break him. He is not broken. And you are not the cause of anything that needs to be forgiven.

Read it again, if you need to: it wasn't you. And now that this question has begun to lose its force, we can look at the rest.

## 3. The real child and the child you imagined

I'm going to touch now on a delicate place, and I want to do it with great care, because it's a place where it's easy to feel accused of something that isn't true. So let me first say, and say clearly, what this chapter is not. I'm not going to tell you that you rejected your child. I'm not going to tell you that you didn't accept him. I'm not going to tell you that you resisted who he is. None of that. If a part of you has braced itself to defend, it can set that defense down. There's no accusation here.

What there is, is this. Every mother, without exception, imagines a child before she has one. During pregnancy, and sometimes long before, we build a child in our heads: what he'll be like, what he'll enjoy, how he'll run to us, what we'll do together, who he'll be. We don't choose to do it. The imagination does it on its own. And then the real child arrives, and the real child is always, always, different from the imagined one. This is true for every mother in the world, with children of every shape. There's nothing about it that's specific to the spectrum. It's not a failing of yours. It's the condition of any mother: we love an idea first, and then we have to let it go in order to love the person.

The difference, in your case, is that the distance between the imagined and the real was greater, or it appeared in a way that hurt more, or that nobody prepared you to feel. And here is the part that almost nobody lets you say: you may have felt a grief. Not grief for your child, who is alive, who is here, who is yours. Grief for an image. Grief for a script you had in your head that life didn't follow. Grief for the parties you imagined and that are difficult, for the conversations you expected to have in a certain way, for the path you thought it was going to be and that is another.

Feeling that grief says absolutely nothing about how much you love your child. I want this to be firmly settled, because this is where mothers condemn themselves. Feeling the distance between what you imagined and what is, is not rejecting what is. It's exactly the opposite. Only someone already deeply connected feels this distance. Someone who didn't love wouldn't notice any difference. Your pain, if it exists, is made of love, not of refusal.

And perhaps you cried, at some moment, over something you couldn't even name. Perhaps it was at a birthday, or when you saw another child do something your child does in a different way, or on an ordinary day for no apparent reason. And perhaps you were ashamed of those tears, and you hid them, and you told yourself you had no right. You did. It's natural that a part of you still searches, now and then, for what it imagined. That doesn't take away your present. It only asks to be seen, so that afterward it can let you see more clearly.

Because this is what happens when the grief for the image is welcomed instead of pushed away: it frees you. As long as the image of the imagined child goes unbidden farewell, it casts a shadow over you. You look at your real child and, without meaning to, you compare him with the one who didn't come. But when you let the image go, with tenderness, without guilt, your eyes clear. And then you see the real child. Whole. With his path, which is not lesser, it's another. With his way of loving, of communicating, of feeling the world, which is not defective, it's his.

Let me give a name to what usually lies beneath this grief, so that you see it clearly and stop punishing yourself for it. It's not the child you grieve. It's the script. It was the image of taking him by the hand to school and him running to his friends without looking back. It was the conversation at dinner about his day, told in words. It was a certain kind of party, a certain kind of hug, a certain kind of future you had drawn without realizing, assembled from what you saw around you your whole life. None of this was a whim. It was love making plans, as love always does. And when life takes another path, there's a part of you that needs to say goodbye to those plans in order to embrace the true path. Saying goodbye to the plans is not saying goodbye to him. It's opening the hands that were full of the image so that they can be filled with him.

And it's important that you know: this grief is not done once and over. It can come back, in waves, in moments you don't choose, and that too is normal. It comes back at a milestone other children reach at another time. It comes back on a date, at a transition, in a new phase. And each time it comes back, it's not a step backward, it's not a sign that you didn't accept. It's just another wave passing through, and you already know that waves pass. Letting it pass, without clinging to it or fleeing from it, is the most loving way to cross it.

And here I need to ask you to take care, because there are two traps around this place, and both of them pull you away from your real child. The first is to romanticize: to say that it's all a gift, that it's a superpower, that there's no difficulty at all. That looks beautiful but it's a way of not seeing him, because it erases what is truly difficult, for you and for him. The second is the opposite, it's seeing only the diagnosis, seeing only what's missing, seeing only what's hard. That too is not seeing him. Your real child is neither an angel nor a problem. He is a person. Whole, complex, with light and with weight, like any person.

I'm not going to tell you "you have to accept," like an order, as if acceptance were a switch you flip out of duty. True acceptance can't be demanded, it happens, and it happens exactly when you stop forcing yourself toward it and allow yourself first to feel what there is to feel. So, if there's grief to be done, let it be done. Without rushing, without guilt, without shame. And on the other side of that grief, what awaits you is not less love. It's your child, finally seen whole, gaining outline and presence before your eyes. Not the one you imagined. Him. And he is more than ever could fit inside your imagination.

## 4. The tired rescuer

Now we come to the center. To the mechanism. To the thing that perhaps nobody has shown you yet, and that changes everything when it's seen.

There is a position into which many mothers of children on the spectrum fall, without ever choosing it, and that I'm going to call, with all my tenderness, the position of the rescuer. I don't say it as a criticism. I say it as a diagnosis, in the kind sense of the word: seeing what is happening, in order to be able to set it down.

The rescuer is the mother who decided, deep down, without ever having said it in words, something like this: if I do everything right, I make up for everything. If I research enough, if I find the right therapy, the right method, the right professional, if I don't fail at anything, then I hold his fate in my hands. I spare him the suffering. I guarantee that he turns out all right. And from that belief, life turns into a mission without pause.

You know the portraits. The mother who doesn't sleep because at two in the morning she's reading studies, forums, articles in another language, looking for an answer she hasn't found yet. The mother who delegates to no one, not to the father, not to the grandparents, not to a therapist, because nobody does it like her, nobody knows the signs like her, and letting go even a little feels like risking everything. The mother who lives in a state of permanent emergency, always on alert, always ready, always with her body in tension waiting for the next problem to solve. The mother who turned love into a full-time job and then some, and who can't remember the last time she wasn't on duty.

There's one portrait in particular that may be hard for you to recognize, because it's the best disguised of them all: the mother who became an expert. She knows more than many professionals, and sometimes she really does. She reads the studies, knows the terms, debates the approaches, and there's a real strength in that, there's real protection in that. But there's also a place where knowledge stops being a tool and becomes armor, a place where studying more is a way of never stopping, of never feeling, of keeping the emergency permanently switched on so as not to have to set it down. Knowing everything about your child's condition can be, without your realizing it, a way of turning him into a project to manage, instead of a person to be with. And a child feels the difference between a mother who studies him and a mother who sees him.

And I want you to see what lies beneath this, because it isn't what guilt tells you. Guilt tells you that if you do all this it's because it still isn't enough, it's because you should do more. It isn't true. If you do all this, it's out of love. An enormous, fierce love, that would do anything. The problem was never a lack of love. The problem is what love, taken to this extreme, does to you, and to your child.

To you, it makes you disappear. The rescuer slowly empties herself out. She stops eating sitting down, stops seeing friends, stops having a thought that isn't about him, stops existing as a person beyond the function. And she reaches a point where she no longer knows who she is outside of it. Ask her who she is, apart from being his mother, and she falls silent, because the answer faded years ago. The cost of rescuing is the rescuer, who spends herself until nothing is left.

And to your child, it does something you'll want to hear calmly. When you are exhausted, in permanent emergency, emptied out, what he receives is not a present mother. It's a competent, tired manager. And a child doesn't need a manager. He needs a mother. He needs someone who is, truly, there, with calm in the body, with space inside, available not to solve but to be. The rescuer, no matter how much she does, can't give this, because to give presence you have to have presence, and presence is the first thing the mission devours.

In my training there's a word for this, and I'll bring it carefully, because it helps to see. It's called the displaced place. It happens when someone, out of love, occupies in another person's life a place larger than her own. The rescuer doesn't occupy the place of mother, which would be her place. She occupies the place of mother, plus the therapist's, plus the protector of fate's, plus the child's own will, plus the entire future. She carries places that aren't hers, and she does it always with the best of intentions, never out of arrogance, always out of fear and out of love. But the consequence is twofold: she depletes herself sustaining too many places, and the child is left without space to occupy his own. Because when a mother lives her child's life from the inside, there's no life left for him to live. His place is occupied, by her, with tenderness, and occupied even so.

There's something else, more subtle, beneath the position of the rescuer. Without meaning to, and always out of love, she occupies a place that is larger than hers. She wants to hold her child's fate, and her child's fate is not hers to hold. She wants to live his life from the inside, anticipate everything for him, spare him everything, and his life is his to live, with its difficulties, with its paths. Occupying a place larger than ours doesn't make us greater. It depletes us. And, without meaning to, it takes space away from the one who should occupy his own place: the child.

I'm not asking you, in this chapter, to change anything. Change comes later, and it comes slowly. For now I only ask you to see. To recognize, perhaps for the first time without judging yourself, the tired rescuer who lives in you. To rest a hand on her shoulder, instead of demanding more from her. Because she has already done everything she knew. She has already given everything she had. And she is tired in a way that deserves, at last, to be seen.

## 5. To accompany is not to carry

There is a question that, when it truly appears, divides life into before and after. It's not a comfortable question. But it's the door. I'm going to let it fall here, on its own, with the space it deserves, and I ask you not to pass over it quickly.

Am I helping him to live, or am I living for him?

Stay with it a moment.

This question doesn't reach everyone in the same way, nor on the same day. Sometimes it comes when your child manages something you had already given up as impossible, and you realize, with a good fright, that he had a strength you weren't counting on, because you were using yours in place of his. Sometimes it comes when you notice that you are more tired than he is, more anxious than he is, more frightened about his life than he himself is. Sometimes it comes in an ordinary moment, in the middle of some task, when you hear yourself living ten years ahead and you ask yourself how long it's been since you were simply here, with him, in this day. However it comes, it always brings the same vertigo and the same release at once. Because it hurts to see that you may have been carrying too much. And it relieves to discover that you can set it down.

Before this question, a mother believes, with total sincerity, that she is only loving. That everything she does is care, is dedication, is what a good mother does. And much of it is, in fact, love. But after this question, she begins to see something else underneath. She begins to distinguish the care that strengthens from the care that replaces. And that distinction is the heart of this whole book.

In my training there's something called the orders of helping. It's simple to say and it takes a lifetime to live. To help is not to do for the other. To help is to support in a way that respects and increases the other's strength, instead of replacing it. And so there are two kinds of help that look the same from the outside and are opposite on the inside. There's the help that strengthens, that says, "I believe you can do it, and I'm here beside you while you try." And there's the help that replaces, that says, without words, "leave it, I'll do it for you, you can't." The first makes you grow. The second, even full of love, makes you small.

The central question, then, is never "how much do I help?" It's another. It's: does my help increase or decrease his strength?

And here I need to be very careful, because it's easy to hear this wrong. I'm not telling you to help less. I'm not telling you to reduce supports, therapies, the accompaniment your child truly needs. That would be dangerous and it would be wrong. To trust is not to neglect. To trust is not to let go of his hand and disappear. Your child needs the real supports, and keeping them is caring. The distinction is not between helping and not helping. It's between accompanying and carrying.

To carry is to live his life from the inside. It's to feel his emotions for him, to solve everything for him before he tries, to anticipate every difficulty for him to the point where he never reaches his own strength, to be ten years ahead suffering a future that is his to cross. To carry is to put your body in place of his body. And nobody can carry, for a whole life, a life that isn't theirs, because we weren't made for that. That's why you deplete yourself.

To accompany is something else. To accompany is to be present beside him, not in front of him. It's to trust that he has a life of his own, with a rhythm of his own, with a strength of his own that you don't always see because you're too busy using yours in place of his. To accompany is to keep all the supports, and at the same time to stop living for him. It's the difference between holding the hand of someone learning to walk and walking for the person, carrying them in your arms for the rest of their life for fear that they'll fall.

And this is why the verb of this book is not to let go, nor to rescue. It is to trust. To trust is not naive trust, it's not closing your eyes and hoping it goes well. It's trusting that your child doesn't need to be rescued from who he is. That his existence is not a permanent emergency awaiting your intervention. That he has a path, a different one, with different timings, and that your task is not to live that path for him, it's to walk beside him.

Think of a few concrete gestures, without demanding anything of yourself yet. There's a difference between answering for him before he tries, and giving him the time, however hard it is to wait. There's a difference between sparing him all frustration, and being beside him while he crosses a frustration that is within his reach. There's a difference between being the permanent bridge between him and the world, and slowly becoming the support he needs less and less as he finds his own supports. In each of these differences lives the same choice: to live for him, or to live beside him.

There's an example I like to give, because it shows the distinction without needing explanation. Imagine he's trying to do something that's hard for him, a task, a gesture, a word, and you see the effort, and you also see that he might manage it, if he has time. To carry is to step in front and do it for him, to spare him the cost, and to spare yourself the anguish of watching him struggle. To accompany is to stay close, with a calm body, saying with your presence "I am here, and I believe you can do it," and to let him get there in his own time, even if it takes long, even if he fails and tries again. Both of these look like love. But one says to him, "you're not capable, let me do it," and the other says to him, "I trust your strength." And it's the second message, repeated a thousand times in a thousand small gestures, that builds inside him the idea that he is capable. No support, no therapy, replaces this message, and only you can give it to him, and you can only give it to him if you're not always stepping in front.

And there's something you need to hear, because fear is going to protest here. To trust does not mean he will manage everything, nor that you will stop protecting him from what truly hurts him. There are things he won't be able to do alone, and that's all right, and keeping those supported is care, it's not carrying. The question is never to demand of him an autonomy he doesn't have. The question is not to rob him of the autonomy he does have. And often, in the midst of love and fear, we rob without seeing, we do for him what he could already do, we decide for him what he could already decide, we feel for him what he could already feel. To trust is to give back to him, gesture by gesture, what is his.

I'm not asking you to answer today the question we began with. I only ask you to let it stay with you. To notice, over the coming days, the moments when you carry and the moments when you accompany. Without judging yourself when you carry. Just noticing. Because to see is already to begin to change. And you, in reading this, are already seeing.

## 6. Who will see him when I am gone

We come to the deepest wound. To the one that lives beneath almost everything else. To the question that robs you of sleep in a way no other does.

Who will see him when I am gone?

Who will know his signs the way you know them? Who will have the patience, the reading, the love that you have? Who will understand him when he can't explain himself? Who will be there when you can no longer be? This question is not exaggeration, it's not drama, it's not a lack of faith. It's a mother's love looking at a reality that most mothers don't have to face with this clarity. And I'm not going to insult you with false guarantees. I'm not going to tell you "it will all be fine," because you don't need me to lie. What I'm going to do is sit beside you in this fear, because it deserves to be accompanied, not silenced.

This fear is real and it is legitimate. And at the same time, without your realizing it, it has an effect on the present that is worth seeing clearly. The fear of the future without you can slowly turn into an endless vigilance. Into a need to control everything, to predict everything, to fail at nothing, because each failure seems to confirm the fear. And that permanent vigilance, born of the purest love, ends up robbing you of the present with your child and robbing you of yourself. You live so deep inside the future he doesn't yet have, that you miss the days he has now, with you, here.

There's an image that may help you. When the fear of the future tightens, it makes you believe that you are the only column holding up the entire roof, and that if you give way, everything collapses. But your child's life is not a single column. It's a structure that builds itself, slowly, with many hands: the therapists who know him, the teachers who learn with him, the supports that exist today and the ones that will exist, his siblings if he has them, the people who haven't yet entered his life and who will, the world itself that changes and becomes, year after year, a little more able to welcome him. You are a precious part of that structure, the most precious while you are here. But you are not the whole structure, and the proof of love is not for you to hold everything alone, it's to let other hands learn to hold too. A child whom many people know how to care for is more protected, not less. A child whom only one person in the world knows how to care for is the most fragile of all. And you don't want that for him, no matter how much the fear pushes you exactly toward it.

Before I show you how to care for this fear, I want to help you ask it a question that changes a great deal: is this fear from today, or does it come from an older story? Because maternal fear rarely springs only from the child of now. Often an earlier story is activated within it, a fear that was already there before, from a loss in the family, from an abandonment, from an illness, from the suffering of someone who came before you, and that the child of today, with no fault of his own, awakens. When the fear tightens, try asking: does what I fear truly belong to this moment, or am I feeling, through my child, a fear that is older than he is? And there's a sister question: within this fear, what is real danger, what is the memory of an old pain, and what is the fantasy of catastrophe that my mind builds? Separating these three things doesn't make the fear disappear, but it takes away half its weight, because much of what terrifies you is not the danger of today, it's the echo of stories that aren't even your child's.

And there is, beneath the fear, something hard to see and freeing to recognize: the illusion that, if you watch and worry enough, you can prevent his suffering. It's an impossible position, that of trying to control the uncontrollable, and it depletes you without protecting anyone, because your child's fate, your child's life, is not, and cannot be, entirely in your hands. There's an inner gesture here that relieves more than all the vigilance, and that comes from the way I learned to look at this: that of bowing, with love, before your limits. Of saying, inwardly, something like: I wish I could protect you from everything, and I can't, it isn't a power that belongs to me, and so I leave with you, with love and with trust, your own life. It's not giving up on him. It's stepping out of the impossible place of someone who believes herself responsible for a fate that doesn't belong to her, and returning to your place, the place of mother, who loves, accompanies, and protects in what she can, without having to carry what no human being can carry. And when you bow like this before what you don't control, something happens: the guilt and the fear, which came from omnipotence, slowly transform into something more bearable and more true, which is sadness and love, without the impossible pretension of being able to do everything.

There is a way of caring for this fear without letting it become a sentence. It's not to solve it, because it isn't solved with words, and anyone who promises you a solution is selling you something. It's something else. It's to recognize that your child's future does not depend on you alone, and that this, as frightening as it seems, is also a release. There's the network that builds itself, slowly, with time: professionals, supports, people, institutions, siblings, the world itself that changes. Your child's life is not resting on your shoulders alone, even if the fear tells you it is. And one of the most loving things you can do for him is, precisely, to let other hands learn to hold him, instead of being the only one who knows. Because a child whom only one person in the world knows how to care for is a more fragile child, not a more protected one. And you don't want that for him.

But I have to tell you something now with great firmness, and with all care, because it is the most important thing in this whole book.

This fear, and this tiredness, and this carrying without pause, have a limit that the body and the soul cannot cross without breaking. And it is here, in this chapter, in this exact place, that the weight we've spoken of can stop being a weight and become something else, with another name, that is no longer treated with books or with understanding. It can become a true depression. It can become an anxiety that incapacitates you, that takes the ground from under you, that no longer lets you function. It can become a caregiver's burnout so deep that you have stopped feeling, sleeping, wanting, being able to get up.

If you recognize yourself in this, hear me with the same seriousness with which you would hear it if it were your child who needed help. If there's a sadness that hasn't passed in weeks. If anxiety takes away your sleep and your air. If you no longer feel pleasure in anything, not even in him. If there are thoughts that it would be better if you weren't here, or that everyone would be better off without you. If the tiredness is no longer tiredness, it's the bottom of a well where you see no light. If you feel you can't take any more and you see no way out.

None of this is weakness. None of this is a lack of love. None of this is solved with more effort, and please, please, don't try to solve it alone with more effort, because effort is exactly what brought you here. This calls for help. Real help, professional help, from someone who knows how to care for the ones who care. A doctor, a psychologist, a helpline, someone. Seeking that help takes nothing away from your place as a mother. On the contrary. It's the most responsible and most loving act you can do, for you and for him. Because your child doesn't need a mother who sacrificed herself until she disappeared. He needs a mother who is whole enough to stay. And to stay whole, sometimes, you have to let yourself be cared for.

Set the book down a moment, if you need to. Breathe. And if any of these lines spoke of you, promise me you won't stay alone with it. There are people who can help you carry. To ask is not to fail. It's to begin to come back.

## 7. To trust is the way back

If you've made it this far, something in you has already moved. Perhaps you don't yet quite know what. But something moved, because to see always changes something, even before we know what to do with what we saw.

I want to show you now, in broad strokes, the way back. Not the step by step, because the step by step doesn't fit in a book and isn't done alone, it's done with time, with presence, and sometimes with someone to accompany you. But the outlines, yes. The map. So you know there's a path, and which direction it points.

The path begins by setting down the rescuer. Not dismissing her, not condemning her, not pretending she was never needed. Thanking her, yes. She kept you on your feet through impossible years. And then, with tenderness, letting her rest. Telling her that you no longer have to rescue anyone, because your child is not drowning. He is living, in his own way, and he doesn't need to be rescued from his own existence.

Then, returning to your place. The place of mother is different from the place of rescuer, of therapist, of manager, of translator of the world. The place of mother is simpler and harder at the same time: it's the place of someone who loves, who is there, who accompanies, and who trusts that her child has a life of his own. When you return to your place, he can occupy his better. There are phrases that help to mark this return, simple phrases you can say inwardly, slowly, until the body begins to believe them. "You are my son. I am your mother." "I accompany you. I do not walk for you." "I trust your life more than I trust my fear." They're not magic formulas. They're reminders of where your place is.

Then, trusting his life. This is the part that costs the most, because to fear, trusting looks the same as abandoning. It isn't. To trust is to keep everything that protects and supports him, and at the same time to stop living his life from the inside. It's to believe that he has a strength that doesn't need to come from you, a path that doesn't need to be the one you imagined, a timing that doesn't need to be yours. "I don't need to correct who you are in order to love you." "I see your path. And I remain in mine." To trust is the verb that replaces carrying, and it's the heart of this whole crossing.

Then, receiving. You can't accompany with empty hands. An exhausted mother doesn't accompany, she drags herself along. That's why receiving is not selfishness, it's a condition: letting yourself be helped, letting yourself be cared for, resting without apologizing, getting back pieces of life that were only yours. Each thing you receive fills again the place from which you give. And what you give full is different from what you give empty. Your child notices the difference, even if he never knows how to name it.

Receiving has a difficulty of its own for a mother like you, and it's fair to name it. Receiving hurts, at first, because it takes you out of the position of the one who endures everything, and that position, however much it depletes you, has become your identity. Who are you if you stop being the one who holds things up? Setting down a little of the weight may bring, instead of immediate relief, a strange wave of guilt, as if resting were a betrayal, as if five minutes of yours were five minutes stolen from him. I want you to know that that guilt is a lie, and it's an old lie, one that wasn't born with you, that many women before you carried, the idea that to love is to disappear, that the good mother is the one who needs nothing. It isn't. The good mother is the one who exists enough to be able to stay. And staying, in your case, is a long-term project that requires you to spare yourself today.

And then, the simplest and the most radical: allowing yourself to exist. To exist as a person, not only as a function. To have five minutes that are yours without that being stealing from anyone. To have a thought, a taste, a desire that has nothing to do with caring. To remember that before you were his mother you were someone, and that that person didn't disappear, she only waited. To exist doesn't make you less of a mother. It makes you a whole mother, beside a whole person.

There's a phrase I saved for the end of this chapter, because it's the one many mothers need to hear more than any other, and at the same time the one they find hardest to believe. To rest is not to abandon. You can rest and still be his mother. Rest doesn't take away your love, doesn't take away your place, doesn't take away anything. It gives you back to yourself, so that you can keep being there. A mother who rests stays. A mother who never rests, one day, breaks. And he needs you whole and on your feet, for a long time, far more than he needs you perfect and exhausted.

Notice that none of these steps asks you to love less, to protect less, to support less. They ask you only to carry less. They are different things, and it's in that difference that all the possible relief lives. You can keep every therapy, every support, every gesture of care, and at the same time set down the weight that was never yours, the guilt that had no origin, the future that was not yours to live today, his life that was not yours to live from the inside. To love with open hands, instead of loving with loaded arms. That's all it is, and that's everything.

These are the outlines. The map, not the journey. The journey is deeper, slower, and needs more than words on a page. But the map already shows that there's somewhere to go. And you, now, already know the direction.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book for a simple reason: because I believe that understanding changes things. It doesn't change everything, and it doesn't change overnight. But it changes enough so that you can stop feeling alone, defective, or guilty of something you never did.

Now you know that the tiredness you feel has a name, and that it isn't weakness. You know that you didn't cause your child's neurodivergence, not with anything you did, nor with any dynamic in your family. You know that feeling the grief for an image takes nothing away from the love you have. You know how to recognize the tired rescuer who lives in you, and to look at her with tenderness instead of demanding more from her. You know how to ask the question that changes everything: am I living for him, or living beside him? You know that your fear of the future deserves care, and you know, above all, when that weight calls for real help. And you know that there is a way back, and that it is called trust.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can love you without carrying your life for you.

And perhaps, deep down, the hardest sentence of all is finally beginning to make sense: I can rest and still be your mother.

Seeing what you were carrying was the first step, and it's not a small one. But what is seen still has to be crossed, and that is no longer done by reading.

It was for that crossing that I created **FreeMe**.

FreeMe is not a course, it's not content to consume, it's not more information to add to what you already have too much of. It's a guided crossing that you do from the inside, at your own pace, to set down what was not yours to carry. And for a mother like you, who carries what no to-do list shows, there's a path designed for your specific pain: the guilt that has no origin, the fear of the future, the control that is born of love, the exhaustion of someone who lives ten years ahead. Not to teach you how to care for him, since you already know that better than anyone. To give you back to yourself.

There, the sentences you read here in silence gain voice and time, spoken slowly, with space for the body to receive them. What was a map here, there becomes ground you walk on. To accompany stops being an idea and becomes a practice. And to trust stops frightening and begins to relieve.

If you feel this is the moment, I'm there. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't the moment yet, that's perfectly all right. What you've read stays with you, like a seed that waits for its season. When it's time, the path will still be here.

But before you close these pages, I ask you just one thing. Today, make a small gesture for yourself. Five minutes that are yours. A glass of water drunk in peace. A moment of doing nothing, without apologizing for it. And, if you can, say inwardly, even if you don't believe it yet: my best is enough. You don't have to believe it yet. It's enough to say it. The body hears. And with time, it begins to remember that you too are someone. That before you are a mother, you are a person. And that that person needs to exist, so that the mother can stay.
