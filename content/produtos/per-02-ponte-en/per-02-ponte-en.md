# The one who is the bridge

**For you, who live in the middle, translating and soothing between others. Because a bridge has no side of its own, and you can stop being a passage and become a destination.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. It speaks of someone who learned to live in the middle, to mediate between others, and there is a point at which this stops being a gift and becomes an erasing of the self. If you recognize in yourself the exhaustion of someone who manages everyone's relationships, an anxiety that never lifts, or the sense that you don't exist outside the role of mediator, you deserve the support of a professional. Understanding your place is a beginning. It is not the whole journey.*

---

## 1. I live in the middle

Notice the place you occupy, in your relationships, in your family, in your groups. You live in the middle.

Between two who don't speak, you're the one who carries the message. Between two who have fallen out, you're the one who soothes things. Between two who don't understand each other, you're the one who translates one to the other, who explains what each of them meant, who softens what hurt, who keeps the peace possible. Always in the middle, always stitching together what threatens to tear.

You are the bridge. The one others cross to reach one another. The one who connects the riverbanks that, on their own, would never touch. The one who is always available to serve everyone's crossing, and who never, herself, arrives anywhere.

And you do it with an enormous talent. You feel what each person feels. You see both sides. You know what to say to one to calm him, and what to say to the other to bring her closer. You are the family's diplomat, the group's mediator, the one who holds together the bonds that without her would break. And everyone counts on this, the way one counts on a sure thing.

Notice how much of your energy goes into this. You're always attentive to the climate between people. Managing tensions that aren't even yours. Anticipating conflicts in order to avoid them. Translating, explaining, soothing, in an invisible and ceaseless work that no one sees, but that consumes you.

And perhaps you don't even remember it ever being any different. There having been a time when you could be with people without managing them, when a family or friends gathering wasn't a job of permanent monitoring, but a place where you simply were. For you, being with others has always been working, watching, mediating, and so perhaps you don't even know what it is, the lightness of being in a group without being responsible for its climate. That lightness exists, and it is possible, and it is one of the things this crossing can give back to you: being able, at last, to be with others as one of them, and not as the one who manages them all.

I want to tell you right away, before anything else, that this is not only a virtue, even though it looks like one. People say you have a knack for getting along with everyone, that you're a peacemaker, that you're good at bringing people together. And there is a true gift in that. But beneath the gift there is something else, older, that needs to be seen.

There is a place you took up early, the place of the middle, and which you never again let go of. You became the bridge, and you forgot that a bridge has a problem. A bridge has no side of its own. It is not a bank. It is only the passage between the banks of others.

This book is not going to teach you to stop caring about relationships, to stop connecting people, to become indifferent to the conflicts of others. I have come to look at something else. At the price of living in the middle. At the fact that, from being a passage for so long, you never had a side of your own, a destination of your own, a place where you are you, and not the connection between two.

And I'll tell you the direction now, so you can read in peace. You can stop being a bridge and stay connected. You can cross over to your own side, have a place of your own, exist at the center and not only in the middle, without losing the people you connect. Your worth is not in being the passage for others. And there is a side, yours, that you never got to inhabit, waiting for you.

I want you to understand, right away, the difference between what it looks like and what it is. What it looks like, from the outside, is a generous, empathetic person, with a rare gift for getting along with everyone. What it is, on the inside, is a person who learned to live in the function of connecting others, and who lost herself in that middle. Your empathy is real, your gift is real, and there is nothing wrong with feeling others. What this book comes to show you is that this gift, put entirely in the service of mediating, became an erasing, and that you can keep your sensitivity without losing yourself in it, using it for yourself too, and not only for others.

This book does not come to tell you that caring for relationships is wrong, nor that you have to become cold, indifferent, someone who turns her back on conflicts. It comes to distinguish caring from compulsive mediating, having empathy from having to manage everyone's emotions, being connected from being the bridge with no side of its own. It comes to give you back a center of your own, a side of your own, without taking away your connection to others, but placing you, at last, yourself too, on the map of the people you care for.

Perhaps you recognize yourself in small things.

In always being the one to take the first step to reconcile those who have fallen out, even when the falling-out isn't yours, and even when no one asked you to.

In feeling responsible when two people you love don't get along, as though it were your mission to unite them, and your fault if they drift apart.

In knowing very well what each person around you feels and wants, and very poorly what you yourself feel and want, as though your antenna were always turned outward.

In having the sense, in moments of silence, that without the role of connecting others you wouldn't quite know who you are, nor what place you'd occupy.

In feeling divided, almost guilty, whenever you have to take a side, as though choosing a position were betraying the one left on the other side.

They are all the same thing. Life in the middle. The bridge that forgot to have a side of its own.

I want to show you how this shows up in everyday life, in things you may already take to be who you are. In a group conversation, you're more attentive to making sure no one feels excluded, to softening what might hurt, to managing the climate, than to taking part with what you yourself think. When someone speaks badly of another person you love, you feel divided, with the urgency to defend, to explain one to the other, to repair the relationship between them, as though it were yours. You receive confidences from both sides of a conflict, and you hold both, you carry both, without ever being able to take a side, because your place is the middle.

And there's one detail that reveals everything: when they ask you what you want, what you think, what you feel, you go blank, or you answer with what would be best for the harmony, and not with what is, in fact, yours. You're so trained to feel others that you've lost access to yourself. You can read everyone's desire in an instant, and about your own desire you hesitate, you don't know, you have to guess. It's as though your antenna were permanently turned outward, toward what others need, and never inward, toward what you need.

And there's something nobody sees, beneath your gift for uniting. People value you for being a peacemaker, easy, good with everyone, and they lean on you to manage their tensions, without imagining what it costs you. They don't see the exhaustion of someone who carries everyone's relationships, the loneliness of someone who is a passage and never a destination, the emptiness of someone who doesn't know who she is outside the middle. They see the gift, not the erasing. And you, valued for uniting everyone, become even more bound to the middle, because now being the bridge is also being loved, and leaving it feels like risking the place that gift gave you.

## 2. Keeping the peace

Go back. To the beginning. To when you learned to live in the middle.

Almost always, it was in a house where there was tension. Where two people, or more, didn't understand each other. Where there was conflict, coldness, silent war, and where peace was fragile, always hanging by a thread.

And you, a sensitive child, felt that tension in your body, before you understood it in your head. You felt the danger of the house breaking apart, of your own people drifting away, of the family coming undone. And you did what a child does in the face of that danger. You tried to keep them together.

You became the one who soothed things. The one who carried the message from one to the other. The one who said the right thing to calm the father, and the right thing to calm the mother. The one who placed herself in the middle of the conflicts, translating, softening, uniting what threatened to break.

Perhaps you were the child between two parents who didn't speak to each other, and who passed the messages between them. Perhaps you were the one who distracted, who cracked jokes, who changed the subject when the tension rose. Perhaps you were the one who comforted one side and then the other, running between the two banks of a divided house.

Or perhaps you were placed, more explicitly, in the middle of a war. The child to whom each side complained about the other, who was asked to take sides, who was used as a messenger, as an ally, as a witness to each one's grievances against the other. And there is no place more impossible for a child than the middle of two people she loves and who wound each other, because whichever side she takes is a betrayal of the other, and the only way to survive is to take no side at all, to be the neutral bridge, the one who belongs to both and to neither. And that forced neutrality, that impossibility of having a side, is the root of your not knowing, today, how to have a side of your own.

Notice what a child comes to know, placed in the middle of banks that don't touch. That having an opinion of one's own, a side, a desire, is dangerous, because it might upset the fragile peace. That safety lies in being neutral, in understanding everyone, in committing to no one. And that learning, which saved you then, left you without the most basic capacity for having a life of your own: that of knowing what you want, what you feel, which side you are on. You learned to be on every side, which is the same as having no side at all.

Notice what a child learns, placed to hold up a house that threatens to break apart. She learns that her place is the middle. That her function is to keep the peace. That love, belonging, the safety of the house depend on her managing to connect what wants to come apart.

And she learns a very particular loyalty. The loyalty of keeping the peace. If I leave the middle, they drift apart. If I stop connecting, they break. The unity of the family depends on me, and so I can't leave the middle, I can't have a side, I can't stop being the bridge.

That loyalty was etched deep. And it governs you still. That's why, as an adult, you remain in the middle. You go on soothing, translating, uniting. You go on feeling that, if you leave the middle, the people you love will drift apart, and that it will be your fault. The child who held up the peace of the house became the adult who holds up everyone's peace, and who doesn't know how to exist outside that post.

I want you to see this without guilt, and without accusing those who raised you. The tension that put you in the middle was not, most of the time, created to wound you. The adults in your house were caught in their own conflicts, in their own incapacities, and didn't see that a child was carrying the peace they couldn't make. They didn't mean to put you in the middle. But they did, without meaning to, by letting you fill the place of mediator of a house that couldn't mediate itself.

And I want you to see where this is stored, because it matters. This loyalty is not just an idea, a habit that can be changed at will. It's etched deep, in a place where keeping the peace between others became linked to your own safety, to your emotional survival as a child. That's why, today, when there's tension between people you love, you don't decide rationally to intervene, your body fires off, the anxiety rises, and you rush to soothe things before you think, like someone answering an alarm. It's not a choice, it's a reflex. And that's why it isn't enough to realize that the relationships of others are not yours to fix: the reflex keeps firing, and it's disarmed slowly, with practice, and often with help.

I want to add a root you may recognize. Sometimes, the child in the middle isn't only keeping the peace, she's securing her own place. In a house in tension, being useful, being the one who unites, who calms, the one no one wants to lose, is a way of ensuring that there's always a place for you, that you're not dispensable, that you serve a purpose. And so the function of the bridge became not only a way of protecting the family, but of protecting your own belonging: as long as you're indispensable to everyone's connection, no one leaves you out. That's why leaving the middle frightens you twice over, because it threatens not only the peace of others, but your place among them.

## 3. Existing in the connection, not at the center

There is something deep here, and I want to name it carefully, because it is the heart of your erasing. You learned to exist in the connection, and not at the center.

Notice what this means. There are people who exist at the center of themselves. They have a side of their own, a ground of their own, a life that is theirs, and from that center they connect to others. First they are, and then they relate.

You, no. You exist in the connection. Your sense of yourself comes from being between others, from connecting them, from being the passage. Without that function, without someone to mediate, without a relationship to manage, you feel that there is no person there, only an emptiness. You have no center of your own. You have only the middle, the bridge, the connection.

And the shadow hiding here is precisely that. The lack of an existence of your own. The sense that, outside the role of connecting others, you don't truly exist. That there is no you, with a side of your own, with a life of your own, but only a function, that of passage between the lives of others.

That's why it's so hard for you to leave the middle. It's not only the fear that others will drift away. It's that leaving the middle confronts you with the question of who you are, you, when you're not connecting anyone. And that question, for someone who never inhabited her own center, opens an abyss. It's easier to keep serving the crossings of others than to face the silence of not knowing who you are, and so you return, always, to the middle, like someone fleeing a question too big.

Notice how this costs you. You live your whole life in the service of the relationships of others, and never of your own life. You know everyone's desires, and you don't know your own. You manage everyone's emotions, and you've lost contact with your own. You're an expert in other people's lives, and almost illiterate in your own, because you never stopped in the middle long enough to look at it.

And there's a deep confusion this creates, that you may live without realizing: you stop being able to tell apart what you feel from what others feel. From absorbing the emotions of others for so long, from tuning in for so long to everyone's states, you've lost the boundary between yourself and others. You feel one person's sadness, another's anger, the group's anxiety, as though they were yours, and you no longer know where they end and you begin. You live permanently invaded by what isn't yours, and that invasion, which you take for empathy, is also a loss of yourself, because a person who feels everything others feel stops having room to feel what is hers.

I want you to see the difference between healthy empathy and this erasing. Healthy empathy feels the other from a center of one's own: I sense what you feel, and at the same time I know what I feel, and I don't lose myself in you. Yours, no. Yours dissolves you into the other, makes you lose your boundary, leaves you with no ground of your own from which to feel. And an empathy that dissolves you is not a full gift, it's a gift without a root, because to truly feel the other you need, first, to exist yourself, to have a side from which to feel. Recovering that side doesn't take away your empathy, it gives it, at last, a ground.

And there's a particular loneliness in this. The loneliness of someone who is always between two, and never truly with anyone. Because the bridge has no relationship of its own. It connects others, but it is no one's destination. People pass through you to reach one another, and you stay in the middle, serving their crossing, without ever being, yourself, the place where someone wants to arrive.

And it costs you your health, too, because managing everyone's relationships is an invisible and ceaseless work that drains you. Your mind never stops monitoring the climate, anticipating conflicts, calculating what to say to each one. It's a permanent vigilance, that no one sees, and that consumes you from within, and many people who live in the middle fall ill, not out of weakness, but out of the exhaustion of carrying, alone, the emotional balance of an entire group.

I want you to feel the weight of this. Imagine living your whole life as a passage. Useful, important, necessary, and never a destination. Always the middle, never the bank. Always connecting, never being sought for yourself. It's a place of a deep loneliness, disguised as usefulness, and perhaps you already take it for normal, so used to it are you.

And there's a particular sadness in realizing that, from caring for the relationships of others for so long, you never cared for your own. That you know how to unite everyone, and you don't have, perhaps, a connection that is only yours, deep, between two, in which you are the destination and not the middle. That you're everyone's confidant, and you don't have, perhaps, anyone to confide in about what is yours, because your place was always to listen, never to be listened to. There is a hunger for a connection of your own beneath so much connecting of others, and it is that hunger, more than anything, that this crossing comes, at last, to feed.

## 4. Who am I when I'm not between two

I'm going to leave a question here. On its own. Don't run from it.

Who are you when you're not between two sides?

Stay with it. Notice the emptiness it opens.

For you, this question may be one of the hardest there is, because it touches your very existence. You know who you are in the middle. The mediator, the translator, the one who unites. But you, outside the middle, with no one to connect, who are you?

Perhaps you don't know. Perhaps, imagining yourself without the role, you feel a blank, an emptiness, almost a disappearing. Because your sense of yourself is glued to the function of the bridge, and without it there is, at the center, a place that was never cultivated.

And the shadow hiding here is the emptiness of your own side. You spent your life serving the banks of others, and you never built your own. You don't have a side of your own, a ground of your own, a center of your own, where you can be when you're not connecting anyone. And without that side, the question of who you are, outside the middle, is left without an answer.

Notice how this imprisons you. You can't leave the middle, even when it drains you, because outside the middle you have nowhere to be. It's like a bridge that's afraid of becoming a bank, because it never knew how to be anything other than a passage. So you stay, eternally in the middle, serving crossings that aren't yours, because it's the only place you know how to exist.

There is also a fragility hidden within your importance as a bridge. Because a bridge does not live for itself. It exists in the service of the crossing of others. And you, from being a bridge for so long, forgot that you are also a person, that you too have the right to a side, to a destination, to a life that is not only connecting the lives of others. The bridge has nowhere to go. And you, by becoming a bridge, slowly lost your own destination.

And I want to tell you something about the terror this question raises. What frightens you about leaving the middle is not only the fear that others will drift away. It's the fear of discovering that, without the function, there is no one there. That you are only the bridge, and nothing more. That is the deepest fear, and the most false. Because beneath the function there is, yes, a person, with tastes, with a way of seeing the world, with desires that never had room to appear. You didn't lose her, you only never let her exist, occupied as you were with serving others. And the emptiness you fear finding is not the absence of a person, it's the silence of a person who was never listened to, and who waits, only, for you to begin to listen to her.

I want you, too, to see the difference between being sought and being used, because it will hurt but it will free you. As a bridge, you are much sought, yes, but for the function: to fix, to mediate, to listen, to unite. You're rarely sought for yourself, for your company, for the person you are. And there is an enormous difference between being needed and being wanted. You have been much needed, and perhaps little wanted for yourself, and that distinction, painful to see, is also the beginning of wanting something else: no more usefulness, but, at last, being wanted for who you are, and not for what you serve.

I want to tell you something about this emptiness, because it begins to transform it. The emptiness you feel when you imagine leaving the middle is not proof that you don't exist outside it. It's the sign of a center yet to be inhabited. Of a side of your own that is there, waiting, but that you never cultivated because you were always busy serving the sides of others. The emptiness is not your truth. It's your own side, yet to be built, calling for you.

Don't answer the question of who you are outside the middle just yet. It's built, slowly, as you begin to inhabit your own center. But leave it open. Because it is in that question that the door lies. On the day there is a side of your own, a place where you are you and not the connection between two, you stop needing the middle in order to exist. And then, only then, can you cross over to your own side.

## 5. From passage to destination

If you exist only as a connection, then there is a passage waiting for you. From passage to destination.

Read this slowly, because it reverses your entire place. Your whole life you were a passage. The middle through which others connect. And I propose something new to you. Becoming a destination. A place where someone wants to arrive, for you, and not a place one passes through to reach another.

Notice the difference. The passage serves the crossing of others. The destination is sought for itself. The passage is useful. The destination is wanted. And you always lived as useful, never as wanted for yourself, because you placed yourself in the position of the one who serves the connection, and not of the one who is, herself, a bank where one wants to arrive.

Becoming a destination means ceasing to live in the middle and beginning to live on your own side. Having a center of your own. A life that is yours, and not the management of the lives of others. Desires of your own, and not the translation of the desires of others. A place where you are you, whole, and where people seek you for who you are, and not for the function you serve.

And I want you to see that being a destination is not closing yourself off, isolating yourself, ceasing to connect. It's the opposite: it's having, at last, a place from which to connect for real. Someone who has no side of her own cannot truly connect with anyone, because there is no center there from which the connection departs. Only someone who exists, with her own bank, can find another bank and build a true bridge, with two ends, and not a passage through which others simply cross. Having a destination of your own does not pull you away from people. It gives you, for the first time, a place from which to meet them as an equal, and not as a service.

I know this frightens you. Because leaving the middle, ceasing to mediate, letting others understand each other, or not understand each other, without you, feels like risking everything. It feels like letting them drift apart. It feels like your fault, if the peace breaks.

But notice this freeing truth. The relationships of others are not your responsibility. Whether two adults speak or not, understand each other or not, draw closer or drift apart, is their business, not yours. You took on, as a child, a mission that wasn't yours, that of keeping others united, and you carry it still. But it isn't yours. Every relationship between two people belongs to those two people, and not to the bridge that connects them.

Notice the weight this takes off you, when you see it for real. All the tension you feel between the people around you, all the disharmony, all the conflict, you carry it as though it were a failing of yours, as though it were your job to resolve it. And it isn't. You're not responsible for guaranteeing that everyone gets along, that there are no conflicts, that peace reigns. That is a crushing weight, and an impossible one, that you took on without anyone truly assigning it to you. Giving that weight back, acknowledging that the harmony between others is not in your hands, is like setting down a backpack full of stones that you carried your whole life without knowing you could put it down.

And I want you to distinguish your desire for peace from your fear of conflict, because they are different things. Wanting peace is healthy. But what moves you, very often, is not love of peace, it's the terror of conflict, etched in a house where conflict threatened to break everything. And so you soothe things not out of choice, but out of panic, avoiding any tension as though it were a mortal danger. Learning that conflict, today, is not the danger it was, that two people can disagree, fall out, and the relationship survive, and you survive, is part of freeing yourself from the middle. Not every tension is a threat, and not every conflict needs to be erased by you.

When you stop being the bridge, two things happen. Either the others find, without you, ways to connect, and you discover that their connection didn't depend on you as you thought. Or they don't connect, and you discover that this is a choice of theirs, and not a failing of yours. In either case, you're freed from a responsibility that was never yours to carry.

Notice, in fact, that by being the permanent bridge you may be, without meaning to, keeping the others from truly connecting. Because as long as you're the one translating one to the other, softening, passing the messages, people never learn to speak directly, to resolve their conflicts, to build their own connections. You became so effective at uniting that the others settled into not knowing how to unite on their own. And by leaving the middle, however much it costs, you give them what your mediation took from them: the opportunity, and the responsibility, to relate directly, as adults, without needing a bridge to reach one another.

And there's a hard truth, but a freeing one, about the relationships that only exist because of you. Some of the connections you hold up perhaps shouldn't, in fact, be held up. There are relationships that would naturally drift apart, conflicts that would resolve through distance, distances that would be healthy, and that you, in your effort to unite everything, keep from happening. Not every connection should be maintained at all costs. And part of leaving the middle is accepting that it is not your job to keep united what, perhaps, should go separate ways. Letting the relationships of others follow their course, even when that course is drifting apart, is not failing, it's respecting that each relationship belongs to its people, and not to you to save.

I want to pause here for a moment, carefully, because there is a point at which living in the middle stops being a pattern and becomes a wound that asks for more. If managing everyone's relationships has drained you to the point of making you ill, if there's an anxiety that won't let go of you, or if the sense of not existing outside the role of bridge brings you an emptiness that hurts, that deserves to be cared for with professional support. And if, beneath your having taken up the middle far too early, there is a house of tension, of fear, of instability, that still runs through you today, then we are in the presence of something that does not undo itself just by reading. Building a center of your own, after a life of existing only in the connection, is rarely done alone. It's done, very often, with hands that accompany you in discovering who you are, outside the middle.

The awakening, when it comes, is a moment of strange lightness. It's the day you let two people understand each other on their own, when you don't carry the message, when you don't place yourself in the middle of the conflict, and you discover that the world doesn't end. That the tension between others exists without destroying you. That you can have a side of your own, without the house breaking apart. It's a small instant, but it changes everything, because it disproves the belief that your existence had to be a passage.

## 6. Crossing over to my side

There is a verb for this crossing. To cross over.

To cross over to your own side. To leave the middle, the bridge, the passage, and arrive, at last, at a bank that is yours. To stop living in the connection between others and begin to live in your own center, with ground, with a side, with a destination.

And I want to show you what it really is to leave the middle and set your feet on a bank, day after day.

Crossing over to your side begins with something hard. Leaving the middle of conflicts that aren't yours. When two people around you fall out, not rushing to soothe things. When someone wants to put you in the middle, carrying messages, taking sides, translating, refusing, with love, that place. Saying, in words or in gestures, that this is between them, and that you are not the bridge.

It's going to cost you. You're going to feel the old loyalty, the old fear that they'll drift apart, the old guilt of not keeping the peace. And you're going to want to return to the middle, to mediate, to unite, so that the anxiety will pass.

But it's precisely there, in that staying outside the middle despite the anxiety, that you begin to cross over. Because each time you leave the middle, and the house doesn't break apart, you learn that you weren't the one holding it together. And each time you refuse to be a passage, you gain a little more of your own side.

Crossing over is also beginning to ask yourself what you want, what you feel, what you desire. To turn the attention, which was always on others, toward yourself. To build, slowly, a center of your own, a life that is yours, a side where you are you and not the management of others.

And I want to warn you about something: at first, that question, what do I want, may have no answer. You'll ask yourself what you feel, what you desire, which side you're on, and find a silence, a blank, an uncertainty. Don't be frightened by that, nor take it as proof that there's no one there. It's only the effect of a whole life turned outward: your access to yourself rusted from disuse. The answer doesn't come right away because the question is new, but it comes, slowly, as you insist on asking it, on stopping to listen to yourself, on giving attention to what's going on with you. Relearning to feel yourself is like relearning a forgotten language: at first it's hard, then it comes back.

And I want to teach you to tolerate the tension of others without resolving it, because that's where a large part of your crossing lies. When there's a conflict around you, you're going to feel an almost unbearable pressure to intervene, to soothe, to unite. Learning to stay with that tension, to let it exist without rushing to resolve it, is a muscle that is trained. At first, the anxiety will be great, you'll feel that something terrible is going to happen if you don't do anything. But, if you stay, you'll discover, slowly, that the tension of others doesn't destroy you, that they resolve it, or not, without you, and that you can exist, whole, in the middle of the discomfort of not mediating. It's in that toleration of the tension of others that your freedom is born.

I want to give you a sign to guide you by. Notice, over the course of a day, how much of your attention is on the relationships of others and how much is on your own life. How many times you busy yourself managing what's going on between people, and how many you busy yourself with what's going on with you. That ratio is the map of your erasing, and you can begin, slowly, to bring more attention to your own side, and less to the middle of others.

I won't give you a method. But I leave you the principle. Leave the middle. Leave the relationships of others to others. And use the energy you spent connecting the banks of others to build, at last, your own. Because you have the right to a side of your own. To a destination. To a place where you are you, and not the bridge through which the lives of everyone pass.

## 7. Ceasing to be a bridge and staying connected

There is a fear that binds you, and I want to go to meet it, because it is what keeps you in the middle. The fear that, if you stop being a bridge, you'll lose the connections. You'll become isolated. You'll be left alone, without the people you connected.

And I want to undo that fear clearly. Ceasing to be a bridge is not the same as ceasing to be connected. They are different things.

Notice. A bridge connects the banks, but has no connection of its own with any of them. It serves the crossing, but is not part of the relationship. When you stop being a bridge, you don't lose connections, because the bridge, deep down, never had connections of its own. You gain, rather, the possibility of having, for the first time, connections of your own, from bank to bank, in which you are one of the ends, and not the middle.

I'll put it another way. As long as you're a bridge, your relationships are all triangular. You, and two others you connect. You're always a threesome, managing the relationship between two. When you cross over to your side, you can begin to have relationships between two. You and another person, directly, without being the middle of anything. And that direct connection, from bank to bank, is deeper, truer, than any bridge, because in it you exist, with your own side, and not as a passage.

And I want you to see that, to have relationships between two, you're going to need to do something that terrifies you: to show your side. To say what you think, what you feel, what you disagree with, what you want, even if that creates tension, even if the other doesn't agree. Because a direct relationship requires two people with sides of their own, and you, trained to have no side, are going to have to learn to appear, to take a position, to risk disagreement. It's going to be uncomfortable, because it goes against everything you learned. But it's only this way, by showing yourself, that you stop being the neutral bridge and become a real person, someone with whom one can have a real relationship, and not just a mediation.

And don't fear that showing your side will push people away. It may push away those who only wanted you as a bridge, as a service, as a mediator, and that loss, however much it costs, is a liberation. But it will bring closer those who want you, who prefer a real person, with opinions and desires, to a neutral and always-available bridge. The relationships that survive your side are the true ones, and the ones that don't survive were never, in fact, relationships with you, they were only with the function you served. Showing yourself is, thus, also a way of discovering who wants you, and not only your usefulness.

I want to show you what opens up when you stop being a bridge. There opens up the possibility of being sought for yourself. Of someone wanting to arrive at you, and not pass through you to reach another. Of being a destination, and not a passage. And there is an enormous joy in this, for someone who always lived in the middle. The joy of discovering that one can be wanted for who one is, and not only for the function one serves.

It's going to cost you, because the old voice, the one that tells you that without uniting others you have no place, is etched deep, and it will appear each time you leave the middle, with its anxiety of always. It will tell you that you're being selfish, leaving the others adrift, abandoning the peace it was your job to make. You won't silence it all at once, and that isn't what's asked of you, what's asked is that you stop running to the middle when it calls. To hear it say go back to the middle, and to answer it, slowly, this is between them, and I have my own side. And each time you stay outside the middle despite it, and the house doesn't break apart, you weaken the old voice, and strengthen a new one, more yours, that knows you have the right to exist without being anyone's passage.

And I want to leave you something, about the ones who watch you, especially if you have children. Because this place is passed on, from generation to generation. If your children see you live in the middle, managing everyone's relationships, erasing yourself to keep the peace, they learn that this is how one loves, and they risk becoming, themselves too, the bridges who lose themselves. The most freeing thing you can teach those who watch you is not the example of the one who unites everyone at the cost of herself. It's that of a person who has a side of her own, who cares for her relationships without losing herself in those of others, who belongs without erasing herself. Sometimes a person crosses over to her side, at last, not only for herself, but to show those she loves that one can be connected without ceasing to exist.

And there also opens up a liberation for the others. Because as long as you're the bridge, you keep people dependent on you to connect, and you keep them, without meaning to, from building connections of their own. When you leave the middle, you let them connect directly, or not, and that, far from pushing them apart, gives them the responsibility for their own relationships, which was never yours to carry.

I want you to see, as well, that crossing over to your side does not make you less sensitive, less good with others, less able to connect those who need to be connected. Your sensitivity stays there, intact. What changes is that it stops being a compulsive obligation, without a boundary, and becomes a choice. You'll be able, yes, to help unite, to mediate when it makes sense, to use your gift, but from a center of your own, and by choice, and not because you don't know how to exist any other way. The gift, put in the service of a life of your own, instead of replacing it, becomes a richness, and not a prison.

There is a joy waiting on the other side. The joy of having a side of your own. Of being a destination, and not only a passage. Of having direct relationships, between two, in which you exist whole. Of using your enormous sensitivity, your gift for feeling others, in the service of your own life, and not only of the management of the lives of others. That joy is your right, and it is on the other side of the fear, on the day you cross over, at last, to your side.

And if there is a single thought you take from these pages, let it be this. You are not the connection between the lives of others. You are a life, whole, with a side of your own, with desires, with opinions, with a center where someone can want to arrive. The function of the bridge was a place you took up to survive and to belong, but it is not who you are. And discovering who you are, on the other side of the bridge, is not losing the connections, it's gaining, at last, the first connection that was always missing: the connection with yourself.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book out of a simple conviction. Understanding changes things.

It doesn't change everything all at once. It doesn't make you, tomorrow, leave a place you took up your whole life. But it changes enough for you to stop confusing your erasing with a gift, and your usefulness with belonging.

Now you know that you live in the middle, and that this is not only a virtue nor a gift, it's a place you took up early, in a house in tension, to keep everything united.

You know that you learned to keep the peace of a house in tension, and that you carry still the old loyalty that, if you leave the middle, the others drift apart, and the fault will be yours.

You know that what moves you, very often, is not love of peace, it's the terror of conflict, and that conflict, today, is not the danger it was.

You know that you exist in the connection and not at the center, and that beneath this lies the lack of an existence of your own, a side of your own yet to inhabit, a boundary yet to recover between yourself and others.

You know who you are, or you still don't, when you're not between two, and that the emptiness that appears there is not your truth, it's your center yet to inhabit, calling for you.

You know that you can go from passage to destination, and that the verb is to cross over, to your side, to the bank that was always yours.

You know that you feel everything others feel, to the point of losing your boundary, and that recovering a center of your own does not take away your empathy, it gives it, at last, a ground.

And you know that ceasing to be a bridge is not losing the connections, it's being able, at last, to have your own, direct, between two, in which you are the destination and not the middle.

I want to leave you with an image, and I want you to keep it.

You are a bridge of stone, ancient, solid, patient, over a wide river. For years others have passed through you, from one bank to the other, and you bear the weight of all of them, loyal, firm, always in the middle. And you never arrived at either bank, because a bridge arrives nowhere, it is only the middle.

But you are not a bridge. You are a person, who learned to live as a bridge. And a person can do what a bridge cannot. She can cross over. She can leave the middle and arrive at a bank. She can have a side, a ground, a place to be that is not the passage of others. She can, at last, stop serving only the crossing of others, and begin to make her own.

That is the crossing. Not ceasing to connect with people. But ceasing to live between them, and beginning to live on your own side. To cross over, at last, to the bank that was always yours, and where you never got to set foot.

I want you to know what to expect from this crossing, so that you don't live it as one more thing to be done to perfection. It's going to be slow, and it's going to have setbacks. Days when you stay on your side, and days when you run back to the middle, to mediate, to soothe, as always. And the old loyalty will use each setback to tell you, you see, your place is to unite others. Don't believe it. The setbacks are part of it. No one leaves all at once a place they took up their whole life. It's left little by little, and each time you stay on your side and no one is lost, a new piece of proof remains, and it's from those proofs, added together, that the bank of your own, slowly, is built.

And I want to tell you one last thing, simple, to close. You were not made to be a passage. The child who placed herself in the middle to keep the house united did what she could, with an enormous love, to hold up what the adults didn't hold up. There is nothing to reproach in her. But she deserved to have had a side, a center, a place where she was herself and not the connection between others. And that side waits for you still, intact, on the other side of the bridge. You don't have to deserve it, nor to earn it by uniting anyone else. It's enough that you cross over, at last, to it.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds up this book. I can stop being a bridge and stay connected. More than that: only by ceasing to be a bridge will I be able, at last, to connect for real.

Crossing over was the first step. But there is a second, and that one isn't done by reading. It's done by leaving, slowly, the middle. By leaving the relationships of others to others. By building, day by day, your own side. And there is a deeper work, with people to accompany you, to inhabit a center that was never cultivated, because no one builds a side of their own from nothing, alone, from one day to the next.

And before you close these pages, I ask one thing of you only. A hard thing, for you.

This week, when you feel the impulse to place yourself in the middle, to mediate, to soothe a tension that isn't yours, don't do it. Let the others understand each other, or not. And instead of spending that energy in their middle, turn it toward yourself. Ask yourself what you want, what you feel, what you desire, in that moment. Notice the anxiety of not uniting anyone, and stay with it, on your own side. Because it is in that staying outside the middle, on your own side, that you begin to cross over, and that you discover, at last, that there is, there, a bank that was always yours, waiting for you.

And if, in doing so, you feel that you're being selfish, cold, abandoning the others to their tension, remember: that thought is the old loyalty, not the truth. The truth is that you're giving back to each one their own relationship, and reclaiming, at last, your place. Leaving the middle is not abandoning anyone. It's ceasing to live the lives of others to begin, finally, to live your own.

Because you deserve to be a destination, and not only a passage, and there is a whole bank, yours, waiting for you to cross over, at last.

Because there is a bank that was always yours, and where you never set foot, waiting for you.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
