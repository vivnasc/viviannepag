# The woman who is afraid of her own size

**For the woman who learned to make herself small so as not to lose anyone. Because your smallness does not protect those you love, it only erases you.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This book touches the fear of occupying your space, and beneath it live, at times, old loyalties and wounds of belonging that stir things deep. If, as you read, you feel an anguish open that does not pass, or a sadness that paralyzes you, please seek the support of a professional. Growing into your size is delicate work, and it deserves, at times, hands to accompany you. Asking for help does not diminish you. It is part of occupying, with care, the place that is yours.*

---

## 1. Shrinking to fit

There is a gesture you have made for so long that you no longer even feel it: shrinking. You make yourself smaller than you are. You lower your voice when you could speak louder. You hide what you know so as not to seem to know too much. You minimize your achievements, "oh, it was nothing," "I got lucky," when it was a great deal and it was merit. You do not occupy the space you would have the right to occupy, in a room, in a conversation, in a life, because occupying it seems to you, somehow, wrong, dangerous, or impolite.

And there is a particular skill in this, that few people notice: you are very good at making yourself small. You learned to read a room and to calculate exactly how much space you can occupy without bothering anyone. You learned to shine enough to be accepted, but never so much that you become a threat. You learned to succeed, but to apologize for it. To be capable, but not to show it too much. To grow, but with an invisible ceiling that you yourself set, always a little below what you could reach, so as not to cross a line that no one marked but that you feel, clearly, you must not cross.

And the most curious thing is that you do have size. You have capacity, you have talent, you have a real greatness that, in the moments when it escapes, surprises others and even yourself. It is not that you are small. It is that you are afraid of being large. There is an enormous difference between having no size and being afraid of your own size, and you are the second: a woman of real greatness who spends her life shrinking to hide it, to contain it, to keep it within limits that make it fit without bothering anyone. Your problem was never the lack. It was the fear of what you have.

And perhaps you recognize this in concrete scenes. It is preparing well for a meeting and then not saying the best idea, afraid of standing out too much. It is having a capacity that others do not have and pretending you do not, so as not to create distance. It is refusing opportunities you would have deserved, with an "I'm not ready yet," when the truth is that you are, but growing frightens you. It is choosing, without noticing, environments, relationships, jobs, below your level, where you feel safe because you do not have to occupy your full size. It is feeling a strange discomfort, almost guilt, in the rare moments when you truly shine, as if you had done something wrong. Each of these scenes has the same signature: a woman of real greatness carefully managing her own light so that it never becomes too much.

And there is a particular exhaustion in this, that few see. It is tiring to live contained, to keep the dimmer always low, to constantly calculate how much space one can occupy, to watch one's own light so it does not dazzle. It is invisible and permanent work, that of keeping yourself smaller than you are, and it wears you out in a way nobody notices, because from the outside you look only like a modest woman, simple, with no great pretensions. Nobody sees the effort it costs you to contain, every day, a greatness that wants to come out and that you hold back. And that chronic containment is one of the most silent exhaustions there is: that of someone who spends her energy not on growing, but on preventing herself from growing.

I want to tell you, right from the start, that this shrinking is not false modesty, nor is it a lack of ambition, nor is it insecurity in the simple sense of the word. It is something else, deeper and more moving: it is an old strategy of protection. At some point, you learned that being large was dangerous, that occupying your space cost something, that growing put you at risk of losing something important. And so, without deciding it, you chose smallness as a way to protect yourself, to belong, not to lose. The shrinking is not a defect of yours. It is an armor you built for a reason, and that we are going to look at carefully, because it is only when one sees the reason for the armor that one can, slowly, begin to set it down.

And there is a particular loneliness in being a woman who makes herself small, that few understand. It is the loneliness of never being entirely known, because you always hide a part of yourself, the larger part, the most luminous. People relate to the reduced version you show, and you are left with the strange feeling of being loved for a small part of what you are, while the larger part remains hidden, unseen, unloved, because you do not let it appear. And that generates a background loneliness, even in the midst of relationships, the loneliness of someone who was never seen whole. To occupy your size is also to come out of that loneliness: it is to let yourself finally be seen as you are, in your greatness, and to discover that you can be loved whole, and not only in the small version you showed. It is a risk, because showing yourself whole exposes you, but it is also the only way to be truly known, and to be loved not for your smallness, but for everything you are.

And I did not come, in this book, to tell you that you have to become an arrogant woman, who occupies everything, who walks over others, who does not care about anyone. That is not what this is about, and that is, in fact, a caricature that you may use to frighten yourself and stay small. What I came to do is to help you see where your fear of being large comes from, what it costs you, and how you can, slowly, occupy the place that life gave you, not to abandon anyone, but to finally exist whole. Because your smallness, contrary to what you learned, does not protect those you love. It only erases you. And there is a way of being whole that does not oblige you to lose what you fear losing.

## 2. Whom do I lose if I grow

Beneath your shrinking there is a question, and you almost never ask it out loud, but it governs you: whom do I lose if I grow? Because your fear of being large is not, deep down, a fear of greatness itself. It is a fear of what greatness may cost you in connections, in belonging, in love. You learned, somewhere, that growing pushes people away, that standing out isolates, that being large separates you from those you belong to. And between being large and belonging, you chose to belong, and you made yourself small so as not to lose your people.

In the way I learned to look at families, there is something that explains much of this: loyalty to the origin. We are profoundly loyal to the group we came from, to the family, to the class, to the milieu, and that loyalty leads us, without our knowing it, not to move away too much, not to rise too far above, not to become too different from those we belong to. There is an invisible force that pulls us toward the level of the group, that makes us feel guilty when we surpass it, that whispers to us that growing too much is a form of betrayal, of abandonment, of "thinking ourselves better." And that force, which is born of love and of belonging, is one of the biggest reasons why so many women keep themselves small.

And I want to add a form of loyalty particularly common among women, and particularly invisible: loyalty to the mother. Many women do not allow themselves to be larger, freer, more fulfilled than their mothers, out of a deep loyalty that says, without words, "I cannot have what she did not have," "I cannot be more than she was," "it would be a betrayal to surpass her." If your mother lived a contained life, sacrificed, small by circumstance or by choice, you may feel, without knowing it, that flourishing is leaving her behind, is having her look at your life and see everything she could not have, and that may be unbearable to imagine. And so you shrink, in solidarity with your mother, so as not to surpass her, so as not to hurt her with your flight. But think: what mother, who truly loves, wants her daughter to stay small to keep her company in smallness? What a mother truly wants is for her daughter to fly higher than she could, for her daughter to have what she did not have. In shrinking out of loyalty to your mother, you refuse, without meaning to, her deepest wish: to see you free. To honor her seriously is not to stay small like her. It is to fly for both.

Recognize the messages that transmitted this loyalty to you, because they are in your memory, said or implied. "Don't show off." "Don't get ahead." "Who do you think you are?" "Don't you think too much of yourself?" "Remember where you came from." "There are people who need it more than you." Each of these phrases, heard as a child, taught you that occupying your space, shining, wanting more, being large, was dangerous, frowned upon, a threat to belonging. And you learned the lesson so carefully that you made it your own, and today it is you yourself who says, on the inside, "don't show off," "don't get ahead," whenever you approach your own size.

And there is an important detail: often, no one explicitly told you to stay small. There was no clear prohibition, no order. What there was was more subtle, and therefore harder to see and to undo: it was what you felt in the air, the reactions, the silences, the way someone's shine in the family was received with suspicion, how whoever stood out was talked about behind their back, how humility was praised and ambition viewed with reserve. You learned, without anyone teaching you with words, that in that milieu it was safer not to stand out, and you adjusted, as children adjust, to the emotional climate around you. That is why the loyalty that binds you is so hard to see: it is not a written rule that you can contest, it is a climate you breathed, a way of being that you absorbed before you were aware, and that today you feel as if it were your own nature, when it is, in truth, an adaptation to an environment that is no longer yours.

And there is often a loyalty even more subtle and more difficult: loyalty to those who were left behind, to those who had less, to those who could not. If you grew up in a milieu where most people did not have your opportunities, if there is in your family someone who suffered lack, who could not flourish, then growing can feel like a betrayal of them, like leaving them behind, like having what they did not have. And your shrinking may be, without your knowing it, a form of painful solidarity: staying small so as not to separate yourself from those you love and who could not be large. It is a love, this, but a love that costs you your whole life, because it asks you to erase yourself so as not to shine higher than those you are loyal to.

I want you to see this loyalty without condemning it, because it is born of love and of belonging, which are good things. The problem is not loving your origin nor wanting to belong. The problem is the way you learned to do it: by shrinking, by erasing yourself, by keeping yourself small, as if the only way to belong were not to stand out. And there is another way, that we are going to discover: a way of belonging that does not require your smallness, of honoring your origin without erasing yourself, of growing without betraying. But first you have to see the loyalty that binds you, and recognize that your fear of growing is, deep down, a fear of losing the love and the place. Because it is only when one sees what one fears losing that one can discover that, after all, one does not lose it.

## 3. The shadow is the power, not the smallness

Let us go now to a truth that may surprise you, and that turns inside out the way you see yourself. You think your problem is smallness, insecurity, a lack of confidence. But your true shadow, the thing you most fear and most hide, is not your smallness. It is your power.

Let me explain, because this is fine. There is a concept, in psychology, called the shadow: the parts of us we do not acknowledge, that we hide, that we project, because we learned they were unacceptable. And almost everyone thinks the shadow is made only of what is bad, ugly, small in us. But there is a part of the shadow that almost no one speaks of, and that is, for many women, the most hidden of all: the golden shadow, what is great, powerful, luminous in us, and that we learned to hide because we were taught it was dangerous, arrogant, threatening. For you, it is not the smallness that is in the shadow. It is the greatness.

This golden shadow is, in a way, harder to integrate than the dark shadow, and for a curious reason. It is relatively easy to accept that we have defects, weaknesses, small parts, because that does not oblige us to anything, we can recognize them and continue as we are. But to accept that we have greatness obliges us to something: to occupy it, to live it, to make something of it, and that frightens, because it brings responsibility. To recognize your power is to recognize that you have the responsibility to use it, to occupy it, not to waste it, and that responsibility may seem too heavy. It is more comfortable to say "I am small" and stay still than to say "I am large" and have to do something with it. That is why the golden shadow stays hidden: not only because greatness frightens through exposure, but because it frightens through the responsibility of honoring it. But that responsibility, seen from another angle, is not a weight, it is a calling: the calling to become everything you can be, which is, deep down, the most dignified thing a life can do.

Notice what this means. You are not afraid of not being enough, deep down. You are afraid of being too much. Too capable, too brilliant, too powerful, too large. Your panic is not looking inside and finding nothing, it is looking inside and finding a force that frightens you, a greatness you do not know how to occupy without losing others, a power you learned to fear. And so you hide it, you contain it, you make it small, not because it does not exist, but precisely because it exists and frightens you. Your smallness is not your truth. It is the disguise you put over your truth, which is large.

And there is a deep reason why we hide greatness and not smallness, worth naming. It is that smallness is safe: no one attacks you for being small, no one envies you, no one makes demands of you. Greatness is exposed: it draws looks, expectations, envy, responsibility. To be small is a hiding place, and to be large is to be in plain sight. That is why, paradoxically, it is more comfortable to live in smallness, even if it erases us, than in greatness, even if it fulfills us, because smallness protects and greatness exposes. But there is a hidden price in that safety: the life not lived, the person not become, the waste of everything you could be. And there comes a moment, for many women, when the price of the safety of smallness becomes higher than the risk of the exposure of greatness, when living contained hurts more than risking being seen. It is in that moment that the crossing becomes possible: when the pain of staying small surpasses, at last, the fear of being large.

And there is a reason why your power frightens you so much, and it connects to the previous chapter: because your power threatens your loyalties. If you are as large as you are, you may surpass those you are loyal to, separate yourself from your group, become different, and that activates the fear of losing belonging. Your power and your loyalty are in conflict: to honor the loyalty, you contain the power; to occupy the power, you would have to risk the loyalty. And you, time after time, chose the loyalty, and buried the power in the shadow, where it bothers you like a covered light, wanting to come out, and you holding it back, because letting it shine seems too dangerous.

And I want you to see how this golden shadow manifests, because it betrays itself in curious ways. It manifests in the intense admiration you feel for women who occupy their space without guilt, an admiration that has, underneath, a mix of envy and longing, because you see in them what you do not allow yourself to be. It manifests in the disproportionate irritation you feel toward people you find arrogant, because they do, without guilt, the thing you forbid yourself, and that stirs you. It manifests in the dreams, in the fantasies, in the moments when you imagine being large, shining, occupying, and then you reprimand yourself for desiring it. All of this are signs of the greatness that lives in your shadow, trying to come out, making itself noticed through the cracks. What you project onto others, the greatness you admire or the arrogance you condemn, is often your own unlived greatness, returned to the world because you do not let it exist in you. To recognize it in others is the first step to recognizing it, at last, in yourself.

I want to tell you something about that greatness you hide: it is not arrogance, nor is it a threat, nor is it "thinking yourself better." There is an enormous difference between occupying your real size and being arrogant. Arrogance is pretending to be larger than one is, at the expense of others. To occupy your size is to be exactly what you are, no more and no less, and to let that exist in the world. You confuse the two things because you were taught to confuse them, to keep you small: they made you believe that being large is being arrogant, so that, afraid of being arrogant, you would keep yourself small. But it is not true. You can be fully large and profoundly humble, occupy your full size and continue to respect and love others. True greatness diminishes no one. Only the false kind does. And yours, which is real, is waiting to be able, at last, to exist.

## 4. The fear of impact

I am going to drop a question here, on its own, and I ask you to stay with it, because it touches the heart of your fear.

What happens when you occupy your space, and the world reacts?

Stay with it a moment.

Because your fear of being large is not abstract. It has a memory, and it has, often, a first time. There was, somewhere, a first time when you occupied your space with naturalness, as children do before they learn to shrink, and when that naturalness was punished, ridiculed, or received with coldness. The little girl who answered too well and was called a show-off. The child who shone and felt the jealousy around her. The young woman who stood out and was put in her place. Those first times taught you, with the force of childhood experiences, that being large hurts, and your system, to protect you, made a decision: never again. Never again shine so much, never again occupy so much, never again risk that pain. And that decision, made by a small version of you to protect you, governs you still today, long after you stopped being that child and the world stopped being that world. To recognize that your fear comes from there, and not from the present, is to begin to free it: because you are no longer the child who was punished for shining, and the world of today is not the one of then, and you can, at last, revise a decision that was made when you had no other way to defend yourself.

And that is why you contain your impact. At some point, perhaps many, you occupied your space, you shone, you stood out, you were large, and the world reacted, and the reaction hurt. Perhaps you shone and someone cut you down, diminished you, put you in your place. Perhaps you succeeded and felt the distancing of people who before were close. Perhaps you occupied your space and were called arrogant, conceited, "full of yourself." Perhaps you were large and felt, instead of applause, the envy, the resentment, the subtle punishment of those who could not bear your greatness. And you learned, from those experiences, that being large has a price, and that the price is high: the reaction of others, the impact you cause, the way your light bothers.

And that is why you contain your impact. It is not only that you are afraid of being large, it is that you are afraid of the effect of your greatness on others. You are afraid of bothering, of threatening, of provoking envy, of making someone feel smaller, of causing reactions you will not know how to manage. And so you diminish yourself preemptively, you shrink before causing impact, you shine with the dimmer always low, so that no one feels dazzled, so that there is no reaction, so that you are safe. Your shrinking is, in part, a way of controlling the impact you cause, of protecting yourself from the reactions your greatness might provoke.

I want you to see what this costs you, because the price is enormous. By containing your impact to protect others and yourself from the reactions, you are preventing yourself from living your life whole, from giving everything you have, from becoming who you are. You are mortgaging your greatness to the comfort of others, making yourself less so that no one feels threatened, and that is not generosity, it is a waste, the waste of a life that could be full and that stays contained out of fear of others' reaction. And the most painful thing is that often it does not even work: the people who were going to envy you or distance themselves do it anyway, and you stayed small for nothing, you lost your greatness without even gaining the safety you were looking for.

And I want to name a specific reaction that may have hurt you more than the others, because it is the most confusing: the envy of those who love you. Sometimes, those who diminish us are not the strangers, they are the close people, the family, the friends, who, without wanting to, cannot bear our shine, because it shows them what they did not become, or leaves them behind. And it is very hard to grow when we feel that our growth hurts, or pushes away, or provokes envy in those we love. But I want to tell you something about this: someone's envy or discomfort in the face of your shine is a wound of that person, not a crime of yours. You can be careful, you can be humble, you can love, and even so your growth may stir the wounds of those around you, and that is no reason to erase yourself. To erase yourself so as not to stir others' wounds is to condemn yourself to a small life for the comfort of others, and that, in the end, neither heals their wounds nor honors your life. Each one is responsible for tending to their own wounds, and your task is not to keep yourself small so that no one has to look at theirs.

And I want to tell you a difficult truth about impact: you are not responsible for others' reaction to your greatness. If someone feels threatened by your shine, that is about that person, about their insecurities, and it is not your task to erase yourself to protect them. You can be large with care, with humility, with respect, and even so there will be those who react badly, because their reaction does not depend on you, it depends on them. To shrink yourself in order to avoid every negative reaction is an impossible task, and one that costs you your whole life. At a certain point, occupying your size means accepting that your greatness will cause some impact, that not everyone will receive it well, and that this is no reason to erase yourself, because the alternative, erasing yourself so that no one reacts, is to erase yourself forever.

## 5. To grow is not to betray

We have arrived at the fear at the heart of everything, and it is important to name it and undo it with care: the fear that to grow is to betray. To betray the origin, the family, the group, the people you are loyal to and who could not be large. This fear is the root of your shrinking, and until it comes undone, you will continue to keep yourself small out of love, thinking that your smallness is a form of loyalty.

So I want to tell you, clearly: to grow is not to betray. To occupy your size abandons no one. These are beliefs, not truths, and beliefs you learned in a context where they perhaps made some sense, but that cost you your whole life. The idea that, in growing, you abandon your origin, rests on a confusion: that of believing that belonging requires being the same. But belonging does not require sameness. You can belong profoundly to a group, to a family, and be very different from them, grow far beyond them, and continue to belong, to love, to be part. True belonging is not lost by your being large. What is lost, sometimes, is a certain form of belonging, the one that was based on you being small like the others, but that form was not love, it was leveling, and there is a greater belonging, based on love and not on sameness, that survives your growth.

And there is a truth that completely turns your logic: your smallness does not help those you love. You keep yourself small, in part, out of solidarity with those who had less, with those who could not, as if by not shining you were keeping them company, not leaving them behind. But think: what does your smallness do, concretely, for them? It does not make them larger. It does not give them the opportunities they did not have. It does not help them at all. It only erases you, and adds to the world one more diminished person, without taking anything from their pain. Your smallness is not a gift for those you love, it is a double loss: you lose your greatness, and they gain nothing. To shrink yourself in solidarity is a solidarity that relieves no one and that costs you everything.

And I want you to think about what you teach those who come after you, because this can give you the courage you lack. If there are, in your life, younger people who watch you, daughters, nieces, students, women who take you as a reference, notice what your smallness teaches them: it teaches them that a woman should shrink, that growing is dangerous, that her place is not to occupy too much space. And notice what your greatness would teach them: that it is possible to occupy your own size, that a woman can be large without apologizing, that growing is legitimate and dignified. By occupying your place, you are not only freeing yourself, you are giving permission to those who come after, opening a path they will be able to follow, interrupting a transmission of smallness that passes from generation to generation of women. Sometimes, the courage we do not have for ourselves we find for them: grow, if not for yourself, then for all the women who will learn, in seeing you large, that they too can be.

And there is more, and it is the luminous reverse of this: your greatness can, that one, help those you love, in ways your smallness never could. A woman who occupies her size, who grows, who becomes what she can be, has more to give, more to share, more capacity to lift others up. She can open paths, she can be an example, she can use her greatness in the service of those she loves, she can show those who come after that it is possible. Your smallness gives nothing to anyone; your greatness can give much. So, to grow, far from being a betrayal of those you love, can be the greatest way of honoring them and of serving them: by becoming everything you can be, and then putting that in the service of love.

And I want to tell you something about what happens, often, when a woman finally occupies her size: most of her fears do not come true. The fear said that you were going to lose everyone, that you were going to end up alone, that you would be rejected, punished, abandoned. And what happens, in most cases, is much less dramatic and much more liberating: some people, yes, distance themselves, precisely those who only wanted you small, and that, although it hurts, reveals itself to be a necessary clearing. But most stay, and many even come closer, because a whole woman is more interesting, more inspiring, more real than a contained woman. And you discover that the world your fear painted, full of punishment and abandonment waiting for your shine, was in large part a fantasy, inherited from old experiences, and that the real world, when you occupy your place, receives you much better than you ever dared to expect.

I want to leave you with a reframing that changes everything, and that comes from the systemic reading: to truly honor your origin is not to stay small like it, it is to grow from it. Those who came before you, especially those who had less, those who could not, gave you a base precisely so that you would go where they did not go. When you keep yourself small out of loyalty, it is as if their effort stopped in you. When you grow, you take what they gave you farther than they could, and that, that growing from the roots, is the highest way of honoring them. Your greatness does not betray your origin. It realizes it. It is the fruit the roots worked for.

## 6. The dignity of existing whole

There is a word I want to bring now, because it is what is, deep down, at stake, beyond the strategies and the fears: dignity. The dignity of existing whole, of occupying the place that life gave you, of being everything you are, without erasing yourself, without apologizing, without asking permission to exist in your size.

Because there is something that your shrinking takes from you, and that is more than opportunities or success: it takes from you the dignity of existing fully. Each time you diminish yourself, that you apologize for what you are, that you hide your greatness, that you occupy less space than you have a right to, you are, without feeling it that way, giving up a dignity that is yours by being born: the dignity of being a whole person, with your full size, in the world. And that giving up, repeated your whole life, wears you out in a deep way, because a part of you knows that it is living below what it is, contained, erased, and that hurts, even when you disguise yourself as modest and simple.

There is a sentence, from the way I learned to look at family systems, that helps here: each person has the right to a full place, and occupying our place takes the place from no one. In a family, in a group, in a healthy world, there is room for everyone whole, the greatness of one does not require the smallness of another. The idea that you have to shrink so others can fit is a distortion, because the space of dignity is not a limited cake in which your slice takes from others'. When you occupy your full place, you are not stealing anyone's place, you are only ceasing to leave yours empty, and a place of yours occupied closes no place of anyone else. On the contrary: when each one occupies their full place, the whole system becomes healthier, more alive, more whole, because it is made of whole people, and not of people who shrink for one another in a general diminishment that serves no one.

To occupy your size is, above all, an act of dignity, and not of ambition. It is not about wanting more out of vanity, about wanting to shine out of ego. It is about recognizing that you have the right, the dignity, to exist whole, to be everything you are, to occupy the space your greatness needs, just as any person has the right to exist in their size. You are not asking for anything special, anything excessive. You are only reclaiming the basic, and dignified, right not to erase yourself, to be, in the world, exactly what you are. And that is not arrogance. It is the simplest and most profound form of respect for yourself, and for the life you were given.

And there is a way to distinguish, in practice, occupying your size from being arrogant, because I know that confusion frightens you and holds you back. Arrogance needs to diminish others to feel large, it measures itself by comparison, it occupies others' space. Occupying your size takes space from no one, because the space of greatness is not limited: your light does not put out others', on the contrary, it gives them permission to shine too. The arrogant person leaves others smaller around them; the person who occupies their size with dignity leaves others more inspired, more authorized to be themselves too. So the test is simple: your greatness, when you occupy it, does it diminish others or free them? If you occupy it with dignity and without needing to step on anyone, it frees, and there is no arrogance in it at all, there is only a whole woman existing, and giving, with her example, permission for others to be whole too.

And I want you to see that existing whole is also a form of respect for life itself. Life gave you a certain size, certain capacities, a certain greatness, and it did so for some reason, or at least not so that you would bury them. When you occupy your size, you are honoring what life gave you, making the gifts you received bear fruit, not wasting the person you could be. When you keep yourself small, you are, with all good intentions, refusing what life offered you, leaving unused what was given to you. To occupy your place is, therefore, an act of gratitude to life: to receive fully what it gave you, and to let it exist in the world, instead of hiding it in the shadow out of fear of others' reaction.

I am not going to tell you that occupying your size is easy, nor that it is done overnight, because to unlearn a whole life of shrinking, and to undo old loyalties, is deep work, done slowly, and often with someone to accompany you, because it touches your deepest bonds and your oldest fears. But I want to tell you that you have the right to do it, that you deserve to exist whole, and that the dignity of occupying your place is not a luxury nor a boldness, it is a right of yours, as much yours as the right to breathe. And that the world, contrary to what your fear says, does not need you small. It needs you whole.

## 7. Occupying the place that life gave

If your shadow is the power, and if to grow is not to betray, then the verb of this crossing is clear, however much it costs: to occupy. To occupy the place that life gave you. To inhabit your own size, without guilt, without apology, without asking permission. I am not going to give you a mold to fill in, because occupying is not done by recipe; I am going to point you toward the direction in which your size lies, and let it be you who grows toward it, at your own pace.

Begin by noticing the small gestures with which you diminish yourself, because that is where it begins. The times you say "it was nothing" when it was a great deal. The times you apologize for occupying space, for speaking, for existing. The times you hide an achievement, a capacity, an opinion, so as not to seem too much. The times you occupy less than you have a right to, in a conversation, in a room, in a relationship. Each of these gestures is a small shrinking, and to occupy your size begins by noticing them, and by beginning, slowly, to do the opposite: to accept a compliment without minimizing it, to let an achievement be recognized, to occupy your space in a conversation, to exist a little more. Not all at once, but in small acts of full presence, that gradually teach your system that occupying your place is not dangerous.

Then, learn to tolerate the discomfort of being seen, because it is going to appear. For someone who got used to shrinking, being seen, occupying space, letting greatness appear, brings, at first, an acute discomfort, almost a panic, the feeling of being exposed, at risk, waiting for the reaction. That is normal, it is your old fear firing. The path is not to eliminate that discomfort before occupying your space, it is to occupy your space despite the discomfort, and to keep discovering, slowly, that the world does not collapse, that not everyone punishes you, that you survive being large. Each time you occupy your place and bear the discomfort without shrinking again, the fear diminishes a little, and your size becomes a little more inhabitable.

Then, learn to receive, because occupying your size passes very much through here. Whoever makes herself small usually has difficulty receiving: compliments, recognition, opportunities, help, love. She always receives with a discount, with an "I don't deserve it," with the hurry to divert the attention. And to learn to receive, to let in what is given to you without minimizing it, is a concrete way of occupying your place, because to receive fully is to recognize that you have the right to what you receive, that you deserve it, that you fit. Next time someone gives you a compliment, instead of returning it with an "oh, it was nothing," try simply to receive it, to say thank you, to let it in. Next time someone offers you an opportunity at your level, instead of refusing it with an "I'm not ready yet," try to accept it, and to grow into it. To receive is the intimate gesture of occupying your size: it is to let in everything that confirms you are large, instead of refusing it at the door because you think you have no right.

And there is a deeper work, which is to make peace with the loyalties that bind you. To recognize that you keep yourself small out of love for those who had less, out of fear of betraying your origin, and to be able, on the inside, to free yourself from that misunderstood loyalty without ceasing to love. To say, internally, to those who were left behind: I love you, I belong to you, and I am going to grow, not against you, but from you, carrying you with me in what I become. I do not need to stay small to honor you. I honor you by being large, and by putting my greatness, too, in your service. This inner gesture, of freeing yourself from loyalty to smallness without losing the love, is perhaps the most important of all, because it is loyalty, more than fear, that has kept you contained.

And I want to give you a gentle way to begin, so that it is not too frightening. You do not need to occupy your full size all at once, in a dramatic gesture that exposes you whole. You can grow one centimeter at a time, in low-risk situations, where the fear is bearable. An opinion spoken whole in a conversation with someone who loves you. A compliment received without a discount. A capacity shown in a safe context. A small space occupied where before you shrank. Each of these small acts of occupying is a training, and the small ones prepare the large ones. As you occupy, safely, small spaces, and discover that you survive, that the world bears you, that the feared reactions do not come or do not destroy you, your system gradually learns that occupying your place is safe, and the fear gradually diminishes, and your size becomes, centimeter by centimeter, more inhabitable. You do not need courage for one great gesture. You need courage for many small ones, and that, you have, or you can train it.

I do not promise you that this will be simple, because occupying your size stirs your oldest fears and your deepest bonds. But I promise you that on the other side there is a life lived whole, and a dignity that comes only from existing in your real size, and a peace that smallness could never give you, because smallness, however safe it seems, always costs you your soul. And I promise you that occupying your place will not isolate you as you fear. It will, yes, perhaps push away those who only wanted you small, but it will bring you closer to those who want you whole, and it will show you that true love does not need you to erase yourself, and that there is a place for you, in your real size, that was always waiting for you, and that only you can, at last, occupy.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It does not change everything at once, and it does not undo in a day the habit of a whole life of shrinking. But it changes enough so you can stop thinking yourself small and begin to see the greatness you have been hiding, and the fear that makes you hide it.

And I want to lay in your hands the truth that lies beneath all this shrinking. To occupy your size is not, deep down, about success, nor about career, nor about ambition. It is about something simpler and more profound: the right to exist whole. Every person is born with the right to be everything they are, to occupy the place that life gave them, not to erase themselves. And throughout life, for a thousand reasons, many women give up that right, they shrink, they make themselves smaller, they live below what they are. To recover that right is not greed, it is justice, justice toward yourself, the justice of letting yourself, at last, exist whole. And when you do it, you discover that occupying your size does not distance you from life nor from others, it brings you closer to both, because a whole person lives more fully, loves more fully, gives more fully. Your whole life has been waiting for you to allow yourself to occupy it. And to allow yourself that is the gift that this book, deep down, wants to give you.

Now you know that you make yourself small out of habit, and that this is not a lack of size, it is a fear of size. You know that beneath the shrinking is the question "whom do I lose if I grow," and the loyalty that orders you to stay small. You know that your true shadow is the power, not the smallness. You know that you contain your impact out of fear of others' reaction, and that you are not responsible for that reaction. You know that to grow is not to betray, and that your smallness does not help those you love, it only erases you. And you know that to occupy your size is, above all, an act of dignity.

And you know, perhaps, that the world needs your greatness, and not your smallness. We live surrounded by women who shrink, who diminish themselves, who do not occupy their place, and the world is poorer for it, because it loses everything they would have to give if they allowed themselves to be whole. Each woman who occupies her size takes nothing from anyone, she adds: she adds her light, her talent, her contribution, and gives permission to others to do the same. Your smallness does not make the world better, it helps no one, it only subtracts from the world the person you could be. Your greatness, occupied with dignity, is a gift, for you and for everyone. It is not out of selfishness that one grows. It is, deep down, out of generosity: the generosity of giving the world everything you are, instead of depriving it of that out of a fear that no longer serves you.

And you know that occupying your size is not an isolated act, it is a new way of being in the world, that is built day by day. There is no moment when you grow all at once and stay large forever. There are a thousand small moments when you choose not to shrink, when you occupy your space, when you let your light appear, and each of them, repeated, gradually makes your size more inhabitable and the world more used to you whole. Begin with the next one. In the next moment when you catch yourself shrinking, occupy a little more. It is in the sum of those small acts of presence that a contained life transforms, slowly, into a life lived whole.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can occupy the place that life gave me.

Seeing your fear of your own size was the first step. But there is a second, and that one is not read, it is done: it is done by occupying, slowly, gesture by gesture, the space you have been avoiding, and by discovering that the world bears you whole.

It was for that crossing that I created **Infonte**.

Infonte is not a place where you learn to be larger than you are, nor to walk over anyone. It is, precisely, a space for you to stop being smaller than you are, to free yourself from the loyalties that keep you small, and to occupy, with dignity and without guilt, the place that life gave you. Not so you abandon your origin, but so you honor it by growing from it, and by putting your greatness, at last, in the service of those you love and of the world.

There, the question "whom do I lose if I grow" ceases to be a threat that shrinks you and becomes a door that opens. Your power ceases to be a shadow you hide and begins to be a light you occupy, without guilt. And to grow ceases to look like betrayal and reveals itself as what it is: the highest way of honoring the life you received and of letting it bear all the fruit it has to give.

If you feel this is the moment, I am there. At [infonte.viviannedossantos.com](https://infonte.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it is not yet the moment, that is okay, and no one hurries you to grow before you are ready. What you have read stays with you, serene, until the day you decide that you have made yourself small enough, and that it is time, at last, to occupy your place.

And I want to leave you with an image, so that it stays growing with you after you close the book. Think of a plant that grew inside a pot too small, and that wound its roots around itself to fit, and that stopped growing, not because it could not, but because the pot did not let it. The plant is not sick, it does not lack strength, it lacks space, and the space it lacks is not the world's, it is the pot's that they put it in, and that it no longer needs. You are that plant. Your size is not small, it was contained by a pot, the loyalties, the fears, the old messages, that no longer correspond to the soil you live in. To occupy your size is to be transplanted into a larger pot, or into the open ground, and to discover that what seemed to be your dimension, that of the small pot, was only the limit they put on you, and that, given the space, you grow far beyond what they ever let you imagine. The greatness was there, wound up, waiting for space. And the space, now, you can give yourself.

And before you shrink yourself again to close the book, I ask you for a single gesture. Today, at some moment, occupy a little more space than you usually do. Say your opinion without softening it. Accept a compliment with a simple thank you, without minimizing it. Stay in a room without shrinking. Let a thing you did well be seen. And notice what appears: the discomfort, the fear of the reaction, the urge to shrink again. And do not go back. Stay, in your size, one more moment. Because it is in that occupying, borne without shrinking, that the habit of a whole life begins to undo itself, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that you have the right to exist whole, and that the world, after all, bears you large.

With all my tenderness,
*Vivianne*
