# The mother who fears being a burden

**For the mother who spent her life never being a burden, and who now, growing old, hides what she needs so as not to get in her children's way.**

*By Vivianne dos Santos*
*Writer, mother of three, in advanced training in Transpersonal Psychology, Psychology and Spirituality, and Systemic Family Constellation Therapy.*

---

*This ebook is a resource for self-awareness and understanding, and it does not replace therapeutic support. This is a book about a crossing that closes an arc: that of the mother who spent her life caring and never being a burden, and who now, growing old, is afraid of becoming a burden, and so hides what she needs. I want to tell you, before we begin, that loneliness and sadness in aging are real, common, and very often unrecognized. If there is a sadness that doesn't pass, an isolation that closes you in, a loss of meaning that drags on, that is not only age, it may be something that deserves the care of a professional, and to seek it is not weakness, it is care. And allowing yourself to receive includes asking for the health support you in fact need.*

---

## 1. I don't want to get in the way

There is a sentence you say often, and that perhaps you no longer even hear yourself saying, so automatic has it become: don't worry about me, I'm fine. You say it when you're not fine. You say it when you need and don't ask. You say it when the pain tightens, when the loneliness weighs, when the body no longer responds as it responded, and even so, to your children's question, you answer that everything's fine, that you need nothing, that they shouldn't worry, that you'll handle it.

Because the thing you most fear, now that the years pass, is not the pain, is not the illness, is not even the loneliness. It is getting in the way. It is being a burden. It is becoming a burden to your children, who have their own lives, their own jobs, their own children, their own difficulties. The idea of being one more worry on their already full agenda is, to you, almost unbearable. And so you hide. You hide the pain so they won't be distressed. You hide the loneliness so as not to make them feel guilty. You hide what you need, and you solve alone what you can still solve, and you pretend to solve what you can no longer solve, all so as not to be a burden.

Perhaps you recognize this in small, daily things. It is saying you ate when you have neither hunger nor the will to cook for yourself alone. It is not telling them about a fall, a dizziness, a pain, so that no one will be alarmed. It is refusing a lift, help, company, with an "it's not necessary, I'll manage," even when you needed it. It is spending the night awake with a distress and, in the morning, on the phone, saying that you slept well. It is seeing the days go by ever emptier and keeping quiet about the loneliness, because you think your loneliness has no right to disturb the full life of your children. Each of these small silences seems a gesture of love and of independence, and in part it is. But, added together, they leave you ever more alone with what you carry, and they deprive those who love you of knowing how you really are.

And there is a nobility in this, and I want to recognize it before anything. To hide one's own need so as not to overburden those one loves is a gesture of love, it is the continuation of a whole life of putting others ahead of yourself. It is not empty pride, it is not foolish stubbornness, it is love, the same love that made you care for them their whole lives, now turned toward protecting them even from the weight of caring for you. I want you to know that I see that love, and that I honor it. This book has not come to tell you that you are wrong to want not to be a burden. It has come to show you another thing, that perhaps you never considered, and that can change the way you live this phase.

And I want to name a very particular loneliness of this phase, because it usually hides behind the not wanting to be a burden. As one grows old, the world shrinks, sometimes: friends who pass away, a companion who is missing, children who live far away and busy, a body that goes out less. And in that shrinking, the loneliness tightens, and you, who never wanted to be a burden, keep quiet about that loneliness too, you don't say that the days are long, that the house is silent, that you miss company, that you would like to be visited more. You keep quiet, because to ask for company seems like asking too much, seems like a burden, seems like dependence. But the need for company, for contact, for not being alone, is not a whim nor a weakness, it is a fundamental human need, at any age. To say that you feel alone, to ask for a visit, to seek company, is not to be a burden. It is to take care of a need as legitimate as the need to eat or to sleep, and that, ignored, does as much harm to your health as any illness.

Because there is a hidden problem in this care of yours not to be a burden, and it is this: in hiding what you need, in refusing to receive, you may be, without meaning to, depriving your children of something they too need. The possibility of caring for you, of giving back to you, of loving you in practice, of closing a cycle that you opened when you cared for them. To not be a burden is not only to protect your children. It can be, without your noticing, to close to them a door to a gesture of love they need to be able to make. And it is of that, of that surprising truth, that we are going to speak throughout these pages.

This book closes an arc, and I want you to know that, because it gives meaning to everything. Your whole life you were the one who carries, the one who gives, the one who cares, the one who sustains. And now, at the end of a long crossing, life turns the question around and asks you the hardest thing of all for someone who spent her life giving: to receive. It is the final inversion, the last threshold. From the love that carries to the love that lets itself be received. And perhaps it is this, of all the crossings, the hardest, because it goes against everything you were, but it is also, for that very reason, the most liberating, because on the other side of it is a truth that frees you of a weight of a whole life: the truth that it was never necessary not to be a burden in order to be loved, the truth that the love you gave can, at last, come back to you, and that you deserve to receive it, simply for being who you are.

But it begins with this, with a question that may bother you and that is worth letting in: and what if not being a burden, after all, were not the greatest gift you can give them? And what if there were, in this phase of life, a love greater than the love of going on not needing anyone, and that love were, precisely, the love of letting yourself, at last, be cared for? Don't answer yet. Just let the question stay. Because it opens the door to one of the most important crossings, and least spoken of, of a whole life: the crossing of learning to receive.

## 2. A whole life never being a burden

To understand your fear of being a burden, we have to see where it comes from, because it wasn't born now, with age. It comes from far away, from a whole life, and perhaps from before your life, from a line of women who all learned the same lesson: a good woman, a good mother, is not a burden, doesn't need, doesn't ask. She gives.

Think of the women of your family, of your mother, of your grandmother. How many of them ever asked for help except under duress? How many allowed themselves to need, to rest, to be cared for? Or did they learn, as you did, that their place was to give, to serve, to sustain, and that to need was a weakness, almost a shame? You grew up seeing this, you absorbed it without words, and you became, you too, a woman who gives and doesn't ask, who cares and is not cared for, who is always available and never needs. That is how you learned to love, and that is how you knew who you were: the one who gives, the one who sustains, the one who is never a burden to anyone.

Perhaps you remember moments when you needed and didn't ask, over a whole life. Illnesses you hid, tirednesses you swallowed, sadnesses you didn't share, helps you refused. And perhaps you remember feeling proud of that, of "not having been any trouble to anyone," of having endured alone. That pride was real, and there is in it a true strength. But I want you to look also at its reverse, with honesty: how many times did that enduring alone leave you more alone than you needed to be? How many times would someone have held you up gladly, and didn't know how, because you hid it? The pride of not being a burden has a price, and the price is a chosen loneliness, a kept distance, a refused love. To recognize this is not to renounce your strength. It is to see that it had a cost, and to ask whether, from here on, it is worth going on paying it.

And for decades, this way of being served you, and served those you loved. You were the pillar, the one who held everything together, the one who was always there. Others could count on you, and you never charged, never asked in return, never were a burden. There was an enormous strength in this, and an enormous generosity, and many people were loved and sustained by that capacity of yours to give without asking. I am not saying it was a mistake. I am saying that it became, over so many years, an identity: you came to be the one who is not a burden, and that came to be your way of being worth something, of belonging, of being loved.

And here is the knot that age comes to tighten. While you were able to give without needing, your identity stayed intact: you were the one who gives, and everything was fine. But the body changes, the strength changes, life changes, and a moment comes when you begin, naturally, to need. And to need, for you, is not only a practical inconvenience. It is a threat to your whole identity, to the way you knew how to be worth something and to belong your whole life. If you were the one who never was a burden, who are you left being when you begin to need? This is the deep wound of this book, and it is not about old age nor about dependence. It is about an identity built on not being a burden, that life, now, obliges you to revise.

And there is a cost in this whole life never being a burden that perhaps you never accounted for: the loneliness of never having let yourself be known entirely. When a person only gives and never receives, never asks, never shows fragility, she ends up keeping herself, without meaning to, at a certain distance from others, even from those she loves. Because true intimacy is made in both directions, in giving and in receiving, in caring and in letting oneself be cared for, in showing the strength and also the fragility. By showing only the strong part, only the part that gives, you perhaps stopped being entirely known, and there is a particular loneliness in that, the loneliness of being loved for your strength without ever having let anyone love also your fragility. To allow yourself, now, to be seen also in what you need, is not only to receive practical help. It is to let yourself, at last, be known and loved entirely, and that is an intimacy that perhaps was missing your whole life.

I want you to see this with tenderness toward yourself, and not as one more failure. There is nothing wrong with your having learned not to be a burden, it was what they taught you, it was how you knew how to love. But perhaps the time has come to question, gently, a lesson that no longer serves you, and that perhaps never was entirely true. The lesson that your worth depends on never needing. The lesson that to love is only to give, never to receive. The lesson that being a burden, needing, asking, make you less worthy of love. Those lessons, inherited from a line of women who erased themselves, can, in you, finally, begin to change.

## 3. The order changes with life

I want to bring you now a way of seeing that comes from my training in family constellation, and that can illuminate all of this: the order of giving and receiving, and how it changes over the course of life.

There is an image that helps to feel this: the image of love between the generations as a river that runs, always, from upstream to downstream, from those who come before to those who come after. The parents give to the children, who give to their children, and the river goes on, generation after generation. But within a life, there is also a smaller movement, of giving back, in which the child, already an adult, holds up the parent who grows old, not to reverse the river, but to care for it in its old age, before turning, himself, toward his own children. To refuse this holding is like putting a dam in the river, it is to interrupt a flow that wants, naturally, to happen. The care that your children would want to give you is not an anomaly nor a weight out of time, it is part of the natural course of the river, and to let it happen is to let love follow its path, instead of damming it in the name of an independence that no longer serves the phase you're in.

Between parents and children, there is a natural order in the flow of love and of care. When the children are small, the parents give, and the children receive. That is how it has to be, it is healthy, it is the right order for that phase. You gave, for decades, and your children received, and that was good and was as it should be. A father or a mother who demanded of small children that they care for them would be reversing the order in a harmful way. In the phase of raising, the care descends, from parents to children, and that direction is the correct one.

But life has phases, and the order, although it keeps a base, goes on changing with them. The body ages, the strength diminishes, and there comes, in the natural order of things, a time when the children, now adults, begin to be able to give back some of the care they received. Not to reverse everything, not to become the parents themselves, but to balance, to give back, to care for the one who cared. This is not a failure of the order, it is the order itself being fulfilled over the course of a whole life: one gives to the children when they are small, and one receives from them some care when one is old, and between the two things love circulates, over the generations, as it always circulated.

I want you to see the beauty of this circulation, because it is one of the most beautiful things there is. The love you gave to your children is not lost, is not exhausted, doesn't end when you stop being able to give it as you gave it. It goes on, and part of it comes back, now, in the form of the care they can give you. It is as if love made a great circle over the course of a life: it descends from you to them when they are small, and rises from them to you when you grow old, and in that complete circle there is a justice and a beauty profound. To refuse the part that rises is to leave the circle unclosed, it is to be left with the feeling that you only gave and never received, when life offers you, precisely now, the opportunity to feel love come back. To let love complete its circle, to receive back a little of the much you gave, is not selfishness nor weakness. It is to let your love story with your children have, at last, both halves, the half of giving and the half of receiving, and be whole.

And here is the key, the most important tuning of this book: what breaks this circulation of love is not your needing. To need is natural, it is part of life, it is what happens to all human beings who live long enough to grow old. What breaks the circulation is the refusal to receive. When you refuse to receive the care that your children, in this phase, could and would like to give you, you are not respecting the order, you are interrupting it. You are keeping love from completing its cycle, from rising again after having descended. The need is not the problem. The refusal to let it be met is what stops the wheel of love.

And I want to add something important, so as not to blame your children in the process. It may happen that your children, in fact, don't see what you need, that they seem distracted, busy, absent. But notice: very often, they don't see precisely because you hide. You spent your life teaching them that you need nothing, that you're always fine, that you solve everything, and they learned that lesson, and so they don't look, don't ask in earnest, don't insist, because you always assured them it wasn't necessary. Don't blame them for not seeing what you tried so hard to hide. The focus of this book is not for your children to learn to see, it is for you to allow yourself to be seen, to show what you need, to open the door. When you do it, many children respond, and respond relieved, because deep down they would like to be able to care and didn't know how, without offending you in your fierce independence.

Notice how this completely reverses the way you've been seeing things. You think that, by not being a burden, by refusing to receive, you are respecting your children, not overburdening them, keeping the right order. But it is the opposite. By refusing to receive, you are keeping yourself rigidly in the place of the one who only gives, even when life already asks for something else, and so you keep the order from being fulfilled, the care from circulating, your children from occupying their place of the ones who now can also care. To allow yourself to receive, in this phase, is not to betray your place. It is to let life, and love, follow their natural course, which includes, yes, the mother who one day, with dignity, lets herself be cared for.

## 4. To receive is also to love

We arrive at the heart of this book, and at the realization that changes everything. I'm going to say it slowly, and I ask you to let it truly enter, because it contradicts a whole life of belief.

To receive is also to love. And to refuse to receive, however much it seems like love, may be, without your wanting it, a way of depriving those who love you.

Stay with this a moment.

You believe that, by not being a burden, you protect your children. And in part it is true, there is a real care in not overburdening them. But there is another truth, hidden, that perhaps you never saw: by always refusing their care, by insisting that you need nothing, by not letting them do for you, you are refusing them something. You are refusing them the possibility of loving you in practice, of giving back to you, of caring for the one who cared for them. And that possibility is not a weight for them. It is, for many children, a deep need: the need to be able to give back, to close a cycle, to express, in concrete gestures, the love and the gratitude they feel.

There are children who carry, a whole life, a kind of debt of love toward the mother, the feeling that they received so much and never could give back in equal measure. For those children, and they are many, to be able to care for the mother who grows old is not a burden, it is a relief, a long-desired opportunity to balance the scales, to do for her something of the much she did for them, to say with gestures the thank you they perhaps never knew how to say with words. When you refuse that care, you don't take a weight from them, you take from them that possibility of settling, in love, a debt they feel. And to let them care for you can be, for them, one of the most important and most healing experiences of their relationship with you, the experience of being able to, at last, give back to the one who gave them so much. Don't deprive them of that out of pride or out of fear of being a burden. That care they would give you can be as precious to them as, for you, was all the care you gave them.

Think of what you would feel, if one day you wanted very much to care for someone you love, to give back everything that person gave you, and she always refused, insisted that she doesn't need you, closed the door to each gesture of yours of care. You would feel, perhaps, a sadness, a frustration, a sense of being kept outside, of not being allowed to love. Well, that is what your children may feel when you always refuse to receive. Not relief at your not being a burden. Sadness at your not letting them care for you, at your denying them the opportunity to love you the way you loved them. Your refusal, which means to protect them, may be depriving them of a gesture of love they need.

Think of a relationship you know and that you admire, in which two people care for each other over time. Notice that what makes it deep is not one of them always giving and the other always receiving. It is the exchange, the coming and going of the care, now one needs and the other holds, now it reverses. It is that reciprocity, that flow in both directions, that creates the intimacy and the depth. A relationship in which one person only gives and the other only receives is not the most loving, it is the most unbalanced, and it ends up weighing, paradoxically, more on the one who only receives, who stays in a position of permanent debt. By always refusing to receive from your children, you keep the relationship in that imbalance, in which you only give and they only owe. To allow yourself to receive is, after all, to balance the relationship, to make it more reciprocal, more mature, more between adults who care for one another, and less between a mother who sacrifices herself eternally and children eternally in debt.

And there is more: by letting yourself be cared for, you give your children something precious, that goes far beyond that moment. You teach them that to receive is not a shame, that to need is not a weakness, that one can be loved even when one is no longer useful. You show them, by your example, a more whole way of living and of growing old, that they will be able to follow when their turn comes. If you spend your life and the end of your life refusing to receive, you teach them that that is how one ought to be, and they will repeat with you, and with their children, the same refusal, the same loneliness. But if you allow yourself to receive, with dignity, you break that inheritance, and you give them permission to, one day, also let themselves be cared for.

There is a way of receiving that perhaps you never considered as such, and that changes the perspective: to let them love you is, in itself, a gift you give. When you accept the care of a child, you are not only taking, you are giving him the joy of caring for you, the sense of usefulness and of giving back, the relief of being able at last to do something for the one who did so much, the peace of not seeing you alone. Look at how you yourself feel when you manage to help someone you love, the joy it gives you, the sense of purpose. Why would you deny that same joy to your children? By receiving, you give them the possibility of feeling what you feel when you give. To receive, seen this way, is not the opposite of giving, it is another way of giving: you give those who love you the opportunity to love you back. And few gifts are as great as that one.

I want to be careful about one thing: this is not an obligation, it is a permission. I am not telling you "you have to let your children care for you," as if it were one more duty to fulfill. I am telling you that you can, that you have the right to receive, that to receive doesn't diminish you nor make you a burden. The difference is important, because to turn receiving into one more obligation would be to betray its meaning. To receive is a permission you give yourself, a door you open when and how it makes sense to you, at your own pace. It is not a duty. It is a right, and it is, you will discover, also a form of love, perhaps the one that costs you most, and therefore perhaps the most precious in this phase of your life.

## 5. I'm only worth something when I'm useful

We are going to descend now to the deepest root of all this, to the hidden belief that sustains your fear of being a burden. And I want to name it clearly, because to see it is the beginning of loosening it. The belief is this: I'm only worth something when I'm useful.

Your whole life, without ever having said it like that, you measured your worth by your usefulness. You were worth something because you cared, because you gave, because you were needed, because others could count on you. Your importance, your place in the world, your sense of deserving to exist and to be loved, were always linked to what you did for others. And while you were useful, this never came to hurt, because your usefulness answered, every day, the question of your worth. You were worthy because you served, and you served a great deal, and so your worth was never in question.

But age brings a threat that no other phase brought: the possibility of ceasing to be useful. Of no longer being able to care as you cared, of no longer being needed, of needing instead of giving. And if your worth always depended on your usefulness, then to cease being useful seems, in the light of that belief, to cease having worth. That is why needing terrifies you on such a deep level: it is not only the practical inconvenience of needing help, it is the terror that, without the usefulness that defined you, you cease to deserve love, to have a place, to be worth something. Beneath the fear of being a burden is this greater fear: the fear that, if I am no longer useful, then I am no longer worth anything.

It is worth seeing where this equation between worth and usefulness comes from, because it is not a truth, it is a construction, and one of the cruelest. We live in a culture that values people for their productivity, for what they produce, yield, contribute, and that therefore treats those who no longer produce, the old, the sick, those who need, as a weight, a cost, a problem. You absorbed that logic, and you turned it against yourself, and now you judge your own worth by the same inhuman yardstick with which the culture judges everyone. But that yardstick is wrong, and it is wrong for everyone, not only for you. No human being is worth something for their productivity. A culture that measures people's worth by their usefulness is a culture that has lost sight of what a person is. You are not a unit of production that stopped yielding. You are a whole life, a story, a love, a presence, and that is not measured in usefulness, and it doesn't diminish when the strength diminishes.

To hide the fragility, then, is not only so as not to be a burden to others. It is, more deeply, so as not to face this truth you yourself fear: the truth that you are becoming dependent, that you are needing, that you are no longer the one who gives. You hide the fragility even from yourself, because to admit it would be to confront the terror that your worth is draining away with your usefulness. And so you live pretending an autonomy you no longer have, spending the last of your strength proving that you don't yet need, all to postpone the meeting with a question that frightens you: who am I, and how much am I worth, when I am no longer the one who cares?

I want you to look at a simple proof that worth doesn't come from usefulness. Think of a baby. A baby is useful to no one, produces nothing, cares for no one, only needs. And yet, no one doubts that a baby has an immense worth, that it is worthy of all love, that it deserves to be cared for. Why? Because the worth of a human being doesn't come from what it does, it comes from being who it is, from existing. If this is true at the start of life, it is true also at the end. You, like the baby, are not worth something for your usefulness, you are worth something for being, and you deserve love and care for the same reason the baby deserves them: because you are a person, and the life of a person has dignity in itself, from beginning to end, regardless of what it can or cannot do for others. The belief that you are only worth something when you are useful is a lie that life, at its start and at its end, disproves.

And here is the awakening of this book, the truth that frees, and I want you to receive it slowly, even if it costs to believe: your dignity does not depend on your capacity to give. Your worth doesn't come from what you do for others, it comes from who you are, and who you are doesn't disappear when the strength diminishes. A person is not worth something for their usefulness, like an object one throws away when it stops serving. A person is worth something for existing, for being who they are, for the life they lived, for the love they gave and that they are, for the presence they go on being even when they can no longer do. You go on being worth something, whole, even when you are no longer the one who cares. And to learn this, after a life of believing the opposite, is perhaps the deepest and most liberating crossing this phase of life offers you.

## 6. Asking for what you need

If your dignity doesn't depend on your usefulness, and if to receive is also to love, then there is a concrete step that opens, and it is the hardest of all for someone who spent her life never asking: asking for what you need. I'm going to speak of it in broad strokes, with care, because I know how much it costs you.

Asking, for you, goes against everything you learned, and so it is natural that at first it feels almost impossible, almost humiliating. Every time you are about to ask, the old voice appears: don't bother them, don't be a burden, solve it alone, they have things to do. And you draw back, and you keep quiet, and you are left without what you needed. I want to tell you that that voice, however strong, is not the truth, it is only the habit of a whole life defending its territory. To ask is not to bother. To ask is to give the other person the information and the opportunity to care for you, and, as we saw, it is very often to give them a gesture of love they need to be able to make. Each small request you dare to make is a training, and the first ones are the hardest, but with time the old voice quiets, and you discover that the world doesn't collapse when you need, and that those who love you, very often, are even happy to be able to help.

I want to give you a way of making asking easier, at the start. You don't have to begin with great requests, nor with declarations of fragility that cost you too much. You can begin with the smallest, and even with what gives joy to those who love you. Asking for company for a coffee. Asking a grandchild to teach you something, to visit you, to tell you about life. Accepting a lift, a dinner, help with a task, with a simple thank you instead of the usual it's not necessary. These small receivings are gentle trainings, and they have an advantage: they give pleasure to the one who gives, and they show you, in practice, that to receive is not a burden, that on the contrary it brings you closer. As you grow used to the small ones, the bigger ones, the ones that in fact cost, become possible. You don't have to transform yourself, from one day to the next, into a person who asks for everything. It is enough to begin to open the door, a little, and to let in, first, what is easiest and sweetest to receive.

And asking includes, with all clarity, asking for the health support you in fact need. I want to be very direct about this, because the fear of being a burden leads many older people to hide symptoms, to postpone appointments, to not say that something hurts or that something changed, for fear of becoming a problem. This is dangerous. The real needs of health are not a whim nor a weight, they are legitimate needs, and to meet them in time is not only your right but a way of taking care of yourself and, indirectly, of those who love you. To hide the illness so as not to be a burden can turn a small problem into a big one, and that, yes, would end up weighing far more. To ask for the health support you need, to go to the doctor, to say what you feel, to accept the necessary care, is one of the most important ways of allowing yourself to receive.

I want to stop here to tell you, firmly and warmly, something important about this phase of life. Loneliness and sadness in aging are real, are common, and are, very often, unseen, because the older person hides them, precisely so as not to be a burden. If you feel a sadness that doesn't pass, an isolation that closes over you, a loss of meaning that drags on, a sense that you are no longer needed by anyone and that life has lost its color, this is not only age, and you don't have to endure it alone as one more thing to hide. It may be a depression, which is treatable, and which deserves the care of a professional. To seek that help is not to be a burden, is not weakness, it is to take care of yourself, and you deserve that care as much as any person, as much as deserved all those you cared for your whole life.

And there is a specific fear that usually halts the asking, above all the asking for health care or more constant support: the fear of losing independence, of becoming dependent, of ceasing to be able to decide about your own life. That fear is understandable and is legitimate, and I want to distinguish one thing: to ask for help in some things is not to lose autonomy in everything. You can need support in one area and go on being fully the owner of your life, of your decisions, of your dignity. To receive care is not to hand over the command, is not to become a child, is not to cease being an adult person with a will of your own. It is only to accept help in what you need, keeping everything else. And very often, paradoxically, it is precisely refusing all help that ends up leading to losing more autonomy, because a problem not met in time grows and takes away more freedom than it would have taken if it had been cared for early. To accept help at the right moment is, after all, a way of preserving independence, not of losing it.

To ask for what you need is, at bottom, an act of trust and of love. Trust that you are worthy of being attended to, that you deserve care, that you are not a burden for needing. And love, because by asking, you open the door for those who love you to be able to love you in practice, and you give yourself the possibility of living this phase not in the loneliness of someone who hides everything, but in the warmth of someone who lets herself, at last, be accompanied. You don't have to do this all at once, nor in a perfect way. It is enough to begin, with a small request, and to discover, slowly, that asking doesn't take away your dignity. It gives it back to you, in the most whole form: the dignity of someone who allows herself to be human, and to be loved, to the end.

---

## 7. The dignity of the last half

I want, in this penultimate chapter, to lead you to look at this phase of life in a way that the world rarely offers, because the world, on this point, is almost entirely wrong. We live in a culture that values productivity, youth, independence, usefulness, and that therefore looks at aging almost only as loss, decline, a problem to manage. And you, who grew up in this culture, perhaps look at your own age with those eyes, seeing only what you lose, what you can no longer do, what you stopped being.

And I want to ask you not to confuse what the culture tells you with what is true. The culture says that to grow old is, above all, to decline, to become irrelevant, to leave the scene. But that is a particular reading, and an impoverished one, of one of the richest phases a life can have. There are those who arrive at this phase with a freedom they never had, free of the pressures to prove, to compete, to correspond, finally available for what truly matters. There are those who discover, now, simple pleasures that the rush never let them savor, relationships tended with more calm, a peace with their own life that only the years bring. I don't ask you to ignore the losses, which are real. I ask you not to let the impoverished reading of the culture rob you of the gifts that this phase also has, and that are as real as the losses, but that no one teaches you to see, because the world, busy fearing old age, forgot to learn to live it.

But there is another way of seeing, older and truer, and I want to offer it to you. Aging is not only loss. It is also a change of form, a passage into a time of life that has its own worth, its own gifts, its own dignity. The first half of life is, very often, the time of doing, of building, of producing, of sustaining. The second half can be the time of another thing: of wisdom, of presence, of transmission, of legacy, of a way of being in the world that is no longer about doing but about being. There are cultures, and there were times, in which the older people were precisely the most respected, the keepers of memory, the ones who advised, the ones who had, by age, an authority that youth could not have. That dignity of age did not disappear because it stopped being real. It disappeared because a culture obsessed with productivity stopped knowing how to see it.

There is, in many traditions, the figure of the elder, man or woman, as the one who, having left behind the struggle of doing, becomes the source of another thing: of serenity, of perspective, of a wisdom one gains only by having lived much. That figure is not worth something for strength nor for productivity, it is worth something precisely for no longer being bound to them, for having arrived at a place from which one sees the whole of life with other eyes. Our culture lost respect for that figure, and so many older people feel useless, discarded, a weight. But the figure goes on being true, and the place goes on existing, waiting for whoever occupies it with dignity. You can be, for your own, for the younger ones around you, that serene presence that no longer competes, that no longer has to prove, and that precisely for that reason can offer something that no one in the rush of doing manages to give: the presence of someone who has already arrived, and who is, simply, with those who are still on the way.

I want you to see what you still have to give, that doesn't pass through the practical usefulness that the old belief valued. You have a whole life of experience, of wisdom won at great cost, that the younger generations need and rarely find. You have a presence, that is worth something in itself, even when you do nothing, because to be there is, in itself, a way of loving and of accompanying. You have stories, memory, roots, that only you can transmit, and that give your own, above all the younger ones, a sense of belonging and of continuity that nothing replaces. Your dignity, in this phase, is not in going on proving that you are useful. It is in being who you are, a whole person, with a life behind her, who goes on mattering, loving, belonging, and deserving love, not for doing, but for being.

I want to speak to you of a very specific form of meaning of this phase, and perhaps the highest: legacy, transmission. You have, within you, a whole life of memory, of history, of knowledge, of love, and there are those who need to receive it, above all the younger ones of your family, who without you would lose the roots, the stories, the sense of belonging to something greater than themselves. To tell who those were who came before, to transmit what you learned, to leave recorded, in words or in presence, what only you know, is an immense service, and it is a service that only the older ones can render. It is not the practical usefulness of caring, it is another thing, deeper: it is being the keeper of memory, the root, the link that binds the generations. That role doesn't diminish with age, on the contrary, only the years make it possible, and in it there is a dignity and a meaning that no youth can have.

And I want to be honest, without romanticizing, because to romanticize old age would be another way of not respecting it. There are real losses in this phase, and they hurt, and I am not going to deny them. The body that no longer responds, the people who pass away, the capacities that diminish, are true losses, and you have every right to mourn them. The dignity of this phase is not in pretending that there is no loss. It is in finding, in the midst of the real loss, the worth that remains, the life that goes on, the love that can still be given and received. It is to hold both things, the loss and the worth, at the same time, without letting the loss erase everything that there still is.

This is, of the whole collection, the most spiritual book, and its spirituality comes from no religion nor from any particular belief, it comes from finitude, from the simple and profound fact that life has an end, and that the last part of it confronts us with the most essential questions there are. What is a life? What is it for? What remains, when the doing ends? Who are we, stripped of all we used to do? These questions, that in the rush of the active life we almost never have time to ask, impose themselves in this phase, and although they cost, they are a privilege, because they are the questions that give depth to a life. To face them, instead of fleeing them, is what can make the last part of life not a decline, but a deepening, an arriving, at last, at the essential, after a whole life occupied with what was urgent but perhaps was not the most important.

There is a spiritual question at the bottom of all this, and it is perhaps the deepest a life poses: who am I when I no longer need to prove usefulness in order to deserve love? And its sister, even harder for you: who am I when my turn comes to be cared for? These questions have no easy answer, and I am not going to give you a ready one. But I want to tell you that to face them, in this phase, is not a sign of decline, it is one of the greatest opportunities for growth that life offers, the opportunity to discover, finally, that one is worth something for existing and not for serving, that one is worthy of love not for what one does but for who one is. Few arrive at this wisdom. You can, precisely now, when life asks it of you.

## 8. The crossing

I wrote this book because I believe in one simple thing: understanding changes things. It doesn't change the age, doesn't give back the strength, doesn't undo the real losses. But it changes the way you live this phase, and that can change everything else.

Now you know that the fear of being a burden, however noble, hides a trap. You know that you spent a whole life never being a burden, and where that lesson came from. You know that the order of giving and receiving changes with life, and that what breaks the circulation of love is not your needing, it is your refusing to receive. You know, and this was the realization that changes everything, that to receive is also to love, and that to refuse it can deprive your children of a gesture they need. You know how to see the hidden belief, the belief that you are only worth something when you are useful, and the truth that undoes it: your dignity does not depend on your capacity to give. And you know that there is a dignity proper to this phase, that is not in proving usefulness, but in being who you are.

And you know, perhaps, that this is the closing of a long crossing, not only yours, but that of all mothers. Because if you notice, all the pains one can speak of to a mother, the guilt, the fear of letting go, the weight of saving, the wish to unite, the longing for those who left, the loneliness of raising alone, the shame of not feeling, the silent regret, all of them have, at bottom, the same root: a love that confused itself with carrying, with giving, with sustaining, with not being a burden. And all of them resolve at the same threshold: the threshold of letting love pass from fusion to relationship, from weight to exchange, from sacrifice to shared life. This book, that of the mother who learns to receive, is perhaps the one that best closes that arc, because it shows that love, in the end, is not only what one gives. It is also, and it is above all, what one lets oneself receive.

You know, deep down, the sentence that holds this whole book: I can receive without becoming a burden.

And perhaps, deep down, the oldest wound is beginning to come undone, in the deepest sentence of all: I go on being worthy of love even when I am no longer the one who cares.

To understand is already a great deal, and in this phase of life it is precious. But after the understanding there is a step that is not read, it is lived, by letting oneself.

It was for that crossing that I created **FreeMe**.

FreeMe is not more information for a life that has already lived so much. It is a space to, at last, let yourself be accompanied. It is a guided crossing that you do from within, at your own pace, to set down the belief that you are only worth something when you give and to allow yourself, at last, to receive. For a mother like you, who spent her life never being a burden and who now fears becoming one, there is a path designed for your wound: the fear of being a burden, the identity of usefulness, the courage to ask, and the serene discovery that you go on being worthy of love even when you are no longer the one who cares. Not to stop giving, for giving was and is your love. To learn that you too have a right to receive, and that to receive, in this phase, is one of the highest forms of loving and of letting yourself be loved.

There, the sentences you read here in silence are spoken slowly, with room and calm for the body to receive them. "My dignity does not depend on my capacity to give." "I have a right to need." "I go on being worthy of love even when I am no longer the one who cares." What here was a map, there becomes a path walked without haste. To receive stops being an idea and becomes a gesture. And the fear of being a burden begins, slowly, to give way to a new peace: the peace of someone who allows herself, at last, to be cared for.

If you feel this is the moment, I'm there. At [freeme.viviannedossantos.com](https://freeme.viviannedossantos.com).

And if it isn't yet the moment, that's all right, and there is time. What you've read is already yours, and the path waits for you, with the patience this phase deserves.

And I want to leave you with an image to keep, because it says what so many words tried to say. Imagine an ancient tree, one of those that for decades gave shade, fruit, shelter to whoever passed. There comes a time when that tree no longer gives so much fruit, when the branches grow heavy, when it itself needs to be held up, propped, cared for by those it once sheltered. And no one looks at that tree and thinks that it stopped having worth, that it became a burden, that it should be ashamed of no longer giving as it gave. One looks at it with reverence, precisely for the years it has, for the shade it gave, for everything it sheltered, and one cares for it with gratitude, as one cares for what is precious. You are that tree. You gave shade and fruit and shelter your whole life. And if now you need to be held up, that doesn't take away your worth, on the contrary, it is the recognition of everything you gave, and the care you receive is the gratitude taking form. Let them hold you up, as you held others up. It is the most natural and most beautiful order in the world.

But before you close these pages, I ask you only one thing, and it is perhaps the hardest gesture this whole book asks of you. Today, or soon, let yourself receive one thing. Accept a help you used to refuse. Tell a child, or someone who loves you, a small need you usually hide. Let them care for you, in a small gesture even, and instead of hurrying to give back or to apologize, stay, just, receiving. And notice what stirs in you: the discomfort, the urge to refuse, the voice that says you shouldn't. And receive all the same. Because it is in that small gesture of letting yourself be cared for, borne without fleeing, that the wound of a whole life begins to come undone, and that you discover, perhaps for the first time, that being loved never depended on not being a burden, and that there is an enormous love waiting for you, on the inside of receiving.

With all my tenderness,
