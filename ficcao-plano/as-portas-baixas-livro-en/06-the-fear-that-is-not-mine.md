## 6. The fear that is not mine

The conversations with the master, and the mirror of the other voices, worked in Modesta for a time, and led her, slowly, to a discovery that changed the way she saw herself: that the fear of standing out that governed her whole life was not hers, it was the mother's, and that she had spent her life obeying an inherited fear, hiding a gift of her own in the name of a fear that was not hers.

The conversations with the master, and the mirror of the other voices, and the voice that failed, joined, in that time, to open in Modesta a chink through which, at last, the light of a new question began to enter. Her whole life she had taken for granted her fear of standing out, as one takes for granted the air; she had never questioned it, it was the water she swam in, the form of the world. And she began, in that time, to question it, to look at it from outside, to ask herself whether that fear she had taken for a truth was really a truth or was only a fear; and that new distance from her own fear, the managing to look at it from outside instead of living it from within, was the beginning of everything, because a fear seen from outside no longer rules as much as a fear lived from within without being seen. Modesta began to see her fear as a thing she had, and not as a truth she was; and to see the fear as a thing one has is the beginning of being able to let it go, whereas to live it as a truth one is makes it impossible to let go, because no one lets go of what they take for a truth, only of what they acknowledge as a fear.

She began with a question she had never asked herself: why do I have such a fear of standing out? And in looking for the answer, Modesta came, as was to be expected, to the day she caught it, and to the mother, to Augusta, and to the lesson the mother had given her: lower your head, do not put on airs, one who shows themselves much catches it much. And she understood that the fear of standing out had not been born in her, it had been taught to her, put inside her by the mother, and that the mother, in her turn, had learned it from her own life as a small and cut-down woman, and that it was, therefore, an inherited fear, passed from generation to generation, from fearful mother to fearful daughter, a fear that came from behind and that Modesta carried as though it were hers without ever having chosen it.

And she understood more: that the mother's fear made sense in the mother's life and perhaps not in hers. Augusta had lived in a time and a circumstance in which perhaps to stand out was indeed dangerous, in which a woman who rose paid dearly indeed; and the mother, from her real fear, had drawn a lesson that had perhaps protected her. But that lesson, made for the mother's life, Modesta had applied to hers, without asking whether it served, and perhaps it did not serve, perhaps Modesta's life was not as dangerous for one who stood out as the mother's, perhaps she could rise without paying the price the mother would pay. Modesta had inherited the mother's fear and applied it to her life as though the two lives were the same, when they were not, and had lived frightened by a danger that was perhaps the mother's and not hers.

And there was the question of the register, the question of the shelf, the one Modesta had never heard but that governed her life: what am I pursuing that is not mine? And the answer, for Modesta, was subtle but clear: she pursued the smallness, the invisibility, the making-herself-small, as though they were her true height, when they were an imposed smallness, inherited from the mother's fear; she pursued, without seeing it, a making-herself-small that was not her nature, because her nature was great, it was the voice, it was the gift, and she spent her life pursuing it in reverse, making herself smaller than she was, obeying a fear that was not hers. What she carried that was not hers was the mother's fear; and what she hid that was hers was the gift; and the tragedy was that, to carry another's fear and to bury a gift of one's own, to live by another's fear and against the gift of oneself.

And there was, in this discovery, a thing that touched her particularly: that to bury the gift protected no one. Modesta had always told herself, without formulating it, that she hid out of a kind of virtue, that not to stand out was not to bother, not to provoke envy, not to overshadow others, a form of delicacy toward the village. And she understood, in that time, that it was a lie: that to hide the voice did good to no one, protected no one, only deprived her of singing and deprived the village of hearing. Who gained from Modesta's voice hidden? No one. The village lost a beauty; Modesta lost the joy of singing; and her hiding, which she disguised as delicacy, as not wanting to overshadow, gave no one anything, only took, from her and from others. And it was to understand this, that her hiding was not a generosity but a loss for all, that took from her the last excuse, because while she could tell herself that she hid out of delicacy, out of not wanting to bother, she had a noble justification for the fear; and on seeing that to hide had no nobility at all, that it did good to no one, she was left without the justification, and the fear appeared to her naked, as what it was, a fear, and not the virtue she had pretended it was.

She thought, in that time, of how many women of her line would have had voices, or gifts, or lights, and would have buried them like her, each one taught by the previous to lower her head, not to stand out, to make herself small. The mother had taught her; the mother's mother would have taught the mother; and so backward, a line of women passing one another the fear of standing out, each one burying what she had, each one teaching the next to bury hers. And Modesta saw herself as a link of that chain, a chain of silent women, of buried gifts, of lowered heads, that came from behind and that passed on ahead; and she understood that to rise was not only a thing of hers, it was to break the chain, it was to be the first, in that line of small women, to rise to her height, to sing her voice, to refuse to pass on the fear. And that idea, that of breaking a chain, of being the one who at last rises in a line of stooped women, gave her rising a greater weight and a greater meaning, because she no longer rose only for herself, she rose for all those who had come before and could not, and for all who came after and to whom she, in rising, would leave the example that one could.

And it was freeing, this discovery, because if the fear was not hers, then she could let it go; if the smallness was inherited and not her nature, then she could rise to her true height; if what prevented her from singing was a fear of the mother's and not a truth about the world, then she could defy that fear, sing despite it, discover whether the danger it announced was real or whether it was only the phantom of an old danger of the mother's. Modesta began to separate what was hers from what was the mother's: the gift, hers; the fear, the mother's; the voice, hers; the silence, imposed. And in separating them she began to be able to choose, for the first time, to keep what was hers and let go of what was not, to keep the gift and let go of the fear, to sing her voice and silence the mother's fear, and that choice, which she had never had because she had never distinguished her own from the inherited, opened to her the possibility of a different life, a risen life, a life in which the voice, at last, came out.

She thought of the mother, in that time, with a new mixture of pity and of revolt. Pity, because she understood now that Augusta had herself been a woman of a hidden gift, of a silenced voice, of a small life, a woman who perhaps had also had something to give and had buried it, and who had taught her daughter what she had learned, the hiding, because it was all she knew. Revolt, because however much she understood the mother she could not help seeing the harm she had done her, that of having passed her the fear, of having taught her to lower her head, of having buried, with the fear, the voice. And Modesta had to learn to hold the two things, the pity and the revolt, without letting one cancel the other: to understand the mother without excusing the harm, to forgive her without pretending that there had been no harm. Because to forgive the mother was not to say that she had done right; it was to understand that she had done what she knew, with the fear she had, and to let go of the revolt that only bound her more; but to let go of the revolt was not to erase the harm, it was to acknowledge it and to follow it, to correct it in her own life, to do differently from what the mother had done. And it was in that balance, between understanding the mother and correcting what she had done her, that Modesta went on finding the strength to rise, because to rise was, at the same time, to honour the mother (giving at last the voice that the mother, with hers, had perhaps wanted to give) and to free herself of her (defying the fear she had taught her).

There was, beneath everything, a question Modesta ended up asking herself and that was the hardest: and what if I am worth something? Because all her hiding rested, at bottom, on the doubt about whether she was worth something, about whether her voice was really good, about whether she really had a gift or whether it was presumption to think so. To hide was also a way of not putting that doubt to the test, of not risking discovering that after all she was not worth so much; while she hid, she could always think, in secret, that she had a beautiful voice, without the risk of showing it and discovering that it was not so beautiful after all. And to rise, to show the voice, was to risk the doubt, to put to the test whether she was worth something; and there was in that one more fear, that of discovering that after all she was not worth something, that she had been mistaken, that the voice she kept as a treasure was after all common. But Modesta understood, with time, that that fear, that of discovering that she was not worth something, was less bad than the eternal doubt of one who never risks knowing; that it was better to show the voice and discover what it was worth, be it much or little, than to keep it eternally without ever knowing; and that, besides, the master, who knew, assured her that she was worth something, and that she had to choose between believing the master and risking showing, or going on in the doubt and the hiding. And she chose, at last, to risk knowing, and discovered, in showing the voice, that she was worth something indeed, that the master was right; but it would have been worth it to risk even if she had discovered that she was worth less, because to live in eternal doubt, without ever putting to the test what one has, is a way of never truly living, suspended in a perhaps that is never confirmed nor denied.

She could not, on understanding this, rise at once, because a fear of a whole lifetime, even inherited, is not let go by understanding that it is inherited; the stooping was in her body, the fear in her bones, and to straighten up would hurt and would take time. There was a resistance, in this discovery, and the resistance had the form of a misunderstood loyalty. Because Modesta felt that to rise, to defy the mother's fear, was in a way to betray the mother, to contradict what she had taught her, to say that the mother had been mistaken; and Modesta, a good daughter, did not want to betray the mother, even dead, did not want to say that she had erred. And that loyalty to the mother's fear, that not wanting to contradict her, bound Modesta as much as the fear itself, because it transformed the hiding into a loyalty, and to disobey the fear into a betrayal. It took Modesta understanding that to be faithful to the mother was not to repeat the mother's mistake, that to honour the mother was not to bury the voice as the mother had buried hers, that one could love the mother and do differently from her; and more, that perhaps the true loyalty to the mother was to do what the mother, at bottom, beneath the fear, perhaps wanted for her daughter and did not have the courage to want — that the daughter rise, that she sing, that she live at her height, what the mother had never been able to. To free herself of the mother's fear was not to betray her; it was, perhaps, to fulfil her deepest wish, the one her fear had smothered, that of seeing her daughter be what she herself had never been.

There was an image that helped Modesta to understand what she had inherited from the mother, and it was that of a plant raised in a small pot. There are plants that, put in a small pot, grow small, not because their nature is small, but because the pot limits them; and if they were moved to a bigger pot, or to the earth, they would grow large, because their nature was large, it was the pot that made them small. Modesta had been a plant like that: her nature was large, the voice, the knack, all the richness she hid; but she had been raised in a small pot, that of the mother's fear, that of the not-standing-out, and had grown small, shrunken, the size of the pot and not the size of her nature. And what the story was offering her was the change of pot, the passage from the small pot of the fear to the wide earth where her nature could, at last, grow to its true height; and Modesta understood that she was not small by nature, she was small by pot, and that to change pots, to leave the mother's fear for the space of her own nature, would let her grow to the height that had always been hers and that the pot had never let her reach.

But the discovery was made, and it was great: Modesta could no longer believe, as she had believed her whole life, that her fear of standing out was a truth and a virtue; she began to see it as what it was, a fear inherited from the mother, and to see a fear as inherited, and not as a truth, is the beginning of letting it go, because a truth is obeyed and an inherited fear can, with work, be given back to the one who gave it.
