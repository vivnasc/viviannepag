## 8. The Keeper of the Register

It was at the House of the Register that Modesta ended up, after the voice that failed her, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that began to loose her throat, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, knew a thing about gifts that Modesta, with all of hers, had never learned.

It was at the House of the Register that Modesta ended up, after the voice that failed her, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that began to loose her throat, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, knew a thing about gifts that Modesta, with all of hers, had never learned. It was a place where Modesta had never gone for pleasure, because it was a place of memory, of kept things, and Modesta, who hid herself, avoided places that made her think of what remains of a person, of the trace one leaves, because she knew, at bottom, that her trace would be small, hidden as she had lived. But she ended up there, after the festival, led by a need she could not name, that of being with someone who would not judge her, and the Keeper, who received everyone without judgement, was perhaps the only person in the village before whom Modesta could show herself without fear, because the Keeper was not the village that cut down, was not the mother who had taught her to lower her head, she was a woman who looked at each one by what they had of best and not by what they had of small. And it was perhaps because she felt, in the Keeper, that look without a cut, without judgement, without the danger of standing out, that Modesta dared to unburden herself, to show the hidden voice, to speak of the buried gift, things she told no one, because before the Keeper, for the first time, she had no fear of being cut down for showing herself.

There was a thing in the Keeper that made Modesta feel safe to show herself, and it was that the Keeper did not belong to the village that cut down. The Keeper was in a way above the envies and the cuttings-down of the village, she kept the memory of all with the same impartiality, she competed with no one, she envied no one; and before someone who neither competes nor envies, a person who has a fear of standing out can, at last, show themselves, because there is not there the danger of the cut. It was for this that Modesta, who showed no one what she had, showed herself to the Keeper: because she felt, in that woman, a look without a cut, a safe place to rise; and it was in that safe place, before a look that would not cut her down, that Modesta dared for the first time to speak of her gift without hiding it, to say I have a voice, a thing she had never said, because to say it was to boast, was to stand out. And that first showing-of-herself, safe, before the Keeper, was a rehearsal of what would come after, the first time she took the gift from its hiding-place and set it in sight of someone without fear, and from that first one, made in safety, it grew less difficult for her to show it to others, because the first time is always the hardest, and Modesta had made it in the safest place, before one who would not cut her down.

She had gone to see to a paper, but she went low, with the humiliation of the festival still upon her, and the Keeper, who saw deep, perceived it, and asked her what was the matter, and Modesta, who never unburdened herself because to unburden was to draw notice and she hid herself even in pain, that time unburdened herself, because the voice that failed had broken something in her and she no longer had the strength to hide it. She told of the voice she hid, the fear of standing out, the solos she gave to others, the master's request, and the humiliation of not having managed to sing. And the Keeper listened, without being impressed nor scandalised, and when Modesta had finished, she asked a question that disarmed her: and that voice, Modesta, whose is it? Did you make it, or was it given you? And Modesta, without quite understanding where the Keeper was going, answered the truth: it was given me, I was born with it, I did not make it. And the Keeper said: then it is not only yours, woman. A voice that was given you is not only yours to hide or to show as you wish; it is a gift, and a gift is a thing one receives in order to give, not to keep. You, in hiding that voice, are keeping a thing that was entrusted to you to give, you are keeping what was not for you to keep, you are denying others a thing that was given you precisely to give them. It is not humility, Modesta. It is like receiving a message for someone and never delivering it.

And the Keeper fetched a book, the one of births, and showed Modesta her line, and beside it, she said, imagine that there was written what each one brought at birth, the gifts. Yours would say: a voice. You brought a voice, Modesta, at birth, like one who brings a message from heaven to deliver to the earth, and you have spent your life without delivering it, with the message shut inside you. And I am going to tell you a thing, from my trade, which is that of keeping what remains of people: what remains of a person are the gifts they gave, not those they kept. The kept gifts are lost, they die with the person, they leave no trace; the given gifts remain, in the memory of those who received them, in the joy they gave. You, in hiding your voice, are condemning it to be lost with you, to leave no trace, when it could remain, in the memory of the village, as one of the most beautiful things ever heard here. Do not hide what was given you to give, Modesta. Deliver the message, before it is too late.

The Keeper told her, in that conversation, a thing Modesta never forgot, a thing about what she saw in the books. I keep here, she said, the memory of all the people who passed through the village, and do you know what most saddens me, of all I see pass? It is not the misfortunes, for those are of life. It is the waste. It is to see pass people who had a gift and did not give it, people who could have given a beauty, a joy, a good thing to the village, and who kept it out of fear, or out of laziness, or out of whatever it was, and who died with it inside, and the gift was lost, and the village was left without it. That is what hurts me, in these books: the lines of the people who took to the grave what they had of best without having given it. And I do not want, Modesta, to write your line so, I do not want to have to think, when I write you, there goes one more who had a voice and silenced it. Give your voice, woman, so that I may be able to write, when your hour comes, there goes the one who sang, who gave us her voice, and not the one who hid it. And Modesta kept that, the idea that her line in the register could be of two kinds, that of the one who gave or that of the one who hid, and that she was in time to choose which, and that to choose to give the voice was to choose a line that the Keeper, and the village, and she herself, would remember with gratitude, instead of a line of waste that no one would remember.

And she said more, the Keeper, and it was what most stayed with Modesta. She said: you confuse humility with hiding yourself, and they are different things. True humility is not to make yourself smaller than you are; it is to be what you are without thinking yourself better than others. You can sing with all your voice, at full voice, and be humble, if you sing to give and not to boast, if the voice is a present and not an exhibition. To hide the voice is not humility, it is fear, and fear, unlike humility, has nothing of virtue. Rise, Modesta, sing with all you have, and do it with humility, which is to sing for others and not for yourself; but sing. Because a voice like yours, silenced, is not humble, it is wasted, and God, or life, or whoever gave you that voice, did not give it you to silence it.

The Keeper, before letting her go, said to her still a thing about humility that settled the question in Modesta's head. She said: there is a false humility that is, at bottom, pride, and you have some of it. To hide your voice, to say there are better ones, to refuse the solos — you think it is humility, and it is pride in reverse. Because a truly humble person receives a gift and uses it with simplicity, without making a drama of it; you make a drama of not using it, you give it an enormous importance by hiding it, as though your voice were so precious that you had to protect it from the world. That is not humility, Modesta, it is to give your voice an importance that humility would not give. True humility would sing without thinking twice, would give the voice naturally, without the theatre of hiding it. It is your hiding that is contrived, at bottom, because it draws attention to your modesty, makes of your humility a spectacle. Be humble in earnest: sing, give the voice, without drama, with simplicity, for that is humility, and not the theatre of hiding yourself. And Modesta was shaken by that, because the Keeper had turned her greatest certainty inside out, had shown her that what she took for humility had, at bottom, a hidden pride, that of giving her own voice too much importance by hiding it, and that true humility would be, on the contrary, to sing with simplicity, making no fuss, to give the gift as one gives a natural thing.

The Keeper, on taking her leave, left her still a phrase about the low doors, because she knew the image that governed Modesta's life. She said: you live in fear of breaking your head on the low doors, and so you lower your head at all of them. But notice a thing, Modesta: most of the doors through which you pass are not as low as your mother told you. She saw one or another low door, in her life, and told you they were all low, and you believed her, and you lower yourself before all. Try to pass upright and see. You are going to discover that you almost never strike your head, that almost all the doors let you pass whole, and that you went through life stooped out of fear of a few low doors you may not even meet. And where you do meet a low one indeed, lower your head at that, with sense; but do not lower it at all, out of fear of one. And Modesta kept that, and it was among the things that most helped her to rise, the idea that she could try to pass upright and see, instead of stooping beforehand before everything; and she began to do it, to pass upright and to see whether she struck, and she almost never struck, and it was so, door by door, discovering that most were not low, that she went straightening up, against the fear of a lifetime and the half-truth of the mother.

And Modesta heard that, and it was as though a door opened — a high door, that time, through which she could pass with her head raised —, because the Keeper had given her two things she lacked: the permission to sing, in showing her that to sing was not a lack of humility, it was to deliver a gift; and the duty to sing, in showing her that to hide the voice was not virtue, it was waste, it was not to deliver a message that had been entrusted to her. The Keeper, in speaking to her of the gift as of a message to deliver, gave Modesta a new way of thinking of her voice that changed everything. Because Modesta had always thought of the voice as hers, a thing of hers, that she could show or hide as she wished, and from the point of view of a thing of hers, to hide it even seemed a right, a modesty. But to think of it as a message, as a thing that had been entrusted to her to deliver, changed the question: because a message is not ours to keep, it is ours to deliver, and to keep a message that was entrusted to us is not modesty, it is a fault, it is to betray the trust of the one who entrusted it. And if the voice was a message, a gift entrusted, then Modesta did not have the right to hide it, she had the duty to deliver it; and what she had taken for modesty, the hiding, became, under this light, a kind of theft, the holding back of what was to be given. And that change of light, from seeing the voice as a thing of hers to seeing the voice as a message to deliver, took from Modesta the comfort of hiding it, because she could no longer see the hiding as a right, she had to see it as a fault, and no one lives well knowing they commit a fault, and Modesta, in seeing her hiding as a fault and not as a right, lost the peace she had had in it and gained the disquiet that would move her to deliver it.

She came out of the House of the Register with a thing she did not have when she entered: the idea that to hide the voice was not the neutral act, or even the virtuous one, that she had always thought, but a fault, a debt, a message undelivered. She had entered thinking that to hide herself was, at worst, a right of hers, and perhaps a virtue; and she came out thinking that it was a fault, that she owed the voice to the village, that she was holding it back like one who holds back what is not theirs. And that change, from seeing the hiding-of-herself as a right to seeing it as a debt, changed everything, because a right can be exercised at ease and a debt weighs, asks to be paid; and Modesta came out of the House of the Register with the new weight of a debt, the debt of the voice unsung, and it was that weight, that of owing the voice and not having given it, that moved her, from then on, to try to pay it, to loose at last what she owed, because one lives badly with a debt unpaid, and Modesta, in seeing her hiding-of-herself as a debt, ceased to live at ease in it and began to want to settle it.

And it was with that permission and that duty, still trembling but already firm in her breast, that Modesta came out of the House of the Register determined to learn, at last, to conquer the fear and to loose the voice, not to boast, but to deliver, before it was too late, the message she had carried since she was born and had never given.
