## 10. Rising

There was a joy, in this rising, that Modesta did not expect and that surprised her, and it was the joy of taking up her space. Her whole life she had shrunk, made herself small, taken up the least; and to discover that she could take up her space, be at ease, exist at her height, was a physical liberation, almost, like one who takes a weight off their shoulders and straightens up and breathes. Modesta discovered that it was tiring to make herself small, that to contain herself, to shrink, to watch herself so as not to stand out, gave an enormous labour, and that to drop that labour, to let herself be the size she was, was a rest. She had spent a whole life of energy keeping herself small, and on ceasing to do it, on letting herself rise, she felt an energy freed, the energy she had spent on shrinking and that now she could spend on living. And there was a simple joy in that, that of one who straightens up after a life stooped, that of one who breathes deep after a life holding their breath, that of one who takes up their place after a life apologising for existing; and Modesta, on tasting it, saw how much her making-herself-small had cost her, not only in voice and in gift, but in energy, in tiredness, in joy, and how much lighter, more restful, more joyful, it was to live at her height than to live shrunken.

To rise, Modesta discovered, was a trade like the others, and like the others it was learned slowly, with knack and with relapses, and she went on learning it, in that time, gesture by gesture, voice by voice, with the singing for the master for a principle and the will to loose the voice for a tool.

There was, at the beginning of the rising, one more fear that Modesta had to conquer: the fear that, in showing herself, in letting it be seen that she was after all great, the village would feel deceived, would say so she was always contrived and pretended to be humble, the hidden one who after all wanted to draw notice. Because Modesta had built, over her life, an image of a small and humble woman, and the village had grown used to it, and to rise was to contradict that image, to risk their saying that her humility had been feigned. And that fear, that of seeming that the humility had been feigned, nearly held her again, because Modesta had an attachment to her fame for humility. But she understood, with time, that it was better to risk their thinking her contrived than to go on buried out of fear of what they would say; that the village's opinion of whether she was humble or contrived was not worth the waste of her voice; and that, besides, whoever knew her in earnest would understand that she did not rise to boast, but to give, and whoever did not know her could think what they wished. And she went on dropping, too, that attachment to the fame for humility, that fear of seeming contrived, which was one more chain, that of one who prefers the good image of being small to the risk of showing that they are great.

There was a natural order in this rising, that Modesta went on discovering: first the body and the voice, then the rest. She began with the most concrete, to straighten the shoulders, to loose the voice, because the concrete is easier to change than the abstract; and as the body straightened and the voice loosed, there went on rising too the rest, the opinion, the presence, the place. It was as though to raise the body taught the soul to rise, as though the new posture went on opening the way to a new way of being; and Modesta discovered that she did not need to wait for the courage to straighten up, that she could straighten up first and let the courage come after, behind the posture. Shoulders back, chin raised, chest open — those gestures, still without courage, went on calling the courage, because the raised body does not match the shrunken soul, and the soul, seeing the body raised, went on rising to accompany it. And it was thereabouts, through the concrete of the body and the voice, that Modesta began, and it was the concrete pulling the abstract, the posture pulling the courage, the loosed voice pulling the loosed soul, all rising together but beginning with the simplest, the body, which was what she could change even without courage, by dint of straightening it.

She began with the voice, but she did not stay in it, because she understood that the hidden voice was only the symptom of a greater thing, the making-small of her whole life, and that to raise the voice was to rise in everything. And she went on rising little by little, in small things first. She began to say her opinion, when she had one, instead of hiding it; she began to let her knack be seen, her merit, instead of sharing it all; she began to straighten the body, the shoulders, the head, to pass through the doors a little more upright, to make herself a little larger. And each of those small risings cost her, because it went against a whole life of lowering herself, and the fear always came, the fear of standing out, the one who shows themselves much catches it much of the mother; but Modesta learned to rise despite the fear, to do the thing with fear and all, and discovered, with surprise, that to stand out a little did not bring the punishment the fear promised, that she could say an opinion and the sky did not fall, that she could let her knack be seen and no one cut her down, that the danger the fear announced was, most of the time, a phantom, the phantom of an old danger of the mother's, and not a real danger of her life.

And she learned to take up her space without apologising, which was among the hardest and most freeing of the learnings. She learned to pass without saying excuse me every minute, to speak without apologising beforehand, to be in a place without shrinking, to take up the place that was hers like any other person. It cost her, because the apologising for existing was as automatic as breathing, and Modesta had to watch herself so as not to apologise, not to shrink, to take up her space; but she went on learning, and each time she took up her place without apologising it was a small affirmation that she had the right to be, that to exist was not a fault, that she fitted in the world like any other. And she went on discovering that no one censured her for taking up her space, that the world had room for her, that her fear of bothering by existing was, like the other fears, a phantom; and that she could be at ease, take up her place, exist at her height, without anyone asking her to shrink, because the only one who had asked her to shrink had been the fear, and the fear, now, she was learning to disobey.

And she went on learning to distinguish the doors. She understood that many of the doors before which she had stooped her whole life were not even low, that it was she who lowered herself out of habit, out of fear, and she began to try to pass through them upright, to see whether she struck her head; and she almost never struck, because almost none was so very low, and Modesta went on discovering that she could pass whole, with her head raised, through most of the doors of life, and that she had lowered herself her whole life without need, out of a precaution that was fear and not necessity. There were, yes, a few truly low doors, a few situations in which it was indeed necessary to lower the head, to be careful, not to stand out; but they were few, far fewer than she had thought, and Modesta learned to distinguish them, to lower her head only where it was indeed necessary and to pass upright in all the rest, instead of lowering herself before all out of blind precaution.

It did not go straight, this rising, and there were relapses, days when the body stooped again, when the voice went back to the undertone, when the old lower your head, do not put on airs sounded within and made her shrink. To rise after a life stooped is like straightening a tree that grew crooked: it is not done all at once, it is done little by little, and the tree always tends to go back to the old curvature, and it must be straightened time after time until it learns the new form. Modesta straightened up, and stooped again, and straightened up anew, and each time she stooped she lost heart, thought she had changed nothing; but the master, and afterwards the memory of the master, taught her not to give up in the relapses, to straighten up anew each time she stooped, to treat the stooping not as a failure but as the old habit pulling, which is conquered with patience and repetition. And she went on straightening up, setback by setback, advance by advance, until the rising became, slowly, less costly, until the body and the voice and the soul went on learning the new form, the raised form, and the old curvature went on losing strength as she contradicted it.

And she learned, above all, to separate the humility from the fear. She understood that she could be humble and raised at the same time, that humility was not to hide what one had of good, it was not to think oneself better than others for having it; and that she could, therefore, sing with all her voice, show all her knack, rise to her height, and remain humble, if she did it to give and not to boast, if the voice and the knack were presents and not exhibitions. There was a day, in the midst of this rising, when Modesta did a thing that for her was enormous and that for another would be banal: in a conversation, in public, she disagreed with someone, said her opinion, different from the others', and held it. She did not yield, did not lower her head, did not swallow what she thought; she said it, and stood by it, in sight of all. And it was a small thing, a disagreement in a conversation, but for Modesta it was to cross a high door with her head raised, it was to take up her place, it was to exist in public with an opinion of her own, a thing she had never done. And she waited for the punishment, the cut, the consequence the fear promised; and it did not come. The people heard her, some agreed, others did not, and the conversation went on, and the sky did not fall, and Modesta understood, with that small disagreement, that she could have an opinion of her own in public and not be cut down, that the danger of asserting herself was, like that of singing, a phantom; and each of those small affirmations, an opinion said, a disagreement held, a place taken up, went on training her to rise in everything, not only in the voice, went on showing her that the world had room for her affirmation as it had for her voice, and that she could be, in everything, the size she was, without the punishment she had feared.

And it was that distinction that freed her, because what had held her her whole life had been to confuse the humility with the making-small, to think that to be humble was to be small; and on understanding that she could be humble and great, humble and raised, humble and singing at full voice, Modesta ceased to have to choose between the humility, which she valued, and the rising, which she feared, because she understood that they were not contraries, that one could have both things, be humble and use the gift, and that the true humility was even that, that of using the gift in the service of others, and not that of burying it out of fear.

There was a body relearning, in this story, and it is not a metaphor: Modesta, who had gone stooped her whole life, had to relearn, physically, to straighten up. The shoulders, fallen from a life of shrinking, were hard to open; the neck, accustomed to lowering, was hard to raise; the chest, closed, was hard to open to let the voice out. And Modesta went on working the body, slowly, straightening the shoulders, raising the neck, opening the chest, and it hurt, as it hurts to straighten what grew crooked, but she went on insisting, because she had understood that the voice needed a raised body, that one does not sing at full voice with the chest closed, that to raise the voice was also, literally, to raise the body. And as the body went on straightening, the voice went on loosing, because the body and the voice went together, and a raised body let out a raised voice; and Modesta discovered that to rise was a whole thing, of the body and the soul and the voice, and that on straightening the body she straightened the soul too, and on straightening the soul she loosed the voice, all linked, all rising together, the whole woman passing, slowly, from the curvature of a lifetime to the height that had always been hers and that she had never taken up.

"Do you know what is strangest, master?" said Modesta, on one of the visits to the old man, already loosing the voice with more ease. "It is that I spent my life thinking that to hide the voice was to be humble, and after all it was the contrary. After all the humility was to sing it, to give it, and what I did, to hide it, was pride in reverse, it was to think my voice too important to risk it, it was to keep it like one who keeps a treasure only for themselves. How did I confuse the two things for so long?"

And the master went on, weak but lucid: true humility, Modesta, is a rare and beautiful thing, and you shall have it, now that you sing. Because true humility is not of one who hides out of fear; it is of one who has a gift, and knows it, and gives it, and even in giving it does not think themselves better than anyone. That is the difficult humility, that of one who shines without boasting, that of one who gives what they have without swelling; yours, that of hiding yourself, was the easy humility, that of one who risks nothing, that of one who spares themselves the labour of being humble in earnest by making themselves invisible. Now you are going to have to learn the difficult humility, that of singing and remaining simple, that of being admired and not swelling, that of giving the voice and not thinking yourself for that better than one who has it not. It is harder than to hide yourself, this humility; but it is the true one, and you are capable of it, because you have a good bottom. And Modesta kept that, the idea that she had before her a new and more difficult humility, that of one who gives the gift without arrogance, and understood that to rise was not to abandon the humility she valued, it was to trade it for the true and more demanding version, that of being great and humble at the same time, which is harder than to be small.

And the master, weak but smiling, said still: because they taught you, girl, and what is taught early is hard to unlearn. But you have unlearned it. You are singing. Better late.
