## 2. The day I caught it

To understand Modesta one must go back to a day, far back, when she was a girl, because it was on that day that she learned the thing that made her hide her voice for the rest of her life: the day when standing out cost her dear.

It must be said, so that the size of the harm be understood, what the voice was for the girl before that day. It was not a talent among others, some skill or other; it was her way of being in the world, her joy, that in which she was fully herself. When the girl sang, she was happy, she was whole, she was herself without reserve; singing was the place where the girl most existed, most gave herself, most was. And it was precisely that the day she caught it taught her to fear and to hide: not some skill, but the place where she most was herself, her fullest way of existing. And so the harm was so deep, because in teaching the girl to hide her voice, they taught her to hide the place where she most was herself, to repress her fullest way of existing; and Modesta, from that day, did not hide only a talent, she hid the part of herself where she most fully lived, and came to live, for that, always a little outside herself, far from the place where she most was herself, which was singing, and which she had learned to fear.

Modesta had had the voice since she was small, and as a small girl she did not yet hide it, because children do not hide their gifts, they show them with the clean joy of one who has not yet learned to be afraid. And the small Modesta sang, loud, freely, with that voice that was already better than the others', and she liked to sing, and sang everywhere, and the voice came out full, without fear, without an undertone, because she had not yet learned to fear it. There was a time, that, when Modesta sang outward, free, and was happy singing, and the voice was a joy and not a danger.

And there was the day. It was at a village festival, or an occasion like that, when the girl Modesta sang, and sang so well, stood out so much, that everyone noticed, and for an instant the girl shone, was the centre, the best, the one who sang like an angel. And in that instant of shining the girl was happy, with the happiness of one who shows what they have of best and is seen; but the instant lasted little, because right after came the consequences of having shone, and the consequences were what taught Modesta never to shine again.

Modesta kept from that day one scene in particular, the one that most marked her, and that was small but that stayed driven into her. After she had sung and shone, a grown woman, a neighbour or an acquaintance of her mother, came up to her, to the girl, with a smile that was not a good smile, and said to her a thing that seemed a praise and was a stab: so now we are the star, are we? Look at the star. And the tone, the tone of mockery, of one who cuts, made the girl feel suddenly ridiculous, exposed, as though to have sung well were a shameful thing, a presumption, a putting-on-airs. And the girl, who an instant before had been happy singing, shrank, blushed, wanted to disappear, and learned, in that look at the star said with mockery, that to shine makes you a target, that one who rises is cut down, that the village does not forgive one who stands out. It was a small stab, of those that adults give children without measuring the harm, but it went deep, and Modesta carried it her whole life, the look at the star, and each time she was nearly standing out she heard it, within, and shrank, so as never again to be the star one mocks.

They came from various sides, the consequences, and Modesta kept them all. There came the envy: other girls, and even grown people, who did not like to see her shine, who looked at her askance, who said things, who made her feel that she had put on airs, that she had shown off, that she thought herself better than the others. There came the humiliation: someone, perhaps out of envy, perhaps out of malice, cut her down, said to her a thing that made her feel ridiculous for having shone, transformed her instant of shining into a shame. And there came, above all, the mother.

It must be said that there had been, before that day, a time when the house still let the girl sing, and that the change was for that more abrupt, more marking. The small Modesta sang about the house, and at first the mother let her, perhaps even liked, in an undertone, her daughter's voice; but the mother liked her daughter's voice inside the house, safe, between walls, and was frightened of it out here, in sight, exposed, because out here the voice was noticed and to be noticed was the danger. And that was the distinction the mother taught the daughter, without saying it in words: that the voice was for inside and not for outside, for home and not for the village, for the safe and not for the exposed. Sing for me, the mother perhaps said, but do not put on airs out there; and the girl learned that the voice had a place, that of inside, that of the hidden, and that to take it outside, to the village, to the light, was to transgress, was to risk, was to put on airs. And that distinction, voice for inside yes, voice for outside no, went on becoming the form of Modesta's singing for her whole life: she sang, yes, but inward, in an undertone, in the safe, and never outward, at full voice, in the light, because for outside she had learned that it was danger.

The mother, Augusta, was a small and fearful woman, herself cut down by a life of being small, and on seeing her daughter shine, and on seeing the consequences of the shining, the envy, the looks, she was frightened, and did what fearful parents do, she taught her daughter her own fear. She took the girl home and said to her, with the distress of one who wants to protect and only knows how to protect by shrinking: did you see what happened? Did you see how they looked at you? That is what comes of it, of putting on airs, of standing out, of showing yourself. One who shows themselves much catches it much, daughter. Lower your head. Do not put on airs. Do not be noticed. The doors of this life are low, and one who does not lower their head breaks it. Learn to pass small, not to stand out, for it is safer. And the girl Modesta, frightened by the consequences of the shining, by the envy, by the humiliation, by the mother's distress, learned the lesson: that to shine is dangerous, that to stand out costs dear, that safety is in making oneself small, in lowering one's head, in not being noticed. And she never again sang outward.

It should be said that the girl, before that day, had been a joyful child, and that the joy was inseparable from the singing. She sang because she was joyful and became joyful from singing, and the voice was the form of her child's joy, loose, free, without fear. And what that day took from her was not only the courage to sing outward; it was part of that joy, because in learning to fear the voice she learned to fear also the joy that loosed it, to distrust the moments when she felt full and wanted to sing, because those moments were the ones that made her stand out. And Modesta went on becoming a more contained woman not only in the voice, but in the joy, because the loose joy led to the loose singing and the loose singing to the danger, and it was better to contain the joy too, not to grow too enthusiastic, to keep everything, the voice and the joy, in an undertone. And it was perhaps that the greater loss of that day, greater than that of the voice: that of the loose joy, that of the capacity to grow enthusiastic without fear, that Modesta went on losing along with the singing, becoming contained not only in the voice but in the very joy of living, always holding herself back, afraid that the joy would lead her to stand out.

And there was, on that day and on those that followed, a change in the very way the mother treated her that reinforced the lesson. While the girl kept herself small, discreet, not noticed, the mother caressed her, approved of her, said she was a good girl, sensible; and when the girl stood out, shone, was noticed, the mother drew back, distressed, reproached her. And the girl, who like all children wanted her mother's approval, learned quickly which was her path: the approval came when she made herself small, and the reproach when she rose. And so Modesta learned to associate the making-herself-small with the mother's love and the rising with the loss of that love, and that association, made so early, was deeper than any word: her body learned that to be small was to be loved and to stand out was to risk the love, and from then on to make herself small became, for Modesta, not only a protection against the danger, but a way of guaranteeing the love, of being the good daughter, the sensible one, the one the mother caressed. And it was for that, too, that it cost her so much to let go of the making-herself-small: because to let go of it was, in the old logic, to risk the love, to cease being the good girl, to become the one who puts on airs, the one the mother reproached, and Modesta carried her whole life that fear that to rise would make her less loved, inherited from a mother who only caressed her when she was small.

It is just to ask whether the mother did right or wrong, and the answer, as almost always, is that she did both. She did right in warning her daughter that the world has envy, that to stand out has risks, that there is a prudence to have; that is true, and a mother who warned of nothing would leave her daughter unprepared. She did wrong in transforming the warning into a prohibition, the prudence into a fear, the care into a total hiding; she did wrong in teaching her daughter to lower her head at every door instead of teaching her to distinguish the low from the high. A good mother would have said: be careful, daughter, for there is envy in the world, but do not hide what you have, learn to give it with tact; Modesta's mother said only the first half, the care, and kept silent the second, the give it all the same, and so she gave her daughter the fear without the courage, the warning without the permission. And it was that half lesson, the care without the courage, that crippled Modesta: because care alone, without the courage to give despite the care, does not protect, it paralyses; and the mother, in giving her daughter only the care and not the courage, did not protect her, she paralysed her, left her afraid of the world and without the courage to face it, which is the worst of inheritances, that of one who is left knowing of the dangers and without the strength to confront them.

It was that day when Modesta buried the voice, or began to bury it, the day when the girl who sang free learned to sing in an undertone, the day when the gift ceased to be a joy and became a danger to hide. And the lesson was so deep, entered so early, that Modesta never again questioned it, carried it her whole life as a truth, the truth that to stand out costs dear and that it is safer to be small, and made of that truth her way of living, always stooped, always in an undertone, always hiding what she had of best, protecting herself from a danger she had learned in a single day of childhood and that she carried, like a sentence, the rest of her life.

There was, at the bottom of that day, a thing sadder still, that Modesta only understood when old: that the mother, in cutting her down, in teaching her not to put on airs, was not only protecting her, she was also, without knowing it, doing to her daughter what had been done to her. Because Augusta had also been, as a girl, one who had perhaps wanted to shine and who had been cut down, and who had learned to lower her head, and who on seeing her daughter shine felt, perhaps, beneath the fear, a thing she would not confess even to herself: a touch of envy, or of distress, at seeing in her daughter the boldness she herself had not had, the shining they had taken from her. And she cut her daughter down, in part, out of the fear of protecting her, but in part, perhaps, out of not being able to bear seeing in her daughter what she had lost in herself, because it is hard, for one who has been cut down, to see another rise, and the cut-down tend to cut down, they pass the cut on, they do to their children what was done to them. Not that Augusta wished it, or knew it; but it was there, beneath, and Modesta, when old, on understanding this, had for her mother a pity greater still, that of a woman who had cut her daughter down in part out of a wound of her own, and who had thus passed the wound on, from cut-down to cut-down, as it went on passing, from generation to generation, in that line of women who made themselves small.

And what Modesta never saw, and that the story was going to show her, is that the mother, in teaching her to lower her head, had not given her protection, she had given her a prison; that the danger of standing out, real on one day of childhood, did not justify a whole life hidden; that the envy of others and the mother's fear were no reason to bury a gift that had been given her to use; and that the lesson she had learned, that of making herself small, however much it had protected her from some looks, had cost her the most precious thing she had, which was the voice, the voice she never sang outward, the voice the world never heard, the voice she was going to carry to the grave unsung if she did not learn, at last, to defy the mother's lesson and to rise to her true height.
