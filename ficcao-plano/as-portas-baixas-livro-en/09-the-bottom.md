## 9. The bottom

The Keeper's permission, and the duty she had shown her, filled Modesta with a new will to sing; but the will, she discovered, was not enough, and it was to discover this that took her to the bottom. Because Modesta came out of the House of the Register full of the will to loose the voice, convinced that now, that she had understood she ought to, she would manage it; and she discovered, on trying, that to understand that one ought to is not enough to manage it, that between the will and the voice there was the fear, and that the fear is not conquered with will, or not with will alone. It was a bitter disillusion, that of wishing and not managing, that of having, at last, the permission and the duty and the will, and even so the throat shutting; because Modesta had hoped that the Keeper's permission would free her, and the permission had freed her will but not her throat, which went on held by the fear despite the will. And it was that distance, between the new will and the throat still held, that flung her to the bottom, because it is a particular torture to wish with all one's strength and not manage it, to have the permission and the duty and the will and even so to fail, and Modesta, who had thought that to understand would be enough, discovered, at the bottom, that it was not enough, that she was going to need more than to understand and to wish, she was going to need a way of conquering the fear in the body, and not only in the head.

But between the Keeper's permission and the loosed voice there was a path, and before Modesta sang outward there was her bottom, there was the time when, determined to loose the voice, she discovered that she could not, that the fear shut her throat even as she wished, and when she nearly gave up for good.

And there was, in that time of trying and failing, a particular solitude, that of one who fights a battle no one sees. Because Modesta's struggle was inward, in the throat, in the fear, and no one around her knew that she waged, every day, the combat of trying to loose the voice and failing; one saw a woman discreet as always, and within there was a war. And that solitude of the invisible struggle weighed, because Modesta had no one with whom to share it, no one to tell I am trying to loose the voice and I cannot, help me; she was ashamed even to admit that she tried, lest someone think that after all she wanted to stand out. It was the master, and only he, who knew of the struggle, because it was to him that Modesta, on his sickbed, dared to confess it; and it was because the master knew, because there was at last someone who knew of her invisible struggle, that Modesta ceased to wage it alone, and a shared struggle is half a struggle, because the weight is divided, and it was when she ceased to fight alone, when the master came to know and to help, that the struggle began, at last, to be winnable.

Because Modesta tried. After the House of the Register, determined, she tried to loose the voice, alone first, at home, where no one heard her, to train; and even alone, even with no one, the voice came out held, small, because the fear no longer needed an audience, it had ingrained itself so much that it shut her throat even in solitude. Modesta discovered, with horror, that she had lost, from so much hiding the voice, the very capacity to loose it, that the throat, accustomed a lifetime to smother, no longer knew how to open, that the fear had become a muscle that shut on its own, against her will. And it was terrifying, because it showed her that the prison was not only out there, in the danger of standing out; it was within, in the very throat, in the very body, which had learned to smother and no longer knew how to unburden.

And there was a despair in this, the despair of one who wishes and cannot, of one who has decided to change and discovers that the decision is not enough, that the fear is stronger than the will. Modesta spent days trying to loose the voice and failing, and each failure sank her more, confirmed her fear that it was late, that she had hidden the voice so long that she had lost it, that she was going to die with it held however much she wished to loose it. And there was a moment, at the bottom, when Modesta nearly accepted that, nearly concluded that there was no remedy, that she would stay silent for ever, that the voice that failed at the festival was the proof that she was not good for singing and that she should resign herself to the undertone until she died.

There was, at that bottom, a thought that frightened Modesta and that made her see the gravity of the place where she was: that of perhaps it no longer being worth trying, of it being too late, of the best being to accept the defeat and fall silent for good. And that thought, that of giving up, had a dangerous seduction, because it was comfortable, because to give up spared her more attempts and more failures, because to go back to the undertone, sad as it was, was safe and known. Modesta was near yielding to that seduction, to giving up, to accepting that the voice that failed at the festival had been the last word, to resigning herself to the undertone until she died; and she would have yielded, perhaps, had it not been for the master, because the bottom has that seduction, that of convincing us that to give up is more sensible than to go on trying. But there is a difference between the bottom where one sets one's feet to climb and the bottom where one lies down for ever, and what decides between the two is, many times, a hand that is held out, and to Modesta the master's hand was held out, and it was that which made of Modesta's bottom a place from which one climbs and not a place where one stays.

But it was master Apolinário, even ill, even ending, who took her out of the bottom, and took her out in a way that was his last lesson. Modesta went to visit him, on his sickbed, to beg his pardon for having failed him at the festival, for not having managed to sing; and the master, weak but lucid, told her that she had failed him in nothing, that he knew what fear was, and said to her the thing that saved her: Modesta, do not try to sing for the village, which is too much for now. Sing for me. Only for me, here, in this room, an old man dying who wishes to hear you. Forget the village, forget the standing-out, forget everything; sing only for a friend who asks you. And Modesta, there, in the room of the sick master, with no village, no audience, without the weight of standing out, only for an old friend who asked it of her, opened her mouth, and the voice came out. It came out small, at first, and then, on seeing that the master heard her with his eyes full, it came out larger, and larger, until it came out whole, the beautiful voice she had, there, in the room, only for the master, and the master wept on hearing it, and said it was this, it was this voice, thank you, and Modesta understood that she had managed it, that the voice had come out, that the fear could be conquered if she sang not for the village, not to stand out, but for someone, to give, for a friend.

There was, in that room of the master's, a silence after Modesta sang, and in that silence the old man said to her still a thing, perhaps the last important lesson he gave her. He said: did you see, Modesta? The voice came out because you forgot yourself. When you sing thinking of yourself, of how they will see you, of whether you stand out, the voice shuts, because the fear is all about you, about what happens to you. When you sing thinking of the other, of giving, the voice opens, because you forget yourself, and it is the fear of yourself that shut your throat. Your fear, girl, is a fear of yourself, of how they see you; and the cure is to forget yourself, to turn outward, to the one who receives. Sing always outward, to give, and forget how they see you, and the voice will always come out, because there is no fear where there is no self to defend. And Modesta kept that as the key it was: that her fear was a fear of herself, of her image, of how they saw her, and that the cure was to turn from herself to the other, from receiving to giving, and that the voice, freed of the weight of a self to protect, came out on its own, like the water of a fountain whose stopper is removed.

It is fitting to say that that first singing in the master's room was not perfect, nor did it need to be, and it was important that it was not. The voice came out trembling, at first, unsure, with faults, far from the clean voice Modesta had when no one heard her; but it came out, and that was what mattered, not that it came out perfect, but that it came out. And the master, on hearing it come out trembling and imperfect, did not correct her, did not point out the faults, received it as it was, with his eyes full, because what he wanted was not a perfect voice, it was Modesta's voice, however it was, loosed at last. And Modesta learned, in that, a thing that helped her: that she did not need to sing perfectly to sing, that she could loose the imperfect voice, trembling, with faults, and that an imperfect voice loosed was better than a perfect voice held. Her fear, in part, was of not being perfect, of failing, of the voice not coming out impeccable; and to free herself of that fear, to accept singing imperfectly, was part of loosing the voice, because one who only allows themselves to sing perfectly does not sing, and one who allows themselves to sing imperfectly, that one sings, and singing goes on improving, but only because they began by allowing themselves the imperfect.

And the master, in that room, gave her still an image that she carried: that of the water. A fountain, said the old man, has no vanity in giving its water; it gives it because it has it, because it overflows, because it is in the nature of water to run and not to stay held. Your voice is like the water of the fountain: it was given you to run, to overflow you, to quench the thirst of those who hear it, and to hold it is like stopping a fountain, which does no good either to the fountain, which rots, or to the one who was thirsty and goes without drinking. Let your voice run, Modesta, as the fountain lets the water run, without vanity, without fear, because it is in its nature, because it was for that they gave it you. And Modesta kept the image of the fountain, that of the voice as water given without vanity because it is in its nature to run; and it helped her, because it took from the question the weight of standing out, of showing herself, of being judged, and put it on another plane, that of a natural thing, the water running, the gift giving itself, without the drama of a voice that exhibits itself, only the simplicity of a fountain that overflows.

And it was that the key that took her out of the bottom: to sing to give, and not to stand out. Modesta understood, in the master's room, that what shut her throat was the fear of standing out, of showing herself, of being judged; and that if she sang not to show herself but to give, not for the village to judge her but for a friend to receive, the fear loosened, because there was no longer standing out, there was giving, and to give did not frighten, to give was good. It is fitting to say that the master, in asking her to sing only for him, did on purpose the wisest thing that could be done: he took away the audience, took away the standing-out, reduced the singing to its purest form, a voice giving itself to a listener who wants it. The old man knew, from his experience as a conductor, that a voice held by fear does not loose itself before a multitude, it looses itself before one, before a friendly listener, without judgement, who only wishes to receive; and that, loosed once before one, it can after loose itself before two, before three, before many, but that it has to begin with one. And it was that he gave Modesta, the one, the first safe listener, and it was from that one that Modesta went widening, slowly, the circle of those who heard her, always in the logic of giving to one and not of standing out before many, until the circle grew enough to include, one day, the whole village. The master, on his sickbed, had given her the last and best lesson of singing: that the voice looses itself in giving itself to one, and that from one one reaches all, but that one always begins with one.

There was, after that first voice loosed in the master's room, a period in which Modesta had to practise, and the practice was itself a learning. Because it was not enough to have sung once for the master; the throat, accustomed to shutting itself, shut itself again, and Modesta had to train it to open, time after time, as one trains a stiffened muscle. She sang for the master, on the visits; then she dared to sing for the woman who helped her at home, then for a trusted neighbour, always in the logic of singing for one, for someone who received, and each time the throat opened a little more easily, the fear tightened a little less, the voice came out a little more whole. It was a training, and like all trainings it had good days and bad, days when the voice came out and days when it shut again; but, on the whole, it went on improving, the throat went on unstiffening, the voice went on relearning to come out, and Modesta understood that to loose the voice was not a single event, a miracle that happens all at once, it was a training, a rehabilitation, a relearning slowly of what the fear had unlearned, and that she had to have the patience to train it, without giving up on the bad days, until the voice was again able to come out at ease.

And from that room, from that voice loosed for the master, Modesta began to come out of the bottom, training to sing to give: she sang for the master, then for the few she held dear, then for a few more, always in the logic of giving and not of standing out, and the throat went on opening, slowly, as the singing ceased to be exhibition and became a present, and Modesta went on relearning to loose the voice, not all at once, not for the whole village, but little by little, for whoever received it, until the voice was again able to come out.
