## 5. The chorus of voices

Modesta was not the only one in the village measuring herself against the question of standing out, and it was in seeing others resolve it, each in her own way, that she began, without knowing it, to see herself.

She began with Glória of the Festivals, whom she knew badly and judged better than she knew.

Glória sang in the choir on the right side, by the columns, and had a good voice, not as good as Modesta's, but good, and she did not hide it: she sang the solos, came forward, opened her arms, and at the festivals it was Glória the village waited to hear. Modesta, on the left side, hidden among the sopranos, looked at her from afar with the distrust of one who was taught to fear those who shine, and said inwardly, there goes Glória putting on airs again. She knew that they spoke ill of her. She had heard, at the church door, the sacristan's wife and the other one, Aurélia of the vegetable gardens, lower their voices when Glória passed: Glória thinks a lot of herself, that is all for showing off, in my day the women did not put themselves forward like that. And Modesta heard, and clung more to her smallness, and said to herself you see, that is what comes of standing out, and was grateful that no one had anything to say of her, hidden as she was.

But there was an afternoon rehearsal, in winter, when master Apolinário had a difficult passage repeated, three times, four, and the choir tired, and at a certain point a voice went off the note, just there beside Glória, it was Conceição, a girl who was distressed over her betrothed and had her head elsewhere, and went off badly, and two or three laughed under their breath, with that choir laugh that cuts more than it seems. And Conceição flushed, and was about to fall apart, and it was Glória who drew near to her, put a hand on her arm, and said, without lowering her voice, in front of all: never mind, woman, this happens to me every time I am in love, sing with me and I will hold you. And she sang beside her the rest of the afternoon, low, holding her up, Glória whom everyone thought only wanted the front. Modesta saw that from her corner and was left with a thing lodged crosswise that she did not know how to name that afternoon, and it was this: that Glória, whom she had painted as showy and vain in order to justify herself to herself, was after all generous, gave her voice without calculation, rejoiced in others', and that she, Modesta, hidden and safe, had never in her life held any voice for anyone, because to hold another's voice one must first not be afraid of one's own.

On the way out, in the churchyard now, Glória waited for her. Modesta, she said to her, and Modesta shrank by instinct, like one who hears her name said loud. You sing so well, woman. I hear you from here, from the other side, even when you hide. Why do you hide? And Modesta lowered her eyes and said the usual, there are better voices, and Glória laughed, but it was not a mocking laugh, it was an almost sad laugh, and she said: there are not, Modesta. You know there are not. I know what you have, that no one gave me half of. I put on airs, they say, and let them say it, for at least I sing. You, who have twice as much, hide. Mind you do not regret it one day. And she went off, upright, with her head raised, Glória who paid the price of standing out and who, Modesta was beginning to see it, kept the essential in exchange for the price, while Modesta, who spared the price, was left without the essential.

Glória would become, with time, a kind of friend, and one of those who helped her to rise, precisely for being everything Modesta did not dare to be.

There was, at the other extreme, a woman of whom the village hardly spoke any more, Idalina of the mill, who died that winter, very old, alone in a house at the head of the bridge. Modesta went to the wake, out of a neighbour's duty, and it was a poor wake, four people, and one of them, a great-niece come from afar, said to the other, low, beside the coffin: and she, what knack did she have? what did she do? And the other shrugged her shoulders: nothing that I know of. She was quiet. I think she embroidered, when she was young. They say she had some knack, but no one knows any more. And it hung there in the air, they say she had some knack, but no one knows any more, said over a dead woman, and Modesta, standing at the back, felt the cold rise up her legs, because she saw there, in that sentence, her own wake: someone saying of Modesta that she was quiet, that I think she sang, when she was young, that they say she had some voice but no one remembers any more. One who hides what they have leaves no memory, Modesta understood before Idalina's coffin; they pass through life without a trace, and die like one who never had anything, because what is hidden is as though it had never existed. And that one, the forgotten Idalina, frightened her more than all the envy Glória suffered, because it showed her that the price of hiding was not the safety she thought, it was the forgetting.

About that time, a young girl came into the choir, Branca, the blacksmith's niece, fifteen years old and a voice she still hardly knew she had. And Modesta, who knew how to listen, understood at once what she had; and understood, with a tightening, another thing: that the girl already hid herself. She sang beneath, measured herself, lowered her head when the master looked in her direction, and once, when he asked who wanted to try the upper part, Branca shrank behind the music stand, exactly the same, and Modesta saw herself at fifteen, and it hurt her as few things had hurt her. On the way out, she caught the girl on the road and said to her, and it was the first time in her life she said a thing like that to anyone: you, Branca, do not do as I did. And the girl, not understanding: how do you mean, dona Modesta? And Modesta, who was about to lower her eyes, raised them: do not hide what you have. I hid mine my whole life and look what I came to, an old woman barely heard in the choir. You sing. Let yourself be heard. And she went off trembling, because she had just said aloud, to another, what she had never said to herself; and she understood, on the road, that if one day she rose she would not rise only for herself, she would rise for that girl, to show her that one could, to be for Branca the example she herself had never had.

It was about that time that she spoke with Felismina, the one from the willow, who was now seventy and who had made the journey the reverse of Modesta's. Felismina had sung silent half her life, and one day, a widow, already late, had dared to let out the voice at a fair, and had never stopped again. They talked at the washing place, on their knees over the stone, and Modesta, not quite knowing why, asked her whether she did not regret it, having begun so late, having exposed herself at the age she had. And Felismina stopped wringing the cloth, looked at her, and said to her the thing Modesta would carry with her: I regret it, yes. I regret the years in which I sang silent, Modesta, those are the ones that hurt me. Of the years in which I sang loud I do not regret one. No one, at the end of life, regrets having sung. What they regret is not having sung, having fallen silent out of fear, and that, my girl, that regret, is the worst there is, because it no longer has a remedy. And she went back to the cloth, and Modesta stayed on her knees on the cold stone understanding that she was going, on the path she was on, straight to the regret of those who fell silent.

It was master Apolinário, once more, who put it all in words for her, at a rehearsal where the two of them stayed behind to tidy the scores, the room already empty. Modesta, he said, without looking at her, lining up the papers, there are two fears in life, and one is much worse than the other. There is the fear of standing out, and there is the fear of dying without having shown oneself. You spent your life full of the first, and fled it so well that you are going to fall into the second, which is the worst of all. The envy of those who hear you is worth more than the silence of those who never heard you. Choose your fear, woman, because you are going to have to live with one of them: either you fear standing out, and you show yourself, or you fear dying silent, and you hide. I, in your place, would choose the first, which at least lets one sing. And he set down the papers, and looked at her over his spectacles, and Modesta had nothing to answer.

There were, it is true, in the village, the voices that confirmed her fear, those that cut down whoever rose: the sacristan's wife, Aurélia, and a few others who watched the churchyard so that no one would put on airs. And those were real, Modesta had not invented them. But she was beginning to see, now, that she had given those three or four tongues a power they did not deserve, that she had organised her whole life around not giving them anything to talk about, and that that was, at bottom, to let them rule her, to let Aurélia of the vegetable gardens decide, with her possible envy, whether Modesta sang or not. Their envy was theirs to feel, she understood, not hers to manage; and to govern her whole life by what Aurélia would say was to hand Aurélia the helm. Perhaps it was time to govern it by her own gift, and leave the envy of whoever had it to whoever had it.

And there was the Fountain Square, where the village crossed paths and where the choir was heard at the festivals, and Modesta noticed, with time, a thing about the village: that the same village that envied one who shone was the one that filled the square to hear it sing. Aurélia, who cut Glória down, was the first to arrive at the festival and the last to leave, her eyes fixed on the choir. The village wanted its voices, its festivals, its music; it became the poorer if no one dared. And Modesta understood that, in hiding the voice, she was not only sparing herself the envy of three or four, she was taking from the whole village a thing it wanted, a beautiful voice; that her hiding was, seen from the full square, an impoverishment, a good thing missing from all those people. To give the voice was not only to risk the envy of a few; it was to give drink to many.

And Modesta stayed with all of that, with the Glória who paid and sang, with the Idalina who hid and was lost, with the Branca who was beginning to hide herself, with the Felismina who regretted the silence, with the master and his two fears, and she began, slowly, to think that perhaps it was time to change fears, to trade the fear of standing out for the fear, more honest, of dying with her voice unsung.
