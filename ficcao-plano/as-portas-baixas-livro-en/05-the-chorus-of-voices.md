## 5. The chorus of voices

Modesta was not the only one in the village dealing with the question of standing out or not, and it was in seeing other people, each in their own way, that she began, without knowing it, to see herself and what she had done with her gift.

Modesta was not the only one in the village dealing with the question of standing out, and there were, around her, various ways of resolving it, that served her as a mirror. And she, who had thought herself unique in her fear, went on understanding that the fear of standing out was common, that many had it, each to their own measure, and that the village was full of people who contained themselves, who hid themselves, who did not give what they had out of fear of what would be said; but she went on understanding also that others did not have it, that there were those who gave what they had without fear, and that those were not worse nor more full of themselves, they were only freer. And in seeing the two kinds, those who contained themselves and those who gave themselves, Modesta began to ask herself to which she wanted to belong, and understood that she had always belonged to the first without ever having chosen, by inheritance and by fear, and that perhaps she could, if she wished, pass to the second, to that of those who give without fear, who were not better than her by nature, they were only less frightened, and fear, unlike nature, can be conquered.

There was Glória of the Festivals — a woman who was talked of in the village —, who had a good voice, not as good as Modesta's, but good, and who unlike Modesta did not hide it, but rather showed it, sang the solos, was noticed, liked to shine. And Glória of the Festivals paid for it, it is true: there was envy, there were those who found her full of herself, there were those who spoke ill of her, who cut her down, because in a village one who stands out always pays something. And Modesta, in seeing Glória pay for the shining, confirmed her lesson, said to herself you see, that is what comes of standing out, and clung more still to her smallness, because Glória was the living proof that to shine costs dear. But with time Modesta began to see another thing in Glória: that Glória, despite paying for the envy, was happy singing, that the enjoyment of letting out the voice, of shining, of giving her music to others, was worth, for Glória, the price of the envy; and that Glória, in the end, had sung, had given her voice to the world, had lived her gift, while Modesta, protected from the envy, had buried hers and had lived nothing. And Modesta began to ask herself, against her lesson, whether it would be worth it to trade the envy Glória suffered for the waste she herself lived, whether it would not be better to pay the price of shining than to pay the price, higher, of never shining.

Glória of the Festivals, it is just to say, became with time a kind of friend to Modesta, and was one of the people who helped her to rise, precisely for being everything Modesta did not dare to be. At first, Modesta looked at her from afar, with the distrust of one who was taught to fear those who shine; but, on drawing near, she discovered in Glória not a rival nor a vain woman, but a generous woman, who gave her voice without calculation, who rejoiced in others', and who, far from competing with Modesta, encouraged her, told her sing, woman, you sing so well, why do you hide. And Glória, who sang without fear, showed Modesta, by example, that one could sing and be a good person, shine and be generous, give the voice and not be for that vain; she dismantled, only by existing, the idea Modesta had that one who shines is full of themselves, that to stand out is vanity. And it was Glória's friendship, as much as the master's words, that helped Modesta to rise, because it gave her a living example, at her side, of a woman who sang without fear and without pride, a proof that one could be both things, and a constant encouragement to do the same.

There was, on the other side, a woman whose name the village had almost forgotten, one who had had, it was said, some gift — a knack, an art, a voice too, perhaps — and who had hidden it her whole life as Modesta hid hers, and who had died without ever having shown it, and of whom now almost no one remembered, because one who hides what they have leaves no memory, passes through life without a trace. And Modesta, in thinking of that forgotten woman, saw her own future: to die without a trace, without anyone knowing what she had had, to carry the gift to the grave and be forgotten as though she had never had anything, because what is hidden is as though it did not exist, and a whole life of hiding a gift ends in a death without memory, in a passage through the village without leaving anything of what one had of best. And that one, the forgotten woman who had hidden her gift, frightened Modesta more than the envy Glória suffered, because it showed her that the price of hiding was not the safety she thought, it was the forgetting, the waste, the death without a trace.

She came to know, in that time of beginning to see, a young girl of the choir, one who had a knack and who was beginning to hide herself as Modesta had hidden herself, to refuse to stand out, to make herself small; and on seeing her, Modesta saw herself when young, and it hurt her, because she understood that the ill was passed on, that the girl was learning, perhaps with Modesta herself, perhaps with the village, perhaps with a mother, the same lesson that had imprisoned her, that of not standing out. And it was to see in the girl her own prison beginning in another that gave Modesta a new will, that of warning, that of telling the girl do not do as I did, do not hide what you have, do not learn to lower your head; and that will to warn, to spare another what she had suffered, was one more engine of her change, because one thing is for a person to want to rise for themselves, another is to want to rise to show another that one can, to be, for the girl, the example she herself had never had. Modesta understood that if she raised her voice she raised it not only for herself, she raised it also for that girl, and for all who came, showing them that one could have a gift and give it without punishment, and that that, that of serving as an example to one who hid themselves, was perhaps an even stronger reason to rise than her own liking for singing.

And between the two, the Glória who shone and paid and the forgotten one who hid and was lost, Modesta saw the two paths and saw that she had chosen the worse. She had always thought that the safe path was that of hiding, and that the dangerous path was that of shining; and she began to see that it was the contrary, that the path of shining, with all its price of envy, led to a life lived, to a gift given, to a memory left, and that the path of hiding, with all its safety, led to the waste, to the forgetting, to a life not lived. Glória had paid a price and was left with the essential; Modesta had spared the price and lost the essential; and between paying the price and being left with the essential and sparing the price and losing the essential, Modesta began to understand which was, after all, the good bargain, and it was not hers.

Glória of the Festivals, it is just to say, was not the full-of-herself and vain woman Modesta, out of defence, had wanted to see in her; she was only a woman who had no fear of giving what she had. Modesta, to justify her own hiding, had needed to see in Glória an exhibitionist, one who boasted, one who sang to show herself; and so she could tell herself that it was better to be like her, hidden and humble, than like Glória, shown and vain. But on coming to know Glória better, Modesta went on seeing that she did not boast, that she sang for pleasure and for giving, not out of vanity, that her lack of fear of standing out was not arrogance, it was health, the health of one who has not learned to be afraid of their own gift. And that dismantled Modesta's defence, because she could no longer see Glória as the bad example that justified her hiding; she had to see her as what she was, a healthy woman who gave what she had without fear, and in seeing her so, Modesta understood that Glória was not the bad contrary of her good humility, she was the healthy contrary of her sick fear, and that it was Glória, and not she, who had the right relationship with her own gift.

There was still, in the choir, a voice that had made the path contrary to Modesta's, and that served her as a mirror in reverse: a woman who had sung in an undertone many years, like Modesta, and who one day, already late, had dared to let out the voice, and who had discovered, in letting it out, the joy she had lost so much time. And that woman, the one who had loosed herself late, said to Modesta a thing that stayed: I regret the years in which I sang silent, Modesta, not the years in which I sang loud. No one, at the end of life, regrets having sung; they regret not having sung, having fallen silent out of fear. And Modesta kept that, the idea that the regret, in the end, is not of having stood out, it is of not having dared, that no one at the edge of death laments having sung too much, and many lament having sung too little; and she understood that she was going, on the path she was on, toward the regret of those who fell silent, and that perhaps it was time to change paths so as not to come to the end with the regret of not having given the voice, which is, according to the one who had loosed herself late, the worst of regrets.

It was master Apolinário, once more, who put the question in words, in a conversation that stayed. Modesta, he said, there are two fears in life: the fear of standing out and the fear of dying without having shown oneself. You spent your life with the first, and fled it so well that you are going to fall into the second, which is much worse. The envy of those who hear you sing is worth more than the silence of those who never heard you. Choose your fear, Modesta, because you are going to have to live with one of the two: either the fear of standing out, and you show yourself, or the fear of dying silent, and you hide yourself. I, in your place, would choose the first, which at least lets one sing. There were still, in the village, voices that confirmed Modesta's fear, people who spoke ill of those who stood out, who cut down those who rose, who watched so that no one would put on airs; and those voices were real, Modesta had not invented them, the village had indeed its dose of envy and of cutting-down. But Modesta had given those voices a power they did not deserve, had organised her whole life around them, had let the fear of what would be said bury her gift; and she began to understand, now, that to give so much power to the envy of others was, at bottom, a way of putting them to rule her life, of living for what they would say, of letting them decide, with their possible envy, whether she sang or not. And she asked herself whether it was worth it to give to the envy of some the power to silence her voice, whether it would not be better to sing and let them envy, to live her life and let them say what they would, instead of living bound to what they might think. Because the envy of the envious is theirs to feel, not hers to manage, and to organise one's life around not provoking envy is to let the envious govern it, and Modesta understood that she had governed her whole life by the possible envy of others, and that perhaps it was time to govern it by her own gift, leaving the envy of whoever had it to whoever had it.

The Fountain Square, where the village crossed paths, was also the place where the choir was heard at the festivals, and Modesta noticed, with time, a thing about the village and the voices: that the village, however much it envied one who stood out, loved to have one who sang. The village wanted its voices, its festivals, its music; it needed one who rose to sing in order to have something to hear. And there was a contradiction in the village, that Modesta went on understanding: that the same village that envied one who shone also needed one who shone, also wanted its voices, also became poorer if no one dared to sing. The envy was of some; but the desire to hear a beautiful voice was of many, of the whole village, which enriched itself with each voice that rose. And Modesta understood that, in hiding the voice, she was not only protecting herself from the envy of some, she was depriving the whole village of a thing it wanted and needed, a beautiful voice; that her hiding was, from the village's point of view, an impoverishment, a good thing that was missing; and that to give the voice would be, more than to risk the envy of some, to enrich the village of many, to give it what it wanted and what it was the poorer without.

And Modesta stayed with that, with the idea that she had to choose between two fears and that she had chosen the worse, and she began, slowly, to think that perhaps it was time to change fears, to trade the fear of standing out for the fear, more honest, of dying with her voice unsung.
