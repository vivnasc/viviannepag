## 11. The solo

There was a day, in that time of learning to rise, when Modesta sang, at last, a solo for the village, at full voice, heard by all, and it was the day she rose for good, and it was also, in a certain way, a farewell and a homage.

It was not an easy decision, and until the day Modesta hesitated, because the memory of the other festival, of the voice that failed her, weighed on her; she was afraid of failing again, of rising and the voice not coming out, of repeating the humiliation before the village and the master. But that time it was different, and Modesta knew it: that time she had already trained, had already loosed the voice for the master, for Glória, for the few, already knew what opened the throat, the turning toward giving; that time she did not rise empty-handed, with only a good intention, she rose with a training done, with a learned way of conquering the fear. And it was that difference, that of rising prepared and not only decided, that gave her the courage to try again despite the memory of the fall; because the first time she had failed for rising without knowing how to conquer the fear, and that time she knew, she had trained, and she had, therefore, a real chance of managing it, and not only the blind hope of the first time.

Master Apolinário was coming to the end, and the choir was preparing a last festival, perhaps the last he would conduct, and Modesta, who had learned to loose the voice in the master's room and afterwards for the few she held dear, decided a thing that months before would have been unthinkable: that she was going to sing the solo of that festival, at full voice, for the village, for the master, before he departed. Not for the solo, not to stand out, but to give the master, at last, what he had asked of her her whole life and that she had failed him at that earlier festival; to deliver, at last, the message she had carried since she was born; to sing, before it was too late, the voice she had hidden a whole lifetime.

It cost her to decide, and until the last moment the fear fought against the decision. In the days before the festival, Modesta woke several times determined to give up, to tell the choir that after all she would not sing, to go back to the undertone where she was safe; and she had to hold to the decision, to remember the master's request, the Keeper, what she had learned, to sing not for the village to judge but to give, for the master. She repeated to herself, in the days before, I am going to sing to give, not to stand out; I am going to forget myself and think of the one who receives; and it was that preparation, not of the voice, for the voice she had, but of the intention, that sustained her against the fear. Because Modesta had learned, in the master's room, that the voice shut when she thought of herself and opened when she thought of giving; and she prepared herself, therefore, turning her head from herself to giving, rehearsing not the notes, which she knew, but the intention of donating, the only one that would open her throat before the village. And she came to the day of the festival armed not with courage, which she did not have, but with that intention, that of giving, which was stronger than courage, because it dispensed with it: she did not need to be brave to conquer the fear, she needed to forget herself, and the forgetting-of-herself, turning toward giving, did what courage would do, it opened her throat.

And she prepared herself, that time, not only with the voice, but with all she had learned. She was not going to sing for the village to judge; she was going to sing to give, for the master, for the church, for God if they wished, but to give and not to stand out. She was not going to rise to show herself; she was going to rise to deliver. And armed with that intention, that of giving and not of standing out, which was the one that opened her throat, Modesta rose, on the day of the festival, to sing the solo, before the full village, before the master who heard her perhaps for the last time, and that time she did not flee, did not shrink, did not lower her head; she rose, at her height, and opened her mouth, and sang.

There was, before the voice came out, an instant of silence, the instant when Modesta, standing, before the village, felt the old fear rise and knew that everything was going to be decided there, in that instant, between yielding to the fear as she had yielded at the other festival or conquering it as she had learned in the master's room. And in that instant, instead of thinking of herself, of how they saw her, of whether she stood out — which was what shut her throat —, Modesta turned, within, to what she had learned: she thought of the master, who had departed and to whom she sang in memory; she thought of the village, to whom she wished to give the voice; she thought of the mother, and of all the silenced ones; she forgot herself, turned toward giving, and it was in that turning from herself to giving, in that instant of silence before the first note, that the throat, instead of shutting, opened. And the voice came out. It came out whole, that time, the beautiful voice she had, the best voice the village choir had known, and it filled the church, and made the village stop, and it was as though, after a life in an undertone, the voice burst at last outward, free, full, glorious, giving the village the beauty she had hidden a whole lifetime. And the village, which had never heard that voice, was left suspended, because no one imagined that Modesta, the humble one, the demure one, the one who always hid herself, had that within, that voice that seemed to come from heaven; and there were those who wept, on hearing it, from the beauty, and the master, he, wept for certain, because he heard, at last, before departing, the voice he had awaited his whole life, the voice that had been worth the wait, loosed, whole, given. She sang, in that solo, an old thing, of those sung at the village festivals, and the choice was not by chance: she chose one of those the mother used to hum at home, in an undertone, on the rare times the mother sang, low, inward, as Modesta had learned to sing. And on singing it at full voice, Modesta gave that melody the mother had hummed in an undertone the whole voice the mother had never given her, loosed for the church the song the mother had kept inward; and it was as though she gave the mother, in that solo, the voice the mother had never had the courage to loose, as though she sang, at last, aloud, what the mother had sung her whole life low. And there was in this a reconciliation and a liberation at the same time: the mother, who had taught her the undertone, received now, in the daughter's voice, the loosed singing she herself had never given; and the daughter, on loosing the mother's song, freed herself of the undertone the mother had taught her, transforming the inheritance of the fear into its overcoming, singing aloud what she had received to sing low.

And Modesta, singing, felt a thing she had never felt: the joy of giving her gift, the joy of loosing the voice, the joy of being, at last, her true size, of rising to her height and singing for the world the most beautiful thing she had. There were, it is true, those who envied, because envy does not disappear, and in the village there were those who, on hearing Modesta, felt the old sting, who said afterwards, in an undertone, just look at Modesta, after all she always knew how to sing and hid it, now she puts on airs. But Modesta, that time, did not let that envy, which existed, matter to her, because she had understood, at last, that the envy of some is the price of giving a gift, and that it is worth paying it; that one cannot give anything beautiful without someone envying; and that to organise one's life around not provoking envy is to let the envious silence what we have of good. The envy, that time, was like a background noise that she heard and did not let matter, occupied as she was with the joy of singing and with the gratitude of most of the village; and that was also a victory, that of singing despite the envy, that of not letting the fear of the envy, which had silenced her her whole life, silence her again, that of accepting the envy of some as the small price of the joy of giving the voice to all.

And there was no punishment that mattered, there was not the danger the fear had promised; there was only the beauty of a loosed voice and the joy of giving it, and Modesta understood, singing, that the fear of a whole lifetime had lied, that to stand out with a gift, to give it, did not cost dear, it cost only the fear of doing it, and that on the other side of the fear was that, the joy of singing, which she had lost her whole life for not daring.

While she sang, Modesta thought of the mother, of Augusta, who had already departed, and it was as though she sang also for her, for the mother who had never sung, who had never risen, who had lived and died stooped. It was as though, on loosing her voice, she loosed also the voice the mother had never loosed, the voice of all the women of her line who had made themselves small, who had lowered their heads, who had buried whatever they had; as though that solo were not only hers, it were of all of them, of generations of silenced women who at last, in Modesta's mouth, sang. And that idea, that of singing for all the silenced ones who had come before her, gave her voice a new strength, a depth, because no longer did one woman sing, many sang, and what came out of Modesta's mouth was the dammed-up singing of a whole line of women who had never dared, loosed at last, given at last, heard at last. And perhaps it was for this that the village was so moved, because it felt, without knowing, that in that voice there was more than one woman, there was an old liberation, the singing of many who had never sung, and that was heard, at last, in the voice of the one who had dared.

There was an instant, in the middle of the solo, when Modesta felt the fear want to come back, felt the throat want to shut, the old tightness rising; and in that instant, instead of yielding to it, she remembered the master, turned still more toward giving, sang still more outward, for the village, for the master, for the dead mother, for the girl of the choir, for all, and the fear, conquered by the giving, drew back, and the voice, instead of shutting, opened still more. And it was that instant, that of feeling the fear come back in the middle of the singing and of conquering it by going on giving, that was the true victory, more than the solo itself, because it was the proof that she now knew, at last, to conquer the fear in the very act, in the heat of the moment, not before nor after but during, turning toward giving each time the fear wished to shut her. And the voice came out to the end, whole, because Modesta, at each threat of the fear, answered with more giving, and the giving conquered the fear, note by note, to the end of the solo.

When she finished, there was a silence, and then the whole village reacted, moved, and Modesta, standing, at her height, heard what she had never heard: the acknowledgement of having given a beautiful thing, not the envy she had feared, but the gratitude of those who received a present. There was, in the village, after that solo, a change in the way they looked at Modesta, and it was a change that taught her. The village, which had always seen her as the discreet one, the humble one, the one who did not draw notice, came to see her with a new admiration, and Modesta, who had feared that they would think her contrived for having risen, discovered that the admiration had nothing of the censure she expected: it was a grateful admiration, that of one who received a beautiful thing and is grateful to the one who gave it. And she understood that she had been mistaken about what the village would do if she rose: she had imagined envy, censure, the look at the star of her childhood; and the village, in its majority, had given her gratitude, joy, clean admiration. There was the envy of some, yes, which does not disappear; but the majority, on hearing the voice, did not envy, they gave thanks, because most people, on receiving a beautiful thing, give thanks instead of envying, and Modesta had organised her whole life around the envy of a minority, forgetting the gratitude of the majority. And it was that discovery, that of the majority giving thanks and only a minority envying, that finished freeing her, because it showed her that in hiding the voice to avoid the envy of a few she had deprived of her voice the gratitude of many, and that the calculation she had made her whole life, that of hiding to avoid the envy, had been a bad calculation, because she had weighed the envy of a few and forgotten the gratitude of many.

And she understood that she had been mistaken her whole life, that to stand out to give a gift does not bring the envy and the punishment the mother had taught her to fear, it brings the gratitude of those who receive; that the danger of standing out, real perhaps on a childhood day, had been a phantom the rest of her life; and that she had buried the voice a whole lifetime out of fear of a punishment that, when at last she dared, did not come, because to give a gift is not punished, it is thanked. There was, in that solo, an instant Modesta kept for ever: the instant when, in the middle of the singing, she raised her eyes, which she kept low out of habit, and looked at the village face to face, singing. Her whole life she had sung with lowered eyes, even in the choir, because to raise the eyes was to face the looks, was to expose herself; and in that solo, at a certain point, without deciding it, she raised her eyes and looked at the church, the people, the faces turned toward her, and sang looking at them, face to face, with her head raised and her eyes lifted. And it was an instant of an enormous daring, for her, to look at the village face to face while singing, and instead of the punishment she expected she found, in the faces turned toward her, not the mockery of the look at the star of her childhood, but the emotion of those who receive a beautiful thing, the eyes of many brimming, the faces open; and Modesta, on seeing those moved faces instead of the mocking faces she had feared, understood, in that instant, with her eyes lifted, that the world was not what the fear had told her, that the faces turned toward one who rises are not mocking faces, they are, in the majority, faces of gratitude, and that she could look at the world face to face, singing, without the punishment she had learned to fear.

And it was that, perhaps, the greater discovery: that the village did not cut her down for singing, it applauded her; that the world did not punish her for rising, it thanked her; and that the fear that had made her hide herself a whole lifetime was, after all, unfounded, or almost, and that she could have sung her whole life and received, her whole life, this gratitude, instead of the silence in which she had buried herself.
