## From the register of Véspera

In the record of the village, in the hand of the Keepers who have kept the book, the following is found:

Modesta, daughter of Augusta and of Véspera folk. She had the best voice the choir of the village ever knew, a voice that made whoever heard it stop, and she sang it her whole life inward, in an undertone, hidden, leaving the solos to others, smothering what she had of best so as not to be noticed.

The name was a promise, of the Véspera kind: her mother wanted her the name of modesty, dreaming of a daughter demure, humble, who would not stand out, because in the village and in her time to stand out, and even more in a woman, was a thing that was feared. And life fulfilled the name beyond what the mother had dreamed: it gave a woman who made herself so small that she almost disappeared, who spent her life stooped like one who passes through low doors, lowering her head so as not to be noticed, without ever understanding that she was taller than the doors she stooped beneath, and that the gift she buried to protect herself was, after all, the only thing she had been given to rise with.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs one who has a gift and buries it out of fear of standing out, nor the true height of one who spent their life stooped making themselves small. There are those who waste what they received, not out of laziness, but out of fear, and die with their voice unsung. For those, books such as this one are needed.

This is a novel of the Fountain Square, like the others on this shelf, but the reverse of them: not the one who runs too much nor the one who lives another's life, but the one who makes herself too small and buries what she has out of fear of standing out. Whoever has ever had a gift and not shown it, whoever falls silent where they could speak, whoever shrinks where they could rise, whoever confuses the fear of being noticed with humility, that one will recognise themselves in Modesta, and will learn with her what she learned late: that a gift is to be given and not to be buried, that to hide is not humility it is fear, and that one can rise to one's full height and sing at full voice without ceasing to be humble, because true humility is not to make oneself small, it is to give what one has without thinking oneself better for having it.
