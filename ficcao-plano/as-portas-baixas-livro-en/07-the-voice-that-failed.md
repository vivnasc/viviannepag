## 7. The voice that failed

There was a moment that took Modesta to the bottom, and it was a moment when the voice failed her when she most wanted to have it, when the fear, trained a whole lifetime, betrayed her in the hour when she, for the first time, nearly dared to sing.

The master's illness was, for Modesta, one more warning of the time that narrowed, because with the master went the only person who knew what she had, who had heard her sing in an undertone and knew the voice that hid there. While the master lived, there was at least one person in the world who knew Modesta's gift, who witnessed it, who kept it in memory; with the master's death, that witness would be lost, and Modesta would be left with her gift completely secret, with no one who even knew it existed. And that idea, that of losing the only witness of her gift, gave Modesta a new urgency, that of singing for the master before he went, of giving him, before the witness was gone, the proof of what he had always known; because there is something unbearable in thinking that the last one who knows what we have is going to depart, and that with him departs the very knowledge of our gift, and Modesta could not bear the idea of the master dying without having heard, at last, the whole voice he had awaited, of letting him depart with her gift still unproven.

Master Apolinário was old, and he fell ill, and it was understood that it was his last season at the head of the choir, perhaps his last festival. And the master, knowing himself to be ending, made Modesta a last request, the request of a man who is going to depart: sing, Modesta, before I go. Give me the joy, before I leave this choir, of hearing your voice sing alone, even once. It is the only thing I ask of you. And Modesta, moved by the old master's request, by the imminence of his departure, felt, for the first time in her life, the will to say yes, to sing, to give the master, before he went, the solo he had asked of her her whole life. And she said yes. She said, at last, yes, and the master rejoiced like a child, and it was set: at the next festival, Modesta would sing a solo, her voice, alone, heard by the village, for the master before he departed.

The days before the festival were, for Modesta, of an agitation she did not know. On one hand, the joy of having said yes, of going at last to sing, of giving the master what he asked; on the other, the fear growing as the day drew near, the old fear of standing out, now multiplied by the responsibility of not failing the master. And Modesta spent those days between the joy and the dread, rehearsing the voice at home, where it came out beautiful, and imagining the church full, where she feared it would not come out; and the nearer the day drew, the more the dread grew over the joy, until, on the eve, it was almost only dread, the dread of one who promised a thing they do not know whether they will manage to keep. She should have understood, in that growing dread, that she was not ready, that a voice held for a lifetime does not loose itself at a festival because it was promised; but the hope, and the love for the master, and the shame of going back on it, led her to go, despite the dread, and it was with all the dread upon her, badly slept, with her throat already tight from the eve, that Modesta came to the day of the festival.

And the day came, and Modesta rose to sing, and it was there that the voice failed her. Because a whole lifetime of fear is not undone with a good intention, and when Modesta found herself there, standing, alone, before the full church, with all eyes on her, about to stand out as she had not stood out since the day she caught it as a girl, the fear of a whole lifetime came all at once, rose up her throat, tightened her chest, and the voice, the beautiful voice she had, did not come out, or came out held, small, trembling, a shadow of the voice it was, smothered by the fear that shut her throat. Modesta, who sang like an angel when no one heard her, when at last everyone heard her could not sing, because the fear, trained a lifetime to hide the voice, hid it from her now too, in the hour when she wanted to show it, and the voice that failed was a humiliation and a defeat, before the village, before the master, before herself.

There was, in the humiliation of that festival, a detail that made everything worse: the silence of the village after the voice had failed her. There was no mockery, there were no laughs, which would perhaps have been easier to bear; there was a constrained silence, a silence of pity, the silence of those who watch someone fail and do not know where to put their eyes. And that silence of pity was, for Modesta, the confirmation of her worst fear: that she was not good for rising, that to try had been a mistake, that her place was the undertone and that she had dared to leave it only to make a spectacle and give pity. She went back to her bench, in the choir, with her head lower than ever, and the rest of the festival was a torment, there, in sight of everyone, after having failed in sight of everyone, and Modesta swore to herself, on that bench, never again, that she would not try again, that she had learned the lesson, that her place was the undertone and that she had been mad to want to leave it. And she came out of that festival determined to bury the voice for good, deeper than ever, convinced that the voice that failed had proved that the mother was right, that to stand out was not for her, that she should resign herself.

There were, after the festival, days when she did not even want to go back to the choir, out of shame, and when she thought of ceasing to sing for good, even in an undertone, of giving up the little singing she had, because the humiliation had been so great that the choir itself, her refuge, had become a place of shame. And it was perhaps that the lowest point, the temptation to give up not only the solo, which she had never had, but the choir, the little she had, to fall silent completely, to bury the voice for good, deeper still, now that to try to loose it had landed her on her face. Modesta was near doing that, of falling silent forever, of leaving the choir, of accepting that the voice was a curse and not a gift and that it was best to have no voice at all; and it was only because the choir, and the voice, even in an undertone, were the only joy of her life that she did not give up entirely, that she went back, with her head low, to singing beneath, because even humiliated she could not live without singing at all, even if in an undertone. And that inability to give up the singing entirely, even after the humiliation, was, without her knowing it, the thread that kept her tied to the voice, the thread by which, later, the voice would be able to loose itself again, because one who gives up the singing entirely does not sing again, and Modesta, for not having managed to give up entirely, kept the thread that would save her.

And there was, in that humiliation, the pain of having failed the master. Because the master had asked her for that as a last wish, and she had wanted to give it to him, and had not managed it, and the old master was going to depart without having heard the voice he had awaited his whole life to hear, and Modesta felt that she had failed him, that she had failed the only one who had always believed in her, and that guilt, that of having failed the master, joined the public humiliation, and Modesta came out of that festival at the bottom, more hidden than ever, convinced that to try to sing had been a mistake, that she was not good for standing out indeed, that her childhood lesson was right and that she should go back to the undertone and never again try to rise.

It must be understood what happened in Modesta's throat at that festival, because it was physical and it was revealing. She rose determined, with the will to sing for the master; but on seeing the church full, all eyes on her, she felt the old fear rise, and the fear, in the body, did what it does: it tightened her throat, dried her mouth, took her air. And when she wanted to sing, the tight throat did not let the voice come out as it should; there came out a small thing, held, trembling, a shadow of the voice, because the voice needs an open throat and air, and the fear had shut her throat and taken her air. It was not that Modesta did not know how to sing, or that the voice was not there; it was that the body, taken by the fear, did not let it come out, shut the instrument. And it was terrifying for Modesta to understand this, that the fear was not only an idea in the head, it was a thing in the body, a throat that shut on its own, an instrument the fear locked, and that she did not command it, that the fear commanded it, and that to want to sing was not enough if the body, out of fear, refused to let her.

It must be said that the master, on seeing the voice fail Modesta at that festival, did not grow angry nor disillusioned, and that was important. Modesta expected the master's disillusion, whom she had failed; and the master, instead, looked at her with an understanding that disarmed her, because he knew the fear, had seen it in many voices, and knew that the voice that failed had been neither the fault nor the weakness of Modesta, it had been the fear, which is not conquered with a good intention. And it was that understanding of the master's, instead of the disillusion she feared, that prevented Modesta from giving up for good that night: because if the master had reproached her, or had been disillusioned, Modesta would have had the confirmation that she had failed, that she was not good enough, and would have buried herself forever; and the master, in not reproaching her, in understanding her, in going on believing in her despite the voice that failed, kept open the door that the humiliation had nearly shut, and it was by that door, that the master kept open with his understanding, that Modesta, later, tried again.

But it was also at the bottom of that voice that failed that something broke in Modesta, and opened her. Because the humiliation was so great, the failing of the master so painful, that Modesta was forced to look, at last, face to face, at her prison, and to ask herself whether she was really going to let the fear defeat her for good, whether she was going to let the master depart without hearing her, whether she was going to go back to the undertone and die with her voice unsung, or whether she was, at last, going to fight the fear, not with a good intention, which had not been enough, but in earnest, with help, with work, to manage, before it was too late, to loose the voice the fear held from her. There was a night, after the failed festival, when Modesta wept as she had not wept in a long while, and the weeping was for a precise thing: for the girl who sang free, far back, before the day she caught it. She wept for that girl, for the voice she had been able to loose without fear, for the joy she had had singing before she learned to fear; and she wept for all that that girl could have been, the singer who could have given her voice to the village a whole lifetime, and who had been cut down, silenced, shrunken, in a day. It was a mourning, that weeping, the mourning of the girl who sang free and of the woman she could have been had they not taught her to fear; and it was a necessary mourning, because Modesta had never wept that loss, never wept the voice she had buried, and there are mournings one must make to be able to go on. In weeping the cut-down girl, Modesta began, without knowing it, to do her justice, to acknowledge what they had taken from her, and to acknowledge what was lost is the beginning of trying to recover it; and it was from that weeping for the cut-down girl that there was born, beneath the despair, a new and stubborn will: that of not letting the girl who sang free die for good without ever singing again, that of giving her, at last, before the end, the voice they had silenced.

And it was that question, born at the bottom of the voice that failed, that took her, at last, in search of help, to the House of the Register, to the Keeper, in search of a way to conquer a fear she could no longer conquer alone.
