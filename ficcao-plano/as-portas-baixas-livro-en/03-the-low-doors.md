## 3. The low doors

Modesta spent her life lowering her head, and the image that best described her, and that gives the book its name, was that of the low doors: she lived like one who passes through low doors, always stooped, always lowering her head so as not to strike it, always making herself smaller than she was to fit without being noticed.

The image of the low doors had come to her from her own mother, who used it, and Modesta had kept it her whole life as a wisdom. The doors of this life are low, said Augusta, and one who does not lower their head breaks it. It was a phrase of one who had learned, in her life, that the world punishes one who rises, and the mother had passed it to her daughter as a counsel of survival, lower your head, pass small, do not break your head on the low doors of life. And Modesta had obeyed, her whole life, had lowered her head at every door, and had never broken her head, it is true; but she had also never passed through any door upright, had never known what it was to cross a door with her head high, had never risked discovering whether the doors were really as low as the mother said. And only very late, on beginning to rise, would Modesta discover that the mother's phrase, however much it came from a real pain, was a dangerous half-truth: because there are indeed low doors, but there are also many high ones, and one who lowers their head at all, out of fear of the low ones, spends their life stooped needlessly, and loses, in the many high doors through which they could pass upright, the dignity of walking with their head raised. The mother had taught her not to break her head; but, in teaching her to lower it at every door, she had taught her also to live stooped, and to live stooped, without ever breaking one's head, is also a way of losing it, the head, not broken but always low.

It was a way of being in the world, and it was seen in everything she did. In a conversation, Modesta never said her opinion if it was different from the others', she lowered her head, agreed, did not stand out. And it was not that she had no opinions, or that they were weak; she had them, and good ones, and often saw farther than those who spoke loud; but she kept them, swallowed them, let the others decide and say, because to say her opinion, above all if it was different, was to stand out, was to rise, was to risk. And the village lost, without knowing it, what Modesta saw and did not say, her good opinions that went unsaid; and Modesta lost the weight she could have had, the influence, the place of one who is heard, because one who does not say what they think does not count, does not weigh, is not heard, and Modesta had chosen, her whole life, not to count, not to weigh, not to be heard, in exchange for the safety of not standing out. She fell silent, and in falling silent she disappeared from the decisions, from the conversations that mattered, from the place of one who shapes things; and she went on, by falling silent, ever more to the margin, ever less present, ever more a shadow that looked on instead of a voice that took part. In work, she did well what she did but never let it be seen that she did it better than the others, she hid her knack, shared the merit, drew back from the recognition. She let others take the credit for what she did better, and even helped them take it, because to receive credit was to stand out; and she saw, often, less able people rise at the cost of what she did and did not claim, did not say this was I, did not defend what was hers, because to defend what was hers would be to rise. And she lost, over her life, much of what was hers by merit, let it go to others, not out of generosity, though it seemed so, but out of fear of claiming, of asserting herself, of saying this merit is mine. And there was in this one more waste, that of a capable woman who let her capacity bear fruit for others, who saw her merit reaped by those who did not deserve it, and who did not claim, and went on falling behind, overtaken by less capable people who did not have her fear of asserting themselves, not because she was worth less, but because she asserted herself less, and in the world, often, it is not the one who is worth most who rises but the one who asserts themselves most, and Modesta, who was worth much and asserted herself not at all, went on staying below while around her rose those who were worth less and asserted themselves more. At a festival, she stayed in the corners, did not dress to be noticed, did not speak loud, did not draw attention; she passed, in everything, small, stooped, with her head low, taking care not to stand out in anything, lest there befall her what had befallen her on the day she shone as a girl.

The image of the low doors had, for Modesta, a literal truth she only saw in the end: that there are, indeed, in life, low doors, places and moments in which one must lower one's head, in which to stand out really costs dear, in which prudence orders one not to rise. The mother had not invented the low doors; they exist. The mistake was not the mother warning her that there are low doors; the mistake was teaching her to pass stooped through all of them, as though all were low, when only some are. A good mother would teach her daughter to distinguish the low doors from the high, to lower her head at the low and to raise it at the high; Modesta's mother, out of fear, taught her to lower her head at all, and so condemned her to pass stooped her whole life, even through the high doors, even where she could pass upright. And Modesta went her whole life confusing them, lowering herself before all doors for not knowing how to distinguish the low from the high, and it was that confusion, and not the existence of low doors, her problem; because there are indeed low doors, and it is wise to lower one's head at them, but it is a prison to lower it at all, and Modesta lived in that prison, that of one who stoops before everything for not knowing, or not daring, to distinguish what is really low from what only seems so.

And the saddest is that Modesta was, beneath the smallness, a great woman: not only in the voice, but in everything, she was a woman of knack, of intelligence, of goodness, of a richness that hid itself all behind the lowered head. She had good opinions she did not say, talents she did not show, a light she smothered, and those who knew her close, the few to whom she let it be seen, knew that there was there a woman much greater than the one the village saw, a woman hidden behind a fabricated smallness. Modesta was like one of those houses modest on the outside that hide, within, large and rich rooms; and almost no one entered enough to see the rooms, because the small façade kept them off, and Modesta saw to it that the façade was as small as possible, so that no one would suspect what was within.

There was a thing Modesta did and that was the height of her making-herself-small: she apologised for existing. She apologised when she passed, excuse me, by your leave, as though to take up space were a fault; she apologised before speaking, excuse me, perhaps it is of no importance, as though her opinion were a bother; she shrank in places, made herself narrow, took up the least possible, like one who begs pardon for being there. And that apologising for existing, that taking up the minimum of space and of attention, was the deepest form of her making-herself-small, deeper than the hidden voice, because it was a making-small not only of the gift but of the very right to be, to take up a place, to exist at ease. Modesta lived like one who is not quite sure they have the right to be, and so she apologised, shrank, made herself minimal, and that was perhaps the thing it would most cost her to unlearn, more than to let out the voice: to learn to take up her space without apologising, to be at ease in the world, to think that she had the right to be, to take up a place, to exist at her height and her breadth, without apologising for it.

The doors she stooped beneath were, many of them, she would understand later, doors that were not even low. Modesta lowered her head out of habit, out of fear, even before doors through which she could pass with her head raised; she shrank in situations that did not require shrinking; she made herself small before people who did not threaten her, on occasions in which she could shine without cost. The fear of standing out had become so automatic that she no longer distinguished the low doors, where it was indeed necessary to lower the head, from the normal doors, through which she could pass whole; she lowered herself before all, out of precaution, out of habit, and so she spent her life stooped even where she could rise, small even where she could be great, hidden even where she could shine without danger.

There was a cost in the relationships, too, because a woman who hides herself hides herself also from those who want to know her, and Modesta had been left, over her life, with few close ones, not for lack of those who wanted her, but because she did not let herself be known. One who makes themselves small, who lowers their head, who hides what they have, gives little to take hold of to one who wants to draw near; and Modesta, in hiding herself, drove away without wishing it those who would want her near, because no one draws near to one who effaces themselves, who does not show themselves, who is always drawing back. There were those who wanted her for a friend and gave up, tired of a woman who never opened up; there were, perhaps, those who wanted to love her and did not know how, before a woman who made herself so small that she was barely seen. And Modesta was left more alone than she needed, not for being unlovable, but for hiding herself too much, and that solitude was one more price of her making-herself-small, the price of one who effaces themselves so much that others, not seeing them, cannot love them.

And that permanent stooping, at the end of a life, had entered her body and her soul. Modesta walked a little stooped, indeed, with sloping shoulders, with her head low, like one who grew used to passing through low doors and no longer straightens up; and the soul was like the body, stooped, shrunken, used to being small, no longer able to rise even when she wished, because the stooping of a whole life is not undone by wishing, it ingrains itself, becomes the form of the body and of the soul, and to straighten up, if she ever tried it, would hurt, as it hurts to straighten a thing that grew crooked.

There was a word the village used for Modesta and that she received as a praise and that was, at bottom, her sentence: discreet. Modesta is so discreet, they said, so demure, she is not noticed, she does not meddle where she is not called, she does not speak loud, she does not show off. And Modesta liked to be called discreet, because it was what she had learned to be, what the mother had taught her to be, and to be praised for it confirmed to her that she did well to efface herself. But the discretion, in Modesta's case, was not the virtue the word suggests; it was an effacement, a disappearance, an absence. To be discreet can be an elegance, that of one who does not impose themselves; but in Modesta it was another thing, it was a hiding, a not-existing, and the word discreet, which seemed to praise her, in truth masked her disappearance, gave a beautiful name to her effacement, and helped her to go on effaced by calling virtue what was fear. And it was also for this that Modesta took so long to see her problem: because the village disguised it as a virtue, called her hiding discretion, her fear demureness, her waste humility, and one who is praised for their own ill takes long to see it as an ill.

Whoever saw her from outside would hardly guess all this, because Modesta functioned, lived, did her duty, and her smallness did not have the dramatic look of a disaster; it had the banal look of a discreet life, of a demure woman, of a good person who is not noticed. And it was that, the banal look of her drama, that made it invisible and so the sadder: because a visible disaster attracts compassion, help, attention; and Modesta's, invisible, banal, with the look of a virtue, attracted nothing, no one saw that there was there a waste, a life lived below what it could be, a buried gift; they saw only a discreet woman, and even praised her. And Modesta carried her drama alone, without anyone seeing it as a drama, with the added burden of being praised precisely for what diminished her; and that solitude, that of suffering an ill that others take for a virtue, that of not even having someone to acknowledge that there is an ill there, was perhaps the worst of her making-herself-small, because an acknowledged ill can be wept, shared, treated, and an ill disguised as a virtue is lived alone, silent, without even the right to call it an ill.

And there was, at the bottom of everything, a waste that was the silent tragedy of Modesta: the waste of a life lived small by a woman who was great, the waste of a voice unsung, of opinions unsaid, of a light unshown, all buried by a childhood fear, all hidden behind a lowered head. And no one wept that waste, because no one saw it, because Modesta hid so well what she had that no one knew what was being wasted; only she, at bottom, knew, in the rare moments when she allowed herself to feel the weight of what she buried, the dull sadness of a woman who knows she has more to give than she ever gave and that the fear does not let her give, the sadness of a voice that is heard within and that is known to be beautiful and that never, never let itself come out.
