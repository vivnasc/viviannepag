## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a woman, in Véspera, who had the best voice of the choir and who only at the end learned to sing it outward.

Modesta, daughter of Augusta, the one who hid a whole lifetime a gift — the best voice the village choir had known —, singing it in an undertone, giving the solos to others, making herself small so as not to stand out, because she had learned as a girl, on a day when to shine cost her dear, that to stand out brings punishment. She spent her life stooped like one who passes through low doors, without seeing that she was taller than the doors before which she lowered herself, and that the fear that stooped her was not hers, it was the mother's. It was needed that the voice fail her when she most wanted it, and a Keeper, and an old master dying, for her to understand what no one had taught her: that a gift is to be given and not to be buried, that to hide the voice was not humility, it was fear and waste, and that one can sing at full voice and be humble, if one sings to give and not to boast.

It was further recorded that, in rising, she opened the way to other hidden voices of the village, and that she made of the choir, in the time that remained to her, a place where the gifts were no longer silenced; and that, in loosing her voice, she loosed also, in a certain way, the mother's and that of all the silenced women of her line, being the first, among them, to sing at full voice.

It was recorded that she learned to rise: to loose the voice, to pass through the doors with her head raised, to take up her true size. And that she sang, at last, the solo for the village, at full voice, and that the village, instead of the punishment she had feared, gave her the gratitude of those who receive a present. And that she said, singing raised: this voice is mine, and they did not give it me to silence it.

And let it be said, for whoever reads and recognises themselves, that this is not a story against humility, but rather in favour of it well understood. Modesta did not trade the humility for vanity; she traded a false humility, which was fear hiding the gift, for a true one, which is to give the gift without arrogance. The lesson is not to cease being humble; it is to understand that true humility does not hide what one received, it uses it in the service of others, and that to hide a gift out of fear of standing out is no virtue at all, it is waste and it is fear. Whoever has a gift, let them give it, with simplicity, without boasting; and whoever hides out of fear, let them rise, let them sing, let them take up their height, because what they received was not given them to bury.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs one who has a gift to conquer the fear of showing it, nor the true size of one who spent their life making themselves small. There are those who bury what they received, not out of laziness, but out of fear, and die with the voice unsung; and there are those who, in time, rise and sing it. For those a book like this is needed. And of all of it a record was made.
