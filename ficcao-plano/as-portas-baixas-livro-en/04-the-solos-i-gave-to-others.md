## 4. The solos I gave to others

The village choir was the place where Modesta's tragedy was best seen, because it was there, in the choir, that she had the gift and hid it, that she had the best voice and gave the solos to others, and it was there that the waste happened, week after week, in sight of whoever knew how to see.

The village choir sang at the festivals, at the mournings, on the great occasions, and was, in Véspera, a beloved institution, the voice of the community in its moments. And Modesta loved to belong to it, loved to be part of that collective voice, and the choir gave her that, a place where her voice, even hidden, served, contributed, was part of a beautiful thing. But the choir was also, for Modesta, the stage of her waste, because it was there, where her voice could most shine, that she most hid it; it was there, in the place made for voices, that the best voice fell silent. And there was an irony in the place Modesta most loved, the choir, being also the place where she most diminished herself, where she most clearly gave others what was hers; as though the love for the choir and the fear of standing out crossed there, in the same place, making of the choir at once her greatest joy and her greatest waste, the place where she most lived her passion and where she most repressed it.

The choir's conductor was a man who knew voices, master Apolinário, a man already of age who had directed the village choir for decades and who knew each voice as a shepherd knows each sheep. And master Apolinário knew, better than anyone, what Modesta had: he knew that that voice, hidden among the others, was the best that had passed through the choir in all his life as a conductor, a rare voice, a voice one hears few times in a lifetime. And he suffered, the master, to see that voice hidden, to see Modesta sing in an undertone, to see the solos go to worse voices while the best dissolved in the choir; he suffered as one who knows an art suffers on seeing a gift wasted, and tried, time after time, to make Modesta let out the voice, to give her a solo, to set her to singing alone.

Modesta's refusals always had the same form, and the master knew it by heart. It was not a dry no, an I do not want to; it was a humble no, a there are better voices, an I am not able, an I prefer the choir, said with her head low, with a modesty that disarmed, because how does one insist with one who refuses out of humility? And that was the involuntary cunning of Modesta's refusal: that it dressed itself in humility, and humility is hard to contradict, because to insist with a humble person seems almost to force them to be vain. The master understood that Modesta's humility was, at bottom, fear, but he had no way of proving it, no way of telling her that is not humility, it is fear, without wounding her; and so Modesta's humble refusal was almost impregnable, protected by the appearance of virtue, and the master, time after time, drew back before it, defeated not by an arrogant stubbornness, which would be easy to contradict, but by a humble stubbornness, which was impossible. And Modesta, without knowing it, used the humility as a shield, hid behind it, protected the fear with the appearance of virtue, and no one could take it from her because no one wanted to force a humble person to cease being one.

And time after time Modesta refused. The master called her, told her Modesta, this solo is for you, this voice is yours, let it come out; and Modesta lowered her eyes, shrank, said no, that she was not able, that there were better voices, that she preferred the choir, and refused, always, with a mild but unshakeable stubbornness, the stubbornness of fear. And the master, who could not force her, gave the solo to another, with grief, and Modesta sang beneath, relieved, and the master looked at her and shook his head, because he saw what was lost, saw the best voice in the village hide itself, and could not understand why a person with that gift buried it, why the best voice hid behind the worse ones.

The choir was, for Modesta, a refuge and a prison at the same time, and that contradiction defined her relationship with singing. A refuge, because there, among the other voices, she could sing, could let out a little the voice she so loved, hidden in the whole, without the danger of standing out; the choir gave her a place where she could sing in safety, diluted, and she loved that place, she went to the rehearsals, to the Masses, to the festivals, because the choir was the only place where her voice, even in an undertone, could exist. But a prison too, because the choir was also where she kept the voice held, where she diluted it so as not to stand out, where she gave the solos to others; the same place that gave her the singing was the one that limited it to the undertone. And Modesta lived that contradiction without resolving it: she loved the choir because it let her sing, and the choir kept her small because it let her sing only hidden; and she clung to the refuge without seeing that it was also the prison, without seeing that the choir, which seemed to her the place where the voice lived, was in truth the place where the voice was kept held, by giving her the comfort of singing diluted and by sparing her, with that comfort, the need to let it out alone.

There was a time when master Apolinário, already tired of seeing the waste, went deeper, and said to Modesta a thing she kept, even without following it at the time. He said: Modesta, God does not give a voice like this to a person for them to hide it. A voice like this is a gift, and a gift is to be given, not to be buried. You, in hiding that voice, are not being humble, you are wasting what was given you, and to waste a gift is an ingratitude toward the one who gave it. Humility is not to hide what one has of good; it is to use it without boasting, it is to put it at the service of others without thinking oneself better for it. You could give this village, this church, the joy of a voice like yours, and you are denying it to them out of fear, and that is not called humility, it is called burying the talent. The master had a word for what Modesta did, and used it sometimes, with a severe tenderness: to bury the talent. It came from an old story, that of the servant who received a talent and, instead of making it yield, buried it in the earth so as not to lose it, and who was rebuked for it, because the talent was to yield and not to bury. And the master told Modesta that she did that, that she buried her talent, the voice, out of fear of risking it, and that that was neither prudence nor humility, it was the fault of the servant of the story, that of one who receives a gift and hides it instead of making it yield. And Modesta, who knew the story, was touched, because she understood that it was indeed that she did, that she had received a talent and buried it, and that if there was one thing the story condemned it was precisely what she did, to hide the gift instead of giving it. But even touched she did not change, because between knowing that one buries the talent and having the courage to unbury it and risk it there is a distance that only fear knows, and Modesta lived on that side of the distance, that of one who knows they bury the talent and does not yet have the courage to unbury it.

And Modesta heard, and it hurt her, because there was there a truth she felt but did not want to see: that to hide the voice was not the virtue she thought, it was a waste, and that what she called humility was, at bottom, fear, and a fear that deprived others of a beautiful thing she could give.

There was a cruel irony in the solos Modesta gave to others: that the voices to whom she ceded them knew, many of them, that they did not deserve them, that there was there, in the choir, a voice better than theirs that hid itself, and some even felt bad about it, or took advantage. There were those who, knowing that Modesta would never accept, offered themselves for the solos with an ease that came from knowing that the best voice would not compete; there were those who shone at the cost of Modesta's silence, received the applause that would be hers if she sang, occupied the place she left free out of fear. And Modesta saw this, saw voices worse than hers receive the applause that could be hers, and felt no envy, or did not let herself feel it, because the envy too would be a form of wanting to stand out; but she felt, beneath, a sadness, the sadness of seeing her place occupied by others, her gift wasted while lesser gifts shone, and that sadness was one more she swallowed, one more she hid, because even the sadness of not singing she had to hide, lest someone understand that she wanted to sing and push her toward the standing-out she feared.

But even so she did not sing. Even with the master's truth hurting within her, Modesta went on refusing the solos, singing in an undertone, giving others what was hers, because the fear was stronger than the truth, and between the waste and the danger of standing out, Modesta always chose the waste, because the waste was safe and the standing-out was dangerous. There was a conversation, between Modesta and the master, that she would recall much later, in which the master asked her a simple thing: do you like to sing, Modesta? And Modesta answered, without thinking, the truth: it is what I most like to do in life. And the master, then, said: and you do what you most like to do in life in an undertone, hidden, fearfully. Does it not seem to you a waste, to do what you most love with fear, instead of doing it with joy? And Modesta was left without an answer, because the master had put his finger on the wound: that she lived her greatest passion, singing, not with the joy a passion deserves, but with the fear that spoiled the passion; that she did what she most loved with bound hands, containing herself, hiding herself, instead of giving herself to it entire. And she understood, with the master's question, that the fear not only prevented her from showing the voice to others, but spoiled for her herself the joy of singing, because one cannot fully love a thing one does with fear, and Modesta, in singing with fear, deprived herself not only of giving the voice, but of enjoying herself the singing she so loved, and that, the fear spoiling her very passion, was perhaps the most intimate waste of all.

Each village festival was, for Modesta, a small torture disguised as joy. A joy, because she loved to sing, loved the festivals, loved to be part of the choir on the great occasions; a torture, because each festival brought the solos, and the solos brought the refusal, and the refusal brought the confirmation of her fear, and seeing her voice hidden while another, worse, shone. And Modesta went to the festivals with that mixture, the joy of singing and the torture of hiding, and came out of them with the joy of having sung and the sadness of not having dared, always the two things, always halfway between the enjoyment and the waste. So was Modesta's life in the choir, years and years of festivals lived halfway, of joys spoiled by the sadness of not daring, of songs enjoyed in an undertone and never loosed; and that life lived halfway, neither entirely glad nor entirely sad, always suspended between what she had and what she did not dare to give, was perhaps the most tiring of all, because a whole sadness is wept and passes, but a life lived halfway drags on, without the relief either of the full enjoyment or of the full mourning, always in the lukewarmness of what could be and is not.

And the years went on passing, and the solos went on going all to others, and Modesta went on growing old in the choir, always the best voice, always hidden, and master Apolinário went on growing old too, and one day he would retire or die, and with him went the only person who knew what Modesta had, and Modesta would be left with her gift completely secret, known to no one, on its way to being lost for good without ever having been heard.

Master Apolinário had, with Modesta, a patience that was love, and he never forced her, though he could, though he had the authority of a conductor to impose a solo on her. He understood that to force did not serve, that a voice held by fear does not loose itself by force, but rather shuts itself more; and so he went on trying slowly, year after year, with an invitation here, a word there, without ever pushing too much, awaiting that Modesta, one day, would loose herself of her own will. It was a gardener's patience, the master's, that of one who knows that a plant does not grow faster by being pulled, and who waters and waits; and the master watered and waited, year after year, Modesta's voice, without seeing it flower, but without giving up watering it, because he believed that that voice was worth the wait, that one day it would flower. And it was perhaps that patience of the master's, that faith he had in her voice for years without return, that most moved Modesta when, at last, she loosed herself: to understand that there had been, her whole life, someone who believed in her voice, who awaited it, who never gave up on it, and that she had nearly let that someone die without giving him what he had awaited, the joy of hearing her sing.

And that was, Modesta understood in her moments of lucidity, the direction in which she went: that of dying with her voice unsung, that of carrying to the grave the best voice in the village without the village ever having heard it, that of wasting entirely a gift that had been given her, and all for a childhood fear, all because of a day when to shine had cost her dear. And that direction frightened her, in the moments when she saw it, but not enough to change it, because to change was to rise, and to rise was what she did not know how to do, what she had learned to fear more than anything, and so she went on toward the waste, giving the solos to others, awaiting, without knowing it, something that would force her, at last, to sing.
