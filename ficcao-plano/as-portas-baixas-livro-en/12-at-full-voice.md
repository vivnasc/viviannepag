## 12. At full voice

Master Apolinário departed shortly after that festival, and he departed, it was said, in peace, because he had heard at last the voice he had awaited, and Modesta was left with the loosed voice and with a new life before her, a life in which she sang, at last, at full voice, and in which she rose, at last, to her height.

The master, before departing, left Modesta a thing she only understood afterwards: he named her, in a certain way, his successor in what most mattered, not in the baton, for that was another thing, but in the care for the voices. He said to her, in one of the last conversations, now it is you who stay to care for the voices of this village, Modesta, you who know what a hidden voice costs and what a loosed voice is worth. And Modesta received that as a charge and an honour, and it was perhaps that, that charge left by the master, that most kept her raised after he departed, because she no longer sang only for herself, she sang also for a charge, that of caring for the voices of the village, of honouring the master by continuing what he had done. And a person who has a charge, a mission, rises better than one who has only themselves, because the charge pulls them upward, gives them a reason greater than their own fear; and Modesta, with the master's charge, with the mission of caring for the voices, had a reason greater than her fear to keep herself raised, to go on singing, not to go back to the undertone, and it was that reason, that of honouring the master and caring for the voices, that held her raised in the time when, without it, she might have drawn back.

Because after that solo Modesta did not go back to the undertone. She had tasted, at that festival, the joy of loosing the voice and the gratitude of those who received it, and she had seen that the punishment she had feared did not come, and that freed her for good, or almost, of the fear of a whole lifetime. She came to sing the solos, in the choir, her voice heard, given, and the village grew used to her, to Modesta's beautiful voice, and came to await it, to ask for it, and Modesta, who had hidden the voice a whole life, discovered, in the end, the joy of giving it, of being the voice of the village, of giving her people, at the festivals, at the mournings, on the occasions, the beauty she had hidden so long. And it was as though the village had gained, suddenly, a voice it had always had and never heard, and Modesta, in giving that voice, recovered, late but recovered, the years of singing she had lost, delivering now, all at once, the message she had kept a lifetime.

Modesta became, with time, a kind of mistress for other hidden voices, as master Apolinário had been for her. Because there were, in the choir and in the village, others as she had been, people with a gift and with a fear of showing it, voices in an undertone, buried talents; and Modesta, who knew that prison from within, recognised them, and drew near to them, and told them what she had learned late: that the gift is to be given, that to hide is not humility, that one can sing at full voice and be humble, that the fear of standing out lies. And to some she perhaps spared what she herself had suffered, years of undertone, the waste of a gift; and it was that, perhaps, the most beautiful thing she did with her recovered voice: not only to sing it, but to help other hidden voices loose themselves, to pass on what the master had given her, to be for others the voice that pushes them to sing. And there was a justice in this, that of the woman who had hidden the voice a whole lifetime becoming, in the end, the one who helped others not to hide it, transforming her story of silence into a lesson of singing, her old waste into a light for those who still hid themselves.

And she rose in everything, not only in the voice. The woman who had gone stooped straightened up, in body and in soul; she came to say her opinions, to show her knack, to take up her place, to be her true size, which was great, instead of the small size she had fabricated out of fear. The young girl of the choir, the one who had begun to hide herself as Modesta had hidden herself, was among the first to change on seeing Modesta rise, and that gave Modesta a particular joy. Because the girl, on seeing Modesta, who had always been the hidden one, loose at last the voice and be loved for it, understood that one could, that there was not the punishment she feared, and began herself too to loose herself, to sing louder, to accept standing out; and Modesta saw in the girl the effect of her example, saw that in rising she had taught another to rise, that her courage had given courage, and understood that this was one of the most beautiful fruits of having risen: not only to sing herself, but to open the way for others to sing, to break, with her example, the chain of fear that passed from woman to woman in the village. The mother had taught her to hide herself, and she had nearly passed that teaching on, to the girl, by the example of her own hiding; and now, in rising, she passed on the contrary teaching, she taught the girl to rise by the example of her own rising, and so she broke, in the girl, the chain the mother had put in her, gave the next generation what hers had not given her, the permission to sing aloud.

And the village discovered, with astonishment, that the humble and effaced Modesta was, after all, a rich woman, full, great, who had hidden all that a whole lifetime behind a lowered head; and Modesta, in rising, in showing herself, did not lose the esteem of the village, she gained it, because the village esteems one who gives what they have, and Modesta, in giving at last what she had, the voice, the knack, the light, became more loved and not less, contrary to what the fear had taught her.

There was a joy, in that new life, that Modesta did not expect and that surprised her: that of discovering that to give the voice made her happy. Her whole life she had thought that to hide the voice was the safe, the comfortable, and that to show it would be the danger, the discomfort; and she discovered, on showing it, that it was the contrary, that to give the voice was a joy, among the greatest she had known, and that to hide it had been, at bottom, a sadness, the dull sadness of a whole lifetime that she had taken for safety. To sing outward, to give her voice, to see the joy of those who heard it, was a new happiness, and Modesta, on tasting it, saw how much she had lost in hiding it, how many years of joy she had traded for a safety that was, after all, sadness. And there was a grief, in that discovery, the grief of the lost years, of all the joy of singing she had not lived out of fear; but there was also a gratitude, that of having discovered, before dying, that joy, of not having died without knowing it, of having still years before her to live it. And Modesta decided to live those years singing, giving the voice, gathering the joy she had hidden so long, recovering, in the time that remained to her, the most of the joy of singing that the fear had robbed her of a whole lifetime.

She thought of the mother, in that time, of Augusta, and made peace with her, in the possible way. She understood that the mother had taught her to lower her head out of love and out of fear, to protect her from a danger that had been real in the mother's life; and instead of hating her for the prison she had put her in, she pitied her, the mother who had lived so small, so stooped, so frightened, without ever rising, without ever singing her own voice, whatever it might be. And she understood that in rising she did what the mother had never done, she broke the curvature that passed from generation to generation, and that perhaps, in rising, she honoured the mother better than if she had stayed stooped, because she gave the mother's life a new meaning: that of a curvature that, in the daughter, at last straightened, that of a fear that, in the daughter, at last was conquered, that of a voice that, in the daughter, at last sang, after generations of silenced and stooped women.

This does not mean that the fear disappeared for good, because the fears of a lifetime do not disappear, they are tamed. Modesta went on feeling, before each solo, the old tightness, the old impulse to shrink, the old look at the star sounding within; but she had learned to sing with the fear and all, not to wait for the fear to pass in order to sing, to sing despite it, turning toward giving. And she discovered that the fear, faced time after time, went on losing strength, it did not disappear but it shrank, became a voice ever lower in there, easy to silence; and that each solo sung despite the fear made the next less frightening, because it accumulated the proof that the sky did not fall, that the village did not cut her down, that on the other side of the fear was the joy of singing and not the punishment it promised. To tame the fear, Modesta understood, was not to cease feeling it, it was to cease obeying it; and she, who had obeyed the fear her whole life, learned, in the end, to feel it and to sing all the same, which is the only victory possible over an old fear, not that of extinguishing it, but that of disobeying it.

There was a thing Modesta did, in that new life, and that was her way of honouring the master: she asked to conduct, herself, the choir, or to help conduct it, and she made of the choir a place where the hidden voices were encouraged to loose themselves. Where master Apolinário had been, for her, the one who pushed her to sing, Modesta became, for the new voices, the one who pushed them, giving each hidden voice the patience and the faith the master had given her, seeking in each choir the voice that diluted itself and telling it what they had told her, let it come out, it is a gift, it is to be given. And so the village choir, under Modesta's hand, went on becoming a place where the voices no longer hid themselves, where the solos went to those who deserved them and not to those who dared, where it was taught, with Modesta's example, that a gift is to be given; and it was that, perhaps, the most beautiful work of Modesta's late life, not only to sing herself, but to transform the choir into a place of loosed voices, to pass on, to many, what the master had given her, to be for the whole village the voice that teaches one not to be afraid of singing.

This does not mean that Modesta became an ostentatious woman, and it would be to misunderstand the story to see her so, as though she had traded the humility for vanity. What changed was not that Modesta came to boast, to want to shine for the sake of shining, to seek applause; what changed was that she ceased to hide what she had, that she came to give the voice with simplicity, that she rose to her height without thinking herself above anyone. She remained humble, in the true sense: she sang to give and not to boast, she received the admiration without swelling, she put the voice at the service of the village and not at the service of her vanity. The difference between the Modesta of before and the one of after was not the difference between humble and vain; it was the difference between a false humility, which hid the gift out of fear, and a true humility, which gave the gift without arrogance. Modesta passed from a false humility to a true one, and not from humble to vain, and that distinction was important to her, who had always valued humility, and who needed to know that in rising she did not lose it, but rather found it true, because true humility had never been to hide the gift, it had always been to give it without thinking oneself better for having it.

There were those who, in the village, on seeing Modesta's change, asked her how it had been, how the woman who had hidden herself her whole life had come to sing at full voice; and Modesta, who had learned not to hide, answered with simplicity, told her story, the fear, the mother, the master, the voice that failed, the voice that at last came out. And in telling it, she understood that her story could serve others, that there were many people with the same prison, with a gift hidden out of fear, and that to tell how she had come out of hers could help others come out of theirs. And she went on becoming, without seeking it, a kind of living testimony that one could, that the fear of standing out was conquered, that it was worth loosing what one hid; and people who hid themselves drew near to her, and Modesta told them what she had learned, and saw some loose themselves, and understood that her story, that of a hidden life and of a late rising, was worth more to others than that of a life always raised would be, because one who always rose does not know how to teach one who is afraid, and she, who had had the fear and conquered it, knew, and could give her hand to one who still had it, from the place where she had once been.

And at a festival, already after the master, Modesta sang a solo, and on rising to sing she made the gesture that became hers, the gesture that was her arrival: she straightened up, raised her head, to her whole height, without lowering herself, and opened her mouth, and sang at full voice, without an undertone, without fear, without hiding herself, giving the village the voice that was hers. It did not all go straight, after that first great solo, and it would be a lie to tell it so; there were days when the fear came back, when Modesta, before a solo, felt the old will to draw back, to give the turn to another, to go back to the undertone; there was even a time or another when she drew back, when the fear won. But the difference, now, is that she already knew the way back to the voice, had already proved that she could loose it, already had the memory of the great solo and of the village's gratitude to hold on to in the days of fear; and when the fear made her draw back, instead of burying herself anew, Modesta remembered that she had managed it, that the voice was there, that it was worth it, and rose again. She did not cease to feel the fear, for the fears of a lifetime do not go; she learned to sing with it, not to obey it, to rise despite it. And she went on becoming, with time, ever more the woman who sang and ever less the one who hid herself, not because the fear disappeared, but because the courage to sing, fed by each solo that went well, went on gaining ground over the fear, until to sing was her normal and to hide herself the rare exception, when before it had been the contrary.

And while she sang, raised, at full voice, heard and loved, Modesta said, within, the phrase that was her liberation, the phrase that had been missing from her whole life: this voice is mine, and they did not give it me to silence it. They gave it me to sing it, to give it, to fill with it the church and the village and the world, and I silenced it a whole lifetime out of fear, and it was a waste, and it is well that I learned, before dying, to loose it. And she sang, raised, whole, at her height, and the voice filled the church, and Modesta, who had spent her life passing stooped through the low doors, passed now, at last, with her head raised, through the highest door of all, that of her own gift at last given, that of her own size at last taken up, that of her own voice at last, and for ever, sung outward.
