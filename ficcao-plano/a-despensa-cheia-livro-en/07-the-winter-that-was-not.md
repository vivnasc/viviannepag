## 7. The winter that was not

The winter Fartura had spent her life waiting for came, at last, but it did not come by the door she waited at, and it did not wear the face she had given it; it came at night, without warning, and it was not hunger: it was Bento falling in the kitchen, at dawn, with his hand on his chest and his face a colour Fartura had never seen on him.

He had got up to drink water, as he did, and Fartura heard the thud, a dull, soft thud, of body and not of thing, and woke already knowing, in that way one knows disasters before seeing them, that it was the disaster, the true one, the one she had been waiting for sixty years with the pantry full. She ran to the kitchen and found him on the floor, propped against the cupboard, livid, sweating, breathing badly, with one hand gripping his chest on the left side and the other trying to hold to the furniture, and saying to her it's nothing, woman, it's nothing, in the voice of one who does not believe what he says, and Fartura, in that instant, kneeling on the kitchen floor beside her husband, understood once and for all, with a clarity the pantry had never given her, which was the winter she should have feared all her life, and it was none of those the grandmother had set in her body.

The neighbourhood came, for in Véspera the thuds of dawn are heard from house to house and no one stays in bed when the neighbour falls. The first to knock was Rosária of the Bridge, the one of the lost ham, who lived now in the house across the way and who had heard the thud and Fartura's smothered cries, and came in in her nightgown and shawl, and knelt on the other side of Bento, and it was she who had the head Fartura did not have, who said unbutton his collar, Aunt Fartura, and lift his head, like this, and who sent her husband, Manuel, running to call whoever is called in these cases. And while they waited, Rosária stayed there, on the kitchen floor, with one hand on Bento's forehead and the other on Fartura's, saying low and certain things, he's breathing, he's breathing, hear him, Aunt, he's breathing well, and half of what she said was a lie and it was the truest thing that could be said, because what Fartura needed on that floor was not the truth, it was a hand, and Rosária, the girl to whom she had taught the closing of hers, opened hers whole over the two of them, that dawn, and Fartura would remember this, afterwards: that in the worst fear of her life it was not the full pantry that stood by her, it was Rosária of the Bridge in her nightgown, who came with an open hand in the dark, and that that, yes, was the plenty that protects, and she had spent sixty years keeping the other. Word was sent to whoever is sent for in these cases, and the night was long and was of fear, of true fear, the kind not covered with jars or sausages, the fear of losing the person with whom one ate badly for forty years in front of a full pantry. And Fartura, sitting at Bento's side while they waited, holding his hand, feeling it cold and trembling in hers, caught herself doing a new sum, a sum she had never done: not of the jars she had, but of the suppers she had not had. Forty years. How many nights would have been like that first good supper, had she opened her hand sooner? How much tender meat went past its point while they ate the leftovers? How much of the two of them's life had rotted on the shelf with the name of providence, waiting for a winter that turned out to be this one, this, a man on the kitchen floor, and against this winter there was no pantry worth anything, because there are losses no plenty covers, and those, precisely those, were the only ones worth fearing, and they were the ones she had never feared, busy as she was fearing hunger.

She thought, in that waiting, of a terrible and exact thing: that if Bento died that night, he would die without having eaten the best of his own house, but for one supper, only one, one Thursday, at the end of forty years. That the whole life she had spared him, all those good morsels she had left on the shelf for a later, all that closed wine, all that cheese that went to the pigs at its point, all of that he had not eaten, and would now not eat, because death does not give back the suppers that were not made, and the full pantry would remain, after him, as proof not of Fartura's providence, but of the life she had not let the two of them live. And that was the thought that split her in two, there, in the kitchen, more than the fear of death: the remorse of the spared life, which is a remorse that has no mending, because the time one kept and did not live is the one good that truly is not recovered, there is no pantry to put it in, there is no shelf to take it from later.

Bento did not die that night. It was the heart, and it had been a warning, of the kind the body sends when there is still time to hear, and with care and quiet Bento recovered, and after some days he was home again, slower, yellower, suddenly older, with a flask of pills on the bedside table and the instruction not to fret and to eat with care, but alive, and Fartura, who had won her husband back when she had already given him for lost, looked at him now with the eyes of one who has received a thing she thought was over, and there was, in those eyes, not a trace of the old fear of hunger, because the old fear of hunger, beside the true fear she had just tasted, had shown itself for what it was: a shadow, a ghost, a winter of lies that had kept her a whole life from seeing the true winter, which was this, that of time running out, that of people going, that of the counted nights one spends eating badly while waiting for a better day.

It was in those days of Bento's convalescence that Fartura changed in earnest, more than she had changed with the marmalade or the supper, because the fright had done in her body what neither the granddaughter's question nor the loss nor the first supper had managed to do at all: it took the fear off the top of her. It did not cure it; the fear of a lifetime is not cured by a fright. But it took it off the top, changed its order, put things in their true place, and Fartura, who had lived sixty years with the fear of hunger ahead of everything, saw it suddenly small and old beside the fear of losing Bento, and understood that she had spent her whole life keeping bread against a wolf that did not come, with her back turned to the wolf that did come, which was time, and that time is not fought by keeping, it is fought by living, which is the exact opposite of what she had done.

She tended Bento with the best of the pantry, now without hesitating, made him broths of the good, gave him to eat what she had spared a whole life, and did it with relish, with a kind of haste even, the haste of one who understood late and wants to recover the time, and Bento let himself be tended and ate the good and said, with his placid humour coming back with his strength, you're giving me the fine, Fartura, I should have fallen sooner, and the two of them laughed, laughed at a thing that had no funniness at all and that had all the funniness in the world, the funniness of people who came to the edge of losing each other and won each other back, and who discover, in that laugh, what is essential and what is fear, and how they had spent their lives swapping one for the other. And there was, in those broths of the good given to the convalescent husband, a thing that was almost a reparation, as if Fartura were paying him, all at once, with all she had that was best, the forty years of bad suppers, and Bento understood it, and let himself be repaired, because he knew that did good to the two of them, to him eating the good at last, and to her giving it at last.

One afternoon, with Bento already getting up, the two of them sitting at the door in the weak sun of the day's end, with the village down below making its usual noises and the dry fountain in the middle of the square, Fartura said to him a thing she had been thinking and that was, for her, almost a confession of a new faith: Bento, I spent my whole life afraid of the winter. And the winter was not hunger. The winter was you falling in the kitchen. And I, with such fear of what did not come, hardly noticed what did. I went on filling the pantry against the cold, and the cold that was icing my house was another, was time passing, and against that there is no pantry. And Bento, in the sun, with her hand in his, thinner now, said only, slowly: well, woman. But we're still here. There's still time to eat the good. And it was the closest thing to a philosophy that Bento ever said, and it was the whole philosophy they needed: we're still here, there's still time to eat the good, which is the only thing one can say with truth in front of the full pantry and the short time, and which Fartura took with her like one who takes a key, the key to the pantry that she had used so many years to close and that she was going, from then on, to learn to use to open.

And the winter of lies, that of hunger, the one the grandmother had set in her body with a closed hand, lost in that kitchen, on Bento's night, the force it had had for sixty years, because Fartura had tasted at last, in the fright, which was the true winter, and whoever knows the true winter ceases to fear the false ones, the same way one who has seen death up close ceases to fear shadows. The habit stayed, which is tenacious, and would make her close her hand many times yet; but the fear beneath the habit, that one, had taken a blow it did not rise whole from, and Fartura, from then on, closed her hand from custom, not from terror, and there is between the two things the difference there is between a door pushed to and a door bolted: one pushed to, opens with a shove; one bolted, needs the key, and the key Fartura had found, without knowing it, on the night she nearly lost the man with whom she had eaten badly her whole life in front of a pantry she had filled, after all, against the wrong winter.

And from that afternoon at the door there was left her, more than anything, a decision that had not yet a shape but had already a root: that of not waiting any longer. She had waited her whole life, waited for the worthy day, the occasion at the right level, the winter that justified the pantry, and the waiting had cost her Clara's marmalade and forty years of bad suppers and had nearly cost her Bento without his having eaten the good; and Fartura, at the door, in the weak sun, with her husband alive beside her, decided, without yet knowing how or when, that she was going to stop waiting, that she was going to make the worthy day herself instead of awaiting it, because she had learned, by force, the thing the grandmother never knew: that worthy days do not come, they are made, and that whoever waits for them dies waiting, with the pantry full, and whoever makes them eats the good while there is mouth, which is all that life, at bottom, beneath all the fears, asks of a person to do.
