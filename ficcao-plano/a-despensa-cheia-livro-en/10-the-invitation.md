## 10. The invitation

The idea of the feast, after it was born on the afternoon of the peaches, grew in Fartura with a force that frightened her, because it was a force the opposite of all those she knew: it was the force of wanting to spend, and Fartura had never, in sixty years, felt the force of wanting to spend.

She knew the other one well, that of wanting to keep, which had moved her a whole life, which was a deep force and worked like thirst, always present, never sated; and she discovered now, with astonishment, that there was a force the size of that one pulling the opposite way, the force of wanting to open, of wanting to give, of wanting to put on the table what one had kept, and that that force, once woken, was not weaker than the other, was even, it seemed to her, stronger, because the keeping one never gave peace and this one, the spending-with-relish one, promised it. She went some days with the two of them inside her, the keeping and the giving, like two horses harnessed pulling in opposite directions, and she leaned, day by day, towards the giving, not because the keeping let her go, which it did not, but because the giving had now, on its side, all that Fartura had learned: the marmalade, the supper, Bento's night, the register, Alegria's peaches, and against all that weight the keeping had only the fear left, and the fear, alone, with no new proofs, was beginning to lose.

She decided it would be on a Sunday, and that it would be for nothing. That was the hardest part to explain, and it was for that very reason that she insisted on it: the feast was not for anyone's birthday, was no saint's day, was no harvest, was no christening nor wedding; it was a feast for nothing, or rather, for a thing that in Véspera was not usually feasted, which was everyone's being alive and there being the means. When she began to invite, that was the question they asked her, one by one, with the strangeness painted on their faces: a feast, Fartura? And on account of what? And Fartura, who still blushed, but who no longer drew back, always answered the same, and each time she said it she grew firmer: for nothing. For our being here. Come along. And there were, in the village, those who stood watching her after she passed, not understanding, because a feast for nothing was, in Véspera, an almost scandalous thing, a waste, and from Fartura no less, from the thrifty, from the woman of good sense, and it ran from mouth to mouth that Fartura was going to give a feast on no day at all, and some found it droll and others found the woman had been odd ever since her husband's illness, and no one guessed that that feast for nothing was the most reasonable thing Fartura had done in her life, and that her reason was so great it did not fit in a saint or a birthday, it fit only in a for-nothing, which is where the greater reasons fit.

Clara, when her mother told her, stood looking at her like one who does not believe, and then laughed, a laugh of relief and astonishment, and said mother, you're going to give a feast for nothing?, and Fartura said I am, and said it with a shy pride, the pride of one doing the bravest thing of her life and knowing that it seems, from outside, the most ordinary, a feast, what is a feast; but Clara knew what that feast was, knew that for the mother to open the pantry in front of the village and spend all at once what she had kept sixty years was the equivalent of another person throwing herself off a cliff, and she looked at her mother with an admiration she had never had for her, the admiration of one who sees someone win, in front of her eyes and already old, the fear of a whole lifetime, and offered at once to help, and went to see to warning whoever was missing, with a girl's gladness, as if it were she having a birthday.

Bento was the one who teased most, with that placid humour of his, now sweeter since the fright of the heart: so the pantry's going to open, he said, I didn't fall for nothing, and laughed, and Fartura scolded him without scolding, and there was between the two of them, in those days of preparation, a lightness they had not had in forty years, the lightness of two people who decided, late but in time, to eat the best of their own house while there is still mouth to eat it. Bento went about the house counting the days to the feast like a child, and it was good to see him so, after the yellow of the illness, with colour in his face again, and Fartura saw that the feast was already doing good before it happened, that just by their awaiting it with relish, instead of awaiting a disaster with fear, the house had already changed its air, one already breathed there another thing, and she thought that perhaps that was the difference between the two lives, the one that awaits the bad and the one that awaits the good: the same waiting, the same not-knowing of the morrow, but in one the house is weighed down with distress and in the other filled with relish, and the morrow, that one, comes all the same, whatever it be, and what changes is only how its eve was lived.

But it would be a lie to tell the preparing of the feast as a calm ascent, and this book does not lie. The fear made its last great onslaught in those days, and made it with all the force of sixty years, because fear, when it senses it is to be evicted from the house where it dwelt a whole life, does not go without a fight. It came at night, as it always came, and this time it did not come whispering it's-to-keep: it came shouting, in the grandmother's voice and with new arguments, doing sums of catastrophe. And if you spend it all on this feast and next year the mountain doesn't give? And if Bento falls again and you need to tend him and have no means left? And if a bad winter comes, a real one, and you threw the whole pantry away on a feast for nothing, out of vanity, out of an old woman's stubbornness to be noticed? Who will come to your aid? Whom will you go and ask, you, who always had the means, on the day you are left without? And Fartura, lying in the dark, heard the fear say all this, and there was a moment, a long one, when she nearly gave up, when she nearly called off the feast, when her hand closed on the coverlet with the old force, because the fear, that night, was convincing, was reasonable, dressed itself in providence as it had always dressed, and seemed, as it had always seemed, good sense.

But Fartura was no longer the woman the fear found alone. She had now, against the voice, things she had not had before: she had Clara's black marmalade, the proof of what the fear did to what was kept; she had the first good supper, the proof that to eat the best did not call disaster; she had Bento's night, the proof of which was the true winter; she had the page of the register, the proof that the hunger had passed three generations ago; she had the afternoon of the peaches, the proof that the chain could be cut; and she had, above all, a question she had learned to put to the fear and that the fear could never answer. Lying in the dark, with her hand wanting to close, Fartura put to the fear the question: and if I keep it all, and the bad winter does not come? I am left with the pantry full and my life empty, like my grandmother, like me until now, and I die full and starved, and Alegria learns from you, and the chain goes on. Is that what you offer me in exchange for not making the feast? The certainty of not living, for the case of a disaster that may not come? And the fear, as always when she set it in the light, was left without an answer, because the fear knows only how to speak of one end, that of the if-it-comes, and has nothing to say of the other, that of the if-it-does-not-come, which is where almost all the days of almost all lives are, the ordinary days on which no disaster comes and on which one could have eaten the best and did not for fear of what did not come.

And Fartura, that night, won from the fear the last great battle, not by killing it, for the fear is not killed, but by disobeying it with her eyes open, which is all one can do and is enough. She opened her hand on the coverlet, on purpose, against the reflex, and left it open, and said to the fear, within, in peace: I have heard you. I know you are right in one thing, which is that life gives no guarantees, that the winter may come. But I am not going to live the eve of a winter that may not even come, and lose for it the one day I am sure to have, which is today. I am going to make the feast. And if the winter comes, I shall meet it as everyone meets it, with what there is and with whoever is here, which is more than my grandmother thought, because she died believing one survives only with a closed hand and alone, and I know now that one does not, that one survives with an open hand and accompanied, and that the pantry that best protects against the winter is not the one full of jars: it is the one full of people who come to our feast and to whose feast we go, because that one, the winter does not eat.

And she fell asleep with her hand open, in peace, with the feast decided for good, and the fear, leant in a corner of the room like an old man who has lost his authority in the house where he ruled a whole life, stayed there, grumbling, no longer able, because the mistress of the house had heard it and beaten it, not by driving it out, for it had its corner and its right to be there, but by ceasing to obey it, which is the only victory that counts over the old fears, and the only one Fartura needed to have so that, the following Sunday, she might open at last the pantry she had kept sixty years, and put the best of it on the table, in front of everyone, on a day that was special for nothing, and that was going to be, for that very reason, the most special day of her life.
