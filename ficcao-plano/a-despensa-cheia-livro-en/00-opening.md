## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Fartura — plenty — daughter of people of the village, born in the second of the years of hunger, when the mountain gave nothing and the river brought nothing and in Véspera the mouths were counted as coins are counted, slowly and with fear. The name was a promise, one of the most anxious the village ever set down: the mother, still carrying her, with the house living on roots and on faith, wanted the daughter to bring in her name the thing that was missing from the table, and called her Plenty, so that the word might do what the earth would not, and the girl might have, at least at her christening, what the house did not have in its chest.

The register says who was born in which year, and the years, sometimes, say more than the names. Whoever reads those of Véspera knows how to read, in the spaces between the lines, the bad winters, because there are years in which many deaths of small ones are set down and few marriages, and those are the years in which hunger passed through the village and took what it wished. Fartura was born in one of those years, and brought the hunger in her cradle without ever having had it at her table, which is the deepest way of having it: she inherited it before she knew it, the way one inherits the colour of the eyes, and carried it a whole life in a pantry she filled and did not eat from.

This is the fourth book of the shelf of the Shop, the shelf of those who do not know how to receive what is theirs, and within it, it is the book of the one who inherited the fear of not having. It tells the story of a woman who came into plenty and never tasted it, who put into the pantry the good the world gave her and kept it for a later that never came, afraid that to open the chest was to call the winter back. It is not a book about money, though money is spoken of; it is a book about what stays in a person when the house taught her too early that abundance is the eve of disaster, and that to eat today what one has is to be left without it tomorrow.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Fartura who keeps the best until it rots, who has her hand closed over the little even when the little is already a great deal, who lives in plenty as one lives on borrowed time, let her know, from the first line, what it took Fartura a whole life not to know: that the full pantry was not made to be looked at, it was made to be opened, and that there is a day, no day at all, a day with no saint and no reason, on which the bravest thing a person can do is to put on the table what she had been keeping for disaster, and make of it, in peace, a feast.
