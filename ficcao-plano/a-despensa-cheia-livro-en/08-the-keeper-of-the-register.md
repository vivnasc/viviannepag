## 8. The keeper of the register

She went to see to Bento's papers at the House of the Register, some papers about the illness that the council asked for, and it was so, for a reason of bureaucracy, that Fartura found herself, one morning, seated on the far side of the Keeper's great table, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps the memory of who has been.

The House of the Register was a house Fartura had known from outside her whole life and from inside almost not at all, because it was a house of the comings and goings of life, of births and deaths and marriages, and Fartura, who had had of those things only the usual ones, had been inside it few times. It was cool like her pantry, of thick walls, and smelled of old paper and of ink, and the shelves rose to the ceiling full of thick-spined books, each one its year, each year its harvest of people. There was there, in those books, more Véspera than in Véspera itself, because there was the whole of Véspera, the living and the dead, the now and the of-a-hundred-years-ago, set down by the hand of the Keepers who had succeeded one another at that table writing who came and who went, with the hand changing from Keeper to Keeper but the formula holding, that of everything a record is made.

The Keeper of that time was a woman of few words and of eyes that had read much death and much birth, and that for that reason no longer took fright at anything nor were deceived about almost anything; she saw to Bento's papers with the calm of one who sees, every day, to things deeper than papers, and asked after his health with that dry care of the Keepers, who ask as one who sets down, without fanfare and without forgetting. And when she had done, instead of dismissing Fartura, she stayed looking at her a moment, in that way the Keepers have, of looking at a person as one reads a page, and said, though it did not much come to the point: you are the Fartura of the year of hunger, are you not. The daughter of Conceição, granddaughter of Rosa.

Fartura said yes, surprised to be known by the year, and the Keeper rose, and went to a shelf, and took down an old book, of a thick spine and gnawed corners, and opened it at a page she seemed to know by heart, without searching, like one who opens her own hand, and turned it towards Fartura, and said: look. And Fartura looked, and it was the page of her years, the years of hunger, and the Keeper passed her finger, slowly, down a column of entries, and Fartura saw what the Keeper wished her to see without saying it: she saw, in that year and the next, the deaths one after another of small ones, the children hunger had taken, the short names of the short lives, one behind the other, more than in a normal year, far more, and beside each one the age, and the ages were of months, of two years, of four, the bare proof, in the hand of an old Keeper, that the hunger the grandmother spoke of was not a tale to frighten girls: it had happened, it had taken all those lives, it was set down there, and the grandmother's closed hand had had, in its time, a reason of flesh and blood, a reason that fit in that column of small names.

Fartura sat looking at the entries of the hunger's dead, and read the names, one by one, slowly, and they were names like hers, names of promise and of exorcism, names the mothers had set on the children begging of the word what the earth denied, and that had not come to save a single one, and Fartura felt, reading those names, a deep and ancient thing, as if those children were hers, sisters she did not come to have, cousins of her own inherited hunger, and understood that the grandmother had invented nothing, that her terror had a face and had a name and was there, and that she, Fartura, born coming out of that hunger, was almost one of those children who survived, one of those the mother had wished to save with a name and who, unlike those of the column, had lived.

The Keeper let her look, without haste, and then said, low: your grandmother's fear was born here, on this page. It was no mania of hers. It was this. Whoever saw this, and your grandmother saw it, surely buried some here, was left with a closed hand for the rest of her life, and whoever stayed beside her learned to close it too, and it passed on. Do not be ashamed of your fear, Fartura. Your fear has a name and a date and is set down in this book. It was true. And Fartura, who expected of the Keeper perhaps a rebuke, or a counsel, felt, instead, a thing she did not expect and needed without knowing it: she felt her fear acknowledged, given a place, taken from the shelf of shame where she kept it and set in the light as a thing that had had reason to exist.

Because there is an enormous difference between a fear one is ashamed of and a fear one understands, and the Keeper, by showing her the page, had given her the second: she had not said to her your fear is foolish, forget it, which was what Clara's head, with every reason and no knack, had been trying to say to her for years; she had said to her your fear was true, see where it comes from, and that sentence, instead of binding Fartura tighter to the fear, loosened her a little from it, because what is understood ceases to govern in the dark, comes up from the cellar and stays at the table, where it can be looked in the eye. Fartura understood, at that table, that she had spent her life wrestling with the fear in two wrong ways: either she obeyed it, and closed her hand, or she was ashamed of it, and shut it, by force, in the cellar, whence it governed all the same, stronger for being hidden; and that there was a third way, which was the one the Keeper showed her, which was to look at it head on, understand it, give it its place and its date, and from there, no longer to obey it nor to hide it, but to live alongside it knowing what it is: an old thing of another time, that has the right to be in the house, but not to rule it.

But the Keeper did not stop there, and that was what made her a Keeper and not only a keeper of books. She turned the page, slowly, forward, to the years that followed, and went passing her fingers down the columns as the years advanced, and said: and now look at this. And Fartura saw the years change in the hand of the Keepers: she saw the deaths of small ones grow rarer, year after year, saw the marriages return, saw the births fill the pages, saw her own name appear in one of the years, Fartura, daughter of Conceição, and after it so many others, saw Véspera coming out of the hunger over the years as one comes out of a winter, slowly and then suddenly, and saw, without the Keeper's saying it, the other half of the truth, the one the grandmother had never told her because she died with a closed hand before learning it: that the hunger had passed. That after those terrible years came good years, and then better ones, and that the village that had lost its small children in a column of entries was the same that, pages on, married and was born and prospered, and that abundance had not been, after all, an eve of disaster: it had been what came after the disaster, and had lasted, and was lasting still, for three generations, while Fartura went on living on the page of the hunger as if the pages that followed were not there, written, in front of her eyes, telling her it passed, woman, it passed long ago, and you are still there.

Your grandmother, said the Keeper, closing the book with care, lived on the wrong page until she died. She stayed on the page of hunger, and did not move to the next, and you inherited her page, you did not inherit your own. Your page, Fartura, is this one, that of the good years, that of the plenty they gave you in your name. You were not born in the hunger; you were born coming out of it, in the second year, when the end was already in sight. Your name was not an exorcism against the hunger that was. It was an announcement of the plenty that was coming. Your mother did not call you Fartura in fear. She called you Fartura in hope. And you made of the name a fear, when it was, from the start, a promise fulfilled.

Fartura came out of the House of the Register with Bento's papers in her hand and with a far greater thing in her head, which was her own story told the right way up, for the first time. All her life she had thought she was called Fartura in mockery of fate, a name of abundance on a woman raised in scarcity, and the Keeper had turned the name inside out for her: the mother had not set on her that name trembling with fear, she had set it on her wagering on the calm that was already announcing itself, and Fartura was, after all, not the ashamed survivor of a hunger, but the first daughter of the plenty, the one born when the hunger was leaving, and who had the duty, almost, to live it, to eat it, to honour it, in the name of all those who had died small in the columns of the bad years without coming to taste it.

She crossed the square thinking on this, and looked at the dry fountain, and the dry fountain, that day, after the register, said a third thing to her, the last it had to say to her in all this book: that a fountain that was dry in a year of hunger and goes on dry is keeping water for no one; it is only refusing to run, from the habit of a time when there was no water, even now that the mountain gives again. And Fartura, for the first time, had pity on the dry fountain instead of seeing herself in it, and the pity she had on the fountain was the pity she was beginning to have on herself, the good pity, the kind that does not soften, that frees: the pity of one who sees a good thing refusing to be what it is, from fear of a time already passed, and who knows, now, that that good thing could run, if it wished, if it let itself, if someone, at last, opened what was closed in it. And in that pity on the fountain went already, without her yet knowing how to say it, the decision of the feast, because whoever has pity on a closed thing ends, sooner or later, by wishing to open it, and Fartura, from that day on, carried with her, beneath everything, the stubborn wish to run, at last, as the fountain did not run, and to give her water all away before her own winter, the true one, the one she had seen on Bento's face on the kitchen floor, should come and catch her still dry.
