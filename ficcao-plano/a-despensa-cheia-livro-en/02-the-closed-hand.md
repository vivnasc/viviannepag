## 2. The closed hand

Fartura learned hunger in a kitchen where bread was never wanting, and that was how it lodged so deep in her: she did not eat it, she heard it, which is the way hunger is inherited.

The one who told it to her was the grandmother, her mother's mother, Rosa, who had been through the bad years as a girl and who carried them in her body in a way that Fartura, as a child, felt without understanding: the grandmother ate quickly, with her head bowed over the plate and her arm curved around it, like one who protects a thing that can be taken from her; the grandmother let not a crumb fall, and if it fell she picked it up with a wetted finger and carried it to her mouth, and this at a full table, with bread to spare; the grandmother kept dry crusts in a little cloth bag she carried in her apron pocket, crusts no one would eat, that crumbled away with age, and that she would not part with, because to part with bread, even dead bread, was a thing the grandmother could not do, not in plenty, not ever. Fartura, a child, had seen her once take a crust already moulded from the little bag, scrape the mould off with her nail, and put it back, not to eat it, for not even the grandmother ate that, but to have it, and that scene, the grandmother scraping mould from a crust she would not eat only to keep it again, stayed in Fartura as the things one sees before understanding them stay, and understands, slowly, a whole life long.

And at night, at the fireside, with the hearth lit and the pot full and the house warm, the grandmother would tell. She told as one prays the rosary, always the same tales in the same order, with the same pauses in the same places, and Fartura, a child, sitting on the low stool with the other children of the house, heard them as one learns a prayer before understanding it: the winter the mountain did not give and the river came dry, the year they ate the seed that was for sowing and so the next year was worse, the weeds that were boiled, the bark of certain trees that was ground and mixed with the little flour to stretch it, the eggs counted one by one for a house of seven, the pig killed too soon out of despair that did not last to winter's end, and the hunger coming into the houses the way the cold comes in, through the chinks, unseen, until it is inside and does not leave. She told of the neighbours who had set out in search and not come back, and of those who had stayed and buried their small ones, and she told it without drama, with the dryness of one who tells a true thing, which is more frightening than any drama, because the drama a child discounts, and the dryness, no.

And there was a tale the grandmother told with a closed hand, and that was the one that stayed in Fartura above all the others. The grandmother would stretch out her arm, in the middle of the fireside, and open her hand in front of the fire, and hold it open an instant, empty, with the firelight crossing her thin fingers, and say: there were those who, at the first cold, ate what they had. They ate it all, the fools, in a month, two, on full bellies while it lasted, and said that no one dies of hunger, that God would provide, that next year it would give. And then she would close her hand slowly, hard, until the knuckles went white in the firelight, and say, lowering her voice: those did not reach the second winter. Those who reached it were those who closed their hand. Those who feared in time. Those who kept the little as if it were less than it was, and stretched it, and hid it even from themselves, and always ate the worst so that there would be worse to eat the next day. Hunger, child, is not beaten by eating. It is beaten by keeping. And whoever does not learn this young, learns it late, with a dead child in her arms, and then she does not forget.

Fartura was six, seven, eight, and did not know what it was to go hungry, and came to know, by that hand that closed, a thing that true hunger might not have taught her so well: that to have was dangerous, that to eat was a risk, that abundance was the antechamber of disaster, and that the only way to be safe in this world was to have one's hand closed over the little, always, even in plenty, above all in plenty, because it was in plenty that the fools dropped their guard and died. The grandmother did not teach her to go hungry. She taught her to fear it, which lasts longer, because hunger passes when the bad year passes, and the fear of it stays forever, and passes from closed hand to closed hand, from grandmother to granddaughter, like an inheritance one does not choose and does not let go. And she taught it to her not with a lesson, for lessons are forgotten, but with the body, with the gesture of the hand closing, with the arm curved around the plate, with the crusts in the apron, which is how the deep things are taught, without words to say learn this, only with one body doing it in front of another body, day after day, until the second body does the same without knowing why.

Fartura's mother, Conceição, inherited it and softened it, because she no longer went through the bad years full on, caught only their tail, the want that came after, which is gentler but still pinches; and she kept, and counted the mouths, and made things stretch, but she already ate some of the good, now and then, in a compromise between her mother's fear and the ease that was coming. And when Fartura was born, in the second of her years of hunger, in a time still of tightness though no longer of death, she set on her that name of promise and of exorcism, Fartura, as if the name could close the door on what the house had known, or open the door to what the house hoped for. And Fartura grew up to be the name in reverse: she had plenty in her christening and fear in her breast, and of all the house it was she, and not her mother, who received the deepest the grandmother's inheritance, perhaps for having been the one who spent the most hours on the low stool at the fireside, the one who heard the hand close the most, the one the name, instead of protecting, marked, because a name that promises the opposite of what is feared is a name that keeps the fear always in sight, every day, in the saying of it.

And she made of the pantry her way of praying the grandmother's prayer, of closing the hand, of stretching the little that was by now a great deal, of eating the worst so there would be worse, of living in abundance like one who lives on the eve, always waiting for the winter the grandmother had set in her body before she had the age to resist it. The grandmother died when Fartura was already a married woman, and died, as she had lived, with her hand closed, with the dry crusts in her apron pocket, and Fartura, who saw to her burial, kept the little bag of crusts in a drawer, not knowing why, and would have it still today if one day, years later, she had not opened the drawer and found the bag full of mould and grub, the bread the grandmother had saved a whole life turned to dust, and had, with a tightening, thrown it out, without understanding, then, that she was throwing out the whole prophecy of her own life, the bread kept until it rots, the plenty turned to refuse by the hand that wished to save it.

Bento knew all of this without knowing it in words, for he had lived alongside her for forty years, and forty years alongside a person teach her better than any confession. Bento had married Fartura young, when she was still only a thrifty girl, and thrift, in a young and poor girl, is virtue and is knack, and Bento took to that woman who knew how to stretch the little and make it go far, who did not squander, who from little made much. But the little had been turning to much, over a lifetime of the two of them working, and the land gave, and the cattle gave, and the children went out and made their own lives, and the house, which had begun cramped, had reached an easeful age, a true plenty, won by the hand; and it was there, in plenty, that Bento discovered he had married fear, and not thrift, because thrift loosens when the house eases, and fear, that one, tightens more, because it has more to lose.

Forty years eating what was left over. Forty years watching the best go past its point in the pantry and go to the pigs, and hearing his wife say it's to keep, it's to keep, and it kept until it rotted. Forty years living in a house that seemed a poor man's and was a comfortable one's, with a rich man's pantry and a beggar's table, and Bento, who was a placid man and did not like to make waves, had said less and less, because saying did no good: he had said, at the start, oh woman, the cheese is eaten while it's good, and Fartura had answered him with such a tightening in her face, such a start, as if he had proposed throwing money out the window, that Bento understood he was not talking about cheese, he was touching a thing that came from far and from deep, a thing that was not meanness, which he would have fought, but was fear, which is not fought, which is only borne or let go, and which his wife did not let go. And he learned to be silent, and to eat what was left over, and to love her so, with that flaw that was no flaw, that was a wound, and which he, over forty years, gradually stopped trying to cure, for no longer knowing how, and for having understood that there are wounds that do not heal from outside, that only the one who has them can reach, if one day she should wish to, or be able to.

But at that point of life, with the two of them already old, the house at ease and the time short, Bento had begun to tire in a new way, not of anger, for he was never one for angers, but of a deep sadness, the sadness of one who sees he will die without ever having eaten the best of his own house. He said it now, at times, under his breath, not to mend Fartura, whom he had given up mending, but to let her hear, as one sows: Fartura, we are going to die with the pantry full. And Fartura heard, and did not answer, because she had no answer, or because the answer, which was the full pantry is what lets us die easy, no longer came to her with the firmness of before, ever since Alegria's question, ever since that chink of light the granddaughter had opened in the always-closed door.

That week, after the granddaughter's question, Fartura caught herself looking at the pantry in another way, and it was a discomfort, because she liked the pantry, took her peace from it, and suddenly the peace came clouded. She went there one afternoon, alone, and stood at the door, looking in as Alegria had looked, and saw, perhaps for the first time, not what she had, but what she was losing: she saw the jam from the first year, dark and over-sugared with age, that no one would eat now; she saw the sausages at the exact point of no longer being good; she saw the feast biscuits, in their unopened boxes, with the date long gone on the labels she did not read because she did not want to read them; she saw the oil gone rancid, the honey crystallised to stone, the marmalade covered with a white fuzz that she scraped off and served all the same, as the grandmother scraped the mould from the crusts, and she saw herself, in a flash, doing the grandmother's gesture, scraping the mould from a thing she would not eat only to keep it again, and she had a shame so deep, so from the root, that she shut the door quickly, like one who shuts the door on a thing she has seen and should not have seen, or has seen and is herself.

Because the truth, the one the grandmother never told her and that perhaps not even the grandmother knew, is that the closed hand does not keep: it squeezes until it spoils. Whoever closes the hand over the bread to save it from hunger, forgets that the bread also dies inside the closed hand, more slowly, but it dies, and whoever wished to save it is left with a hand full of meal, with the grief of having lost it all the same, without even having had the pleasure of eating it. The grandmother had reached the second winter with her hand closed, it was true; but she had reached it, and lived sixty years more with her hand closed in plenty, and died with dry crusts in her apron pocket, and no one ever knew whether the closed hand had saved her from hunger or had condemned her to a hunger of another kind, to a hunger that is not cured by eating because it is not in the belly, it is in another place, in a deep place where a child of eight heard a hand close in the firelight and learned, before knowing what she learned, that in this world there is no safe plenty, there are only eves.

That night, Fartura did not do the sum of the pantry. It was the first night in sixty years that she did not do it, and she lay in the dark, beside Bento sleeping his placid sleep, with her right hand resting on the coverlet, open, not knowing why she left it open, looking at it in the half-dark like one who looks at a thing that is hers and does not know, a hand that had closed a whole life and that she, that night, tried leaving open, only to see, only to feel what it was. And she fell asleep so, with her hand open, and it was a different sleep, neither better nor worse, only different, the sleep of one who let fall, for one night, a thing she had carried so long she no longer knew she had it in her hand, and who in letting it go felt, before anything, not relief, but the strange emptiness of the palm with nothing in it, the emptiness of one who has set down a weight and does not yet know what to do with the freed hand.
