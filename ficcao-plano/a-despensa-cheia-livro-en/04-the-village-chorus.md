## 4. The village chorus

In Véspera, Fartura had the name of a virtue, and that was the worst of her prison: that the whole village praised her through the bars.

It was said of her, in the square, at the Shop, at the church doors, by the washing-troughs where the women still went more for talk than for laundry, that she was a thrifty woman, a woman of good sense, a woman who knew the worth of things. Mothers held her up as an example to their daughters: learn from Fartura, that one wastes not a grain. The newly married asked her for advice on how to make things go far, how to stretch a stew over three days, how to use what others threw out, and she gave it with the authority of one who had the fullest pantry in the village, and no one asked her, because no one saw, that that full pantry was the poorest table in the village, and that making things go far, in Fartura's house, did not mean using well: it meant not eating.

She remembered one in particular, Rosária of the Bridge, who had married young and poor and who had appeared at Fartura's door in the first spring of her marriage, her apron still too clean, with a girl's bashfulness and a question on her back that was hard to get out. Aunt Fartura, she said at last, leaning against the doorpost, you who know, I have a ham that my father-in-law gave us at the wedding, and Manuel wants to start it, and I don't know whether to let him, whether to keep it, for it's the one good thing we have in the house and I'm afraid to eat it and then have no more. And Fartura, in that time still with no chink opened in the inner door, had received the question as one receives a disciple, and had taken Rosária to the pantry, and shown her the shelves, and taught her, with the patience of one who teaches a prayer, the law of the house: keep it, child. Hang it in the cool, cut from it only on Sundays, a thin slice, and let it last the year. The good is not eaten all at once, it is stretched. Whoever eats the best first is left with the worst for the end, and the end is always longer than one thinks. And Rosária had heard her with open eyes, grateful, and gone away with the lesson in her body, and Fartura had felt herself, that day, what she always felt on giving that advice: useful, solid, mother of the village, keeper of a knowledge. Only now, with the chink open, did the memory of Rosária come back to her with another weight, and Fartura saw, recalling her, not a disciple she had helped, but a young girl whom she, with the best of intentions and the authority of the fullest pantry in the village, had taught to close her hand, to ruin a ham by sparing it, to fear the end before having lived the beginning. She had learned afterwards, in passing, that Rosária's ham had been lost, had bred maggots on the rod from being so kept, and Rosária had wept for it, and Fartura, at the time, had thought the fault lay in the damp of the house at the Bridge, and not in the lesson. It was the lesson. It had always been the lesson.

The village saw the pantry, which she showed on the rare occasions someone came in and stood open-mouthed at the plenty of the shelves, and did not see the table, which she hid, which she always laid quickly and with the worst when there was a visit, and from what it saw it made praise, and the praise, which should have been a comfort, was in fact the bolt on the outside of the door that Alegria's question had opened from within.

Because there are prisons one bears for being prisons, and there are others, deeper, that one bears for being praised. Had the village called Fartura mean, miserly, grasping, perhaps Fartura would one day have rebelled against the word and against the thing, perhaps the shame of the ugly word would have made her change just to be rid of it. But the village called her thrifty, of good sense, provident, and against those words there is no rebellion, because they are good words, they are words a person wants to have, and Fartura clung to them as she clung to the little heap, and made of the virtue the village granted her one more reason not to change, because to change would be to belie the praise, would be to come down from the altar they had set her on, would be to confess to the village that the woman of good sense was, at bottom, a woman afraid, and that Fartura could not do, for the shame of being seen afraid was, in Véspera, near as deep as the shame of the fear itself. In Véspera, to show a wound was to give the village a thing to talk about, and the village talked, and Fartura would rather be praised through the bars than be remarked upon for the wound, which is a choice many a person makes, in Véspera and beyond it, and which keeps more prisons shut than all the bolts in the world.

There was that week an occasion that put it all to the test, and it was a small thing, of the kind that seem nothing and say everything. A girl of the neighbourhood married, Aninhas, one of those Fartura had seen born, and they did what is done at weddings in Véspera, which is for each house to contribute what it can to the table of the feast, for in Véspera a wedding is not the family's, it is the street's, and the table is made of many pantries; and it was Benvinda, of the Shop, who went round gathering, door to door, what each one gave, and went marking it in the ledger, not to charge, for that was not charged, but so that it should be known, in the end, who had given what, for in Véspera everything is known and everything is set down, one way or another.

When she reached Fartura's door, Benvinda knew, as the whole village knew, that that was the fullest pantry in the parish, and waited, without saying it, with the basket on her arm and the pencil behind her ear, for a full contribution to come from a full pantry. And Fartura went to the pantry, and stood before the shelves, with Benvinda at the street door waiting, and lived there, in a few minutes, the whole war of her life.

Because the part of her that wished to give, that was ashamed not to give, that had seen the granddaughter's apple and the dry fountain, that part wished to take from the shelf the best, the sausages at their point, the good jam, the honey, and send to the girl's feast a plenty worthy of the name; and for a moment Fartura even reached out her hand to the good sausages, even touched them, and felt the anticipated pleasure of giving the good, of seeing Aninhas's table with the best of her pantry, of being, for the first time, plentiful in truth and not only in name. But the other part, the old one, the one of the closed hand, the one that heard the grandmother say those who ate it all at the first winter did not reach the second, that part squeezed her hand over the best and pushed it to the back of the shelf, to what was going off, to what she would have to throw out all the same, and said to her give of that, you won't miss that, the good is to keep, and Fartura, that time, still obeyed, but she obeyed already hearing herself obey, which was the new thing.

She gave, to Aninhas's feast, two jars of jam from the year the jam was already turning, and an end of sausage that was at the limit, and she did it blushing, for the first time blushing, because for the first time she saw herself giving the worst while having the best, and saw in that, by the light of the open chink, not the virtue the village granted her, but a small indignity, the indignity of one who sends to others' tables what she does not want at her own, and keeps the good for an imagined disaster. Benvinda received the jars without comment, with that look of hers that saw everything and said almost nothing, but Fartura, who in those days was seeing herself in others' eyes as she had not in years, read in Benvinda's look a thing that made her blush more: she read that Benvinda knew. That Benvinda, from her side of the counter, with the ledger of half the village, knew exactly what went into those two turning jars out of the fullest pantry in the parish, and that she said nothing, not for not seeing, but for seeing too much, and for having, she too, her knot with giving, and for knowing that others' wounds are not cured by pointing them out at the street door.

Fartura stood at the door watching her go off with her shamed contribution in the basket, and felt the urge to call her back, to say wait, take these instead, the good ones, but she did not call her, because sixty years of a closed hand do not open all at once, they open joint by joint, and that day the hand bent only enough to see what it was giving, and not yet enough to give another thing. But the seeing was already new. Before, Fartura gave the worst and thought herself generous, because she gave; now she gave the worst and saw herself giving the worst, and between thinking oneself generous and seeing oneself goes the whole distance of the world, goes the distance that separates the sleeping from the woken. And the woken may shut their eyes again, but they already know they shut them, and that knowing, small and stubborn, is the beginning of all that changes in people.

On the wedding day, Fartura went to the feast, like the whole village, and sat at the long street table, and ate, and at a certain point a dish passed in front of her with the jam she had given, the one turning, and Fartura saw it served, saw a neighbour put on her plate the jam that was hers and taste it, and saw, on the neighbour's face, a small drawing-back, the drawing-back of one who tastes a thing no longer at its point and is too polite to say so, and Fartura felt a shame that burned her ears, the shame of having sent to Aninhas's feast, in front of the village, a turning jam, she, Fartura, the thrifty, the woman of good sense, the one with the fullest pantry in the parish. And she understood, at that table, a thing the fear had never let her see: that the closed hand did not protect her from shame, it gave it to her; that keeping the best did not make her rich in the village's eyes, it made her, in the eyes of whoever tasted the turning jam, a small thing disguised as a great one; and that the village's praise, the thrifty, the good sense, was a garment that fit her on the outside and that within, in that dish, showed its lining.

At night, lying down, Fartura could not make her peace with herself as she used to after a contribution, saying I gave, I did my part, I was one of those who gave; because the truth the chink showed her was that she had not given, she had got rid of, she had sent out what she would lose all the same and called that giving, and there are in Véspera, as everywhere, many who confuse getting rid of with giving, and who think themselves generous for dumping on others what no longer served them; and Fartura, who had always counted herself among the generous, found herself that night among those who get rid, and it was a bitter discovery, of the kind that take sleep away instead of bringing it.

Bento, beside her, felt her shifting, and asked her in the dark, without turning, what is it, woman, and Fartura, who never spoke of these things, who kept them as she kept everything, that night, perhaps for the dark, perhaps for no longer bearing to have them only to herself, said: I gave Aninhas's feast the turning jam. I had the good, and I gave the one turning. And a neighbour tasted it in front of me and made a face. And Bento was quiet a moment, for he did not expect that confession from a woman who never confessed anything, and then said, with the tired gentleness of forty years: well, you gave, Fartura. You always give what's turning. To this house, to yourself, to others. You always give the turning and keep the good for no one. And he did not say it in anger, he said it in pity, with the pity of one who loves a person and watches her do herself harm for forty years without being able to stop, and Fartura, in the dark, received Bento's sentence without defending herself, because it was true, and because the truth, at that point, already came in without finding the door closed, for the door had been open a chink since the granddaughter's question and had not closed again.

I keep the good for no one, Fartura repeated under her breath, to herself, after Bento fell asleep, and the sentence stayed turning in her head the whole night, because it was the most exact definition of her life that anyone had ever made, and it had been made by Bento, the placid man who had eaten what was left over for forty years and who, it turned out, had been seeing everything, silent, from his place at the poor table, in front of the full pantry. I keep the good for no one. And the village calls me thrifty. And I let myself be called, because it is easier to be praised through the bars than to confess that I am imprisoned, and that the one who imprisoned me was a hand closing in the firelight, fifty years ago, and that I never opened it again, and that I am afraid, an old and reasonless fear, that to open the hand is to let in the winter my grandmother set in my body before I knew what a winter was.

The village slept, and Fartura's pantry, at the back of the house, was full as always, and the best went on there, kept for no one, going past its point in the cool dark; but something was already different, because the mistress of the pantry, for the first time in sixty years, was awake knowing herself awake, and whoever wakes does not go back to the same sleep, however much the sleep calls her back, and however much the village chorus, outside, goes on singing at her door the old song, that of the thrifty woman, that of the woman of good sense, the pretty song that kept her imprisoned a whole life and that she was beginning, at last, slowly, to hear for what it was: not a praise, but a lullaby, of the kind sung to put one to sleep, and she no longer wished to sleep.
