## 9. The inheritance that does not pass

The thing Fartura still lacked to understand, and that she understood one afternoon with the granddaughter, was that fear does not end in the one who has it: it passes, if it is not cut, and the place where she could cut it was small, was seven years old, was called Alegria, and was, without anyone noticing, learning.

Because children learn what is done in front of them, not what is said to them, and Alegria, on Wednesdays, at her grandmother's house, had for years been learning Fartura's pantry without anyone teaching her anything on purpose: learning that there is food to eat and food to keep, learning that the best is at the back of the shelf and is not touched, learning, by the grandmother's gesture of closing the cool door, that to have is a serious thing and is protected. She had not yet learned the fear, for Alegria was by nature joyful and the mother did the opposite at home; but she was on the edge of beginning to learn it, in the way these things are begun, by a repeated gesture, by a sentence heard many times, and Fartura, without knowing it, was the teacher, and the lesson was the closed hand, and the class was every Wednesday.

It was a Wednesday afternoon, one of those the granddaughter stayed with her, and Alegria asked for a simple thing: she had seen, in the pantry now emptier but still plentiful, a jar of peaches in syrup, the good ones, the kind Fartura bought and kept, round and golden inside the glass, and she asked, with her eyes shining, with her mouth watering, may I, Grandma? And Fartura, with her back turned, washing the dishes, opened her mouth and was about to say, without thinking, in the voice that was not hers but the grandmother's, in the voice of sixty years, was about to say those are for later, it's to keep, today eat of yesterday's fruit; was about to say, to the granddaughter, to the Alegria of the name against the custom, the first lesson of the closed hand, was about to set it in the child's body as it had been set in hers, without malice, even with love, with the crooked love of one who thinks she protects by teaching to fear.

And she stopped.

She stopped with the sentence halfway out of her mouth, with the dishwater running from her hands, because she heard herself, because ever since the open chink she heard herself, and what she heard was the grandmother, was the hand closing in the firelight, was the whole chain reaching there, to that kitchen, to that child, and she understood, with a chill down her back that turned her round, that she was about to do to Alegria what the grandmother had done to her mother and the mother had done to her: to pass on the fear, with the best of intentions, dressed as good sense, and to condemn one more woman of that house to live in plenty with a closed hand, to eat the worst in front of the best, to keep her life until it rotted. She was about to set on the granddaughter the jar of marmalade that would be lost. She was about to be, for Alegria, the Rosa she had been for her, the hand that closes in the firelight that a child sees and never again forgets. And she stopped, because she saw all this in an instant, with a clarity that was new and that hurt her, and understood that that instant, that sentence suspended halfway out of her mouth, was the exact place where the chain passed on or was cut, and that it was in her hands, in that second, at the dish sink, to decide which of the two things she would do.

She turned round, with her hands still wet, and looked at Alegria, who waited for the answer with the confidence of children not yet taught to fear, and Fartura, instead of the grandmother's sentence, said another, a new sentence, a sentence that cost her as it costs to lift a weight one has carried a whole life in the opposite direction, and said: you may. Go, open it, eat as many as you like. And she added, because she wished to add it, because it was important that the child hear it, because it was that sentence, and not the other, that she wished to leave in the granddaughter's body: always eat the best, Alegria. The best is to be eaten. Whoever keeps it, loses it. Do you hear? The best is for today.

Alegria, who had no notion of what went in that sentence, of the weight of sixty years and three generations the grandmother was lifting so as not to set it upon her, said only thank you, Grandma, and went to the pantry, and opened the jar of good peaches, and ate them there, with the syrup running down her chin, with the whole, guiltless relish of one who never learned that to eat the good is dangerous, and Fartura stayed watching her eat, with her hands still dripping from the dishes, and it was, of all her crossing, perhaps the deepest moment, deeper than the marmalade and the supper and even than Bento's night, because it was the moment in which she cut the chain. Not her chain, which was already cut halfway and she still unravelling it; the chain of Alegria. The granddaughter would not inherit the closed hand. She would inherit a grandmother who said to her eat the best, it's for today, and that sentence, set in a child's body instead of the other, was the inheritance reversed, was the fountain running again after three generations dry, was the hunger ending not in the belly, which had ended long ago, but in the fear, which was where it lasted.

Because this is what the books of the register do not say and only people know: that there are inheritances that are not in the land nor the money nor the names, they are in the small gestures, in the way of laying the table, in what is said to a child who asks for a peach, and that those inheritances pass surer than the others, from closed hand to closed hand, and that to cut them is the hardest and most important thing a person can do for the one who comes after, because it is to give her not what one has, but what one is, cleaned of the fear one received. Fartura could not give Alegria the fearless childhood she herself had not had; but she could not give her the fear, could stop in her the chain, could be the woman of that house in whom the hand, finally, instead of closing in front of the fire, opened in front of a granddaughter eating peaches, and said eat, child, it's for today.

And she understood, watching Alegria eat, a thing that eased her of an old guilt: that it was not too late. That an inheritance could be changed at fifty-eight, not backward, for backward there is no mending, the marmalade was black and the forty years of bad suppers were lived, but forward, in the granddaughter, in what was not yet made. That the chain came from very far, from Rosa, from the hunger, from the firesides, and had passed through Conceição and through her, but that it did not have to pass through Alegria, and that it was enough, to stop it, for one woman, in one second, at the dish sink, to choose one sentence instead of another. So little, and so everything. Sixty years of fear, three generations of the closed hand, and the place where it was cut was a sentence said to a child on a Wednesday afternoon, and Fartura had had it in her hand, the sentence, the two sentences, and had chosen the right one, and it was of that, more than of anything else in this book, that she was proud for the rest of her life.

That night, she told Clara, when she came to fetch her daughter, what had happened, not in these words, for Fartura was not one for great words, but in her way: she said, almost in passing, putting away the dishes, I gave Alegria the good peaches. And told her to always eat the best. And Clara, who understood at once what her mother was telling her beneath that simple sentence, who understood that her mother was telling her I did not pass to your daughter what was passed to me, was a moment unable to speak, and then embraced her mother, there in the kitchen, an embrace the two of them were not in the habit of giving, for they were of a family of closed hands also in embraces, and it was an awkward and whole embrace, and Clara said in her ear, with her voice trembling, thank you, mother, and Fartura did not understand at once why the daughter thanked her for some peaches, and then understood that the daughter was not thanking her for the peaches, she was thanking her for the cut chain, was thanking her for the thing she herself, Clara, had tried to do her whole life and that only the mother, the mistress of the pantry, the keeper, the one of the closed hand, could truly do, which was to end in herself what came from behind, so that it should not reach ahead.

Because Clara, with all her open-handed rebellion, with all the peaches she gave her daughter and all the cakes she left begun on the kitchen, knew, at bottom, that she could not cut the chain alone: she could divert it in her house, could not pass it to Alegria from her side, but the chain came from the mother, and while the mother had it alive, closed, every Wednesday, it passed all the same, by the far side, by the grandmother's pantry, by the hand that closed in front of the girl. Only Fartura could cut Fartura. Only the keeper could stop the chain of keeping, because the chain of keeping does not break from outside, with reasons and with rebellions, that only closes it tighter; it breaks from within, in the very hand that holds it, on the day that hand, in front of a granddaughter asking for peaches, decides to open. And it opened. And Clara, who had waited for that her whole life without believing it would come, embraced her mother weeping, and the two of them, in that kitchen, more than mother and daughter, were two women of one same house taking leave, together, of a dead grandmother who had bound them both, and loosing themselves from her, at last, with an awkward embrace and some peaches eaten by a child who would never know, until she was grown and read perhaps a book like this one, what that afternoon had had of enormous.

And it was from that afternoon of the peaches that Fartura knew what she had to do, though she did not yet quite know how, nor when: she knew she had to open the pantry. Not little by little, not one jar at a time with her heart pounding, that she had been doing already; she knew she had to open it all at once, in plain sight, in a feast, in front of the family and the village, to seal the thing, to make it true, to say to the world and to herself, with one great gesture and not with a hundred small ones, that the house of hunger had ended in that generation, that the full pantry would at last be what it should always have been, not a coffer of fear, but a table, and that she, Fartura, was going to make with the best she had kept her whole life the thing the best is made for, which is not to last on the shelf: it is to be eaten, with relish, among those one loves, on a day that is special for nothing but for being alive and having the means, which is, after all, the only special day there is, and which is available every day, to whoever has the courage to recognise it.
