## 5. What is lost by keeping

It was on a Thursday, on no day at all, that Fartura decided to set the pantry to rights, and to set the pantry to rights, for a woman who never took from it anything but the worst, was a thing she had not done in years, because setting to rights forces one to see, and Fartura, who lived off the pantry, also lived off not seeing it whole.

She lived off it in pieces: she went to the front of the shelves, took the turning, shut the door, and the whole pantry, the true one, the one of the back and the height, the one of the best kept, that one always went unseen, like a room of a house one inhabits and does not enter, present and shut at the same time. The sum at night was in the head, was of numbers, so many jars, so many sausages, and numbers do not smell or show fuzz; one thing is to count what one has, another is to look at it. Fartura had counted for sixty years and looked for none, and that was why setting to rights, that Thursday, was less a cleaning than a settling of accounts, the settling the open chink had been asking for ever since the granddaughter's question.

She went into the pantry with a cloth and a basin, set on cleaning, and what she found was less filth than loss, because a pantry where one keeps and does not eat is not dirty: it is full of dead things, well ordered, with the year written on the lid. She began with the top shelves, the ones at the back, where the oldest was, what she put back there precisely to save it, to keep it from use, and it was there that the loss came to her all at once. She took a jar of jam and the lid gave with a sour smell that threw her face back; she took another and it was crystallised to stone, the sugar all parted from the fruit, a thing that was no longer jam nor anything; a box of feast biscuits, unopened, with the paper intact and the ribbon still tied, and inside the biscuits turned to soft, grub-eaten meal, eaten by the grub that had got in no one knew where and that had eaten, it, what Fartura had kept and not eaten; the honey of a good year turned to a dark mass stuck to the glass, with not even a smell; and the sausages, the ones she had cured with such care and hung and gone on leaving for later, those were some still edible, at the limit, and others covered with a fuzz that no longer scraped off, lost for good, years of her work and Bento's hung rotting on a rod, kept with so much love that they spoiled.

Fartura kept putting in the basin what was lost, and the basin filled quickly, and each thing she put there was a small jolt, because each thing had been, in its time, money, or work, or a summer afternoon sweating over the fire making preserve, and was now there, in the basin, on its way to the pigs, lost not from waste, for Fartura had never wasted anything, but from the opposite of waste, from the excess of keeping, which comes, in the end, to the same place. And there came into her, shelf by shelf, a truth that left her without ground: that she, the thrifty, the woman of good sense, the one the village held up as an example, had let spoil, over the years, more food than the most wasteful of the village's women, only she had let it spoil slowly, in secret, behind a shut door, with the name of providence, and that her slow and shamed waste was, in quantity, greater than the quick and open waste of one who ate the best while it was good.

But there was a thing that made her stop, and sit on the pantry stool with it in her hands, and it was a small jar, of thick glass, with a cork lid, and on the lid, in her hand but younger, firmer, the hand of thirty years before, was written: Clara's marmalade. Fartura knew that jar without having it in her day-to-day memory, in that way one knows the things one has kept so deep one no longer thinks of them. She had made that marmalade the year Clara was born, from the first quince tree that truly bore in their yard, a quince tree Bento had planted the year of their marriage and that had been slow to take, and that bore, at last, the year their daughter was born, as if the tree and the girl had agreed it, and Fartura had kept that marmalade, in that time still of tightness, as one keeps a thing that is more than food: she had kept it as one keeps a date, a beginning, the sweetness of a year in which, despite everything, a daughter was born to her and the quince tree bore. It was to be opened on a special day. It was for Clara, one day. It was the one thing, in the whole pantry, that Fartura had kept not from fear, but from love, and it was for that very reason, for being of love, that she had kept it better than everything, deeper, more protected, for a later worthy of what it meant.

And the later had not come. Thirty years. Clara had been born, grown up, been the girl of the silent rebellions, married, made her open-handed house, had her daughter, set Alegria on her against the custom, and the marmalade of the year she herself was born had gone on the shelf safe, waiting for a special day that Fartura never thought special enough, because a day worthy of opening Clara's marmalade would have to be a perfect day, and perfect days do not come, they are made, and Fartura had never learned to make them, only to wait for them. She had thought of it, of the marmalade, on some great days: at Clara's wedding she had even gone to fetch it and put it back on the shelf, because a wedding already had so many sweets, what for that one, it was kept for something more intimate; at Alegria's christening she had thought of it again, and again left it, for when the granddaughter was older and could know what it meant; and so, from postponed great day to postponed great day, the marmalade had stayed, always spared from the present day in the name of a worthier future day, until the future ran out inside the jar without her noticing.

And now she opened it, thirty years on, on some Thursday, with her heart already knowing what she would find, and she found it: Clara's marmalade, the one from the year she was born, was black, dry, stuck to the glass, beyond saving, dead from being kept, and Fartura was left with the open jar in her hands, looking at that thing that had been sweet and had been love and had been a beginning, and that she had turned, by dint of keeping it for a day worthy of it, into a thing that no longer served for anything, not to eat, not to give, not even to remember with pleasure, because the memory came now spoiled by the loss.

And Fartura wept, in the pantry, sitting on the stool, with her daughter's dead marmalade in her hands, and it was a weeping she had not wept in years, perhaps ever, a deep weeping, of sobs, that came to her from farther off than that jar, a weeping that was not for the marmalade, which was only marmalade, but for what the marmalade said to her, which was her whole life put in that jar: that she had kept the best, the of love, the of truth, waiting for a worthy day, and that the worthy day had never come because she had never made it, and that all she had kept for that day was being lost while she waited, and that she was going to die, as Bento said, with the pantry full and her daughter's marmalade black at the back of a shelf, without ever having opened it, without ever having made the day that deserved it, because she was afraid to spend the sweetness on a day that was not perfect, and by dint of waiting for the perfect she had lost the good, which was what there was.

This was the thought that floored her, there on the stool: that the fear of spending badly had made her spend nothing, and that to spend nothing was not to keep, it was to lose slowly, with the aggravation of losing without even having had the pleasure of using. Whoever eats the best on an ordinary day, at least ate it. Whoever keeps it for a perfect day that does not come, loses it all the same, but loses it without having tasted it, which is the worst of losses, the double loss, that of what was lost and that of what one never came to have. The grandmother said those who ate it all at the first winter did not reach the second; but the grandmother had never said, because perhaps she did not even know, that whoever eats nothing at the first winter from fear of the second, reaches the second, and the third, and every winter of life, always with the pantry full and always hungry, and dies full and starved at the same time, with the daughter's marmalade intact and black at the back of the shelf, kept with so much love that it was lost entire.

But before Bento came in, before the weeping ended, there was still a thing in that pantry, at the back, against the coolest wall, beneath the lowest shelf, that Fartura had not opened in so long she had almost forgotten it: the chest. It was the trousseau chest, the one of dark wood with iron corners, that had come with her to that house on her wedding day and that her father-in-law had set there, at the back of the wine room, and that Fartura, over the years, had gone on covering with sacks of flour and earthenware pots until the chest disappeared beneath the plenty, as everything that was hers disappeared beneath what she kept. That Thursday, in taking the pots from the front to reach the bottom shelves, the chest appeared, and Fartura, her eyes still undried, knelt before it and lifted its lid, which creaked as creaks what has been long shut.

Inside was the trousseau. The linen sheets her mother had embroidered for her, Conceição, stitch by stitch, at the fireside, in the years of the betrothal, with the two of their initials interlaced in a corner; the lace hand-towels; a white coverlet, the top one, the one put on the bed only when a visit of respect came, and that had come, in forty years of marriage, to their bed half a dozen counted times. It was all yellowed. The linen, kept in the dark without ever taking sun or use, had the sad tone of things that age without serving, and the white coverlet had, along one fold, a brownish line, the mark of the crease, because a cloth folded forty years in the same place ends by wearing through at the fold as a much-trodden path wears, only that cloth had never been trodden, it had worn from standing still, which is the most useless way for a thing to wear. Fartura passed her hand over the yellow linen, over her mother's stitches, and understood that the chest was the pantry in small, and the pantry was life in large, and that everything in her house obeyed the same law: the best at the back, in the dark, kept for a later at its level, yellowing as it waited. The good coverlet had waited for a worthy visit that never proved worthy enough to come; and it had yellowed in the fold; and Fartura had never slept beneath it in her own bed, in her own marriage, with her own husband, from always thinking it a pity to spoil it, that it was to keep, that it would serve one day. She closed the chest slowly, with a trembling hand, and was left with that joining the marmalade and the sour jam and the honey in stone, all of it saying the same thing through the same mouth: that she had kept not only food, she had kept her whole life in chests and on shelves, and that life, kept, yellowed at the fold like her mother's linen, and did not unyellow afterwards, however much one wished, because there are creases that stay.

Bento found her so, coming in, with the open jar and her eyes red, and did not understand at once what was happening, and took fright, asked what is it, woman, did you hurt yourself, did you fall, and Fartura, without letting go of the jar, told him the truest thing she had told him in forty years: I spoiled Clara's marmalade. The one from the year she was born, from your quince tree. I was keeping it for a special day. For thirty years. And the day never came. And now it's black, Bento. I lost it from keeping it. And Bento, who was a placid man and had no great words, who remembered the quince tree and the year and the girl, did the one thing there was to do, which was to sit beside her on the pantry stool, in the midst of the dead jars and the lost sausages, and put his hand on her shoulder, and stay there, silent, letting her weep the marmalade and all the marmalade meant, because there were things that not even Bento, in forty years, had managed to say to Fartura, and that a black marmalade said to her on a Thursday morning, on no day at all, which is how truths tend to come.

That afternoon, Fartura did a thing she had never done: she took from the pantry, shelf by shelf, everything that was lost, and filled two pails with what had spoiled from keeping, the sour jars, the honey in stone, the grubbed biscuits, the fuzzed sausages, Clara's marmalade, all of it, and took it to the pig-yard, and threw it out with her own hands, she who never threw anything out, and it was painful and it was clean at the same time, because to throw out what is already lost is not to waste: to waste was to have kept it until it was lost. The true waste was done years before, in the dark, silent, with the name of providence; that, there, was only to acknowledge it, was to set in plain sight the reckoning of a life of the closed hand, and Fartura looked at the two pails of dead food and understood, with a cold clarity, that this was what the closed hand kept, at bottom: not the bread for the winter, but the rotting of the bread, the slow loss disguised as providence, the postponed hunger that in the end is never postponed, only swapped for another.

And when she went home, with the empty pails and the pantry emptier too, for the first time in sixty years, Fartura did not feel the distress she had always felt at seeing the pantry the less. She felt, instead, a thing she could not name and that only much later, when the book of her life was already far on, she would know to call by its name: she felt space. Space on the shelf, and space within her, the space that is left when one sets down a thing one has carried a long time, and that at first frightens, because it seems a lack, and that then, slowly, is found to be another thing: to be room for what is yet to come, to be the clean shelf where, one of these days, one might put not what one keeps, but what one will eat. And she looked at the empty marmalade jar, which she had washed and kept for not being able to throw out the glass too, and thought that perhaps, one day, she would make another marmalade, of another year, and that this time she would not keep it thirty years waiting for a worthy day: she would eat it with Clara and Alegria on some afternoon, and the day, for being with them, would be worthy, because the worthiness of the day, she understood now, is not in the day, it is in who shares it, and it is, above all, in not postponing it.
