## 11. The eve

On the Saturday, the eve of the feast, Fartura opened the pantry wide, flung open the door that for sixty years she had kept pushed to, and began to take from it, with her own hands, the best she had kept her whole life, to cook it all, at once, for the next day.

It was a long work and it was, without her expecting it, a joy, a physical joy, of the arms, of taking from the shelves what had stood there so long and carrying it to the light of the kitchen, to the table, to the pots. She began with the back and the height, with where she kept the best, with where the hand reached with difficulty, and it was on purpose that she began there, with the most protected, the most spared, because she wished that to be it, the best of all, the most kept, that went to the next day's table, and not the going-off, which was what she had always given. The good sausages, the ones still at their point, that she had cured and spared; the beans and the chickpeas of the best years; the fine oil she used drop by drop and that she now set running into the pans without counting, hearing the thick thread fall into the pan with an almost sinful relish, she who measured the oil by the drop; the honey for the sweets; the good flour, the clean wheat, that she kept and gave at the table the worst of; the summer preserves, the tomatoes, the peppers, all she had made sweating over the fire to fill shelves and that was at last to fulfil the end the tomato and the pepper are made for, which is not to last in a jar, it is to give flavour to a table.

The pantry emptied itself over the day, and each thing that came off the shelf into the pot was, for Fartura, a small liberation, the liberation of a thing that had been imprisoned awaiting a day that never came and that was, at last, the next day, to be eaten with relish among people. And as the shelves showed themselves, as the bare wood appeared that had been covered with plenty for decades, Fartura had, instead of the tightening she expected, a deeper breath, as if each shelf that emptied were a piece of chest unclenching, and she understood, midway through the day, that it was not only the pantry emptying: it was she, it was the fear, it was the knot of sixty years coming out of there by the armful, turned into food, on its way to being feast.

Clara came to help, and Alegria came with her, and Fartura's kitchen, which had always been the kitchen of a woman alone making the little go far in silence, filled that Saturday with three generations cooking the much with noise, and it was a sound that house did not know, the sound of a family preparing a plenty to spend, and not to keep. Alegria stirred the batters and stole bits and no one said to her it's to keep; Clara cut and seasoned beside her mother, and at a certain point the two of them, mother and daughter, were side by side kneading, with their hands in the same dough, and neither of the two said anything, but the two felt that this had never happened, mother and daughter making food together to spend, and that it was a thing that should have happened a whole life and only now happened, and better late, the two of them thought without saying it, better late than the black marmalade, better late than never. And Fartura, in the midst of the two, directed the whole thing with a new authority, the authority not of one who spares, but of one who gives, and discovered that that authority suited her better, that it was more her own, that perhaps it had always been hers and only the fear had hidden it beneath the other.

Bento sat in a corner of the kitchen, still sparing himself on account of the heart, but present, peeling what they gave him to peel, watching his wife and his daughter and his granddaughter empty the pantry he had watched fill for sixty years, and at a certain point Fartura noticed that his eyes were moist, and went to him, and he said to her, low, for the others not to hear: I never thought to see this, woman. The pantry open. I thought I'd die first. And Fartura sat a moment beside him, in the midst of the work, and the two of them stayed looking at the near-empty pantry, with the shelves showing for the first time in decades, and there was in them, in neither one nor the other, the distress Fartura had always expected to feel before a pantry the less; there was, instead, a strange peace, the peace of one who has undone at last a knot that pinched so long it had become part of the body, and discovers, in undoing it, that one breathes better, that one would always have breathed better, that the knot never held anything worth the want of breath it cost.

The last thing Fartura took from the pantry, at the day's end, was a bottle of old wine, of the good, the kind her father-in-law had set to make and that had been in the pantry since before Clara's marmalade, kept for an occasion at the right level, and Fartura took it up, wiped the dust off it with her apron, and stood a moment looking at it, thinking of the marmalade that had been lost from awaiting the occasion at the right level, and decided, there, that that bottle would not have the marmalade's fate. It would not wait any longer. The occasion at the right level was the next day, was the feast for nothing, and Fartura set the bottle on the table, in plain sight, for the next day, and it was like setting a flag, the flag of the house that changed its law, the flag that in that house, from then on, the occasion at the right level is tomorrow, is today, is the day one is alive and has the means, and not a perfect later that never comes and that makes the best rot in the waiting for it.

When Clara and Alegria went off, at nightfall, with everything ready for the next day, with the pots full and covered and the kitchen smelling of feast, Fartura stayed alone in the kitchen with Bento, in the midst of what they had made, and went to the pantry, one last time that night, and stood at the door looking at the near-bare shelves, with the few remains of what would not be spent, and waited to feel the fear, waited for the grandmother's voice, waited for the tightening of seeing the pantry the less. And it came, yes, one last time it came, faint, a whisper only: and if tomorrow it is wanting. But Fartura, at the door of the empty pantry, answered it, in peace, for the last time in this book, and it was an answer sixty years in the making that came out of her at last whole: if tomorrow it is wanting, tomorrow we shall see. Today there is to spare. And what is to spare, grandmother, is not to keep until it rots. It is to put on the table. You did not come to learn this, and you died with dry crusts in your pocket, and I had pity on you all my life without knowing I had pity on myself. But I learned. Late, but I learned. And Alegria is already born knowing it, because I will not teach it to her in reverse. It ended in me, grandmother. The hunger ended in me.

And it was, that, the true leave-taking of the grandmother, the one Fartura had never made at the burial, years before, when she had buried her with a closed hand and kept the little bag of crusts in a drawer not knowing why; it was there, at the door of the empty pantry, on the eve of the feast, that she buried the grandmother at last in earnest, not with grief, but with pity and with gratitude and with a goodbye, the pity for a woman who had lived and died frightened, the gratitude for her having reached the second winter and for there being therefore a Conceição and a Fartura and a Clara and an Alegria, and the goodbye of one who keeps the good inheritance, life, and gives back to the earth the bad inheritance, the fear, and leaves it there, with the grandmother, where it should stay, in the past, on the page of hunger, which is respected and not inhabited.

She closed the door of the empty pantry slowly, not like one who bolts a coffer, but like one who closes the door of a room where someone slept who has now gone, with respect and without fear, and went to bed, and slept, on the eve of the feast, the deepest and lightest sleep of her life, the sleep of one who emptied at last the pantry of fear and filled it, for the next day, with food to give, which is the only plenty that truly warms, because it is the only one that does not rot: the one spent among those one loves, and which, in being spent, instead of running out, multiplies, in the mysterious way the things that are given multiply, and the things that are kept, always, dwindle.
