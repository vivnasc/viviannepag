## 12. The feast on no day

The Sunday dawned on no day at all, a Sunday like so many, with the usual Mass and the usual bell and the village making its usual gestures, and it was on that Sunday like so many that there was made, in Fartura's house, the feast the village would recall long after Fartura was no longer here, not for the plenty of the table, for plentiful tables the village had seen many, but for the rare thing that feast was: a feast that commemorated nothing but being alive, given by a woman who had opened, to make it, the pantry everyone knew she kept like one keeps the soul.

Fartura rose early, with her body wanting to do the gestures of the fear, wanting to go to the pantry to count what was there, and found the pantry empty, and instead of the tightening that emptiness would always have given her, she felt a nervous gladness, the gladness of one about to give a feast, the first of her life, and afraid only that it should go well, which is another fear, a new one, a fear of feast and not of hunger. She put on the good clothes, the kept ones, and this time she put them on without hesitating, and even hung at her neck a chain that had been her mother's and had been in a box for years awaiting an occasion, and the occasion was that one, and Fartura laughed at herself in the mirror, at the life she had spent keeping chains for occasions that did not come, and put on the chain, and it suited her, and it was one more small thing taken out of the drawer of the later for the day of today.

They all came. Clara came, and her husband, and Alegria, who came in running and was the first to sit at the table; the neighbourhood came, the near and the farther off, for a feast in Véspera is the street's; Benvinda of the Shop came, who shut the shop on a Sunday afternoon, a thing she did not do, to go to Fartura's feast for nothing, because Benvinda, who had her own knot with giving and receiving, wished to see up close a woman open her hand, perhaps to learn, and brought, she who charged badly and gave too much, a bunch of flowers from her yard, and it was one of the few times in her life one saw Benvinda give a thing without marking it in the ledger; and there came even, at the end of the afternoon, passing by, the Keeper of the register, who was not one for feasts but of that one wished to go, and stayed a while, at the door, and looked at the table and at Fartura with those eyes that had read the page of hunger, and made the mistress of the house a single nod, a nod of the head that said, without words, you have moved to your page, woman; and Fartura understood the nod, and it was of the afternoon's moments the one that most filled her, because it came from the one who knew where she had come from, from the one who had the book in which was written the year of hunger and her name being born of it.

The table was a thing to astonish, and the astonishment, in the village, was not the plenty, it was the source of the plenty: it was to see on Fartura's table what everyone knew she kept and never gave, the good sausages, the fine oil shining in the dishes, the clean-wheat bread, the tender meat, the preserves of the best years, the honey sweets, all that had slept sixty years behind a cool door, now in the light, on the table, being eaten, with relish, by people who laughed. There were those who, at the table, said low to the one beside them this is what Fartura kept, and there were those who tasted the sausages and shook their heads with respect, because they were good, were of the best eaten in the village, and one saw there, in the quality of what was on the table, what Fartura's pantry had been, and what it had been to hide it a whole life, and how much good had rotted within it awaiting that day that she at last, at fifty-eight, had decided to make.

And Fartura, at the head, ate with them, of the same, of the best, without seeking on the plate the worst, without keeping half, without doing sums; she ate as she had never eaten, not from greed, for it was not greed, but from an old and just hunger, the hunger of sixty years of poor tables in front of full pantries, sating itself at last, that Sunday, all at once, in peace. And she noticed the people eating what was hers, and instead of the tightening of seeing what was hers being spent, she felt a new thing, broad, warm, which was the relish of giving, the relish she had never allowed herself, the relish of seeing on others' faces the pleasure of a thing that came from our hand, and she understood that that relish, that of giving and seeing eaten, was greater than the relish of having, incomparably greater, and that she had spent her life swapping the greater for the lesser, the plenty of giving for the plenty of keeping, and that only now, old, she tasted at last the true one, the one that sates.

At a certain point, Bento rose, slowly, with the care of a man who came near to leaving and stayed, and took up the bottle of old wine, the father-in-law's, the one that had waited longer than the marmalade and had not had, thanks to Fartura, the marmalade's fate, and opened it there, in front of everyone, and the gesture of opening that bottle was, to whoever knew, more eloquent than any toast, because it was the whole house opening in that cork. He served Fartura first, which was not the custom, the man to serve the woman first, and he did it on purpose, and said only, raising his glass, without great words, for he was never one for great words: to Fartura, who opened the pantry. And the village raised their glasses to Fartura who opened the pantry, and some understood the whole weight of that sentence and others understood only that one toasted the mistress of the house, but all toasted, and Fartura, at the head of her feast for nothing, with the empty pantry behind her and the full table in front, with her husband alive and her daughter beside her and her granddaughter eating the best without guilt, felt a thing she had not felt in sixty years and could not, at the moment, name, and that was, simply, plenty, the true one, the one her name had always promised and that she had always put off: not the plenty of having, but that of giving and sharing and being among one's own with an open hand, which is the only plenty that sates, because the other, that of having, however full it be, always leaves hunger, the hunger of not having used it.

Alegria, at a certain point, climbed onto her grandmother's lap, with her mouth smeared with sweet, and asked her, with the frankness of children who say the great things without knowing they are great: Grandma, and tomorrow, do we eat what's left over? And Fartura, who in another time would have heard in that question the confirmation of her fear, what is left over is kept, heard in it, that time, a good thing, and answered, laughing, with the granddaughter on her lap: tomorrow, if there's any left, we eat it; and if there's none, we make it again. For the pantry, Alegria, is not for being full. It is for being filled and emptied, filled and emptied, like the belly, like the village, like life. Full and still is what's no good. And Alegria, who understood half, understood what was needed, which was that with her grandmother one ate the best and that tomorrow was not a coffer, was only another day on which one would also eat, and that stayed in her body, in the child, instead of the fear, and that was the true inheritance passed at that table, more than the sausages and the sweets: there passed a granddaughter who would grow up knowing that the pantry is opened, and with that Fartura's house changed its law not for one generation, but for those to come, which is the farthest a person can send a change, to send it to those not yet born.

The afternoon turned to night, and the feast undid itself as the good feasts undo themselves, slowly, with the people going off full and content, thanking at the door, saying it was long since they had been to a feast like that, without quite knowing why, without knowing that what they had felt of different was the air of a house where, for one day, one spent without fear. And Fartura stayed, at the end, at the door of the house, watching the last guests go down the street, with Bento beside her and the empty pantry behind. And she looked, from up there, at the square, and at the dry fountain in the middle of the square, the fountain that had kept her company all her life confirming her fear, and the fountain was still dry, for fountains do not fill on account of the feasts of men; but Fartura, that night, looked at the dry fountain and no longer saw herself in it, and that was the greatest proof of all, greater than the feast: that the fountain was still dry and she no longer.

Because she had been, for sixty years, a dry fountain, full within and giving nothing; and that night, with the empty pantry and the house still warm from the feast, Fartura was at last a fountain that had run, that had given all its water, that had emptied itself in giving, and that was, for that, at peace, with the deep peace of one who fulfilled what she was made for, which is not to keep the water, it is to give it, even if to give means to run dry, because a fountain that gives its water and runs dry one night fills again from the mountain; but a fountain that never gives, for fear of running dry, that one is dry forever, full and dry at once, which is the worst of drynesses, and it was of that, of the worst, that Fartura, at that feast on no day, at sixty, with her hand at last open, was cured. And she went into the house, with Bento, and the empty pantry held no fear for her, because she knew, now, that an empty pantry after a feast is not a pantry in want: it is a pantry that served, and that will fill again, from the earth, from the work, from the summer that comes, to empty itself again at another feast, which is what pantries, and people, and fountains, and lives, are made to do, fill and give, fill and give, to the end, which will come, but which comes, to the woman who has learned this, without catching her with a closed hand.
