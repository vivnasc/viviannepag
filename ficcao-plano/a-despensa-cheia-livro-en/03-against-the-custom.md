## 3. Against the custom

Clara was the one thing Fartura had done in her life without closing her hand, and she had done it without noticing, the way the good things one did not plan are done: the daughter had come, had been loved with an open hand because a mother's love does not fit in a pantry, and had grown up watching her mother keep everything but her.

And perhaps it was for that, for having been the one thing the mother did not keep, that Clara turned out the opposite. Early, still a girl, she began to chafe at the pantry, not in words, for in Fartura's house those words were not spoken, but in small gestures of mute rebellion: she ate the good cheese when her mother was not looking, opened the feast biscuits and faced the lecture, said mother, this will spoil, and got the it's-to-keep of always. There was a time, in her adolescence, when the thing between the two of them came to be war, a silent war of the pantry, Clara opening what her mother closed, the mother closing what Clara opened, and neither of the two knowing that they were not fighting each other, they were each fighting, from their own side, the same dead grandmother, the same hand closing in the firelight of the same fire, Clara fleeing it, the mother obeying it, and the chain passing between them in the shape of a quarrel.

And when she made her own house, Clara took care to make it the opposite of her mother's: she had little, for she was young and earned little, but the little she had, she ate; she did not keep the best, she put it on the table; and when her daughter was born, in a gesture the village remarked on and that Fartura felt like a needle without knowing why, Clara did not set on her a name of anxious promise, of the kind the family had given for generations, did not set on her Fartura or Abastança or Provisão; she set on her Alegria, against the whole custom, like one who cuts a chain, like one who says, without saying it, this one will not inherit the fear. Fartura, on hearing the granddaughter's name, said nothing, but she felt, and the thing she felt, and was ashamed of, was almost a reproach, as if the daughter, by setting a name of joy on the child, were speaking ill of the names of anxiety, speaking ill, at bottom, of her, of the mother, of the grandmother, of the whole house of closed hands, and Fartura was a long time understanding that Clara was not speaking ill of anyone; she was only trying to have the chain stop at her, and a name was the first stone she had to cross the river by.

Fartura had never quite understood the daughter, and the daughter knew it. There was between the two a solid love and an equally solid strangeness, the love and the strangeness of those who want each other much and understand each other not at all, because they saw the world from two opposite places: Fartura saw it from the place of one who fears to lose, and Clara from the place of one who fears not to live, and from those two positions there is no easy bridge, there is only love crossing by boat, slowly, never quite reaching the far bank. They argued little, for neither of them was one to argue, but they stumbled much, in those small things where people's wounds touch without meaning to: Clara offered the mother a good thing and the mother kept it, and Clara was hurt; the mother spared the daughter an expense and the daughter felt treated like a poor woman, and was hurt the other way; and the two went on wanting each other over the top of the stumbles, which is how families want each other, rarely in a straight line.

That week, after Alegria's question and the night of the open hand, Fartura went to her daughter's house to take her some things, as she did, and the visit, which used to be the same as all the rest, was different, because Fartura went different, with the chink of light still open in the inner door, seeing what she had always seen without noticing.

Clara's house was poor compared to Fartura's pantry, but alive in a way Fartura's house was not. In the kitchen, in plain sight, on a plate, was the good fruit, the ripe, the kind to be eaten that day, not the kind kept for later; and there was a cake begun, half eaten, on no one's day, with the knife still beside it and the crumbs on the cloth, a cake being eaten through the week because it was wanted, and not a whole cake kept for a visit that might come; and Alegria, coming in from school, went to her mother's little pantry, a pantry of four things, without even a door, only an open shelf, and took from it what she wished, and ate, and no one said to her it's to keep, and Fartura saw that, the granddaughter opening the pantry without fear, eating the best there was because the best there was was for eating, and felt two things at once, a distress and an envy, the distress of one who sees waste, and the envy, deep, shameful, of one who never, in sixty years, ate an apple just because she fancied it.

Mother, said Clara, at a certain point, while Alegria ate, and she said it gently, for Clara had learned that with her mother gentleness went further than reason, mother, I know you brought all this, and I thank you, but I don't want Alegria always eating the worst thinking she's safe. I want her to eat the best while it's good, you understand? I don't want her keeping her life. And she said it without hardness, but with a firmness that was new, the firmness of a mother defending her daughter from an inheritance, and Fartura understood, in that sentence, that Clara was not only speaking of the pantry: she was setting a border, she was saying to the mother this far, with me you did what you did, with your granddaughter you do not, and there was in it, beneath the gentleness, a lioness firmness that Fartura, instead of taking ill, admired, because it was a firmness she herself had never had, the firmness of protecting a child not from hunger, but from the fear of hunger, which is harder to see and more important to fight.

And Fartura, who on another day would have answered with the it's-to-keep of always, with the you-don't-know-what-hunger-is, with the the-day-will-come-when-you-thank-whoever-kept, that day did not answer at once, because Clara's sentence, I don't want her keeping her life, struck her in a place that Alegria's question had already opened, and Fartura understood, in a flash that hurt her, that this was exactly what she had done: she had kept her life. She had not kept food; she had kept her life. She had put it in the pantry, behind the cool door, with the year written on the lid, and stretched it, and eaten always the worst of it so there would be worse to eat the next day, and the best of her life was now dark and over-sugared with age on a shelf, rotting from being kept, waiting for a disaster that did not come, while she went on dying on her feet, slowly, in plenty.

You think I kept my life, said Fartura, and it was not a question, it was a thought spoken aloud without meaning to, and Clara, surprised that her mother had heard instead of answering, that her mother had received the sentence instead of parrying it with the shield of custom, was also left without a ready answer, and said only, gentler still, I think you were very afraid, mother. All your life. And that the fear was real, I know it was, the grandmother went through what she went through and you heard what you heard, I caught a little of it too, I still knew the great-grandmother with the crusts in her apron. But the fear has no reason now, mother. There's no hunger now. There hasn't been hunger in this house for thirty years. And you go on keeping the bread for a winter that has already passed.

It was the most direct thing Clara had ever said to her mother, and she said it without hardness, with her eyes burning a little, and Fartura received it as one receives a diagnosis one feared to hear: without defending herself, because there was no defence, and with a relief hidden beneath the fright, the relief of someone at last saying aloud the thing she felt at bottom and could not name. There's no hunger now. There hasn't been hunger for thirty years. And she keeping the bread for a winter that has already passed. It was so simple, said like that, and so true, and it had been there, in plain sight, for thirty years, and Fartura only now heard it, because there are truths that are in front of one's eyes and that are only seen when a chink opens and the light strikes them, and the chink, in that case, had been a child's question and a daughter's firmness in defending that child from what she herself had received.

Alegria, who ate her apple, a stranger to the grown women's talk, asked suddenly, with her mouth full, Grandma, do you want some? And she held out to her grandmother the apple begun, with the mark of the little teeth, in the simple gesture of children who share what they are eating without making anything of it, without calculation, without the weight that grown people put on the giving and the receiving, only because the apple was good and the grandmother was there. And Fartura looked at the held-out apple, at the good apple, ripe, bitten, that was of the day and not of the later, and felt the urge to refuse it, out of habit, out of that old reflex of not taking the best from anyone, of keeping the worst for herself so there would be better for others, and she was already about to say you eat it, child, it's yours, when she heard herself say another thing, a thing that came out of her from a place she did not govern: I want it.

And she took the apple, and bit into it, there, in her daughter's kitchen, a bite of a good apple just because the granddaughter offered it and she wanted it, with no day to it, without keeping it, without earning it with any suffering, and the apple was sweet, was at its point, and Fartura, who had eaten the worst for sixty years, felt in her mouth the taste of the best with a kind of vertigo, like one who tastes a forbidden thing and discovers that the forbidding was her own, hers alone, and that on the far side of the forbidding there was not disaster, there was only a sweet apple on some afternoon. It was a tiny instant, a bite, and it was, nonetheless, one of the hardest things Fartura had done in her life, because to do it she had had to disobey a hand that had been closing for three generations, and the apple, in her mouth, tasted of that, tasted of the disobedience and the sweetness at once, which are, when tasted together, the exact flavour of freedom.

Clara saw, and said nothing, and did well to say nothing, because any word would have spoiled it; but she kept the scene for herself, the mother eating the daughter's apple, and understood that something had shifted in the mother, something that she, Clara, had been trying to shift for years with reasons and could not, and that had shifted, after all, with a child's question and an apple offered without second thought. The deep things shift like that, Clara thought, not with those who push them from outside, with those who loosen them from within, and Alegria, without knowing it, had been the lever that she, the daughter of the open hand, had never managed to be, because Clara pushed with reason, and reason strikes the wound and closes it tighter, and Alegria pushed nothing, only offered an apple, and the offering without weight went in where the reason with weight had never gone.

Fartura went home at the end of the afternoon with the strange sense of one who brings back less weight than she took, though she took an empty basket of the things she gave and came back with it empty all the same. She passed by the square, and this time she looked at the dry fountain, looked at it on purpose, as she had not looked in years, and the dry fountain, which had always confirmed her fear, the fountain that said water goes, the full is left empty, that afternoon said another thing to her, or she heard another thing, which may be the same: it said to her that a dry fountain is not a spared fountain. A dry fountain is a fountain that gives water to no one. And that she, Fartura, with the full pantry and the poor table, with the best kept to rot and the worst in her mouth, had made of herself a dry fountain, full within and giving nothing, neither to herself nor to her own, and that this was not being safe, it was only a slower and more polite way of going hungry in the midst of plenty.

At home, Bento asked her how the visit had gone, and Fartura, putting the basket in its place, told him, without looking at him, in a voice she did not recognise herself, so light it was: I ate an apple. And Bento, who had known her for forty years and who knew how to read in her what she did not say, understood that his wife was not telling him about fruit, and asked no more, but that night, going to bed, he noticed that Fartura, before putting out the light, lingered a moment looking at her right hand, opening and closing it slowly in the candlelight, like one who tries out a joint that has long been stiff and begins, with care, to see whether it still bends, and like one who discovers, with surprise, that it bends, that it always bent, that the stiffness was not in the joint, it was in the fear of using it.
