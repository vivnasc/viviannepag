## 6. The first good supper

In the week after the marmalade, Fartura tried to do a simple thing, and discovered there was nothing simple in it: she tried to put on the table, on some supper, the best of what she had.

The idea came to her at night, after throwing out the pails, and it was an idea so small it shamed her: to make Bento, once, a good supper. Not for a feast, not for a visit, not to celebrate anything; a good supper just because the two of them were alive and had the means, which was, after all, reason enough, though Fartura had spent her life thinking it was not. The idea, small as it was, cost her her sleep, because the fear, which hung about but was not beaten, found it dangerous and came to say so: what for, spending the good on a supper with no reason, keep it for when someone comes who deserves it, keep it for an occasion. And Fartura, lying down, answered it for the first time with a question that disarmed it: and what if the occasion is this one? And what if the occasion is our being alive, Bento and I, in this kitchen, tonight, with health and with the means? What better occasion are you waiting for, that never comes? And the fear did not know how to answer, because the fear knows only great occasions, and life, almost all of it, is made of small occasions, and whoever waits for the great ones to live, does not live.

In the morning she went to the Shop and bought, for the first time without asking for what was going off, what was best: a piece of tender meat, of the kind she usually left for others, some new broad beans, a cheese at its point, and, in a fit that made her blush in front of Benvinda, a bottle of wine that was not to keep, it was to drink that night. Benvinda marked it all in the ledger, and this time, seeing what Fartura was taking, she looked up and said only, with a half-smile that was not mockery, is it a feast today, Fartura?, and Fartura, red, answered it's no feast, and it was the truest and hardest thing she could say, because that was exactly it, it was a feast on no day at all, and it was for being no feast that it cost so much. And Benvinda, who saw everything, understood that that purchase was, for Fartura, an enormous thing disguised as a small one, and instead of marking it on tick, as always, she said, on an impulse, this one you take outright and with no record, to christen it, and Fartura, who was about to refuse out of habit, held her tongue, and paid on the nail, and it was also that, paying outright for a good thing to spend on the very day, a small loosening of the hand, of the kind that go on making, summed up, the great loosening.

At home, she cooked with her hands trembling a little, and it was ridiculous that they trembled, and she knew it was ridiculous, but the hand closed for sixty years does not open without protest, and at each gesture the voice came to her, the grandmother's voice, the inner voice, saying this is too much, this isn't spent on an ordinary day, keep half, leave the meat for tomorrow, the wine corks again and lasts, what do you open the whole of it for. And Fartura cooked and answered the voice, not aloud, which would have seemed mad, but within, in a dialogue that was the true matter of her life in those days: she answered today I don't keep, today I eat, and the voice insisted and tomorrow?, and she said tomorrow there will be, and the voice said and if there isn't?, and there, in that and-if-there-isn't, was the knot of it all, the root fear, the house of hunger, and Fartura, for the first time, instead of yielding to the and-if-there-isn't as she had yielded all her life, kept it in front of her and looked at it, and asked herself, slowly, with the spoon in her hand: and if there isn't, what? Do I go hungry one night? At fifty-eight, with the house at ease, with my daughter near, with the village around, with the pantry still half full, what hunger am I going to suffer for having eaten a good supper on a Thursday? And the voice did not know how to answer, because the voice was of another time, of a true winter fifty years ago, and had no answer for the now, only had the fear, and the fear, set in the light, with no winter to sustain it, is only an old habit pretending to be prophecy.

She laid the table with care, and that too was new, to lay the table with care for the two of them alone, for she only laid the table with care for visits, and for visits with the worst; she put on the good cloth, the one that was kept, the plates that were not the everyday ones, and in doing it she felt herself almost doing a forbidden thing, using for herself what was kept for others, and she had again to answer the voice, which said the good cloth spoils, gets soiled, and she said a cloth that is never used serves nothing either, better a stain on a used cloth than a clean cloth in a drawer a whole life, for a cloth is to be put on the table, as food is to be eaten, and as life, she understood slowly, is to be lived, and not kept clean in a drawer waiting for an occasion worthy of it.

They sat at the table, the two of them, with the tender meat and the beans and the good cheese and the opened wine on the good cloth, and at first Fartura could hardly eat, so strange it was to eat the best, so deep was the reflex of seeking on the plate the worst to begin with; she took the tender meat and her hand wanted to leave it for last, or for Bento, or for tomorrow, and Fartura had to force herself, to carry to her mouth the best morsel on purpose, against the reflex, like one relearning to walk. But Bento, on the far side of the table, ate slowly, with a silent and enormous relish, without the knots she had, and at a certain point he set down his fork and said, without drama, in the barest voice Fartura had heard from him in forty years: it's forty years since we ate like this, Fartura. Forty years. And it was no reproach, it was almost wonder, the wonder of a man who discovers, at the table of his own house, that he had been hungry for forty years without knowing it, and that his hunger was not of the belly, it was the same as Fartura's by another road, the hunger of one who lives beside plenty and does not touch it, the hunger of one who eats what is left over beside one who keeps what is good.

And Fartura looked at her husband eating with that relish, and the guilt she expected to feel, the guilt of spending the best, did not come, or came and was swept away by a greater thing, which was to see Bento happy with so small a thing, a good supper on some night, and to understand that that small thing had been within the two of them's reach every day of forty years, and that she, with her closed hand, had denied it to him and to herself, not from malice, never from malice, but from a fear that in the end had eaten their life without ever having saved them from anything. Forty years of bad suppers in front of a full pantry. Forty years waiting for disaster and losing life in the waiting for it. And Fartura, at that table, ate at last, ate her share of the tender meat, drank of the opened wine, and it was good, it was all good, and the world did not end, and disaster did not knock at the door drawn by the smell of plenty, and the next morning there was still food in the pantry, less, but there was, and the sun rose all the same, and life went on, lighter by one supper.

There was a moment, midway through the supper, when the two of them fell silent, with the wine half drunk, and Bento said, looking at his wife over the candle-flame: do you remember when we married, Fartura, and we ate what there was, and laughed? And Fartura remembered, and the memory came to her whole, with smell and all, the way the memories come that were kept well for never having been spent. The first winter married, in the small house that was theirs before this one, with a table that limped on one leg and that Bento shimmed with a bit of roof-tile, and a broth of cabbage and little more, and Bento breaking the bread and giving her the crumb and keeping the crust for himself, and she protesting and he laughing, that the crust is what has the taste, woman, the crumb is water. They had nothing and ate everything, because the little there was either was eaten or was lost, for there was no pantry to keep it in, no high shelf, no year written on any lid; there was a pot, and what was in the pot was eaten at night, and the next day another was made with what there was, and it was so, day by day, with an open hand by force and not by virtue, because poverty compels the open hand the same way plenty tempted hers to close. She remembered one night, the first they killed a rabbit they had raised themselves, and how they had eaten it all, the two of them, without keeping a piece for the next day, and how they had laughed for being full, and how Bento had said to her, at that limping table, by the light of an oil lamp, today we're rich, Fartura, today we eat meat; and they had been rich, that night, richer with one rabbit eaten than sixty years later with the fullest pantry in the village, because wealth, she understood now, at the table of the good supper, was not in what had been kept, it was in what was eaten with relish and with whom one loved, and the two of them, in that first winter, with nothing, had been more plentiful than they would ever be again until that Thursday.

When did we start keeping everything, he asked, and Fartura knew how to answer, because she knew: it started when the house eased. While we were poor, we ate the little, that didn't keep. It was when we came to have that I began to keep. Plenty is what closed my hand, Bento. Poverty never closed it; it was plenty. And the two of them stayed thinking on this, on the strange thing it was, to have had an open hand in poverty and a closed one in plenty, and they understood, without saying it in those words, that Fartura's fear had never been of real hunger, that one they had been through together without her closing her hand; it was of possible hunger, of imagined hunger, of the hunger that abundance seemed to announce, and that for that reason had only appeared when abundance appeared, like a shadow that exists only where there is light.

But it would be a lie, and this book does not tell it, to say the hand opened that night for good. It did not open. Two days later, Fartura caught herself buying again what was going off, keeping again the good, seeking on the plate the worst; the habit of sixty years does not die in one supper, and it would come back many times yet in this book, because that is how the deep things change, not in a straight ascent, but in a back-and-forth, two steps opening the hand and one closing it, and whoever expects to change all at once gives up at the first relapse, and whoever understands that the relapse is part of the road goes on. Fartura, that time, relapsed and noticed she relapsed, which was the new thing: before she closed the hand and thought herself right; now she closed the hand and noticed it, and heard herself saying the it's-to-keep of always and thought there I go, and that there-I-go, that small distance between doing and seeing oneself do, was the chink widening, slowly, from within.

What was left of that first good supper was not the changed pantry, which did not change yet, it was another thing, more hidden and more firm: there was left the proof. Fartura now had, against the grandmother's voice, a proof of her own, lived in the body, that to eat the best on an ordinary day did not call disaster, that the open hand did not let in the winter, that on the far side of the fear there was not hunger, there was a good supper and a happy husband and a night of light sleep. And a lived proof is worth more than all the reasons Clara had given her over the years, because reasons go in by the head and the head does not govern fear; the proof goes in by the body, and it is in the body that the fear dwells. The grandmother had set the fear in her body with a hand that closed in the firelight; and it had taken, sixty years on, another gesture in the body, a hand that takes up a fork and carries to the mouth the best without punishment, to begin, only begin, to take it out of there.

And that night, before lying down, Fartura did another small thing that cost her more than it seemed: she went to the pantry chest, lifted its lid, and took from it the white coverlet, the good one, the trousseau one, the one that had yellowed at the fold from forty years kept waiting for a worthy visit. She shook it out, spread it, and the brownish crease was there, the mark of the stopped time, that no longer came out; but Fartura, instead of folding it again with grief, put it on their bed, on her own bed, and smoothed it with her hands, and when Bento came into the room and saw it, he stopped at the door, and understood, without her saying anything, what that coverlet was there to say, and said nothing either, but lay down beneath it with the care of one who lies down beneath a thing that waited forty years to serve, and the two of them slept, for the first time, beneath the good coverlet, crease and all, and the crease did not spoil the sleep, rather gave it deeper, because there is a special rest in using at last what was kept too long, the rest of a thing that fulfils, late, what it was made for.

That night, going to bed, Fartura did a new thing: instead of doing the sum of the pantry to call sleep, she remembered the supper, Bento's face, the taste of the meat, the wine, his question about the time when they ate what they had, and she fell asleep not counting what she had kept, but savouring what she had spent, and discovered, with surprise, that one could fall asleep so, that what was spent and was good warms as much as what was kept and waits, or more, because the kept warms in the head, with the sum, and the spent warms in the body, with the memory, and it is the body, and not the head, that needs warming to sleep at ease. And she dreamed, that night, a thing she did not dream, she dreamed of the house full of people eating, a long table and people laughing and the pantry with its door open, and she woke in the morning with the dream still in her mouth, and did not understand, yet, that the dream was a plan, that it was life showing her, in dream, before the head thought it, what she would end up doing.
