## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Fartura, daughter of people of the village, born in the second of the years of hunger, opened the pantry she had kept sixty years and made, with the best there was in it, a feast on no day at all, to which the village came, and which was recalled long after her. Be it set down that she ate, at her own table, the best of her own house, a thing that seems small and that took her a whole life, and which is, of those this Keeper records, among the hardest a person does: not to give to one who is hungry, for that the heart teaches, but to eat without guilt what one has, when one grew up learning that to have is the eve of losing.

The register does not usually keep feasts, for feasts are many and the book is of births and deaths and marriages, of the comings in and goings out of life. But this Keeper, who saw the page of hunger and the small names it took, understood that Fartura's feast was, in its way, a death and a birth: the death of a fear that had come into that house three generations before, by a hand that closed in the firelight, and the birth of a new law, the law of a house that learned, late but in time, that the pantry is for filling and emptying, and not for keeping until it rots. There died, at that feast, the hunger that no longer had a belly to dwell in and dwelt in the fear; and there was born, at the table, a girl named Alegria who shall grow up without it, because the grandmother, instead of setting it in her body as it had been set in her own, said to her, with peaches in her hand, eat the best, it's for today.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Fartura, in a full pantry before a poor table, in a hand that closes over the little that is already much, let her know what Fartura learned and what this house records: that abundance was not made to be had, it was made to be lived; that the best kept for a perfect day rots awaiting it, because perfect days do not come, they are made; and that the greatest courage, for whoever inherited the fear of not having, is not to keep more: it is to open the hand, to put on the table what one had been keeping for disaster, and to make of it, among those one loves, on a Sunday like so many, a feast.

Fartura moved to her page. That of hunger was left behind, read and respected, but behind. And the fountain in the square is still dry, for fountains have their time and their mystery; but in Fartura's house, since that Sunday, when there is, one gives, and when one gives, one eats, and what is left over is not bolted away: one keeps the little there is to keep, and of the much one makes a table, which is what the much asks, and what this village, book by book, is slowly learning.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
