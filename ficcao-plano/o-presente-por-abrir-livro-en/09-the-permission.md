## 9. The permission

The courage Dádiva spent a life not having, that of receiving, did not come to her all at once nor by a great gesture; it came in pieces, in small gestures, and the first was so small and so hard that she knew, in doing it, that it was the hardest thing she had done in her life.

She had promised herself to open the parcel the day after Estima's night, and when the next day came she did not open it, because she perceived, drawing near to the furniture, that she was not ready, that to open the parcel all at once, after three years, was to leap an abyss, and that she, who had never received anything, did not know how to receive all at once the whole love of a daughter wrapped for three years; she needed to learn first, with small things, to let in, before she could let in that. And that was what she did. She decided, with the cold clearness of one who knows herself, that she was going to train at receiving, as one trains a muscle never used, slowly, with small things, until she had the strength for the great one.

And she was not ashamed to think of it so, as a training, because she perceived that it was just that: that most people learn to receive as children, naturally, receiving from their parents without thinking of it, and that she, whose childhood had taught her the opposite, had to learn it already a woman, by force, on purpose, like one who learns to read late, with effort and without shame of the effort, because what is shameful is not needing to learn a thing late, it is dying without having learned it from the pride of not needing it.

And the training was this, and it was heroic for being so tiny: she began to let herself be given to. When a neighbour brought her a thing, instead of the there was no need, Dádiva forced herself to say thank you, and to take the thing, and to keep it, and it was an agony, each time, to feel the hand held out towards her, to feel herself on the side of receiving, but she forced herself, and said thank you, and the sky did not fall, and the neighbour went away content, and Dádiva was left with the thing in her hands and her heart beating from the effort, and thought I did it, I received, and I did not come down to the floor, and I am standing all the same. And each time she received a small thing without returning it, the door of entry, which had been bolted a lifetime, bent a little the rusted hinge, and Dádiva went on discovering, with surprise, that receiving did not diminish her, that people did not look down on her for accepting, that they looked at her, on the contrary, with more love, because in receiving from them she let them in, and whoever lets us in becomes nearer to us, and more loved, than whoever only gives to us and never lets us give to them.

There were setbacks, because there always are. There were days when her hand drew back again of itself, when the there was no need came out before she thought it, and when she, afterwards, scolded herself, there I go again; but the new thing was that, was noticing it, was hearing herself refuse and perceiving that she refused, and each time she heard herself, the next time she refused less, and the muscle, slowly, setbacks and all, gained strength, and Dádiva went on learning, gesture by gesture, dish by dish, thank-you by thank-you, the thing that had seemed to her impossible her whole life and that was, after all, like everything, only a matter of practising it until it stopped hurting.

With Estima, the training was deeper and sweeter. Dádiva began to let the daughter give her things. She let her make her supper, once, and stayed to eat it while the daughter served, and it was strange to her like everything, to be seated being served by the daughter, but she saw Estima's happiness, and she stayed. She let her take her for a walk, let her pay for the coffee, let her, at last, be the one who gives, and each time she let herself, she saw the daughter fill, and perceived, for good, in the body, what Brandura had told her: that in letting the daughter give to her, she was making the daughter the greatest present, that of letting her love her mother, and that her refusal of a lifetime, which she had thought protected the daughter from overburdening her, had been after all the opposite, had been to deprive her of the love she wished to give, had been to leave her with full hands and nowhere to set them, three years, twenty years, a life.

Mother, Estima said to her, one afternoon, seeing the mother accept from her a small gift without returning it, mother, you are different. You are letting. And Dádiva said, with a new humility, I am learning, child. Late, but I am learning. Brandura told me I loved by halves, that I gave and did not let myself be given to. And she was right. I spent a life giving to all of you, and never let any of you give me anything, and I thought that was being good, and it was only being alone. I am learning to receive, child. And it costs me as giving never cost me. But I am learning.

And there was an afternoon when Dádiva did the thing the Keeper had asked of her, to receive on purpose, upright, with health, without the fever forcing it. She went to the house of a neighbour, one of those she had always given to, and instead of going to give, she went to ask. She went to ask a small help, a thing she did not truly need, but invented on purpose, to force herself to hold out a hand, and knocked at the neighbour's door, and said, with her heart pounding, with her voice trembling, with the mother's shadow shouting within her that she was coming down to the floor: oh neighbour, could you give me a hand with a thing? And the neighbour, astonished that Dádiva, the angel, the one who always gave, should ask something of her, opened the door and her heart, and helped her with an enormous gladness, with the gladness of being able, at last, to repay the angel a little of the much she had received, and Dádiva, in receiving that help she had invented only to train herself, felt, instead of the floor she feared, a warm thing, the thing of making someone happy by letting them give, and perceived, upright, with health, by choice, that the Keeper was right: that to receive by choice was the courage she lacked, and that she, at that door, asking a help she did not need, had just had it, for the first time in her life, on purpose.

She went home from that afternoon with a lightness she did not know, the lightness of one who has come down from the wall of her own will and discovered that the floor was not her mother's floor, it was a level place, beside the others, of equal to equal, where one gives and receives by turns and where, therefore, one is not alone. And she looked at the parcel, coming in, and for the first time was not afraid of it. The muscle of receiving, which she had trained those days with small things, was stronger; she had already received from neighbours, already received from the daughter, already asked on purpose; and the parcel, which for three years had been the impossible thing, the daughter's love she could not let in, began now to seem to her not an abyss, but the next step, the greatest, yes, but the next, in the same direction as the small ones, the direction of entry, the direction of letting herself be loved.

And she decided, that night, that she was not going to open the parcel in secret, alone, quickly, like one fulfilling a thing that costs. She was going to open it with Estima. She was going to call the daughter, and say to her come, child, let us open it together, and she was going to receive the present in front of the one who had given it, and let the daughter see her mother receive, at last, the love she had put in her hands three years before, because to receive the present alone would still be to receive by half, would be not to give the daughter the pleasure of seeing it received; and Dádiva, who had learned at last that to receive is a present one gives to the one who loves us, wished to make the daughter that present entire: that of letting her see the mother open, at last, after a lifetime, the door of entry, and let in what the daughter, for three years, had been waiting at the door.
