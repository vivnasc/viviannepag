## 2. The shame that could be seen from the street

Dádiva learned not to receive in a house where much was received, and that was the knot: that everything that came into that house came from outside, as a favour, and came with a shame that could be seen from the street.

The house of Dádiva's mother was one of the poor of the village, one of those that live on what others give, and the mother, Conceição, was a woman of receiving all her life, not because she wished to, but because there was no other way: she received the bread that was left over in the great houses, she received the clothes the children of others no longer wore, she received the alms disguised as a loan and the loan disguised as alms, and she received all this with a deep shame, a shame that bent her back and lowered her eyes, a shame that Dádiva, as a child, saw in her mother each time someone stopped at their door with a thing to give.

And she saw it in a way she never forgot: she saw her mother open the door, perceive that another gift from outside was coming, and make herself small, shrink, say thank you in a voice that begged pardon for existing, and take what they gave her as one takes an ember, quickly, so that the humiliation of receiving should last little in the hands. And then, the door closed, the mother would stand a moment, with the received thing in her hands, and have on her face a shadow that Dádiva learned to know before she could name it, the shadow of one obliged, once more, to hold out a hand, and the child, in the corner, looked at that shadow on her mother's face and decided, without words, in the way children decide the things that will govern their whole lives: not I. I shall not be like this. I shall not receive. I shall be on the other side.

There was one scene in particular that stayed in Dádiva above all the rest, and it was small, of the kind that seem nothing. There came to their door, one winter, the maid of a great house, with a basket of things left over, and said, without malice, with the indifference of one running an errand, the mistress sends this for the poor of the lower street. And Dádiva's mother, hearing that, the poor of the lower street, understood that it was she, that she was the poor of the lower street, that this was what the houses above called them, and she received the basket with her head bowed, and the child Dádiva, beside her, saw her mother called poor at her own door and bow her head, and felt a shame that was not hers and that was hers forever, and decided, in that instant, with more force than ever, that never, never would anyone call her the poor of the lower street, that she would be on the side of the one who sends the basket, and not on the side of the one who receives it with bowed head, whatever it cost.

Because there were, in the village, the two sides, and the child Dádiva saw them well: the side of the houses that gave, the great ones, the upper ones, where the hand stretched outward, full, from above downward, with the ease of those who have; and the side of the houses that received, the poor ones, the lower ones, where the hand stretched out to receive, empty, from below upward, with the shame of those who need. And the child Dádiva, of the lower house, of the hand held up, learned that those two sides were the map of the world, and that on one you stood and on the other you were bent, and that the only salvation, the only way of not having on her face the mother's shadow, was to cross from one side to the other, was to rise, was to come to be one of those who give, whatever it cost, because to give was to stand and to receive was to be bent, and Dádiva, more than anything in life, more than being rich or loved or happy, wished never again to be bent as she had seen her mother bent at the door, with the shame that could be seen from the street.

And she succeeded. That was, at bottom, the work of her life: to cross from one side to the other. She worked, married an honest if poor man, made her house with the sweat of the two of them, and as soon as she had enough for herself she began to give, to give to others, to become the one who gives, and each thing she gave was a brick in the wall that separated her from her mother's house, was a proof that she was no longer of the hand held up, was already of the hand held down, was already on the good side, the side of standing. And the village, which did not know this, which saw only the generous Dádiva, kept confirming the work for her with its praise, you are an angel, Dádiva, and each praise was one more brick, and the wall rose, closed itself, until one day, without Dádiva noticing, the wall that separated her from her mother's house separated her also from everyone, because a wall that only lets out and never lets in does not separate only from the bad, it separates from everything, and Dádiva was left on the inside, alone, standing, yes, but alone, with the love of others on the outside, knocking at the door of a wall she had built against her mother's shame and that had ended by being built against the whole world.

Because what the child Dádiva could not understand, and what the woman Dádiva spent a life not understanding, is that her mother's error was not receiving: it was the shame. The mother did not bend because she received; she bent because she believed receiving diminished her, because the village had taught her that to hold out a hand was to be less, and it was the belief, and not the gesture, that put the shadow on her face. There were, in the village, those who received with their heads high, who accepted help without bending, who said thank you and stood all the same, because they knew that to receive takes nothing from anyone, that between the one who gives and the one who receives there is a trade of equals and not a fall; but Dádiva's mother did not know this, and the daughter learned the error entire, shadow and all, and instead of learning to receive with her head high, which was what the mother lacked, she learned not to receive at all, which was worse, because the mother at least received, and lived, and the children ate; and Dádiva, by refusing to receive so as never to have the shadow, refused also what is good to receive, love, care, a lap, and made of herself, by dint of not wishing to be her mother's held-out hand, a woman who held out her hand for nothing, neither for the bread of others, which was humiliating, nor for the love of others, which was not, but which she, in her fear, no longer knew how to tell apart.

And this was the saddest of all: that Dádiva, in fleeing her mother's shame, had lost the ability to tell bread from love. For the mother, they were different things: bread was received with shame, but love, that of the daughter, that of the husband, that the mother received well, that did not bend her. Dádiva, however, had shut the door to everything that came from outside, without distinction, and treated the love they wished to give her as she treated alms, drew back from it, returned it, defended herself from it, because in her head, built in girlhood, everything that came in from outside was alms, was the lower house, was the shadow, and she no longer knew that there are things that come in from outside and do not bend us, that lift us, and that love is the chief of them, and that to refuse it, in the name of not being bent, is the surest way of being left, at the same time, standing and alone.

The mother had died long ago, and had died, as she had lived, receiving, now the care of her children, and Dádiva had cared for her at the end with a fierce devotion, but even there, even caring for the dying mother, Dádiva was on the side of giving, and the mother on the side of receiving, and Dádiva never perceived that the greatest thing she could have given her mother, at the end, was not the care, it was to let her see the daughter receive without shame, was to show her that the shadow was over, that the daughter she had wished to spare the cold of the held-out hand had learned, at last, to hold it out without cold; but that Dádiva could not give her mother, because she did not have it, and the mother died watching her daughter give, give, give, and perhaps she died content to see her so on the upper side, not knowing, poor woman, that she had condemned her, with the name and with the shame, to a standing solitude that was, in its way, as cold as the held-out hand she had wished to spare her.

It was of all this, or of a part of all this, that Dádiva began to remember in those days, after Brandura's sentence, after looking at the daughter's parcel in another way. She did not remember in these words, for Dádiva was not one to explain herself to herself; she remembered in images, in the mother's face at the door, in the shadow, in the maid of the great house, in the poor of the lower street, in the child's decision in the corner, and there came into her, slowly, an understanding that hurt: that the wall that had saved her from her mother's shame was leaving her as alone as the shame had left the mother bent, and that perhaps the two of them, the mother who received with bowed head and the daughter who did not receive at all, were after all the same wound, seen from two sides, the wound of a house that learned that to receive is to come down, and that passed that wound from mother to daughter changing only its form, from the shame of receiving to the fear of receiving, which seem opposites and are siblings.

And she thought of Estima, and it hurt her. Because if she, Dádiva, had made of not-receiving her wall, what would she be teaching the daughter? What inheritance did Estima carry from a mother who never let her give anything? And Dádiva, who had spent a life fleeing being her mother, perceived, with a shiver, that perhaps she was being the mother in another way, leaving the daughter a lack as the mother had left her a shadow, and that the chain, whichever it was, of shame or of fear, went on passing, and passed by the unopened parcel on top of the furniture, which was, she perceived that night, not a forgotten present, but a lesson she was giving the daughter without meaning to: the lesson that in that house one does not receive, neither the bread, nor the love, nor a birthday present, and that to give to Dádiva is to throw a thing onto a piece of furniture where it stays unopened, gathering dust, three years, a life, always.
