## 3. The daughter who wished to give

Estima loved her mother with a full love and held against her a small and ancient resentment, and the love and the resentment were, at bottom, the same thing: Estima wished to give to her mother, and the mother did not let her, and there is no resentment deeper, between those who love each other, than that of not being allowed to give back.

From a child, Estima had brought her mother things. A drawing, a flower from the path, a pretty stone, and later, a woman, a kerchief, a sweet, a day of rest, and everything, everything, the mother returned, with the there was no need, with the keep it for yourself, with the I need nothing. And Estima grew up with the strange sense of being, in her bond with her mother, always the debtor, always the one who receives and never manages to pay, not from any meanness of the mother, who was the opposite of mean, but from excess, from a generosity that left no room at all for the daughter's, that took up the whole side of giving and pushed Estima to the side of receiving and held her there, and Estima, who loved her mother, wished only, once in her life, to change sides, to be the one who gives and the mother the one who receives, and never managed it, and that, over the years, became a deep lack, the lack of a daughter who cannot be a daughter entire because the mother does not let her.

Estima remembered a time, as a small girl, when she had saved coins for months to buy her mother a kerchief on her birthday, and how she had given the kerchief to her mother with her heart overflowing, and how the mother had said to her, with all tenderness and no malice, ah child, what did you spend your little money on me for, keep it for yourself, and how she, a girl, had been left with the kerchief back in her hands, and with a disappointment she could explain to no one, the disappointment of having her present refused with love, which is the most confusing of disappointments, because there is no one to complain to, the mother was good, the mother only did not want the daughter to spend, and yet the daughter was left with the present ungiven and with her heart aching, and learned, that day, a thing that marked her: that to give to her mother was useless, that the mother did not receive, and that her love, Estima's, would have to live a whole life without the pleasure of being handed over.

Because to be a child is not only to receive: it is also to give, and whoever loves a mother wishes, when their turn comes, to care for her, to repay her, to set her down to rest, and Estima wished that with all her soul, wished to have her mother seated while she served, wished to do for her what the mother had done for her her whole life, and the mother would not let her sit, would not let her serve, always rose first, always did it herself, and Estima was left empty-handed, watching her old mother serve her, a grown woman, and there was in that a pain Estima could explain to no one, the pain of loving a person who will not let you love her back, who accepts your love only in the form of letting her give you hers, and never in the form of your giving her yours.

Estima's husband, who saw this from outside, said to her sometimes, to console her, your mother is like that, don't take it ill, she loves you in her own way; and Estima knew it was true, that the mother loved her, and much, but said to her husband a thing that was the core of her hurt: she loves me, yes, but she only lets me be loved by her. She never lets me love her back. And love is in both directions, or it goes crooked. I am a daughter to one side only. And the husband did not know what to answer, because Estima was right, and because there are wounds in families that are not consoled from outside, that are what they are, and that are only healed if the person who has them, one day, wishes to see them, and Estima's mother had never, in forty-eight years, wished to see hers.

The present had been the last great attempt, and the most hurt. Three years before, on her mother's birthday, Estima had decided to give her a thing the mother could not return with the keep it for yourself, a personal thing, chosen with care, a thing that would say mother, this is for you, only for you, let yourself receive it. She had saved to buy it, chosen it over weeks, thinking of her mother, of what the mother never had for herself, of what the mother needed and never gave herself, had wrapped it with her own hands, with a bow that took her time, and had given it to her mother in front of the family, on the birthday, with her heart full, awaiting seeing her open it, awaiting, at last, seeing her receive. And the mother had received the parcel, because one does not refuse a present in front of others, but had said I'll open it at my leisure, and had set it down, and the leisure had not come, and the parcel had stayed on top of the furniture, three years, unopened, and Estima had perceived, with a hurt that did not pass, that the mother could not manage even that, that not even a birthday present, wrapped with love, could the mother receive, and that the mother's wall was so high that not even a daughter's love, set in a parcel, crossed it.

At first Estima waited. Each time she went to her mother's house she looked at the parcel hoping to see it opened, and saw it always closed, and went away with the stab. Then she asked, once, twice, mother, won't you open the present?, and the mother said I will, I will, leave it, and Estima perceived that to ask was worse, that the mother grew anxious, that the parcel became between the two of them a heavy thing, and she stopped asking. And the parcel stayed there, silent, gathering dust, and became, without anyone saying it, the symbol of all that was lacking between that mother and that daughter: the love Estima could not hand over, Estima's hand held out to her mother and always drawing back with nothing, the proof, set on top of a piece of furniture, that in that house love went one way only, from mother to daughter, and never, never, from daughter to mother.

And there was more, and it was what hurt Estima most, what she said only to her husband, at night, low: that she was afraid the mother would die with the present unopened. That one day the mother would go, as the father had gone, and that she, Estima, would be left with that parcel in her hands, opened at last after the mother's death, seeing what the mother had never wished to see in life, receiving alone the present she had given and that the mother had not let in, and that this, that parcel opened after the funeral, would be the last image of the bond of the two of them, the image of a love the daughter wished to give and that the mother took to the grave unopened. Estima had nightmares about it. She dreamed she opened the parcel at the edge of her mother's grave, alone, and woke weeping, and the husband asked her what is it, and she said the present, I dreamed of the present again, and the husband embraced her and said nothing, because there was nothing to say, but to wait for the mother, one day, before dying, to change, and that hope was each year smaller.

In those days, without Estima knowing why, the mother began to be different. She went to her daughter's house, one afternoon, a thing she did not do much, for it was always Estima who went to her mother's, and sat down, and Estima, surprised, set about serving her, coffee, a cake, awaiting the mother to rise and do it herself, as always; but the mother, that time, stayed seated. She let herself be served. It was a small instant, the mother seated letting the daughter set the coffee before her, and Estima scarcely breathed, afraid of spoiling it, afraid the mother would rise suddenly and again take from her hands the pleasure of serving; but the mother stayed, and drank the coffee her daughter had made, and said it's good, child, thank you, and it was the first time in years that Estima heard her mother receive from her a thing without the there was no need, and it was so little, a coffee, and so everything, that Estima had to go to the kitchen so that the mother would not see her eyes.

What is it, mother, Estima asked, when she came back, and she asked it with care, because she felt something had shifted in the mother and was afraid to startle her. And Dádiva, seated, with the daughter's coffee before her, said a thing Estima did not expect, a thing it cost her to say, because it was one of the first times in her life she said aloud a weakness of hers: Brandura said a thing to me, child. She said that whoever only gives also wearies the one who loves them. And that to receive is a present one gives to the one who loves us. And I stayed thinking. All my life I thought that to give was the best one could do for others. And perhaps it is, but perhaps it is not enough. Perhaps there are people who need to give to me, and I never let them, and that, child, perhaps was selfishness of mine, was my wanting to stay always on the good side and not let you have yours.

Estima heard this from her mother and had to hold herself, because it was the thing she had hoped to hear for twenty years, the thing she had never thought she would hear, and said only, with her voice trembling, it's that, mother. That's exactly it. I only want, once, to give you a thing and have you let me. And the mother and the daughter stayed there, in the daughter's kitchen, around a coffee the daughter had made and the mother had drunk, and said no more, because there was no need, but the two of them perceived that something had begun, that the wall had a crack, and Estima, taking her leave, thought of the parcel on top of her mother's furniture, and had, for the first time in three years, a small hope: that perhaps, perhaps, the mother would one day open it, and let her, at last, be the daughter who gives.
