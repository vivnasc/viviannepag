## 8. The wall resists

Her health recovered, her strength recovered, Dádiva did what people do when the body stops forcing them to change: she went back to her old habits, and the wall, which the fever had opened, set about closing again, because walls, when they sense they are going to fall, rise with more force.

Scarcely could she get up before Dádiva went back to the service of others with a haste that was suspect, the haste of one fleeing again. She went back to caring, to giving, to offering herself, to being on the side of giving, and the village, relieved to have its angel back, received her with praise, Dádiva is well again, thank God, here she is already tending to everyone; and Dádiva, in the praise, felt the old comfort, the comfort of the high place, and settled back into it, and each service she did was a brick put back in the wall, and what she had received in the fever, the care, the love, the gladness of letting others give, began to seem to her, now upright, with her guard raised again, a weakness of when she was ill, a thing the fever had made her do and that health should correct.

Because this is what walls do, and Dádiva was living proof of it: they use health to rebuild themselves. In weakness, the person receives, because they have no strength to refuse; but the moment they recover their strength, the first thing the fear does with it is raise the wall again, close the door again, and call that going back to normal. And Dádiva, in going back to normal, in going back to giving and refusing, felt she was doing what she should, that the weakness had passed and she was herself again, without perceiving that the self she went back to was precisely the illness, the illness of the wall, and that the days of the fever, in which she had received, had been, those, the days of health, the health of the heart, which she now traded, without noticing, for the health of the body and the illness of the soul.

And when the neighbours, who had liked caring for her and had learned the way, still brought her, from new habit, a thing, a dish, a thank-you, Dádiva, upright, recovered, went back to the there was no need, went back to drawing back, went back to returning, and did it now with a new force, almost with sharpness, because she had to push harder what the fever had opened in her, had to close quickly the door that had stood open, and the neighbours, who had come towards her with pleasure, felt the door shut in their faces, and drew back, hurt, and said among themselves Dádiva is well now, she no longer lets, and went back to leaving her alone at the top, which was what she, without knowing it, went back to asking.

Estima was the one who most suffered the relapse. During the illness, Estima had had at last the mother who let herself be cared for, and it had been the happiest thing of her life; and now, with the mother well, she saw her shut the door again, saw her go back to the I need nothing, child, saw her refuse again everything she tried to give, and Estima, who had tasted at last the pleasure of giving to her mother, felt it taken from her again, and it was worse than ever, because now she knew what she lost, now she had tasted, and the mother's relapse was not, for Estima, only going back to the usual: it was losing a thing she had had, which is always worse than never having had it.

And there was, between the two of them, a night of truth that was the bottom of the book. Estima, hurt, went to her mother's house, and this time she did not keep silent, and said to her, with her eyes burning, mother, I do not understand you. You were ill and you let me care for you, and it was the most beautiful thing that happened between us in twenty years, and I thought something had changed. And now you are well, and you have closed everything again, and gone back to not letting me give you anything, and I feel outside again, mother, the daughter again who cannot be a daughter. Do you prefer to die alone on your pedestal to letting me love you? Is that it? Do you prefer to be the saint who needs no one to being my mother, who lets me care for her? Because I do not want a saint, mother. I want a mother. And a mother lets herself be loved.

And she said more, and it was what most hurt Dádiva, because it touched the parcel: and that present, mother. It has been there three years. I no longer even ask you to open it. But look well at it, mother, before I leave. That is me. It is my love for you, wrapped, on top of a piece of furniture, for three years, that you never wished to open. And if you die without opening it, mother, I will be left with that image for the rest of my life: that of the love I wished to give you and that you left unopened until you died. Do not do that to me, mother. Please, do not do that to me.

And Estima left, weeping, and Dádiva was left alone in the front room, with her daughter's fever still in the air, and looked at the parcel, and it was the hardest night of the book, because that night Dádiva had before her, in all their force, the two Dádivas: the one of the wall, who said close, protect yourself, to give is safe, to receive is your mother's floor, do not come down; and the one of the fever, who had received and not fallen, who had heard Brandura and the Keeper and the fountain, who had seen her daughter's happiness caring for her, and who said open, come down from the wall, let in, do not die alone at the top. And the two Dádivas fought, that night, before the parcel, and the one of the wall was strong, was of a whole lifetime, was the older; but the one of the fever had now, on her side, her daughter's face leaving in tears, the promise to the dead Brandura, the truth of the Keeper, and had, above all, a thing the wall had no answer for: the question of what the wall was for, if the price of it was to die alone with the daughter's love unopened on top of a piece of furniture.

What is it for, to stand upright, Dádiva asked the wall, that night, if I stand upright and alone, and my daughter leaves my house weeping because I do not let her love me? What is it for, to owe nothing to anyone, if the price is to receive nothing from anyone, not even a daughter's love? You, wall, saved me from my mother's shame, and I thank you. But you are costing me my daughter. You are costing me Brandura, who died asking me to open that. You are costing me myself, who am dry within from so much giving and never receiving. And I no longer want to pay you that price. And the wall, like all walls set before the question of what they are for, was left without an answer, because walls know how to defend but do not know how to say what the defence was worth, and Dádiva's defence, she saw it now, had cost her a whole life of company, in exchange for a safety that after all had never saved her from anything, had only left her alone above.

And she perceived, that night, a thing that decided her: that the wall no longer protected her from the mother, because the mother had been dead for years; it protected her only from herself, from her own possibility of being loved, and that to go on defending it was to go on defending herself from love, was to choose, from fear of a girl's shame, the solitude of a woman's life, and that this, said like that, before the parcel, on the night the daughter left weeping, was of so great a sadness, and of so evident a uselessness, that Dádiva, for the first time, could not defend the wall nor herself, and stayed there, in the dark, surrendered, not yet able to open the parcel, but already unable to go on justifying having kept it closed.

She did not open the parcel that night, not yet, but she drew near to it, and took it, and for the first time did not draw back her hand; she stayed with the parcel in her hands, in the dark of the front room, feeling the small weight of her daughter's love of three years before, and said to it, to the parcel, or to the daughter, or to herself, a thing that was already almost the surrender of the wall: tomorrow. Tomorrow I open you. I cannot die like this. I cannot do this to you, child. And she went to bed with the parcel still on top of the furniture, but with the decision made beneath the fear, the decision to come down at last from the wall, to open at last the door of entry, to receive, at the end of a life of giving, the one thing left to receive, which was the love her daughter had been putting in her hands for three years and which she, from fear of coming down to her mother's floor, had never had the courage to let in.
