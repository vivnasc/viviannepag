## 11. The eve

On the Saturday night, the eve of the Sunday on which she was to open the present with her daughter, Dádiva stayed alone at home with the parcel, and it was a long night, because the wall, which she was going to bring down the next day, made, as the walls that are going to fall do, its last guard.

She sat in the front room, before the furniture, with the parcel in front of her, and stayed looking at it, and the old voice rose, the mother's voice, the voice of the poor house, the voice that said do not receive, to receive is to come down, remember the shadow, remember the door, remember the cold, remember the maid of the great house and the poor of the lower street. And Dádiva heard it, one last time, and this time did not push it far off; she heard it to the end, with pity, with the pity she had learned to have for her mother and for herself, and answered it, in the dark of the front room, slowly, like one who speaks with a dead woman.

I know, mother, she said. I know that you received with shame, and that you wished to spare me that shame, and that you gave me the name of Dádiva so that I should be always on the side of giving, far from your shadow. And I thank you, mother. You did what you knew, with what you had. But you were wrong in one thing, and I was wrong with you for a whole life: the shame was not in receiving. It was in your thinking that receiving put you below. And I, so as not to have your shadow, stopped receiving at all, and was left as alone above as you were left bent below. And tomorrow, mother, I am going to do the thing you did not know how to do and that I too did not know a whole life: I am going to receive with my head high. I am going to open my daughter's present, and let her love come in, and I will not feel below, I will feel loved. And this, mother, is not betraying you. It is healing you, in me. It is ending in me the shame you carried, so that my daughter does not inherit it, neither your shadow, nor my fear.

And it was, that, the leave-taking of the mother, the true one, the one Dádiva had never made, and she made it in peace, before the parcel, on the eve of opening it, and in making it she felt the wall, which had defended her a life, come down at last, not crash all at once, but lower slowly, with respect, as one lowers an old flag that has served its time, and over the lowered wall Dádiva saw, for the first time, the whole level place, the village beside her, the daughter at the door, the world of equal to equal where she was going, the next day, to come down for good.

And she perceived, there, a thing that moved her: that the mother, in giving her the name of Dádiva, had wished to give her a present, the greatest she knew how to give, that of sparing her the cold she herself knew; and that she, Dádiva, had spent a life using that present in reverse, making of the name a wall instead of a blessing, and that to open the daughter's parcel the next day was, also, to open at last the mother's present, the one of the name, to receive it as it had been given, with love, and not as she had carried it, with fear; and that, in receiving, at last, with her head high, she was going to honour the mother better than she had honoured her with a whole life of not receiving, because the mother had not wished her only on the side of giving; the mother had wished her without the shadow, and the shadow, Dádiva saw it now, is not taken away by not receiving, it is taken away by receiving without shame, which was what she was going, at last, to do.

She took the parcel, that night, and held it in her lap a long time, without opening it, for she was to open it with the daughter; but she held it in her lap, felt its small weight, and thought of Estima choosing it, three years before, saving to buy it, wrapping it with that bow that took her time, giving it to the mother with her heart full, awaiting seeing her open it, and seeing it set down, and closed, year after year, on top of a piece of furniture, and Dádiva wept, with the parcel in her lap, from remorse and from love, from remorse for the three years, for the whole lifetime of the daughter's love left unreceived, and from love for that daughter who, despite everything, despite a mother who never let her give anything, had gone on wishing to give, had gone on waiting, three years, for the mother to open her present, without giving up, with a faithfulness that was the proof of a love greater than Dádiva's whole wall.

And she perceived, with the parcel in her lap, on the eve, a thing that filled her and eased her: that it was not too late. That Estima was still there, waiting, on the outside of the door, with the love ungiven but alive, not dead; that she, Dádiva, still had time to open the door with the daughter living and in front of her, and not as Estima had feared, after the funeral, alone, with the parcel opened at the edge of the mother's grave. She was going to open it in life. She was going to give the daughter what the daughter most wished, which was to see the mother receive. She was going to do, the next day, with health, by choice, in front of the one who loved her, the thing she had lacked to do her whole life, and which was, after all, the simplest and the hardest of all: to let herself be loved.

She put the parcel back on the furniture, carefully, to open it the next day with the daughter, and went to bed, and slept, on the eve, a sleep she did not know, the sleep of one who is going, at last, to open the door of entry, after a life with it bolted; and she dreamed of her mother, but not of the mother bent at the door, of the mother with her head high, receiving a thing from someone and smiling, the mother the mother never came to be and that Dádiva, in the dream, gave her, because in healing in herself the mother's shame, Dádiva healed also, in a way, the mother, gave her, late, the bearing the mother never had, and the mother, in the dream, received with her head high, and was beautiful so, and Dádiva woke in the morning with that image of her mother and with the day before her, the day of opening the present, the day of receiving, at last, after a life of giving, the love that awaited her, wrapped, for three years, and within, for a whole life.
