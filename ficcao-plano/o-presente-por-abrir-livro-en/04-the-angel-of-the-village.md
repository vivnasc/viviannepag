## 4. The angel of the village

In Véspera, Dádiva was called the angel, and that was the pretty name that bolted her door from the outside: because to an angel no one gives anything, of an angel one only asks, and Dádiva, made an angel by the village, was condemned to a whole life of a hand held out outward and never inward.

It was said of her, in the square, at the church doors, in the houses she entered to care for: Dádiva is an angel, is a saint, there is no one like her, what would become of this village without Dádiva. And it was said in truth, with sincere gratitude, because Dádiva really did what they said she did, really came to the aid of half the village, and the village, which received so much from her, repaid her in the only way it knew, with praise, with the title of angel, without perceiving that that title, instead of filling her, emptied her, because it set Dádiva in a place where she was no longer a person, she was a function, she was the one who gives, and to a function one does not offer a lap, does not take soup, does not care for it when it falls ill, because a function does not fall ill, a function does not need, a function is always available, and it was in that place of function, of angel, of saint, that the village, with the best of intentions, with too much gratitude and too little attention, was leaving Dádiva alone.

Because there is a hidden cruelty in the gratitude that makes a person an angel, and it is this: that in thanking them for giving, it confirms to them that their place is to give, and takes from them, without meaning to, the right to receive. Had the village said to Dádiva you do too much, rest, let us care for you, perhaps Dádiva would have learned to receive; but the village said to her you are an angel, and against the you-are-an-angel there is no learning possible, because the angel does not receive, the angel serves, and Dádiva clung to the title as one clings to a crown, and made of it one more reason never to drop her guard, never to let anyone care for her, because to let anyone care for her would be to belie the angel, to come down from the altar, to confess to the village that the saint too felt the cold, also needed a lap, was also, beneath the function, a woman alone who gave too much perhaps because she had never learned to let herself be loved.

And there was another thing, deeper, that the title of angel hid, and that Dádiva was beginning now, slowly, to glimpse: that to give, for her, was not only kindness. It was also power. Whoever gives stands above whoever receives, stands on the side of standing, has the advantage of being the needed and not the needy, and Dádiva, who had grown up seeing her mother bent on the side of the one who receives, had clung to the side of the one who gives not only from kindness, but from a deep need never again to be bent, to be always above, to be always the one who can and never the one who needs. And the title of angel gave her that, gave her the high place, and for that it was so hard to let go, because to let it go was to risk being again the girl of the lower house, of the hand held up, and Dádiva preferred the solitude of the altar to the shame of the floor, without perceiving that between the altar and the floor there was a third place, the level place, that of the one who gives and receives by turns, of equal to equal, which was the only one where one stands, at the same time, upright and accompanied, and which was precisely the place she had never learned to occupy.

It cost her to admit it, but it was so: there was, in Dádiva's giving, beneath the true kindness, a thread of pride, the pride of being the one who can, the one who has to give, the one always on the upper side of the trade. When she entered a house to care, she felt, without wishing to feel it, a satisfaction that was not only that of doing good; it was also that of being needed, of being the one who is wanted, of being, in that house, the important one. And when she left, with the others' thank-you at her back, she carried with her not only the good tiredness of one who has given, but also, hidden, the confirmation that she was on the right side, the side of the one who gives, far from the lower house. And it was that thread of pride, more than the kindness, that kept her from receiving, because to receive was to let go of the thread, was to come down from the position of one who can to that of one who needs, and Dádiva clung to the thread with more force than she clung to her own kindness, not knowing that the thread of pride and the rope of solitude were, after all, the same thread.

There was that week an occasion that showed it to her. A woman of the village fell ill, and they did what is done, arranged among the neighbours who would care, who would take what, and Dádiva, of course, was the first to offer, to take the heaviest shift, to say I'll take the nights, it costs me nothing. And one of the neighbours, a young woman, newly come to the village, who had not yet quite learned Dádiva's place, said a simple thing, without malice, that stayed hurting Dádiva: oh Dádiva, but you always do everything, you never let others do. I also wanted to help, and you always take my place. And Dádiva, who expected the praise of always, took that instead, and at first she nearly took offence, nearly answered I take no one's place, I only help; but the young woman's sentence was sister to Brandura's, and Dádiva, with the chink already open, heard it: you never let others do. You always take my place. And she perceived, with a new discomfort, that her generosity had a side she had never seen, the side of not letting others give, of taking up all the giving and leaving none over for anyone else, of being so much the one who gives that she took from others the pleasure and the right of also giving, and that this, which seemed kindness, was, seen from another angle, a form of keeping everything, of not sharing even the place of the one who helps.

Because there are those who give too much as there are those who receive too much, and the two unbalance the trade, only the one who receives too much is ill thought of and the one who gives too much is praised, and so the one who gives too much is never corrected, and goes on giving too much a whole life, thinking themselves ever more virtuous and growing ever more alone, because a trade where one only gives and the other only receives is not a trade, it is a dependence, and it wearies both sides, the one who always receives, who feels in debt and diminished, and the one who always gives, who feels, at bottom, though they do not say it, used, and who goes on gathering, beneath the generosity, a mute resentment, the resentment of one who gives so much and to whom no one gives anything, and who cannot complain, because the fault is theirs, who never let them.

And Dádiva, that week, caught herself touching that mute resentment, which she had never admitted to having, and which was the most hidden part of all. Because beneath the angel, beneath the saint, beneath the one who always gave with a smile, there was a weary woman who, very deep down, in a place she never visited, asked herself, sometimes, at night, alone: and me, who gives to me? I give to everyone, and me, who cares for me? And at once she pushed the question far off, ashamed to have made it, because an angel does not ask that, an angel gives and is silent, but the question was there, was part of her, and was the proof that giving without receiving did not fill her as she pretended, that it left her, at bottom, hungry, the hunger to be cared for, the hunger to receive, the hunger she disguised as generosity and that was, after all, the oldest hunger, that of the girl of the lower house whom no one asked whether she wanted anything, because she was of the poor of the lower street, and to the poor one gives what is left over, one does not ask what they want.

Dádiva went home with the young woman's sentence, and joined it to Brandura's, and to the two joined Estima's face, the daughter's lack, and the unopened parcel, and began to see, that week, a portrait of herself she had never seen: not that of the angel, but that of a woman who had made of generosity a wall, who gave so as not to receive, who served so as not to be served, who occupied the high place of the one who gives so as never to have to come down to the place, which she thought low, of the one who receives, and who had been left, by dint of being always above, alone above, with no one at her side, because at one's side one stands only of equal to equal, and Dádiva had never let anyone set themselves at her side, only below, receiving, or far off, praising.

At night, at home, she looked again at the parcel, and the young woman's sentence joined Brandura's question, and the parcel answered both: you take the place from others, the young woman had said; do you let yourself be received by anyone?, Brandura had asked; and the parcel, on top of the furniture, with the bow tied three years before, was the answer to both, was the proof that Dádiva took from the daughter the place of the one who gives, and that she let herself be received by no one, neither by a daughter, nor in a birthday parcel, and Dádiva, that night, drew near to the furniture, and put her hand on the parcel, for the first time in three years she put her hand on it with the intention of opening it, and felt her heart race, and her hand tremble, and the old tightening rise, the tightening of receiving, the cold of the held-out hand, the mother's shadow at the door, and she did not open it, not yet, she drew back her hand, but she stayed looking at it very close, and said to it under her breath, to the parcel, or to herself, a thing that was the beginning of everything: one of these days I'll open you. One of these days I'll let myself. And she went to bed with her heart still beating from the almost, from the almost having received, and perceived, lying down, that to open that parcel was going to be, for her, harder than everything she had done for others her whole life, because to give had always been easy for her, and to receive, that, that small thing, to unwrap a daughter's present, was the hardest thing in the world, because it was to come down from the wall, was to risk the mother's shame, was to let in, at last, the love she had spent a life keeping on the outside of the paper.
