## 12. The unopened present

The Sunday came, and Estima came, and she came with a girl's nervousness, because she knew what that Sunday was, and the mother awaited her with the parcel already taken off the furniture and set on the table, in plain sight, in the middle, as one sets a thing one is at last going to look at head on.

The two of them sat at the table, with the parcel between them, and stayed a moment silent, because there were three years in that moment, and there was, before the three years, a life; there was the kerchief bought with saved coins that the mother had returned to a girl, there were all the refused presents, there was Estima's hand always held out and always drawing back with nothing, and all of that was there, on the table, inside that parcel of faded paper, awaiting to undo itself, at last, in the undoing of the bow. And it was Estima who said, low, with her eyes already burning, open it, mother.

And Dádiva took the parcel, and her hands trembled, trembled as they had never trembled in giving anything at all, because to give had never cost her and that, to receive, to open, to let in, was the hardest thing of her life, and Estima saw her mother's hands tremble and perceived, in that trembling, all that the gesture cost, and felt the urge to help her, to open it herself, but she did not, because it was the mother who had to open it, it was the mother who had to receive, and Estima waited, with her heart in her mouth, for the mother to do, at last, alone, the gesture that cost her more than a whole life of gestures of giving.

Dádiva undid the bow, the bow Estima had tied three years before and that had been tied there all that time, and to undo it was like opening a sealed thing, like opening a door kept shut so long the lock had gone to rust, and the paper, faded by time, gave way, and Dádiva opened the parcel, slowly, with care, like one who unwraps a fragile thing, and inside was a shawl.

It was a shawl of fine wool, soft, of a warm colour, folded with care, and Dádiva, on seeing it, perceived at once all it meant, and Estima, seeing the mother perceive, said it, with her voice trembling: it is to wrap you, mother. You wrap everyone. You care for everyone, you cover everyone, and no one ever covers you. I chose you a shawl so that, at least once, it would be you who is covered. So that you would have a thing of mine around your shoulders, warming you, when I am not there. It is only a shawl, mother. But it is my lap, it is my arms, it is the care you never let me give you. And it has been three years waiting to warm you.

And Dádiva stayed with the shawl in her hands, and perceived that the daughter, three years before, had chosen her the exact present, the present that said everything: a shawl for the woman who covered everyone and never let herself be covered, an embrace of wool for the one who gave a lap to half the village and never let herself be taken onto a lap, and that that present had been three years unopened not by chance, but because it was precisely that, to let herself be covered, to let herself be warmed, to let herself be cared for, the one thing Dádiva did not know how to do, and that to open it was to receive it, and to receive it was to let herself, at last, be wrapped by the daughter, to come down from the wall, to enter the lap, to be the covered one and not the one who covers. And she perceived, with a tightening, that the daughter knew her better than she knew herself, that at twenty-odd years Estima had already read her whole wound and chosen her the exact remedy, and that that remedy had been three years a hand's breadth from her, on top of a piece of furniture, awaiting her having the courage to take it.

And Estima rose, and took the shawl from her mother's hands, and did the thing the shawl was made for: she put it around her shoulders. She covered her mother. And Dádiva, feeling the daughter's shawl enfold her, feeling herself covered, wrapped, cared for, by the daughter, she who had covered everyone and never let herself be covered, felt a thing rise that was not the mother's shadow, was not the cold of the poor house, was not the shame of the held-out hand; it was the opposite of all that, it was the warmth, it was the lap, it was a daughter's love enfolding her, at last, after a life, and Dádiva wept, with the shawl on her shoulders, wept as she had never wept, not from sadness, but from a thing that had no name and that is what one feels when one receives, at last, the love one kept a life on the outside of the door, and that comes in, all at once, all of it, and fills, and warms, and one perceives how much cold one had a whole life without knowing, from so much not letting oneself be covered.

Thank you, child, said Dádiva, and it was the first time in her life she said thank you in truth, without the there was no need beneath it, without drawing back, without returning; a whole thank-you, received, with her head high, with the daughter's shawl on her shoulders, and Estima, hearing that thank-you, perceived that the mother, at last, had received, not only the shawl, but the love, that she had opened, at last, the door of entry, and embraced her mother, with the shawl between the two of them, and they stayed so, mother and daughter, embraced, around a parcel opened at the end of three years, and Estima had, in that embrace, what she had awaited a life: the pleasure of having given the mother a thing the mother had received, the pleasure of being, at last, the daughter who gives and is received, the closing of the circle the mother's wall had denied her since forever.

And it was not only the shawl, and the two of them knew it. It was the door. It was Dádiva opening, with that parcel, the door of entry of her life, the one bolted since a girl looking at her mother bent at the door of a poor house. It was the shame of three generations ending there, at that table, because Dádiva, in receiving her daughter's shawl with her head high, did what the mother never knew, to receive without diminishing herself, and in doing it she cut the chain, did not pass it to Estima, left the daughter not a mother who does not let herself be loved, but a mother who learned, late but in time, to let herself be loved, and that is the good inheritance, the one Estima was going to carry, and the one she was going, perhaps, one day, to pass to her own children: that of a house where one gives and receives, where love goes in both directions, where the door of entry is open.

They stayed the whole afternoon together, mother and daughter, and Dádiva did not take off the shawl, she went about the house with it, covered, and it was strange and it was good to go about covered, she who always covered others, and each time she felt the shawl on her shoulders she felt the daughter, the daughter's care, the love she had received at last, and she perceived, slowly, through that afternoon, that Brandura had been right to the end: that to receive is a present one gives to the one who loves us, and that she, in receiving that shawl, had made the daughter the greatest present of the two of theirs' lives, that of letting her, at last, love her mother. And they spoke, that afternoon, as they had never spoken, because the open door let through not only the shawl but the words, and Estima told her mother things she had never told her, and the mother heard them, and told also hers, the poor house, the shadow, the maid of the great house, and the two of them, that afternoon, knew each other more than in forty years, because between one who only gives and one who only receives there is no true knowing, only service, and the knowing, the of-equal-to-equal, only began when the door opened.

And at the end of the afternoon, when Estima went away, Dádiva was left alone at home, with the shawl on her shoulders, and looked at the furniture where the parcel had stood three years, and the furniture was empty now, without the unopened parcel, and Dádiva, instead of the strangeness of seeing it empty, felt a good thing, because that emptiness on top of the furniture was the proof that she had opened, at last, the door, that the love that had been shut there three years was now within her, on her shoulders, in her breast, received, and that she, Dádiva, the one who gave to half the village and received from no one, was now, at last, at the end of a life, a woman who also received, who let herself be covered, who let herself be loved, and who was, therefore, for the first time since girlhood, not alone at the top of a wall, but in the level place, beside her own, covered by a shawl that was a daughter's arms, and warm, at last, with the warmth she had spent a life giving to others and denying to herself, and which now, with the door open, she let, at last, come in.
