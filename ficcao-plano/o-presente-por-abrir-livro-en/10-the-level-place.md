## 10. The level place

As Dádiva learned to receive, the village noticed that the angel had changed, and the change did not make her less loved; it made her, for the first time, accompanied, because a person who only gives stands above everyone, and a person who gives and receives stands beside them.

At first the village was surprised. It was surprised that Dádiva, in helping, now let others help too, that she no longer took the whole service for herself, that she said, in caring for a sick person, you take the afternoon, I'll take the night, sharing where before she had hoarded; surprised that she accepted the dishes, the thank-yous, the compliments, without returning them, saying thank you where she had always said there was no need; surprised, above all, that Dádiva began, now and then, to ask, to say can you give me a hand, to let herself be helped, she who had never asked for anything. And the village, which had the angel on an altar, saw the angel come down from the altar, and at first almost pitied her, as if the saint had become less of a saint; but soon it perceived that the Dádiva come down from the altar was better than the Dádiva on the altar, because the one on the altar gave from above and no one came near her, and the one come down gave at one's side and let herself be approached, and was more a person, and nearer, and for that more loved.

Because there is a thing the village learned with Dádiva's change, without putting it in words: that whoever only gives, at bottom, puts us in debt, and the debt keeps us off; and whoever also receives, frees us of the debt, and the trade draws us near. While Dádiva had only given, the village had loved her with gratitude, which is a love from afar, a love of one who owes; when Dádiva began to receive, the village began to love her with friendship, which is a love from near, a love of equals, and that, the passing from gratitude to friendship, was what coming down from the altar gave Dádiva, and it was more than she had had in forty-eight years of giving without receiving: it gave her people at her side, instead of people below thanking and people above praising.

And a beautiful thing happened, which Dádiva had not foreseen: the neighbours began to care for her in turn. Not because she needed it, but because the bond had become a two-way one, and in a two-way bond the care goes in both directions; and Dádiva, who had cared all her life without being cared for, came suddenly to have women who brought her things, who asked how she was, who noticed her, and perceived, with astonishment, that she was being cared for, she, for the first time since her husband, and that this, instead of diminishing her, filled her, warmed her, made her house less silent, and that after all, in opening the door of entry, there had come in not only the daughter's love, there had come in that of the whole village, which had been all that time outside knocking, wishing to come in, and which she had kept out calling it gratitude and fear.

And there was an occasion that sealed the change in the village's eyes, and it was small and it was everything. Dádiva fell ill of a cold, a thing of nothing, and instead of hiding it, instead of the it's nothing, don't trouble yourselves of always, Dádiva did the thing she had never done: she sent word to the neighbour next door, I'm a little poorly, would you mind bringing me a loaf? And the neighbour brought the loaf, and Dádiva received it, and thanked her, and the neighbour went away feeling useful to the angel, and Dádiva was left with the loaf and the strange gladness of having asked a neighbour for a loaf, she, who gave bread to half the village, and perceived that that asked-for loaf was worth, to her, more than all those she had given, because that one had been received, had been letting in, had been coming down to the level place of one who asks a neighbour for a loaf as everyone asks, without diminishing herself, of equal to equal, in the place where, at last, she was not alone.

And she thought, with that loaf in her hands, of her mother. She thought that the mother had received the bread of others her whole life with bowed head, with the shadow, and that she, Dádiva, had just received a loaf with her head high, without shadow, with pleasure, and perceived that she had done, at last, the thing the mother had lacked, which was to receive without diminishing herself, and that in doing it she healed, in a way, the mother, gave her, late, the bearing the mother never had, and undid, in that loaf received without shame, the knot of three generations, the belief that to receive is to come down, which the mother had carried bent and she had carried upright and alone, and which ended there, in a loaf asked of a neighbour on a morning of a cold, almost without her noticing, in the way the great things end, in a small gesture.

Estima saw all this, and filled with a hope she no longer had. She saw the mother change, saw her receive, saw her come down from the altar, and had before her, she knew it, the final proof, the parcel, and did not ask for it, did not hurry it, because she had learned, she too, that such things are not pushed, but she awaited it, with her heart, and went to her mother's house and looked at the parcel on top of the furniture, and this time did not carry the stab of always, carried a hope, because the parcel was still closed but the mother was no longer the same, and Estima felt it was only a matter of time, that the door that had opened to the neighbours, to the loaf, to the small receivings, would open, one of these days, to the great one, to hers.

And it was Dádiva who chose the day. One afternoon, at the daughter's house, after letting her serve her the coffee, after drinking it and thanking her with her eyes in her eyes as Brandura had taught her, Dádiva said to the daughter a thing Estima had awaited for three years and no longer thought she would hear: child, will you come to my house on Sunday? I want to open the present with you. That one. The one you gave me three years ago. It is time now, is it not? I have learned enough to open it. And I want to open it with you, in front of you, for you to see me receive. Which is what I have owed you for three years: not the opening of it, that is the least of it; it is letting you see me receive your love. Will you come?

And Estima, who did not expect it, had to set down her cup so as not to drop it, and said only, with her whole voice trembling, I'll come, mother. Of course I'll come. And the two of them stayed there, in the daughter's kitchen, around the coffee, saying no more, because there was no need, and Estima perceived that the Sunday drawing near was going to be, for the two of them, more than opening a parcel: it was going to be the day the mother, at last, after a lifetime of giving, let herself be received by the daughter, the day the door of entry of Dádiva's house, bolted since a girl at the door of a poor house, opened, at last, to let in the love that had been waiting three years on top of a piece of furniture, and had been, waiting to come in, in truth, a whole life.
