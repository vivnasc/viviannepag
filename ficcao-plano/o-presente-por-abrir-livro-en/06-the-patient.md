## 6. The patient

It was a little after Brandura's funeral that Dádiva's body did what she never let her body do: it stopped. And in stopping, it forced her to the thing she most feared, the thing she had fled her whole life, the thing that was, after all, what was needed for the book to turn: it forced her to receive.

She had been tired a long time, with a tiredness she never confessed, because to confess tiredness would be to ask for rest, and to ask was of the lower side; and the weeks watching over Brandura, and then the death, and the grief she did not let herself have because she had no time, were the last drop, and one morning Dádiva, rising to go and care for someone or other, felt the world spin, and fell, there, on the bedroom floor, and lay a while unable to rise, she who raised half the village, on the floor, alone, with her body telling her enough, with the fever rising, with the house silent around her and no one to pick her up, because Dádiva was always the one who picked up and never the one picked up.

And she lay there, on the floor, longer than she should have, not only because she had no strength, but because, even fallen, the first thing that came to her head was not to ask for help; it was that no one could see her like this, weak, on the floor, that she had to rise before anyone knew, that Dádiva could not be on the floor, because Dádiva was the one who raised others from the floor. And she tried to rise alone, and could not, and dragged herself to the bed, and got into it, and set about hiding, because even bedridden, even with fever, Dádiva's first instinct was to hide her need, was not to give trouble, was to go on, even ill, being the one who does not weigh.

She managed to reach the bed, and there she lay, with fever, two, three days, and at first she did what she always did, she hid, told no one, sent word to those who waited for her that she could not come but it was nothing, that she was only a little poorly, that they should not trouble themselves; because even ill, even bedridden, Dádiva saw to it that no one should be troubled with her, saw to it that she went on, even in illness, not weighing, not needing, not receiving, and she lay there wasting away with fever, alone, faithful to the end to the wall, resolved to fall ill without giving trouble as she had lived without giving trouble, to be, even in illness, the one who is no trouble. And there was in this an obstinacy that was almost tragic: a woman with fever, alone, refusing to let the village she had cared for her whole life now give her a little of what she had given it, and preferring to waste away faithful to the wall than to live with the wall open.

But the village, which had received so much from her, did not leave her. It was Estima who raised the alarm, going to her mother's house and finding her with fever, and from there happened to Dádiva what she had done to others her whole life, and never, never, let be done to her: the village came to her aid. The dishes began to arrive at the door, the soup, the messages of recovery, the neighbours offering to stay, to watch, to care; there came the half of the village Dádiva had cared for, now turned towards her, with their hands full of what she had always given them and never let them give back, and Dádiva, in bed, with fever, saw all that arrive with a panic greater than the fever, the panic of being on the side of receiving, the panic of the floor, the mother's shadow at the door, and her first reaction, even with fever, was to refuse: there's no need, she said, from the pillows, I'm well, take that away, don't trouble yourselves, I need nothing.

I need nothing. She said it to the dishes, said it to the neighbours, said it to the daughter, and it was Estima who, that time, did not let her. Estima sat at her mother's bedside, and took the refusal from her hands with a new firmness, the firmness she had learned to have, and said to her: mother, now you hush and you let. All your life you cared for everyone, and no one let you care for yourself because you would not let. Now you are ill, and the village wants to care for you, and I want to care for you, and you will let, mother, even by force. It is not there was no need. There was need, yes. For thirty years there was need and you never let. Now let.

And Dádiva, weak with fever, with no strength for the wall, did, for the first time in her life, the thing she had never done: she let. She let the daughter put a cool cloth on her brow. She let the neighbour bring her the soup. She let herself be cared for, and it was, at first, an agony, because each care she received was a stab in the wall, was coming down to the floor, was the mother's shame; but the fever took from her the strength to refuse, and as the days passed and she, with no strength, went on receiving, went on receiving, there happened to her a strange thing, a thing Brandura had promised her and she had not believed: receiving, which she thought humiliating, did not humiliate her. The neighbours who brought her soup did not look down on her, as she feared; they looked at her with love, with the gladness of being able, at last, to repay, with the pleasure of closing the circle, and Dádiva saw in their faces, in receiving, not the contempt she expected, but the same gladness she herself felt when she gave, and perceived, slowly, with the fever going down and the truth coming up, that in letting herself be cared for she was losing nothing, she was giving others the pleasure of caring for her, she was making them the present Brandura spoke of, the present of letting them give.

And there was a moment, in the middle of those days, that stayed in Dádiva: an old neighbour, whose dying husband Dádiva had cared for years before, came to sit at her bedside, and stayed there, silent, keeping her company, and at a certain point said, with brimming eyes, Dádiva, you cared for my man like no one, and I could never repay you, and I was left a whole life in your debt. Let me now, at least, keep you company. Let me repay you a little. And Dádiva, in bed, heard that let-me-repay-you, and perceived, suddenly, the weight she had put on others without knowing: that in never letting anyone repay her, she had left half the village feeling in debt to her, carrying a debt of gratitude she had never let them settle, and that that old neighbour, in asking to keep her company, was not asking a favour, she was asking to be freed of a debt, and that Dádiva, in letting her stay, was doing her a good, the good of letting her pay, of letting her be equal, of taking her out of the place of debtor where Dádiva's own generosity had put her.

And Estima. Estima, those days, was the most transformed of all, and Dádiva, from her bed, saw it, and it hurt her and filled her at once. Estima cared for her mother with a devotion that was of dammed-up years, with the fierce happiness of one who has waited a lifetime for the turn to repay and has it at last, and Dádiva saw in her daughter's face, caring for her, a gladness she had never seen, the gladness of being able to give to her mother, of being, finally, the one who gives, and perceived, with a tightening of remorse, how much she had taken from her daughter in never letting her give her anything, how much of that gladness the daughter had awaited a whole life and she had denied her, and that it had been enough for her, Dádiva, to fall ill, to be weak, to drop her guard by force, for the daughter to have at last what she had always wanted, which was to care for her mother, to give to her mother, to be the daughter entire and not only the one who receives.

Mother, Estima said to her, one night, setting the broth before her, with a new shyness, mother, do you know that this, caring for you, is the thing I most wanted in my life? And you never let me. I had to wait for you to fall ill to be able to do for you what you do for me since I was born. And I am not reproaching you, mother, I am only telling you: see how good it does me. See how happy I am, giving you the broth. You always took this from me. And Dádiva, in bed, heard her daughter, and watched her give the broth with that happiness, and wept, low, with what the fever no longer accounted for, wept to see the good she was doing her daughter just by letting herself be cared for, and perceived, at last, in the body and not in the head, the whole truth of Brandura: that to receive is a present one gives to the one who loves us, that her refusal of a lifetime, which she had thought generosity, had been a theft, the theft of the pleasure of giving from all who wished to give to her, and above all from the daughter, from Estima, who had awaited a lifetime for the turn to care for her mother and to whom the mother, with her wall, had always denied it.

And when, after some days, the fever passed and Dádiva rose, she rose another, not cured of the wall, for such things are not cured by a fever, but with a crack in it through which had come in, at last, a truth that did not go out again: that she had been, those days, on the side of receiving, the side she had feared her whole life, and that she had not fallen to her mother's floor, that she had not had the shadow, that she had, instead, received love and given, in receiving, gladness to those who loved her, and that that side, the side of receiving, was not the floor she thought, it was a good side, a side of being a person, the side she lacked to occupy to be, at last, entire, and not only upright and alone at the top of the wall she had built, in girlhood, against the shame of a mother at the door of a poor house, a whole life ago.
