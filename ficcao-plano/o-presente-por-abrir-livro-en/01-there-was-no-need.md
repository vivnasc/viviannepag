## 1. There was no need

Dádiva had a phrase she said more often than good morning, and she always said it the same way, with the same smile and the same small drawing-back of the body, like one who parries a blow with skill: there was no need.

She said it when something was offered to her, and much was offered, because Dádiva was the one who cared for half the village and the village, in its way, repaid her: a neighbour brought her eggs in thanks for some days' company kept by a sickbed, and Dádiva said there was no need, and made a point of not taking the eggs, or took them drawing back, as if they were hot; someone thanked her with a compliment, said to her you are an angel, Dádiva, I don't know what would become of us without you, and Dádiva said there was no need, give over, anyone would do it, and returned the compliment before feeling it, before letting it in, like one who returns a letter without opening it. That was Dádiva's art, the art of a lifetime: to give with both hands and receive with none, to stand always on the side of the one who offers and never, never, on the side of the one who accepts, because to accept, for her, had a taste she could not bear, the taste of being in debt, the taste of coming down from the place of the one who gives to the place of the one who needs, and from that place Dádiva fled as one flees a thing that does real harm.

And it was noticed, in Véspera, that small drawing-back of her body when something was offered to her, though no one knew how to read it: it was a half-step backward, a shrug of the shoulders, a way of putting her hands behind her back, the small gestures of one defending herself from a thing coming towards her, and the thing coming was only a dish, a kindness, a thank-you, but Dádiva defended herself from it as from a danger, because to Dádiva it was a danger, the danger of receiving, and her body, which never drew back to give, which always went forward to offer, always drew back to receive, as if to receive and to give were, in her body, two opposite movements, one open and easy, that of giving, and another shut and impossible, that of receiving.

And it did harm, it is true, though she had never told anyone why, nor even herself in words. It came from far off, the harm of receiving, it came from her mother's house, but of that the book will speak further on; for now it is enough to say that Dádiva had reached forty-eight to be, in Véspera, the very embodiment of giving: she cared for the old whose children were far away, watched over the sick, ran the errands of those who could not, took soup to whoever fell ill, helped at births and at funerals, which are the two ends where a village most needs hands, and did all of this without charging, without weighing it, without ever saying she was tired, with an availability that seemed to have no bottom and that the whole village took for a blessing, without noticing that a fountain that always gives and never receives is not a bottomless blessing: it is a fountain emptying itself within, slowly, and that one day, if no one lets it fill, runs dry.

Dádiva's days were all alike and all full, and full of the same: of others. She rose early, and before thinking of herself she already thought of who needed her that day, who was in bed, who had been left alone, who had a child far off and a hurt close by, and she left home with the list of others in her head and no place in it for herself, and went about the village from door to door, giving, doing, tending, and came home at night tired in a way she called good, the tiredness of one who has spent herself whole on others, and which was, in truth, the tiredness of one who never lets herself be filled, the dry tiredness of a fountain that gives without receiving. And the village loved her for this, and said so, and Dádiva grew in the village's love as a plant grows toward the sun, but it was a love from afar, the love one has for one who serves us, and Dádiva, who had the love of the whole village, had the love of no one close, because to have the love of one close one must let someone come near, and to come near is to give and to receive, and Dádiva only let near those who came to receive from her, never those who came to give to her.

She had been widowed young. Her husband, a good and quiet man, had died some years before, and since then Dádiva's house had grown quieter and she more giving still, as if the giving were the way of not feeling his absence, of filling with others the emptiness he had left, and the village, which noticed everything, said that Dádiva, poor thing, had thrown herself into the service of others so as not to think, and was half right; the other half, which the village did not see, was that her husband had been the one person Dádiva had let, in life, give her something, care for her a little, and that with him had gone also the one chink by which, now and then, Dádiva had received; with him dead, that chink had closed, and Dádiva had been left only giving, with no door of entry for whatever might come from outside towards her. The husband knew how to receive her, it was his way, he knew how to come near Dádiva beneath the wall, with the patience of quiet men, and Dádiva, with him, and only with him, let herself, now and then, be cared for; and when he died, there died with him the one person who knew the way beneath the wall, and the wall closed for good, with no one inside who knew how to open it.

There was the daughter left to her. Estima lived nearby, married, with her own life, and loved her mother with a love that had a thorn inside it, and the thorn was this: that Estima had never, in all her life, managed to give her mother anything. Everything she tried to give her, Dádiva returned, with the there was no need, with the keep that for yourself, you have your own house, with the I need nothing, child, and Estima had grown up and become a woman with that lack stuck in her throat, the lack of a mother who would let herself be repaid, who would accept of her, at least once, what the mother had given her her whole life. Because Estima wanted to repay her mother, not as a debt, but as love; she wanted, once, the pleasure of being the one who gives, and the mother, with her tireless giving, with her tender refusal of everything, always took that pleasure from her, always left Estima on the side of the one who receives, which is a good side when one trades, but which wounds when it is the only one they let you occupy.

And there was, in Dádiva's house, a sign of all this, a sign in plain sight of whoever came in and that no one saw because it seemed only an object: on top of a piece of furniture in the front room, against the wall, stood a parcel. It was a gift parcel, with the paper already a little faded by time, and the bow still tied, and it had never been opened. Estima had given it to her mother three years before, on a birthday, and Dádiva had received it, because one does not refuse a present in a daughter's hands in front of others, but she had not opened it there, had said I'll open it at my leisure, and the leisure had not come, and the parcel had stayed on top of the furniture, three years, with the bow tied, awaiting a leisure Dádiva never found, because leisure was not what was lacking: what was lacking was the courage to receive, to unwrap, to see what the daughter had chosen for her with love and let that love come in, and Dádiva, who opened the door to half the village to give, could not open, in three years, a parcel a daughter had given her, because to open it was to receive it, and to receive was the one thing Dádiva did not know how to do.

Estima saw the parcel each time she went to her mother's house, and each time she saw it she carried in her breast a small stab, the stab of seeing her present unopened, year after year, in the same place, with the same bow, gathering dust; and at first she had asked, mother, won't you open it?, and Dádiva had said I will, I will, leave it; and then she had stopped asking, because to ask and hear the leave it was worse than the silence, and the parcel had become, between the two of them, a thing not spoken of, a wound wrapped in gift paper on top of a piece of furniture, saying, to whoever could read it, all that was left unsaid between that mother and that daughter, which was that one knew how to give and not to receive, and the other wished to give and was not let, and that the two of them loved each other over the top of it, but with the parcel always there, closed, between them. And the dust gathered on the bow, fine, and each layer of dust was a month, was six months, was a year, and the parcel grew, from being so still, so faithful to its place, almost a piece of furniture itself, a thing of the house, and Dádiva scarcely saw it any more, in the way one stops seeing what is always in the same place, and that was the worst of it, that the daughter's love had stood still long enough to become invisible, to become part of the furniture, to stop hurting from being so habitual.

That morning, Dádiva had gone early to the house of Brandura, an elderly neighbour who had fallen ill and was bedridden, and had done what she did, opened her windows, changed her linen, given her the broth, and was already leaving when Brandura, from the bed, took her hand with her weak hand and said a thing Dádiva did not expect and that left her disconcerted: thank you, Dádiva. You let me receive you so well. And Dádiva, who always gave and to whom no one usually said that in that way, was already about to answer with the there was no need of always, but Brandura squeezed her hand and added, with the mild wisdom of those in bed who have time to think: and you, Dádiva, do you let yourself be received by anyone? Or is it only you giving, always? Because whoever only gives, my child, also wearies the one who loves them. Mind you, to receive is also a present one gives to the one who loves us.

Dádiva left Brandura's house with that sentence stuck in her, and it was the first time in her life that a thing said by a person she was going to give to stayed turning over within her about herself. To receive is also a present one gives to the one who loves us. She did not understand at once, or understood and pushed it far off, as she pushed everything that came towards her; but the sentence did not go, it stayed, and through the day, while Dádiva did her tasks, the sentence came back, and came back with Estima's face, with the lack stuck in the daughter's throat, with the unopened parcel on top of the furniture, and Dádiva began to feel, without yet knowing how to say it, that perhaps there was a side of generosity she had never seen, a side in which to give too much, and never to let oneself receive, was not the greatest of kindnesses, was a fine way of letting no one come near, of standing, alone, in the high place of the one who gives, with the hand always held out outward and never turned to receive.

At night, at home, alone, Dádiva sat in the front room, and her eyes went, as they always went without her wishing it, to the parcel on top of the furniture. Three years. The faded paper, the tied bow, Estima's present unopened. And Dádiva stayed looking at it in another way, with Brandura's sentence in her head, and saw, for the first time, not an object she had not yet had the leisure to open, but a portrait of herself: she saw herself, wrapped, with the bow tied, on top of a piece of furniture of life, for years, awaiting a leisure that did not come, not letting herself be opened by anyone, with the love of others on the outside of the paper, gathering dust. The parcel was her. And what was inside it, what the daughter had chosen for her with love three years before, was the proof of a love that she, Dádiva, had not let in, that she had refused without even seeing, that she had put on a piece of furniture to gather dust, as she had put, her whole life, all the love others had wished to give her and that she returned with a smile and a there was no need.

She did not open it that night. Not yet. But for the first time in three years she did not look at it as a postponed task; she looked at it as a question, Brandura's question made object: and you, Dádiva, do you let yourself be received by anyone? And she went to bed with the question, and slept badly, the sleep of one who has seen a thing that had been in front of her eyes for three years and that only that night, by the light of a sentence said by an old woman in bed to whom she was going to give, she at last began to see. And in the dark, before falling asleep, she caught herself thinking a thing she had never thought: that perhaps it was not leisure she lacked to open the parcel, perhaps it was courage, and that perhaps the courage to receive was a thing one could learn, as she had learned to give, and that perhaps there was still time, at forty-eight, to learn the other half of what the whole village thought she already knew entire, which was to love.
