## 7. The keeper of the register

Risen from the fever, still weak, Dádiva went to the House of the Register to see to a paper of Brandura's, who had died with no children near and whose affairs Dádiva, as always, had offered to close; and it was so, seeing to the papers of a dead woman she had cared for, that Dádiva sat before the Keeper, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps who gave and who received.

The House of the Register was cool and smelled of old paper, and the shelves rose to the ceiling full of thick-spined books, each one its year, and there was there, in those books, the whole memory of the village, who was born, who married, who departed, and also, in the margins and the lists, who gave and who received, because in Véspera, in the old days, everything was set down, even the help of the great houses to the poor, even the inscriptions on the bread lists, and the books kept, without meaning to, the old map of the two sides, the upper and the lower, that Dádiva had carried in her body since girlhood.

The Keeper, who saw people as she saw the entries, with a look that read between the lines, saw to Brandura's paper, and then, instead of dismissing Dádiva, stayed looking at her, and said: I heard you were bedridden. And that the village came to your aid. And that you, for the first time, let it. Dádiva, surprised the Keeper knew, or not so surprised, for in Véspera everything is known, said yes, that she had been ill, that the village had been good. And the Keeper said, with that dryness which in the Keepers is a form of tenderness: it was the first time you received, was it not, Dádiva. At forty-eight. It took a fever for the saint to drop her guard.

And Dádiva, who expected perhaps a praise for the illness well borne, took the truth instead, and the Keeper, seeing her disconcerted, rose, and went to a shelf, and took down an old book, and opened it, and turned it towards Dádiva, and said: look. And it was the page of the years of Dádiva's mother, Conceição, and the Keeper passed her finger down some entries, and Dádiva saw her mother's name, and beside it, in entries of other hands, the records of what in Véspera was set down of the poor houses, the helps, the council's supports, the inscriptions on the lists of those who received the bread of the great houses, and the Keeper said: your mother is here many times, Dádiva. On the side of those who received. All her life. And you grew up seeing this, seeing your mother on this side of the book, and swore to yourself you would never be here. And you are not. You, in my book, are always on the other side, the side of those who give. But see one thing.

And the Keeper turned the pages, and showed Dádiva the entries of the houses that gave, the great ones, and said: these houses, the upper ones, the ones that gave the bread to your mother, thought themselves above her. They gave from above downward, with the ease of those who have, and some gave with kindness and others gave with contempt, but all gave from above. And your mother received from below, with shame. And you looked at this, as a girl, and decided to be of these, of the upper ones, of those who give. But what you did not see, Dádiva, is that between giving from above and receiving from below there is a third place, which is not in this book because the book only sets down the houses, it does not set down the hearts: it is the place of one who gives and receives without rising or coming down, of equal to equal. Your mother was not bent because she received; she was bent because she thought receiving put her below. And you are not upright because you give; you are alone because you think receiving would put you below. It is the same belief, child. Your mother and you believe the same thing, that to receive is to come down. Only she received coming down, and you, so as not to come down, stopped receiving. And you were left as alone above as she was left bent below.

Dádiva heard this from the Keeper and it was as if a veil were taken from her, because it was the thing she had begun to glimpse with Brandura and with the fever, said now with the cold clearness of one who reads the books of the village: that she and her mother were the same wound, the mother receiving with bowed head, she not receiving at all, the two of them believing that to receive is to come down, the two of them held to a map of the world of only two sides, the upper and the lower, without the third, the level one, the of-equal-to-equal, where one stands, at the same time, upright and accompanied. And she perceived that she had spent a life fleeing the mother and being the mother, that the wall that had saved her from the shame had left her as alone as the shame had left the mother, and that the only way out was not to give more, that that only raised the wall higher, it was to learn to receive without feeling below, which was the thing neither the mother nor she had learned, and which was, after all, the key to everything.

And there was a thing, on the page, that the Keeper did not comment on and that Dádiva saw alone, and that stayed with her: that beside the entries of the helps the mother had received, there was, in later years, the name of Dádiva, now on the side of those who gave, on the side of the houses that helped, and Dádiva perceived, seeing her own name pass from the lower side to the upper side along the pages, that she had achieved, yes, what she had sworn in girlhood, that she had risen, that she had changed sides, that the book confirmed it; and she perceived, in the same instant, that her victory had been also her prison, that in rising so resolutely to the side of the one who gives she had closed herself forever to the side of the one who receives, and that the book of the village, which seemed to prove her right, gave her, read with care, the sentence: here is Dádiva, said the book, who managed never to be on the side of receiving, and who therefore was left never receiving anything, not even what does good.

Your mother died receiving and feeling below, said the Keeper, closing the book. You are on the way to dying without receiving, and feeling above, and alone. There is a third road, Dádiva, and you tasted it this week, in the fever, without meaning to: you received, and did not feel below, you felt loved. It was the first time. Now the question is whether you have the courage to do it on purpose, with health, without the fever forcing you. Because to receive by obligation, bedridden, without strength, that life forces on us all one day. To receive by choice, upright, with strength, that is courage. And it will ask of you more courage, to open a door to let in, than a whole life asked of you to open doors to give. But that is the one you lack, child. The door of entry.

Dádiva left the House of the Register with her mother on her mind, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain, and the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her she had never thought: that a fountain is not only the water it gives; it is also the water it receives, from the mountain, from the rain, from the springs, and that a fountain that only gave and never received ran dry in a day, because the giving of a fountain lives off its receiving, and that she, Dádiva, who had wished to be a fountain of giving for half the village, was dry within for precisely that, for never having let anything in, for having wished to be a fountain that only gives, which is a thing that does not exist in nature, because in nature everything that gives, receives, and only men, in their stubbornness, invent giving without receiving and call it virtue, and run dry.

And Dádiva went home, that afternoon, with the truth of the Keeper and that of the fountain and that of Brandura, all saying the same thing in different mouths, and looked, coming in, at the parcel on top of the furniture, and the parcel was the door of entry the Keeper spoke of, the one door of Dádiva's house that gave inward and not outward, and it had been shut for three years, with the bow tied, awaiting that the mistress of the house should have, at last, the courage she lacked, the courage not to give, of which she had plenty, but to receive, to let in, to open, to the love of a daughter, the door she had kept bolted her whole life against the shame of a mother, and which was, she knew it now, the one door left to open to stop being, at last, alone at the top of her own wall.
