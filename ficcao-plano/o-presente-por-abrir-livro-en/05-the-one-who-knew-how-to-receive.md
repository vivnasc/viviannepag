## 5. The one who knew how to receive

Brandura taught Dádiva to receive in the only way such things are taught, which is not by words, it is by example: she let herself be cared for with a grace Dádiva had never seen, and in that grace Dádiva saw, for the first time, that receiving could be beautiful.

She went there every day, to Brandura's house, and at first she went as she went everywhere, to give, to do, to tend, with the efficiency of one fulfilling a service; but Brandura, who was bedridden and had time, and who was a woman who saw people, gradually changed, without fuss, the way Dádiva was there. She did not let her come in and go out only doing; she made her sit, asked her things, thanked her in a way that was not the thank-you in passing, it was a thank-you that looked into the eyes and lingered, and Dádiva, who always gave and to whom no one usually paid that attention, began to feel, in Brandura's house, not like the one who gives, but like someone who was also cared for, the other way round, because Brandura, even bedridden, even receiving everything, found a way to give Dádiva a thing no one gave her: she let her be a person instead of a function.

And Dádiva noticed, above all, how Brandura received. She received the broth with a relish that filled the one who gave it, said this broth tastes of life to me, Dádiva, with a gladness that made the broth worth the trouble; she received the care without diminishing herself, without the shadow Dádiva knew from her mother, head high on the pillow, with a dignity of one who accepts what is given as one accepts a right and a grace at once; and when Dádiva did her a thing, Brandura did not say there was no need, did not draw back, did not return it; she said thank you, how good, and let the good come in, and in letting it come in she made Dádiva feel useful, dear, important, in a way the there was no need of others never made her feel.

Dádiva noticed, for instance, one small thing Brandura did that left her thinking: when Dádiva arranged her pillow, Brandura did not say ah don't trouble yourself, leave it; she said ah, I'm much better like this, you have good hands, Dádiva, and in saying it she gave Dádiva the pleasure of having good hands, turned a gesture of service into a gesture of received affection, and Dádiva left there with that you-have-good-hands warming her, and perceived, slowly, that Brandura, in receiving well, gave her more than she herself, Dádiva, gave Brandura, and that this was a mystery she had never understood: that whoever receives well gives as much as whoever gives, and sometimes more, because they complete what the other began, and close the circle, and make the giving worthwhile.

And it was there, in watching Brandura receive, that Dádiva perceived a thing that turned her world inside out: that when someone receives well what we give them, they are giving us a thing. That Brandura, in accepting the broth with relish, in thanking with her eyes in our eyes, in letting herself be cared for without shame, was giving Dádiva the pleasure of having given, was closing the circle, was completing the trade, and that this, that generous receiving, was worth as much as giving, or more, because without it the giving stays halfway, stays falling into emptiness, stays like Estima's present on top of the furniture, given and not received, love fallen on the floor for want of someone to pick it up. And Dádiva, who had spent a life thinking the good was all on the side of giving, saw, beside Brandura's bed, that the good needs both sides, that to give without someone who receives well is like speaking with no one to hear, and that she, in never receiving, in returning everything, did to those who wished to give to her what no one did to her and what hurt: she left them with full hands and nowhere to set them, with the love ungiven, as she had left Estima for a lifetime.

You know how to receive, Brandura, Dádiva said to her, one afternoon, and she said it almost with envy, with the good envy of one who sees in another a thing they lack. And Brandura, from the pillow, smiled, and said, with the clearness of those near the end who no longer have time for roundabouts: I learned, Dádiva. I was not born knowing. I too was one of those who gave and did not receive, like you, when I was young. I thought asking was weakness and receiving was debt. And I fell ill, and had to learn, by force, that either I received or I died alone, and I chose to receive, and discovered it was no weakness at all, it was the other half of love. Look, Dádiva, whoever only gives loves by halves. They love others, but do not let themselves be loved, and to love is in both directions, or it is not loving entire, it is only being useful. And you, my child, you are very useful. But I wonder whether you let yourself be loved, or whether you only let yourself be needed.

That sentence, do you let yourself be loved or only let yourself be needed, stayed in Dádiva as few things had stayed, because it put the finger on the exact wound: Dádiva let herself be needed, yes, the whole village needed her; but did she let herself be loved? Did she let someone want her, not for what she did, but for herself, who would care for her, who would give to her without her having given first? No. And the difference between being needed and being loved, which Dádiva had never made, opened to her there, beside Brandura's bed, like an abyss and like a liberation: she perceived that she had spent a life ensuring she was needed, because to be needed was safe, was to be on the upper side, was not to depend; and that she had never let herself be loved, because to be loved was risky, was to be on the side of receiving, was to depend, and Dádiva had a terror of depending, the terror of her mother's house, and so she had traded love for usefulness, and had been left very useful and little loved, or rather, loved by many and let by none to love her, which is the finest solitude there is, that of one who is dear to the whole village and lets no one come near.

And she perceived, that afternoon, a thing that made her shudder: that to be needed could even be a form of binding people, and that to be loved was to leave them free. Whoever makes themselves only needed, binds; the others stay tied to them by the need, by the debt, and that seems love but is a leash. Whoever lets themselves be loved, that one risks, because love, unlike need, can go away, it chooses, it does not bind; and Dádiva, who had a terror of being left, had chosen, without knowing it, need instead of love, had chosen to bind instead of to risk, and had been left with many people bound to her by need and no one tied to her by the free choice of love, and that was, she perceived now, the difference between the crowd that called her angel and the solitude in which she went to sleep every night.

Brandura worsened, in those weeks, and Dádiva cared for her to the end, and it was a mild end, in Brandura's way, who received death as she received everything, head high on the pillow, with gratitude, without bending. And in the last days, Brandura, who already spoke little, said to Dádiva a thing that was almost a testament, a parting present: Dádiva, when I am gone, do me a favour. Go home and open your daughter's present. I know you have it there, closed, the whole village knows. Open it, my child. For your daughter, and for yourself. Do not die as you were going to die, with all the love unreceived. I die receiving, and see how good it is. Do you the same, but start early, start in life, that is better. And Dádiva promised, her voice caught, and Brandura died some days later, in peace, leaving Dádiva, as a last care, not a thing for her to give, but a thing for her to receive, which was the whole lesson of Brandura's life put in a sentence: open the present, let yourself be loved, do not die with the love unreceived.

And Dádiva left Brandura's funeral, at the end of that afternoon, with the promise weighing on her and easing her at once, and went home, and stood at the front-room door looking at the parcel on top of the furniture, and this time she did not say one of these days; this time she felt, for the first time, that the day was drawing near, that Brandura, in dying, had left her not only the lesson but the obligation, the sweet obligation of keeping a promise to a dead woman, and that to keep that promise was, after all, to do herself the greatest good of her life, was to open, at last, after so much giving, the one thing left to open, which was herself, wrapped for a lifetime, with the bow tied, awaiting, like the daughter's present, someone with the courage to unwrap her, and that courage, Dádiva perceived, looking at the parcel on the night of Brandura's funeral, was not going to come from outside: it had to be she, at last, who gave herself the permission to receive.
