## 6. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to some papers of the house on the heights at the House of the Register, alone like everything, and it was thus, by one more thing she did without asking a hand, that Soledade found herself seated before the Keeper, and the Keeper, who saw people as she saw the houses, saw in the woman the cistern, and told her the truth that was missing.

The Keeper saw to the papers, and noticed Soledade, the way she did everything alone, refused help even for small things, did not let them take the papers to relieve her; and asked her, with that manner of one who reads between the lines, Soledade lives alone in the house on the heights, does she not? The one with its own cistern? And Soledade, with pride, I do, yes, and I have my own water, I never needed to come down to the fountain. And the Keeper looked at her, and said a thing Soledade did not expect: and that is your pride, I know. But let me tell you a thing I have learned to see, in this book, over the years, in the houses of cistern and in the women of cistern, which are the same thing.

And the Keeper said: a cistern gathers the water of the rain and of the spring, and it is a good thing, it is plenty, it is independence, not to need to come down to the fountain. But the cistern has a danger the fountain does not have. The fountain comes from the mountain, runs always, is living water, never runs dry, because it is connected to a vein of water that comes from far and does not stop. The cistern, no. The cistern has only the water it gathered, its own, shut in, still; and in a winter without rain, in a dry summer, the cistern, however deep, goes down, and down, and if the drought lasts, it runs dry, because it is connected to nothing else, it has only what it gathered, and what is gathered runs out, and the cistern that prides itself on not needing the fountain is the one that runs dry when the fountain, that one, goes on running. The houses of cistern are the first to run dry in the great droughts, Soledade. Those of the fountain, never.

And Soledade heard this, and it went deep, because she understood that the Keeper was not speaking only of the water. And the Keeper, seeing that she understood, continued: you are a cistern, Soledade. You gathered everything of yourself your whole life, sufficed to yourself, never asked, never came down to the fountain, never let in the water from outside, and you have in this your pride. But you are in a drought, now, in age, in illness, in solitude, and your cistern is going down, and there is nowhere for more water to come from, because you never connected yourself to any fountain, never let anyone in, never asked, and the water you gathered of yourself is running out. And people, Soledade, are not made to be cisterns. They are made to be fountains, connected to one another, giving and receiving water, asking and rescuing, because that is how the water runs and no one runs dry; and you, by making yourself a cistern, by not asking, cut yourself from the vein that connects people, and you are running dry alone in the house on the heights, not for lack of water in the world, for there are fountains around you, your son, your neighbour, the village, but for never having opened a pipe that would let the water from outside into your cistern.

And the Keeper leafed through the book, and showed her an entry, and said: and look, Soledade. Do you know what is, in my experience, the saddest thing that is written in this book? It is not poverty, nor illness, nor misfortune. It is the death of a cistern. It is when there comes to me the entry of a person who lived surrounded by people who would have helped her, who had children, neighbours, a village, and who died alone in her house, of dryness, not for there being no one to rescue her, but for never having asked, for having said all is well to the end, for having shut the door to all the water from outside; and the saddest, in the entry of those, is that on the other side of the door they kept shut there was, almost always, people wanting to enter, people who would have given everything for an open door, and who stayed outside a whole life, watching the cistern run dry without being able to pour it a drop, because the cistern would not let them. Do not die so, Soledade. You have a son who wants to enter. You have a neighbour who watches your smoke. Open a pipe. Ask. For to ask is not to come down defeated to the fountain, it is to connect your water to that of the others, so that none of you runs dry alone.

And Soledade left the House of the Register with the truth of the cistern weighing on her and opening something in her, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain, the fountain of Véspera, the one she had never used for having her own water; and she looked at it, that time, not with the pride of one who does not need it, but with another thing, with a kind of new envy: because the fountain was surrounded by people, people filling their pitchers, talking, crossing paths, helping one another set the pitcher on the head, and the water of the fountain ran, alive, shared, and never ran dry; and Soledade understood, looking at the fountain full of people and the house on the heights empty up there, that her whole life she had chosen the cistern out of pride, and that the fountain, which she had scorned for being of those who need, was after all the place of life, of the meeting, of the water that does not run dry because it is shared, and that perhaps it was time, at last, to come down, not defeated, but human, and to put herself, she too, beside the fountain, among the people, to ask and to give, as those ask and give who do not want to run dry alone.
