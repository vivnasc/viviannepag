## 10. At the foot of the stairs

And the day came of the greatest trial, the scene the whole book walked towards, small and enormous: Soledade at the foot of another house's stairs, knocking at a door, asking, by her own choice, before despair, and bearing the hardest thing of all for a cistern, which is not to knock, it is to stay and bear the door's opening.

It was so. There was a night on which Soledade, alone in the house on the heights, felt unwell, not fallen yet, not in despair yet, but with a tightening she knew, that of the old fright, and she understood that she was going to need help, and she had, at that moment, the choice the book had been preparing for her: either she waited, as always, for it to pass, or to worsen, or for them to find her fallen, which is to ask late, in despair, without choice; or she went down, now, on her own legs, while she still could, and went to knock at a door, and asked, by her decision, before falling, which is to ask early, by choice, with dignity. And Soledade, for the first time in her life, chose the second. She dressed, took the lantern, and went down, in the dark, down the mountain, to another person's house, to ask for help, exactly like the child of fifty years ago, but now by choice and not by order, now an adult and not a girl, now deciding to go down instead of waiting for the misfortune.

And the descent was the whole book made path. Because while she went down, in the dark, with the lantern, to knock at the door of another house to ask, Soledade was reliving the night of childhood, the other descent in the dark, the other going to ask for help, and she went with all the fear of that night rising through her body, the fear of the door that does not open, and each step down the mountain was a step against fifty years of not asking, was the child and the old woman going down together the same mountain, one trembling for the other, and there was, in each metre, the temptation to turn back, to climb, not to ask, to bear it alone as always; but Soledade did not turn back, she went down, because she had now, against the fear, all she had learned, the neighbour, the son, the Keeper, the doors that had opened, and she went down to the foot of another person's stairs, and stopped before the door, and it was there that the greatest battle was fought.

Because to knock was hard, but to knock Soledade could still do; the hardest, the thing the door of childhood had made almost impossible for her, was what came after knocking: it was to wait. It was to stay there, at the door, with the request made, exposed, at the mercy, waiting for the door to open, without knowing whether it would open, reliving, in that wait, the wait of the child at the door in the dark that did not open; and it was that wait, and not the knocking, the greatest trial, because in the wait was all the fear, that of having asked and possibly not being opened to, that of having exposed herself for nothing, that of the door, like the childhood one, falling silent and leaving her outside. And Soledade knocked, and waited, and her heart beat like the child's, and each second of closed door was fifty years weighing on her, and she wanted to flee, wanted to leave there before knowing, so as not to risk the closed door; but she bore it. She stayed. She bore the door's opening, which is the greatest courage of a cistern, to stay at the door bearing the terrible time between knocking and being opened to, without fleeing, trusting, against fifty years of proof to the contrary, that this time the door would open.

And the door opened. The light came on, footsteps were heard, the door opened, and on the other side was a person, with the face of sleep and the concern of one woken in the middle of the night, but with the door open, and on seeing Soledade there, at the foot of the stairs, unwell, in need, the person did what the door of childhood had not done: she took her in. Come in, Soledade, what is wrong, come inside, sit down, let me help you; and Soledade, on hearing the come in, on seeing the door open wide, on being pulled into a warm house instead of left in the dark at the closed door, felt undone within her, all at once, the night of fifty years ago. Because the door had opened. The door, that time, had opened. And the child who had stayed at the closed door in the dark, and who still lived within Soledade, on the inside of that door of fifty years ago, was at last taken in, fifty years later, by a door that opened, and understood, the child and the old woman together, that after all the world was not the door that did not open, that there were doors that open, that one knocks and they open, that one asks and they come to the rescue, and that she could, at last, stop living behind the closed door of childhood, because she had just knocked at another, and borne it, and seen it open, and entered.
