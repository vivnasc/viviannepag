## 1. The cistern of her own

Soledade took pride in the cistern, and it was an old pride, inherited, of the Highlanders, the house on the heights that gathered its water from the rain and the spring and did not need to come down to the square, to the fountain, where the whole village went to fetch its own; and that pride of the house was the pride of the woman, because Soledade was, herself, a cistern, one that gathered everything of herself and asked nothing of anyone.

She lived alone, in the house on the heights, at sixty, and did everything alone, and always had. She cut the wood, saw to the garden, mended what broke, carried what was heavy, governed the house, and never, in anything, asked a hand of anyone; and when they offered, she refused, with a courteous but whole firmness, thank you, I do not need it, I will do it, which was the phrase of her life, I will do it, and she did, however much it cost, however old she was, however much the body no longer gave, I will do it, alone, without asking, because to ask, for Soledade, was the one thing she did not know how to do, or that she knew she did not do, for an old reason that lay at the bottom of her like the water at the bottom of the cistern, gathered and shut in.

And the village respected her for it, and admired her. They said of her, that one is made of iron, that one needs no one, that one at sixty does what many young ones do not, that one suffices to herself; and Soledade heard, and liked to hear, because the sufficing was her pride, was what she was, the one who does not need, the cistern that has its own water and does not come down to the fountain; and each time she refused a help, said I will do it, carried alone what another would ask to have carried, she felt a deep satisfaction, that of owing nothing to anyone, of sufficing to herself, of being independent like the house on the heights, with water of her own, with no need of the square.

But there was, beneath that pride, a thing Soledade did not say and that was to be the book: that her sufficing was not only strength, it was also a wall. The house on the heights, with its cistern, did not need the fountain, it is true, but it was also far from the fountain, far from the square, far from the gathering of the village around the water, far from the talk, the sharing, the life that is made where people cross paths filling the pitcher; and Soledade, in the house on the heights, sufficing to herself, was also alone, apart, outside, and the independence that was her pride was also her isolation, because one who does not need the fountain does not go to the fountain, and one who does not go to the fountain meets no one, and the cistern of one's own, which spares the descent, spares also the meeting.

And the body, at sixty, was beginning to tell her that the sufficing had a limit. There were things that already cost her, the wood, the weights, the garden, and that she did all the same, with effort, with pains, because I will do it, because to ask, no; and there were days on which, alone in the house on the heights, she felt that if something happened to her, a fall, a sudden ill, there was no one, there was no one to notice her, because she lived apart, shut in, without letting anyone in, and the independence that gave her pride was, seen another way, a danger, the danger of one who, by not needing anyone, has no one, and may fall in the house on the heights and stay there, fallen, with no one knowing, because no one enters, because she never let anyone in.

There was a son, emigrated, far away, who telephoned, and was the only bond, and even that one Soledade did not let in. The son asked, mother, is all well, do you need anything; and Soledade always answered the same, all is well, son, I need nothing, all is well; and the son, far away, soothed by the all is well, did not insist, because the mother had always been so, made of iron, of not needing, and he had grown up hearing her say I will do it, and had got used to a mother who did not ask, and so did not see, from afar, that the all is well was the greatest lie of the house on the heights, the lie Soledade told everyone, the son, the neighbour, the village, the lie of the sufficing, which hid a woman who needed, like everyone, and who had decided, fifty years before, on a night, not to let it be known again.

And there was a night, at the start of the book, on which Soledade, alone in the house on the heights, felt her chest tighten, a pain that frightened her, and she stayed there, alone, with the pain, without calling anyone, because to call was to ask, and she did not ask; and the pain passed, that time, but it left her a fright, and the fright left her a question, the first of the book: and if one day it does not pass? And if one day I fall, and there is no one, because I never let anyone in? And Soledade, with the question, did not change at once, for one does not change a cistern of fifty years in a night; but the question stayed, dripping, at the bottom of her, and it was the first drop of a new water, the suspicion that the sufficing, which had been her pride, might be, in the end, her sentence, that of dying alone in the house on the heights, of dryness, for never having learned the one thing the cistern lacked: to let in the water from outside, which is, in people, to ask.
