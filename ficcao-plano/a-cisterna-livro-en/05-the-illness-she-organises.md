## 5. The illness she organises

Soledade fell ill, in earnest this time, of an illness that asked for treatment, trips to the larger town, care, and Soledade did what a cistern does when it falls ill: she organised the illness so as not to have to ask, which is the most extreme form of not asking, to turn her own weakness into a work to be done alone.

Because to fall ill, for most people, is the hour of asking, it is when even the most independent accept a hand, a lift, company, care, because the sick body compels; but Soledade, no. Soledade, on learning that she had to go to the larger town to be treated, instead of asking the son to come, or the neighbour to take her, or whoever a help, set about organising the illness alone, as she organised everything: she studied the bus timetables, calculated how to go and return on the days of the treatments, found a way to do everything by herself, the old sick woman catching the bus alone to go and be treated and returning alone to the house on the heights, and telling everyone, the son, the neighbour, all is seen to, I manage, I need nothing.

And it was a spectacle of stubbornness that moved and revolted, the sick Soledade refusing that the illness should force her to ask. The body told her ask, you are ill, you cannot alone; and she answered the body I will do it, and did, exhausted, trembling, catching buses, making alone a journey no one should make alone when ill, only so as not to ask, only so as not to owe, only to keep standing, even ill, even falling, the pride of the cistern that suffices to itself; and there was in this a courage, yes, but it was a courage turned inside out, a courage in the service of fear, the enormous courage a person spends so as not to do the small and terrifying thing that is to ask.

And the son, on learning of the illness, wanted to come, and offered, mother, I will come there, I will take you to the treatments, I will stay with you that time; and Soledade refused, do not come, son, it is not worth it, all is seen to, I manage, make your own life; and the son insisted, and she refused again, with firmness, and the son, from afar, unable to come by force, was left with the powerlessness of one who wants to help and is not let, with his mother ill refusing him entry, and he was hurt, and said to her on the telephone, in a voice Soledade did not forget: mother, you are taking from me the one thing I can give you. I cannot be near, I live far away, and when there is one time when I could come, do something, be of use to you, you do not let me. Why? Why do you prefer to go ill, alone, on the bus, to letting your son care for you? What did I do to you, mother, for you not to let me help?

And the son's question, what did I do to you for you not to let me help, shook Soledade, because the answer was nothing, the son had done nothing to her, and yet she refused him, and she understood, with the question, that her refusal had nothing to do with the son, it had to do with the door of childhood, with the night on which she had asked and they had not opened to her, and that she was making the son pay for a door that had shut fifty years before, refusing him entry not for distrusting him, but for having learned, as a child, to distrust all doors; and that the son, who was a door that wanted to open, who offered himself, who asked to enter, was being treated by her as the door that did not open, shut out, refused, without understanding why, because the why was not his, it was of a night of fifty years ago that she had never told him.

And it was in that illness, organised alone with so much effort, that Soledade touched the bottom of the book, and that it was necessary to touch in order to turn. On one of the trips, alone, ill, on the bus, exhausted, trembling, with her body crying out to her that she could not, Soledade felt herself, for the first time in fifty years, not proud of her sufficing, but tired of it, tired to the bone of doing everything alone, of not asking, of organising even the illness so as not to owe anything to anyone; and there came to her, in the midst of the tiredness, a new wish, almost a desire, that frightened her and that relieved her at the same time: the wish to ask. To stop. To let someone take her, someone care, the son come, the neighbour accompany her, to rest, at last, from the sufficing, to set down the cistern; and she understood, with that wish, that perhaps it was not weakness to want to be cared for, that perhaps it was the most human thing there is, and that she, by refusing it her whole life, had not been strong, had only been faithful to a closed door, and that it was time, at last, ill, tired, to do the thing she had never done, and that her illness, perhaps, was the occasion to learn it: to ask, before the body, as the doctor of other stories had said, forced her to ask in the worst way, fallen, without choice, late.
