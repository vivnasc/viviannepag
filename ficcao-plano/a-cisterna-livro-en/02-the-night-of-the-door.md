## 2. The night of the door

Soledade decided not to ask again on a night, as a child, and the decision lasted fifty years, and it was because of a door that did not open, because there are doors that do not open in childhood and that close forever within a person, and she spends her life behind them, on the inside, without knocking at another.

She was small, she would have been eight or nine, and the house, in that time, was passing through a great trouble, of the kind there are in the poor houses of the mountain, an illness, a want, a night of despair; and the mother, or whoever it was, sent the child Soledade down, in the dark, down the mountain, to ask for help, to knock at the door of a house of people who could help, to ask, in the family's name, an urgent help. And the child went down, in the dark, with fear, with the cold, with the weight of going to ask, which even as a child cost her, and reached the door, and knocked, and waited, and knocked again, and on the inside there was light, there were people, voices could be heard, and the door did not open.

It did not open. The child knocked, and called, and said who she was, and what she needed, and on the other side the house fell silent, put out its light, feigned itself empty, and the door did not open; and the child Soledade stayed there, at the closed door, in the dark, in the cold, with the request in her mouth and no one to hear it, and understood, with all the terrible clarity of children, a thing that stayed with her forever: that she had asked, that she had exposed herself, that she had put herself in another's hand, in the humiliation of knocking and asking, and that the door had not opened, that the other had seen her small, in need, at his mercy, and had shut the door in her face, and that to ask, therefore, was this, was to expose oneself for nothing, was to humble oneself to be met with the closed door, was to be left at the mercy of one who could, simply, not open.

And the child went back up, in the dark, alone, with the trouble of the house unresolved and the closed door at her back, and on that climb, alone up the mountain, she decided a thing, without words, with her whole body, with her whole wound: she decided never again. Never again would she ask. Never again would she expose herself to that, to the knocking, to the needing, to the door that does not open. She would do everything alone, from there on, cost what it might, so as never again to have to go down in the dark to ask, so as never again to be left at anyone's door with the request in her mouth and the door in her face; and from that night the child Soledade began to become the cistern, to gather everything of herself, not to need, to suffice to herself, because to suffice to herself was the only way she found not to feel again what she had felt at the closed door, the humiliation of asking and not being opened to.

And it was that night, and not another thing, the source of the cistern. All of Soledade's independence, all the pride of the I will do it, all the refusal of help, all the all is well of fifty years, came from that door that did not open to a child in the dark. Because a child to whom a door is shut when she asks learns that to ask is dangerous, that to need is to be at the mercy, that to depend is to risk humiliation; and so as not to feel it again, she shuts herself, makes herself of stone, decides not to need, and grows the cistern Soledade became, the one that gathers everything of herself so as not to have to knock at any door, the one that would sooner run dry than risk again the closed door.

And the worst, which Soledade only came to understand much later, is that that door, shut once, in childhood, she had come to see in every door. Each time she could ask, as an adult, she saw, without realising it, the door of childhood, the light inside, the voices, and the door that does not open; and so she did not ask, because to ask was, for her, forever, to go down in the dark and knock at a door that will not open; and it never occurred to her that there were doors that open, that most doors open, that that one, the childhood one, had been one, a misfortune, a cruelty of a house on a night, and not the law of all doors; but the child did not know how to do that sum, she generalised the closed door to all doors, and the woman lived fifty years treating all doors as the one that did not open, and so never again knocked at any, and so ran dry.

And when Soledade, at sixty, with the pain in her chest and the fright of the empty house on the heights, began to suspect that the sufficing was going to kill her, it was to that night that she had, without meaning to, to return, because it is always to the source that one has to return to understand the river; and on remembering the closed door, the child in the dark, the decision she had taken climbing the mountain, she understood, for the first time in fifty years, that her dryness was not strength, it was wound, that she did not ask not for being strong but for being afraid, the old fear of the door that does not open, and that if she wanted, at last, not to die a cistern, she would have to do the hardest thing she could imagine, harder than all the wood and all the weights of fifty years: she would have to go down again in the dark, and knock again at a door, and risk again that it would not open, in order to discover, if it opened, that not all doors are the one of childhood, and that she could, at last, let in the water from outside.
