## 4. The smoke that is missing

It was a neighbour of the half-slope, who saw the house on the heights from afar, who noticed what Soledade hid, and she noticed it by the smoke, because there are things only one who looks from afar and with care sees, and the care of that neighbour was what began to pierce the cistern from outside, while the son pierced it from within.

The neighbour was called by some name of the mountain, and lived on the half-slope, lower than the house on the heights, and from her window one could see Soledade's house, up there, and one could see, in the morning, the smoke of the chimney, the sign that Soledade had risen, lit the fire, was well. And the neighbour, over the years, without ever speaking of it, had got into the habit of looking in the morning at the house on the heights, in search of the smoke, like one who checks whether the old woman of the heights, who lives alone and lets no one in, is still well; and it was the only watch Soledade had, without knowing it, the eye of a neighbour who saw her smoke, because the smoke was the only thing of Soledade's she could not hide, that rose from the chimney in sight of anyone who cared to look.

And there was a morning on which the smoke did not come. The neighbour looked, as she always looked, and the chimney of the heights was out, without smoke, and it was late already for that, for Soledade lit it early; and the neighbour found it strange, and waited, and looked again, and nothing, and her heart tightened, because a smoke that is missing in the house of an old woman who lives alone means, many times, a misfortune; and the neighbour, instead of doing what Soledade would do, which was not to meddle, to leave each to her own, climbed the slope, went to the house on the heights, to knock at the door, to see what it was, because she was no cistern, she was one of those who notice the smoke that is missing and climb to see.

And well she did, because Soledade had fallen. It had not been grave, a fall, an indisposition, but she had stayed on the floor, unable to get up well, alone, without lighting the fire, without calling anyone, because to call was to ask, and even fallen on the floor of the house on the heights Soledade had not called, had borne it there, waiting to right herself alone, because I will do it, because to ask, no, even fallen; and it was the neighbour, who had noticed the smoke that was missing, who found her, and lifted her, and came to her rescue, and saw to her, and stayed with her, and did for her what Soledade would never let be done if she were on her feet to refuse it.

And Soledade, rescued by the neighbour, felt two things at once, and the struggle between them was the book. She felt, first, the shame, the old humiliation, that of having been seen fallen, at the mercy, in need, which was exactly what she had spent her life avoiding, which was the door of childhood in reverse, to be caught on the floor needing one who would lift her; and she wanted, with all the force of the cistern, to refuse, to say I am well, it is not needed, I will get up, go away, I manage; and she almost said it. But she felt, beneath the shame, another thing, new, that disarmed her: she felt relief. The enormous, physical relief of not being alone, of having someone there, of being lifted from the floor by another person's hands, of not having to right herself alone one more time; and it was the first time in fifty years that Soledade felt the relief of being rescued, and it was so deep, and so unknown, that it left her confused, because all her life they had told her, and she had believed it, that to need was humiliation, and there, fallen, rescued, she discovered that to need, and to let oneself be rescued, was also, beneath the shame, a relief, a rest, almost a sweetness, that of not being alone.

And the neighbour, on leaving, after seeing her well, said to her a simple thing that stayed with Soledade. She said: oh Soledade, I see your smoke every morning, from down there. It is my way of knowing that you are well. Today there was no smoke, and I climbed. If one day you need it, do not wait for the fall. Light two fires, make two smokes, or put a cloth at the window, arrange a sign with me, and I will climb. I will not leave you up there alone. And she went away, and Soledade stayed with that, with the I see your smoke, with the I will not leave you up there alone, and understood, shaken, that after all she was not so alone as she thought, that there was, on the half-slope, a person who watched her smoke every morning, who cared, who had climbed; and that her solitude, her cistern, her not needing, were not so true as she believed, because there were people willing to enter, to come to her rescue, to watch her smoke, and it was she, and only she, who did not let them, who shut the door, who said all is well, who refused; and that perhaps her solitude was not a misfortune that had fallen on her, but a door she kept shut, and that on the other side there was, after all, those who wanted to enter, if she one day opened it.
