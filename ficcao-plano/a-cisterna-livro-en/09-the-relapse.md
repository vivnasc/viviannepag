## 9. The relapse

But one does not open a cistern of fifty years without its shutting again at the first thing, and Soledade had her relapse, and it came in the form the relapses of those who learned not to ask always come in: under cover of not wanting to be a burden, which is the fine excuse with which the old pride shuts itself again after a chink of opening.

It came when the son returned to his life, after having come and cared for her a time. Soledade, in that time the son had been there, had asked, let herself be cared for, and it had been good; but the moment the son left, and she was again alone in the house on the heights, the old reflex returned, now dressed as delicacy: she began to think that she had asked too much already, that she should not abuse the neighbour, that she should not call the son again, that she had troubled them enough, that now she had to go back to doing things alone so as not to be a burden; and behind the I do not want to abuse, which seemed consideration, was the relapse, was the pride shutting the pipe again, the cistern gathering itself again, the all is well returning to her mouth.

And she went back to doing everything alone. She stopped asking the neighbour, stopped telling the son how she was, went back to the all is well, and in a short time she was shut again, running dry again, carrying alone again what she should share; and the worst is that she convinced herself it was kindness, that she was sparing the others, not weighing on them, when she was only retreating to the known wall, taking refuge in the I do not need it that was more comfortable to her than the new humility of asking. And the body, again, warned her, because on going back to doing everything alone her strength failed her again, and there was a bad day, and Soledade found herself again on the floor of the house on the heights, alone, not calling, and it was on that floor, in the exact repetition of the fall of before, that she woke to the relapse.

And it was the neighbour, again, the one of the smoke, who showed it to her. She climbed, because there was no smoke, found her, came to her rescue, and this time, instead of only helping, she scolded, with the affection of one who scolds because she cares: oh Soledade, well? Again? You learned to ask, you were asking, you were better, and now you have shut yourself again, gone back to the all is well, gone back to falling alone. Why? And Soledade, ashamed, said the truth: I did not want to abuse it. I had already asked you, had already called my son, I thought I should not trouble you again. And the neighbour, on hearing the I did not want to abuse it, shook her head, and said to her the thing that made her stay: Soledade, to abuse is to ask for what one does not need, or to ask only of one until one wears him out. To ask for what one needs, shared among those who love us, is not to abuse, it is to live. You do not abuse me by asking me a help now and then, as I do not abuse you if one day I need it and ask you. That is how people live, asking and giving, by turns. What is not to live is what you were doing, running dry alone so as not to trouble anyone. That is not not abusing. That is pride by another name.

And Soledade, on hearing pride by another name, understood that the neighbour was right, that the I do not want to abuse was the disguised pride, the old cistern shutting itself with a pretty excuse; and she understood a deep thing about learning to ask: that it was not a thing one learns once and it stays, it was a thing one has to learn again every day, against the habit of fifty years, which always returns, at the first respite, to shut the pipe; and that what mattered was not never to relapse, for that was impossible, it was to recognise the relapse when it came, dressed as not abusing, as all is well, as I will do it, and to open the pipe again, to ask, to let in again. And she went back to asking, of the neighbour, of the son, and this time with the awareness that she was going to have to do it always, that the cistern was always wanting to shut itself, and that to live connected to the fountain was a work of every day, and not a victory of one time; and she understood that to have at her side one who would scold her, who would tell her Soledade, you have shut yourself again, was the difference between running dry alone and being pulled back to the water each time she retreated, and that it was that, that of having one who calls us back to the fountain, the greatest gift of having, at last, let someone in.
