## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Soledade — solitude — of the Highlanders, of the house up on the heights, the one with its own cistern, and the name was a sentence before it was a name: they called her Soledade, solitude, and she fulfilled the name to the letter, making of solitude not a misfortune that fell on her, but a fortress she raised, a house high on the mountain, with water of her own, that never needed to come down to the square, to ask of the fountain, to depend on anyone, and that had in this, her whole life, her pride and her sentence.

The register keeps the houses and what each one has, and the house of the Highlanders, in the register of Véspera, is set down as a house of its own cistern, one of the few that gather their water and do not need the fountain of the square; and that was, on the mountain, a sign of plenty and of independence, the house that suffices to itself, that does not come down to ask, that has its own; and Soledade was the owner of that house and that cistern, and she was herself, one sees on reading the book, a cistern in person, a woman who gathered everything of herself, who sufficed to herself, who never came down to ask anything of anyone, and who had in this, like the house, the pride of not needing and the dryness of one who never lets in the water from outside.

This is the second book of the shelf of the Mountain, the shelf of those who ask what they had to become in order to bear up, and within it, it is the book of the one who never asks. It tells the story of a woman of sixty who does everything alone and always has, who takes pride in not needing anyone, who to her emigrated son always answers all is well, who to the neighbour who notices always refuses the help, and who learned, as a child, on a night the book keeps, that to ask was not safe, and who decided on that night not to ask again, and kept the decision for fifty years. It is not a book against strength nor against sufficing to oneself; it is a book about one who shut herself into a cistern so as not to depend, and about the price of that dryness, and about the courage, the greatest of all for one who never asks, of knocking at a door before despair, and of bearing the door's opening.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Soledade who never asks, who does everything alone out of pride and out of wound, who answers all is well when it is not, who would sooner run dry than depend, let her know, from the first line, what it took Soledade fifty years to learn: that to suffice to oneself is not not to need, it is not to let anyone in; that the cistern that never receives water from outside, however much it gathers, in a dry summer runs dry; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to bear up more alone, it is to come down from the house on the heights, and to knock at a door, and to ask, before despair, and to bear the hardest thing of all for one who shut herself a whole life: that the door should open.
