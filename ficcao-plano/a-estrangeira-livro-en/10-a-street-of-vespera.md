## 10. A street of Véspera

Peregrina only noticed she belonged to the street on the day the street needed her without her thinking about it.

It was one morning, going down to the fountain with a piece of work in her hand to show Graça, and crossing the street where she lived she became aware, suddenly, of a thing that had been made underneath, without her seeing: she knew everyone. She greeted this one and that one by name, good morning, senhor Aurélio, and your leg, is it better; she knew the stories of the houses, who lived in each, who was ill, who was to marry; and the street, which on arrival had been to her a corridor of strangers, was now her street, peopled with known faces, with people who knew her, who asked after her, who counted on her to take in a hem or keep a key or carry a message.

She had entered, without noticing, into the small economy of favours of the street, which is where a person belongs in earnest, more than in the papers. She kept the key of the next-door neighbour's house, who went to the doctor in the big town on Wednesdays. She minded the grandson of the woman at the end of the street, an hour, when the grandmother could not, and the boy already called her aunt Peregrina, and there was nothing that warmed her more, because a child who calls us aunt in a street is an entry worth more than the one in the Keeper's book. She watered the mallows of the one across the way in summer. And they gave her, in exchange, eggs, and greens, and company, and a turn in the conversation, and the place in the shade at the end of the day. That was how the street had been taking her for its own, not by a decision, but by a thousand small exchanges, thread by thread, the way a blanket is woven.

She stopped in the middle of the street, with the work in her hand, with that sudden awareness, and understood that she had, without noticing, made memories there. That street already had, for her, stories: the house where such a thing had happened, the corner where she had met Graça, the doorstep where she sat at the end of the afternoon when the heat would not let her sew. And to have memories in a place, she understood, is the beginning of being of that place, because belonging is made of memories, and she, who had arrived with none, now had her own, made there, hers, that no one could take from her nor deny her.

And she understood, with this, the answer to what had hurt her most at the festa, the you can't understand, that's for one who's of the land. It was true she would never have the old memories of the village, those from before her arrival, those of the stories of thirty years ago. To those she would stay of away, always. But she was making new memories, those of now, those of the time when she was already here, and to those she belonged by full right, because she had lived them. And a few years from now, the children who saw her every day at the window sewing would grow up with Peregrina in their childhood memories, like a person of here, of always, of their street; and when those children were grown, Peregrina would be, to them, one of the village, of here, because she had been there their whole lives. Slowly, a generation ahead, she would become of here not in her own memory, but in that of others, which is where belonging, in the end, settles.

In autumn, at the festa of the end of the harvests, she found herself on the other side of everything. At the first festa, on arrival, she had laid the tables like one who offers herself, from outside, hoping to be accepted, and had been the tourist who helps. That year, she was one of those who direct: she went to the festa committee, decided what was bought, distributed the tasks, scolded one who was late, laughed with the others in the communal kitchen making the food for the whole village, with her hands in the dough and her apron dirty, one of many, indistinct, of within. And at a certain point, in the midst of the heat and the noise, a girl from away arrived, on a visit, offering to help, with that air of one who wants to be accepted; and Peregrina recognised herself in her, and said to her, naturally, come on, take those platters, carry them over there; and in ordering the one from away, in being the one of here who distributes the tasks, she understood the road she had walked, from the one who asks to help to the one who shares out the help, from the doorstep to the fire, from the guest to the owner of the house.

Winter came, and with it Peregrina's first Christmas in the village, and it was then that she measured, all at once, the distance she had walked. All her life Christmas had been the call. Far away, in the flat, it was the mother cooking the supper Portuguese in a country that did not make that supper, and at the end of the night the dear call to the village, the receiver passing from hand to hand, and Peregrina, a child, talking with cousins she barely knew, wishing happy Christmas to faceless voices, and hanging up with the feeling that the true Christmas was happening somewhere else, on the other end of the line, without her. Christmas had always been the Christmas of others, heard on the telephone.

And that year, no. That year Peregrina made the supper in the grandparents' house, which was hers, and Graça came, and they went to midnight mass, and in the street, on coming out, in the cold, with the whole village together, there were those who wished her happy Christmas by name, oh, Peregrina, happy Christmas, and she answered the same, and went home by her street, with all the windows lit, and understood, in the middle of the dark street with the lights at the windows, that she was on the inside of the telephone. That the Christmas she had heard her whole life from the other end of the line was that one, and that she was in it, at last, in person, and not on the telephone. She called the mother, of course, for the mother was alone, far away, and it was the mother's turn to be the faceless voice on the other end of the line; and Peregrina, on hanging up, felt, for the first time, the sadness the other way round, that of being inside the Christmas and having the mother outside, and understood that to belong to a place is also this, it is to begin to be, for someone, the voice from within that another hears from without.

And in spring came the deepest sign of all, the one she would never have expected: they asked her to be a godmother. The girl whose wedding dress she had made was expecting a child, and came with her husband, embarrassed and glad, to ask Peregrina if she would be godmother to the child, that they had thought of her, that they were fond of her, that they wanted her of family. And Peregrina, who had come from so far, who had been the foreigner, the tourist, the one it doesn't take, was left speechless, because to be godmother to a child of a land is the most of-within thing that land can give to anyone: it is to say to her you stay tied to us for ever, you stay responsible for one of ours, you stay family by choice and not by blood, which is the only family one chooses. She accepted, with her eyes full, and at the christening she held the child at the font, and the priest said her name, Peregrina, godmother, before the whole village, and it was an entry worth more than all the papers she had come to see to at the House of the Register: that of having, in that land, a child who was a little hers, and who would grow up calling her godmother, and to whom she, one day, would tell where she came from, of there and of here, of the two lands the godmother was made of.

And there was a day that sealed everything, and it was small and it was enormous.

A young woman came to the village, from away. Peregrina saw her in the square, lost, with a bag in her hand and that air she knew so well because it had been her own: the air of one who does not know where she is nor to whom she belongs. She was another who was coming back, it was understood afterwards, a daughter of emigrants like her, come looking for the land of her parents, with the same hope and the same mistake. Her name was Esperança — hope — and she was in front of the church looking at a paper with an address on it, not knowing the way.

She came up to Peregrina, hesitant, with the accent of nowhere that Peregrina recognised like a mirror.

"Excuse me. Can you tell me where this house is? I'm from here... well, I'm not quite from here, but my family is. I'm a bit lost."

And Peregrina knew how to answer.

She knew the way, she knew the house, she knew whose it was, she knew the story of the family that lived there. She knew the things of here that the woman from away did not know, the same ones she had not known on arrival and that had earned her the tourist. And in answering, in being, for that foreigner, the one of here, the one who knows, the one who guides the one who arrives, Peregrina felt a thing that filled her eyes: she understood that she had become of here. That she was no longer the lost one they called tourist. She was the one who guided the lost one. That she had passed, without noticing, from the side of outside to the side of within. And that the sign that one belongs to a land is not to stop finding it strange, it is to be, one day, the person to whom another, more of a foreigner, asks the way.

But she did not stop at the way. She looked at Esperança, lost in the square with the bag in her hand, and saw herself, on the half-past-five bus, and did for her what the Keeper had done for her. She took her for a coffee. She sat down with her. And she told her what the Keeper had told her, and what the fountain had taught her, and what she herself had learned, by force, in two years of a half-packed bag:

"You're not of here, yet. I wasn't either. You'll hear many a time that you're a tourist, that you talk like a foreigner, that you can't understand the things of here. You'll have the urge to pack the bag at the first disappointment."

"And do I?" asked Esperança, with the cup in her hands, in earnest. "Do I go back?"

"No. Look at me. I almost went back, three or four times. Each time I went back I'd begin again of away somewhere else, and somewhere else, at the first person who said a crooked thing to me, I'd leave again. I went about so for thirty-eight years." Peregrina laid her hand on the table, slowly. "Half this village is of people who came back, like you, like me. You're not alone, even if you feel it. The land you want to belong to is not going to adopt you, because no land adopts anyone. But you can come to be of here, if you stay, if you give yourself, if you build a life here. Home is not waiting for you anywhere. It's made. And it's made where one decides to stay."

Esperança heard her with the eyes of one who hears a thing she needed to hear, and Peregrina, in saying it, understood that she belonged now in a way even deeper, because to belong truly to a land is to be able to receive in it one who arrives, is to be, for the one who comes from away, part of the within that takes her in. She, who had never belonged anywhere, was now, in that square of Véspera, a root already firm where another, still loose, could hold on to begin its own.

Esperança appeared, and stayed, and appeared again, and the two of them went becoming friends, the two from away, the two learning to be of here; and Peregrina was for Esperança what the Keeper had been for her, the living proof that it was possible, the hand held out in the dark. And without anyone arranging anything, there was forming in the village a discreet circle of those who had come back, or of the children of those who had left: Esperança, and another who arrived the following year, and the Keeper herself, who had also been born away, and Peregrina at the centre, without meaning to, because she was the one who had first understood the way. They gathered sometimes, told their stories of two lands, laughed at the same pains, at the poor thing, at the it doesn't take, and gave one another the thing they had most lacked: someone who understood without its needing to be explained. And Peregrina understood that the village had, beneath the everlasting village, a village of returners, hers, that of those who were of neither here nor there and who had made it theirs by force of staying; and that she, the foreigner, not only belonged now to the village, but had, within it, a people who were the most hers of all, that of her equals, that of the split who chose to stick.

She walked Esperança to the house she was looking for, introduced her to those who were there, gave her her name and her door, come round whenever you like, and went home at the end of the afternoon alone, and stopped again in the middle of the street, her street, and looked at it with the eyes of one who looks at her own home. Without the bag half-packed. Without the foot out. Without the feeling of being passing through. She looked at the street of Véspera the way one says it's here, it's this one, it's mine, not because she had been born in it, but because she had chosen it, and stayed, and worked it, and made in it memories and ties and roots. And she understood that the home she had spent her whole life looking for in the lands of origin was there, in that street she had not inherited and had made her own, brick by brick, gesture by gesture, day by day, from the within to the without; and that she was, at last, after thirty-eight years, at home.
