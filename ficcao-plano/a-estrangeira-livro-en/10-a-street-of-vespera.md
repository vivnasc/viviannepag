## 10. A street of Véspera

And it was in a street of Véspera that Peregrina saw, with her eyes, the home she had been building within, because the home that is made within, when it is made, comes out, and gains walls, and streets, and people, and becomes a place where one lives, and no longer only a decision in the head.

It came about slowly, over the months, and Peregrina only noticed it when it was already made. One day, crossing the street where she lived, she became aware that she knew everyone, that she greeted this one and that one by name, that she knew the stories of the houses, who lived in each, the joys and the sorrows of the street; and she became aware that the street, which on arrival had been strange to her, full of people she did not know, was now her street, peopled with known faces, with neighbours, with people who knew her, who asked after her, who counted on her; and she understood, stopped in the middle of the street, with that sudden awareness, that she had, without noticing, made memories there, that that street already had, for her, stories, the house where that happened, the corner where she met so-and-so, the place where such a thing came to pass, and that to have memories in a place is the beginning of being of that place, because belonging is made of memories, and she, who had arrived with none, now had, after some months of living and of staying, her own, made there, hers, that no one could take from her nor deny her.

And she understood a deep thing about memories, that answered what had most hurt her at the feast, the you cannot understand, that is for those who are of the land. It is that she would never have the old memories of the village, those from before arriving, those of the stories of thirty years back, and to those she would remain from away, always; but she was making new memories, those of now, those of the time in which she was already there, and to those she belonged by full right, because she had lived them; and that the village, from there on, would have a layer of memories that were hers, Peregrina's, that she had lived, and that the children of the street who saw her every day would grow up with Peregrina in their childhood memories, as a person of here, of always, of their street; and that after some years, when those children were grown, Peregrina would be, for them, one of the village, of here, because she had been there their whole lives, and that so, slowly, a generation ahead, she would become of here not in her own memory, but in that of the others, which is where belonging, in the end, settles.

And there was a day that sealed the turn, and it was small and it was enormous, as the scenes of Véspera are. There came to the village a new person, from away, another who returned, or who came for the first time, lost, a foreigner, with that air of one who does not belong that Peregrina knew so well because it had been her own; and that person, in the street, lost, asked Peregrina a thing, a direction, a name, one of those things that Peregrina, on arrival, had not known and that had earned her the tourist; and Peregrina knew how to answer. She knew the direction, she knew the name, she knew the story of the house, she knew the things of here that the person from away did not know, and on answering, on being, for that foreigner, the one of here, the one who knows, the one who belongs, the one who explains the land to whoever arrives, Peregrina understood, with an emotion that filled her eyes, that she had become of here; that she was no longer the lost one they called a tourist, she was the one who guided the tourist; that she had passed, without noticing, from the outside to the inside, and that the sign that one belongs to a land is not to stop finding it strange, it is to be, one day, the person to whom another, more a foreigner, asks the way.

And she did for that new person what the Keeper had done for her: she did not treat her as from away, did not laugh at what she did not know, took her in, showed her the village, told her what the Keeper had told her, that half the village was of people who returned, that she was not alone, that if she stayed and gave herself, she would come to belong; and on doing for the new foreigner what they had done for her, Peregrina closed a circle, and understood that she belonged now in a still deeper way, because to belong truly to a land is to be able to receive in it whoever arrives, is to be, for what comes from away, part of the within that takes it in; and that she, the one who had never belonged anywhere, was now, in that street of Véspera, someone to whom another foreigner could hold on, a root already firm where another, still loose, could steady itself to begin its own.

And she looked at the street, her street, at the end of that day, and looked at it with the eyes of one who looks at her home, and it was the first time in her life she had looked at a place so, without the half-packed bag, without a foot outside, without the sense of being passing through; she looked at the street of Véspera as one who says it is here, it is this one, it is mine, not because she had been born in it, but because she had chosen it, and stayed, and worked it, and made in it memories and bonds and roots, and she understood that the home she had spent her whole life looking for in the lands of origin was there, in that street she had not inherited and had made her own, and that she had built it, brick by brick, gesture by gesture, day by day, from the within outward, and that she was, at last, after thirty-eight years, at home.
