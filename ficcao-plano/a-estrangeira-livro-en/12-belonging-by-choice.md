## 12. Belonging by choice

And the day came on which Peregrina understood, with her whole body and not only with her head, that she belonged; and it was without a feast, as the climaxes of Véspera are, an ordinary day on which she, without looking for it, felt herself, for the first time in her life, at home, and understood that home had not come from a land, it had come from a choice, hers, to stay and to give herself.

It was at a village feast, of the great ones, of those at which the whole land gathers, and which Peregrina, on arrival, years before, had lived as the supreme proof of her exclusion, the feast where they had told her you cannot understand, that is for those who are of the land. And at that feast, now, years later, Peregrina was in the midst, not from outside; she knew the people, had friends, laughed with them, knew the stories, and when there was talk of an old thing of the village she did not know, instead of the stab of before, someone told it to her, and she heard it, and came to know it, and the memory of the village, which had been barred to her as a land within into which she would never enter, opened to her, slowly, told by those who wanted her inside; and Peregrina understood that the land within, that of the memories, was not a closed club to one who arrived late, it was a house whose door opens to one who stays long enough for them to want to open it to her, and that hers had opened, not because she had been born there, but because she had stayed, and given herself, and those of here had come to want her inside.

And there was a moment, in the middle of the feast, that was the climax, and it was a phrase. One of the cousins, the same who years before had called her a tourist, introduced her to someone from away, and said, without thinking, naturally: this is my cousin Peregrina, she is of the village. She is of the village. The cousin who had called her a tourist said it, said it without noticing what he said, with the naturalness of a settled truth; and Peregrina, on hearing the she is of the village said by one who had treated her as from away, felt a thing that filled her chest and her eyes, felt that it had happened, at last, what she had waited her whole life for, that someone of here had said her of here, without her asking, without her pretending, as an obvious thing; and she understood that belonging, which she had sought as a feeling within herself, had been fulfilled from outside, in the mouths of the others, on the day those of here began to say her of here, and that that day had come, not by origin, but by her having stayed the time and given the giving that makes a land, slowly, begin to call ours one who plants herself in it.

And at that feast, hearing herself called of the village, Peregrina did the sum of the whole crossing, and understood what she had learned, that turned inside out the fate she was born with. She had learned that belonging is not inherited, it is chosen; that she had spent thirty-eight years waiting for a land to adopt her, and that none adopts, that one is of a land not by its taking us, but by our giving ourselves to it; that home is nowhere waiting, it is made, and it is made where one decides to stay; and that she, by deciding to stay in the village, by setting down the bag, by planting herself, by showing her two halves, by crossing the disappointments without fleeing, by making memories and bonds and roots, had built the home that her whole life had lacked, and that that home, by being built and not inherited, was more hers than any land of origin, because what is built is more ours than what falls to us.

And she understood the last truth, the one that answered the mother and the summer of the cousins: that she was not less than those who have a single land, she was more, because she had two origins and a chosen land, three belongings where the others have one; that her condition, which she had suffered as lack, was a wealth, that of one who is of more than one place; and that to be a foreigner, which had been her wound, had been also, she understood now, what had forced her to learn the thing those of a single land never learn, because they never need to: that belonging is a decision and a work, and not a chance of birth, and that one who learns to belong by choice can make a home anywhere, which is a freedom that those bound to a single land do not have. The cousins belonged to the village by not having known another; Peregrina belonged to it by having chosen it among those she knew, and the chosen belonging, she understood, is worth more, because it is free.

And she looked at the feast, the whole village gathered, her people, and looked at it as one who looks at her own, without a foot outside, without the bag, without the doubt; and she felt, for the first time in her life, the simple and enormous thing everyone feels early and that she had waited thirty-eight years to feel: she felt herself at home. Not in the home she had inherited, nor in the one the mother had painted, nor in any land of origin; in the home she had made, in that village she had chosen and that had chosen her back, among those people who were hers by decision and by time and by giving, and not by blood nor by map; and she understood that the foreigner from here, the one who was from nowhere, had become, at last, of somewhere, of the side she had chosen, and that to be of somewhere, she discovered, is not a thing one receives, it is the most one's own thing there is, because it is the only land that truly belongs to us, the one we decide is ours, and in which, therefore, we stay.
