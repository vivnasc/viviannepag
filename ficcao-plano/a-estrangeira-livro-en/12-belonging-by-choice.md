## 12. Belonging by choice

The festa of the end of the harvests came round, as festas come round, in the same square, with the same long tables laid in the street, the same roast, the same wine. It was a year on from the other one, the one at which they had told her you can't understand, that's for one who's of the land. And it was at that festa, the same one, that Peregrina understood, with her whole body and not only with her head, that she belonged.

She came early, as the other time, to be of the work. But this time she was not the tourist who strove to help; she was Peregrina, who laid the tables with Graça laughing at her side, who knew where the cloths were kept, who directed the places the way one directs who is of the house. She sat in the midst of the people, and ate, and laughed, and at a certain point, down the table, someone again began to speak of an old story of the village, another quarrel, another water, another boundary. And Peregrina, who did not know it, was about to fall silent, out of habit, was about to let it pass; but the old man telling it, noticing that she did not know it, turned to her and said:

"You don't know this one, miss? Now, it's worth hearing. It went like this..."

And he told it to her. It was the story of two families of the lower street who had fallen out over some water, thirty-odd years back, an irrigation channel that one diverted by night to water first, and the other who put a stone in it, and the stone that appeared one day in the bed of the one who had diverted the water, inside the house, with no one knowing how, and the two families who never spoke again, and the children who to this day do not marry one another on account of a water that no longer even runs there, for the channel dried up and went into a pipe. The old man told it, and the others filled it in, ah, it wasn't quite like that, the stone was the middle one, no, it was the other, and they laughed, and argued over the details of a quarrel of dead people as if it were yesterday's, because that is how villages keep their memory, alive, in argument. And Peregrina listened, and came to know it, and the inner village, the one of memories, the one that had been barred to her like a closed land she would never enter, opened to her slowly, told by those who wanted her inside it. They were not her memories, they never would be, she had not lived that water; but now she knew them, and one who knows the story of a land begins to be able to tell it to one who arrives after, which is the beginning of being of it. And she understood, hearing the story for which a year before they had sent her away, that the inner land was not a club closed to one who arrived late. It was a house whose door opens to one who stays long enough for them to want to open it to her. And hers had opened, not because she had been born there, but because she had stayed, and given herself, and those of here had come to want her within.

And there was, in the middle of the festa, the moment that was the climax of everything, and it was a sentence, said without weight, which is how the sentences that weigh most are said.

There appeared at the festa an acquaintance of Aurélio's, people from the big town, from away, and Aurélio, introducing the circle, came to Peregrina, put his hand on her shoulder, and said, with the naturalness of one who says an obvious thing:

"And this is my cousin Peregrina. She's of the village."

She's of the village. Aurélio said it, the same Aurélio who a year before had called her tourist, who had told her you can't understand. He said it without thinking, without noticing what he said, the way one says a settled truth. And Peregrina, on hearing the she's of the village said by the one who had treated her as of away, felt a thing that filled her breast and her eyes. It had happened, at last, what she had waited her whole life for: someone of here had said her of here, without her asking, without her feigning, like a thing that is not disputed. And she understood that belonging, which she had sought as a feeling inside herself, had been fulfilled from outside, in the mouth of others, on the day those of here began to say her of here; and that that day had come, not by origin, but by her having stayed the time and given the giving that makes a land, slowly, begin to call its own one who plants herself in it.

She said nothing. She smiled, shook the hand of Aurélio's acquaintance, and turned to Graça, who had heard, and Graça winked at her, and in that wink there went a whole festa.

Later in the night, Glória came to sit beside her, with a glass in her hand, and the two of them stayed watching the festa a while, silent. Glória was the cousin of the laugh on the threshing floor, thirty years ago, the one who had told her off you go to your own country, the first mouth to put the of away on her. And she said, without knowing what she said, or knowing it, for sometimes villages cure on the sly:

"Oh, cousin, I was foolish with you sometimes, you know? That tourist business. It was in jest, but maybe it wasn't funny at all." She took a sip, without looking at her. "I'm glad you stayed. Uncle Firmino's house was falling down, and now it has light at night. It does the street good."

And Peregrina, on hearing that from the cousin who had sent her away at eight, understood that the wound of the threshing floor was closing there, without ceremony, on a bench at a festa, with a glass of wine in her hand. She did not tell Glória she had hurt her her whole life. There was no need. She only said, it's good to be back, cousin, and it was true, and it was the first time the word back came out of her without hurting.

And later, when the music took hold, and the older ones began the songs of the land, the everlasting ones, the ones sung at the festas since before everyone there, the thing happened that sealed the night. They began a song, and Peregrina knew it. She knew it because grandmother Aurora had taught it to her, as a child, in the nights of the kitchen, that's not how it goes, my treasure, it goes like this, and she had repeated it without ever coming to sing it with anyone, because she had never been of those who sing, she had always been the one from away who listens. And that night, without thinking, low at first, then with voice, Peregrina sang. She sang the grandmother's song with the whole village, in the square, around the tables, and Glória beside her looked at her in astonishment, you know this one then?, and she knew it, she had known it for ever, from the mouth of a dead grandmother who had wanted to make one of the land of her and had not had the time; and in singing the grandmother's song with the whole village, thirty years later, Peregrina felt grandmother Aurora there, and felt that she was fulfilling, that night, the promise the grandmother had made her in her arms, we'll yet make one of the land of you. It had taken thirty years and the grandmother's death and a whole life of wanderings, but the song came out of her, at last, in her mouth, with the others, and no one found it strange, and it was the deepest proof of all, because to belong to a land is, in the end, to know its songs and to be able to sing them without going out of tune with the chorus.

Later, alone a moment beside the fountain, with the noise of the festa behind her, Peregrina reckoned up the whole crossing, and understood what she had learned, which turned inside out the fate she had been born with.

She had learned that belonging is not inherited, it is chosen. That she had spent thirty-eight years waiting for a land to adopt her, and that none adopts, that one is of a land not by its taking us, but by our giving ourselves to it. That home is nowhere waiting, it is made, and it is made where one decides to stay. And that she, in closing the bag, in undoing the box, in putting the machine at the window, in giving herself to Graça, in crossing Bonança without fleeing, in taking in Esperança, had built the home that had been lacking to her her whole life; and that that home, by being built and not inherited, was more hers than any land of origin, because what is built is more ours than what falls to us.

And she thought, beside the fountain, that night, of the girl of eight on the threshing floor, the one who had heard the cousins' laugh and learned that she was from nowhere. If she could, she would cross the thirty years and go to her, and say in her ear the thing it had taken her a whole life to learn: don't cry, for they're right and they're not. It's true you're from nowhere, yet. But that's not a fate, it's a task. You weren't born of a land, you're going to have to make one, and it'll cost you, and you'll go about lost for many years thinking the fault is the lands'; but one day you'll understand that the land you belong to wasn't waiting for you anywhere, it was waiting inside you, for you to make it. And you'll make it. And you'll sing the grandmother's song with the whole village, and you'll have a home with your name, and no one will send you off to your own country, because your country you yourself are going to choose, and chosen it costs more and is worth more. The girl on the threshing floor could not hear her, but Peregrina, the woman, heard herself, and it was like closing, at last, the door that had opened that August afternoon, thirty years ago, and had stayed open making a draught in every house she had slept in.

And she understood the last truth, the one that answered the mother and the summer of the cousins: that she was not less than those who have only one land. She was more. She had two origins and a chosen land, three belongings where the others have one. Her condition, which she had suffered as a lack, was a wealth, that of one who is of more than one place. And to be a foreigner, which had been her wound, had also been what obliged her to learn the thing that those of one land only never learn, because they never need to: that to belong is a decision and a work, and not an accident of birth. The cousins belonged to the village for not having known another. She belonged to it for having chosen it among the ones she knew. And chosen belonging is worth more, because it is free.

She went back to the festa, to her people, and looked at them without the foot out, without the bag, without the doubt. And she felt, for the first time in her life, the simple and enormous thing everyone feels early and which she had waited thirty-eight years to feel: she felt herself at home. Not in the home she had inherited, nor in the one the mother had painted, nor in any land of origin. In the home she had made, in that village she had chosen and that had chosen her back, among those people who were hers by decision and by time and by giving, and not by blood nor by map.

That night, at home, before going to bed, she called the mother, out of hours, and the mother took fright, what's happened, daughter, at this hour; and Peregrina said, nothing's happened, mother, it's good news, I just wanted to tell you a thing. Today Aurélio introduced me to someone and said I was of the village. And the mother fell silent, on the other side of the world, and then Peregrina heard her cry, but it was another crying, not the one of guilt, it was the one of relief, and the mother said, low, so you stayed, daughter. You really stayed. And Peregrina said, I stayed, mother. And the mother, crying and laughing, and the vine, is it really dried up? And Peregrina laughed too, it is, mother, but I planted something else in the yard, and that one's taking.

In the yard, the rose plant she had planted on the afternoon she closed the bag had taken, and had given, that spring, the first roses, and the patch beside it already gave her what to eat. It had taken a whole rose plant growing, from the dry stem to the first flowers, for Peregrina to make root; and it was right that it should be so, because to take root takes the time of a plant, it is not done in a day, it is done by waiting, with the patience of one who waters what does not yet bear and trusts that it will bear. She cut a rose, the first one, and put it in a glass on top of the sewing machine, at the window, in sight of the street, and did not send it anywhere, did not keep it for a journey, left it there to open and to wither in its own time, in a house where she would be to see it wither and to see the next one born.

When she hung up, she looked at the house, the photographs of the two lives on the walls, the crockery of the two lands, the machine at the window with a work begun, the blanket from the fair over there on the grandparents' sofa. And she looked up there, at the top of the wardrobe, where the bag was, put away, closed, the bag of one who stays and only travels sometimes. And she understood that the foreigner of here, the one who was from nowhere, had become, at last, of somewhere. Of the side she had chosen. And that to be of somewhere is not a thing one receives. It is the most one's own thing there is, the only land that truly belongs to us: the one we decide is ours, and in which, for that reason, we stay.

The following Sunday, Peregrina went up to the cemetery of the serra, to the father's grave, to take the rose, the first of the rose plant she had planted. It was a clear morning, with the whole serra in sight, that serra the father belonged to, in the photograph, leaning against the ox cart, at fourteen. She put the rose on the grave, and stayed there a while, in the sun, and spoke with the father, the way one speaks with the dead, low, more inward than outward. She told him she had stayed. That the house where he had been a boy now had a new roof and light at the windows. That the village already called her of the land. That she, the daughter he had given up to lose by taking her away, the one who had not known how to reap nor read the bells, had learned, at last, to be of that land, not like him, who was born of it, but in her own way, by choice, by staying. And she told him, finally, the thing that had made her climb the serra that morning: father, you came back to the land for good, and I brought you, and I stayed to keep you company. You're no longer alone on the serra. I'm here. And she went down the cemetery path with the village down below, the clay-coloured tiles, the fountain in the square, the smoke of the chimneys, and it was the first time she looked at that whole landscape and did not think where am I from, she thought another thing, simpler and deeper, that she had never thought of any place in the world: she thought I'm home.
