## 11. The two halves

What Peregrina still lacked, to take root for good, was to make peace with the thing she had hidden her whole life: her two halves. And it was Graça, without knowing it, who opened that door for her, sewing the two together in the kitchen, one winter afternoon.

Because Peregrina, even taking root, even sewing in plain sight, went on treating the half of over there as a thing to be measured. She showed it in the trade, for that was safe, it served the village; but in herself, in her mouth, in her ways, she went on wiping out the accent when she could, hushing the memories of the country where she was born, feigning herself more of here than she was. She had learned, since the summer of the cousins, that to show the of over there put her of away, and out of habit she was still hiding it, without noticing that in wiping out one half she stayed split, living with only one on show, and that the belonging she built so rested on a hiding, and what is hidden, one day, appears, and brings it all down.

That afternoon, sewing with Graça, she let slip a memory of over there. A beautiful thing, from when she was small, a street festa of the neighbourhood, with lights and music and some smell, and she told it laughing, and halfway through the story she fell silent, with the old reflex, ah, sorry, I'm talking to you about things of over there that interest no one.

Graça set down her sewing.

"Why do you stop?" she said. "Go on. I like to hear you talk of over there. It's a part of you. And it's a part I don't have, that gives me the world. You're always hiding that half, as if it were a fault. And it's not. It's a wealth. It's what you have to give that none of the rest of us has. Don't hide it. Show it."

And Peregrina, with the needle in her hand, on hearing Graça ask her for the half she hid, want the half she thought she had to hush in order to be accepted, understood a thing that changed everything. True acceptance was not to be accepted by hiding half. It was to be accepted by showing both. And she had found, at last, people who wanted her whole, Graça, her circle, the village that sought her out for her being who she was; and it was she herself, and not the village, who still had to accept her two halves.

And she began, from that afternoon on, to make peace with her double origin. Instead of hiding it, she showed it.

She gave a dinner, one Sunday, and invited the whole circle, Graça and Graça's man and two or three others, and instead of making only food of here, so as not to draw attention, as the woman of before would do, she made both kitchens. She put on the table the dishes of here, that she had learned from the aunt, and beside them the dishes of over there, those of childhood, those the mother made in the flat, with the strange smells that once had shamed her in the school lunchbox. And at first there were those who wrinkled their noses at a smell, and those who asked, with a village mistrust, is this to be eaten? And Peregrina, instead of hiding, explained, this is such and such, over there it's made with this, try it; and they tried, and one liked it, and another liked it more, and Graça scraped the pot and asked for the recipe, and Graça's man, who was of few words, said only, this is strange but it's good, and Peregrina understood that the strange but it's good was she herself, was her whole life put on a plate, and that there, at the table, for the first time, the of over there did not put her of away, it put her in the middle, giving her own to eat what no one else knew how to give them.

She told the memories of over there without shame, and those of here. She let the accent be what it was, of nowhere, without hiding it.

That accent, which had given her away her whole life on both sides, which she had spent thirty years trying to file down, now to seem more of over there, now to seem more of here, she stopped fighting it. One day, someone in the shop, from away, asked her, you're not from here, are you, you talk in a way; and instead of the old sting, instead of explaining and blushing, Peregrina laughed and said, I am and I'm not, it's a long story, but home's up at the top of the street, come round. And in saying it she understood that the accent had ceased to be a shame and become a signature, the mark of who she was, of one who had two lands in her mouth; that no one in the village talked like her, and that that, instead of putting her of away, made her recognisable, unique, the Peregrina who talks in that way, as there is the one who limps and the one who sings well. The accent was the map of her two lands drawn in her voice, and she stopped wanting to wipe it out, because to wipe it out was to wipe out half of herself, and she no longer wanted to have less, she wanted to have both, on show, even in the way of saying the words.

And she discovered, around that time, that the two lands she was made of served the village for a thing no one else there could give. Half the houses of the village had people away, children, brothers, grandchildren emigrated, and there were papers that came from there, letters, forms, official things in the tongue of over there, that the old people did not know how to read nor answer; and they began to knock at her door, first one, then another, oh, Peregrina, you who know the tongues and the ways of over there, help me make sense of this, help me write to my son. And Peregrina read them the letters, and wrote them the answers, and saw to their papers of over there, and sat with the old people at the table explaining to them the world of away where their children were, which she knew from within and they did not; and she understood that her condition of the border, of one who has a foot in each world, made of her the village's bridge to over there, the translator between those who stayed and those who left. What had made her of away her whole life, the having two worlds, was exactly what made her, now, indispensable to a village half made of people longing for those who are far. No one is more of-within a land of emigrants than the one who knows how to speak with those who have gone. She mixed the two tongues when it pleased her, and when a word of over there escaped her she no longer blushed, she explained it, and Graça learned it, and was amused by it, and sometimes used it too, and it was lovely to hear a word of over there in the mouth of a woman of the village who had never left it.

And in showing the two halves, she discovered that they did not put her of away. On the contrary: they were what she had most her own, most rich, most interesting. The village, far from rejecting her for them, esteemed her for them, because she brought, from her two lands, things the village lacked, and to give those things was her way of belonging, not by wiping out the difference, but by offering it.

And she understood, at last, the deepest thing, the one that answered the summer of the cousins and cured it: that she was not half of here and half of there, split, wanting. She was whole of both. Double. Rich. Her two halves, which she had lived as a division that left her with no land whole, were after all two belongings, and not half of either. She had, instead of less than those who have only one land, more: two heritages, two tongues, two worlds. And her condition of the border, of the between-place, that she had suffered her whole life as a curse, was, seen with other eyes, a gift, the gift of one who belongs to more than one place, of one who is a bridge between lands, of one who carries from one to the other what each one lacks.

And there was a small thing that gave her the measure of everything. She had a word of over there, an affectionate word the mother had said to her as a child, of the sort that have no sure translation, and which escaped her sometimes; and instead of swallowing it, as she had swallowed it her whole life, she began to let it out, and to explain it, that's how they say it over there when you're very fond of someone. And one day she heard Graça say it, to her little daughter, in her arms, without thinking, the word of over there in the mouth of a woman of the village who had never left it; and Graça's daughter, a few months later, said it too, to a doll. And Peregrina, on hearing her word of over there in the mouth of a child of here, felt a thing no document had given her: she understood that she was leaving something of hers in that land, that the land was becoming a little hers, that a generation from now there would be in the village a word that had come with her, from far, the way the water of the fountain comes from the serra, and that no one would remember any longer that it had been the foreigner who brought it. That is how a person enters a land for ever: not when the land accepts her, but when she leaves in it a thing that stays after her.

Graça, without knowing it, had given her new eyes. It was she who had said, that first afternoon, I envy you having two lands; and it had taken hearing, from the mouth of one who had only one, that two were wealth and not lack, for Peregrina to begin to believe it. And in ceasing to see the two halves as a lack and beginning to see them as a wealth, in ceasing to hide them and beginning to show them, she became, at last, whole. Not of here nor of there, but of both, and whole in both, which is a greater belonging, and not a lesser, than that of those who have only one.

The proof came when she went back over there, once, to see to papers that had been left unfinished, and to see the friends. She had imagined herself going back over there the way one goes back home, for over there had been her home her whole life. And the opposite happened, and it was a revelation to her. The known streets were strange. The friends were the same and were no longer, they talked of things she had ceased to keep up with, they had lives that went on without her, and they looked at her with that slightly distant affection of one who looks at someone who has left, so, how's Portugal, do you like it there? And Peregrina understood, in the everlasting café with the everlasting friends, that she was now, there, the one who had left. That over there too she was already the one from away, the one who went, and that the home of over there, which she had kept as a certainty, had become, without her noticing, a home of visit, the way the village had been her whole life.

And by the third day she felt a longing. But not for over there, being over there. She felt a longing for the village. For the machine at the window, for Graça, for her street, for the smell of the serra in the morning, for her house with the new roof. And when she caught the plane and then the bus, and got off in the village, at the end of the afternoon, and saw the serra open to the right and the fountain in the square, she felt a thing she had never felt arriving anywhere: she felt that she was arriving home. Not the home of paper, not the home of origin. The home she had made. And she understood, with her feet in the village, that the question of the thirty-eight years, where am I from, had at last an answer, and that the answer was neither over there nor the place where she was born: it was here, because it was here that she, on coming back from away, felt that she was coming back.

That was how Peregrina ceased to be the foreigner who belongs nowhere and became the one who belongs to two. And she understood that to take root, for her, was not to choose one land and amputate the other. It was to plant herself in one, in the one she had chosen, without cutting the other, carrying it with her, making of the village her home without ceasing to bear, within, the other land, the way one has in the house where one lives the photographs of the house where one was born, without one denying the other. The photographs of the friends of over there hung, now, beside those of the family of here, on the walls of the grandparents' house, and they no longer looked strange. They looked like the life of a woman who had two lands and had made of them one single home.

And that, she understood, looking at the walls one night, was the richest home of all: that of one who has two origins and joins them in one single life, because it holds more world. She had just built it, with the sewing at the window, with Graça in the kitchen, with the photographs on the walls, with the two halves at last on show. And she fitted in it, for the first time in her life, whole.
