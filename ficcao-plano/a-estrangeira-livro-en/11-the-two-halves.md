## 11. The two halves

What Peregrina still lacked, to take root for good, was to make peace with the thing she had hidden her whole life, which were her two halves, and it was the most intimate crossing of the book, because while she lived her double origin as a lack to be hidden, she would never be whole anywhere, not even in the home she had built.

Because Peregrina, even taking root in the village, went on treating the half of there as a thing to silence. She had learned, since the summer of the cousins, that to show the of-there set her apart, and so, on becoming of here, she tended to hide the of-there still more, to erase the accent, to silence the memories of the country where she was born, to pretend herself wholly of here; and she was, without noticing, doing to the village what she had done to the school of there, amputating a half to fit, and by amputating it she remained divided, went on living with only one half shown, and the belonging she so built was a belonging of half, fragile, because it rested on the hiding of the other, and what is hidden, one day, appears, and brings everything down.

And it was one of the new friends, of the village, who opened her eyes, and it was with tenderness. Peregrina, one day, let slip a memory of the country where she was born, a thing of there, beautiful, and at once fell silent, with the old reflex of hiding; and the friend said to her: why do you stop? Tell it. I like to hear you speak of there, of the things of there, it is a part of you, and it is a part I do not have, that gives me world. You are always hiding that half, as though it were a defect, and it is not, it is a wealth, it is what makes you different, it is what you have to give that none of the rest of us has. Do not hide it. Show it. And Peregrina, on hearing the friend ask her for the half she hid, want from her the half she thought she had to silence to be accepted, understood a thing that changed everything: that true acceptance was not to be accepted hiding a half, it was to be accepted showing both, and that she had found, at last, people who wanted her whole, and that it was to herself, and not to the village, that it still remained to accept her two halves.

And she began, then, to make peace with her double origin. Instead of hiding it, she showed it: she spoke of her two lands without shame, told the memories of there and those of here, cooked for her friends the dishes of the two kitchens, mixed the two languages when it suited her, let the accent be what it was, of nowhere, without hiding it; and on showing the two halves, she discovered that they did not set her apart, that on the contrary, they were what she had most her own, most rich, most interesting, and that the village, far from rejecting her for them, esteemed her for them, because she brought, from her two lands, things the village lacked, and that to give those things was her own way of belonging, not by erasing the difference, but by offering it.

And she understood the deepest thing of the book, the one that answered the summer of the cousins and healed it: that she was not half of here and half of there, divided, lacking; she was whole of both, double, rich; that her two halves, which she had lived as a division that left her with no whole land, were after all two belongings, and not half of none, and that she had, instead of less than those who have a single land, more, two inheritances, two languages, two worlds, and that her frontier condition, of the in-between place, which she had suffered her whole life as a curse, was, seen with other eyes, a gift, the gift of one who belongs to more than one place, of one who is a bridge between lands, of one who carries from one to the other what each does not have. And on seeing her two halves so, she ceased to feel them as a lack, and began to feel them as a wealth, and on ceasing to hide them and beginning to show them, she became, at last, whole, of neither here nor there, but of both, and whole in both, which was a greater belonging, and not a lesser one, than that of those who have only one.

And it was thus that Peregrina ceased to be the foreigner who belongs nowhere, and became the one who belongs to two, and made of her double origin not the wound that set her apart, but the bridge that put her within more than one world; and she understood that to take root, for her, was not to choose one land and amputate the other, it was to plant herself in one, in the one she had chosen, but without cutting the other, carrying it with her, making of the village her home without ceasing to carry, within, the other land, like one who has in the house where she lives the photographs of the house where she was born, without one denying the other; and that it was that, the home of one who has two origins and joins them in a single life, the richest home of all, because it holds more world, and that she, on making peace with her two halves, had just built it, and fitted in it, at last, whole, for the first time in her life.
