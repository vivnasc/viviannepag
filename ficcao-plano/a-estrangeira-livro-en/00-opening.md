## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Peregrina — the pilgrim — daughter of people who left, born between one land and another, and the name was a prophecy: the mother, who emigrated young and had her daughter already abroad, gave her the name of one who is always on the way, of one who wanders, without knowing that she was setting in her daughter's cradle the fate of being from nowhere, of having her feet in one land and her heart in another and her home in neither, which is what happens to the children of emigration, who are born foreigners on both sides, of here for having been born there, of there for being of here.

The register keeps those who arrive and those who leave, and there are names that appear twice, with two addresses, split down the middle: in the column of those who emigrated, in that of those who returned, and never whole in a single land. Peregrina was one of those names, she was in the register of Véspera as daughter of the village and as one from away at the same time, because abroad she had always been the Portuguese one, the one from here, of the accent and the strange smells, and here, when she returned for good, she was the one from away, the one who spoke differently, who had ways that were not of the land, whom no one quite knew where to place, and she least of all.

This is the fifth book of the shelf of the Long Table, the shelf of those who ask what their place is among the others, and within it, it is the book of the one who belongs nowhere. It tells the story of a woman who returned for good to the land of her parents and discovered that the land was not hers, that the cousins treated her as a tourist, that the mother, who never returned, did not understand those who return, and that she had two homes and none, two tongues and neither one whole, two banks and the river in between, belonging to neither bank. It is not a book against leaving nor against returning; it is a book about one who was born between two lands and was never of either, and about what it takes, at last, to have a home, when one discovers that home does not come from the land one is from, it comes from the land where one decides to stay.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Peregrina who belongs nowhere, whose heart is divided between two lands and whole in neither, who looks for the home where she was born and does not find it, who feels herself a foreigner here and there, let her know, from the first line, what it took Peregrina a whole life not to know: that belonging is not inherited, it is chosen; that the home one looks for abroad is built first within, and then, brick by brick, in the land where one decides to take root; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to go on looking for the land one belongs to, it is to choose one, and to plant oneself in it, and to discover, with astonishment, that one belongs not to where one is born, but to where one takes root, and that to take root is a decision, and not an origin.
