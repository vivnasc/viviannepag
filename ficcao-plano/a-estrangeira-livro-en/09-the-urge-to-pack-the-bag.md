## 9. The urge to pack the bag

The bag was on top of the wardrobe, high up, and Peregrina thought she had beaten it. But a defence of thirty years is not beaten in one autumn, and at the first wound to the depths the bag called her, and Peregrina went to fetch the step-ladder.

It came by way of a misunderstanding. A woman of the village, Bonança, had taken her a dress to make for a wedding, and Peregrina had made it in her own manner, with an idea of over there, and the woman, on trying it on, did not like it, found it odd, different from what she had expected, and said so clumsily, with that frankness of villages that takes no measure:

"Ah, this isn't what I wanted. This is fashion from your land, maybe, but here we don't wear it like this. Here we like things plainer. You forget sometimes that you're not over there."

You forget that you're not over there. It was only that, and Bonança was not even unkind, she was only a woman who did not like a dress. But the sentence struck the exact spot, the spot of the summer of the cousins, of the you talk like a foreigner, of the you're from away, and Peregrina, who had been sewing in plain sight, giving the village what she brought from over there, feeling herself at last useful and wanted for being who she was, felt suddenly, with all the old force, that she had been wrong. That the village would never accept her. That what she brought from over there, which she had begun to offer with pride, was still what put her of away. That she had been a fool to close the bag, to undo the box, to give herself.

She remade the dress as Bonança wanted, plain, with nothing of over there, and handed it over, and Bonança was pleased, but Peregrina was left with the wound open, and that night, alone, the everlasting urge came to her, the strongest of all: to pack the bag. She went to fetch the step-ladder from the pantry, set it beside the wardrobe, climbed up, and stayed there, on top of the step-ladder, with her hand on the bag, up high, in the dark, and her heart telling her take it down, pack the bags, go away before it hurts more, this isn't your land, it never will be, you saw clearly, Bonança told you to your face.

And she stayed there, on top of the step-ladder, with her hand on the bag, a long time. There was a moment when she almost took it down. She already had it by the handles, already felt it coming down, and her fingers, by themselves, were already feeling for the clasp, by heart, because the body knew flight by heart, knew how to fold quickly and shut clasps and go away before it hurt more; and for an instant, on top of the step-ladder, in the dark, Peregrina was once again wholly the woman of the packed bag, the woman of her whole life. And while she was there, with her hand on the clasp of the bag, there came into her head, without her calling it, the mother. The mother at the window of the flat, far away, looking at a street that was not hers, with the village of paper in her head, saying one day we'll go back home, and never going back, and dying, she would die, at the window of a place she had never called home, longing for a place that no longer existed. And Peregrina understood, with the bag in her hands, that if she took it down, if she left again, she was going to be the mother. She was going to spend her life making of the land she left a land of paper, always at the window of the next, always saying one day, and dying with no home at all, because home is not the one one keeps in one's head, it is the one one bears with one's body, with the disappointments and all. The mother had stayed on the side of the leaving in the Keeper's book, only on the leaving. And Peregrina, on top of the step-ladder, with the bag in her hands, was one gesture away from staying the same.

And it was in that moment, with the bag in her hands, that Peregrina stopped, and instead of taking it down, asked herself the question she had learned to ask, and asked it aloud, alone in the room in the dark, the way one speaks to the woman of thirty years before:

"Am I leaving because the village rejected me, or am I leaving because I was hurt and leaving is my way of not having to cross the hurt?"

And she stayed with the bag in her hands letting the question work. Bonança had not rejected her. Bonança had not liked a dress. It was a disappointment, of the sort there are in any land, even in our own, even among our own. And Peregrina was doing the everlasting thing, reading in the first disappointment the confirmation that she should leave, generalising from a dress to the whole village, from a woman to all people, exactly as she had generalised, at eight, from a cousin's laugh to all lands. The disappointment was not a sign. It was only life. And those who have a home do not pack the bag at the first disappointment. They cross it. They stay in spite of it. It is by staying in spite of the disappointments that they have a home.

And she understood more, with the bag in her hands, and it was what made her put it back up high: that if she left now, at the first wound, she was going to do what she had done her whole life. Because the truth, which that night she looked at head-on for the first time, is that the village was not the first bag. She had changed cities, over there, three or four times, always at the first thing that went wrong, a job that soured, some neighbours, a love; she had had a man, some years, a good man, and at the first time the relationship asked more than she knew how to give, she had done what she knew how to do, she had packed the bag, and told herself that he did not understand her, that he was not the right person, that it was not the right place, always the fault of the outside, never hers. She had always seen herself as the one who searches, the pilgrim, the one who has not yet found her place; and she understood, with the bag in her hands, on top of the step-ladder, that she had never been the one who searches, she had always been the one who flees, and that she had put the pretty name of search on a whole life of flights. If she left now, she was going to begin again of away somewhere else, and somewhere else, at the first Bonança, she would leave again, and would go about so her whole life, from land to land, from man to man, from half-packed bag, always fleeing the first wound, always beginning again of away, and would die with no home and no one, not for any land rejecting her, but for never staying the time it takes for a land, or a person, to become hers. The home she so sought was not a land without disappointments, for that one does not exist. It was a land where she decided to stay even with the disappointments, to cross them instead of fleeing them, because it is the crossing of the disappointments of a land, and not their absence, that makes of it our home.

She put the bag back up high. She came down from the step-ladder, put it away, and went to bed, and it was hard for her, and it cost her not to flee, because to flee was the known rest. But she stayed.

The next day, Graça noticed. She came to the house to drop off a piece of sewing and found Peregrina silent, closed, with the machine stopped at the window and the clothes unsewn, and she knew at once, for friends know.

"You've got the face of someone about to flee," said Graça, sitting down without being asked, which was what Peregrina liked most in her. "I know that face. I saw it once, when you arrived. What was it?"

"Nothing. Bonança didn't like a dress."

"And you, on account of a dress of Bonança's, were going to fetch the bag from the wardrobe again." It was not a question. Graça looked at her, serious for once. "Listen, Peregrina, hear me. Bonança is like that with everyone. She didn't like a dress of mine two years ago and I didn't leave the land because of it, I went and got another fabric. You can't go off at the first person who says a crooked thing to you, or you'll spend your whole life going off. There's foolish people here too, like everywhere. You stay with the other ones. You stay with me." And, lower, pressing her arm: "Don't leave me, now that I'm fond of you."

And Peregrina, on hearing don't leave me now that I'm fond of you, felt a thing the bag had never given her in thirty years: she felt that there was, in that land, someone who would miss her if she left. And one who has, in a place, someone who would miss her, is no longer only passing through that place. Graça was a root, and roots are not carried in a bag.

And that afternoon, at the kitchen table, Peregrina told Graça a thing she had never told anyone: she told her of the other times. Of the cities she had left, of the jobs, of the good man she had given up at the first difficulty, of a whole life of packing the bag at the first wound and calling it a search. She told it with shame, expecting perhaps that Graça would think her weak, a runaway, of no use. And Graça heard her, and at the end did not judge her, she said only, with that wisdom of villages that has been to no school: ah, Peregrina, everyone flees from something. Some flee with the bag, others stay still and flee inward, which is worse. You at least are trying to stay now. That's already more than a lot of people who never left here and who go about fleeing their whole lives without taking a step. And Peregrina, on being heard without being judged, on putting her deepest shame on the table and the friend not getting up from the table, felt a thing that bound her to the village more than any roof: she felt herself known. Because a land where they know the worst of us and stay all the same is a land where one can, at last, rest from fleeing.

And the next day she did a thing the woman of before would never do: instead of fleeing Bonança, she went to her. She found her in the square, and instead of avoiding her, she drew near, and spoke, and the two of them said what they had to say, and Bonança, seeing that Peregrina had not taken it ill, softened, and said that after all the first dress was not even ugly, it was only different, and that perhaps for the next she would risk a thing more in the manner of over there, that it did no harm to try; and they were well, as those are who fall out and make up, who sometimes even draw closer than before.

And that week she did a thing with the bag that was her way of beating it for good, at least for a while. She took it down from up high, opened it, and instead of leaving it empty and ready, ready as it had been her whole life, she filled it. She filled it with winter blankets, the ones used only a few months and that need a place to keep the rest of the year, and closed it, and put it back up high, now heavy, now full, now a chest and no longer a bag. A bag full of blankets is not picked up in a hurry to flee; it has to be emptied first, and one who has to empty the bag before fleeing no longer flees at the first wound, flees slowly, or does not flee. Peregrina turned the bag of flight into a coffer of one who stays, and it was a small gesture and it was enormous, because it was to take from flight its instrument, it was to make the leaving hard on purpose, it was to put weight where there had always been hurry.

And she understood, in making up the misunderstanding instead of fleeing it, that she had just done a thing she had never done in thirty-eight years: cross the first disappointment of a land without leaving. And that that, that staying in spite of the pain, was the deepest root of all, the one that grows only to those who do not flee. Each disappointment crossed without packing the bag, from then on, instead of putting her of away, would put her more within. Because to belong, she discovered that night, is not to have no wounds in a land. It is to have wounds there and stay, and heal them there. And it is of the wounds healed in a land, as much as of the joys, that belonging to it is made.
