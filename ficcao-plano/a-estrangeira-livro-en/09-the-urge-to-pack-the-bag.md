## 9. The urge to pack the bag

But one does not undo in a season a whole life of a half-packed bag, and Peregrina had her relapse, and it came in the form the relapses of those who do not belong always come in: in the form of a sudden urge to leave, to pack the bag, to go, which dressed itself as a good reason and which was, deep down, the old fear fleeing the taking-root.

It came by a disappointment, small, of the kind that before would have thrown her straight into departure. There was a misunderstanding with people of the village, one of those things there are between neighbours, a misunderstanding, a crooked word, nothing grave, but enough for Peregrina to feel, again, with all the old force, the you are from away, the you are not of here, the you will never be one of ours; and the misunderstanding, which for one of here would be only a misunderstanding, for Peregrina was more, was the confirmation of all she feared, that she had been wrong to stay, that the village would never accept her, that it was not worth taking root in a land that at the first thing put her outside; and there came to her, strong, irresistible, the urge of always: to pack the bag, to go, to look elsewhere, to go back there, or to go to a new land, any, where she would begin again, where the first disappointment would not hurt so much because she would not have set down the bag.

And she almost did it. She even thought of where she would go, of doing sums, of imagining the departure, and there was, in the idea of leaving, an enormous relief, the relief of one who returns to the known, because to leave was what Peregrina knew how to do, it was her safe zone, it was easier to leave at the first pain than to stay and cross it; and the half-packed bag, which she thought she had set down, was after all there, to hand, waiting, because a defence of thirty years is not set down in a season, and at the first wound the hand went straight to the bag, to the habit, to the flight.

But she had now, against the urge to leave, the truth of the Keeper and the ground she had planted, and instead of packing the bag, Peregrina stopped, and asked herself the new question: am I leaving because the village rejected me, or am I leaving because I was hurt and leaving is my way of not having to cross the hurt? Is this disappointment the proof that I do not belong, or is it only a disappointment, of the kind there are in any land, even our own, even among our own, and that those who belong cross without packing the bag because they know that belonging is not never having disappointments, it is staying in spite of them? And she understood, with the question, that she was doing the of-always: confusing a pain with a sign, reading in the first disappointment the confirmation that she should leave, when the disappointment was the sign of nothing, it was only life, which has disappointments everywhere, and that those who have a home do not pack the bag at the first, they cross, and stay, and it is by staying in spite of the disappointments that they have a home.

And she understood more, and it was what made her stay: that if she left now, at the first pain, she would do what she had done her whole life, she would begin again from away somewhere else, and somewhere else, at the first disappointment, she would leave again, and would go about so her whole life, from land to land, with the bag half-packed, always fleeing the first wound, always beginning again from away, and would die without a home, not because any land rejected her, but because she never stayed long enough for a land to become hers; and that the home she so sought was not a land without disappointments, for that does not exist, it was a land where she decided to stay even with the disappointments, to cross them instead of fleeing them, because it is to cross the disappointments of a land, and not their absence, that makes of that land our home.

And she stayed. She bore the hurt of the misunderstanding without packing the bag, crossed it, spoke, mended the misunderstanding as those who stay mend it, and on the far side of the hurt the village had not rejected her, it had had, with her, a misunderstanding of neighbours, of the kind that are resolved and that, resolved, even draw near; and Peregrina understood, on resolving instead of fleeing, that she had just done a thing she had never done, had just crossed the first disappointment of a land without leaving, and that this, that staying in spite of the pain, was the deepest root of all, the one that only grows for one who does not flee, and that each disappointment crossed without packing the bag, from there on, instead of putting her outside, put her more within, because to belong, she discovered at last, is not to have no wounds in a land, it is to have wounds there and to stay, and to heal them there, and it is of the healed wounds in a land, as much as of the joys, that belonging to it is made.
