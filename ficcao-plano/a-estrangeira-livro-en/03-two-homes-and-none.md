## 3. Two homes and none

Peregrina had, at thirty-eight, two homes and none, and it took returning to understand, with all clarity, the thing she had always known underneath: that to have two lands is not to have double, it is to have neither whole, because a person does not divide without staying divided.

She had the home of there, the country where she was born, where she had spent her life, where she had her friends, her habits, the everyday language, the streets she knew by heart, the flavours of childhood, which were of there and not of here, however much her mother cooked Portuguese; and she had the home of here, her parents' village, the language of home, the smells of the holidays, the grandparents now dead, the family, the roots her parents had told her of, the land she came from without being of there. And she thought, before returning, that having two homes she was richer than one who has a single one, that she belonged to two worlds, that she had double; and she discovered, on returning, the opposite truth: that having two homes she had none, that to belong to two worlds was to belong to neither whole, that the double was after all half, half of here and half of there and the whole nowhere.

Because the home of there, now that she had returned, she missed. She found herself, in the village, longing for the things of there, the friends of there, the streets of there, the way of living of there, which after all, she discovered, were also hers, had also made her, and which, being here, she missed as one misses a home. And the home of here, now that she was in it, was not the one she had imagined, it was strange, it was from away, it did not receive her as its own. And Peregrina was left in the middle, longing for there while she was here, and with the memory of having longed for here while she had been there, understanding, at last, the curse of the in-between place: that one who has two homes always longs for the other, is always in the wrong one, always misses the one she is not in, and never, in any home, is wholly at home, because half of her always stays in the other.

And there was, in this condition, a particular loneliness, hard to explain to those who had only one home. The friends of there did not understand why she had returned, they told her but you were of there, you were born here, this is your country; and the family of here did not understand that she should miss there, they told her but now you are home, you are in your own land, what do you lack; and neither the ones nor the others understood, because the ones and the others had a single home, and one who has a single home does not understand one who has two and none, does not understand that one can miss a land while in the other that is also ours, does not understand the division, the between, the divided, and Peregrina felt herself alone in her condition, misunderstood on both sides, a foreigner not only of the lands, but of the people, who did not grasp what it was to be split down the middle.

And she understood, in those first times in the village, that the division was not only geographical, it was of everything. It was of the language: she thought half in one, half in the other, dreamed now in one now in the other, and had no language that was only hers, whole, truly mother, because the mother one, that of home, she spoke with the accent of there, and that of there, which she spoke without accent, was not that of home. It was of the references: the songs of childhood were of there, the family stories were of here, and there was no one with whom she shared both, because those of there did not know those of here and those of here did not know those of there. It was of the very body, of the gestures, of the manners at table, of the way of greeting, which were half of here half of there, and which in each land betrayed the other. Peregrina was, in everything, a mixture, and the mixture, which could be wealth, was lived as lack, because in each land the mixture was what set her apart, was the wrong half, the one that did not belong.

And there was a moment, in those days, in which Peregrina understood the depth of the problem. It was in a village shop, buying a thing, and she did not know the name of a common object in the Portuguese of Portugal, she knew it in the language of there and she knew the Portuguese her mother used, which was an old Portuguese, of the emigrant, stopped in the time of departure; and the woman of the shop looked at her, and understood that she was not of here, and Peregrina felt the shame of not knowing how to name, in her own land, a common thing, and understood that the language of her parents, which she thought was hers, was a language of thirty years back, frozen in emigration, no longer the one spoken in the village, and that she spoke, after all, a third language, neither that of there nor that of here, the language of those who left, stopped, belonging to neither of the two lands, and which was, itself, the proof that Peregrina was from nowhere, not even on her lips.

And she went to bed, that night, with the measure of the problem taken: she did not have two homes, she was not rich in belongings, she was poor of them, she had two lands and none, two tongues and neither whole, and she was alone in a condition that neither those of there nor those of here understood. And she understood that if she wanted, at last, to have a home, to have a land, to belong, it would not be by finding the right land, because neither of the two was, and there was no third; it would have to be in another way, a way she did not yet know, but that she began to guess was not in the land, but in her, in the decision to make one of the lands hers, not because it was, but because Peregrina chose it, and clung to it, and planted herself in it, by dint of wanting, until she made root where she had none by birth.
