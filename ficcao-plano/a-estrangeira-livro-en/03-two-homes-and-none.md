## 3. Two homes and none

The thing broke open in a shop, on account of a word Peregrina did not know.

She went to Quitéria's grocery to buy what the house lacked, still in the first weeks, when she was trying to make her parents' house look like a house of her own. Quitéria was at the counter, in her apron, weighing beans for a customer, and Peregrina waited her turn, and when her turn came she asked for the things on the list, the oil, the soap, and then pointed at a thing behind the counter, a common utensil, a kitchen one, of the sort there is in every house, and found herself without the word. She knew it in the tongue of over there. She knew the name her mother used, an old name, of another time. But the name of here, the name of now, the one Quitéria would say, that one she did not have.

"That," she said, pointing. "That thing there."

Quitéria looked where she was pointing, and then at her, with that look villages have that measures a person in a second and files her in a drawer.

"You mean this?" And she said the word, the simple word, that everyone in the village had known since they were small.

"That one," said Peregrina, her face heating. "Sorry. Sometimes I get the names muddled."

"You're the one who came back, aren't you? Dores's daughter." Quitéria wrapped the thing in brown paper, without malice, with the direct curiosity of one who has no time for roundabout ways. "Aye. There's names here that are learned at home, when you're small, at the table. One who wasn't at the table doesn't have them. It's no fault. It's only that you weren't there."

One who wasn't at the table doesn't have them. Peregrina left the shop with the thing wrapped in brown paper and with Quitéria's sentence hurting her more than the sentence deserved, because Quitéria had not been unkind, she had been exact, and exact things hurt more than unkind ones. It was true. She lacked the tongue of here, the tongue of home, the one learned at the table, when small; and her mother, who should have given it to her, had given her a frozen tongue, stopped on the day she emigrated, a Portuguese of thirty-odd years ago, with words no one in the village used any more and without the words that had been born since. Peregrina spoke, she understood that afternoon, a third tongue. Not the one of over there, which she spoke without an accent but which was not the tongue of home. Not the one of here, which was the tongue of home but which she did not speak well. A third, the tongue of those who left, frozen, which belonged to neither of the two lands and which was, itself, the proof that Peregrina was from nowhere, not even in her mouth.

She wanted, around those days, to make a cozido in the manner of the land, to feel of here through her belly, since through her mouth she could not. She bought what memory told her, and set to the pot, and made the cozido the way her mother made it, far away, on Sundays. And Graça, who was not yet a friend, was only a neighbour who passed by and stayed to taste, tasted and said, frankly, ah, this is a cozido, it is, but it's the old way, it's how my grandmother made it, nobody makes it like that here any more. Peregrina understood, with the spoon in her hand, that she had cooked a third cooking, the way she spoke a third tongue: neither the one of over there, nor the one of here-now, but the one of here thirty years ago, the one her mother had carried in her luggage and frozen in the flat, and which no longer existed in the village save in the mouths of the very old. Even in her eating she was from away, or worse, she was from a time that no longer existed.

Even the bells were a code she did not have. Over there were no bells, or there were and they said nothing; in the village, the church bell marked the hours, and the masses, and the dead, and each peal meant a thing, and everyone knew how to read the peals from small. One morning, the bell rang in another way, slow, spaced out, and Peregrina, at the window, did not know what it was; and she saw the women come out to their doors, and cross themselves, and say a name among themselves, ah, it was so-and-so, poor man, it was to be expected; and she understood that the bell had announced a death, and that the whole village had known, in the peal, who had died and what to feel, and she had stood at the window understanding nothing, like a foreigner in a land whose tongue she does not speak. Those of here read the air, the bells, the silences, the looks, all an alphabet learned at the breast and which she had never learned. And she understood that to be of a land is not only to speak the tongue of mouths, it is to speak the tongue of things, of bells, of seasons, of customs, and that of that tongue, the deep one, she was illiterate, and that one illiterate in a land is a foreigner even if her father and mother were born there.

She had thought, before coming back, that to have two homes was to be richer than one who has one. That she belonged to two worlds, that she had double. And she discovered, now, the truth the other way round: that having two homes she had neither whole. That the double was after all the half, half of here and half of there, and the whole nowhere at all.

Because the home of over there, now that she had come back, she missed. She found herself, in the village, longing for the things of over there, and the longing took her by surprise, in stupid places. One afternoon, at the window of her parents' house, she found herself longing for a street of over there, a street with no charm at all, with a café and a bus stop, where she had never been especially happy, and she had to sit down, so deep did the longing come. Longing for the friends of over there, for the way of talking of over there, for the flavours of childhood, which were of over there and not of here, however much the mother had cooked Portuguese. And the home of here, now that she was in it, did not receive her. It was strange. It was from away.

The mornings were the worst. She would wake, in the early days, and there was a second, before opening her eyes, in which she did not know where she was. The body, which had slept thirty-eight years in a room of a city of away, with the noises of away, the traffic, the neighbours, the horn at six, woke waiting for those noises and found the silence of the serra, or a cockerel, or a bell, and for a second did not know what land it was in, nor what life, and had to remember, every morning, I'm in the village, I came back, I live here now. And that second of not knowing, repeated every morning, was the division made flesh: not even the body, which is the oldest and most stubborn part of us, knew yet which land it was of. There, she dreamed of the serra. Here, she woke waiting for the city. She was always behindhand with herself, with her body living in one land and her memory in another, and that difference of hours between the body and the life was the most physical thing emigration had left her, more than the accent, more than the names she lacked: that of waking, every morning, not knowing in which house.

She telephoned, around those days, a friend of over there, the best she had left behind, looking for a known voice. She told her how it was, the village, the house, the strangeness. And the friend, on the other end, with all the affection in the world, did not understand her.

"But you always wanted to go there," said the friend. "You were always on about your land, your father's village. You're finally in your own land, and you're complaining?"

"It's not quite my land," said Peregrina.

"How is it not? You were born here, that's true, but you're Portuguese, you always said so. You'll get used to it. A year from now you won't even remember us."

Peregrina hung up and stayed with the telephone in her hand, in the silent house, and understood a new loneliness, deeper than that of not belonging: that of not being understood. The friend of over there did not understand that she should long for over there while being here, because the friend of over there had only one home. And the family of here did not understand that she should long for over there while being here, in their land, because the family of here also had only one home.

She proved it one Sunday, at aunt Remédios's table. She let slip that she longed for a thing of over there, a food, a place, and a strange silence fell over the table, and the aunt, hurt without meaning to show it, said, ah, daughter, but you're home now, in your own land, what is it you miss of all that? And Aurélio, joking but not only, so you don't like it here? Did you come against your will? And Peregrina saw she had trodden on a thing, that to say longing for over there, at the table of those of here, sounded of ingratitude, as if their land, which had received her, were not enough for her; and she hushed the longing for over there at the table of those of here, as she had hushed her whole life the things of here in the midst of those of over there. There was no table, in any land, where she could lay both longings at once. One who has only one home does not understand one who has two and none. Does not understand that one can be in the land one wanted and long for the one one left. Does not understand the division, the between, the divided. And Peregrina, in the midst of the two, was misunderstood on both sides, a foreigner not only of the lands, but of the people, who had nowhere to fit a woman split down the middle.

There was a night, around then, when the division appeared to her whole. She could not sleep, and got up, and set to tidying the house, and went to take, at last, out of the boxes, the few things she had brought from over there, hers, the only ones that were hers in that house of grandparents' furniture. And in tidying them she became aware of a thing: that she did not know where to put them. The photographs of over there, of the friends of over there, looked strange on the walls of the house of here, beside the photographs of the family she barely knew. The crockery of over there, beside the aunt's crockery, looked of another world. The books, half in one tongue, half in the other. And Peregrina, standing in the middle of the room, with her things in her hands, not knowing where to put them in the house that was meant to be hers, understood that the house was a photograph of her life: two halves that did not match, joined by force, and neither at home.

She put her things, in the end, into one box, and the box in a corner, without spreading them through the house. She told herself it was only for now, until she knew. But the truth, which only much later she learned to look at, was another: she did not spread her things through the house for the same reason she did not unpack the bag. To spread the things was to stay. It was to commit herself to that house, to that land. And Peregrina did not commit herself, because to commit herself was to risk giving herself to a place and being sent away, as Glória had sent her, off you go to your own country; and as long as she had her things in the box and the bag half-packed, she was safe, she could leave at any hour, on the day the land hurt her in earnest.

She went to bed with the measure of the problem taken, and it was a bitter measure: she did not have two homes, she was poor of them. She had two lands and none, two tongues and neither whole, and she was alone in a condition that neither those of over there nor those of here understood. And there, in the bed of the borrowed house, with the box of her things in the corner and the bag at the foot, Peregrina began to suspect, still without knowing how to say it, that the home she so sought was in no land waiting to be found. That perhaps there was no land she belonged to by origin, not here nor there nor anywhere at all. And that, if she wanted a home, she was going to have to make it, and not find it, and to make it not in the land she was from, which was no land's, but in the land where she decided to stay, planting herself in it by force of wanting, which is the only root that grows to one who was born with none.
