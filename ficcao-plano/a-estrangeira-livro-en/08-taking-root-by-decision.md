## 8. Taking root by decision

To take root, Peregrina discovered, is not a feeling that arrives. It is a work that is done, day by day, with the hands, and she had good hands, and it was by the hands that she began.

She brought a trade from over there. She sewed. Not the sewing of mending, the other, the sewing of making, of cutting and giving shape, that she had learned in an atelier far away, where they made things no one in the village made, with certain finishes, certain ideas, a way of joining fabrics that was of over there. And in the first months she had hidden that, as she hid everything in her that was of away, because the of away put her of away. But, decided to stay, she did the opposite: she put the machine at the window, in the grandparents' house, and began to sew in plain sight, and when a neighbour asked her to take in a dress, she did it, and did it well, with the finishes of over there, and the neighbour showed the next, and the next the next.

And a thing happened that Peregrina had not expected: what in her was of away, what put her of away, was also, seen the other way round, a wealth she brought to the village and that the village did not have. The women began to seek her out not in spite of her being of away, but because she was of away, because she sewed in a way the village did not have, with ideas of over there, with the world in them. Quitéria, of the grocery, the same of the one who wasn't at the table doesn't have the names, took her a coat to mend and came away with the coat turned into a thing better than she had bought, and told it in the shop, and from then on the machine at Peregrina's window did not stop.

And there came to her, around that time, a girl of the village, of the younger ones, who had a hand for sewing and nothing to do in the place, and asked her to teach her, to show her what she brought from over there, the shapes, the finishes. And Peregrina taught her. She sat her at her side at the window, and showed her what she had learned in the atelier of away, and the girl learned, and at the end of a few months she was already sewing things no one in the village had sewn before; and Peregrina understood, watching the girl sew in the manner of over there, that she was leaving in the village a thing that would stay after her, a knowing of away planted in a girl of here, who would pass it to another, and she to another, until no one remembered any longer that it had come with the foreigner. That was how a person enters a land for ever: not only by doing, but by teaching, by leaving in the land a knowing the land did not have and that comes to be its own. The girl was, without knowing it, the first root Peregrina cast into the future of the village.

And the sewing gave her, besides bread, a thing she had not expected: it gave her the street. Because a seamstress measures people, and to measure a person is an intimacy, and while she measured and tacked, the women of the village talked, talked the way one talks to one who has one's arms open taking measurements, and told her things, the husband's illness, the daughter who did not write, the sorrow, the small joy; and Peregrina's house, with the machine at the window, came to be a place where one stopped, where one unburdened oneself, where one knew the things of the street. Without noticing it, Peregrina came to know the village from within, the pains of each house, the stories of now, not those of thirty years ago, which those she still did not have, but those of now, the ones being made, and at those she was present, and into those she entered, because a seamstress who listens is a confidante, and a confidante is of within. The machine at the window, which she had put there to sew in plain sight and overcome her shame, had become, without her meaning it, the confessional of the street, and the foreigner, the confidante of the village.

And Peregrina understood, sewing at the window, a thing that turned the summer of the cousins inside out: that her condition of the border, of one who has two lands, that she had suffered her whole life as a lack, could be lived as a gift. One who has two lands has two riches to give. The village, which had treated her as a tourist for her not knowing the things of here, began to esteem her for her knowing the things of over there, which it lacked. And to give the village what she brought from away, instead of hiding it, was her own way of belonging, not by wiping out the difference, but by offering it.

The ties came after, and they came by way of Graça.

Graça was a woman of the village, of Peregrina's age, of easy laughter and no holding of her tongue, who took her some trousers to take in and stayed for the conversation, and came back the next day with no trousers at all to take in, only for the conversation, and by the third day the two of them were already laughing in the kitchen as if they had been laughing for twenty years.

And there was a day, at the end of a few months, when the thing turned the other way round, and it was then that Peregrina understood she had a friend in earnest. Graça appeared at her door at night, with red eyes, on account of a sorrow of hers, a thing of the marriage, of the man, that no one in the village could know because in the village everything is known; and she came to Peregrina precisely because Peregrina was of away, because to one of away one can tell what one does not tell to those of here, for those of here talk. And Peregrina listened to her the whole night, in the kitchen, and made her a tea, and did not judge her, and kept the secret; and seeing Graça confide a sorrow to her, weep in front of her, she understood a new thing: that it was no longer only she who needed to be accepted, there was now someone who needed her, who sought her out, who confided to her what hurts. And one who, in a land, is sought out in the hours when one weeps is no longer in that land of away. The condition of foreigner, which had hurt her, revealed itself there to be a service: it was to her, the one from away, the one outside the gossip, that Graça could open her heart without fear. What put her of away, put to service, put her, after all, on the side of within. Graça did not see her as a tourist. She saw her as Peregrina, with her story, her two lands, her accent of nowhere, and liked her so, whole, and had a curiosity about the life of over there, and asked, tell me what the city was like, tell me what school is like over there, and Peregrina told, and Graça listened with open eyes, and said ah, what luck, to know two worlds.

She was the first person, in the village, who wanted her whole. And Peregrina, who had lived on the defensive, at the door, decided to stay, let her in. She accepted the invitations instead of fearing them. She went to the festas and stayed to the end. She came to know, through Graça, other people, and went making, slowly, a circle of people who were hers.

And there was a thing that put her within more than anything, and it was a mourning. A woman of the village died, suddenly, and the daughter, distraught, had nowhere to dress the mother fittingly for the grave, for the good clothes no longer fit the sick body. Someone remembered Peregrina. And Peregrina, at night, with Graça keeping her company, mended the dead woman's clothes, let them out, sewed, left her decent, and took nothing for it, for that is not charged. At the burial, the dead woman's daughter came to her, with red eyes, and pressed her hand, and said only, my mother went to the earth as she should because of you, and I'll never forget it. And Peregrina understood, in that pressing of the hand, that she had entered the village by a door no accent shuts: the door of serving one's own in the hours when they hurt most. One who sews the shroud of another's mother becomes, for that house, of within. The festas give us acquaintances; it is the mournings that give us a place.

And if the mourning put her within, it was a wedding that put her at the centre. A girl of the village married, of Graça's circle, and came to Peregrina, shy, to ask if she would make her dress, that she had seen what she sewed and that she did not want one from a shop, she wanted one of Peregrina's. And Peregrina made it for her, with the ideas of over there and her hands trembling with pleasure, and the girl married in her dress, and on coming out of the church, amid the rockets and the rice, with the whole village clapping, it was a little of Peregrina that went there in front, in the stitches of the dress, in the sight of everyone. And by the mourning and the wedding Peregrina understood that she had entered the two doors by which a village lets in one who comes from away: that of dressing its dead and that of dressing its brides, which is to say being present when it hurts and when it is celebrated, which is all that is needed to be of a house.

And it was thus, festa by festa and mourning by mourning, that Peregrina came to have, for the first time in the village, people who knew her whole and who wanted her so; and she felt born in her the deepest root of all, that of the ties, which no land gives by origin and which is the one that makes of a place our home.

There was an afternoon, in the kitchen, with Graça, that Peregrina kept. They were sewing, the two of them, Graça learning to make a hem in the manner of over there that Peregrina was teaching her, and Graça, suddenly, with no relevance to anything, said:

"You know what? I've never left here. I was born in this village, I'll die in this village, and sometimes I think I know nothing of the world. And you know two. I envy you that, Peregrina. Truly. You think it's a misfortune, to have two lands. I think it's the richest thing there is."

And Peregrina, with the needle in her hand, stayed thinking about that, because it was exactly the opposite of what she had always felt. To her, to have two lands had been to have half of each and neither whole. And Graça, who had only one, looked at her two with envy, and called them wealth. And perhaps Graça was right, and Peregrina did not yet know how to see it, but she was beginning.

It did not all go straight. There were days when the old feeling came back, when a tourist from a cousin, a word she lacked, an old story she did not know, threw her again into the of away, into the doubt, into the urge to fetch the bag from the top of the wardrobe. But they were fewer and fewer, those days, because beneath them the new ground was growing, that of the roots she planted, the house of her own, the machine at the window, Graça, the circle of people. And that ground, made by her, held her better than any land of origin, because it was a chosen ground, worked, won, and what is won holds more than what is inherited.

And she understood, taking root so, slowly, by work and by decision, the truth the summer of the cousins had hidden from her: that to belong was not to hide the half that was not of the land, it was to show both and find someone who wanted them. Home was not the place where one fitted by hiding half of oneself. It was the place where one could be whole. And she, in giving herself to the village with her two halves on show, in sewing in the manner of over there instead of hiding it, in telling Graça the life of over there instead of hushing it, was doing the opposite of what she had done her whole life. She was appearing whole. And she was discovering that she fitted, not into a land that asked half of her, but into a life she was building and that held her whole.

One night, alone, after Graça left, Peregrina passed the house in review, the photographs of the two lives on the walls, the crockery of the two lands in the kitchen, the machine at the window with a work begun, and thought that that, months ago, had been the house of others, and that now it was hers, not by having changed its walls, but by her having changed her way of inhabiting it. She put her hand on the machine, still warm from the day's work, and understood that she was becoming of here. Not by having changed her origin, for that does not change. But by building a life here. Belonging, after all, was this. Not a feeling the land gives us. A thing we do to the land, planting ourselves in it, and waiting, with the patience of roots, for them to take.
