## 8. Taking root by decision

Having decided to stay, Peregrina began to take root, and to take root, she discovered, is not a feeling that arrives, it is a work that is done, day by day, gesture by gesture, and it was in that work, and not in a land waiting, that she went, slowly, making the ground she had never had.

She began with the small things, which are the fine roots, the first. Instead of living in the family house as in a place of passage, with the others' things, she began to make it hers: she mended it, changed things, put in it things of hers, of the two lands, without hiding either, the crockery of there beside that of here, the books in the two languages, the photographs of the two lives, and made of the house, for the first time, a place that was hers, that held her whole, the two halves, without having to hide one to fit; and by making of the house a place of her own, she began to feel herself, within doors, at home, not in the village yet, but in the house, which was the first ground.

Then came the roots of the trade. Peregrina, who had brought from there a knowledge, a way of working, instead of hiding it for being from away, set about using it in the village, offering what she knew how to do, and discovered that what in her was from away, which set her apart, was also, seen the other way round, a wealth she could give to the village, a thing the village did not have and that she brought from her two lands; and by giving the village what she brought from away, instead of hiding it, she began to be useful, to be sought, to have a place, not in spite of being from away, but for being from away, because her from-away served for something, brought novelty, brought world; and Peregrina understood that her frontier condition, which she had lived as lack, could be lived as gift, that one who has two lands has two riches to give, and that the village, which had treated her as a tourist for not knowing the things of here, began to value her for knowing the things of there, which it lacked.

Then came the roots of the bonds, which are the deepest, and the slowest. Peregrina, who had lived on the defensive, at the door, began to give herself to people, to accept the invitations instead of fearing them, to go to the feasts and stay until the end, to make friendships not with the distrust of one who fears rejection, but with the giving of one who has decided to stay and therefore can give herself; and she made friends, slowly, people who did not see her as a tourist, who saw her as Peregrina, with her history, her two lands, her accent of nowhere, and who liked her so, whole, not in spite of the two halves, but with them; and on having, for the first time, people in the village who knew her whole and so wanted her, Peregrina felt born a root no land gives by origin, the root of the bonds, that of having, in a place, people who are ours, and which is that, more than origin, that makes of a place our home.

And she understood, taking root so, slowly, by work and by decision, a truth that turned inside out everything she had learned in the summer of the cousins: that to belong was not to hide the half that was not of the land, it was to show both, and to find those who wanted them; that home was not the place where one fitted by hiding half of oneself, it was the place where one could be whole; and that she, by giving herself to the village with her two halves shown, without hiding the of-there to seem of here, was at last doing the opposite of what she had done her whole life, was appearing whole, and was discovering that she fitted, not in a land that asked her for half, but in a life she built and that held her whole.

And it was not sudden, and it was not easy, and there were days on which the old feeling returned, on which a tourist from a cousin, a word she did not know, a reference she lacked, threw her again into the from-away, into the doubt, into the wish to pack the bag; but they were fewer and fewer, those days, because beneath them grew the new ground, that of the roots she planted, her own house, the useful trade, the bonds, and that ground, made by her, held her better than any land of origin, because it was a chosen ground, worked, won, and what is won holds more than what is inherited. And Peregrina understood, after some months of taking root, that she was becoming of here, not by having changed origin, which does not change, but by being building here a life, and that belonging, after all, was this, not a feeling the land gives us, but a thing we do to the land, planting ourselves in it, and waiting, with the patience of roots, for them to take.
