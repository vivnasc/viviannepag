## 5. The cousins call her tourist

Tourist was the word the cousins put on her, without malice, in jest, and it was in jest that it hurt most, because the jest says what seriousness hushes.

They called her that for everything. When she did not know a way, oh, tourist, let me take you. When she did things in the manner of over there, more quickly, more after the city, here comes the tourist with her hurry.

At the uncle's pig-killing, in December, she understood it from head to foot. The whole family of the village gathered in the yard, early in the morning, with the cold steaming in their mouths, and there was an old knowing going round, who held, who caught the blood for the cabidela, who saw to the guts, and everyone knew their place by birth, except her. They gave her a basin to hold and Peregrina, seeing what it was she was to hold, turned pale and had to step away, her stomach turning, and the cousins laughed, not with malice, with the gladness of those who are at home watching the one from away unable to bear what for them was the most natural thing in the world. Oh, tourist, said Aurélio, laughing, the knife in his hand, this isn't for city folk. Go on inside and make the coffee, that's what you're good for. And Peregrina went inside to make the coffee, and made it, and was ashamed of having been ashamed, and stood at the kitchen window watching the yard full of people who belonged to that without thinking, to that knowing, that cold, that blood, and feeling herself, more than ever, of another place.

And Peregrina laughed with them, because it was in jest, because there was no malice in Aurélio's voice nor in Glória's eyes; but each tourist was a needle, because each tourist said to her you're on a visit, you're not from here, and it was the family that said it to her, in the land that was meant to be hers, and it was worse to hear it from them, there, than to have heard it from the schoolmates, over there, because from the schoolmates she expected it, and from the cousins she expected the opposite.

The big festa of the village was where the thing broke open inside her, and it was over a trifle.

It was the festa of the end of the harvests, with the whole village in the square, the long tables laid in the street, the roast, the wine, the music later on. Peregrina had helped to lay the tables, on purpose, to be of the work and not of the visitors, and had sat among the cousins and their people, and for a while it went well, she laughed, she ate, she felt almost of within. And at a certain point, down the table, they began to speak of an old story, a quarrel of two families of the village from thirty years back, a matter of some water, or of a boundary, of the sort that villages keep from generation to generation. Everyone at the table had an opinion, and told their piece, and laughed, and Peregrina, who did not know the story, asked, with the innocence of one who wants to join the conversation:

"But what happened, exactly? I never heard that story."

A small silence fell over the table. And Aurélio, good-humoured, his glass in his hand, said the sentence, without thinking what it weighed:

"Ah, tourist, that's a thing of here. That can't be explained to one who's from away. That's for one who's of the land, who lived it. You can't understand."

You can't understand, that's for one who's of the land. They laughed, went back to the story, and Peregrina was left outside it, her glass in her hand, and felt a thing she had never felt with such force. It was not being put outside a joke. It was being put outside a land within the land. Because there was, she understood in that instant, a village beneath the village, made of shared memories, of the stories of thirty years ago, of the quarrels, the marriages, the dead, and into that inner village, the one of memories, she could never enter, however long she stayed, however much she tried, because those memories belonged to those who had lived their lives there, and she had arrived late, and to a memory one does not arrive late.

And she heard, once, what was said of her when she was not meant to hear, which is where the truth of villages lives. It was at Quitéria's grocery, behind the shelves, waiting her turn, and two women were talking without knowing she was on the other side, and one said, apropos of who knows what, the one from Firmino's house, the one who came back; and the other, ah, the foreigner; and the first, poor thing, she goes about wanting to be of here, but it doesn't take, folk raised away are always half, never of here nor of there. There was no malice in the voice, there was worse, there was naturalness, the naturalness of one who says a thing everyone knows and no one disputes. Peregrina stood behind the shelves, her heart beating, and waited for the women to leave so as not to have to face them, and left the shop without buying anything, and the sentence stayed hammering at her the whole day, it doesn't take. It was the village's sentence on her, said behind her back, without rancour, the way one speaks of the weather: the foreigner, poor thing, it doesn't take. And the worst of it is that she herself, at that time, believed the sentence.

She tried, days later, to enter by way of the memory, which was the door she lacked. She asked aunt Remédios to tell her the old stories of the village, the quarrels, the courtships, the dead, to learn them, to have them too. And the aunt told her some, willingly, but at a certain point stopped, and said, without malice, with the truth of old women: ah, daughter, these things I can tell you, but they're not the same thing told. You weren't here. One who wasn't here hears the story, but doesn't have it. It's like telling a blind man what green is. He comes to know there's green. See it, he doesn't. And Peregrina understood, with the aunt, that to the memory of a land one does not arrive from outside, that it is not enough that they tell it, one has to have lived it, and that that, the memory, was the one thing she could never gain however long she stayed, because the time one has not lived is not got back by being told.

She tried, the following week, to do what she had always done, to hide the half that gave her away. She set to talking more in the manner of the village, to hush the references of over there, to ask after the old stories so as to learn them, to strive to seem of here. And it was worse, because the striving showed. A person who strives to belong gives away, in the very striving, that she does not belong, because those who belong do not strive, they are; and the cousins saw the striving, and were amused by it, and the tourist, instead of stopping, redoubled, because now it was the tourist who strove to seem of here, which was even more tourist than the one who did not even try.

There was an afternoon when Glória, without malice, said to her, laughing:

"Oh, cousin, you go about now saying things like us, but it sits odd on you. Talk the way you talk, you'll be understood all the same. Forced doesn't suit you."

And Peregrina understood, with that, that there was no way out by striving: if she talked in her own manner, she was the tourist; if she strove to talk in their manner, she was the tourist striving, which was worse. There was no way to win the game, because the game was lost from the start, in the memory she did not have and could not gain.

The first winter was the longest of her life. The village closes in the winter, the nights fall early, the doors shut, each to their own hearth and their own family, and Peregrina, who had no family hearth nor family to share it with, spent the nights alone in the big cold house of the grandparents, listening to the wind on the serra and the river down below, with the television keeping company to one who has none. The cousins had their houses, their wives, their children, their full nights; they invited her now and then, out of duty, and she went, and was well received, but she was the guest, the one who comes and goes, never the one of the house; and she walked home on foot, in the dark, in the cold, to the empty house, and understood that to be alone in a city of away, where one is a foreigner but there are people in the streets at all hours, is less alone than to be alone in a village in winter, where everyone has their fire lit but us. Over there she had been the foreigner in the midst of the crowd. Here she was the foreigner in the midst of others' hearths, watching, from the dark streets, the yellow windows of the houses where the others were of family, and having none that was hers.

She left Glória's house hurt, and instead of going home she walked through the village, at night, alone, by the upper path, so as not to talk to anyone. The serra was a black shape against the sky, and the river, down below, said its everlasting thing, and Peregrina reckoned up her condition with a new and bitter clarity. She would never be of here, however long she came back, because to be of here was to have the memories of here, and those she did not have and could not gain. And she would never again be of there, because she had come back, because she had cut, because there too she was already the one who left. She was condemned to the between, to the tourist of here and the emigrant of there, with no entry into the inner land of either of the two.

And it was at the bottom of that night, at the bottom of the exclusion, that the everlasting urge came to her, the strongest of all: to pack the bag. To go back, to over there, or to go to a third land, to some other place, to begin again, looking, always looking, for the land that would receive her. She sat on a wall, in the dark, and imagined the leaving, and in the imagining there was an enormous relief, that of one who goes back to what she knows how to do, because to leave was what Peregrina knew how to do, it was her oldest art, and the bag was there, half-packed, waiting, since she was eight.

And it was in the middle of that thought of flight, sitting on the wall in the dark, that there came to her, for the first time clear, the suspicion that was going to save her. It came as true suspicions come, without warning, almost a question asked by another person inside her: and what if there's no land that receives me because belonging is not received? And what if I've been waiting thirty-eight years for a land to adopt me, and the truth is that no land adopts anyone? And what if one is of a land not because it wants us, but because we want it and stay in it and work in it and make memories in it, ours, the ones of now? And what if I belong nowhere not because no land wants me, but because I never gave myself whole to any? I was always outside, waiting, with the bag half-packed, ready to leave for the next one, looking for a home that would only come if I stopped looking for it and began to build it.

Down below, in the village, the lights of the houses were going out one by one, the houses of those who were of here, of those who had their fire and their people and had never in their lives had to ask themselves where they were from. Peregrina looked at them from above, in the dark of the wall, and for a moment she hated them, those lights, the ease of those people, the belonging that had cost them so little; and right after she was ashamed of the hatred, because she understood it was not envy of what they had, it was envy of not having had to win it, and that perhaps, who knows, what one wins is worth more than what one inherits, even if it costs a life. But that was a thought for later. That night, on the wall, there was only the cold and the urge to flee and, beneath it, that suspicion making its way.

She stayed on the wall a long time, in the dark, with the suspicion growing. It was a hard suspicion, because it took away her excuse. As long as the fault was the lands', that did not receive her, she was a victim, and victims have nothing to do but suffer and leave. But if the fault was also hers, of the half-packed bag, of the never giving herself, then the solution was also hers, and she could change it, and that was at once terrifying and the first good news in a long time, because it gave her back what the condition of foreigner had taken from her: the power to do something.

She got up from the wall late, and went down home, and on going in she looked at the bag, at the foot of the bed, with the lid raised, as always. And for the first time, instead of seeing it as her salvation, the thing that let her leave before it hurt, she saw it as what it perhaps was: the chain that kept her from belonging anywhere. She did not close it that night. She did not have the courage. But she looked at it in another way, and to look in another way is the beginning of everything, and she slept, for the first time since she had arrived, not with the question where am I from, but with another, newer and harder: and what if the land I belong to is none of the ones I have, but the one I decide to make mine?
