## 5. The cousins call her tourist

The cousins of here called Peregrina a tourist, without malice, in jest, and it was in jest that it hurt most, because the jest says what seriousness hides, and in the cousins' jest was the truth Peregrina had come to flee and that awaited her there too: that she, for those of here, was not of here, she was a visit, one from away, a tourist in the land of her own parents.

They called her so for everything. When she did not know a thing of the village, a path, a name, a custom, they laughed, oh tourist; when she found strange a thing that for them was normal, oh tourist; when she did things in the manner of there, faster, more of the city, oh tourist; and Peregrina laughed with them, because it was in jest, because there was no malice, but within, each tourist was a stab, because each tourist told her you are not of here, you are visiting, you are from away, and told it to her the family, the cousins, the people who ought to be hers, in the land that ought to be hers, and it was worse to hear it from them, there, than to have heard it from the schoolmates, there, because from the schoolmates she expected it, and from the cousins, in her own land, she expected the opposite, she expected the you are of here, you are ours, and got the tourist.

And there was, in the word, an injustice the cousins did not see. For them, who had been born in the village and never left it, to belong was the most natural thing in the world, it was the air they breathed, it had cost them nothing, they had not had to win it; and so they did not see the privilege it was, and treated as a tourist, lightly, the cousin who would give everything to have what they had for free, a land, a belonging, a ground. The cousins belonged without merit, by having stayed; and they mocked, without malice, the one who did not belong by having left, or by her parents having left, as though Peregrina's lack of belonging were a joke, and not a wound, as though to be from away were a choice of hers and not the fate emigration had left her at birth.

And Peregrina, in the midst of the tourists, went on feeling herself more and more what she did not want to be, and began to do what she had always done, to hide the half that betrayed her: to speak more in the manner of the village, to silence the references of there, to pretend to know things she did not, to strive to seem of here, so that they would stop with the tourist. And the more she strove, the worse it was, because the striving showed, and a person who strives to belong betrays, in the very striving, that she does not belong, because those who belong do not strive, they simply are; and the cousins saw the striving, and found it amusing, and the tourist, instead of stopping, redoubled, because now it was the tourist who strove to seem of here, which was even more a tourist than the one who did not even try.

And there was a day on which the thing burst within her, and it was over a trifle. They were all at a village feast, and there was talk of an old story, of a quarrel of families from thirty years back, that everyone in the village knew and had an opinion on, and Peregrina, who did not know it, asked what it had been, and a silence fell, and a cousin said, laughing, oh tourist, that is a thing of here, you cannot understand, that is for those who are of the land; and Peregrina, on hearing the you cannot understand, that is for those who are of the land, felt a thing she had never felt with such force, felt herself excluded in a deep way, put outside not of a jest, but of belonging itself, of the common memory, of the history of the land, as though there were a land within the land, made of shared memories, into which she could never enter because she had not lived there, because she had arrived late, because she was from away, and into that land within, that of the memories, no striving of hers gave her entrance.

And she left the feast early, hurt, and walked through the village at night, alone, and did the sum of her condition with a new and bitter clarity: that she would never be of here, however much she returned, however much she strove, because to be of here was to have the memories of here, and those she did not have and could not gain, because they were of those who had lived their lives there; and that she would never again be of there, because she had returned, because she had cut with there, because there too she was now the one who left; and that she was condemned to the between, to the tourist of here and the emigrant of there, with no entrance into the land within of either. And it was at the bottom of that night, at the bottom of the exclusion, that Peregrina touched the despair of the book, and that it was necessary to touch in order to turn: she thought of giving up, of going back, of there, or of going to a third land, to some other place, in search, always in search, of the land that would receive her; and it was halfway through that thought of flight that there came to her, for the first time clear, the suspicion that was to save her: and if there is no land that receives me because belonging is not received? And if I have spent thirty-eight years waiting for a land to adopt me, and the truth is that no land adopts anyone, that one is of a land not by its wanting us, but by our wanting it and staying in it and working in it and making memories in it, and that I belong nowhere not because no land wants me, but because I never gave myself whole to any, always went about from away, waiting, with my bag half-packed, ready to leave for the next in search of the home that would only come if I stopped looking for it and began to build it?
