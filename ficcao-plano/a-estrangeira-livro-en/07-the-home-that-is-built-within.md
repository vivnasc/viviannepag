## 7. The home that is built within

Peregrina understood, after the Keeper, that the home she had spent her life looking for abroad is built first within, and it was the hardest thing to accept, because it is easier to blame the land for not receiving us than to discover that the home was a thing we ourselves had not made.

Because until then Peregrina had put the problem outside herself: the fault was the emigration's, which had made her from away; the fault was the land of there's, which treated her as Portuguese; the fault was the village's, which treated her as a tourist; the fault was the cousins', the exclusion, the history she did not know; and while the fault was outside, the solution was outside too, it was to find a land that received her, and as she did not find it, she was condemned, she was the victim of a fate that came from outside and that she could not change. And what the Keeper had opened to her, and that she went on slowly letting in, was the opposite: that the problem was not only outside, it was also within, in the way she lived herself, always from away, always with her bag half-packed, always waiting, never given whole to a place; and that if the problem was also within, then the solution was also within, and she could change it, which was frightening, because it took away her excuse, but freeing, because it gave her back the power.

And she began to understand a thing about herself: that she had never, anywhere, given herself whole to a place. Abroad, she had always lived as one passing through, with the idea that the real land was Portugal, that one day she would return, that it was provisional; and by living there as provisional, she had never made root there, never given herself, always stayed half, waiting for the true land. And here, now, she did the same in reverse: she lived as one passing through, with the idea that perhaps she would return there, or go somewhere else, with her bag half-packed, without giving herself to the village, without making bonds, waiting to see whether the village received her before she gave herself; and by living here so, waiting, on the defensive, she never made root here either, and confirmed herself from away, not because the village put her outside, but because she herself put herself outside, by not giving herself, by not staying, by always keeping the possibility of leaving.

And she understood that it was this, and not the land, that made her a foreigner everywhere: the half-packed bag. A person with a half-packed bag is a foreigner in any land, even her own, because she does not plant herself, does not give herself, does not commit, always stays at the door, ready to leave, and one who stays at the door never enters, and one who never enters never belongs, not by fault of the house, but by not getting past the door. And Peregrina had lived her whole life at the door, of here and of there, at the door of the two lands, waiting for one to pull her inside, without understanding that no one could pull her, that to enter was a gesture of hers, that it was she who had to set down the bag, to close the door behind her, to say I stay here, here is my home, and to begin to live as one who stays, and not as one who is passing through.

And there was, in this discovery, a part that hurt, which was to recognise the fear beneath the half-packed bag. Because Peregrina kept the bag half-packed not by chance, she kept it out of fear: fear of giving herself to a land and being rejected, as she had been rejected in the summer of the cousins; fear of closing the door behind her and discovering that the house did not want her; fear of committing and suffering; and the half-packed bag was the defence against that fear, it was the I do not give myself so that they will not hurt me, it is what the child of emigration does who learned early that to give oneself to a land is to risk the laugh, the exclusion, the go back to your own country. And Peregrina understood that her non-belonging, which she lived as a fate come from outside, was in part a protection she herself kept, it was safer not to belong anywhere than to give herself to a land and risk not being accepted; and that while she protected herself so, with the bag half-packed, she would be safe from rejection, but she would be also, forever, without a home, because there is no home for one who does not risk making one.

And she decided, in those days, after the Keeper, to do the thing she had never done: to set down the bag. To decide to stay. Not because the village was her land of origin, which it was not; not because the village received her with open arms, which it did not; but because it was there she was, and it was there she was going to build, by decision, the home she had never had by origin. And it was an intimate decision, first, without telling anyone, a decision within, which was where the Keeper had said home began: she decided to stop living herself as one passing through, to stop putting the fault on the land, to stop waiting to be received, and to begin, she, to give herself, to stay, to commit to that village, to make it hers, not by origin, by choice. And on taking that decision within, she felt a strange and new thing: she felt, for the first time, a beginning of ground beneath her feet, not because the land had changed, but because she had changed the way of being in it, and the ground, she understood, was not the land, it was the decision to stay in it, and that, at last, depended only on her.
