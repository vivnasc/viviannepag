## 7. The home that is built within

The decision did not come in a flash. It came on an afternoon of rain, with the house in darkness, and it was so small that Peregrina almost did not notice it: it was the taking up of the box.

The box had been in the corner of the room since she arrived, closed, with her things inside, hers, the only ones in that house of grandparents' furniture. The photographs of the friends of over there, the crockery of over there, two or three books, a blanket she had bought at a fair over there and was fond of. She had left them in the box all those months, and told herself it was only for now, until she knew, until she decided. And on that afternoon of rain, with nothing to do, with the Keeper still fresh in her head and the fountain and the water that becomes of here by its course, Peregrina sat on the floor beside the box and opened it.

And she understood, on opening it, what she had been doing by leaving it closed. She did not spread her things through the house for the same reason she did not unpack the bag: to spread them was to stay. It was to say to that house, to that land, I'm staying, this is mine. And she did not say it, because to say it was to risk giving herself and being sent away, as Glória had sent her on the threshing floor, off you go to your own country. The closed box and the half-packed bag were the same thing, they were the defence of one who had learned early that to give oneself to a land is to risk the laugh, the exclusion, the door shut in one's face. As long as the things were in the box, she was at the door of her own house, with one foot out, ready to leave. And one who is at the door never enters, and one who never enters never belongs, not by fault of the house, but by not crossing the threshold.

She took the things out of the box, one by one, slowly. She hung the photographs of the friends of over there on the walls, beside the photographs of the family she barely knew, and for a moment they looked strange, the two lives mixed, but she let them be, because that was how she was, the two lives mixed, and a house of her own had to hold her whole, with both halves on show, and not only with the half that fitted. She put the crockery of over there beside the aunt's crockery. She spread the blanket from the fair over there on the grandparents' sofa. And the house, by the end of that afternoon, ceased to be the house of others lent to the one who came back and began to be, for the first time, a place that contained her, the two lands within the same walls.

She touched the house too, which until then she had not dared to touch, it being others'. She took from a wall a dark and ugly picture that had been there since the grandparents and that no one liked but no one took down, and put in its place a thing of her own. She opened a window that had been nailed shut for years, in the room that faced east, and let in the morning light, which had not come into that house in a generation. She painted a wall of the kitchen a colour the mother would never put up, a colour of over there, cheerful, that to aunt Remédios would seem a folly. And in touching the house, in taking down the ugly picture and opening the nailed window and painting the wall her own colour, Peregrina understood that she was doing a thing no one had done to that house in thirty years, since the grandparents had died: she was living it. A house no one touches is a house of the dead, a museum; and she was taking the house out of the museum, putting in it her colour, her light, her disorder of the living, and making of it, from a house of the dead lent to the one who came back, a house of a living woman who stayed.

The greater gesture was still wanting, and Peregrina went to the bedroom, and looked at the bag, at the foot of the bed, with the lid raised these months, with that jumper still on top of the chest of drawers. And she stayed a long time looking at it, because she knew what she was going to do and it cost her the way it costs to tear out a root that was after all no root at all, was only the habit of having none. She took the jumper, folded it, put it away in the drawer, with the others. She took from the bag what was still in it to take. And she closed the bag. She closed the lid, and the clasp made that sound clasps make, dry, and Peregrina stayed on her knees on the floor, beside the closed bag, her heart beating, because she had just done a thing she had never done in thirty-eight years: she had taken her foot out. She had decided to stay.

She carried the bag up onto the top of the wardrobe, high up, where the bags of those who stay and only travel sometimes are kept, and not at the foot of the bed, where sleeps the bag of one who is passing through. And in putting it up there, out of reach, she felt a strange thing, at once a fear and a ground. The fear was the everlasting one, that of having given herself, of no longer being able to flee quickly if the land hurt her. And the ground was new: it was the first time she felt, beneath her feet, something firm, not because the land had changed, but because she had changed the way of being in it. The ground, she understood, was not the land. It was the decision to stay in it. And that, at last, depended only on her.

There was, of course, the part that hurt, and Peregrina lay down that night to feel it whole. Because in closing the bag she was giving up the excuse. As long as the fault was the lands', that did not receive her, she had nothing to do but suffer and wait. Now not. Now the fault was also hers, of the bag, of the never giving herself, and the solution was also hers, and that put into her hands a work she did not know how to do and that frightened her: the work of giving herself to a land, of staying, of committing herself, of risking. And there was, beneath the fear, the fear at the bottom, the one of the summer of the cousins: that of giving herself and being, once again, sent away.

And there was a gesture, in the following weeks, that sealed the decision better than any thought: she had the roof seen to. The grandparents' house had a leak in the roof, for years, and the aunt had told her so on arrival, and the father, she remembered now, had said it once to the mother, the house needs a roof, Dores, that'll come down one day; and it had never been done, because to mend the roof of a house no one lives in is to throw money at a place of passage, and the whole family, of here and of there, had always treated that house as a place of passage. Peregrina called the roofer, and paid, and watched him go up and set the new tiles, and there came to her, in watching the tiles being set, a strange joy, because to mend the roof of a house is the most opposite thing there is to packing a bag: it is to wager that one will be there when it rains. The father had seen the roof needed it and had not done it, because the father, he, always came back, but came back passing through. It was the daughter, the one who had never been of here, who set the tiles. And she understood, watching the man up there, that she was doing for the house what she was going to do for herself: setting it to bear the rain, instead of leaving it ready to go away.

And there was, some weeks later, a thing she only noticed by its absence, and which was the surest sign of all. She became aware, one afternoon, at the machine, that for days she had not thought of going away. Her whole life, in every house, there had been a little corner of her head always occupied with the leaving, with the if this goes wrong I'll go there, a plan of flight always at the ready, like one who lives in a house knowing where the back door is. And she noticed, that afternoon, that that corner had been empty for days, that she was making no plans of flight, that she was not measuring the lands in search of the next. It was not that she had stopped being afraid; it was that the fear no longer came dressed as leaving. And a person who stops making, without noticing, plans of flight from a place, is a person who has begun to live in it in earnest, because to live is this, it is to stop having one's head at the back door.

She had now, against the fear, the water of the fountain. She remembered it, lying in the dark, the water that came from far and became of here by its course, and said to herself: I don't have to be of here to begin to live here. I have to begin to run here, and not somewhere else. And she fell asleep, that night, without the bag at the foot of the bed for the first time, and it was a different sleep, not better nor worse, deeper, the sleep of one who sleeps in a house where she has decided to wake the next day, and the next, and the next, instead of the light sleep of one who sleeps with the bag packed, always half awake, always halfway to the door.

There was a gesture she made every morning, always, in every house she had slept in: on waking, before anything else, her eyes went to the bag, to see if it was there, ready. It was a gesture so old that she had never noticed it, the way one does not notice one's own breathing. That first morning, Peregrina woke, and her eyes went to the place at the foot of the bed where the bag always slept, and did not find it, and for an instant she had a start, and then she remembered, it's up there, closed, and understood, with the start passing, that what had gone was not the bag, it was the possibility of fleeing, the back door always open. And that it was with that she had to learn to live: without an emergency exit.

In the morning, before her coffee, she did a thing she had not planned and that she understood, halfway through, to be the continuation of the gesture of the evening before. She went to the yard, the abandoned yard of the grandparents' house, all overgrown, where the vine the mother wept for had been, and set to clearing it. She tore out the weeds with her hands, without gloves, until they were in the earth, and dug a corner, and went to Quitéria for some seeds and a rose plant, and planted them. The yard was not to give anything to the house; no one needed that vegetable patch. It was only to plant. It was to put in the earth a thing that had to stay, that had to make root, that obliged her to stay to see it grow, because one who plants commits to the time of a plant, and Peregrina needed to commit to something that bound her to the ground. The neighbour of the wall, seeing her on her knees in the earth, said to her, over the hedge, so you're going to sow, miss? And Peregrina, with her hands dirty, answered I am, and it was the first time she said a thing like that, a thing of the future, of one who counts on staying for the harvest, and the word came out of her firm.

A few days later, she invited aunt Remédios for a coffee, and it was the first time she invited anyone to that house as being hers, and not as being the family house where she was sleeping. The aunt came in, and looked around, and saw the wall painted the colour of over there, and the window open letting in the east, and the photographs of the friends of over there beside those of the family, and the ugly picture that was missing, and stayed a moment quiet, with that stillness of old women who are deciding what to feel. And then she said, with the frankness of the serra: it's different, this. My mother would turn in her grave if she saw the wall that colour. But it's well. It's got life. For thirty years this house was waiting for someone to live it, since your grandparents died, and no one lived it, they came here in the summers and shut it up again. You came to live it. It does the house good to be lived. And she went off with the coffee drunk, and Peregrina stayed with that it's got life warming her more than the coffee, because it was the first time someone of the family recognised, without reservation, that she was doing a good thing there, taking the house out of mourning, putting it to live, and that that, and not the blood nor the accent, was what made of her, slowly, the owner of that house.

On waking for the day, the first thing she had seen was the chest of drawers without the jumper, and the wall with the photographs of the friends of over there, and the blanket from the fair on the sofa as she passed through to the kitchen. The house was different. It was little, it was only a box undone and a bag put away up high, but it was everything, because the home she had spent her life looking for out there, she understood that morning, making the coffee in the grandparents' kitchen that was beginning to be hers, was not found, it was made; and it was made from the inside out, first in the head, in the decision to stay, and then in the walls, in the things, in the gestures, slowly, day by day; and she had just begun to make it, with a box and a bag, the evening before, in the dark, with no one watching, which is how true homes are begun, without witnesses, from within, in the place where a person decides, at last, to stay.
