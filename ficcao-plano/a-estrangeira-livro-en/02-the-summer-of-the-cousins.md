## 2. The summer of the cousins

Peregrina learned not to be from where she was in one summer, as a child, and it was a summer like so many, of holidays in Portugal, but it was in it that she split down the middle, and never again knew how to put herself back together, because there are wounds of childhood that do not close, they stay, and the person grows around them, crooked.

She was seven or eight, and it was the first return trip she clearly remembered, the first in which she understood things. Her parents brought her every summer to the village, and until then it had been good, it had been a feast, the granddaughter who came from abroad, the grandparents, the cousins, the heat, the freedom of the village that she did not have in the city of there; and Peregrina adored those summers, and through the year counted the days for them, because abroad, at school, she was the Portuguese one, and in Portugal, on holiday, she was at last, she thought, from home.

And it was in that summer that she understood that she was not. She was with the cousins, the cousins of the village, her own age, and they were playing, and Peregrina, in the middle of the game, said a thing, a word, one of those words she said in the manner of there, or mixed, half in one language half in another, as the children of emigration speak, without realising it, and the cousins stopped, and looked at one another, and laughed. It was only that, an exchanged look and a laugh, but Peregrina, a child, understood everything in that look: she understood that she had said a thing that was not said, that she spoke differently, that she was not like them, that there was, between her and the cousins, a difference she had not known existed until that look, and that this difference set her apart, even there, even in the midst of the family, even in the land where she thought she was from home.

And from that look on, in that summer, Peregrina changed, without deciding it. She began to be careful of what she said, to silence the words that might sound of away, to imitate the way the cousins spoke, to hide what in her was of there, so as not to see again that look, that laugh; and by hiding the of-there to fit into Portugal, without knowing it, she began to do the same she already did, without knowing it, abroad, where she hid the of-Portugal to fit into the school of there; and the child was left hiding half of herself on each side, being half a person in each land, keeping the of-there when she was here and the of-here when she was there, and never, in any land, able to be whole, because to be whole was to show the two halves, and each land accepted only one.

And that was what she learned in that summer, and what became the fate of her life: she learned not to be from where she was. She learned that to be accepted she had to hide the half that was not of that land, and by hiding a half she ceased to be herself, became a reduced, careful, watchful version, always afraid the wrong word might slip out, the wrong manner, the wrong half, and betray her as the one from away. And a child who learns to hide half of herself to be accepted becomes an adult who does not know who she is whole, because she never showed herself whole to anyone, because she always divided, always gave half, always kept the other, and at the end of a life like that she no longer even knows which of the halves is the true one, nor whether there is a true one, nor whether beneath the two hidden halves there exists a whole person, or only two halves that never joined.

And there was a detail, in that summer, that Peregrina never forgot, and that hurt more than the cousins' laugh. At the end of the holidays, at the parting, one of the cousins said to her, without malice, with the innocent cruelty of children: well, goodbye, go on home to your own country. And Peregrina, seven or eight, returned to the country where she was born with that phrase hammering, go on home to your own country, and on arriving there, on going back to the school of there, at the first quarrel she heard the other, the one of always: go back to your own country. And the child Peregrina, with the two phrases, one from each side, understood a thing that marked her forever: that everyone, on both sides, sent her home to her own country, and that no one, on either side, thought her own country was there; that on both sides she was the one from away, on both sides they told her go home to your own country, and that her own country, the one to which everyone sent her, did not exist, was nowhere, was a place everyone named and no one knew where it lay, because it was the place of those who are from nowhere, which is the non-place of the children of emigration.

And it was with that wound of the summer of the cousins, of the country everyone told her was hers and that lay nowhere, that Peregrina grew up, and it was that wound, more than any plan, that made her return, thirty years later, to the village: the hope, never spoken, of finding at last that country, of hearing someone tell her you stay, you are of here, this is your land; and it was that same hope that the village, by treating her as a tourist, by asking her where she was from, by saying ah when she said from here, undid again, confirming to her, at thirty-eight, what the cousin had told her at eight: that her land was not there, that she was, there as everywhere, the one from away.
