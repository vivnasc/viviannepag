## 2. The summer of the cousins

There is one summer in Peregrina's life that she went back to always, without meaning to, the way the tongue goes back to the place of the broken tooth. She was seven, or eight, and it was the summer in which she learned the thing that governed all the rest: not to be from where she was.

Her parents brought her to Portugal every August. They made the long journey by car, two days and a night, with the car loaded to the roof with presents for the family, coffee, and things from over there that here there were none of, because one who comes back from away cannot come back empty-handed, it is a law older than the borders. The mother counted the borders the way one says a rosary, this one left, this one left, and at each one there was a tightening, the papers, the guards, the language, and the mother held the document bag against her chest as if her whole life were in it. Peregrina rode in the back seat watching the landscape change colour until it was the right colour, the colour of the serra. And at the last border, the one into Portugal, the mother always began to cry, quietly, without letting the father stop driving, and said we've arrived, Firmino, we've arrived home, and the child in the back seat learned, without knowing it, that home was a place one went to once a year and from which one wept on arriving and on leaving, a place that was not where one lived, but where one ought to have lived. They reached the village by night, and the grandparents' house opened all lit up, and there were people at the door, and grandmother Aurora took her in her arms and said this one is mine, and for a month Peregrina was the granddaughter who came from far, the princess of the summer, and she loved those Augusts with a love that lasted her the whole year, counting the days to the next.

Grandmother Aurora was the only person in the world who made her feel of here without reservation. She taught her the songs of the village at night, in the kitchen, with her mother already asleep, and corrected her words with patience, that's not how it goes, my treasure, it goes like this, and Peregrina would repeat it, and the grandmother would laugh and say we'll yet make one of the land of you. There was no time. Grandmother Aurora died in the winter Peregrina was eleven, far away, on the other side of the border, and Peregrina did not come to the burial because it was school term, and she wept for her in a room of a country that was not hers, alone, and understood, without knowing how to say it, that she had lost the only root that held her to that land. After the grandmother, the Augusts were never again what they had been, and the this one is mine was never said again by anyone.

And all year long, far away, the mother filled her head with the village. At night, making the supper in the kitchen of the flat, she told her the village the way one tells a fairy tale: the fountain in the square, the grandfather's vine that gave the best grapes on the street, the festas, the names of the people, the smell of the bread in the communal oven, the serra. She told it with a longing that was catching, and the child Peregrina grew up desiring that village she barely knew, thinking it the most beautiful place in the world, counting the days to the Augusts. The mother, without meaning to, planted in the daughter the same longing that gnawed at her, a longing at second hand, inherited, for a place where the daughter had never lived; and it was that borrowed longing, deeper than any memory of her own, that made of Peregrina, as a child, a foreigner the other way round: a girl who lived in one land and longed for another, who was in one place and dreamed of another, with her body there and her heart in her mother's village. It was no wonder that, thirty years later, she went back looking for that village: she had been looking for it since she was five, at night, in the kitchen, in her mother's voice. What she did not know, and the mother did not either, is that the village of the stories did not exist, that the mother had given her a longing for a place of paper, and that to go back would be, first of all, to lose the invented village in order to have to make a real one.

That summer, she did not yet know it. She came to know it in the middle of an afternoon, on the threshing floor, with the cousins.

They were playing at what one played, a thing of hands and songs, sitting on the warm ground of the threshing floor in the shade of the fig tree, and there were the three of them, Glória, who was her age, and an older cousin, and Peregrina in the middle, happy, with her knees dirty, feeling at last at home. And she said something. A word. One of those words that came out of her the way they come over there, or mixed, without her noticing, because at home, far away, they talked like that, in a Portuguese with holes that the other tongue stopped up. She did not remember the word. She remembered what came after.

Glória stopped clapping. She looked at the older cousin. And the two of them laughed.

That was all. A look and a laugh. But the child Peregrina understood everything in that look, with the terrible clarity of children, who see the essential because they have not yet learned to cover it with explanations: she understood that she had said a thing one did not say, that she talked differently, that she was not like them, and that that difference, which she had not known existed until that laugh, put her outside. There. In the middle of the cousins. In the land where she thought she was at home.

"What is it?" she asked, the smile still half on her face.

"Nothing," said Glória, laughing. "You just talk like a foreigner, cousin. You say everything back to front."

Like a foreigner. The older cousin laughed again, and said something low to Glória, and the two of them laughed once more, and Peregrina was left outside the laugh, which was the worst place to be, because a laugh we are not part of is a door that shuts with us on the outside.

From that afternoon on, she changed. She did not decide to change; she changed, the way children change, underneath, in the place where the things that last are learned. She began to take care with what she said. To think twice before each word, to hush the ones that might sound of away, to sharpen her ear to the cousins' words in order to imitate them. She spent the rest of that August keeping watch over her own mouth. And in keeping watch over her mouth so as to fit into Portugal, she did, without knowing it, what she already did on the other side without knowing it, where she kept watch over the other mouth, the Portuguese one, so as to fit into the school over there. The child was left hiding half of herself on each side. Half a girl in each land, keeping the over there when she was here and the over here when she was there, and never, in any land, able to be whole, because to be whole was to show both halves, and each land accepted only one.

She learned, that summer, the art that was to keep her company her whole life and that no schoolmistress teaches: the art of not being from where one is. Of entering a land with half of oneself hidden in one's pocket, the way one enters a house with mud on one's shoes and walks on tiptoe so as not to dirty it.

And there was, at the end of that August, a farewell she never forgot, and which hurt more than the laugh on the threshing floor.

It was at the door of the grandparents' house, with the car already loaded, the mother crying in another way now, crying the parting. The cousins came to say goodbye. And Glória, embracing her, with the innocent cruelty of children, who say the truths that grown-ups have already learned not to say, let slip in her ear, without any malice:

"Goodbye then, cousin. Off you go to your own country."

To your own country. Peregrina got into the car with the sentence hammering, and made the two days of the journey back with it, off you go to your own country, and reached home, far away, and in September went back to school.

The school over there was another land, and in that land Peregrina was the Portuguese one. She remembered the room, the desks, the smell of chalk, and she remembered above all the lunch hour, which was the worst, because it was the hour of the lunchbox. The other girls brought the food of over there, the food of everyone, and Peregrina brought her mother's, who cooked Portuguese even in the country of away, and her box smelled of something else, of frying, of garlic, of a kitchen that was not that land's; and she had only to open the box for one or another to wrinkle her nose, what's that, what's that smell, and Peregrina learned to eat quickly, with her head down, or to keep the box for last, or to say she was not hungry, so as not to open in front of the others the smell of home that gave her away.

There was a girl, Sandrine, or some name of over there like that, who quarrelled with her once over a trifle, a turn at skipping, a girls' dispute, and who at the end, finding nothing better to wound her with, threw at her the sentence everyone in that land knew how to throw at the children of those who had come from away:

"Go back to your own country!"

And the child Peregrina, seven years old, or eight, with the two sentences in her head, one from each side of the world, made the sum that split her life in two and that no grown-up helped her to undo, because no grown-up knew she had made it. The sum was simple and it was terrible. Everyone, on both sides, sent her away to her own country. And no one, on either side, thought her own country was there. On both sides she was the one from away. On both sides they told her go back to your own country. And her own country, the one they all sent her to, did not exist. It lay nowhere. It was a place everyone named and no one knew how to open on the map, because it was the place of those who are from nowhere, the no-place of the children of those who left.

There was the father, in those summers, and the father was the reverse of her: he reached the village and was of here at once. He let go the city man he was over there and put on the old boots that stayed in the house from one year to the next, and went to the fields with his brothers and cousins as if he had never left, and reaped, and carried, and knew the names of the plants and the tools and the hills, all that one learns as a child and does not unlearn. One summer, Peregrina was about ten, the father wanted to take her with him, to teach her, to give her a little of what was his. He put a small sickle in her hand and showed her the way of it, and Peregrina tried, and could not, and tired quickly, and was afraid of the sickle, and the cousins her age, who had reaped since they were small, did it as a game while she fumbled. And the father did not scold her, no, he laid his hand on her head and said, never mind, daughter, this isn't for you, you're of the city; and he said it with tenderness, but Peregrina heard, beneath the tenderness, a thing small and sad, which was the father understanding, there, in the field, that the daughter was not of his ground, that he had given her up to lose by taking her away, and that all that knowing he had in his body would die with him, because the daughter was of another land and could not inherit it. It was the only time she saw him like that, with that sadness beneath the tenderness. They went home, and the father never took her to the fields again, and Peregrina understood, without knowing how to say it, that she had disappointed the father not in a thing she had done, but in a thing she was: from away.

Years later, a grown woman, she told this story to her mother, once, on the telephone, and the mother fell silent on the other end, a long time, and then said only, in a small voice: I didn't know you carried that, daughter. I thought the summers were your joy. And they were, mother, said Peregrina. They were my joy and the place where I learned I had no land. The two things in the same month. It was all in the same month, always.

And the cruellest of it, she understood as a grown woman, is that that summer taught her the wrong thing, and taught it to her so deep that it took her thirty years to unlearn. It taught her that belonging was a thing one has or does not have, that one inherits or does not inherit, like the colour of one's eyes, and that she, by the bad luck of being born away, did not have it, and that was that. No one told her, at that age when one learns everything in earnest, that the belonging of children, the belonging of origin, the effortless one, is only half the story, and the half that is lost; that there is another, the grown-ups', which is not inherited, it is made, and that that one was within her reach as it is within everyone's. She grew up thinking she lacked a thing of birth that she would never have, when what she lacked was a thing she had not yet made. And between thinking we lack what cannot be had and understanding that we lack what we have not yet made lies the difference between a life of despair and a life of work; and Peregrina lived thirty years on the wrong side of that difference, weeping for a lack that was after all a task not begun.

It was with that wound of the summer of the cousins that Peregrina grew up, and it was that wound, more than the death of the father or the weariness of the life over there, that made her go back, thirty years later, on the half-past-five bus. The hope, which she never told to anyone, not even to herself, of finding at last that land. Of hearing, once and for all, someone of here say to her you stay, you're ours, this is your land. And it was that same hope that the village, at the first poor thing, at the first ah, at the first you talk like a foreigner, undid for her again, confirming to her, at thirty-eight, at the table of a hen killed out of love, what Glória had told her on the threshing floor at eight: that her land was not there, that she was, there as everywhere, the one from away.

That first week, in her parents' house, Peregrina dreamed of the threshing floor. Of the fig tree, of the warm ground, of the laugh. She woke in the night, in the bed of the borrowed house, with the cousin's sentence in her mouth like a taste, off you go to your own country, and looked at the bag, at the foot of the bed, with the lid raised, and understood, still half asleep, a thing that in the morning she preferred to forget: that the bag had been packed since that summer. That she had packed it at eight, on the threshing floor, in the instant of the laugh, and had never again unpacked it, in any land, always waiting for the sentence that sent her away, always ready to obey it.
