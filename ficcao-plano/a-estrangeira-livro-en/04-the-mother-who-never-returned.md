## 4. The mother who never returned

Peregrina's mother never returned, and it was the mother, more than anyone, who did not understand that the daughter should return, because the mother belonged to a land that no longer existed, and one who belongs to a land that no longer exists does not understand one who looks for a land that does exist.

The mother had emigrated young, with the father, in search of a living, and had made her life there, and had stayed there, and was of there now, in her way; but she was of there without ever having ceased to be of here, because the mother's heart had stayed in the village she had left, in the village of thirty-odd years ago, in the one that existed when she departed, and the mother had lived her whole life abroad longing for a village that, in her head, had stopped on the day of departure, with the people who were there, the customs there were, the house as it was. And the mother had fed that stopped village, told it to the daughter, made of it a lost paradise, the land they belonged to, the true home, far away, waiting; and Peregrina had grown up hearing of that village as of the home to which one day they would return, and it was in part because of the mother's stories that she returned, in search of the village the mother had painted for her.

And she discovered, on returning, that that village did not exist. The mother's village, the one stopped in the time of departure, with the people and the customs and the house of thirty years ago, that one had died, had gone with time, the people had died or changed, the customs had altered, the village was another, alive, present, different from the one the mother kept; and Peregrina understood that she had returned to a village that existed only in the mother's head, to an emigrant's paradise that was an old photograph, and that the real village, that of now, did not await her with open arms as the mother's did, because the real village did not even know who she was, she was only one more who returned, the daughter of some who had emigrated long ago.

And when Peregrina telephoned the mother, far away, telling her how hard it was, how she felt herself from away, how the village was not what the mother had told, the mother did not understand. The mother said but how do you feel yourself from away, it is our land, it is home, they are our family; and Peregrina explained, and the mother did not grasp it, because the mother did not live in the real village, she lived in the village of memory, and in the village of memory they belonged, they were from home, they were received; and the mother could not see that the daughter, in the real village, was the one from away, because the mother had never returned there, had never confronted memory with the present, had never had to discover that the stopped village does not exist, that to return is to return to a place that changed, and that one who returns does not find the home she left, finds a new place, where she has to begin again like a foreigner.

And there opened, between mother and daughter, a new distance, which was not of kilometres, it was of worlds. The mother lived in the world of those who left and did not return, those who keep the land stopped and die longing for a village that no longer exists, faithful to a home of memory; and Peregrina now lived in the world of those who returned and discovered that the home of memory is a lie, that the land changed, that returning is not arriving home, it is arriving at a strange place that happens to bear the name of home. And the mother, in her world, could not help the daughter in hers, could not teach her to belong to the real village, because she herself had never belonged to any but the stopped one; and Peregrina understood that she was alone in this too, that the mother, who had brought her to that land with her stories, could not give her the land, because the land the mother had was of paper, was of longing, was not a ground on which one could set one's feet.

And there was a conversation, on the telephone, that hurt Peregrina and that made her understand a thing. The mother, on one of those calls, said to her, with an old grief: daughter, I should never have left. If I had not left, you would be of there, you would have a land, you would not be like this now, divided. The fault is mine, that I left. And Peregrina, on hearing the mother blame herself, understood two things at once: she understood the mother's pain, who carried the guilt of having made the daughter a foreigner; and she understood that the mother, in saying that, was still bound to the idea that belonging comes from the land, that if she had stayed the daughter would belong, that the problem was the departure. And Peregrina, who began to guess at another thing, thought, without yet saying it to the mother: and if belonging does not come from where one is born, mother? And if I, even having been born there, even having returned here, can belong, not because of the land, but because of a decision of mine? And if home is neither the one one leaves nor the one one inherits, it is the one one builds, and if I can build it here, or anywhere, by dint of wanting?

And it was that question, born of the mother's grief, the beginning of Peregrina's turn, because until then she had looked for home in the right land, first in that of there, then in that of here, and the mother had taught her to look for it so, in the origin; and it was on hearing the mother blame herself for the departure that Peregrina began to suspect that she was looking wrong, that home was in no land waiting to be found, that perhaps there was no land she belonged to by origin, and that, if she wanted a home, she would have to make it, and not find it, and to make it not in the land she was from, for she was of none, but in the land where she decided to stay, planting herself in it by choice, which is the only root that grows for one who was born with none.
