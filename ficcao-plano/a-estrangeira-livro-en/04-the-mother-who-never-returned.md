## 4. The mother who never returned

The mother called on Sundays, at the same hour, with the punctuality of those who live far away and make of the telephone a mass. Dores was now seventy-something, alone in the flat far away, in the neighbourhood they had emigrated to, Firmino's widow these two years, and her life was small and ordered, the house, the church on Sundays, the other emigrant women of her age, and that call to the daughter who had gone back to the land she herself had never seen again.

"So, daughter. How's the house? The work in the yard, have you done it?"

"I have, mother. I cleaned it all."

"And the vine? Your grandfather's vine, does it still bear? That grape was the best on the street."

"The vine dried up, mother. Years ago. It's gone."

There was a silence on the other end, one of those silences in which one hears the other person recomposing the world.

"Dried up?" said the mother, and there was in her voice a small disbelief, almost hurt, as if Peregrina had given her bad news of a person and not of a plant. "But it was so good, that vine. It gave shade to the whole threshing floor. You don't remember, you were small."

Peregrina remembered. But she did not say so, because she was beginning to understand a thing: that the mother did not live in the real village. The mother lived in another village, one that had stopped on the day she emigrated, with the people who were there then, the customs there were then, the vine that bore then. The mother's village was a photograph of thirty-odd years ago, and the mother had fed that photograph her whole life, far away, told it to the daughter as one tells a paradise, made of it the true home, there at the bottom of the longing, waiting. And Peregrina had grown up hearing of that village as of the land they would one day go back to, and it was in part because of the mother's stories that she went back, looking for the village the mother had painted for her.

And she had discovered, on arriving, that that village did not exist. It had died with time. The people of the mother's photograph had either died or changed, the customs had altered, the vine had dried up. The real village, the one of now, living, present, was not the mother's, and did not wait for Peregrina with open arms as the mother's waited, because the real village did not even know who she was, she was just one more who came back, the daughter of some who had emigrated long ago.

And the worst of it is that the mother, on the telephone, could not help her with any of this, because she did not know the real village. When Peregrina, one Sunday, let the truth slip, told her she felt of away, that people treated her as a tourist, that the village was not what the mother told, the mother did not understand.

"But how can you feel of away, daughter? It's our land. It's your grandparents' house. They're our family."

"I know, mother. But they don't know me. I grew up far away. To them I'm the one from away."

"That's the early days. Give it time. Our people are good people."

"Our people are good people, mother," said Peregrina, with patience. "But you never came back. You don't know what it is to come back. The village changed. It's not the one you left."

And the mother fell silent again, and Peregrina understood there was no way to explain it to her, because the mother had never confronted memory with the present, had never had to discover that the stopped village does not exist, that to go back is to go back to a place that has changed, and that one who goes back does not find the home one left, one finds a new place where one has to begin again like a foreigner. The mother had stayed on the side of over there, in the world of those who leave and do not come back, those who keep the land stopped and die longing for a village that no longer exists, faithful to a home of paper. And Peregrina was now on the side of here, in the world of those who came back and discovered that the home of memory is a lie, that the land changed, that to come back is not to arrive home, it is to arrive at a strange place that happens to have the name of home.

Peregrina remembered the Sundays of over there, as a child, and understood now that they were already the village of paper being built in front of her. On Sundays, the mother cooked Portuguese the whole day, with the kitchen of the flat smelling of the serra, and put on an old record of a song from the land, and at the end of the afternoon made the call to the village, the dear call, counted by the minute, with the whole family round the telephone over there passing the receiver from hand to hand. And the mother, after hanging up, stayed at the window of the flat, in a neighbourhood of a country of away, looking at a street that was not hers, and said, to no one, one day we'll go back home. Home was always there. The place where they lived, where the daughter was born, where their whole life was, that one was never home. It was always the place where one waited, at the window, to go back home.

The father was different. Firmino went back, every four years, for the holidays, and saw the real village, and saw the vine wasting and the people changing and the house needing a roof, and went back to the flat and said nothing to his wife of what he had seen, left her her intact village, out of love, because there are loves that are made of not undoing the other's illusion. Once only, Peregrina, already a teenager, heard him say to the mother, low, in the kitchen, the village isn't what you think any more, Dores; and the mother answered, without turning, don't tell me that, Firmino, leave me the village as it was, it's the only thing I brought from there whole. And the father fell silent, and never said it again, and carried to the grave what he knew, so that the wife could go on keeping the vine alive in her head.

And it was a grave that brought her back. The father, dying, far away, in a hospital of a country that was not his, at the end of a life of away, asked one thing only, and asked it with the little voice he had left: that they take him to the land. He did not want to stay in the grave of over there, beside where he had lived forty years; he wanted the grave of the village, beside his parents, in the serra, because a man who was always of here, even living there, wants to be of here too after he is dead. And they took him. It was Peregrina who saw to everything, to the papers of the transfer, to the journey of the coffin, and who brought the father to the village, one winter, to bury him where he was born. And the mother did not come. The woman who had never come back in life did not come back even to bury her husband, and she said it was her heart, that the doctor would not let her travel, and it was true, but it was also the deeper truth, that the mother could not, could not see the village changed, not even to put the man in the ground; she preferred to weep for him there, alone, at the window, with the village of paper in her head, rather than see him go down into the real village. And Peregrina buried the father alone, with the family of here around her, people she barely knew, and seeing the coffin go down into the ground of the serra she thought a thing that was the seed of everything: my father came back, at last, for good. And I brought him. And perhaps I'll stay too. It was the father's grave, more than the emptiness of the life over there, that made her stay; she came to bury the father and stayed beside him, in the only land where she now had someone of hers under the ground, which is a root no other equals.

Why had the mother never come back? Peregrina had asked her once, as a child, and again as a grown woman, and the mother always answered sideways, with something, there was no money, there was no time, your father was the one who went to see the family. But Peregrina, now, in the house in the village, was beginning to suspect the truth, and the truth was deeper than money and time: the mother had never come back because she did not want to spoil the photograph. As long as she did not go back, the village stayed intact, perfect, the vine bearing, the people alive, the house waiting. To go back was to risk seeing the vine dried, and the mother preferred to die with the vine alive in her head rather than see it dried with her eyes. Firmino went back, now and then, and went back and kept silent, did not tell his wife what he saw, left her her village of paper, out of love, because there are loves that are made of not undoing the other's illusion.

There was one Sunday, near the end of that autumn, when the call hurt more than the others. The mother was low, her voice small, and in the middle of the conversation, with no relevance to anything, she said the thing Peregrina never forgot:

"Daughter, sometimes I think I never should have left."

"Don't say that, mother."

"I should say it, because it's true. If I hadn't left, you'd be of there. You'd have a land. You wouldn't be going about now like this, split, not knowing where you're from. I did that to you. It's my fault, that left in search of a life and gave you a life with no land."

Peregrina stayed with the telephone against her cheek, in the grandparents' kitchen, with the afternoon light coming in sideways, and felt two things at once. She felt the mother's pain, who had carried for decades the guilt of having made of her daughter a foreigner, and that that guilt was the true mourning of emigration, the one that is not spoken, the one of having given the children a future and taken away a land. And she felt, beneath the pain, a new thing, that she did not yet know how to say but that she was beginning to guess: that the mother, even blaming herself, was still held by the same idea that held her, that belonging comes from the land. The mother thought that if she had stayed the daughter would belong, that the problem had been the leaving, that the land is what gives or takes belonging.

And Peregrina, that Sunday, with the mother crying quietly on the other side of the world, thought a thing she did not say, because she did not yet have the courage nor the words, but which stayed in her like a seed: and what if it's not so, mother? And what if belonging doesn't come from where one is born? And what if I, even having been born there, even having come back here, can belong, not because of the land, but because of a decision of mine? And what if home is not the one one leaves nor the one one inherits, it's the one one builds, and what if I can build it here, or anywhere, by force of wanting?

And there was, in that call and in all of them, a current that pulled her back, and Peregrina had to look at it head-on. The mother was alone, on the other side of the world, old, ill, at the window of a flat, and the daughter was all she had left. Would it not be more just, Peregrina thought on the bad nights, to go there, to keep the mother company in her last years, instead of standing here insisting on a village that called her tourist? Was that not a daughter's duty? And it cost her, and weighed on her, and there were Sundays when she almost decided to pack the bag again, now with a good reason, the best of all, the mother. But she understood, slowly, that to go there was not to go back to the mother, it was to enter with her into the same house of paper, it was to start living, she too, at the window, waiting for a land that did not exist, keeping a dead village in her head. The mother had chosen her life, had chosen to leave and not come back, had chosen the village of longing; and Peregrina could love her, telephone her, visit her, weep for her when the hour came, but did not have to repeat her life, did not have to inherit the mother's exile as she had inherited the frozen tongue. She could be a good daughter at a distance, and be, at the same time, a woman with a land of her own, a thing the mother had never had; and perhaps that was the greatest love she could give the mother: not to go and die at the window beside her, but to have, at last, in the family, someone who had arrived home, so that the mother could die knowing that the daughter, at least the daughter, had stopped going about looking.

And she understood, in that moment, with a tenderness that surprised her, that the mother had not given her a false land out of malice nor out of carelessness. She had given her the only land she had to give. A woman who emigrates carries in her breast the village of the day she left, and that is the one she cooks on Sundays, and that is the one she tells the children, because it is the only one she has; she cannot give the village of now, for that one is no longer hers, it stayed behind living without her. The mother had given her the possible village, the one of paper, the one of longing, and it had been a gift, even if a gift no good to live in, only to dream. And perhaps, thought Peregrina, her own work, the daughter's, was to take that gift of the mother's, the dreamed village, and make of it, at last, a real village, liveable, of flesh and stone, which the mother had never been able to make because she never came back; and to give the mother, one day, the news that the village of paper had become a real home, with the daughter inside it.

"You're not to blame for anything, mother," was what she said, aloud, with tenderness. "You gave me two lands. I'm the one who hasn't yet learned to live with that. But I'm learning."

The mother settled a little, they spoke of other things, of the mass, of a neighbour of over there who had fallen ill, and they said goodbye until the next Sunday. And when she hung up, Peregrina stayed a long time sitting in the kitchen, in the dark that was falling, with the seed of that thought growing inside her. The mother had taught her to look for home in the right land, first in the one of over there, then in the one of here, in the origin, always in the origin. And it was on hearing the mother blame herself for the leaving, on seeing the mother held, at seventy, by the same idea that held her at thirty-eight, that Peregrina began to suspect she was looking wrong. That she was going about, like the mother, blaming the leaving, waiting for a land to receive her. And that, perhaps, home was in no land waiting to be found, but yet to be made, waiting to be built, in the only place where a home is built: where one decides to stay.
