## 6. The Keeper of the divided entries

She went to see to the papers of the family house at the House of the Register, papers of one who returns and has to prove she is of here to be of here on the documents, and it was thus, by one more proof of non-belonging, that Peregrina found herself seated before the Keeper, and the Keeper showed her a thing that changed the book: that she was not alone, that half the village had entries split down the middle.

The Keeper, who saw people as she saw the entries, saw to the papers, and noticed Peregrina, her accent, her manner, and asked her, without wounding her, are you one of those who returned? And Peregrina, tired of explaining, gave the bitter answer: I am one of those who are from nowhere. I returned to the land of my parents and I am the tourist; if I returned to the land where I was born, I would be the emigrant. I am not of here nor of there. I belong nowhere. And the Keeper, who had heard that from many people, did not console her with lies, did better: she rose, and went to the shelves, and began to take down books, and to open them, and to show them to her.

And she said: look, Peregrina. Do you see this entry? It is of a man who went to Brazil at twenty and returned at sixty, and died saying he was of neither here nor there. And this? It is of a woman who went to France, and the children were born there, and returned, and are like you, divided. And this, and this, and this. Half this village, Peregrina, half the entries of this book, are of people who left and returned, or of children of those who left, and all of them, all, somewhere in this book, have your pain written, that of being of neither here nor there. You think you are the only one, that it is a fate yours alone, and you are surrounded by people just like you, a whole village half made of returners and of children of returners, all split down the middle, all feeling themselves from away, and all thinking themselves alone, because the shame of not belonging makes us silent, and by being silent we do not see that at our side is another the same.

And Peregrina, on seeing the divided entries, on understanding that half the village was like her, felt, for the first time, a thing she did not expect: she felt herself less alone. Because if her pain was the pain of half the village, then her pain did not set her apart, it put her, on the contrary, within, within a community she had not seen, the community of the divided, of those who returned, of those who have two lands and none; and that community, she understood, was also a land, a land made not of those who were born and stayed, but of those who left and returned, a land of the frontier, of the in-between place, that had as many people as the other, and to which she belonged by full right, because it was exactly what one had to be to belong to it: someone who is of neither here nor there.

And the Keeper said more, and it was what most shook her: you go about looking for a land that will receive you whole, Peregrina, and that land does not exist, neither for you nor for anyone, because no one is received whole by a land, not even those born here, who also hide halves, also do not all fit, also suffer. The belonging you look for, that of being of a land by origin, without striving, by root, that is an illusion, it is the belonging of children, the one we lose the moment we grow up; and the belonging in earnest, that of adults, is not that of where one is born, it is the one one builds, it is that of a land one chooses and to which one binds oneself, not because it is ours, but because we make it ours, living in it, working in it, making friendships in it, making memories in it, planting in it something that grows. You are not of here, it is true, you were not born here; but you can come to be of here, if you stay, if you bind yourself, if you build a life here, because to be of a land is not only to have been born in it, it is also to have chosen it and put roots in it, and that, that of one who chooses, is a belonging your cousin who was born here will never have, because she is of here by chance, and you can come to be of here by decision, which is more.

And the Keeper leafed still through the book, and showed her a last entry, and said: look at this one. It is of a woman who returned like you, divided, the tourist, the one from away; and she stayed; and after some years she already had here her friends, and the house mended, and a trade, and people who depended on her, and her own memories made here, and when she died, this entry says she died of the village, of here, of ours, because after some years the village was already hers, not because she was born, but because she stayed and built. Do you see? The belonging was not at the beginning, it was at the end; she was not of here when she arrived, she became of here by living. And you, Peregrina, can do the same, if you stop looking for the land that will receive you and begin to build the land to which you give yourself. The home you look for is nowhere waiting. It has to be made. And it is made where one decides to stay.

Peregrina left the House of the Register another, not because the pain had passed, but because the pain had changed meaning: it was no longer the pain of not belonging anywhere, it was the beginning of the perception that she could come to belong to one, not by origin, but by building; and she crossed the square, and looked at the fountain of Véspera, and the fountain said a thing to her, that the water it gave came from the mountain, from far, from away, and that no one asked the water whether it was of here, because the water, by running in the fountain of the village, by quenching the thirst of the village, by being part of the village, became of the village, wherever its source was; and that she, Peregrina, was like that water, came from far, from away, from two sources, but could, if she ran there, if she gave herself there, if she made her life there, become of the village, not by the source, which was from away, but by the course, which could be of here, if she decided to run there and not elsewhere.
