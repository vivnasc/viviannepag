## 6. The Keeper of the divided entries

She went to the House of the Register on account of a paper. One who comes back from away always has papers to see to, proofs to give of being of here in order to become of here on the documents, and Peregrina went to hand in who knows what of who knows what, one more proof of non-belonging, and it was thus, by a piece of paperwork, that she came to sit before the Keeper.

The House of the Register was cool and smelled of old paper and of wax. The Keeper was a woman of a certain age, her glasses on the tip of her nose, with that stillness of one who spends her life listening and writing down what she hears. She saw to the paper slowly, in an upright hand, and as she wrote she kept looking at Peregrina over her glasses, in no hurry, in the manner of one who reads a person the way she reads an entry.

"You talk differently, miss," she said, without lifting her face from the paper. "Are you one of those who came back?"

Peregrina, who had explained that a hundred times, and was tired of explaining it, gave the bitter answer, the true one, the one she never gave:

"I'm one of those who are from nowhere. I came back to my father's land and I'm the tourist. If I went back to the land where I was born, I'd be the emigrant. I'm not of here nor of there. I belong nowhere."

The Keeper set down her pen. She looked at her a moment, over her glasses, and did not console her, which was what everyone did, ah don't say that, miss, of course you're of here. She did another thing. She got up, slowly, with a hand to her back, and went to the shelves, the high ones, and began to take down books, one, two, three, thick black-covered books, and to bring them to the table, and to open them, and to search with her finger.

"Come here. Look."

Peregrina drew near. The Keeper had her finger laid on an entry, in an old hand, and read:

"This man went to Brazil at twenty. He came back at sixty-three, bought a house up there on the upper street, and died at seventy telling whoever would listen that he was of neither here nor there. I have him here, in the book, on both sides: in the one who left and the one who came back. Never whole." She turned the page, went to another book. "And this one. She went to France with her husband, the children were born there, they all came back. The children are like you, miss. Split down the middle. They come here to see to papers and they talk to me the way you talked to me just now, that they're from nowhere." She brought her open hand down, lightly, on the pile of books. "Half this village, miss. Half the entries I keep are of people who left and came back, or of children of those who left. You think you're the only one. And you're surrounded by people the same as you. A whole village half made of returners, all split down the middle, all feeling themselves of away, and all thinking themselves alone, because the shame of not belonging makes us hush, and by hushing we don't see that beside us is another the same."

Peregrina looked at the open books, the old letters, the names of people she did not know who had their pain written there, and felt, for the first time since she had arrived, a thing she had not expected: she felt herself less alone. Because if her pain was the pain of half the village, then her pain did not put her outside. It put her, on the contrary, within. Within a community she had not seen, that of the split, that of those who came back, that of those who have two lands and none; and that community was also a land, a land of the border, with as many people as the other, and to that one she belonged by full right, because it was exactly what one had to be in order to belong to it: someone who is of neither here nor there.

The Keeper sat down again, and took off her glasses, and cleaned them on the tip of her apron, and said, more slowly, the thing Peregrina took home and never again let go of:

"You go about looking for a land that will receive you whole. And that land doesn't exist, neither for you nor for anyone. No one is received whole by a land. Not even those born here, who also hide halves, who also don't all fit, who also suffer. The belonging you're looking for, that of being of a land of origin, effortless, by root, that's a belonging of childhood, and it's lost the moment one grows. The belonging in earnest, the grown-ups', is not the one of where one is born. It's the one that's built. It's the one of a land we choose and to which we tie ourselves, not because it's ours, but because we make it ours, by living in it, working in it, making friendships in it, making memories in it, planting in it something that grows." She put her glasses back on. "You're not of here. That's true. You weren't born here. But you can come to be of here, if you stay, if you tie yourself, if you build a life here. And that belonging, that of one who chooses, is a belonging your cousin who was born here will never have, because she's of here by accident. You can come to be of here by decision. And by decision it's more."

She then searched for a thing, carefully, in a more recent hand, and turned the book to Peregrina without saying anything, her finger on a line. It was the father's name. Firmino, of the house up top, left in such a year, and there, further down, along the years, the returns, came back, came back, came back, all the holidays marked, the man who went and came, always of here. And beside it, the mother. Dores, married Firmino, left in the same year. And only that. Left, and never again a single line of came back. The mother was in the book of Véspera only on the side of those who leave, never on the side of those who come back, because she had never come back.

"Your mother is here as a half, miss," said the Keeper, low. "Only on the side of the leaving. Your father is whole, because he came back. And you, now, are writing your own line. You can stay like your mother, only on the leaving, keeping a land of paper on the other side of the world. Or you can put, beneath the left, the thing your mother never put, and that there's still time for: came back, and stayed."

Peregrina stayed looking at the mother's name, alone in the column of the leaving, and felt a deep pity for the mother, and a fear of being like her, and the two at once gave her, when she did not expect it, a new urge to stay.

The Keeper leafed once more through one of the books, searched, and turned it to Peregrina.

"And look at this one. It's of a woman who came back like you, split, the one from away, the tourist. And she stayed. At the end of a few years she already had her friends here, and the house done up, and a trade, and people who depended on her, and memories of her own, made here. When she died, her entry says she died of the village, of ours, of here. Not because she'd been born. Because she stayed and built. See? The belonging wasn't in the beginning. It was at the end. She wasn't of here when she arrived. She became of here by living." She leafed back, and the finger stopped on another line, and the voice changed. "And look at this one, which is the reverse. Another who came back, and couldn't bear the doubt. At the first winter, the first time they called him the one from America, he packed his bags and went off again, and then from there again to here, and from here to somewhere else, always looking for the land that would receive him with open arms, and he never found it, because it doesn't exist, and he died in a boarding house of some city or other, of nowhere at all, with his entry split in three places and whole in none. The difference between the two, miss, wasn't luck. It was that one stayed and the other fled. Those who stay become of here. Those who flee at the first doubt stay of away for ever, and the fault isn't the lands', it's the hurry to leave." She closed the book carefully. "The home you're looking for isn't anywhere waiting. It has to be made. And it's made where one decides to stay."

And she said, as she closed the book, a thing Peregrina did not expect, low, like one who confides a secret: I know what I'm talking about, miss, it's not only from the books. I too was born away. My parents emigrated, and I was born there, and I came here as a small child, and for years I was the foreigner, the one who talked odd, the one who doesn't take. And today I'm the Keeper of the Register of this village, the one who keeps the memory of everyone, the one who says who's of here and who's not. Me, the one from away. Do you know how that happened? I stayed. I stayed and stayed and stayed, until the village forgot that I wasn't of here. Belonging, miss, is a stubbornness. It's won by whoever stays longer than the village takes to doubt. And she looked at Peregrina over her glasses, and there was in her eyes, which had read so many lives, a tenderness of one who recognises her own in another's. Don't give up at their doubt. Their doubt tires before your stubbornness, if your stubbornness is great.

Peregrina left the House of the Register another woman. Not because the pain had passed, but because it had changed direction: it was no longer the pain of belonging nowhere, it was the beginning of the perception that she could come to belong somewhere, not by origin, by building. And she now carried a living proof that it was possible: the Keeper herself, the one from away who had become the owner of the village's memory. She crossed the square slowly, with the seen-to paper in her hand, and stopped beside the fountain, and stayed looking at it.

The fountain of Véspera ran as it had always run. And Peregrina, that time, looked at it thinking of what the Keeper had said, and the fountain told her the thing that was missing, in the mute way fountains tell things to one who is ready to hear them. The water that ran there came from the serra, from far, from another side, from a spring no one saw. And no one asked the water if it was of here. No one said to that water, which came from away, go back to your own land. Because the water, by running in the fountain of the village, by quenching the village's thirst, by washing the faces of the men who came from the fields, became of the village, whatever the spring it was from. It was of here by its course, not by its origin.

And Peregrina did a thing she had not done since she was a child: she went up to the spout, cupped her hands, and drank. The water was cold, of the serra, and tasted to her of an old thing, of a summer thirty years ago, of the grandmother giving her to drink from that same fountain one hot afternoon. She drank from the fountain of the village, she who had her own water at home, she who had come from a city of taps, and in drinking that water that came from far and became of here by running in the square, it was as if she made, without any ceremony, a small vow: I too come from far, I too can become of here if I run here long enough. She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand, and stayed a while longer listening to the water, and for once did not think she was from away; she thought the water was too, and no one sent it off.

And Peregrina understood, beside the fountain, that she was like that water. She came from far, from away, from two springs. But she could, if she ran there, if she gave herself there, if she made a life there, become of the village, not by the spring, for that was of away and was always to be so, but by the course, which could be of here, if she decided to run there and not somewhere else. She put her hand in the water, which was cold, and let it run between her fingers a while, and went home with a new thing in her breast, which was not yet belonging, but was the beginning of it: the suspicion that perhaps she did not have to find her land, perhaps she only had to choose it, and to stay in it long enough for it, slowly, like the water on the stone, to come to call her its own.
