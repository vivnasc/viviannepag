## 1. The one from here and the one from away

Peregrina returned for good to her parents' village at thirty-eight, after a life abroad, and thought she was returning home, and discovered, on arriving, that she was returning to a place where she was as much a foreigner as everywhere else, because home, she understood slowly, was not that one, was none, was a thing she had never had and went looking for on the wrong map.

Abroad, in the country to which her parents had emigrated and where she had been born, Peregrina had always been the Portuguese one. However well she spoke the language of there without an accent, however much she went to the school of there, however much her friends were of there, there was always a thing that set her apart: the name hard to pronounce, the different food her mother made, the smells of home that were not those of other houses, the Sundays talking to the family in Portugal on the telephone, the holidays spent in a land her schoolmates could not place on the map. And when they quarrelled with her, at school, in the playground, there always came, at the end, the words that put her in her place: go back to your own country. And Peregrina, a child, went home thinking which was her country, because the one where she was born, they told her, was not it; and the other, Portugal, she barely knew, only from the holidays; and she was left with no country at all, a girl without ground, the one from here treated as the one from there.

And she thought, her whole life, that the problem was being abroad, that if she returned to Portugal, to her parents' land, to the village they had left, then she would belong, then she would finally have a country, then she would stop being the one from away and become, at last, the one from here. And it was with that hope, more than with anything else, that Peregrina, after her father died and her life abroad grew empty, decided to return for good, to sell what she had, to pack her bags, and to settle in her parents' village, in the house the family still had there, convinced that she was returning to her own land, that she was going, at last, to belong.

And she arrived, and was received, and at first it was good, it was a feast, the relative who returned from emigration, and the cousins embraced her, and the aunt killed the hen, and there was a lunch, and Peregrina felt, on that first day, that she had done right, that she had come home. But the feeling lasted little, because already at the first lunch she began to notice the things, the small things that told her no, that she was not of there: the way they said certain words, which she said another way, and the cousins laughed, not with malice, but they laughed; the references everyone had and she did not, the names of the people of the village, the old stories, the sayings, the family quarrels of thirty years back that she had not lived; and Peregrina, in the midst of the lunch of the family that received her, felt the thing she had come to flee and that awaited her there too: she felt herself from away.

And it went on confirming itself, in the days that followed, that the village was not the home she had imagined. In the village, Peregrina was the one from away. She spoke differently, with an accent that was of nowhere, half of here half of there, and people noticed, and asked her where she was from, and when she said from here, I am the daughter of Joaquim and Conceição, who emigrated, people said ah, and in that ah was everything, was the you are of the emigration, you are from away, you are not quite of here; and Peregrina, who had returned to the land fleeing the you are from away, heard the same you are from away in the land where she thought she would be, at last, from within.

And she began to understand, with a tightening, that she had been mistaken, that the home she sought was not in her parents' village, as it had not been in the country where she was born, that she was from away on both sides, a foreigner of here and of there, with two lands and none, and that returning had solved nothing, only changed the place where she felt herself from away. There, she was the Portuguese one; here, the one of the emigration. There, she missed Portugal; here, she missed the country where she was born, the friends of there, the ways of there, which after all were also hers, and which, being here, she now missed. And Peregrina found herself in the middle, in an in-between place, belonging to neither of the two banks, with her heart divided, half there half here, and whole in neither, and understood that the problem was not the land, it was her, it was a thing she carried within and that changed land with her, the sense of being from nowhere.

And there was a night, soon after arriving, on which Peregrina sat in the village house, her parents' house, the house that ought to be hers, and looked around, and the house was full of the family's things, but had nothing of hers, nothing she recognised as her own, and she felt herself there as much a foreigner as she had felt her whole life in the house of there, and she made, for the first time, the question that was to cross the book: if I am not of there, and I am not of here, where am I from? Where is my home? To what land do I belong? And she did not know how to answer, because the answer, which she did not yet know, was not a land, it was a thing she had not yet learned to do, and which is not done to the land that falls to us, it is done to the land we choose, and which has a name that Peregrina, that night, did not yet know how to say: to take root.
