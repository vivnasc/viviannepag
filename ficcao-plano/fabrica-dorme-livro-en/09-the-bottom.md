## 9. The bottom

But between the seed and the spring there is the winter, and before Libânia learned to inhabit life there was the bottom, there was the morning she did not get up, and that morning was the lowest point of her whole life.

It is fitting to say, so that it be understood, that Libânia had never been a person to let herself be cast down. She had crossed hard things in her life — the poverty of a girl, the death of the parents, the widowhood, the years of announced closings in which she slept three hours a night — and had crossed them all on her feet, working, because the work had always been the crutch that kept her standing in every misfortune: however bad it was, there was the shift, there was the factory to open, there were the machines, and as long as there was that she had something to hold on to. The work had been her way of not thinking, of not feeling, of not collapsing; before any pain, Libânia threw herself into the work, and the work saved her, not because it resolved the pain, but because it smothered it, left it no room. And what made that winter so dangerous, more dangerous than all the previous misfortunes, is that they had taken from her precisely the crutch: for the first time in her life, Libânia had a pain with no work in which to hide it, a pain loose, with no shift to smother it, and she discovered, then, that she had never learned to suffer, because every time she had suffered she had worked instead of suffering, and that now, without work, all the pain she had not suffered over a lifetime came to her at once, with nothing to contain it, and it was that, the pain put off of a whole lifetime, that crushed her against the mattress that morning.

It had been forming for weeks, the bottom, though Libânia did not notice it, as one does not notice the tide rising until the water reaches our knees. She had stopped arranging tasks, first, because even invented tasks wear out, and a house can only be scrubbed so many times. Then she had stopped seeking work, because there were no longer doors to knock at. Then she had begun to eat little, to eat standing, not to make food only for herself because to cook for a person who did not yield even that did not seem to her to deserve it. And finally she had begun to have no wish for anything, to spend afternoons sitting without doing and without resting, in a state that was not repose, it was absence, and Libânia, who had always known how to distinguish the good tiredness of a day's work, did not recognise that new tiredness, that did not come from doing and that no rest cured, because it was the tiredness of having nothing to live for, the deepest of all.

It was a morning like the others of that winter: the five, the body waking for the factory that was not there. But that morning, instead of getting up and throwing herself into the struggle against the empty hours, Libânia stayed lying down. Not out of laziness, not out of rest: out of a thing deeper and darker, a thing she had never felt in sixty years, and that was the question what for. What for get up, if there is nowhere to go? What for fill one more day with invented things to pretend that I have something to do? What for exist, if I am good for nothing, if the one thing I knew how to do has ended, if no one needs me, if I have nothing to prove to anyone and therefore, in the account I learned at twelve years old, I have no reason to be on my feet?

And she stayed lying down, the whole morning, in the dark of the room, with the curtains closed, and it was the first time in her life that Libânia did not get up in the morning, and it was terrifying, because she realised, lying there, that she was doing what Engrácia had done, that she was entering the road that had made Engrácia ill, the road of one who, the work taken away, finds no reason to get up, and wastes away, and lets themselves go. And she realised, with a cold terror, that it was possible to die of that, to die of having nothing to live for, and that she, the strong one, the forewoman, the one who had held the factory through every closing, was there, lying down at eleven in the morning in the dark, nearer to letting herself die than she had ever been, defeated not by the factory, but by the discovery that without the factory she did not know what to exist for.

There passed through her head, that morning, the darkest thing that had ever passed through it, and it passed quietly, without drama, which is how dark things pass through the head of one who is at the bottom: the thought that perhaps it would be easier not to be. It was not a wish to die, it was worse, it was an indifference toward living, a what for go on so great that it dissolved the border between being and not being, and Libânia, who had always had a horror of weakness, was frightened by the calm with which that thought settled in, without revolting her, as though it were already part of the landscape. She remembered Engrácia in bed, Engrácia's still gaze, and realised that Engrácia's still gaze was that, was a person who had ceased to find reasons to be there, and that she herself was now with that same gaze turned to the ceiling of the room, and that between her and Engrácia there was no longer any difference, the forewoman and the worker made equal by the same dark, and that perhaps it was that, in the end of everything, the fate of one who made of work the only reason to live: to be left, without it, with no reason at all.

How long she stayed so, she could not say. She lost count of the hours, she who had counted hours her whole life, and it was perhaps that loss of count the deepest sign of how far she was, because a Libânia who loses count of the hours is a Libânia who has already let go of all the moorings. The room went on gaining and losing light behind the closed curtains, and she did not move, and the hunger came and passed without her heeding it, and the thirst too, and there was a moment, in the middle of that endless morning, when Libânia thought, with a coldness that would frighten her if she still had strength to be frightened, that if she stayed there long enough perhaps there would simply cease to be a Libânia, and that that did not seem to her bad, it seemed to her rest, and it was that finding rest in the disappearing that marked her for ever, because it was the proof of how deep a person can sink to whom they have taken the one thing that made them want to be.

It was Felismina who saved her, that morning, and saved her in a way that was itself the lesson. Felismina, who went about making sweets and being reborn, marvelled at not seeing Libânia, and went to her house, and knocked, and Libânia did not open, and Felismina, who knew that dark because she too had crossed it, did not go away: she insisted, came in, opened the curtains, and found Libânia lying down, with her eyes open, in the dark of midday, and understood everything, and sat on the edge of the bed, and said a thing that Libânia carried for the rest of her life.

Felismina was not frightened by what she saw, and that, for Libânia, was the first thing that helped. Everyone who visited her that winter was frightened, or made a scene, or told her that she had to react, that a woman like her could not be so, and those reactions, well-intentioned, only weighed on her more, because they asked her to get up to reassure those who saw her, and she had no strength to reassure anyone. Felismina did not. Felismina opened the curtains slowly, let in only a little light, and sat on the edge of the bed without haste, without alarm, like one who arrives at a place they know, and it was that calm, the calm of one who has been there and knows that one comes out, that began to reach Libânia through the dark. Felismina did not tell her get up, did not tell her you have to react, did not tell her anything the others said. She stayed. And only at the end of a while, with her hand in hers, did she speak.

"I know what that is, Libânia," said Felismina. "I have been there, when the factory closed. I was lying down as you are, asking what for. And I am going to tell you what took me out of there, which was not getting work, it was another thing. It was realising that I had spent my life asking what I was good for, and that that was the wrong question, the question that had been put into my head by a world that only values us for what we produce. Things do not all have to be good for something, Libânia. A flower is good for nothing and no one asks it what it is good for. A sunset is good for nothing. An afternoon doing nothing is good for nothing. And they are among the most beautiful things there are. I learned, with the closing, to stop asking what I am good for and to begin asking what I like, and it was that which got me out of bed: not a reason to be useful, but a will to live, which is a different thing. Get up, Libânia. Not to be useful. To live. Come and make sweets with me, not to sell them, to eat them, for it is so long since you did a thing only for the pleasure of doing it that you have forgotten that that exists."

Libânia did not answer at once. She stayed lying down, with Felismina sitting on the edge of the bed, and there was a long silence, in which she wanted to say that she could not, that she had no strength, that what for. But Felismina did not let go of her hand, and said nothing more, she stayed there, only, keeping her company in the dark, and it was that company, more than the words, that began to open a chink of light, because Libânia, who had spent her life supporting everyone and never letting them support her, felt, that morning, for the first time in sixty years, what it was to be supported, to have someone who stayed at her side without asking her for anything, who did not want her yielding nor serving nor proving anything at all, who only wanted her to get up and live. And that experience, that of being supported without having done anything to deserve it, was almost as strange and as hard to accept as everything else, because she did not know how to receive, she only knew how to give, and to receive Felismina's hand was, in its small way, the first time Libânia let herself be wanted only for being who she was, lying down and useless and yielding nothing, and she discovered, with astonishment, that Felismina stayed all the same.

They went to make sweets, that afternoon, in Felismina's kitchen, and it was the strangest thing Libânia had done in her life: to be making a thing not to yield. Her hands, used to producing, did not know how to handle that dough for pleasure, and at first she did everything in a rush, with the efficiency of the factory, wanting to dispatch it, and Felismina laughed and said slowly, Libânia, this is not to finish, it is to make, and Libânia had to oblige herself to slow her hands, which wanted to go quickly, and it was in that slowing of the hands, ridiculous and small, a woman of sixty years learning to stir a sweet slowly, that something deep began, because it was the body unlearning the haste, accepting that there were things one does without a useful end, only for the pleasure of the making. She got herself dirty with flour, tasted the raw dough with her finger as children do, laughed with Felismina at a thing of no importance, and at a certain point, in the midst of the laughter, she stopped, astonished at herself, because she realised that she was laughing, that she was enjoying herself, she who that morning had been lying down thinking it was better not to be, and she realised that it was possible, then, to pass from the dark to the flour in one afternoon, that the bottom was not a place where one stays trapped, it was a place from which one can come out, if someone gives us their hand and some sweets to make.

And Libânia got up, that morning, not because she had found a reason to be useful, but because Felismina held out her hand and pulled her out of the dark, and it was the first time in her life that Libânia got up from bed not to work, but only to live, to be with a friend, to make a thing for the pleasure, and it was little, and it was everything, and it was the beginning of the climb. Years later, when she was already on the other side, Libânia was to think many times of that morning, and to tell of it, to whoever needed to hear it, as the most important day of her life, and was to astonish whoever heard her, because it was the day she had been nearest to letting herself die, and how could the worst day be the most important. But it was, and Libânia knew why: because it had been at the bottom, and only at the bottom, that the old Libânia had at last spent herself. As long as she had had strength to arrange tasks, to knock at doors, to pretend, the disease had stayed standing, disguised as bustle; it took her being left with no strength at all, lying in the dark unable to get up, for the bustle to end and what was beneath to show itself whole. The bottom took from her the last flight. There was no longer a task to invent, no longer a door to knock at, no longer a way not to feel, and it was there, all the flights spent, that she had at last to look face on at the thing she had fled her whole life, and to look at it face on was the beginning of letting it go. That is why the bottom was important: not in spite of being the bottom, but because it was the bottom, because only there, in the place where there is no longer anything to do, is a person who did their whole life obliged, at last, only to be, even if it is to be a person lying in the dark, yielding nothing, and to discover, with astonishment, that even so they go on existing.

Because the bottom, Libânia realised, is not where one stays: it is where one sets one's feet to get up again, and she had set her feet at the bottom that morning, in the question what for, and had discovered, with Felismina's help, that the answer was not a reason to be useful, but a will to live, and that that will, however small it was, however much she had to relearn it from the beginning, was enough to get up, and that to get up to live, and not to work, was the new thing she was going to have to learn to do every morning, from then on, at five, or later, at the hour she felt like, because there was no longer a shift, no longer a factory, no longer a what for, there was only, if she learned it, the pleasure of seeing one more day.
