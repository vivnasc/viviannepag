## 10. Five in the morning, of another thing

To inhabit life, Libânia discovered, was a trade like the others, and like the others it was learned slowly, with knack and with relapses, and she went on learning it, that spring that was coming, gesture by gesture, hour by hour, with Felismina for a master and the will to live for a tool.

And there was an irony in that, that Libânia did not fail to notice and that even drew from her, sometimes, a smile: that she, the best worker of the Spinner, the one who had learned all the trades of the factory and done them better than anyone, was now, at sixty years old, making the hardest learning of her life, and it was the learning of not doing. She took up the thing with the seriousness with which she had taken up everything, and found herself treating the inhabiting as she had treated the spinning, wanting to learn it well, wanting to be good at that too, and Felismina laughed at her and told her that she was wanting to be the best pupil at doing nothing, that that was still the factory talking, and Libânia laughed, and realised that she was right, that even to unlearn competing she competed, and that the road was long, but that at least now she laughed at herself, and to laugh at herself was, she realised, a thing she had never done in her life, because a person who takes themselves as seriously as she had taken herself, who makes of their own life a permanent proof, has no room at all for laughter about themselves, and that new laughter, mild, about her own exaggerations, was perhaps the first sign that she was healing.

Felismina was a master to her without ever giving a lesson, only by example and by a phrase or two said at the right moment. It was a way of teaching that Libânia, used to the factory, where one was taught to command and to correct, at first did not even recognise as teaching: Felismina did not tell her do it so, she showed her how she did it, and let Libânia follow when she felt like it, without hurrying her, without judging her, without ever telling her that she was doing it badly. And it was that absence of judgement, Libânia discovered, that went on curing her, because she had spent her life being judged and judging, measuring everything by what yielded and what did not yield, and to be beside someone who did not measure her, who expected no result from her, who liked her company whether she had done much or nothing, went on teaching her, slowly, in the body, a thing that no word would teach her: that one could be accepted without being judged, wanted without yielding, and that that, far from being laziness, was perhaps the cleanest form of one person being with another.

She began with the five in the morning, which were the heart of the wound. She went on waking at five, the body did not unlearn in a few months what it had learned in sixty years, but instead of getting up distressed for a factory that was not there, or of staying lying down in the dark asking what for, Libânia began to do a new thing: she got up, made a tea, and went to watch the sunrise. She sat at the window, or at the door when it was fine, with the tea in her hands, and watched the day be born over the village, over the river, over the closed Spinner in the distance, and at first that seemed to her a waste, a thing of people with nothing to do, and her conscience hurt at being there idle producing nothing at five in the morning. But she went on getting used to it, and went on discovering, slowly, that the sunrise was beautiful, that it had colours she had never seen in sixty years of factory, that each morning was different, and that to watch the day be born was good for nothing and was, exactly for that, one of the purest things one could do with an hour: to live it, without working it, without deserving it, only to be in it, watching the light come.

There was one morning, of those first ones, when she watched an especially beautiful sun be born, the whole sky the colour of embers over the river, and Libânia, alone at the door with the tea, felt a thing she had not felt since she was a girl, if she had ever felt it: a joy with no motive. She was not happy about anything, she had done nothing, there was no reason at all for that joy, and it was exactly that which made it new and almost frightening — a joy that was not the prize of anything, that did not come from a task fulfilled nor from a work well done, that came only from being alive watching the sky burn. She wept, with the tea in her hands, but it was a weeping different from all those of that winter, it was a weeping of one who finds, late, a thing that had been hidden from them their whole life, and Libânia realised, that morning, weeping from joy at the door of her house before a dawn, that she had lived sixty years without ever having known that this existed: that one could be happy for free, without deserving it, only for being in the world on a beautiful morning, and that that free happiness, which she would have despised as a thing of idle people, was perhaps the most serious and most true thing life had to give.

The five in the morning, which had been the factory's, and then the void's, and then the what-for's, went on becoming, that spring, of another thing: they went on becoming the place where Libânia learned to exist. Each morning at sunrise was a small lesson of inhabiting, an hour in which she practised the hardest thing, which was to be alive without doing anything to deserve it, and it was on those mornings, more than in any conversation, that the knot went on untying itself, because there are things one does not learn with the head, one learns them with the body, and Libânia's body went on learning, morning after morning, at sunrise, that one could be on one's feet at five without it being to work, that one could exist without producing, that the day was born for her as it was born for Venâncio, as it was born for everyone, free, without her having to deserve it.

It did not go straight, of course, for these things never go straight. There were relapses. There were mornings when the old tightness came back with all its force, when to watch the sun rise seemed to her again a waste of time, a shame, and when Libânia got up from the chair in the middle of the tea with the old urgency to do something, anything, so as not to be idle. There were days when the peace of the day before undid itself for no reason and she caught herself scrubbing the floor at seven in the morning, back to the flight. But the difference, now, and it was an enormous difference, is that she already knew what that was, already put a name to it — it is the fear of not being worth anything, she said to herself, it is the girl of twelve years afraid of being the mouth too many again —, and to put a name to it took half its force, because a distress with a name is no longer an abyss, it is an old acquaintance, and Libânia went on learning to treat hers as one treats an insistent acquaintance: without fleeing it, without obeying it, letting it pass. She went back to the chair. She took up the tea again. And she began again to learn, from the point where she had stumbled, because Felismina had warned her that this was not a thing one learns once and it stays, it was a thing one relearns every day, and that to relapse was not to fail, it was only part of learning.

She made a kitchen-garden. Not to sell, not for anything useful, though it gave vegetables: she made a kitchen-garden because Felismina told her that to work the earth did good, and Libânia, who had never had time for a kitchen-garden, discovered, that spring, the pleasure of putting a seed in the earth and of watching it grow, slowly, without haste, a pleasure that was the opposite of the work of the factory, because at the factory she produced, and in the kitchen-garden she cared, and to care for a thing that grows at its own rhythm, without shifts, without whistles, without proof, was a way of inhabiting time that the factory had never taught her. The kitchen-garden had the rhythm of life, not that of production, and Libânia, on entering that rhythm, went on entering, without noticing, the rhythm of existing, which is slow, which is free, which is not measured in pieces produced but in mornings lived.

She learned, slowly, to do things that were good for nothing, which was Felismina's lesson. It was hard at first, harder than it seems, because each time she sat down to do nothing, or to do a thing only for pleasure, the old voice came to tell her that she was wasting time, that that was proper to idle people, that she ought to be doing something useful. And Libânia had to learn to argue with that voice, to answer it that to waste time was, after all, a way of having time, that a whole life spent not wasting it had been a life without ever having had it. She sat down to listen to the river, one afternoon, only to listen to the river, and at first the distress was so much that she almost got up, and then it passed, and she stayed there a whole hour listening to the water doing nothing, and when she got up she felt, instead of the guilt she expected, a kind of peace, as though that wasted hour had been, of all the hours of her life, one of the few truly lived, because it had been the first in a long time in which she had been only present, without producing, without proving, without owing.

And she came to be with the workers, but in another way. She was no longer the forewoman, and it cost her at first to cease being it, to cease feeling herself responsible for all of them, to want to command them, to want to hold them; but she went on learning to be with them as a friend, not as a chief, to listen to them instead of directing them, to share the end instead of managing it, and she discovered, in that change, an intimacy with the former workers that she had never had while she had been the forewoman, because the forewoman is always a step above, and the friend is at the side, and at the side one knows people in a way that from above one never knows them. It cost her, this change, more than it seems, because to cease being the forewoman was to cease having a clear place in the world, and Libânia, without knowing it, liked to command, liked to be the one who knows, the one who resolves, the one the others seek. To command had been, her whole life, one of the ways of feeling herself important, and to renounce commanding was to renounce that importance, to come down from the step, to put herself at the level of the others, and there were days when she caught herself giving orders at the workers' table out of old habit, organising their sweets as she had organised the shifts, and she had to bite her tongue, to fall silent halfway, to learn to swallow the order that already came to her mouth and to replace it with a question, and what do you like to do instead of do it so. It was a whole re-education, that of one who unlearns to command to learn to accompany, and Libânia, who had re-educated so many workers at the factory, discovered that the hardest pupil she had ever had was herself, and that to re-educate oneself, at sixty years old, against the habits of a life, was the slowest and most thankless work she had ever put her hands to, and also, she realised, the most important, because it was the only one whose product was herself.

She drew near again to Engrácia, the one who had sunk, and instead of trying to put her to work, as the old Libânia would have done, she sat with her, and was there, and went on teaching her, slowly, what Felismina had taught her, which was that one did not need to serve to exist, that one could have one's hands idle and be alive, and Engrácia, with Libânia's company, went on coming out of the bottom, slowly, as Libânia had come out, and it was that, perhaps, the thing that most gave Libânia back the sense of being worth something: not to go back to working, but to help Engrácia learn to live without work, to be useful in a new way, not by producing, but by supporting, not as a forewoman, but as a friend.

"Do you know what is strangest, Felismina?" said Libânia, one morning, the two of them making sweets, not to sell, to eat. "It is that I, now, do less than I did my whole life, and I feel more..." — she sought the word — "more whole. How can that be? I worked sixty years and always felt myself owing; now I do almost nothing and I feel, for the first time, as though I owe nothing to anyone. How does one do less and be worth more?"

And Felismina laughed, and said: it is not that you are worth more, Libânia. It is that you were always worth the same, and only now you have noticed. The work never made you worth more; it only made you feel that you had to deserve it. Now that you have stopped deserving it, you see what was always there: that you are worth something, and that is that, and that it has nothing to do with what you do. You took sixty years to stop working enough to see it. But you saw it. Better late.

They were silent stirring the sweet, and Libânia thought of the question she had asked — how does one do less and be worth more — and realised, slowly, that the question was still badly put, that it still contained the disease, because it measured the being-worth by the doing, whether much or little. The true answer, which she was beginning to feel and did not yet know how to say, was that the being-worth had nothing to do with the doing, neither much nor little, that they were two separate things she had spent her life confusing, and that the day she ceased to link them — when she could do much or nothing and be worth exactly the same in both — would be the day she would be, at last, cured. She was not there yet. But in that kitchen full of the steam of sweets, beside Felismina, with her hands all smeared and yielding nothing useful, Libânia was nearer that cure than in sixty years of factory, and she knew it, and kept that afternoon as one keeps a date, because it was the afternoon she realised that one could be alive, and well, and whole, without doing anything that was good for something.
