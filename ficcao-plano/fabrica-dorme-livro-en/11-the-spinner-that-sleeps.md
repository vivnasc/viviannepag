## 11. The Spinner that sleeps

There was one morning, that spring, when Libânia went back to the Spinner, and it was the first time she had gone back there since the day she had handed over the key, and she went back not to weep the factory, but to make peace with it.

She had not planned to do it that day. She had woken at five, as always, made the tea, watched the sun rise, and in the middle of the morning, without deciding, she found herself walking toward the river, toward the Spinner, with her feet taking her there again, as in the first days of the closing they had taken her halfway down the cobbled street. But that time it was different: that time she was not led by longing nor by blind habit, she was led by a serene wish to go, to see, to take her leave properly. She was no longer the Libânia of the first days, who went to the factory without thinking because the body pulled her; she was a Libânia who chose to go, with eyes to see, to look face on at what for months had terrified her too much to look at. And she noticed that difference, on walking, and she herself took it for a measure of how far she had come: that the same path that in winter had humiliated her — the forewoman dragging herself to a dead factory — led her now, in spring, with her head raised, of her own will, to do a thing she had chosen. The path was the same. The one who walked it was already another.

The factory was closed, of course, and from outside the abandonment could already be seen coming: the weeds growing in the courtyard, the dull windows, the faded sign. But Libânia knew a way to get in, and she got in, and walked through the closed Spinner, through the factory that slept, and it was a stroll through her whole life, because each corner of that factory had a memory, each stopped machine had a name, and Libânia walked about there slowly, in the silence, passing her hand over the cold machines, remembering the girls who had worked there, the shifts, the whistles, the forty-three years of her life that were kept in that corpse of a factory.

She had wanted to come, and had put it off a long time, because while she had been at the bottom she would not have borne that silence, and even afterwards, already learning to inhabit, she had avoided the Spinner as one avoids a mourning one does not yet have strength to do. But that spring she felt, one morning, that she was ready, that she needed to go back inside one last time, not to bind herself again to the past, but precisely to be able to let it go, because there are things one only lets go of after one takes them up again carefully and says goodbye to them. She went to Preciosa, who still had a service key kept from who knew what arrangement, and Preciosa, without asking what for, understood, and lent it her, and said only: go gently, Libânia, for that in there moves one. And it moved her.

She went in by the small door, that of the office, and the smell was the first thing to receive her, that smell of wool and of oil and of dust that was the smell of her whole adult life, and that was still there, clinging to the walls, waiting for her, and Libânia had to lean against a wall, on feeling it, because the smell brought her everything at once, the mornings, the girls, the youth, the years, and it was like opening a door into herself. She walked through the big hall, where the light came in dull through the dirty windows and fell on the stopped machines as it falls on things in abandoned places, a light of an end, and she heard her own steps echo in the silence, she who for forty-three years had never heard her own steps there because of the noise, and it was that detail, the hearing herself walk, that told her more than anything that that had truly ended.

She stopped in the middle of the hall and did a thing she had not planned: she spoke aloud, alone, to the empty factory, and said the names. The names of the girls who had entered there with her, fifty years ago, girls as she had been, many already dead, others scattered across the village and the world. She said them one by one, slowly, like a prayer, and the hall gave her back her voice in an echo that was almost as though all the girls answered, and Libânia realised that that factory had not been only machines and shifts, it had been a place where whole lives passed, where girls had made themselves women and women had made themselves old, where there had been courtships at the end of the shift, friendships for life, quarrels, marriages arranged at the gate, children announced in secret among the looms, deaths wept together. The Spinner had been the house of a whole people, and to close it had been to close a house full of beloved ghosts, and Libânia, on saying the names aloud in the empty hall, made for all of them, and for herself, a wake they had never had, because a factory that closes has no funeral, it stays only there to rot without anyone weeping it by name, and she wept it, that morning, with all the names, and it was that, she realised, the farewell she needed to make.

And at first the pain came, of course, the pain of seeing dead what had been so alive, the pain of knowing that that would never go again, that the world that had lived there had ended. But the pain, that morning, instead of crushing her as it had crushed her in winter, gave way, slowly, to another thing: to a gratitude, and to a farewell. Because Libânia, in the midst of the factory that slept, realised a thing that freed her: that the Spinner had given her a good life. It had taken her out of poverty, given her a trade, a place, a pride, sustenance for the children, meaning for the days; the Spinner had been good for her, and she for the Spinner, and forty-three years together were not a thing to weep, they were a thing to be grateful for. The factory had not betrayed her by closing: it had fulfilled its time, as everything fulfils its time, and ended, as everything ends, and Libânia, in the midst of it, instead of weeping it as a victim weeps what was taken from them, thanked it as one thanks a good thing one had and that ended.

She stopped before the Stubborn One, the machine she had christened at fifteen years old, now silent for ever, covered in dust, and passed her hand over the cold iron, and it was like touching a dead companion. She spoke to it low, without shame, because there was no one there: we worked together, she said, you and I, a whole life, and it was good, our life. You did not complain, I did not complain. We gave wool to half the village. And now the two of us sleep, you for good, I for a few hours, for I still get up tomorrow. And on speaking with the dead machine she realised that she was not speaking only with the machine: she was taking her leave of a version of herself, of the Libânia-machine, of the Libânia who for forty-three years had been as much of iron and as tireless as the Stubborn One, and that that Libânia too was going to stay there, in that hall, sleeping with the machines, because to come out of the factory alive she had to leave inside the part of herself that was only factory, and take with her only the part that was a person.

And she realised, above all, the thing she had come there to realise: that she was not the Spinner. That the factory was one thing, and she was another, and that the factory could die without her dying, because she was not the factory, she was Libânia, and Libânia survived the Spinner, as she had survived the father, as she would survive everything that was not herself. During the winter, Libânia had confused herself with the factory, had thought that if the factory ended she ended, but that morning, in the midst of the Spinner that slept, she realised that she was alive, that she breathed, that she was there standing in the midst of the corpse of the factory, alive, and that therefore she was not the factory, she was the one who survived the factory, the one who stayed when the factory went away, and that that one, the one who stayed, was who she was truly, beneath all the badges: a living woman, who had existed before the Spinner and existed after it.

Before going out, she went up to the small office, hers, where she had done accounts so many dawns with Preciosa, and sat one last time at the desk, in the worn armchair where she had sat forty years, and stayed there a moment, looking out the dirty window at the courtyard with weeds, and it was in that armchair, more than in any other place, that she felt the weight of what her life had been and the serene strangeness of no longer being. There she had decided dismissals and admissions, there she had wept hidden on bad days, there she had received the news of the closing standing because she had not even sat down. It was the small throne of her small kingdom, and the kingdom had ended, and the queen was no longer a queen, and Libânia discovered, sitting there, that that no longer hurt her like a death, it hurt her like a longing, which is a mild pain, a pain that already has within it the acceptance. She passed her hand over the desk, got up without haste, and it was that the true farewell, that of the armchair, that of the place from which she had commanded, because to take her leave of the machines had been to take her leave of the work, but to take her leave of the armchair was to take her leave of the power, of the status, of the important Libânia, and it was perhaps that, the hardest and most freeing farewell, because it was to let go not of what she did, but of what made her feel above the others.

She came out of the factory, and shut the door by which she had gone in, and took her leave of it, of the Spinner that slept, with a serene tenderness, like one who takes their leave of a house where they were happy and leaves behind. And she went to the workers, and told them where she had been, and proposed to them a thing that had been born in her at the factory: that they gather, the former workers, not to remake the factory, for that could not be, but to do together something, for pleasure, for the company, whatever it was — Felismina's sweets, sewing, what they felt like — not to prove anything, not to deserve anything, but for the pleasure of going on together, of going on having a reason to see one another, a shared joy. And the workers, who went about each in her own end, thought the idea good, and began to gather, and Engrácia, who had sunk, was among those who most revived with it, because she discovered that she could have her hands busy without it being at the factory, for pleasure and not out of duty, and Libânia watched Engrácia revive and realised that she had, at last, found a way of being useful that did not enslave her: to help the others learn what she had learned, that one can live without a factory, that work can be pleasure and not proof, that life is worth the trouble even without a badge.

That idea of the workers together was born in her, Libânia realised afterwards, from what she most knew how to do and from what she most was going to have to unlearn at the same time, and she had to temper it carefully so as not to spoil it. Because the temptation, in the old Libânia, would have been to organise that as a factory: timetables, targets, who did what, how much was produced, and to give it a useful end, to sell, to yield, to justify. And she caught herself, at first, wanting to do exactly that, wanting to put order, wanting to turn the gathering of the workers into a small business of sweets, and she had to check herself, to realise that if she did that she was only remaking the Spinner on a small scale, putting them all again, and herself, into the wheel of the proof. And she checked herself. She let that be what it wanted to be: a gathering of women without a timetable and without a target, in which some days much was done and on others nothing, in which what mattered was not the sweet but the afternoon, and it was the first time in her life that Libânia organised something not to yield, and it cost her, and it was, perhaps, the hardest proof of all she had learned, because it demanded that she use her greatest talent, that of making things go, in the service of letting them go slowly.

And Venâncio, the brother, learned of that, and went to see, and on seeing the sister making sweets with the workers, for pleasure, he laughed and said, with his malice: well then, sister, you have finally taken to living without proving anything? Welcome to the club. And Libânia, who had despised him her whole life, laughed with him, and said, with a new humility: you were right, Venâncio. Not in everything, for you exaggerated to the other side, you never did anything and that is no life either. But you were right at bottom: I spent my life paying a debt I did not owe. And the two siblings, the one who had never worked and the one who had worked too much, made, that spring, a kind of peace, the peace of those who realise that each had taken to an extreme the same truth — that one exists without having to prove it — and that the truth, as almost always, was in the middle: neither Venâncio, who had never given anything, nor Libânia, who had given herself entirely, but a thing between the two, to work for pleasure and not for proof, to give without draining oneself, to exist without owing.

They came to see one another, after that, a thing that in sixty years they had never done by choice. Venâncio appeared, sometimes, on the afternoons of the workers, and sat in a corner eating sweets and saying jokes, and the women scolded him for doing nothing and gave him sweets all the same, and Libânia laughed to see the brother there, in the midst of the former workers, the man who had never worked in the midst of those who had worked their whole lives, all at last equal, all eating sweets without deserving it, and she thought that there was there, in that kitchen, a crooked and beautiful justice, that of people who had spent their lives on opposite sides of one same lie discovering themselves, in the end, on the same side of one same truth. And once, on taking his leave, Venâncio said to her, now without malice, with a man's awkward tenderness who is not used to it: it is well that I found you, sister, before one of us goes. And Libânia, who had despised him her whole life, realised that it was true, that she had nearly let die, without ever knowing him, the only brother she had left, and that the closing of the factory, which had taken so much from her, had given her at least this: a brother, in the end, and the peace with him, and the end of a war of sixty years that after all neither of them had won, because it was a war between two wounded of the same wound, and of those there is no winner, only one who, with luck, makes peace before the end.
