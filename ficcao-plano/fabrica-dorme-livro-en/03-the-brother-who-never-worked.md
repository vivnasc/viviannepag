## 3. The brother who never worked

Libânia had a brother, Venâncio, and Venâncio was, in everything, the reverse of her, and it was for being the reverse of her that he became, that winter, the mirror that most made her burn.

They were, the two of them, the proof that from the same house come opposite temperaments, because they had eaten the same little bread, slept in the same poverty, heard the same silent accounts at the table, and had come out of there one killing herself with work and the other fleeing it like the devil from the cross, and Libânia had never understood how, from the same root, could have been born two branches so crooked toward sides so contrary.

Venâncio had never worked. Not out of illness, not out of misfortune: out of temperament, out of a way of being in the world that Libânia had never understood and that had always seemed to her, at the same time, a scandal and a mystery. Venâncio had gone through life without ever taking up a trade in earnest: some odd jobs, some attempts that he dropped at the first effort, some schemes that never came off, and through all of that an astonishing capacity to live at the expense of others, of the parents while they were alive, of the sisters, of the women he got involved with, of the village that went on sustaining him with small favours. And the most astonishing thing, for Libânia, was not that Venâncio lived without working: it was that he lived without working and without guilt, without shame, without the least doubt that he deserved everything he received. Venâncio existed at ease. He woke late, ate what there was, sat in the sun, and never, not once, had it crossed his mind that he had to prove anything whatever to deserve the bread he ate.

Libânia had despised the brother her whole life. She had despised him with the contempt of the one who works for the one who does not work, the most intimate and most fierce contempt there is, the contempt of one who sweats for one who does not sweat. For Libânia, Venâncio was a parasite, a layabout, one who lived at the expense of others without deserving it, and each time she saw him in the sun, at ease, while she spent herself at the factory, she felt a just anger, the anger of the ant at the grasshopper, and that anger had been, for sixty years, one of the things that made her work more: so as not to be like the brother, to deserve what he did not deserve, to be the worthy one of the family against the unworthy one.

And there was in that anger, she would realise only much later, a thing she had never wanted to admit: that she needed Venâncio to measure herself by. The lazy brother was the reverse that gave her meaning, the dark background against which her own figure shone. As long as there was a Venâncio doing nothing, the Libânia who did everything had a living proof, there at her side, that she was on the right side, that she was the good one, the worthy one, the one who was worth something. To despise the brother was, without her knowing it, a way of praising herself, of confirming herself every day in her superiority as a worker, and so, at bottom, the lazy brother was almost necessary to her, as to the saint the sinner is necessary to feel themselves a saint. It had never occurred to her that her virtue and his vice were the two ends of the same fear, and that she clung to work with the same desperation with which he fled it, only hers they called merit and his they called shame, and that difference of name had left her, her whole life, on the side of one who judges instead of the side of one who is judged, and it was comfortable to judge, and she had judged the brother sixty years without ever turning inward.

There was between the two of them an old account, of those that families do not close. When the parents grew old, it had been Libânia who sustained them, with the wage of the factory, and it had been she who paid the burial of each one, and Venâncio had appeared at the funerals empty-handed and left them empty-handed, and Libânia had never forgiven him for it, and she said it, the few times they spoke, with that acidity she had kept her whole life: that he had never given anything to anyone, that he had lived on everyone's back, that it was a shame for a grown man never to have put a penny in the house. And Venâncio shrugged his shoulders, without defending himself, with a calm that enraged her more than any excuse, because it was the calm of one who does not feel that they owe, and Libânia, who felt that she owed even the air she breathed, could not bear to see a man who owed nothing to anyone and slept at ease.

But that winter, with the factory closed, Venâncio came to visit her, and the visit was another thing, because this time Libânia did not look at him from the height of her work: she looked at him from the bottom of her void, and what she saw disarmed her.

She had invited him in, a rare thing, and Venâncio had come in, and sat in her kitchen as he sat everywhere, at ease, without haste, and had accepted the coffee, and Libânia, making him the coffee, found herself observing him in a new way, without the filter of the usual contempt, and what she saw surprised her. She saw an old man, it is true, with little, with worn clothes and worn shoes, a man who by the accounts of the world had been nothing and no one; but she saw also a man at peace with that, a man who did not apologise for existing, who did not go about, like her, making her whole life the bow of one who asks leave to be here. Venâncio was here like one who is in a place that is theirs by right. He asked leave of no one. And Libânia, who had asked leave her whole life, who had deserved each crumb with sweat, looked at that ease of the brother and, for the first time, instead of reading it as effrontery, read it as a kind of freedom she had never had.

Because Venâncio, at ease, in the sun, without ever having worked, was well. He was at peace. He existed tranquil, without proof, without a badge, without having to deserve anything, and that tranquillity, which Libânia had always despised as the tranquillity of the parasite, seemed to her now, from the bottom of her void, a thing she did not have and that perhaps she needed: the capacity to exist without proving. Venâncio had never proved anything and existed at peace; she had proved everything her whole life and, the proof taken away, came apart. And there was in this an injustice that burned, but there was also, beneath the injustice, a question Libânia had never dared to ask: and what if Venâncio, in his calm of one who never deserved anything, knew a thing that she, in her sweat of one who deserved everything, had never learned? And what if to exist without proving were not only laziness, but also, in some crooked way, a wisdom?

And there was another thing Libânia saw, in that visit, that disarmed her even more than the brother's peace: she saw that Venâncio, in spite of everything, was happy in his way. Not rich, not respected, not anything she had been; but he had his days, his conversations at the café, his afternoons in the sun, his small pleasures, and he enjoyed them without guilt, with a capacity to make the most of the little he had that she had never had. Venâncio knew how to be on a terrace a whole afternoon watching the people go by and finding that good. Libânia, on a terrace, at the end of five minutes, felt she was wasting time and got up. The brother, who had never won anything, knew how to enjoy everything; she, who had won everything, did not know how to enjoy anything, because to enjoy is to receive without deserving, and she had never learned to receive, only to deserve. And it was thereabouts, by that ashamed envy of the brother's capacity to simply enjoy life, that Libânia's armour cracked enough for her to do a thing she had never done: to open herself to Venâncio.

Venâncio noticed that the sister was low, and asked, in his way, without much tact, what was the matter, and Libânia, who had never told him anything, told him, because she was so at the bottom that she no longer had strength for pride. She told him of the factory, of the closing, of the empty five in the morning, of not knowing who she was without the work. And Venâncio heard, and at the end was a moment silent, and then said a thing that made him, without his knowing it, the bearer of the key of the book, a thing said without malice, with the simplicity of one who never thought much but sometimes, for that very reason, sees clearly:

"You worked your whole life to prove what, Libânia?" he said. "To whom? I never proved anything to anyone, and here I am, and the sun rises for me as it rises for you. No one asked me for proofs, and I did not give them either, and I exist all the same. You gave proofs your whole life, and look at you, now that you have no one to give them to, it is as though you had ceased to exist. I think you went wrong somewhere, sister. I think you spent your life paying a debt you did not owe. No one asked you to prove that you deserved to be here. You were here, and that was that. Like me. The difference is that I always knew that, and you never knew it, and that, sister, with all respect, must be a much more tiring life than mine."

Libânia did not answer him. She stayed looking at the brother, and wanted to tell him that he had not the least idea what it was to work, that it was easy to philosophise about the uselessness of effort for one who had never exerted any, that if everyone were like him there would be no factory nor bread nor anything, and all that was true and she knew it was true. But she said nothing, because beneath the anger, for the first time in sixty years, she felt a thing she had never felt toward the brother: a kind of envy. Not of his laziness, that she went on despising. Envy of the peace. Envy of seeing him sitting there, without a factory and without ever having had a factory, and being whole, while she, with sixty years of factory, was in pieces. How could it be that the one who had never done anything was whole and the one who had done everything was undone? What account was that, that came out the reverse of everything she had learned?

And he went away, Venâncio, at ease as he had come, leaving the sister with the phrase lodged crosswise, and Libânia stayed a long time mulling over that, with the anger and the astonishment mixed, because the brother she had despised her whole life had just told her, without knowing it, the deepest truth of her life: that she had spent sixty years paying a debt she did not owe, proving a worth she already had, deserving an existence that was hers by right. And that Venâncio, the parasite, the layabout, knew a thing that she, the worker, the worthy one, had never known: that one exists first, and that the rest, the work, the merit, the proof, comes after, or does not come, and that existence does not depend on that, that existence is free, is of one who is born, and not of one who pays for it with sweat. She hated the brother for telling her. And she could not, however much she tried, undo what he had said, because it was true, and the truths said by the people we despise are the hardest to swallow and the most impossible to spit out.

That night, lying in the dark, Libânia tried to set up against the brother the defence of a whole life, and could not close it. She said to herself: but if everyone were like him, there would be no world, no factory, no bread; Venâncio could only allow himself the luxury of proving nothing because there were always people like me proving everything and sustaining him. And this was true, and gave her right, and even so it did not relieve her, because the question the brother had put to her was not whether it was worth working — of course it was, someone has to spin the wool, someone has to open the factory in the morning. The question was another, deeper, and it was that one that did not let her sleep: not whether it was worth working, but whether it was worth living clinging to the idea that one only exists while one works. Venâncio had worked too little, and that was a fault, and she did not forgive him for it. But she had bound her own existence to work, and that, she realised now for the first time, was perhaps a fault too, of another kind, a fault against herself, and perhaps the two extremes, that of the brother who had never proved anything and hers who had proved everything, were two errors, and not an error and a rightness, as she had always thought. She fell asleep without resolving it, and it was the first of many nights in which the brother's phrase worked within her, slowly, as the water works the stone.

The hardest thing to accept, and what most made her mull it over on the following nights, was having to admit that she had learned something from Venâncio, precisely from Venâncio, the brother she had chosen, her whole life, as the example of what one should not be. It was as though the world had turned inside out: the ant having to learn from the grasshopper, the worthy one having to learn from the unworthy one, the one who had spent herself having to hear a truth from the mouth of the one who had spared himself. It cost her pride, and Libânia's pride was great, it had been her column for sixty years. But beneath the wounded pride there was an honesty that she had also always had, and that honesty obliged her to acknowledge that the brother had said a truth, and that a truth is a truth whoever it comes from, and that perhaps it had been necessary for Venâncio to say it, precisely because he was the reverse of her, because no one who resembled her would ever say it to her, all those who resembled her applauded her work. It had taken the opposite of her, the one she despised, to tell her the thing no one else would tell her, and there was in this a lesson Libânia did not yet know how to formulate but that she sensed: that sometimes the truth we most need comes from the person we least want to hear, and that to refuse it because of who says it is to be left without it out of pride, and the pride, she realised now, cost her her whole life, and perhaps it was time for it to cost her less.
