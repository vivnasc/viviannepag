## 4. The chorus of the workers

The Spinner had not closed only for Libânia: it had closed for all of them, for the dozens of women who had spent their lives there, and Libânia, who had been the forewoman, felt herself responsible for all of them, as she had always felt, and it was in seeing what the closing did to each of them that she realised that the end of a factory is the end of many worlds at the same time, each coming apart in its own way.

And she went on feeling herself responsible for them even when there was no longer a factory nor a post to justify that responsibility, and that, she realised later, was also a symptom of her disease: the forewoman could not detach herself from being a forewoman. She went to visit them one by one, as though it still fell to her to watch over the shift, took them things, gave them advice, wanted to put them all to safety, and there was in this goodness, but there was also Libânia's inability to exist in any way other than caring for others, ordering, serving, because to serve was her only known form of being worth something, and even ruined, even at the bottom, she still tried to be worth something by the care she gave the workers, without seeing that she was doing with them what she had done her whole life with the work: using them, without malice, to feel useful, to flee the void, to have, still, someone to be the forewoman of. Only much later, already more cured, would she realise that to care for the workers out of duty was different from being with them out of pleasure, and that she had begun with the first and would have to learn the second.

There was Engrácia, who sank. Engrácia was one of the oldest, near Libânia's age, and had put into the factory, like Libânia, her whole life, and with the closing she collapsed: she stopped going out, stopped tidying herself, wasted away in a way that was frightening, and when Libânia visited her, she found her sitting, looking at her hands, saying I do not know what to do with my hands, Libânia, these hands worked sixty years and now they do not know how to be still, it hurts me to have my hands idle. And Libânia saw, in Engrácia, what she herself could be, because she too did not know what to do with her hands, with the hours, with the body trained for work and suddenly without work, and Engrácia was the warning of the bottom one could reach.

Engrácia had a phrase that stuck to her and that she said to everyone, and that Libânia could not get out of her head: I am no longer needed by anyone. She said it without drama, like one who notes the weather, and it was perhaps for saying it without drama that it hurt so much, because it was the voice of one who has already given up arguing their own sentence. I am no longer needed by anyone. And Libânia, on hearing it, realised that it was exactly that terror that also inhabited her, put into naked words: that the worth of a person was the lack they made, and that the work taken away, which was the lack she made, she would cease to be needed, and therefore to be worth something, and therefore to have a reason to be there. Engrácia said aloud what Libânia scarcely dared to think, and so to visit her was at the same time a duty and a torment, because it was like visiting her own future sitting in a kitchen chair looking at hands that no longer served.

Engrácia had been, at the factory, one of the best, and that made her collapse even harder to see, because it was not a weak woman who fell, it was one of the strong ones, one of those who had never missed a day, one of those Libânia pointed to the new ones as an example. And perhaps it was precisely for having been one of the best that she fell the deepest, because the one who most identifies with the work most loses themselves when the work ends, and Engrácia, like Libânia, had been one of those who put into the factory not only their hands but their whole soul, and in losing the factory she lost the soul, or at least the place where she had kept it. Libânia, on visiting her, recognised herself in her in a way that hurt, she saw in Engrácia what she would be if she did not find a way out, and so the visits were a cruel mirror: she went to see Engrácia and saw herself, she went to support her and realised that she had no support to give that she did not know, at bottom, how to give first to herself, and she came out of Engrácia's house more frightened than she had gone in, because she had spent an hour looking, in a kitchen chair, at her own possible future.

There was Genoveva, who denied. Genoveva pretended the factory had not closed: she went on speaking of it in the present, when the factory reopens, she said, I will go back to its section, and she found reasons to believe that the closing was temporary, that new people were coming to buy the factory, that it could not end for good. She lived in a factory that no longer existed, and Libânia, at first, thought that madness, and then realised that it was only a way of surviving, Genoveva's way: not to accept the death, to keep the dead one alive in her head, because to accept that the factory had closed was to accept that the life one had around it had ended, and Genoveva had no strength to accept that, and so she did not accept it, and lived, in her way, in a phantom factory.

Genoveva, with time, was the one who broke Libânia's heart in a quieter way, because her denial went on wearing away, month after month, as the factory did not reopen and the rumours of buyers undid themselves one by one, and Libânia went on watching Genoveva wake slowly, against her will, to a reality she had tried to keep at a distance, and it was a waking worse than the sleep, because Genoveva had spent on the denial the strength she could have spent on beginning again, and when she at last accepted that the factory was not coming back, she was already too old and too tired to invent anything else, and she stayed there, in the middle, without the phantom factory that had sustained her and without anything to replace it. And Libânia realised, watching her, that to deny the loss does not put it off, it only keeps it whole for a worse day, and she was afraid of doing with her own mourning, in her way, something similar — of filling the days with tasks so as not to have to accept that the life she had had was over.

And there was Felismina, who was reborn, and she was the one who most astonished Libânia. Felismina, with the closing, instead of sinking or denying, did a thing no one expected: she flourished. She discovered that she had always wanted to do something else, sweets, jams, and that she had never done it because the factory ate her days, and now, without a factory, she set about making sweets, and selling them, and she went about more alive than ever, and said to Libânia, with a joy that was almost offensive, ah Libânia, I am even grateful for the closing, you know, at the factory I was a machine, I did what I was told, and now I do what I like, and I was never so happy. And Libânia heard Felismina and felt a confused thing, between envy and distrust, because she did not understand how one could be grateful for the closing of a factory, and she did not understand, above all, how Felismina knew what she liked to do, when she, Libânia, had not the least idea what she liked to do, because never, in sixty years, had she asked herself what she liked, only what was necessary.

Once, she asked her, Felismina, half by challenge, half by true distress: but how did you know that you liked to make sweets? How does one know a thing like that? And Felismina laughed and said she had not known, that she had tried, that she had made a sweet one day because she felt like it and that she had liked making it, and had made another, and another, and that it had been so, by making, that she had discovered she liked it, not by thinking. And she added a thing that stayed with Libânia: you are never going to discover what you like with your head, Libânia, sitting thinking. The liking is not thought, it is tried. What you lack is the courage to try, because to try is to do a thing that may be good for nothing, and you never in your life did a thing that was good for nothing. Well, that is what you have left to learn: to do things that are good for nothing, only to see whether you like them. And Libânia was left thinking that perhaps she had, at fifty-eight years old, to learn to play, she who had never played, not even as a child, because as a child she was already a mouth to justify and had never had time nor permission for the sacred waste of doing a thing only for the pleasure of doing it.

There were still others, that Libânia went on seeing, each one with her way of taking the closing, and it was as though the factory, in closing, had opened the people and let one see what each of them had inside beneath the worker. There was the one who clung to the grandchildren and made herself a full-time grandmother, and got by like that, transferring to the grandchildren the giving she had given the factory. There was the one who took to the church, filling with prayers the hours she had filled with shifts. There was the one who truly fell ill, of the body, as though the body, without the effort that organised it, had lost its bearings and begun to fail. And Libânia looked at all of them and saw that the closing had invented nothing in any of them: it had only taken off the lid, and let out what was already there. Those who had, beneath the work, a life, a faith, some grandchildren, a liking, clung to that and floated; those who had nothing beneath the work, like Engrácia, sank, because they had been taken the one thing they had and beneath it there was no ground. And Libânia, with a chill down her spine, asked herself which group she belonged to, and was afraid of the answer, because in sixty years she had never cultivated anything beneath the work, not faith, not pastimes, not even the children, to whom she had given everything except presence, and she suspected, with dread, that she was one of those who had no ground.

And it was that, Libânia realised, the difference between her and Felismina, and the thing that most disquieted her: Felismina, beneath the worker, had a person, a person with likings, with wishes, with a thing she wanted to do and that the factory had smothered; and Libânia, beneath the forewoman, did not know whether she had a person, did not know whether there was, beneath the work, a Libânia with likings and wishes, or whether the work was all there was, and beneath it, nothing. Felismina took off the badge and found a person waiting. Libânia was afraid of taking off the badge and finding no one.

They had all gathered, once, in the month after the closing, for a lunch that Felismina had got up, and it was a strange lunch, that Libânia did not forget. There they were, around the table, the women who for decades had seen one another every day inside the noise of the machines, and now they saw one another in silence, at the table of a house, not quite knowing what to say to one another outside the factory, because the factory had always been what they had in common, and the factory taken away they discovered, with some fright, that they scarcely knew one another outside it. They spoke of the closing, of course, and of the severance, and of the men from outside, and of the pity, and then a silence fell, and it was Felismina who broke it, speaking of her sweets, animated, and Genoveva who said, low, that when it reopened, and the whole table lowered their eyes, because no one had the courage to contradict her, and Engrácia who said nothing, sitting in a corner, looking at her hands on the tablecloth as though they were someone else's. And Libânia, at the head out of habit, because the forewoman always sits at the head, looked at that chorus of women undone each in her own way and felt the weight of no longer having anything to order them to do, no order to give, no factory to open the next morning, and realised that her authority, which had been everything, had come apart with the noise of the machines, and that there, at that table, she was the forewoman of no one: she was only one more woman without a factory, equal to the others, and that that, after a life of being the one who commanded, was among the hardest things to accept.

The three workers were, Libânia realised, three answers to the same question, the question the closing put to all of them: what are we when what we did ends? Engrácia answered: nothing, I sink. Genoveva answered: I pretend it has not ended. Felismina answered: I discover another thing that I am. And Libânia, the forewoman, the one who had always known what to do, did not know what her answer was, and saw herself in the three, afraid of ending like Engrácia, with the temptation to deny like Genoveva, and with a painful envy of Felismina, who seemed to have found the one good answer, that of discovering, beneath the work, a person who existed in herself.

There was, in the midst of all this, a guilt Libânia carried and did not tell anyone: the guilt of having been the forewoman. Because she had been, for forty years, the face of the factory for those women, the one who had given them orders, the one who had pressed them on the days of production, the one who had made them stay one hour more when the order asked it. And although she knew, with her head, that the closing had not been her fault, that it had come from afar, from the men from outside, from the numbers, she felt, with her chest, an absurd responsibility for them, as though she owed them, now that they were all at the end, a reparation for all the hours she had taken from them in the name of the factory. She looked at the sunken Engrácia and thought: how many times did I make her stay later, how many evenings did I rob her of, how many sunny afternoons, and for what, for the factory to close all the same. And there was in this guilt, she realised later, still the same disease: that of one who thinks themselves responsible for everything, that of one who cannot bear not to have the reins, that of one who confuses caring with controlling. But in the winter she did not see that; she saw only the undone workers and herself as the one who, in a way, had led them their whole lives toward a precipice she had not seen coming.

And there was, that winter, a thing that frightened Libânia more than everything: Engrácia fell ill. Not of an illness with a name: she fell ill of having nothing to do, she wasted away, and ended up in bed, and the doctor from outside, on seeing her, said a thing that Libânia took home and that did not let her sleep — that there were people who fell ill from the end of the work, that the body and the soul, trained their whole life for the effort, on losing the effort lost their bearings, and that that killed as well as a real illness, slowly, from within. And Libânia, on hearing that, and on seeing Engrácia in bed, wasted away, realised, with a shiver, that she was looking at a possible road, hers, if she did not find, like Felismina, a way to exist without the factory, and that that winter was not only about losing a job: it was about whether she was or was not going to manage to discover, beneath the forewoman who no longer existed, a Libânia who existed in herself, before the lack of work wasted her away as it wasted away Engrácia, who had not, beneath the worker, found anyone waiting.
