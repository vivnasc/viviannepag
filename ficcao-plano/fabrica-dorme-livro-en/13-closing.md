## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a year, at the riverside, when the factory fell asleep and a woman woke.

Libânia, daughter of poor people, a worker and then forewoman of the Spinner, who at twelve years old put the first wage on the table and saw, for the first time, the father look at her with pride, and who learned that Friday the thing that cost her her whole life to unlearn: that one is worth something by working, and that love is paid for with sweat. She worked sixty years paying a debt no one had collected from her, held the factory through every closing, and it took the factory closing for good for her to discover, at the bottom of the winter and in the word of a Keeper, that her line in this book had been written at birth, before she did anything whatever, and that no closing of a factory erases it.

It was recorded that she learned to inhabit the life she had always worked: to watch the sun rise without deserving it, to care for a kitchen-garden without hurrying it, to be with her own without commanding them. And that she helped another, Engrácia, to come out of the same dark, and that she made peace with the brother who had never worked, and that she understood, at last, that the truth was in the middle: neither to give oneself entirely nor never to give anything, but to work for pleasure and to exist without owing.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs a girl of twelve years to confuse love with the wage, nor how many years it takes a woman to discover that she was worth something before the first shift and would be worth something after the last. For that, books like this one are needed.

And with this is closed, for now, the shelf of those who were worth something without knowing it, as the others were closed before: that of those who learned to be loved, that of those who let go of the weight, that of those who let themselves be cared for, that of those who came back, that of those who forgave, that of those who felt. Seven shelves, seven veils, seven ways for a person to come to the same and only truth of the village — that one exists before doing anything whatever, and that it is enough, to have a right to one's own line, to have been born. Of all of it a record was made. And whoever reads, let them recognise themselves.
