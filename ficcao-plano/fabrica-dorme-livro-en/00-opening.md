## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Libânia, daughter of people of the village, born in a poor house with too many mouths. She was a worker and then a forewoman at the Spinner, the wool-mill by the river, and she held it through every announced closing, until the closing that had no turning back.

The name was a promise, of those of Véspera: she was born in a year of hunger, and the mother wanted her to have the name of a thing that burns and gives off scent, the incense that is burned and rises, in the hope that the daughter would be worth, one day, more than the house could give her at birth.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not ask anyone what they did to deserve their line, because the line is of one who was born, and not of one who worked. But there are people who spend their lives thinking they have to deserve their own line, and who only discover, in the end, that it was already there before they did anything at all. For those a book like this one is needed.

This is the last of the shelf of the seven, and it closes it, as one closes a factory at the end of the day, turning the key slowly. It tells the story of a woman who confused, for sixty years, doing with being worth, and who only learned the difference when they took the doing from her and she had to discover, from the bottom, whether she was still worth anything. It is a story of a factory, but it could be of any trade, of any house, of any person who learned too early that love is earned and rest is deserved. Whoever learned so, let them read. And whoever, reading, recognises themselves in some Libânia or other who wakes at five in the morning with nowhere now to go, let them know, from the first line, what she took a lifetime to know: that she existed before, and will exist after, and that it is enough, to have a right to one's line in this book, to have been born.
