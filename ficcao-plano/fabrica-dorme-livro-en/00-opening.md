## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Libânia, daughter of people of the village, born in a poor house with too many mouths. She was a worker and then a forewoman at the Spinner, the wool-mill by the river, and she held it through every announced closing, until the closing that had no turning back.

The name was a promise, of those of Véspera: she was born in a year of hunger, and the mother wanted her to have the name of a thing that burns and gives off scent, the incense that is burned and rises, in the hope that the daughter would be worth, one day, more than the house could give her at birth.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not ask anyone what they did to deserve their line, because the line is of one who was born, and not of one who worked. It does not say what it costs one who confused, for a whole lifetime, doing with being worth, nor what is left of a person when the doing is taken from them and they must discover, from the bottom, whether they are still worth anything. For that, books such as this one are needed.
