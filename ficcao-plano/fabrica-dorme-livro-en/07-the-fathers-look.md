## 7. The father's look

There are wounds one carries one's whole life without knowing one has them, because they were made so early that they seem not to be wounds, they seem to be the shape of the world. Libânia had one of those, and had never called it a wound, she called it character, she called it being hard-working, she called it pride; and it took a factory closing, and it took a Keeper, for the wound, at the end of sixty years, to let itself at last be seen for what it was.

It was to the Keeper of the Register that Libânia ended up telling the root-scene, that of the father's look, and she told it without meaning to, because she had gone to the House of the Register for another thing and the conversation led her there, as the conversations with the Keeper always led people to the places they did not know they needed to visit.

The House of the Register was, in Véspera, the place where the memory of who was born, married and departed was kept, and the Keeper was the woman who kept the books, and there was in the village an old wisdom about that house: that people went there to see to a paper and came out with another thing, that the Keeper had the gift of asking questions that opened doors people did not even know they had shut. Libânia did not much believe in those things, she had always been one with her feet planted on the ground, one of accounts and of machines, but she was so at the bottom that winter that she went everywhere they treated her with gentleness, and the Keeper treated her so.

She had gone to see to a paper for the pension, and the Keeper, on seeing her face, asked her how she was, and Libânia, who went about telling the misfortune to whoever asked her because she no longer had strength to hide it, told it, and the Keeper heard, without haste, with that whole attention of one who has all the time in the world for a person, and at a certain point she asked a thing no one had asked her: and when did it begin, Libânia, this of your thinking you have to work to be worth something? Was there a day? And Libânia, who had never thought that there was a day, realised, with the question, that there was, and told it.

She told of the poor house, the too many mouths, the girl who was a weight. She told of the first week of work, at twelve years old. And she told of the Friday, the wage on the table, and the father's look. She told how the father, who had always looked at her with the tired account of one who has too many mouths, looked at her, that Friday, with pride, with respect, with a thing resembling love, and how she, at twelve years old, realised that she had come to be worth something, and that before she was not worth anything.

There was a thing in that scene that Libânia had never said to anyone, not even to herself, and that only there, with the Keeper, she let come out: that she, that Friday of the twelve years, on seeing the father's look change, had felt at the same time joy and a deep thing that only now she knew how to name, which was fear. Joy at being at last worth something; and fear, a cold fear, of being able to lose it again. Because a child who realises they have come to be worth something for a thing realises, in the same instant, that they can cease to be worth anything if that thing fails them, and Libânia had come out of that Friday not only with the pride of yielding, but with the terror, for ever, of one day not yielding, and it had been that terror, more than the pride, what had moved her sixty years, what had made her never miss a day, never rest, never stop, because to stop was to risk seeing again in someone's look the tired account of one who held her a weight. Her whole working life had been, at bottom, a flight from that fear of twelve years, and the closing of the factory was the fear realising itself at last, was the thing she had fled her whole life catching her, old, with no more factory to run to.

She told it slowly, because she had never told it to anyone, nor knew that it was a story one could tell, she had always thought that it was only the way things were, and on putting that into words, before the Keeper, she heard it for the first time from outside, as though it were the story of another person, and the story of another person made her feel a pity that her own had never made her feel. A girl of twelve years, she thought, a girl of twelve years realising that she only comes to be loved when she begins to yield. And she said, aloud, without noticing that she said it: poor child. And the Keeper, low, asked: what child, Libânia? And Libânia realised, with a tightening in her throat, that the child was her, that she was at last feeling pity for herself as a girl, she who had never allowed herself any pity, and it was that pity, the late pity of the woman for the girl she had been, the first thread of compassion that Libânia ever extended to herself.

And on telling it to the Keeper, sixty years later, Libânia heard herself, for the first time, say that aloud, and on hearing herself she realised the size of the thing: she realised that her whole working life had not been love of work, nor ambition, nor duty; it had been a girl of twelve years trying, for ever, to reconquer every week the father's look of pride, to flee, for ever, the look of the tired account, to prove, week after week, for sixty years, that she deserved the bread, that she was not the mouth too many, that she was worth something.

The Keeper let her speak to the end, without interrupting her, and when Libânia fell silent there was a silence in the House of the Register, the thick silence of the places where memory is kept, and it was in that silence that the Keeper asked the question that opened the whole wound, and she asked it slowly, carefully, like one who puts the knife where they know blood will come out:

"And your father," asked the Keeper, slowly, "did he ever look at you with pride for something other than the work? As a small child, before the factory, did he ever look at you only for being his daughter?"

And Libânia searched in her memory, searched a long time, and did not find it. She did not find a single memory of the father looking at her with pride or with tenderness before the first week of work. Before the factory, the father was the tired account, the weight, the too many mouths. The father's love had begun on the Friday of the first wage, and Libânia realised, in the House of the Register, with the Keeper before her, the hardest thing of her life: that she had never been loved by the father for being who she was, only for doing what she did, that the father's love had always been a payment for the work, never a gift for the existence, and that she had learned, from that father, that love is paid for with work, and that therefore never, in sixty years, had she allowed herself to believe that she deserved love, or existence, or even rest, without paying for it with the sweat of each day.

And on saying it aloud, there, she realised a thing that shook her even more, because it took from her the one defence she had left, which was the anger. She tried to feel anger at the father, in that moment, now that she saw what he had done to her, and she could not, because she saw, with the same clarity, that the father had not done that out of malice: the father was a poor man, crushed by the mouths he could not feed, and the joy he had felt on the Friday of the daughter's first wage had been a true joy, that of one who sees a relief come to a drowning house, and the pride with which he had looked at her had been real pride, not a calculation. The father had loved her truly that Friday. The problem had not been the father not loving her; it had been her having learned, from that love, the wrong lesson: that love had come with the work, and that therefore, without work, the love went away. The father had given her too much pride for what she did and too little tenderness for what she was, and he had done it without malice, like one who passes on a poverty they inherited, and Libânia realised that the wound had come to her not from a bad father, but from a poor father who only knew how to love in the way of the poor, by the bread that comes into the house, and that that, the poverty of the love, had been the deepest inheritance she had received, deeper than the hunger, because the hunger had passed and that had stayed within her chest sixty years.

"You know, Libânia," said the Keeper, and went to fetch a book, the one of the births of the year Libânia had been born, and opened it, and showed her the line, "look. Here is your birth. This line was written when you were born, days old, before you did anything whatever. Before the factory, before the first wage, before you proved anything whatever. You were already here, in the book of the village, with a line of your own, only for having been born. The book did not wait for you to deserve the line. It gave it you at birth, as it gives it to everyone, because the line is of one who is born, not of one who works. And I am going to tell you a thing, from my trade, which is the trade of keeping who existed: I never, in all these books, in centuries of records, saw a line that said that someone deserved to be born for having worked. The lines are all of people who were born and that was that, who existed and that was that. Your worth, Libânia, is written here since you were born, and it was not the factory that wrote it, it was the birth. The factory gave you a trade, and it was good, and it ended. But your worth was not in the factory. It was in this line, from the beginning, and it is here still, now that the factory has closed, the same as it was, because this line is not erased by the closing of any factory."

Libânia stayed a long time looking at that line. It was a line like the others on the page, in the same old hand of a Keeper who had already died, and it had nothing special, and it was exactly for having nothing special that it shook Libânia to the bottom: because there she was, recorded, counted, part of the village, with the same right to a line as any other person, and no one had asked her, on writing it, whether she was going to be hard-working or lazy, useful or useless, a mouth too many or a hand to yield. They had given her the line at birth, free, as one gives it to all, because the line is not a prize, it is a right of one who is born. And Libânia, who had spent her life convinced that she had to deserve her place in the world, saw, written in ink fifty-eight years ago, that her place had been given her before she deserved anything whatever, that it had always been there, that no one could take it from her, neither the closing of the factory, nor the age, nor the father with his tired account. The line depended on nothing. The line was hers because she had been born, and that was that.

She passed her finger over the line, slowly, and it was a gesture the Keeper let her make without saying anything, and it was like touching a truth so simple that it was hard to fit: that she was enough. That she had always been enough. That the girl of twelve years had not needed to put the wage on the table to deserve to be alive, because she was already alive, she already had her line, and that the father, in only looking at her with pride after the first wage, had not given her worth — he had confirmed for her, without meaning to, the wrong idea that worth is given, when the worth was already there, written, from the beginning.

And Libânia looked at her line of birth, written fifty-eight years ago, before she had done anything whatever, and it was as though she saw, for the first time, the proof of a thing the father had taught her not to believe: that she had existed, and been worth something, and counted, before the first week of work, before the look of pride, before everything. That there had been a Libânia prior to the worker, a Libânia baby, girl, who was worth something only for existing, and that that Libânia was still there, beneath sixty years of work, waiting to be recognised, waiting for someone, at last, or for herself, to tell her what the father had never told her: that she was enough, that she deserved to be here, that she was worth something, not for what she brought to the table, but for being, simply, the one who was born.

And yet, on leaving the House of the Register that day, Libânia did not come out cured, she came out worse, because to see the wound is not to heal it, and sometimes to see the wound is to open it for good. She had lived sixty years with her logic intact — I work, therefore I am worth something — and the Keeper had just shown her that the logic was false, and a person can pass through life well with a lie that sustains them and collapse on the day the lie is taken from them without their being given another thing to lean on. That is what happened to Libânia. She came out of the House of the Register with the old certainty broken and with no new one to replace it, staggering between a worth she had discovered to be false and a true worth she did not yet know how to feel, and it was in that gap, between the fall of one certainty and the arrival of the other, that she sank deepest that winter, because there is a moment, in every cure, when the person is worse than when they were ill without knowing it: the moment when they have already seen the truth but cannot yet live it. And it was toward that moment, toward that bottom, that Libânia walked in the days that followed the conversation with the Keeper, without knowing that the worst had still to come before the better could come.
