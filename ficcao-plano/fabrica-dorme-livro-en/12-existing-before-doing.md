## 12. Existing before doing

A year passed over the closing of the Spinner, and when the spring came again, Libânia's second spring without a factory, she realised, one morning, that she no longer counted the days by what she had ceased to have, but by what she had learned to have, and that that exchange, tiny as it was, was the measure of all that had changed within her in the hardest and truest year of her life.

If someone had told her, on the day she handed over the key, that a year later she would look at the closing of the Spinner without hatred, perhaps even with a kind of ashamed gratitude, Libânia would have thought it an insult. And yet, there she was, in that second spring: capable of thinking that, if the factory had not closed, she would have died without ever knowing who she was, would have worked until she dropped, would have gone to the grave with the badge on her chest and without ever having watched a sun rise at leisure, without ever having made peace with the brother, without ever having apologised to the children, without ever having known that she was worth something other than the yielding. The closing had taken from her everything she thought she was, and in that theft, without meaning to, it had given her the one thing she had never had: herself. She did not say this to anyone, because there were still many people in the village suffering the closing and it was no time for gratitudes; but she thought it, low, on the mornings at the door, and it was a thought that left her at peace, that of the worst thing of her life having given birth to the best, which is how it sometimes happens, though one only sees it afterwards, and from back to front.

She was not cured, and she knew she was not. She had learned, over that year, that this was not a disease one cures for good, with a day on which one wakes healthy and never again falls ill; it was more like the kitchen-garden, a thing one cares for every day and that, if one neglects it, takes weeds again. There were good days and bad days. There were mornings when she woke whole and mornings when she woke with the old distress of not being worth anything, with the temptation to throw herself into a task to feel herself a person. The difference, and it was all the difference, is that now she knew the way back. She knew that the distress passed if she did not obey it. She knew how to sit at the door and wait for it to pass. She knew how to call Felismina on the days when alone she could not manage. She had, at last, at the end of a life, tools to care for herself, and to have those tools was, she realised, what made her different from the Engrácia of the dark and the Libânia of the bottom: not that she no longer fell, but that she now knew how to get up, and that to get up, now, was not for the factory, it was for life.

She still woke at five. The body never unlearned it, and Libânia no longer expected it to unlearn it, and she had made peace with that old waking as one makes peace with a scar: it is not beautiful, but it is the proof of a life, and it hurts little, and at the end of some time one no longer even notices it. She woke at five, and instead of the tightening of the factory that for months had tightened her chest at that hour, she felt now a thing almost peaceful, a kind of complicity with the dawn, as though that hour, which had been the hour of duty sixty years running, were now, at last, an hour of hers, the only hour of the day in which the whole village slept and she was awake and alive, alone with the dark coming undone, with no one needing anything, with no shift, no whistle, no proof.

She made the tea. She sat at the door. She watched the day be born. And her conscience no longer hurt at being there idle producing nothing, because she had finally realised, at the end of that year, a thing that Felismina had told her and that she only now felt in the body and not only heard with the head: that to watch the sun rise was not to do nothing. It was to do the hardest thing she had ever tried, which was to be there. To be in her life, within it, present, without working it, without deserving it, without justifying it to anyone. Sixty years of doing, and the hardest thing after all was to be there, and she was learning it at sixty-odd, slowly, as one learns everything that is important, with relapses and with patience, morning after morning, at the door, with the tea cooling in her hands and the day being born free over the village and over the closed Spinner in the distance, which it no longer hurt her to look at.

The children had come, that spring, and the visit had been different from all the others, and Libânia kept it as one keeps a rare thing. They had come worried, warned by someone in the village that the mother had been low with the closing, expecting to find her defeated, and they had found a mother they did not know: a mother with time. A mother who sat with them at the table without getting up every five minutes to see to something, who asked them about their lives and stayed to hear the whole answer, who took them to see the kitchen-garden and the place from which she watched the sun rise, and who, at a certain point, said to them a thing that left them speechless, because they had never heard her say anything like it. She said: forgive me. Forgive me for never having had time for you when you were small. I thought I loved you by working, that I gave you what I never had, and I did, but I was lacking to you in what is not bought, and only now, old and without a factory, have I realised that to be with you was a thing I owed you and that I never gave you. And the children, grown men, did not know what to say, because they had come to support a mother who was low and were being asked forgiveness by a mother who seemed, for the first time, whole. And it was a late and imperfect reconciliation, that did not undo the years of absence, but that opened, between them, a door that had always been shut, and Libânia realised that to learn to inhabit her own life was also giving her back, into the bargain, those she had driven away by never having inhabited anything.

The kitchen-garden had borne. It was a small kitchen-garden, without method, without the factory discipline that Libânia had put into everything her whole life, and it was exactly for being so, undisciplined, that it did her good: because in the kitchen-garden she did not command, she accompanied, and she had learned, with the cabbages and the tomato plants, the thing she had never learned with the machines, which was to wait. The machines gave when one pressed the button; the earth gave when it felt like it, at its rhythm, and Libânia, who had spent her life forcing results, had learned in the kitchen-garden to let a thing grow at its own time without hurrying it, and in that learning there was, without her giving it a name, a cure: that of one who unlearns the haste of proving and relearns the leisure of living.

She was with the workers. They gathered three times a week, in the kitchen of one or another, and made things — sweets, jams, a bit of sewing, an embroidery — not to sell, for that they would have to decide one day and had not yet decided, but for the pleasure of having their hands busy in company, which was, they all discovered, the thing they most missed of the factory: not the work, the company of the work, the being together with a small purpose between their hands.

And there was, on those afternoons, a new joy that none of them had known in the time of the Spinner, and that was exactly the joy of doing the same things without a master, without a shift, without the fear of failing. They laughed more than they had ever laughed at the factory, told one another their lives, spoke of the husbands and the dead and the grandchildren, and the sweet went on being made amid the conversation, almost by chance, and it was the best sweet they had made in their lives, they said, because it was the first made without haste. Genoveva, who had taken so long to wake from the phantom factory, was now among those who spoke most, as though the gathering of the women had given her back, in another way, the company she had not wanted to lose and that was, after all, what she had sought in the factory that did not come back. And Libânia, on looking at that kitchen full of women laughing and stirring pots with no master at all, realised that they had found again, without the factory, the best thing the factory had given them — the company of one another — and thrown away the worst — the obligation, the fear, the proof — and that it was possible, then, to keep the wheat and let go of the chaff, to keep what work has of good, which is to make with one's hands beside one one cares for, and to let go of the part that enslaves, which is to have to do it to deserve to exist. And Engrácia, who had sunk, was now among those who most enlivened those afternoons, and Libânia, on seeing her laugh at the table with a spoon of sweet in her hand, remembered Engrácia lying in the dark, and measured by that, by the distance between those two Engrácias, the distance she herself had crossed, because Engrácia, at bottom, had been her mirror, and to take her out of the bottom had been, in part, a way of Libânia taking herself out.

It was on one of those afternoons that the whole thing settled in her head, all at once, with the clarity with which sometimes a truth that has been forming for years appears suddenly, whole, as though it had always been there. They were making quince paste, the kitchen full of sweet steam, and Genoveva, joking, asked the table what each of them wanted written on her grave, and there was that half-nervous laugh that is made when one speaks of death in jest, and each one said hers: good mother, good wife, hard-working. And when it came to Libânia, she, who a year before would have said without thinking good forewoman, or worked to the end, or did her duty, was silent, stirring the quince paste, and then said, slowly, a thing that surprised even herself to say:

"I want nothing of what I did to be there. I want only my name. Libânia, and the dates, and nothing more. Because I spent my life thinking that I was what I did, and after all what stays of a person is not what they did, it is that they were. I want it to stay that I was. Only that. That I existed. That I was here. The rest — the work, the factory, the shifts — that was what I did while I was here, and it was good, and it ended. But that is not what I was. I was a person. And I took sixty years to discover it, but I discovered it, and it is the one thing I want to take to the grave: that I discovered, before dying, that I was worth something for having been born, and not for having worked."

And there fell a silence in the kitchen, the steam of the quince paste rising, and none of the workers said anything, because they all realised that what Libânia had said was not about the grave, it was about all their lives, women who had spent their existence being worth something for what they produced and who were there, now, learning, late, beside one another, with a spoon of quince paste in their hand, that they were worth something else, something that is not produced and is not lost when a factory closes, something that had been in them since they were born and that would stay there until they died, intact, beneath all the trades: the simple fact of existing.

That afternoon, on going home, Libânia passed again by the closed Spinner, as she always passed, and stopped, as she always stopped, but that time she stopped in another way. She looked at the sleeping factory, at the weeds in the courtyard, at the faded sign, and instead of the pain, instead of even the gratitude, she felt a new thing, a light thing, almost glad: she felt that that factory and she were no longer the same thing, that the factory could sleep for ever and she went on awake, alive, standing on the road, with the quince paste in her hands and all the years ahead, and that that — to be alive outside the dead factory — was the final proof, the only one that interested her now, that she had never been the Spinner, that she had always been Libânia, and that Libânia, that one, did not close.

She thought, in that moment, of the husband, of the gentle man who told her sit beside me and whom she had never known how to obey, and she was sorry not to have him now, to sit at last beside him doing nothing, to tell him that she had learned, late, what he had wanted to teach her. But she thought also that perhaps it was not too late for everything: that she could honour that man by learning, now, to be there, that each morning she spent at the door watching the sun doing nothing was, in a way, a late answer to his invitation, a sitting down at last, sixty years later, in the lap she had refused. And she thought of the father, and no longer with grief, but with a kind of tired forgiveness: the father had given her what he had, which was little, and taught her what he knew, which was wrong, but he had done it without malice, and she forgave him, not because the father deserved the forgiveness — that was still the old account, that of deserving —, but because to carry the grief weighed on her and she no longer wanted weights, she wanted to go light the rest of the way.

She looked at the factory and said to it, low, without drama, like one who speaks with an old acquaintance: sleep. Sleep at ease, for I no longer need you to know that I exist. And she went away, up the road, to her house, to her kitchen-garden, to her life that she was finally learning to inhabit, and behind her stayed the Spinner sleeping its sleep of a factory ended, and before her opened the rest of her life, which was little in years and immense in mornings, each of them being born free, each of them to be lived and not deserved, each of them confirming, at break of day, the truth that had cost her her whole life to learn and that she now knew in the body, in the kitchen-garden, in the hands, in the reconquered five in the morning: that she existed before doing anything whatever, and would exist after she could no longer do anything, and that between one thing and the other, in that interval of light that is called a life, the only task that truly fell to her — the one they had never taught her, the one she was learning now, late and whole — was not to work, nor to prove, nor to deserve, it was simply, while it lasted, to be alive and to notice it.
