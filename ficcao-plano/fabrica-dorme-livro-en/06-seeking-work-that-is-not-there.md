## 6. Seeking work that is not there

Libânia set about seeking work, and the search for work at fifty-eight years old, in a village without a factory, was one of the hardest things of that winter, not for what it did to her purse, but for what it did to her soul.

She began by getting her papers in order, with the care with which she got everything in order. She wrote, in her hand of one who had learned to write late and so wrote with care, a sheet with her working life, the years at the Spinner, the functions, the responsibilities, and on writing it she felt again the old pride, because it was a fine sheet, a full life, forty-three years of rising and of not failing, and she thought, naively, that with that sheet any master would want her. She did not yet realise that the sheet that filled her with pride was, for the masters, a museum sheet: the admirable story of a worker of a world that had ended, written in an old hand, with nothing of what the new times asked. She took the sheet from door to door like one who carries a treasure, and she saw, door by door, the treasure being worth less and less, she saw the men look at the sheet out of politeness and hand it back without interest, and she went on learning, with each handing-back, that the thing she had of most value, her working life, had no market, that no one bought forty-three years of the Spinner, and that therefore, in the raw account of the world, she was now worth little more than if she had never worked, which, for one who had made of work the measure of everything, was a kind of annihilation.

Because Libânia did not need to work to eat. She had the small pension from the factory, and the house was hers, and the children were raised and away, and she, alone, with little, managed. It was not hunger that set her seeking work: it was another thing, deeper, that at first she did not realise and that she went on realising as the doors closed.

It was, besides, the first time in her life that Libânia had enough without having to earn it every day, and that, which for another person would be a rest, for her was a torment, because the pension reached her at the end of the month without her having done anything to deserve it, and Libânia did not know how to receive money she did not sweat for. She looked at that money as at an alms, felt almost ashamed to draw it, and counted it and recounted it and spent it with a tightening, because in her head money was the measure of work, and a money with no work beneath it seemed to her a money with no ground, almost stolen. It was the logic of the twelve years still working: the bread is deserved, and a bread that is not deserved tastes of guilt. And it was for not being able to bear that sensation of receiving without deserving that she threw herself into the search for work with the fury of one who flees a shame, without realising, yet, that what shamed her was not not working, it was the idea, put into her by force as a child, that one who does not work has no right to eat, nor to rest, nor even, at bottom, to exist. She was not seeking a wage. She was seeking a proof. She was seeking someone who would tell her come and work for me, because that come and work for me was, for Libânia, the confirmation that she was still worth something, still served, still deserved, and each door that closed, each no, madam, we have no work, and you are already of an age, did not take her bread, it took her being.

She knocked at many doors. She went to the shops of the village, to the town hall, to the farms, to ask whether they needed someone for whatever it was, for cleanings, for sewing, for accounts, for whatever there was, and almost everywhere she heard the same: that there was no work, that everything was bad since the factory had closed, that she understood, and besides, madam, with all respect, you are already of an age, and there was work that was for young people. And Libânia heard the you are already of an age and it was like hearing you are no longer worth anything, you no longer serve, you no longer deserve, and each time it hurt more, because each no was a confirmation of what she most feared: that she had ceased to be useful, and that if she had ceased to be useful, then she had ceased to exist, because for Libânia to exist and to be useful were the same thing since that Friday of the twelve years.

And there was a particular humiliation in it being her, precisely her, knocking at those doors. Because Libânia had been, in the village, an authority: the forewoman of the Spinner, the one who gave work, the one who decided who entered and who left the factory, the one sought out by the mothers who wanted to set their daughters to yielding. For forty years they had come to her to ask for work, hat in hand, and it was she who gave or did not give, it was she who was on the side of one who decides. And now everything had reversed, and it was she, the forewoman, who went from door to door hat in hand, asking for what for a lifetime she had given to others, and that reversal had a bitter taste that went far beyond the age or the money: it was the fall from a place, the loss of a position that had been, for decades, the column of her pride. She felt that the village watched her fall, that the same people who had respected her now watched her beg, and the shame of that, the shame of having been someone and no longer being, was sometimes harder to carry than the lack of work itself.

There was one door, in particular, that hurt her. She went to a factory of another village, farther off, that still operated, to offer herself, and the man who received her, on learning that she had been forewoman of the Spinner, was even kind, praised her, said he knew of her reputation, that she was a good professional, but that no, that he had no place, that with her age it did not pay to train her, and he dismissed her with a handshake and an it is a pity, and Libânia came out of there with the it is a pity echoing, and she realised, on the way back, that that man had given her, without meaning to, a cruel portrait of her situation: that she was a good professional of a profession that no longer existed, an expert in a dead trade, a forewoman of a closed factory, and that all her worth, all of it, was bound to a thing that had ended, and that therefore her worth had ended with it, because she had never had any worth that was not that of the work.

The journey back from that factory was long, and Libânia made it looking out the window of the bus, and it was on that journey that she allowed herself, for the first time, to weep for the closing, alone, with her face turned to the glass so that no one would see. She had not wept since the last day of the Spinner, and not even then had she wept, because the forewoman does not weep; but there, on the bus, far from the village, with no one who knew her, she let herself weep low, and it was a weeping that surprised her with its depth, because it was not only for the factory, it was for a whole life that she saw, in that moment, with a new clarity: a life in which she had given everything and in which, in the end, no one had a place for her, in which she had proved for sixty years that she was worth something and in which it had been enough for a factory to close for all the masters of the world to tell her it is a pity. She wept the injustice of that, and she wept also, without yet knowing it, a deeper thing: she wept the girl of twelve years who had learned to be worth something by the work, and who now, old, discovered that she had wagered her whole life on a worth that could be lost, on a worth that depended on a factory, on a master, on a body that grows old, and that therefore had never been a secure worth, had never been truly hers.

There was, in that search, a haste that she herself did not understand. It was not the haste of one who is hungry, for hungry she was not; it was the haste of one who flees, the haste of covering the hole before the hole swallowed her, and the more doors closed the faster she ran to the next, like one who, sinking, clings to everything that floats. And each closed door did not make her stop to think, it made her accelerate, seek farther, lower what she asked, until she reached the point of offering herself for what she had never in her life thought of doing, only so that someone, anyone, would tell her yes, would give her a task, would save her from the void with some work or other. And it was that blind haste, she realised later, the clearest sign that it was not work she was seeking, because one who seeks work for money ponders, chooses, calculates; and she pondered nothing, clung to the first thread, like the drowning one, and the drowning one does not choose what they cling to, they cling to whatever there is, and she was drowning, not in poverty, but in void, and she sought in the work not a sustenance, but a plank not to go to the bottom.

And it was in that desperate search, in that knocking at doors that did not open, that Libânia touched the bottom of her wound, and she touched it because of a thing Felismina told her, the worker who had been reborn. Libânia, unburdening herself, told Felismina of the humiliation of seeking work and not finding it, and Felismina, who went about happy with her sweets, looked at her and said, with the clarity of those who have cured themselves, a thing Libânia did not expect: but Libânia, you do not need work, you have the means to live. What you are seeking is not work, it is someone to tell you that you are still worth something. And that, woman, that one does not ask of a master. That is a thing you have to tell yourself, and no master is going to give it you, however many doors you knock at. You go about asking the masters for a thing only you can give yourself, and that is why none gives it you, because it is not theirs to give.

Libânia wanted to argue with her. She wanted to tell her that it was easy to talk so for one who had a knack for sweets and a life ahead, that not everyone knew how to invent a joy out of nothing like Felismina, that there were those who only knew how to work and for whom to take the work away was to take everything away. But Felismina, who knew her, did not let her flee, and said more, and said it with gentleness, which was what hurt most: look, Libânia, I have also been where you are. I also knocked at doors. I also needed someone to tell me that I was worth something. And I only began to come out of there the day I realised I was begging others for a thing I had to learn to give myself. You are no less than you were when you commanded the factory. You are exactly the same. What changed was that you ceased to have someone to tell you that you were worth something, and as you never learned to say it yourself, you were left with nothing. But the lack is not of work, woman. It is of a voice within you that tells you what your father never told you.

There was still, after that, a last attempt, and it was the one that made her hit the bottom of the humiliation. She learned that they needed a woman to clean a shop, at night, after closing, a work of little, of floors and of windows, and Libânia, who had been forewoman of a factory of more than a hundred workers, went to offer herself to clean a shop at night, and the owner, a woman younger than her, measured her from top to bottom and said that that was hard work, that she did not know whether she would hold up, at her age, and Libânia, who had held up sixty years of factory, felt in that I do not know whether she will hold up such a humiliation that the blood rose to her face, and she had to hold herself back so as not to answer badly, and she came out of there, and it was the last door she knocked at, because she realised, on the way home, that she had humiliated herself enough, that she went about begging strangers for the right to be useful and that each begging left her smaller, and that if she went on she would end up accepting to clean floors at night only to have someone to prove to that she still served, and that that, more than poverty, would be the final defeat of the girl of twelve years, the proof that sixty years later she went on putting the wage on the table waiting for a look of pride that no one was going to give her any more.

And Libânia was left with that, and it hurt her, because it was true: she was not seeking work, she was seeking worth, she was asking others to confirm for her that she deserved to exist, and that, she realised, was a bottomless well, because no confirmation from others ever sufficed, never was enough, she had worked sixty years gathering confirmations and it had been enough for a factory to close for all of them to undo themselves, because they were confirmations from outside, and what was empty was within, and what is empty within is not filled with anything from outside, however much one works, however many doors one knocks at, however many proofs one gives. Libânia's hole was not a hole of work: it was a hole of self-worth, opened at twelve years old by a father's look, and no work in the world had filled it nor was going to fill it, because it was a hole that was only filled from within, with a thing Libânia had never learned to give herself: the conviction that she was worth something by existing, and not by what she did.

And yet, even realising this, even knowing that Felismina was right, Libânia could not stop seeking work from one day to the next, because to know a thing with the head and to feel it in the body are two very distant things, and between one and the other there is a long road. She went on knocking at some doors, now more out of habit and out of fear of the void than out of hope, and each no went on hurting, although it now hurt in another way, with a shadow of awareness behind it, Felismina's voice telling her low that is not what you are seeking. It was like one who knows they are drinking too much and drinks all the same, knowing: the lucidity had come before the strength to change, and Libânia had still to cross that bitter time, the time in which one already sees the error and still cannot not commit it, and it was that time, that of seeing and not managing, that went on pushing her, without her knowing it, toward the bottom where everything was going to change, because there are errors one only lets go of after hitting the ground with them.
