## 8. The register

The conversation with the Keeper did not end at the line of birth, and what came after was, for Libânia, one of the deepest things of that winter, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, had about the worth of people a wisdom that came from centuries of seeing them be born, work and die, and of knowing what remained of each one when everything else went away.

Libânia came back to the House of the Register more times, that winter, not for any papers, only to be with the Keeper, and she marvelled at herself for it, she who had never had time nor knack for conversations, who had always thought visits a waste of time. But there was in that house of old books, in the smell of the paper and in the measured voice of the Keeper, a quiet she found nowhere else, and Libânia, without saying it, went there to fetch it, went there like one who goes to a place where the void hurts less. The Keeper always received her, without haste, and let her stay, and went on showing her the books, the old records, the hands of the Keepers who had preceded her, and Libânia went on realising that that house kept a deeper memory than that of the factory, a memory that was not of those who had worked, but of those who had existed, and that perhaps it was for that she felt at peace there: because it was the only place in the village where her worth did not depend on producing anything, where it was enough to have been born to have one's line, the same as everyone's.

"I see the whole village pass through these books," said the Keeper, putting away the book of births. "And I see all the trades pass. I see the farmers, the spinners, the grocers, the seamstresses enter the records, and I see them leave, when they die, and I am going to tell you a thing I have learned: that the trade does not stay. What a person did does not stay in my books, Libânia. I do not write, on the line of anyone's death, she was a good spinner, he was a good farmer. I write the name, and the parents, and the children, and the dates. What stays of a person, in the records, is not what they did: it is that they were, that they existed, that they were among us, that they loved and were loved, that they had a place. The work is what one does while one is alive, and it is important while one does it, but it is not what stays, it is not what defines a person before eternity. What stays is the having existed."

The Keeper spoke of that with the serene authority of one who deals with death every day and so knows better than anyone what life is worth. She showed Libânia, without drama, a page of the book of deaths, some page, with its names and its dates, and said: do you see? Here is not written what these people did. It does not say whether they were rich or poor, whether they worked much or little, whether they were useful or useless. It says only that they existed, and when they ceased to exist, and who was left to weep them. For death, Libânia, we are all equal: what stays of us is not the record of work, it is the place we occupied in the heart of those who knew us. And that place is not won by working, it is won by being there, by loving, by being. There are people who worked a whole life and who left no longing at all, because they were never truly with anyone. And there are people who did little and who left a hole in the world, because they knew how to be there. What stays is the presence, not the production.

And Libânia, on hearing this, realised a thing that changed everything: that she had put her whole life into what does not stay, into the work, and almost nothing into what stays, into the existing, the loving, the being. That she had been an excellent forewoman and a stranger to her own children, that she knew the machines of the factory better than she knew her grandchildren, that she knew everything of wool and almost nothing of herself, of her likings, of her wishes, of the person she was beneath the badge.

She thought of the grandchildren, whom she scarcely knew, and the shame of that hurt her more than the closing. She knew the names, of course, and the birthdays, and she always sent them her money, in her way, because to give money was the only form of love she had learned; but she did not know what games they liked, nor the fears they had, nor their voices on the telephone, because she had never had time, because the time was the factory's, and the grandchildren, like the children before them, had grown up seeing the grandmother as a respectable and distant figure who worked much and appeared little. And the Keeper was right, Libânia realised with a tightening: when she died, the grandchildren would not weep her as one weeps a grandmother of a lap and of stories, they would weep her as one weeps a good person one knew little, and that, the lukewarm longing of those who scarcely knew us, was the bitter harvest of a whole life spent producing instead of being there, and Libânia, who had always boasted of owing nothing to anyone, realised that she did owe, and much: she owed presence, the presence she had never given, and that that debt, unlike those of money, which she had always paid, had stayed unpaid her whole life. She had invested everything in what is wiped out and almost nothing in what lasts, and now, with the closing, what is wiped out had been wiped out, and she was left only with the little she had cultivated of what lasts, and that little frightened her, because it was indeed little.

Libânia was silent digesting that, and there was in her a resistance, the resistance of one who hears the meaning of a whole life called into question. Because if what stays was not the work, then what had her life been? Sixty years of factory, of five in the morning, of bleeding hands and bent backs, sixty years of not failing, of proving, of rising — all that, then, was what, smoke? It was hard for her to accept that the thing she had put everything into was the thing that did not stay, because to accept it was to call into question not only the future, but the whole past, was to have to look back and see, instead of a life well spent, a life spent on what is wiped out.

"Then I wasted my life," said Libânia, and it was a bitter conclusion.

"No, woman," said the Keeper, with a serene firmness. "Do not say that. You did not waste your life. You worked, and your work fed a family, and held a factory that fed half the village, and that is no waste, it is a worthy life, it is a good life. The error was not that you worked. It was that you thought you were only worth something by working. It was that you put into the work not only your hands, which is what the work asks, but your whole existence, your right to be alive. The work was meant to be a thing you did, and you made of it the proof that you deserved to be, and that was your wound, and the fault was not yours, it was a father's who taught you, without meaning to, that love is paid for. But it is not late, Libânia. You are still alive, you still have years, and you can learn now what you did not learn as a girl: that you exist by right, that you were already worth something before working and are worth something after, that your line in this book was written at birth and is not erased by the closing of any factory. What you have to do is learn to inhabit that line. To exist in it. To occupy your life not as one who works it, but as one who lives it."

Libânia clung to the distinction the Keeper made, between the working and the thinking that one is only worth something by working, because it was a distinction that saved her from having to renounce her whole life. They did not ask her to regret having worked, nor to think she had done wrong; they asked her only to separate two things she had glued together at twelve years old and that had gone glued for sixty — the doing and the being worth something. She could go on being who she had been, a woman of work, of skilled hands, of pride; she had only to cease making her right to be alive depend on it. The work could go back to being what it should always have been: a good thing one does, and not the proof that one deserves to exist. And there was an enormous relief in this separation, though it came accompanied by vertigo, because if her worth did not come from the work, then where did it come from? The Keeper said it came from birth, from the line, from the simple fact of being; but that, for one who had learned the contrary for sixty years, was a land without a map, and Libânia realised that she was going to have to learn to live in that new land without any of the old certainties that had guided her, only with the Keeper's word and with a line of old ink guaranteeing her that she was enough.

Inhabit. The word stayed with Libânia, because it was exactly what she did not know how to do: to inhabit her own life. She had worked it, sixty years, but never inhabited it, never simply been in her life, lived in it, as one lives in a house, with pleasure, with leisure, without having to deserve it. And she realised that it was that which she had before her, in the years that remained to her: to learn to inhabit the life she had always worked, to dwell in it, to exist in it without proof, and that that, at the end of sixty years of work, was the hardest and newest thing that had ever been asked of her, harder than any shift, than any announced closing, than any machine, because it was to learn to do the one thing she had never done: nothing. To be there. To exist. To be, without doing.

She thought of the word as she would think of a new trade, because it was so that her spirit took hold of things: by comparison with work. To inhabit a life must be like inhabiting a house, and she thought of her house, and realised, with a start, that not even her house did she inhabit. Her house was a place where she slept and from which she left at five; she had never been there by day, never sat in a chair only for the pleasure of being seated, never looked out the window except in haste, never made of the house a dwelling, only a resting-place between shifts. And if not even the house, which was of brick and tile, she inhabited, how was she going to inhabit life, which was a thing much harder to dwell in? She realised that she was going to have to learn the two things at the same time, the house and the life, and that perhaps to learn to be in one was to learn to be in the other, and that the Keeper, without saying it in those words, had given her the task of the years that remained to her in a single verb, and that that verb, to inhabit, was going to be the hardest she ever conjugated, she who had conjugated her whole life only the verb to do.

The Keeper, before letting her go, said to her one more thing, and said it putting away the books, without solemnity, like one who comments on the weather: you know, Libânia, I see a lot of people like you pass through here, people who retired and were left without ground, and almost all are men and women who were good at what they did. Those who did the work badly leave it without grief. Those who gave themselves, those are the ones who suffer, because they put themselves wholly into what they did and kept nothing for themselves. You were one of those who give themselves. And that, which was your greatness while there was a factory, is now your wound, because you gave yourself so wholly that no Libânia at all was left outside the work. But there is, Libânia. There always is. It is only unworn, like a garment one kept one's whole life for an occasion that never came. The occasion has come now. Wear it.

The Keeper, that winter, was for Libânia what Felismina was in another way: a master of a wisdom the factory had never given her. But they were masters of different things, Libânia realised later, and she needed both. Felismina taught her through the body, through the liking, through the making-sweets-without-end, taught her to live; the Keeper taught her through the word, through the memory, through the books, taught her to understand. And without understanding, Libânia would not have managed to live in another way, because she was a person of reasons, she needed to understand why she should change before the body accepted changing, and it was the Keeper who gave her the reasons, who put into words the wound of a life, who showed her, in ink, on the line of the birth, the proof that she was worth something before doing. The reason came from the House of the Register; the practice came from Felismina's kitchen; and Libânia, between the two houses, between the two teachings, went on remaking herself that winter, slowly, as one remakes a person who came apart, not all at once, not by miracle, but piece by piece, with the help of those who, having come apart before, know the way back.

She came out of the House of the Register with the word inhabit in her pocket, like one who carries a seed, and crossed the village, and passed by the closed Spinner, and looked at it, at the corpse of the factory that had been her life, and instead of the usual pain she felt, for the first time, a different thing: a small wish, still without form, to learn to live without it, to discover, beneath the forewoman, the Libânia the Keeper swore existed since birth, the Libânia of the line written before any work, the Libânia who was worth something, simply, for being. She did not know how. But for the first time she wanted to, and to want to, she realised, was already a beginning, was already the seed of the word inhabit germinating in the dark, slowly, as seeds germinate, unseen, waiting for the spring.
