## 5. The void of the hours

The weeks after the closing were, for Libânia, a struggle against the hours, because the hours, without a factory, were an enemy, were an abyss, were a desert that opened before her every morning and that she did not know how to cross.

Preciosa came to her on a Thursday, with a basket of cabbages on her arm, and Libânia opened the door with a dishcloth in her hand and a smell of bleach behind her, because it was nine in the morning and she had already scrubbed the kitchen twice.

"I brought you some cabbages," said Preciosa, and stopped in the doorway, sniffing the air. "Did you wash this just now?"

"I washed it yesterday. And today. It had dust."

Preciosa came in, set down the basket, and stood looking at the kitchen from top to bottom, slowly, in that way she had of seeing everything at once, the same with which she saw a sheet of accounts in the small office. The floor shining. The curtains, which Libânia had taken down to wash, hanging on the line in the yard and dripping. The ironing board set up in a corner, with a shirt yet to be pressed.

"Sit down," said Preciosa. "I will make the coffee."

"I will make it."

"Sit down, Libânia."

And it was Preciosa who put the water on the fire, in Libânia's kitchen, a thing that had never happened in forty years, because at the Spinner and outside it the one who did was always Libânia, and Libânia stood beside the table not knowing what to do with her hands, and the hands, with no task, went by themselves to fold the dishcloth in four, and to unfold it, and to fold it again.

"You do not stop," said Preciosa, with her back turned, stirring the coffee. "I come here at nine in the morning and the house is as though people were coming to lunch. Why did you wash the curtains?"

"They were dirty."

"They were nothing of the kind." She turned, with the two cups. "They were clean, Libânia. You washed them because it was nine in the morning and you do not know what to do with nine in the morning."

Libânia did not answer. She sat down, at last, because Preciosa put the cup before her and pulled out the chair, and she drank the coffee standing with her body and sitting with the rest, on the edge of the chair, like one who sits in a waiting room.

"I sleep badly," she said, at the end of a silence, and she said it low, because it was the first time she said it to anyone. "I lie down and I have no sleep, because I did not spend the day. Yesterday I got up at three and went to do the ironing."

"At three in the morning."

"A shirt of my boy's, that he left here I do not know how long ago and never came back for." Libânia looked at the board, at the unfinished shirt. "I ironed it at three in the morning, a shirt no one is going to wear, and halfway through I caught myself and was afraid, Preciosa. I was afraid of myself. Because I realised that it was not tidying, it was flight. I was fleeing the silence with an iron in my hand."

Preciosa set down the cup. She did not say poor thing, she did not say you have to react, she made none of the faces the others made. She only stayed looking at her friend, with that gentleness of hers that hurt more than any hardness, and at the end she said:

"I warned you. That afternoon, in the office, do you remember? I told you that you were going to have to learn to live without this, which was the thing you never knew how to do. I did not tell you out of malice. I told you because I have the kitchen-garden, and the grandchildren, and my man, and I have somewhere to fall when the factory lets go of me. What do you have, Libânia? Beneath the factory, what do you have?"

"I have the house."

"The house is where you sleep. I am asking you what you have." Preciosa waited, and as Libânia did not answer, she softened her voice. "You have nothing beneath, sister. And it is no shame, it is only the truth. You gave yourself entirely in there. I kept a bit of me on the outside, and you kept nothing, and now the factory has closed and I am left with my bit on the outside and you are left with the nothing. That is why you wash clean curtains at nine in the morning. Because the nothing frightens, and the work covers the nothing."

Libânia stayed looking at the bottom of the cup. Out in the yard, a curtain drained, drop by drop, into the silence.

"And how does one learn?" she asked, at last, and it was a true question, with no hardness at all, almost a girl's. "To live without this. How does one learn?"

"I do not know whether it is taught," said Preciosa. "I think that perhaps it is like everything: one tries, and one fails, and one tries again. But the first thing, Libânia, the first of all, is for you to stop covering the hole. As long as you cover it with tasks, you are never going to see it, and what one does not see one cannot treat. You have to leave it uncovered a while. You have to bear one morning without scrubbing anything and see what appears. It frightens, I know. But it is thereabouts."

The two of them stayed silent, and Preciosa, who had her life out there waiting, the kitchen-garden and the man and the grandchildren, got up at the end of a while, and at the door she turned once more, with the empty basket in her hand:

"Keep the cabbages. And do me a favour: today wash nothing else. Sit at the door one hour, even if it is only one, and bear it. Tomorrow I will come and learn whether you bore it."

She went out, and Libânia was left alone in the kitchen that shone, with the unpressed shirt on the board, and the silence closed around her as the water closes over a thing one lets go into the river. She tried to do what Preciosa had asked. She sat at the door. She bore perhaps ten minutes, with her hands fidgeting in her lap, and at the tenth time one of the hands went by itself in the direction of the ironing board, Libânia got up, and went to press the shirt, and while she pressed it she wept with rage against herself, because not even one hour at the door was she able to bear, not even one hour, she who had borne sixty years of factory.

And then she tried to fill them, the hours, with the desperation of one who is drowning. When the house no longer gave anything to scrub, she went out to offer herself. She went to the house of Aurora, the neighbour next door, knocking at the door to ask whether she needed help with a bit of sewing, a cleaning, whatever it was, and Aurora, a younger woman, who did not understand what was happening, stood at the door looking at her with a face of astonishment that was already half pity.

"Help with what, dona Libânia?"

"With whatever you need. With a bit of sewing. Washing the windows. With whatever there is."

"But I... I do not need anything, dona Libânia." Aurora hesitated, and looked at her in a way that Libânia knew, the way of one who looks at a thing that has broken. "Are you well, senhora?"

"I am. I am fine. It was only in case you needed it."

And she went back, down the cobbled street, with Aurora's poor thing burning on her back even though Aurora had not said it, because there were things one did not need to say, they were in the look. And that afternoon, and on those that followed, there went on being noticed around Libânia that village compassion that is half pity and half gossip, and she felt it, felt the looks as she passed, and she began to go out only very early or at the end of the day, to avoid the square, to swerve away from the conversations, to shut herself, without noticing, into an isolation that she took for dignity and that was only one more step down.

There was, in the midst of those weeks, an afternoon that Libânia did not forget. She decided, with an effort, to do what she saw others do: to sit in the sun doing nothing, like a person who rests. She went to the square, chose a bench, and sat down, determined to be a woman who sits in the sun. At her side, two benches along, was old Aprígio, who had been a porter at the Spinner in the old time and who now spent his afternoons there, in the sun, with his stick between his knees, watching the people go by.

"Well then, dona Libânia," he said, marvelling at nothing, because he had reached the age at which nothing marvels. "Have you come to sit down?"

"I came to catch a bit of sun."

"It does good. I do this every afternoon. I sit here, I watch the people pass, and when the afternoon ends I go home. It is my life now, and it is no bad life."

Libânia tried to imitate him. She sat up straight, set her hands in her lap, and tried to watch the people pass as Aprígio watched, without haste, without a destination, only being there. She bore it a little. But her hands began to fidget in her lap, and there came over her the sensation that everyone who passed looked at her and thought *what is that woman doing there idle*, the forewoman of the Spinner sitting on a bench in the middle of the afternoon like one who is no longer good for anything, and at the end of ten minutes her heart began to beat as though there were a danger, and Libânia got up.

"Are you going already?" said Aprígio. "But the senhora has only just arrived."

"I remembered a thing I have to do."

"Indeed," said the old man, with no malice at all, and it was worse for having no malice. "You, the ones who work, it is always so. You never learned to be. I did not know how either, you know? It cost me a year to learn to sit on this bench without feeling myself useless. A whole year. Now I manage it. You will manage it, give yourself time."

Libânia went home almost running, with her heart leaping, like one who flees a fire, and at home, trembling, leaning against the shut door, she realised the size of what was happening to her: that she was not capable of sitting on a bench ten minutes, that the simple doing nothing gave her a physical distress, of the body, real, like a smoker the lack of tobacco. It was not a lack of will. It was incapacity. They had atrophied in her, her whole life, the muscle of being, and now, when she needed it, she discovered that she did not have it, that she had never exercised it. An old man of eighty years, who had carried loads his whole life, had learned in a year to sit on a bench; and she, who had taught half the village to work, did not last there ten minutes.

And there was another thing, deeper, that those weeks brought her, and that was the discovery of Sundays. Sundays had always been, even in the time of the factory, the difficult day, the only one on which the Spinner did not open, and Libânia, her whole life, had crossed Sundays counting the hours until Monday, seeing to a thousand things at home so that Sunday would pass quickly. But in the time of the factory Sunday ended, and Monday came, and Monday saved her. Now, without a factory, every day was Sunday, and that was perhaps the cruellest discovery of that winter: that she had spent her life enduring the bad days on account of the good ones that came after, and that suddenly there were no longer good ones to come after, there was no longer a Monday that saved Sunday, only an endless line of Sundays, all alike, all empty, with nothing ahead to give them meaning.

It was on one of those Sundays, at night, that Libânia sat at the kitchen table, exhausted with an exhaustion that was not of the body, and did, with the pencil on an old sheet of factory accounts, the accounts of her life. She calculated, more or less, how many hours she had worked: forty-three years, twelve hours a day many times, six days a week, and the number came out enormous, it filled the sheet, almost a whole life of hours of work. And then, on the other side of the sheet, she tried to put the hours she had lived outside the work, hours only hers, of pleasure, of rest, of simply existing, and she was left with the pencil in the air, because there were almost none to write, the far side of the sheet stayed almost blank, and Libânia looked at that sheet, one side full and one side empty, and realised, with a tightening, that her life had been that: one side full of doing and one side empty of living. She had passed through life working it, and almost never living it. And now, at fifty-eight years old, with the factory closed, she saw before her all the years of the empty side of the sheet, and she did not know what to write in them, because she had never learned, because no one had taught her, because in a house of too many mouths no one teaches a child to live, one is taught to yield.

It was a withdrawal, she realised, and the comparison came to her without her seeking it, with the sheet of accounts before her: she was like one who lost a drug. The work had been her drug, the thing that made her exist, that filled the void, and now, without the dose, came the tremor, the distress, the desperate need for a task, of anything to do. And like those who lose the drug, she went about seeking it everywhere, in curtains washed twice, in offers of help to Aurora, in shirts pressed at three in the morning, without seeing that the problem was not the lack of work, it was the dependence on it.

She folded the sheet of accounts, slowly, and put it away in the drawer, and she stayed sitting at the kitchen table, on the Sunday night, without getting up to do anything, and it was the first time she obliged herself to bear the silence without fleeing it, and it lasted little, and it was little, but Preciosa had said that it was thereabouts, that one had to leave the hole uncovered a while in order to be able to see it, and Libânia, on that Sunday night, at the table, left it uncovered the time of a cup of tea, and what she saw, at the bottom of the hole, was the question the factory had never let her ask and that now pursued her about the whole house: who are you, Libânia, when you are doing nothing? And she did not know how to answer. But, for the first time, she stayed sitting with the question instead of getting up to flee it, and it was little, and it was everything, because it was the first hour, in sixty years, that she spent not covering what hurt her.
