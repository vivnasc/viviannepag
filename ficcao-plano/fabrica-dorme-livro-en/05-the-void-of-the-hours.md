## 5. The void of the hours

The weeks after the closing were, for Libânia, a struggle against the hours, because the hours, without a factory, were an enemy, were an abyss, were a desert that opened before her every morning and that she did not know how to cross.

There is a way of time passing that only one who has worked much knows, and that is a disguised blessing: the work devours the hours, and one who works does not see the time pass, wakes and it is night, begins the week and it is Friday, and life drains away so, quickly, full, without those dead hours in which one is left alone with one's own head. Libânia had had that blessing her whole life, without knowing that it was a blessing disguised as a curse, or a curse disguised as a blessing, she did not rightly know; the work had spared her, for sixty years, the weight of the empty hours, and had spared her, above all, the encounter with herself that the empty hours force. And now, without work, she paid for everything at once: there came to her lap all the hours she had never had to cross, and with them came the encounter put off a whole lifetime, the encounter with a Libânia she had never known because she had never given herself time, and that encounter, in the empty hours, was the thing that most terrified her, because she was afraid of going to herself and finding no one at home.

For forty-three years, Libânia's hours had been full, marked, organised by the factory: at five she got up, at six she was at the Spinner, and from there until night the hours were the work's, each one with its task, each one full, and Libânia crossed them without seeing them, because an hour full of work is not seen, it is lived. And suddenly, with the closing, the hours emptied, and Libânia discovered a terrible thing: that an empty hour is much longer than a full hour, that time, without work, stretches in an unbearable way, and that a day without a factory was longer than a week of factory, because each empty hour had to be crossed one by one, consciously, and Libânia did not know how to do that, did not know how to cross an empty hour, had never had any.

And so she tried to fill them. She tried, with the desperation of one who is drowning, to fill the empty hours with anything that resembled work. She cleaned the house, from top to bottom, and when the house was clean, she cleaned it again, because a clean house is an empty hour, and Libânia could not bear the empty hours. She washed curtains that were washed, scrubbed a floor that shone, tidied cupboards she had tidied the day before, and found herself inventing tasks as one invents excuses, undoing a thing only to have to do it again, and there was in that a distress she recognised and did not know how to check: the distress of one who needs to be busy so as not to have to meet the silence.

She offered herself for work that there was not, she went to the neighbours' houses to ask whether they needed help, with a bit of sewing, a cleaning, anything, and the neighbours, not knowing what was the matter, marvelled, and Libânia realised she was cutting a poor figure, the forewoman of the Spinner offering herself for cleanings, but she could not stop, because to stop was to be left with the empty hours, and the empty hours were the void, and the void was not to exist. She even got up in the middle of the night, once, to do the ironing, because she had woken and the house was in silence and the silence was unbearable, and the iron, at least, made noise and gave her hands something to do, and it was while ironing at three in the morning, a shirt no one was going to wear, that Libânia caught herself and was afraid, because she realised that that was not tidying, it was flight, and that she was fleeing, with her hands full of invented tasks, from the one question the factory had never let her ask and that now pursued her about the whole house: who are you, Libânia, when you are doing nothing?

The neighbour women began to talk. In a village, everything is noticed, and it was noticed the forewoman of the Spinner offering herself for cleanings, strolling without a destination, knocking on doors, and there formed around Libânia that village compassion that is half pity and half gossip, and she felt it, felt the looks as she passed, the poor thing said low, and that was for her almost worse than the void itself, because Libânia had always been the one looked at from below upward, the respected one, and to be looked at with pity was a humiliation that added to all the others. She stopped going out at the hour when there were people in the street. She came to do the errands very early or at the end of the day, to avoid the square, to avoid the conversations, and she went on shutting herself, without noticing, into an isolation that she took for dignity and that was only one more step down, because the void of the hours, shut up at home and with no one, grows, and Libânia, in fleeing the looks of pity, was giving herself more to the one look she could not flee, which was her own, the hardest of all, the one that repeated to her, from the mirror, that she was no longer good for anything.

Because it was that, Libânia realised in those weeks, what terrified her in the empty hours: that in them she ceased to exist. While she worked, she existed; the task made her exist, gave her a place in the world, a reason to be on her feet. Without a task, in the empty hours, Libânia had no place, no reason, no proof that she was on her feet for some motive, and that lack of motive was an abyss, was the confirmation of her deepest fear: that without work she did not exist, that it was only the work that made her exist, and that the work taken away no one was left, only a body at five in the morning with nowhere to go.

It was a withdrawal, Libânia realised, and the comparison came to her without her seeking it: she was like one who lost a drug, and the work had been her drug, the thing that made her feel well, that made her exist, that filled the void, and now, without the drug, came the withdrawal, the tremor, the distress, the desperate need for a dose, a task, anything to do. And like those who lose the drug, Libânia sought it everywhere, in cleanings, in offers of help, in anything that resembled work, without realising that the problem was not the lack of work, it was the dependence on it, it was her having made of work the only source of existence, and that as long as she sought another dose she was never going to cure the dependence, she was only going to put it off.

Sundays had always been, even in the time of the factory, the difficult day, the only one on which the Spinner did not open, and Libânia, her whole life, had crossed Sundays counting the hours until Monday, seeing to a thousand things at home so that Sunday would pass quickly. But in the time of the factory Sunday ended, and Monday came, and Monday saved her. Now, without a factory, every day was Sunday, and that was perhaps the cruellest discovery of that winter: that she had spent her life enduring the bad days on account of the good ones that came after, and that suddenly there were no longer good ones to come after, there was no longer a Monday that saved Sunday, only an endless line of Sundays, all alike, all empty, with nothing ahead to give them meaning. And it was there, in that discovery, that Libânia began to realise that she had a problem that was not resolved by getting work, because it was not a lack of work: it was a lack of a reason to get up that was not the work, and that one, she realised with dread, she had never had, never, in her whole life, on any Sunday.

The body, which for forty-three years had been her most faithful tool, began to give signs of not knowing how to be still. Everything hurt her, now that she did not work, pains she had never had when she spent herself from sun to sun, as though the body, without the effort it was used to, took to complaining of tedium. She slept badly, she who had always fallen asleep from exhaustion the moment she put her head on the pillow; now she went to bed without tiredness, because she had not spent the day, and stayed hours with her eyes open in the dark, with her head grinding, and she realised that the work had given her, her whole life, this too: a good tiredness that made her sleep without thinking, and that without that tiredness she was left, at night, with a head she had never learned to silence, because she had always silenced it on account of the worn-out body. Without a factory, she discovered that she had a head, and that the head, left alone in the dark, was not good company for one who had never learned to be with herself.

There was a night, in the midst of those weeks, when Libânia sat at the kitchen table, exhausted with an exhaustion that was not of the body, and did the accounts of her life, and the accounts gave her a thing that frightened her. She calculated, more or less, how many hours she had worked in her life: forty-three years, twelve hours a day many times, six days a week, and the number was enormous, it was almost a whole life of hours of work. And then she tried to calculate how many hours she had lived outside the work, hours only hers, hours of pleasure, of rest, of simply existing, and she realised, with a tightening, that there were almost none, that her life had been almost all work, that she had passed through life working it and almost never living it, and that now, at fifty-eight years old, with the factory closed, she saw herself before a thing she had never had and did not know what to do with: time. Time to live, and not to work. And she did not know how to live, she only knew how to work, and time, that good that everyone wants and that she had never had, was to her, now that she at last had it, not a present, but a condemnation, because it was time to exist, and she did not know how to exist, she only knew how to produce.

She tried, once, to do what she saw others do in the free hours, and it was a disaster that taught her much. She sat in the square, one afternoon, on a bench in the sun, as she saw the retired ones sit, determined to rest, to do nothing, to be a person who sits in the sun. She lasted perhaps ten minutes. At the end of ten minutes she was distressed, with her hands fidgeting in her lap, with the sensation that everyone who passed looked at her and thought what is that woman doing there idle, and she got up, and went home almost running, with her heart racing, like one who flees a danger. And at home, trembling, she realised the size of what was happening to her: that she was not capable of sitting on a bench ten minutes, that the simple doing nothing gave her a physical distress, real, of the body, like a smoker the lack of tobacco. It was not a lack of will. It was incapacity. They had atrophied in her, her whole life, the muscle of being, and now, when she needed it, she discovered that she did not have it, that she had never exercised it, that it was like asking one who never walked to get up and walk. And it was from that afternoon on the bench that Libânia stopped scolding herself for not managing to rest and began, slowly, to treat herself with more patience, to realise that to learn to be was going to be as slow and as hard as it would have been, for an adult who never walked, to learn to stand.

And it was that, Libânia realised that night, the thing she had to learn, if she did not want to end like Engrácia: she had to learn to inhabit time instead of filling it, to live the hours instead of working them, to exist instead of producing. And she had not the least idea how it was done, because no one had taught it her, because she had learned, at twelve years old, that time is to be deserved with work, and never, in sixty years, that time is to be lived. She noticed, in those weeks, a thing she had never noticed: that everyone around her seemed to know how to be. The old men on the bench in the square knew how to be. The neighbour women in conversation at the door knew how to be. Even the cats in the sun knew how to be, lying for hours with no guilt at all. Only she did not know, and that made her feel, for the first time in her life, behind, as though everyone had learned in childhood a simple thing that had been hidden from her. And it was true that it had been hidden from her: in a house of too many mouths, no one teaches a child to be, one is taught to yield, and Libânia, who had learned to yield better than anyone, came old without knowing the thing the cats know from birth, which is to be in the sun without thinking that one should be doing something else. She looked at the cats, almost with envy, and thought that she had to learn from a cat to live, she who had taught half the village to work, and there was in this a new humility, painful, that of one who realises that what they know is of no use for what they need, and that they are going to have to be an apprentice again, old, of the simplest thing in the world.

And she looked at the empty hours she had before her, the years of retirement, the years without a factory, and instead of seeing them for what they were, a second life, an opportunity, she saw them as a desert, as a void, as the daily proof that she was no longer good for anything, and she realised that she was going to have to learn, from the beginning, at fifty-eight years old, the thing she should have learned as a child and that the factory had never let her learn: that to exist is enough, that to live does not need to be deserved, that time is not a debt to be paid with work, it is a gift to be inhabited with pleasure.
