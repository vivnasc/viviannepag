## 2. The first week

To understand Libânia one must go back to the first week, the first week of her work, back then, when she was a girl, because it was in that week that she learned the thing that made her who she was, and that now, with the closing of the factory, was undoing her.

The house where Libânia was born was poor, and it was poor with the poverty that has too many mouths: many children, little bread, and that silent accounting that is done in poor houses, in which each child is, before all, a mouth, a cost, one more division of a bread that is not enough. Libânia was one of the middle ones, neither the eldest who helped nor the youngest who was coddled, she was one of the middle, one of the mouths, and she grew up feeling, without anyone telling her, that she was a weight, that the house had too many and that she was part of that too-many, that her existence was a cost the parents carried without joy, because in a house of too many mouths there is no joy in the mouths, there are accounts.

She remembered small things of that poverty, things others would have forgotten and that she had kept because they were, without her knowing it, the first lessons of her life. She remembered the mother dividing the soup and counting, without meaning to, the spoonfuls that fell to each one, and realising, as a girl, that there were too many mouths for the pot there was. She remembered being the one who ate afterwards, the one who was left with what remained, because she was one of the middle, and from the middle one does not claim. She remembered, above all, a time when she fell ill, as a small child, and having heard, from the room, the father say to the mother, low, that illness was all that was needed, one more expense, and having realised, with the terrible clarity of children, that to be ill was to be an even greater weight, that even to fall ill she cost the house, and having made, that day, without knowing it, the promise that would govern her life: that of not costing, of not weighing, of making herself, whatever it cost, a mouth that paid for itself.

Venâncio, the brother, was of the same house and the same poverty, and had drawn from it the contrary lesson, and that Libânia would only understand much later. Where she had heard "do not cost", Venâncio seemed to have heard "then it is not worth trying", and from a boy he shirked the work, found ways not to go, left the weight for the others, and the parents scolded him and gave him up for lost, and Libânia despised him, as a girl, with all the contempt of one who kills herself proving she is worth one who does not even try. It did not occur to her, then, that the two of them were fleeing the same wound by opposite doors: she working too much so as never to be the weight, he not working at all so as never to subject himself to having to prove it. She would take sixty years to realise that the brother she most despised was the only one who had seen, in his crooked way, the thing that escaped her her whole life.

And then came the age to go to the factory, and Libânia went, at twelve years old, as the girls of the village went, and at the end of the first week she brought home her first wage, and it was there, on the Friday of that first week, that the thing happened that decided her life.

That first week had been hard like everything that is first. The noise of the machines, which she would later come to call music, was at first an assault to her, and at the end of the first day her ears were ringing and her hands were bleeding from the rough thread she was not made for. Her feet hurt from standing twelve hours, her back hurt from bending over the machine, and at night she wept low on the straw pallet so that the siblings would not hear her, because she was twelve years old and wanted her mother and not the factory. But she got up the next morning, and went back, because to give up did not even cross her mind: to give up was to go back to being the mouth that did not yield, and she already sensed, without knowing how to say it, that the factory was her only way out of that place, that it was there, in that noise and that bleeding of the hands, that it was decided whether she was going to be a weight her whole life or whether she was going, at last, to come to be worth something. She endured the whole week without complaining to anyone, and on the Friday, when they put the wage in her hand, she felt a thing she did not know how to name and that was, she would realise much later, the first time she had felt proud of herself: she had endured, she had yielded, she had a money of her own, earned by her, to take home.

She brought the money, little, the wage of an apprentice of twelve years, and came into the kitchen where the father was sitting at the table, at the end of the day, with the mother at the fire and the small siblings about the corners, and she laid the coins on the wood, before him, without saying anything, because there was nothing to say, the wage of the children was the house's and was handed over so, in silence.

The father looked at the money. He did not take it up at once. He looked at those coins on the table, and then raised his eyes, slowly, and looked at the daughter, standing before him with her hands still bleeding from the thread, and he stayed a moment looking at her without saying anything, and Libânia, at twelve years old, saw happen in the father's face a thing she had never seen in it. His eyes, which had always looked at her with the tired account of one who has too many mouths, changed. They straightened. They lit up.

"The little one yielded," said the father, and it was not to her that he spoke, it was to his wife, but he spoke of her. "Did you hear, Adelaide? The little one already brings."

The mother came to the table, wiping her hands on her apron, and looked at the money, and looked at the daughter, and in her eyes there was another thing, wetter, that Libânia did not know how to read then and would only read much later: there was pity, and there was pride, and there was a thing resembling a plea for forgiveness.

"Twelve years old," said the mother, low.

"Twelve years old and already brings," said the father, and took up the money at last, and counted it slowly, coin by coin, and as he counted it he smiled, a small smile that Libânia had never seen turned toward her. "Look at this one. I always said this one was of those who would come through." And he turned to the daughter, and said to her, to her now, looking her in the eyes: "Come here. Sit here beside your father. Today you eat first."

And Libânia sat beside the father, that Friday, and it was the first time in her life that the father called her to his side, and it was the first time in her life that the father looked at her with pride, with respect, with a thing resembling love, because until then the father had looked at her as he looked at the mouths, with the tired account of one who has too many, and now, with the money in his hand, the father looked at her in another way, looked at her as one who brought, who gave, who counted, who was worth something.

It was not only the father's look. It was everything that came with the look, that Friday and on those that followed. It was the mother serving her the soup first, her, the one who brought, when before she was the last. It was the father addressing her at the table, telling her the things of the village as one tells one who counts, ceasing to treat her as one of the little ones and coming to treat her as one of the house. It was feeling, in a few weeks, her place in the family change at its foundation: she ceased to be the middle mouth, the one one did not notice, and became the Libânia who worked at the Spinner, the one who gave, the one who had a weight at the table because she put weight on the table. And a child of twelve years does not distinguish these things, does not know they are learning a lesson, only feels the cold and the warmth, and Libânia had felt the cold of not being worth anything and the warmth of coming to be worth something, and her body learned, before her head could argue, that the warmth came from the work and that without work the cold came back, and that learning of the body, made at twelve years old, was deeper than any idea, because the things the body learns as a child are not unlearned with arguments, they cling to the bones.

And the girl Libânia, at twelve years old, realised, in that look, the lesson that governed her sixty years: that she had come to be worth something. That until that Friday she was not worth anything, she was a mouth, a weight, a cost; and that from that Friday she was worth something, because she brought money, because she worked, because she gave instead of only taking. The father's look changed that Friday, and with it Libânia changed: the girl who was a weight became the young woman who was worth something, and the difference between the two was the work, was the wage, was the weekly proof that she deserved the bread she ate, that she deserved to exist in that house, that she deserved the father's look of pride.

And Libânia clung to that proof with the force of one who found, at last, the way to be loved. She worked. She worked like no one, her whole life, because each week of work was a week in which she deserved to exist, in which she deserved the look of pride, in which she ceased to be a mouth and was someone.

And what made that prison so perfect, so impossible to see from within, is that it brought real rewards. The Libânia who worked too much was praised, was promoted, was respected; the world, which pays well one who gives themselves without measure, went on giving her, in exchange for her giving, exactly the signs she needed never to doubt that she was going by the good way. Each rise in the factory confirmed for her the lesson of the twelve years; each praise of the master was the father's look again; each worker who called her dona Libânia, with respect, was a crumb of the food she lived on. And so the wound went on disguising itself as virtue, because no one corrects a person who kills themselves working — on the contrary, they hold them up as an example —, and Libânia crossed life being pointed to as a model, the hard-working woman, the one who had reached forewoman, without anyone, not even her, realising that beneath the model there was a terrified girl fleeing, every day, from a cold she had felt at twelve years old and that she had never again allowed herself to feel. She rose in the factory, from apprentice to spinner, from spinner to shift leader, from shift leader to forewoman, and each rise was one more proof, one more step in the demonstration that she deserved, that she was worth something, that she was not the weight she was born. And never, in sixty years, did she stop proving it, because to stop proving it was, in her head, to go back to being the mouth, the weight, the girl the father looked at with the tired account, and that was worse than death.

The father had died long ago, and had died proud of the daughter, of the Libânia who had reached forewoman, and Libânia had wept for him and kept his pride as the most precious thing she had, without ever realising what that pride had cost her: that to deserve it, she had had to spend her life proving, working, demonstrating, and that never, not once, in sixty years, had she allowed herself to exist without earning it, to rest without having gained it, to be loved without having proved it. The father had loved her when she began to yield, and Libânia had learned, with that conditional love, that love is earned with work, and that without work there is no love, no worth, no existence worthy of the name.

And what she had never wanted to see, and that the closing was going to force her to see, was that she had done to her children what the father had done to her, in her way, without malice, even thinking she did well. She had had two children, and had raised them from afar, from the factory, because her way of loving them had been to work so that they would not go through what she had gone through, and into that she had put everything, and not into her hands and her lap and being there, because to be there did not yield, and one who learns that love is to give does not know that to be there is also to give. The children grew up with the mother at the Spinner, with the mother arriving tired and leaving in the dark, with the mother proud of the sustenance and absent from everything else, and they left the village as soon as they could, and they got on with her at the distance of those who respect a mother who sacrificed herself and do not know a mother one plays with, because that mother had never had time to exist. Libânia said, the few times she spoke of the children, that she had given them everything, and it was true that she had given them everything that is bought, and it was true, also, though she did not say it nor think it, that she had been lacking to them in everything that is not bought, and that she had repeated with them, without noticing, the only grammar of love she knew: that of the father, that one is worth something by giving, and that to give is the wage on the table, and never, never, simply to be there.

There was, in Libânia's life, a single time when she came near to learning something else, and she lost it, and only now, with the closing, did she remember it. It had been in the time of Joaquim, the husband, a gentle man she had married young and who had died early, and who loved her in a way she had never known how to receive. She remembered one evening, in particular, a winter evening like so many, he sitting beside the fire with his pipe, she coming and going, putting away the crockery, folding the clothes, sweeping what was already swept, and Joaquim following her with his eyes, and at a certain point holding out his hand to her, slowly, without haste:

"Libânia. Come here. Leave that."

"I have the crockery unfinished."

"The crockery waits. I do not." And he smiled, and patted the bench beside him, with his palm, by the fire. "Sit here beside me. Only that. You do not have to be always doing, woman. Sit a while doing nothing, with me."

And Libânia looked at his outstretched hand, at the empty place on the bench, and did not know how to go. She did not know what one did sitting beside a man with nothing to do with one's hands, and that, instead of calling her, gave her a distress she did not understand, and she answered that she was coming, that it was only to finish the crockery, and got up to see to something or other, and Joaquim lowered his hand slowly, and sighed, and let her go. And Libânia took that sigh for a man's laziness, for the temperament of one who likes to see his wife still so as to have company, without realising that it was pity, that it was a man watching the wife flee his lap for a task, incapable of simply being with him. Joaquim died early, and Libânia wept for him and worked, as always, and only now, sixty years of work later, did she realise that that man had offered her, without knowing it, the cure, that that *sit here beside me* was exactly what the Keeper and Felismina would tell her decades later, and that she had had, at home, in the evening, beside the fire, the whole lesson at his outstretched hand, and had refused it, because a woman who learned that love is earned with work does not know how to sit down to receive a love that asks no proof at all, she flees from it, because a free love frightens more than the lack of love one who only knows the love that is paid for.

And it was for that, Libânia realised now, with the factory closed, with the five in the morning empty, that the closing of the Spinner was not only to lose a job: it was to lose the proof. It was to go back to being the mouth too many, the weight, the girl the father looked at with the tired account, before that Friday that had saved her. Without a factory, without work, without a wage to put on the table, Libânia went back to the place from which the first week of work had taken her: to the place of one who is not worth anything, of one who does not deserve, of one who exists without proof and therefore, in the crooked logic she had learned at twelve years old, does not deserve to exist. The closing of the factory did not leave her only without work. It left her without the one thing that, in sixty years, had proved to her that she deserved to have been born.
