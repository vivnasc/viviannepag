## 12. I have arrived

Time passed, and when Aurora looked back she understood that she had become, without noticing it, a woman she did not know: a woman who stopped, who stayed, who savoured, who was where she was — a woman who, for the first time in her life, had arrived. And the way she knew she had arrived was by a word, a small word she said one day, without thinking, and that was the word her whole life had lacked.

It took its time, that morning of arriving, and it did not come suddenly; it came at the end of a long learning, of many relapses, of many days when Aurora went back to running and had to go back to stopping. It was not a sudden miracle, it was a slow conquest — though the word conquest no longer served, because the arrival is not conquered, it is let happen —, the fruit of months, perhaps years, of patient training of the art of stopping, of being, of being enough. And when at last it came, that morning of I have arrived, it came mild, without fanfare, as the true things come, without the din of the victories Aurora knew, with the simple silence of a water that fills a tank and overflows. There was no festivity, there was no public, there was no trophy; there was only a woman sitting at the door, with a tea, feeling, for the first time, that it was enough. And it was more that morning without fanfare than all the summits with festivity, because the summits with festivity had left her empty and that morning without festivity filled her, and Aurora understood that the true arrivals are so, mild, silent, without a public, unlike the victories, which are noisy and empty, and that she had traded her whole life the mild and full arrivals for the noisy and empty victories, and that now, at last, she learned to prefer the mild full to the noisy empty.

It was on a morning, any morning, with nothing special about it, and it was perhaps for having nothing special about it that it was the morning of the arrival. Aurora was sitting at the door, in the sun, with a tea, with nothing to do, with no goal, with no road to open, only being, watching the village wake, feeling the sun. And there came to her, from the depths, a sensation she had never had, a sensation of simple fullness, of being well, there, in that moment, without needing anything more, without anything lacking, without the and now? pointing to the next. And Aurora heard herself say, softly, to herself, a word she had never said in all her life of victories: I have arrived. I have arrived. I am here. And here is enough.

She noticed, that morning, that the word arrived had two lives, two senses, and that she had only known one. Her whole life she had used arrived in the sense of arriving at a place, at a summit, at a goal — I have reached the top, I have reached the end, I have got there —, an arrived of movement, of a destination reached. And she discovered now the other sense, the arrived of one who stops and is enough — I have arrived, I am well, that is enough —, an arrived of repose, of fullness, that was not about having reached a place, it was about being whole where one is. And she understood that she had pursued her whole life the first arrived, that of reaching, and that what she lacked was the second, that of being enough; and that the two, though the same word, were almost opposites, because the first is an arrival that at once comes undone into a new departure, and the second is an arrival that stays, that does not depart for anywhere, that is enough. Aurora had learned, at last, the second arrived, the one that was lacking, and it was to say it, that morning, in its new sense, that made her feel, for the first time, what the word promised and had never given: the repose of one who arrived and stays, and not the restlessness of one who arrives and departs.

And it was the first time in her life that Aurora said I have arrived and felt it, without the father's voice, the and now?, coming right after to spoil everything, because she had learned to silence it, because she had given herself, at last, the permission to arrive that the father had never given her. And she had reached no summit, that morning; she was sitting at the door of her house, with a tea, having conquered nothing, having climbed nothing; and yet she had arrived more that morning, in that I have arrived said in the sun with nothing special, than in her whole life of summits, because the arrival, she understood at last, had never been a place up there, it had always been this: the capacity to be where one is and to find it enough, to stop running and to feel that one has enough, to say I have arrived not because one reached a top, but because one stopped needing more.

She came to have, in that new life, mornings like that, mornings of I have arrived, and they went on becoming her food, her anchor, the daily proof that she had changed. They were not mornings of anything special; they were common mornings, of sun, of tea, of being; but Aurora had learned to see them as arrivals, to savour them, to say to them I have arrived, and that new capacity, that of transforming a common morning into an arrival, was the greatest conquest of her life, she who had conquered so much. Because to conquer summits, she had understood, anyone of hunger and talent conquers; but to transform a common morning into an arrival, to know how to be in a morning with nothing special and to feel it enough, that is a rare art, rarer than to conquer summits, and it was that art Aurora had learned, late, and that was worth more than all the summits she had conquered, because the summits gave her emptiness and the common mornings gave her, now, fullness. And there was a justice in this, that the woman who had climbed the greatest summits came to find the fullness not in them, but in a common morning at the door of her house, because it showed that the arrival had never been on the summits, it had always been in the common mornings, within reach of everyone, and that she had lost her life climbing summits when the arrival awaited her, all the time, on any morning, if she stopped to live it.

And she understood, that morning, the irony of her whole life: that she had run her whole life in search of an arrival that was, all the time, within reach of a simple decision, that of stopping and of contenting herself; that she had climbed all the stairs in search of a thing that was not at the top of any, that was down below, stopped, in the decision to be enough; that she had worn herself out pursuing a finish line that always drew back, when the arrival had never been a line to reach, it had always been a decision to take, the decision to say I have arrived, here, now, that is enough. It had taken her a whole life, and an empty summit, and a Keeper, and a sister, to understand a thing so simple; but she had understood it, at last, and better late, because the years left to her, those, she was going to live arrived, and not running, and a life arrived, however short, is worth more than a whole life of running without arriving.

This does not mean that Aurora became a stopped woman, doing nothing, because that was not what she learned, and it would be easy to misunderstand. Aurora went on doing, being capable, putting her hand to things and doing them well, because that was hers, it was a gift, and it was not a matter of renouncing it. What changed was not the doing, it was the relationship with the doing: Aurora ceased to do in order to arrive and began to do for pleasure; she ceased to do in order to rise and began to do in order to live; she ceased to do because the and now? pushed her and began to do because she felt like it, or not to do when she did not feel like it, without guilt. The difference was all interior, invisible from outside, but it was all the difference: the same capable Aurora, the same woman of doing things, but free of the tyranny of the goal, of the next step, of the and now?, doing now from the arrival and not in search of it, which gave her doing a lightness and a pleasure it had never had when it was running.

And she understood, with time, that there was a new freedom in being able to choose, at last, her goals, now that she knew how to arrive. Because one who does not know how to arrive does not choose goals, they are dragged by them, they run after the next without stopping to ask whether they want it; and one who knows how to arrive can choose, can say this goal I want, this one not, this one is worth it, this one not, because they no longer need any to be worth something, and so they can choose them freely, for pleasure and not out of hunger. Aurora discovered that, knowing how to arrive, knowing how to be enough, she had gained the freedom to choose what she pursued, free of the father's and now?, and that that freedom — that of pursuing only what was hers and only because she wanted it, and of stopping when she wished — was the true arrival, not an end of all movement, but a movement at last free, chosen, enjoyed, from a ground of contentment and not from an abyss of hunger.

She thought of the sister, Conceição, who had arrived early, in her small life, and she no longer looked at her from above nor from below, she looked at her as an equal, with gratitude, because it had been the sister, the one who had stayed, who had taught her what she, the one who had risen, did not know: to arrive. And she thought of those who, in the Square, knew how to be in the sun without hurry, and she no longer found them people without ambition, she found them masters of an art she had learned late, the art of arriving, of being, of being enough. It happened to her, in that new life, a thing the old Aurora would have thought impossible: she began to help others to arrive. Because there were, in Véspera and beyond it, people as she had been, people running without arriving, caught in the stairway with no top, and Aurora, who knew that prison from within, began to speak with those people, to tell them her story, to tell them what she had learned late: that the arrival is not on the next step, that one can run forever and never arrive, that to arrive is to stop and to be enough. And some heard her, and to some she perhaps spared years of empty running; and Aurora discovered, in that, a new meaning, that of transforming her story of running into a light for those who still ran. And there was a beautiful irony in this: that the woman who had run her whole life after empty goals found, in the end, a full goal, that of helping others to stop, and that that goal, unlike all the others, filled her instead of emptying her, because it was a goal of arrival and not of flight.

And she thought of herself, of the Aurora she had been, the first at everything and arrived at nothing, and she had for her a sad tenderness, and forgave her the running, because she understood that she had run the best she knew how, with the hunger they had put inside her, and that she had not known how to stop because no one had taught her, and that now, at last, she had learned, and that to learn to arrive, even late, was the best victory of her life, the only one that was not a step to the next, the only one in which she could, at last, stay.

It was not a perfect cure, and it would be a lie to tell it so, because the and now? does not die for good, it sleeps and wakes, and there were days, after that morning, when the old hunger came back, when Aurora caught herself running again to a goal, measuring herself by what was lacking, hearing within the father's and now? But the difference, now, is that she already knew the way back, already knew how to stop, already knew how to say enough, had already tasted the savour of arriving; and when the hunger came back, instead of obeying it and running, Aurora recognised it — it is my father's and now?, it is not me —, and stopped, and breathed, and went back to her I have arrived, to her morning in the sun, to her contentment. She did not cease to feel the hunger; she learned not to obey it, which is a different and possible thing. And she went on becoming, with time, more and more the arrived woman and less and less the runner, not because the runner disappeared, but because the arrival went on gaining ground on her, day after day, I-have-arrived after I-have-arrived, until the arrival was her normal state and the running the exception, when before it had been the contrary.

And on that morning, and on those that followed, Aurora did what became her new way of living: she stopped, she stayed, she savoured, and said, to herself, without looking at the next step she no longer sought, the phrase that was her arrival and her repose: I have arrived. I am here. And here is enough. Aurora's life, at the end of this story, did not become stopped, and it would be a mistake to understand it so, as though to arrive were to cease doing. Aurora went on active, capable, doing things, because that was her nature and there was no renouncing it; what changed was that she came to do from the arrival and not in search of it, for pleasure and not out of hunger, choosing and not being dragged. She went on, even, reaching things, because she was capable; but she reached them now in another way, without the hurry of leaping at once to the next, stopping in each one to savour it, saying to it I have arrived before going on, if she went on at all. The difference was not in doing less, it was in arriving at what she did, in inhabiting each deed instead of crossing it running; and that difference, invisible from outside, was all the difference, because it transformed the same active life from an empty running into a series of full arrivals. Aurora proved, with her new life, that one does not need to stop doing to arrive, that one can be active and arrived, capable and content, that the problem had never been the doing, it had been the never arriving at what one does; and that the cure was not to cease doing, it was to learn to arrive at what one does, to inhabit it, to be enough in it, and that so one could have the two things, the capacity and the arrival, the doing and the being, that she had always thought incompatible and that after all only needed to be reconciled.

And the father's and now?, that one, went on falling silent, slowly, defeated not by a greater victory, which was what it had always asked, but by a thing it had never understood and that the daughter had at last learned: that there is no victory that silences the and now?, and that the only way to silence it is to stop answering it, is to tell it enough, I have arrived, and to stay, at last, where one is, which is, after all, and always was, the only place where one can arrive.
