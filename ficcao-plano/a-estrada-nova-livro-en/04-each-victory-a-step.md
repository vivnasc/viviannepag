## 4. Each victory, a step

There is an image, that of the horizon, that serves for what Aurora's life was: the horizon is that line where the sky meets the earth, and that seems a place one can reach, and toward which one can walk; but however much one walks one never reaches the horizon, because it draws back as one advances, it keeps always the same distance, it is a line that exists for the eyes and not for the feet, a place one sees and does not reach. Aurora's arrival was a horizon: she saw it, there, ahead, within reach of one more effort, of one more step; and she walked toward it, ran toward it; and it always drew back, kept always the same distance, one more step ahead, because it was a horizon and not a place, a line that draws back and not a place where one arrives. And Aurora ran her whole life after her horizon, without ever understanding that it was a horizon, that it drew back by nature, that there was no walking that reached it; and it took the empty summit to show her that the horizon had drawn back once more, even from the top, for her to understand, at last, that it was not a place, that she would never arrive, that she had run her life pursuing a line that existed only to make her run.

The mechanism of Aurora's life was simple and cruel, and she knew it by heart without ever having put a name to it: each victory, instead of being an arrival, became at once the step of the next, and so no victory got to be savoured, because it was swallowed, in the instant it happened, by the goal she already pointed to.

Aurora had a phrase that said much, and that she repeated without understanding what it revealed: that's done, and now? She said it at each thing that ended, at each deed she completed — that's done, and now? —, and the phrase had two parts that said everything: the that's done, with which she dispatched what had ended, without savouring it, and the and now?, with which she leapt at once to the next. There was never, between the that's done and the and now?, the space of a savouring, of a rest, of an I managed it; the that's done swallowed the deed at once and the and now? launched at once to the next, without an interval, without breath, without arrival. And it was so at each thing, from the smallest to the greatest: that's done, and now?, that's done, and now?, a whole life of that's dones and and nows chained without end, without ever an enough, without ever a let us stay here, without ever an interval in which one could say I have arrived. Aurora's own phrase, the that's done and now?, was her illness in three words, the illness of one who dispatches what they have and runs to what is lacking, and she said it a thousand times a day without ever hearing what it revealed, until the day when, at last, she heard it, and understood that that little phrase she said without thinking was the portrait of her whole life, and that to change the life passed, also, through changing the phrase, through learning to say, instead of that's done and now?, a new phrase: how good, I will stay here a while.

Aurora reached a thing — a deed, a recognition, a rise — and there was an instant, a single one, in which she could have stopped to enjoy, to say I managed it, to savour. But that instant did not get to exist, because Aurora's head, trained by the father's and now?, was already on the next step before her feet even landed on the step reached. Scarcely did she reach a summit than she already saw from there the next summit, higher, and instead of enjoying the summit she was on, she felt only the distance left to the next, and that distance robbed her of the enjoyment of what she already had. It was like climbing a mountain and, at each summit, instead of admiring the view, seeing only the next mountain, larger, to be climbed, and feeling, instead of the pride of having climbed, the anticipated tiredness of the climb that was left.

There was a moment, in each victory, when the arrival was possible, and Aurora always let it pass. It was the moment right after conquering, the instant when the thing was done, reached, when one could stop, breathe, say I managed it, savour. That instant existed, in each victory, and was the door to the arrival; but Aurora never went in by it, because scarcely was the victory done than her head had already leapt to the next, and the instant of arrival passed without being lived, shut without her entering. It was like coming home and not going in, staying at the door and at once departing for another house; the house was there, the arrival was there, to hand, and Aurora passed by its door without going in, always on the way to the next. And they were thousands, across her life, those instants of arrival not lived, thousands of doors through which Aurora could have gone into the arrival and did not, because she always went in passing, and the sum of all those wasted instants was her life: a life full of doors to the arrival, all passed in haste, none crossed, and in the end a woman who had been a thousand times at the door of the arrival and never gone in, because to go in required stopping, and she never stopped.

And so Aurora never celebrated anything. Her victories passed without festivity, because for her they were not arrivals, they were points of passage to the next goal, and who celebrates a point of passage? There was a particular sadness in seeing Aurora receive a victory, for one who knew how to look, and few knew. The others, in bringing her their congratulations, saw a woman of success receiving one more success, and did not understand why she seemed, in the moment of triumph, more tired than glad, more absent than present; they attributed it to modesty, or to the tiredness of the effort, and did not imagine the truth, which was that Aurora, in the moment of triumph, was no longer there, was already at the next goal, and that the triumph, for her, was not an arrival to celebrate, it was a departure for the next running. And so the moments that for the others were the heights of Aurora's life, the triumphs, were for her the emptiest moments, because they were the moments in which she most clearly felt that to arrive brought nothing, that the summit reached was empty, that the running had no end; and Aurora learned to hide that emptiness in the triumphs, to smile, to thank, to feign the joy she did not feel, because to tell the truth — that she felt nothing, that the triumph was empty — would be incomprehensible to those who celebrated her, and she had no way to explain that the woman who seemed to have everything did not feel she had anything.

The others celebrated for her, the village, the family, those who admired her, and found it strange that she did not celebrate, that she received each deed with an air already distant, already elsewhere; and Aurora let them celebrate, and smiled, but within she was already running, already on the next step, and the festivity of the others sounded distant to her, almost incomprehensible, because for them she had arrived and for her she had arrived nowhere, had only passed one more point in the running without end.

And there was a word for what Aurora had, though she did not know it: that of one who is never satisfied, of one who always runs to the next goal without enjoying the reached one, of one who makes of life a stairway with no top. It is a way of being in the world that the world, in fact, rewards, because the world likes one who runs, who produces, who conquers without stopping, and gives them victories, recognition, success; and so one who runs like that is rarely warned that they run badly, on the contrary, they are applauded, given as an example, envied. Aurora had been applauded her whole life for the very thing that consumed her, given as an example for the running that emptied her, and so it had never occurred to her that there was something wrong, because everything around her told her that she was doing well, that she was a model, that she did what one should do. It is one of the hardest things, to understand that one is doing badly when everyone applauds us, to question a way of living that the world rewards; and Aurora only managed it when the empty summit showed her, without appeal, that the world's applause did not fill her emptiness, that she could be applauded to the end and die empty, and that what the world rewarded was not what would make her arrive.

And there was an enormous cost in this never arriving, a cost Aurora went on paying without tallying it. She paid it in enjoyment: she never enjoyed her victories, and a life of unenjoyed victories is, strange as it seems, a life poor in joy, because joy is not in winning, it is in savouring what one has won, and Aurora won and did not savour. She paid it in rest: she never rested, because to rest was to stop, and to stop was to fail, and so she ran without rest a whole life, and came to the height exhausted with an exhaustion that was not only of the body, it was of the soul, the exhaustion of one who runs for sixty years without ever stopping. And she paid it, above all, in presence: she was never present anywhere, because she was always, with her head, on the next step, and a person who is always on the next step is never where they are, never lives the present, always lives in a future that, when it comes, is no longer lived, because they are already in another future.

There were relationships Aurora lost for this, and each was a price she only tallied in the end. There was a man who loved her, in times past, and who wanted a life with her, and who went on tiring of loving a woman who was always elsewhere, always at the next goal, always putting off living until after one more conquest; and he went, that man, because no one bears to love one who is always departing, and Aurora saw him go and did not stop, because to stop to hold him was to lose time in the running, and the running came first. There were friendships that withered because Aurora never had time, was never present, was always running; there were people who went on drawing away from a woman with whom one could not simply be, because she was always going somewhere. And Aurora went on being alone, in the running, surrounded by admirers and with no one close, because admirers are won by winning and closeness is won by being present, and she won much and was present never, and so she had all of the first and nothing of the second.

It was the presence, perhaps, that cost her most, though she only saw it in the end. Because in being always on the next step, Aurora was not present in the things that mattered: she was not present in the people, who spoke to her and she was elsewhere; she was not present in the moments, which passed and she did not see them because she looked at the next; she was not present in her own life, which she lived running, without inhabiting it, always with her eyes on what was lacking and never on what she had. And a life not inhabited, however full of victories, is a life that passes by the one who lives it, and Aurora, at the height of her successes, had the strange and painful sensation of not having lived, of having run a whole life without living it, of coming to the end, or near it, with a full curriculum and an empty life, because she had traded living for winning, and winning, in the end, was not living.

There was an image Aurora used, later, to explain what her life had been, and it was that of a hunger that eats and never sates itself. There are hungers like that, she said, hungers in which one eats and eats and never becomes full, because the hunger is not of the stomach, it is of another thing, and what one eats is not what it asks. Aurora's hunger was of those: she ate victories, devoured them, one after another, and never became full, because her hunger was not of victories, it was of a thing the victories did not give — the sensation of being enough, of being worth something, of being able to stop —, and so she could eat victories forever and never sate herself, like one who is hungry for affection and tries to fill it with food, and eats and eats and goes on hungry, because food does not kill the hunger for affection. Aurora fed her whole life, with victories, a hunger that was not of victories, and so the hunger never passed, however much she won, and she confused the hunger that did not pass with a lack of victories, and won more, in a circle without end, without understanding that she was throwing the wrong food at a hunger it would never sate.

And that hunger robbed her even of the present, because one who is hungry is not in the present, they are in the next food. Aurora, with her hunger to arrive, was always at the next victory, never at the present one, and so her life, seen from within, was a succession of futures that never got to be presents, because scarcely did a future come than she was already in the next future. She always lived tomorrow, Aurora, never today, always at the goal that was coming, never at the place where she was, and a life lived always tomorrow is a life that is never lived, because life only happens today, in the present, and one who is always in tomorrow passes by the whole of it, as Aurora had passed, with the sensation, in the end, of not having lived, of having always been about to live, from the next victory on, without the next victory ever bringing the living it promised.

And the worst is that she did not know how to stop. Even beginning to see the cost, even beginning to feel the emptiness, Aurora could not stop running, because to run was all she knew, it was her identity, it was what made her be Aurora; and to stop put a deep fear in her, the fear of, without the running, being no one, of discovering that without the victories there was no Aurora, that she was only the running and that stopped nothing was left. And so she went on running, exhausted and empty, atop a life of successes, not knowing how to stop, not even knowing whether there was anyone beneath the runner, with no one yet to tell her that the arrival she pursued was not on the next step, that it never would be, and that the only way to arrive was the only one she had never tried: to stop running.
