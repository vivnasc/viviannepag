## 4. Each victory, a step

There was a man who loved Aurora, and who was called Tomé, and it was he who said to her, with his case packed at the door, the sentence she would only understand thirty years later: you are never where you are, Aurora. You are always on the next step.

Tomé was a good man, of Véspera, who had a metalwork shop and a pair of calloused hands and an easy laugh, and he had loved Aurora in the time when she was still young and already the first, and had wanted a life with her. Aurora had wanted him too, in her way, which was a way always deferred: after this one, she said, after I get this thing, we marry, we settle, we stop. And Tomé waited for an after this one that never came, because each this one was at once the next, and he went on tiring of loving a woman who was always elsewhere, always at the next goal, always putting off living until after one more conquest.

The scene of the leaving, Aurora kept her whole life without understanding, for decades, why it hurt. Tomé appeared one night with a cardboard case in his hand, those metalworker's hands gripping the handle of the case as though gripping a decision, and said, without anger, which was the worst: Aurora, I am going. It is not for another woman, it is not for want of love, it is for the tiredness of loving you from afar. You are always departing, even when you are sitting here in front of me. I speak to you and you are elsewhere, in the thing that is lacking, in what comes next. I do not want to be one more stop in your running. I want to be a place you arrive at, and you do not arrive anywhere, woman. You are never where you are. You are always on the next step.

And Aurora, who that night did indeed have her head on a thing she had to see to the next day, heard him, and felt a deep sorrow, and even so, even in that sorrow, found herself thinking of the next day, of the thing she had to see to. She did not run after him. She stood at the window watching him go down the street with the case, and let him go, because to stop to hold him was to lose time in the running, and the running came first. Only thirty years later, in the time of the arrival, would Aurora seek out Tomé, already old, to tell him he had been right; but in the time of the running she let him go and turned at once to the goal, and the goal swallowed the loss, as it swallowed everything.

Because the mechanism of Aurora's life was simple and cruel, and she knew it by heart without ever having put a name to it: each victory, instead of being an arrival, became at once the step of the next, and so no victory got to be savoured, because it was swallowed, in the instant it happened, by the goal she already pointed to.

She had a phrase that said much, and that she repeated without understanding what it revealed: that's done, and now? She said it at each thing that ended — that's done, and now? — and the phrase had two parts that said everything: the that's done, with which she dispatched what had ended, without savouring it, and the and now?, with which she leapt at once to the next. There was never, between the two, the space of an I managed it, of a rest. The that's done swallowed the deed and the and now? launched her to the next, without an interval, without breath, without arrival, a whole life of that's dones and and nows chained without end. Aurora's own phrase was her illness in three words, the illness of one who dispatches what they have and runs to what is lacking, and she said it a thousand times without ever hearing what it revealed, until the day when, at last, she heard it, and understood that that little phrase was the portrait of her whole life.

The others celebrated for her, the village, the family, those who admired her, and found it strange that she did not celebrate, that she received each deed with an air already distant, already elsewhere. There was a time when this was seen, and whoever saw it did not understand what they saw. Aurora had reached a great thing, and Conceição, the sister, gathered the family at home, set a table, made a sweet, called the neighbours, all to celebrate Aurora. And Aurora came to the party that was hers, sat at the head, and the others raised their glasses, and said pretty things, and she smiled and thanked them, and within she was already in the next thing, already measuring the distance left to the next summit. Halfway through the party, Conceição noticed that her sister sat with the glass in her hand and her gaze on the wall, absent, in the midst of all the noise, and she drew near and said low, in her ear: oh Aurora, this party is yours. Are you here? And Aurora gave the smile of one who has been caught and said yes, that she was, and she was not, and Conceição understood it and fell silent, and was left with a misgiving she would only know how to name years later.

The others, in bringing her their congratulations, saw a woman of success receiving one more success, and attributed her absent air to modesty, or to the tiredness of the effort, and did not imagine the truth: that Aurora, in the moment of triumph, was no longer there, was already at the next goal, and that the triumph, for her, was not an arrival to celebrate, it was a departure for the next running. And so the moments that for the others were the heights of Aurora's life were, for her, the emptiest, because they were the moments in which she most clearly felt that to arrive brought nothing, that the summit reached was empty. And she learned to hide that emptiness in the triumphs, to smile, to thank, to feign the joy she did not feel, because to tell the truth would be incomprehensible to those who celebrated her, and she had no way to explain that the woman who seemed to have everything did not feel she had anything.

There was an image that served for what Aurora's life was, that of the horizon. The horizon is that line where the sky meets the earth, and that seems a place one can reach; but however much one walks one never reaches it, because it draws back as one advances. Aurora's arrival was a horizon: she saw it, there, within reach of one more effort, of one more step, and she walked toward it, ran toward it, and it always drew back, one more step ahead, because it was a line that draws back and not a place where one arrives. And Aurora ran her whole life after her horizon without ever understanding that it was a horizon, and it took the empty summit to show her that it had drawn back once more, even from the top, for her to understand, at last, that she had run her life pursuing a line that existed only to make her run.

And there was an enormous cost in this never arriving, a cost Aurora went on paying without tallying it. She paid it in enjoyment: she never enjoyed her victories, and a life of unenjoyed victories is, strange as it seems, a life poor in joy, because joy is not in winning, it is in savouring what one has won. She paid it in rest: she never rested, because to rest was to stop, and to stop was to fail, and she came to the height exhausted with an exhaustion that was not only of the body, it was of the soul. And she paid it, above all, in presence: she was never present anywhere, because she was always, with her head, on the next step, and a person who is always on the next step is never where they are.

It was the presence, perhaps, that cost her most, and Tomé had been only the first to tell her so. Because in being always on the next step, Aurora was not present in the things that mattered: she was not present in the people, who spoke to her and she was elsewhere; she was not present in the moments, which passed and she did not see them; she was not present in her own life, which she lived running, without inhabiting it, always with her eyes on what was lacking. There were friendships that withered because she never had time; there were people who went on drawing away from a woman with whom one could not simply be, because she was always going somewhere. And Aurora went on being alone, in the running, surrounded by admirers and with no one close, because admirers are won by winning and closeness is won by being present, and she won much and was present never.

There was an image Aurora used, later, to explain what her life had been, and it was that of a hunger that eats and never sates itself. There are hungers like that, she said, in which one eats and eats and never becomes full, because the hunger is not of the stomach, it is of another thing, and what one eats is not what it asks. Aurora's hunger was of those: she ate victories, devoured them, one after another, and never became full, because her hunger was not of victories, it was of a thing the victories did not give, the sensation of being enough, of being worth something, of being able to stop. Aurora fed her whole life, with victories, a hunger that was not of victories, and so the hunger never passed, however much she won; and she confused the hunger that did not pass with a lack of victories, and won more, in a circle without end, without understanding that she was throwing the wrong food at a hunger it would never sate.

And that hunger robbed her even of the present, because one who is hungry is not in the present, they are in the next food. She always lived tomorrow, Aurora, never today, always at the goal that was coming, never at the place where she was, and a life lived always tomorrow is a life that is never lived, because life only happens today, in the present, and one who is always in tomorrow passes by the whole of it, as Aurora had passed, with the sensation, in the end, of not having lived, of having always been about to live, from the next victory on, without the next victory ever bringing the living it promised.

And the worst is that she did not know how to stop. Even beginning to see the cost, even beginning to feel the emptiness, Aurora could not stop running, because to run was all she knew, it was her identity, it was what made her be Aurora; and to stop put a deep fear in her, the fear of, without the running, being no one, of discovering that without the victories there was no Aurora, that she was only the running and that stopped nothing was left. And so she went on running, exhausted and empty, atop a life of successes, not knowing how to stop, not even knowing whether there was anyone beneath the runner, with no one yet to tell her, as Tomé had told her late and she had heard late, that the arrival she pursued was not on the next step, that it never would be, and that the only way to arrive was the only one she had never tried: to stop running.
