## 8. The Keeper of the Register

It must be said that Aurora had never been a person for going to the House of the Register, nor for Keepers, nor for conversations about the soul, because those things were, for her, stops, and stops were lost time. Her whole life had been of action, of doing, of rising, and not of stopping to think, to feel, to converse about what went on within; and so she came to the House of the Register already late, already at the bottom, already without an alternative, because a woman of action turns to the soul only when the action fails, and Aurora's action had failed at the empty summit. It was the bankruptcy of the action that took her to the soul, the exhaustion of the doing that opened her to the feeling, and perhaps it has to be so for people like Aurora, people who run, who only stop when the running fails, who only look within when the without is exhausted. The Keeper received her without reproaching the delay, without telling her why did you not come sooner, because she knew that each one arrives when they can, and that Aurora arrived now because now was when she could, because now was when the running had failed enough to let her stop and enter.

It was to the House of the Register that Aurora ended up, after the empty summit, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that began to show her where the arrival she had pursued in vain was, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, knew a thing about the worth of people that Aurora, with all her victories, had never learned.

The House of the Register was a strange place for Aurora, because it was the opposite of everything she valued. It was a place of stopping, of memory, of old and still things, a place where nothing rose nor ran, where time was kept instead of being spent in conquering. Aurora, her whole life, had avoided places like that, places of stopping, because they gave her a restlessness, the restlessness of one who cannot bear to stop; and it was significant that it had been a place of stopping, the House of the Register, that gave her what the running never had. Because the Keeper, in that still place, did not measure her by the running, did not ask her what she had conquered, was not impressed by her successes; she looked at her with the calm of one who measures people by another thing, and in that calm, in that not being measured by the running, Aurora found a rest she had never found, the rest of being before someone who did not ask her to rise, who accepted her stopped, who saw her not as the winner but as the person. And it was that rest, that of being seen without being measured by the running, that opened Aurora to what the Keeper had to tell her, because a person only listens when they feel accepted, and Aurora, for the first time, felt accepted not for what she did but for what she was, and it was that acceptance that let her, at last, listen.

She had gone to see to a paper, but she went lost, with the emptiness of the summit still upon her, and the Keeper, who saw deep, perceived it, and asked her how she was, and Aurora, who never unburdened herself because to unburden oneself was weakness and she had no time for weaknesses, that time unburdened herself, because the empty summit had broken something in her and she no longer had strength either for the running or for the mask. She told everything: the life of rising, the first at everything, the father's and now?, the summit reached and the emptiness she had found there, the discovery that the arrival did not come. And the Keeper listened, without being impressed by the successes that impressed the village, with the serenity of one who measures people by another measure, and when Aurora finished, she asked a question that disarmed her: and when did you arrive, Aurora? In your whole life, was there a moment, a single one, when you felt that you had arrived, that it was enough, that you were well where you were? The Keeper's question — when did you arrive? — stayed echoing in Aurora, because it was a question she had never asked herself and that, asked, revealed everything. Other people, asked, would know how to point to moments when they felt arrived: the day of the wedding, the birth of a child, a happy afternoon, a moment of peace. Aurora searched, and found none, because even the moments that for others would be arrivals, she had crossed running, her eyes on the next; and the total absence of a single moment of arrival in a whole life was, itself, the most eloquent answer, the proof that something was profoundly wrong, that a life without a single arrival was not a life well lived however full of deeds. And the Keeper, in asking the question and seeing Aurora find no answer, did not need to say anything more, because the very absence of an answer said everything: a woman who, asked when she arrived, cannot point to a single moment, is a woman who never arrived, however much she has risen, and that finding, which the Keeper's question forced, was the beginning of everything.

And Aurora searched, and did not find, because there had never been, because her whole life had been to run and never to arrive, and the Keeper, seeing the emptiness where an answer ought to be, said what she had to say.

You never arrived, Aurora, said the Keeper, because you go about looking for the arrival in the wrong place. You look for it at the height, on the next step, in the victory that is lacking, and it is not there, it never was, and so you never reach it, however much you climb. The arrival is not a place up there, woman. It is a thing that is done here, where one is, when one stops running enough to realise that one has already arrived. You have already arrived, Aurora, you arrived long ago, a thousand times; you only never stopped to feel it, because scarcely did you arrive than you were already running to the next. The arrival is not lacking to you because you did not reach it; it is lacking because you never stopped in it. And the Keeper went to fetch a book, that of the births, and showed Aurora her line, written long ago, before all the successes. Look, she said. Here you are, newborn, before you were the first at anything, before you climbed a single step. And you already had worth, Aurora, all the worth, written here, at birth, without having done anything to deserve it. Your worth was not at the top of any stair; it was here, in this line, from the beginning. You did not have to rise to be worth something. You were already worth something. You rose your whole life trying to reach a worth you already had from birth, and so the rising never sufficed you, because one does not reach by climbing what one already has by being born.

The line of birth had on Aurora an effect no victory had had: it calmed her. Because her whole life had been an attempt to prove her worth, to conquer it step by step, and the line told her that worth was not conquered, that it was already there, written at birth, given for free; and if the worth was already given, then there was nothing to conquer, and if there was nothing to conquer, then she could stop running, because the running had always been, at bottom, a running after worth, and the worth had been hers from the beginning. It was like telling one who runs after a thing that that thing is already in their pocket, that it always was, and that they can stop because they already have it; and Aurora, on understanding that the worth she had pursued her whole life was already hers from birth, felt able, at last, to stop, because the running had lost its object, and what remained was not to run to gain the worth, it was to stop to enjoy it.

The Keeper spoke with the serenity of one who measures people by a measure that is not the world's, and it was perhaps for that that her words reached Aurora where the world's never reached. Because the world had measured Aurora her whole life by the measure of the rising — how much you rose, how much you won, how high you got — and by that measure Aurora was a success, and that measure had been her prison, because a person measured by the rising has to rise always to be worth something. The Keeper measured by another measure, that of the entries, that of what remains of a person when one takes away everything they did: and by that measure, Aurora discovered, she was worth the same as everyone, neither more for having risen nor less, she was worth something for having been born, for having a line, for existing. And to hear herself measured by that other measure, after a life measured by the rising, was for Aurora a relief that disarmed her, because it was the first time anyone had looked at her and measured her not by what she had conquered, but by what she was, and to discover that by that measure she was enough, that she did not have to rise anything more to be worth something, was like setting down, suddenly, a weight she had carried since she was a girl without knowing she could set it down.

And she said more, the Keeper, and it was what stayed most with Aurora. She said: that and now? that pursues you, Aurora, the one that does not let you arrive, is not yours, it is your father's, and your father has died, and you go on obeying him, running to his and now? that never falls silent. But you are no longer obliged to obey him, woman. You can silence the and now? yourself, you can tell it enough, I have arrived, I stay here, you can give yourself what your father never gave you, which is the permission to stop, to arrive, to be enough. No one is going to give you that permission, Aurora, because the only one who could give it to you, your father, never knew how to give it; you have to give it to yourself. And the day you tell yourself you have arrived, that is enough, I am well, is the day you arrive, at last, at the end of the running, not because you reached the top, but because you stopped running.

The Keeper left her too an image, in farewell, that Aurora carried and that served her many times. She said: think of a person who walks along a beautiful road, Aurora, and who goes so fast, so with their eyes on the end of the road, that they see nothing of the way — neither the trees, nor the flowers, nor the people they cross, nor the views. They arrive at the end exhausted, and if they are asked what the road was like, they do not know how to say, because they did not see it, they went too fast, their eyes on the end. You were that person, your whole life. The road was your life, and you did not see it, you went with your eyes on the end, on the goal, on the next step, and life passed you by, all of it, without you seeing it. But there is still road, Aurora. There is still way left to you. And you can do it in another way, slowly, seeing the trees, the people, the views, living the way instead of running to the end. The end comes all the same, it comes for everyone, there is no need to run to it; what is needed is to see the road while one goes, which is the only thing one takes. And Aurora kept that image, that of the road one does not see for running to the end, and it was with it in her head that she began, slowly, to slow her step, to look around, to see, at last, the road of her life, that had always been there and that she had never seen for going too fast.

And Aurora heard that, and it was as though a door opened, because the Keeper had given her the key she lacked: that the arrival was not ahead, in what was left to climb, it was there, where she already was, awaiting only that she stop and recognise it; and that the and now? that prevented her from arriving was not a truth, it was the voice of a dead father that she could, at last, silence; and that she had the right, and the power, to give herself the permission to arrive, that the father had never given her. There was a silence, in the House of the Register, after the Keeper's words, and in that silence Aurora wept, and it was a weeping that surprised her, because she had not wept in a long while, she had no time to weep, weeping was a stop and she did not stop. But there, in that still place, before that woman who measured her by another measure, Aurora wept, and she wept her whole life of running, the father who had never let her arrive, the years spent rising, the people lost, the empty summit, everything; and the Keeper let her weep, without interrupting her, because she knew that that weeping was needed, that it was the weeping of a life that had never stopped to weep, and that to weep was, for Aurora, the first true stop, the first time she stopped enough to feel what she had run her life not feeling. And it was in that weeping, more than in any word, that Aurora began to arrive, because to weep is a form of stopping, of being, of feeling, and Aurora, in weeping at last, stopped at last, and to stop was the beginning of everything.

The Keeper was, in that time, what the sister would be in another way, and Aurora needed both: the Keeper gave her the understanding, the sister would give her the living. The Keeper explained to her, with the wisdom of the entries, why she had never arrived — the root, the father's and now?, the line of birth, the worth prior to the rising; and the sister would show her, by example, how one lived arrived. Aurora needed both because to understand without living is not enough, one can understand everything and go on running, and to live without understanding is not either, one can stop without knowing why and go back to running at the first chance; but to understand and to live, the two together, that changes a person, and it was with the Keeper's head and the sister's example that Aurora went on tracing the path out of the running and into the arrival.

She left the House of the Register with a new word, to arrive, and for the first time that word did not mean to reach a summit, it meant to stop at one, and it was with that new word, still strange but already germinating, that Aurora began, slowly, to learn the hardest thing for one who ran a whole life: to stop running, and to discover that, stopped, she had already arrived.
