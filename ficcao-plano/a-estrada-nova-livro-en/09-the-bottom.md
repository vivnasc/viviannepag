## 9. The bottom

Aurora's bottom did not have the dramatic form that bottoms usually have. It was not a visible fall, a disaster the village could point to; it was a silent, inner bottom, the bottom of a woman who on the outside went on successful and who on the inside was coming apart. And perhaps for being invisible it was harder, because a visible bottom attracts help, people who come; a bottom hidden beneath a life of success attracts nothing, and one crosses it alone. It was Conceição, the sister, the only one who saw it, because she was a sister and saw beyond the façade.

She saw it one morning, at the door of Aurora's house, when she went there to take some eggs. She found the door ajar and her sister sitting at the kitchen table, still in her night clothes, halfway through the morning, with a cup of coffee in front of her, cold, with the skin of the milk making a ring around the rim. Aurora, who rose her whole life before the light, was there at eleven o'clock, stopped, looking at the wall. Conceição set the eggs on the dresser, without fuss, and instead of asking what was wrong, sat down in front of her sister, and stayed.

You have not eaten, she said, after a while, looking at the coffee with skin. And you have not got up for anything. How long have you been sitting there, Aurora?

And Aurora, who never unburdened herself, who her whole life had held unburdening to be weakness, said, without turning, with a flat voice that frightened her sister more than a shout: since I woke. I do not know what to do, Conceição. For the first time in my life I do not know what to do. I have nothing to climb. I stopped, as the Keeper said, as you say, I stopped running. And to stop is not the rest you have. To stop is this. It is to be sitting looking at the wall with panic, with a panic you have no idea of, the panic of having no step ahead, of not knowing who I am if I am not climbing.

Conceição gave her no counsel. She got up, threw out the coffee with skin, washed the cup, put fresh water on the fire. And she said, with her back turned, to the sink: then sit there and I will make you another. And today you do nothing, Aurora. Today is only to be. I will stay with you doing nothing, for of that I know.

That was how the bottom began, and that was how the sister sat down in it beside her.

Because Aurora had stopped, and to stop did not bring her the arrival at once; it brought her, first, the panic. A person who ran a whole life does not know how to be stopped. The stopped hours were unbearable to her, because her head, trained for the next step, went on looking for it, and on not finding it entered distress. The first stopped days were like the first days of one who leaves a vice, with all that has of trembling. The running had been, for Aurora, a vice in the deep sense of the word: a thing she depended on, that gave her a discharge, a meaning, and without which she went into withdrawal. The body asked for the running, the head asked for a goal, and the lack hurt.

Conceição, who saw this, did a simple and stubborn thing: she began to take her sister to the vegetable garden. Conceição had, behind the house, a patch of earth with broad beans, cabbages, a bean plant that climbed up some canes, and a few rose bushes that gave nothing of use and that she had there only because. She took her sister there one afternoon, put a hoe in her hand, and set her to hoe between the cabbages.

Aurora hoed ten minutes and stopped, with the hoe in the air, distressed. Conceição, this leads nowhere, she said. I hoe here the whole afternoon and at the end of the afternoon it is the same, it is earth, it is cabbage, I climbed nothing, I achieved nothing. What is this for?

And Conceição, bent over the bean plant, without turning, said the thing that stayed: it is for nothing, sister. That is the grace of it. You hoe so the cabbage grows, and the cabbage grows in its own time, not in yours, and you cannot hurry it, you cannot win it, you cannot be the best at making the cabbage grow. You can only accompany it. Look at this bean. I put the seed, I watered, and now it is in its hands, not in mine. I come here every day to look, not to hurry it, for it is not hurried, but to be watching it grow, which is a thing you have lacked your whole life: to watch grow without hurrying. You never watched anything grow, Aurora. You planted everything and pulled everything up before its time to see if it had grown already.

Aurora was left with the hoe in her hand, looking at the bean plant, and said nothing, but she came back the next day, and the next, and went on watching the bean grow, slowly, without hurrying it, and it was the first thing in sixty years that she accompanied without trying to win.

There was a danger, in that time, that Conceição caught and that Aurora did not see. It was that Aurora, used to transforming everything into a goal, began, without noticing it, to treat the learning to stop as one more project to conquer. She was going to stop better than anyone. She was going to be the first to arrive. She got up in the morning and made a list of what she had to do to arrive, as she had made lists her whole life. And Conceição, seeing her one afternoon writing down on a paper the exercises of arriving she was going to carry out, laughed at her with tenderness and took the paper from her hand.

Look at this, she said, still laughing. You made a list to arrive. A list, Aurora. You are trying to arrive as one climbs a hill, with effort, running, to the letter, and it is not so. To arrive is not conquered, sister. To arrive is the contrary of conquering. It is to stop conquering. You cannot win the arrival as you won the other things, because the arrival is not won, it is let happen, and you do not know how to let happen, you only know how to win. Tear me up that list. The first thing you have to do to arrive is to stop making lists to arrive.

And Aurora tore up the list, slowly, and was left with the pieces in her hand not knowing where to put them, because to tear up a list was, for her, almost a blasphemy, and to understand that her own way of doing things — running, competing, conquering — was what prevented her from arriving, was one of the hardest truths of that time.

The panic of being stopped had a root, and Aurora went on discovering it: the fear that, stopped, she would be confronted with the question she had avoided her whole life, and what if all this was not worth it? And what if I ran my whole life for nothing? While she ran, the question did not arise, because the running presupposed that it was worth it. Stopped, the question came, terrible, and Aurora was in panic of it, because she suspected the answer.

The question came whole on a winter night, and it was the worst night of the bottom. Aurora, who had not slept for days, got up in the small hours, and in a distress she did not know where to take went to the cupboard where she kept the tin box, the box of prizes, that she had carried from house to house her whole life and that she had never opened to enjoy, only to see what was lacking. She opened it, by the weak light of the kitchen. There they were, in order, the marks notebook from the village school with the teacher Anacleto's sentence, the medal of blue ribbon, papers, ribbons, a whole life of victories arranged in a tin box that fit on her lap. And Aurora put the box on her lap and looked at it, and instead of the pride she should feel there came to her a thing that brought her down: that that was everything she had. Sixty years. A tin box. And no person beside her, at that hour, not a child sleeping in the house, not a man in the bed, not anyone to say to I am distressed; only the box, cold, on her lap, full of proofs of a running that had taken her nowhere.

And Aurora wept, in that kitchen, by the weak light, with the tin box on her lap, and it was the weeping of a whole life dammed up, because she had never wept, had never stopped to weep, the weeping had always been a stop and she did not stop. The whole weeping of sixty years came at once, the life of running, the people lost, the present never lived, the father who had never let her arrive, the mother who had told her rest and whom she had not heard. She wept for the girl of the marks notebook who had run home full and gone out of the yard empty. She wept for the medal of blue ribbon she had given the father and the father had given her back with an and now? instead of an enough. And it was terrible and it was necessary, because there are weepings that are cleansings, and that one cleansed Aurora of a whole life of unwept running.

And in the middle of that night there was a knock at the door, low, and it was Conceição. Aurora opened it, with her face undone, with the tin box still in her hand, and she was going to hide the box, was going to wipe her face, was going to say she was well, out of habit, out of shame; but Conceição, at the door, with her shawl over her shoulders, saw it all in a glance, the face, the box, the hour, and did not let her pretend. She came in, shut the door to the cold, and said only: the light on at three in the morning is seen from my window, sister. For three nights I have seen it. Today I came.

And she said no more. She did not say you have everything to be happy, she did not say what weeping is that, she did not say any of the phrases the others said. She sat beside her sister, took the tin box from her hands with care, set it on the table, and held her hand, and stayed. She let her weep the life she had not lived, without ordering her to stop. And it was that simple presence, that of a sister who stays while the other weeps, by the weak light, with the tin box set on the table between the two, that began to show Aurora what the arrival was that she had sought in vain on the summits: it was this. It was someone who stays. It was to be with one who holds our hand. It was the presence she had traded her whole life for a tin box full of ribbons, and that was worth, she understood now, more than everything that was in the box.

When there was already light behind the serra, Conceição was still there, and she made coffee, fresh coffee, and cut bread, and the two ate at the table, with the tin box open between them, and Conceição took the medal of blue ribbon, turned it over in her hand the way Firmino had turned it, and said, without grief, only with an old pity: you know what, Aurora? This medal was never yours. It was always the father's. You won it and gave it to him, and he gave it back to you with a task on top. I want to see you keep one of these things for yourself, one day. Not to show, not to add to the box. To enjoy.

And in the days that followed Conceição did a thing that moved Aurora and that was, in her way, the opposite of the running: she took her sister's deeds, one by one, from the tin box, and celebrated them with decades of delay. One afternoon, she set the table with the good cloth, opened a bottle of her husband's wine, and said: today is the party of the big town's medal, that you never had. And they raised their glasses, the two of them, to the girl on the cart with the blue ribbon in her hand, forty years later. Another afternoon it was the party of the teacher Anacleto's distinction. And Aurora wept at those late parties, but it was another weeping, a sweet weeping, because it was like going to fetch, one by one, the joys she had left along the way and giving them, at last, the place they deserved, the place the father's and now? had denied them at the time.

There was, on the way from the bottom to the arrival, a small exercise that Conceição taught her and that changed Aurora more than any reasoning. At the end of each day, the sister told her, instead of thinking of what was lacking, of what you did not manage, of the next step, you stop a moment and think of what was good today. Only that. What you had today. What arrived. And Aurora, who had ended her days her whole life thinking of what was lacking, began to end them thinking of what she had, and at first it cost her, the head always fled to what was lacking; but she insisted, every night, what was good today, what I have, and slowly the head went on learning to see the full instead of the empty, and Aurora went on discovering that her days, seen by what they had of good, were full days, enough, and that the difference between an empty life and a full life was not in what the life had, it was in where one looked.

And she went on discovering, with surprise, that that stopped Aurora, who had so frightened her, whom she had thought empty, was not the emptiness she feared. She was, on the contrary, the fuller of the two, because she was the one who could, at last, feel what she had, instead of running to what was lacking; she was the one who watched the bean grow, and the light behind the serra, and the sister's hand in hers at the table in the morning. And to feel what one has, Aurora understood at that bottom, with the tin box open and the coffee steaming and the sister beside her, is the beginning of all arrival.
