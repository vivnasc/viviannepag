## 9. The bottom

It is just to say that Aurora's bottom did not have the dramatic form that bottoms usually have, it was not a visible fall, a disaster, a loss the village could point to; it was a silent, inner, invisible bottom, the bottom of a woman who on the outside went on successful and who on the inside was coming apart. And perhaps for being invisible it was harder, because a visible bottom attracts help, people who come, compassion; an invisible bottom, beneath a life of success, attracts nothing, because no one sees it, no one imagines that the woman of success is at the bottom, and she crosses it alone, without help, because to ask for help would be to admit the bottom and no one would believe it. Aurora crossed her invisible bottom alone, smiling for the village that admired her and coming apart within, and it was Conceição, again, the only one who saw, because she was a sister and saw beyond the façade; and it was because Conceição saw what no one else saw that she could help her, sitting beside her sister at the invisible bottom where no one else knew she was, and keeping her company in the dark that the village, dazzled by the brightness of Aurora's successes, did not even suspect existed.

But between understanding and managing there is a path, and before Aurora learned to arrive there was her bottom, there was the time when, having stopped running, she discovered that she did not know how to be stopped, and when the stopping, far from being the rest it promised, was, at first, a torture.

It should be said that no one expected this bottom of Aurora's, and that she herself hid it as much as she could, because a woman of successes cannot collapse, she has no leave for it, everyone counts on her strength. The village saw the successful Aurora, and did not imagine her emptiness; and Aurora, used to being the strong one, the first, the one who wins, did not know how to show herself low, was ashamed to be so, because in her head to be low was to fail, and she did not fail. And so Aurora's bottom was a hidden bottom, lived within with the face of success without, and that made it more solitary, because a person who collapses and hides it collapses alone, without the relief of being seen, of being sheltered. It was Conceição, the only one, who saw beyond the mask, because she was a sister and knew her, and it was for that that Conceição could help where no one else even saw that help was needed: because she was the only one who saw, beneath the victorious Aurora, the collapsed Aurora, and who, instead of letting her pretend she was well, sat beside her and let her not be.

Because Aurora stopped, after the empty summit and the House of the Register, she stopped running, but to stop did not bring her the arrival at once; it brought her, first, the panic. A person who ran a whole life does not know how to be stopped, and Aurora, stopped, felt herself lost, useless, distressed, like a body used to movement that suddenly does not move and does not know what to do with the stillness. The stopped hours were unbearable to her, because her head, trained for the next step, went on looking for it, and on not finding it entered panic, and Aurora found herself wanting to run again, to invent goals, to look for a stairway to climb, only to flee the anguish of being stopped. To stop, which she had imagined would be the rest, was after all a combat, the combat against a whole life of movement, against a body and a head that did not know how to be still, against the panic of the emptiness that the stillness brought.

The first stopped days were, for Aurora, like the first days of one who leaves a vice, with all that has of trembling and of distress. Because the running had been, for Aurora, a vice, in the deep sense of the word: a thing she depended on, that gave her a discharge, an adrenaline, a meaning, and without which she went into withdrawal. And to stop was to go into withdrawal: the body asked for the running, the head asked for a goal, the adrenaline was lacking and the lack hurt, and Aurora had, in those first days, all the symptoms of one who lets go of a vice, the irritation, the distress, the temptation to relapse, to go back to running only to calm the lack. And she understood, with that, how much the running had been a vice and not a choice, how much she had depended on it, how much she had got hooked on the adrenaline of winning, and she understood that to let go of the running was going to be like letting go of any vice, hard, with withdrawal, with relapses, and that she was going to need to bear the lack without relapsing, to cross the withdrawal, until the body and the head got unhooked from the running and learned to live without it. It was hard, that weaning from the running, as hard as any weaning; but Aurora crossed it, with the sister's help, and on the other side of the withdrawal, when the lack of the running at last slackened, she found a thing she did not know: the calm, the quiet of a body that no longer needs to run, the peace of one who got unhooked from the adrenaline and learned to live without it.

And there was, in that panic, a hard discovery: that Aurora did not know who she was when stopped. Her whole life she had been the runner, the first, the one who rose; the running taken away, who was she? She did not know. Stopped, without goals, without the rising, without the running that had defined her, Aurora did not recognise herself, did not know what to do with herself, did not even know what she liked, what she wanted, who she was beneath the runner. She discovered, with horror, that she had made herself so wholly the running that, stopped, almost no one was left, that she had not cultivated an Aurora beyond the one who ran, and that to stop was, therefore, to come face to face with an emptiness, no longer the emptiness of the summit, but the emptiness of herself, of a woman who had worn herself out running and who did not know who she was when she did not run.

There was, at that bottom, a danger Aurora had not foreseen, and it was that of wanting to make of the stopping itself one more running. Because Aurora, used to transforming everything into a goal, began, without noticing it, to treat the learning to stop as one more project to conquer, one more stair to climb: she was going to stop, she was going to arrive, she was going to be the best at arriving, she was going to conquer the arrival as she had conquered everything. And it was Conceição who caught her at this, who laughed at her with tenderness and told her, sister, you are trying to arrive as one climbs a hill, with effort, running, and it is not so, to arrive is not conquered, to arrive is the contrary of conquering, it is to stop conquering. You cannot win the arrival as you won the other things, because the arrival is not won, it is let happen, and you do not know how to let happen, you only know how to win. And Aurora understood, with that, that her own way of doing things — running, competing, conquering — was what prevented her from arriving, and that she was going to have to unlearn not only the goals, but the very manner of pursuing goals, because to apply to the arrival the logic of conquest was to guarantee never arriving, since the arrival was, precisely, the abandonment of the logic of conquest.

The panic of being stopped had, for Aurora, a root she went on discovering: the fear that, stopped, she would be confronted with the question she had avoided her whole life — and what if all this was not worth it? And what if I ran my whole life for nothing? While she ran, the question did not arise, because the running presupposed that it was worth it, that the arrival was coming; stopped, without the running to presuppose the meaning, the question came, terrible, and Aurora was in panic of it, because she suspected the answer. And it was to face that question, stopped, at the bottom, that freed her, because in facing it she discovered that the answer was not the one she feared: the running had not been worth it, it was true, but the life had not been for nothing, because the life was not the running, the life was everything she had crossed running without seeing, and it was still there, to be lived, waiting. The running had been for nothing, yes; but the life not, because the life had not ended, and Aurora was still in time to live it, to inhabit it, to arrive. And it was to distinguish the running from the life — the running that had been in vain, the life that still remained — that took from the question its terror, because the question was only terrible while the running and the life were the same thing; separated, the question had an answer: the running was not worth it, but the life is, and it is still here, and there is still time.

It was Conceição, the sister, who helped her at the bottom, and she helped her in a simple way, in her own way. Seeing Aurora distressed, stopped and not knowing how to be, Conceição did not give her great counsel; she took her, simply, to do small things, things of being and not of rising. She took her to sit in the sun of the Square doing nothing; she took her to tend a vegetable garden, to watch grow slowly a thing that could not be hurried; she took her to be with her nephews, to listen, to talk, to share an afternoon without an objective. And at first Aurora could not bear it, found it a waste of time, felt the distress of not being rising anything; but Conceição was patient, and went on showing her, without words, only by example, that one could be without rising, that there was a life in the small and stopped things that she had never lived, always running over them. And Aurora went on learning, slowly, with her sister, to be stopped without panic, to inhabit an hour without filling it with goals, to do a thing for the pleasure and not for the conquest, and each one of those small learnings was a little of solid ground forming beneath her feet, a little of arrival.

There were days, at that bottom, when Aurora was afraid she had destroyed her life, when the stopping seemed to her not a cure but a defeat, when she thought she had let go of the running too late, that she had wasted her life running and that now, stopped, all that was left her was to contemplate the waste. They were the darkest days, in which the emptiness of the summit joined the emptiness of herself, in which Aurora saw herself as a woman who had run her whole life for nothing and who now, stopped, did not even have a life to live, because she had not cultivated any beyond the running. And there was a bottom within that bottom, a night when Aurora wept all she had never wept, the whole life of running, the people lost, the present never lived, the father who had never let her arrive, and it was a weeping of a whole life dammed up, because Aurora had never wept, had never stopped to weep, and all the weeping of sixty years came at once, that night, and it was terrible and it was necessary, because there are weepings that are cleansings, and that one cleansed Aurora of a whole life of unwept running, and left her, in the morning, empty but washed, ready, at last, to begin to learn the new thing.

And it was Conceição who held her in that weeping, who came, who stayed, who let her weep without ordering her to stop, without telling her that she had everything to be happy, without any of the phrases the others said; Conceição, who knew her sister, simply let her weep the life she had not lived, and held her hand, and was there, and it was that simple presence, that of a sister who stays while the other weeps, that began to show Aurora what the arrival was that she had sought in vain on the summits: it was this, it was someone who stays, it was to be with one who holds our hand, it was the presence she had traded her whole life for victories and that was worth, she understood now, more than all of them.

It was Aurora's bottom, that time of learning to be stopped, because it was hard, because it forced her to face the emptiness of herself and the panic of the stillness, because there were days when she nearly went back to running only to flee the difficulty of stopping. But it was also the beginning of the climb — or rather, the beginning of the arrival, because the metaphor of the climb no longer served —, because it was in stopping, and in bearing the panic of stopping, and in learning, with her sister, to be, that Aurora began to discover, beneath the runner, an Aurora who could exist without running, a woman capable of being, of enjoying, of arriving. There was, on the way from the bottom to the climb, an important step, and it was Aurora beginning to do a simple thing every day, at her sister's suggestion: at the end of the day, instead of thinking of what was left to do, of the next step, of what she had not managed, to stop a moment and think of what had been good in that day, of what she had, of what she had arrived at. It was a small exercise, almost ridiculous for a woman of her deeds, but it was revolutionary, because her whole life Aurora had ended her days thinking of what was lacking, and to end them thinking of what she had was to invert the gaze, was to train the head to see the full instead of the empty, what one has instead of what is lacking. And at first it cost her, the head always fled to what was lacking, to the next step; but Aurora insisted, every night, on what was good today, on what I have, on what I arrived at, and slowly the head went on learning to see the full, and Aurora went on discovering that her days, seen by what they had of good instead of what they lacked, were full days, good, enough, and that the difference between an empty life and a full life was not in what the life had, it was in where one looked, whether at what is lacking, and then everything is empty, or at what one has, and then everything is full.

And she went on discovering, with surprise, that that stopped Aurora, who had so frightened her, was not the emptiness she feared; she was, on the contrary, the fuller of the two, because she was the one who could, at last, feel what she had, instead of running to what was lacking, and to feel what one has is the beginning of all arrival.
