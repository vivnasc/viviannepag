## 2. And now?

To understand Aurora one must go back to a kitchen, to the kitchen of Firmino's house, and to two words that were said there whenever the girl brought a victory, and that governed her life: and now?

Aurora was nine years old on the day she always remembered first, when her memory went back to that house. It was a June afternoon, and she was coming from the village school running up the cobbled street with her marks notebook pressed against her chest, pressed so hard that she reached home with the mark of the cover on her skin. On the last page, in the fine hand of the teacher Anacleto, the teacher of Véspera in that time, was written what she knew by heart without yet having read it: best pupil of the school, distinction, and below it a sentence the teacher rarely wrote, in living memory no child like this has passed through here. Aurora climbed the street repeating the sentence like a prayer, in living memory, in living memory, and imagining the father's face, and not only the face, the thing she was going to fetch from the father's face, which was a rest, an you have arrived, a now you can stop.

Firmino was in the yard, bent over the iron of a hoe he was mending, and he straightened up on hearing her. He was a dry man, of large hands, of a deep brow that seemed always to be doing a sum. The girl held out to him the notebook open at the page, and stood watching him read, with her whole heart in her throat. Firmino read slowly, moved his lips over the teacher Anacleto's sentence, and Aurora saw born in his eyes a thing that was pride, it was surely pride, and she almost stopped there, almost arrived there, in that second, at the edge of the yard, with the smell of the iron and of the earth.

Very good, said Firmino. And he set the notebook on the rim of the well. Very good, Aurora. Best of the village school.

And the girl breathed, and opened her mouth to receive the rest, the rest now, the I am proud, the that is enough. But the father had already gone back to fetch the hoe, and it was from there, half turned away, with no malice at all, with the most natural voice in the world, that the rest came, which was not what she awaited:

And now? The village school is small, daughter. How many are they, twenty children? There must be big schools in the big town, with fifty, with a hundred, and there must be girls there you cannot even imagine. To be the best of twenty is easy. I want to see you the best of a hundred. I want to see where you really get to.

Aurora took the notebook from the rim of the well. The mark of the cover still burned in her chest, but it was another thing that burned her now, within, and that she would not know how to name for fifty years: the sensation of having come home full and of going out empty, of having brought a victory and of carrying off a new debt, the debt of the next step. She did not cry. Firmino's children did not cry over a thing like that. She went to her room, opened the notebook at the page of the distinction, and instead of savouring it set herself to thinking of the big town, of the hundred girls, of what she would have to do to be better than they. She was nine years old and was already on the next step before her feet had settled on what she had conquered.

It must be said, so that it be understood, that Firmino's and now? was not said with malice, and that perhaps that was its strength and its poison. If the father had humiliated her, if he had told her you are no good, if he had torn up her notebook, Aurora would perhaps have rebelled, fled, shut the door to that voice. But the father did not humiliate her. The father admired her. The and now? came wrapped in pride, came said with shining eyes, and so the girl did not know how to refuse it, she embraced it, made it hers, because it came wrapped in love and it was impossible to distinguish the love from the poison. How to refuse a father's love? Aurora did not refuse it. She took the and now? as proof of love, and ran to satisfy it as one runs to deserve love, and it was so that the poison entered, wrapped in love, indistinguishable from it.

Firmino was a man of humble origin and of wounded ambition, a man who had dreamed of rising and had not risen, who had stayed where he was born, and who put upon his brilliant daughter all the dream he had not realised in himself. Aurora did not know this as a child; she knew it from her own mother's mouth, years later, on an afternoon when the mother told her, low, like one who confesses, a thing that explained everything.

The mother was called Aurélia, and she was a mild woman, of a mildness that Firmino's house always smothered. It had been she who put the name to her daughter, the name of the dawn, of the breaking of the day, dreaming of a girl who would rise and go far; and she would see, late, what life had made of her wish, a daughter who always rose and never stopped at the noon of having arrived. It was Aurélia who told Aurora, in a kitchen already of winter, what Firmino had been before her. That your father, daughter, when he was young, wanted to go to the city to learn figures, to be a bookkeeper, to leave here. That he gathered money, that he spoke with people, that he had it a hand's breadth away. And that his father, your grandfather, in the year he was to go, fell ill, and your father stayed, to take care of the land and of the old man, and never went again. And he was left with that crosswise his whole life, Aurora. He did not go to the city. He was not a bookkeeper. He stayed here, at the hoe, doing sums in his head that no one asked of him. And when you were born and gave the signs you gave, he saw in you the going to the city he never made. He put upon you what he could not be. I saw him put it, and I did not have the strength to prevent it.

Aurora kept that, and she would come to understand it better with time. But in the time of the girl there was no explanation at all, there was only the father, and the hoe, and the rim of the well, and the and now? that came with each victory, without fail, fast, without giving her even an instant of enjoyment.

It was the haste, more than the demand, that marked her. Firmino acknowledged the deed, said very good; but the very good lasted a second, and at once came the and now?, and there was not, between the two, the space of a breath in which the girl could stay in what she had done. Aurora learned to do the same, to dispatch her deeds and to leap at once to the next, because it was so the father received them and it was so she learned to receive them. There was a time when the thing stayed in her body so that she would tell it, old, to her sister. She had won a prize in a contest in the big town, the first girl of Véspera to go and to win, and she came back by cart with a medal of blue ribbon, and the whole village came to the window to see her pass, and she carried the medal in her closed hand to give it to the father, not to herself, to the father. Firmino took the medal, turned it over, weighed it, and the girl saw again the shine in his eyes, and again she waited. And Firmino said, with the medal in the palm of his hand: very good. And at once, without setting the medal down, without letting go of the ribbon: and now the district one, Aurora. This is the big town's. The district one is lacking. A girl like this cannot stop at the big town.

And he gave her back the medal, and Aurora was left with it in her hand, the blue ribbon, the only thing she had touched that day that had a weight and that was hers, and instead of hanging it she kept it in a tin box where the marks notebook already lay and where everything would go on ending up, prize by prize, without any of it filling the box within. She carried the tin box her whole life, from house to house, heavier from year to year, and never opened it to enjoy what was there; she opened it, when she opened it, to see what was still left to gather.

She remembered another scene, and another, always the same. She bringing a summit and the father pointing her at once to the next; she coming to fetch a rest and receiving a task. Never, not once, did she leave a conversation with the father with the sensation of being enough, of having done enough, of being able to rest. The father always sent her back to the running, and the girl went back, awaiting the day when at last she would get far enough for the father to say enough. That day did not come.

There was, it is true, one time that nearly was that day, and it was perhaps for having nearly been that it bound Aurora to the running more than all the others. Aurora was already a woman, and had reached a great thing, the greatest until then, a thing that was spoken of beyond Véspera, and she went to the house, the old house, to tell it to the father, who was already aged, drier still, with his hands trembling lightly on the cane. She told him. And Firmino was quiet a moment, a rare thing in him, and his eyes filled, and he said, with a voice Aurora had never heard from him, a voice almost undone: you arrived, daughter. You arrived where I never arrived. You went to the city for me.

And Aurora felt, for a second, what she had pursued her whole life: the arrival, the rest, the father saying you arrived. It was a second of light such as she had not had another. But Firmino, as though he repented of having stopped, as though the very word arrived burned the mouth of a man who had never said it, straightened up on the cane and added, lower, almost asking pardon for having let her rest: and now do not stop, for it is now that you must go higher, now that you have a name. Now is when no one can hold you.

And the second of light shut as fast as it had opened. But it had existed. And it is of the cruellest things there are, to taste for a second what one wants and at once to lose it, because one who tastes it is left knowing that it exists, that it is possible, that it is one step away; and Aurora spent the rest of her life running after that second, after that you arrived, daughter the father had said once and never again, and Firmino died without repeating it to her, leaving her running forever after a lost second.

The mother died first, Aurélia, and she died telling her daughter, from the bed, the last mild thing of a life of mild things smothered: rest, daughter, when I am no longer here. Do not run so much. No one asks it of you. And Aurora, standing at the edge of the bed, with the haste already pulling her toward the next goal even there, even at that hour, heard the mother as she had always heard her, from afar, as though the mild voice reached her from another room, smothered by the father's voice, which was the one that ruled the house and that went on ruling in Aurora's head long after the house no longer existed. Only late, very late, would Aurora come back to that sentence of the mother's, rest, no one asks it of you, and understand that there had been, in the house, a voice pulling her toward lightness, a voice she had not heard because the father's was stronger, and give the mother, with decades of delay, the rightness the father had never let her have.

Firmino went years after his wife, and he went running, in his way. Aurora was at the head of the bed. The father, already losing himself, already speaking with people who were not in the room, gripped her hand with the little strength he had, and she waited, for a remnant of child that was still in her, waited for him to say, at least at that hour, at least at the end, the word. And Firmino opened his eyes, fixed them on her, knew her, and said, with the last ordered breath he had, the only thing that came out whole, which was the thing he was: and now, Aurora? And now what? And he closed his eyes. And he died so, with his and now? in his mouth, without ever having said enough to himself nor to his daughter, and left Aurora at the head of a dead man's bed understanding, with a cold that lasted her years, that the question did not die with the man, that it stayed, that it passed inside the one who had heard it a thousand times.

Because the cruellest of all was this: that, the father dead, when there was no longer anyone in the yard asking and now?, Aurora went on asking it of herself, at each victory, and now?, and then what? Firmino's voice had become hers. The finish line that drew back was now she herself drawing it away, at each step, without needing the father to do it. She carried the father within, the father and the father's and now?, from house to house, as she carried the tin box, and the box filled with prizes and the head filled with and nows, and neither of the two things, however much they filled, gave her the one she wanted, which was to be able to set down the box, to silence the voice, and to say, once in her life, by a well or by a bed or on some ordinary morning, the word that in that house was never said: I have arrived. Enough. Rest.

And she would say it, at last, much later. But to say it she first had to understand a thing that Firmino's house had hidden from her her whole life: that the goal she pursued had never been hers, it had been the father's; that she had spent her life running to the city the sick grandfather had prevented the father from going to; and that one can run a whole life paying off another's hunger without ever paying it off, because one does not kill a person's hunger by eating through another's mouth.
