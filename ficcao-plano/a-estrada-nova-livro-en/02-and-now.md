## 2. And now?

There are questions that build and questions that destroy, and the and now? of the father was one of those that destroy, though it seemed one of those that build. It seemed a question of encouragement, of incentive, of one who pushes toward more and better; and it was, at bottom, a question of dissatisfaction, of one who is never content, of one who denies worth to what one has in the name of what is lacking. A building question would be and how was it?, or did you like it?, or are you content?, questions that invite one to savour what one has done; the and now? does not invite one to savour, it invites one to leap, it denies the present in the name of the future, and so it destroys, because it destroys the capacity to be in the present, to enjoy what one has, to arrive. And the girl Aurora, always hearing the and now? and never the did you like it?, learned to ask herself the question that destroys and never the one that builds, learned to ask herself always and now? and never did you like it?, and so she never knew how to savour nor to arrive, because the question she asked herself, inherited from her father, was a question that denied the present, and one who always asks themselves what is lacking never sees what they have. Only much later did Aurora learn to change the question, to ask herself, instead of and now?, did I like it?, was it good?, is it enough?, and discovered that to change the question changed everything, because the question we ask ourselves shapes what we see, and one who asks themselves what they have sees the full, and one who asks themselves what is lacking sees the empty, and she had spent her life asking herself the question of the empty and was going, at last, to learn to ask herself the one of the full.

To understand Aurora one must go back to a word, or rather, to two, that the father said to her whenever she brought a victory, and that governed her life: and now? It was those two words, repeated a thousand times across her childhood, that taught Aurora never to arrive.

It must be said that the and now? was not said with malice, and that perhaps that was its strength and its poison. If the father had been cruel, if he had humiliated, if he had despised, Aurora would perhaps have resisted, rebelled, fled; but the father was not cruel, the father was ambitious for her, and the and now? was said with love, with pride, with the wish to see her go farther, and so Aurora did not resist it, she embraced it, made it hers, because it came wrapped in love and it was impossible to distinguish the love from the poison. The father loved her, and wanted her far, and always pushed her more, and all of that was love, and the and now? was the form of the father's love, and how does one refuse a father's love? Aurora did not refuse it; she took the and now? as proof of love, and ran to satisfy it as one runs to deserve love, and it was so that the poison entered, wrapped in love, indistinguishable from it, and it was for coming wrapped in love that it did so much harm, because what comes wrapped in love is not rejected, it is embraced, and Aurora embraced her father's and now? her whole life, thinking she embraced his love, without ever managing to separate one thing from the other, the love that nourished her and the demand that consumed her.

The father was called Firmino, and he was a man of humble origin and of wounded ambition, a man who had dreamed of rising and had not risen, who had fallen short of what he had wanted to be, and who put upon his daughter, upon the brilliant daughter who had been born to him, all the dream of rising he had not realised in himself. He was not a bad man, Firmino; he loved his daughter, in his way, and the pride he had in her was true. But his love and his pride had a form that marked Aurora forever: Firmino never contented himself with what his daughter reached, he always pointed her to the next step.

Aurora brought a top mark from the school, the best of the class, and ran to show it to her father, with a full heart, awaiting his pride. And Firmino looked at the mark, and said very good, and right after, before the daughter even savoured the very good, came the and now?, and then what?, you are the best of the class, but what of the whole school?, of the village?, there must be better ones elsewhere, you have to go farther. And Aurora, who had come to fetch a rest, an you have arrived, an enough, a pride that would let her stop and enjoy what she had done, received instead a new step, a new goal, the confirmation that what she had done did not suffice, that there was always more, that she had not yet arrived. And she went back to the work, the girl, running to the next step, in the hope that that one, at last, would be the one that would make the father say you have arrived, rest, I am proud and that is enough. But that step never came, because each step reached brought at once the and now?, and the finish line always drew back, one more step ahead, always out of reach.

She remembered concrete scenes, Aurora, that she had kept her whole life without understanding why they hurt. She remembered the day she was the best of the village school, the best of which there was memory, and ran home with the news burning in her breast, sure that this time, this time for certain, the father would say enough, I am proud, that is enough; and Firmino heard, and said very good, and right after, there must be bigger schools elsewhere, with better pupils, you have to see how you fare against those. She remembered another time, and another, always the same, she bringing a summit and the father pointing her at once to the next, she coming to fetch a rest and receiving a new task. And she remembered the sensation, always the same, of coming home full and leaving empty, of bringing a victory and carrying away a new debt, the debt of the next step, and of never, not once, having left a conversation with the father with the sensation of being enough, of having done enough, of being able to rest. The father always sent her back to the running, and the girl went back, and ran, awaiting the day when at last she would get far enough for the father to say enough, a day that never came.

And there was a detail in that and now? of the father's that made it more venomous still: that it always came fast, without giving the girl even an instant of enjoyment. It was not that the father did not acknowledge the deed; he acknowledged it, said very good, but the very good lasted a second and at once came the and now?, and it was the quickness, the lack of an interval between the acknowledgement and the new demand, that taught the girl not to savour, because there was no time to savour, the savouring was interrupted at once by the new goal. If the father had let the very good breathe, stayed in it a while, let the girl enjoy it before pointing to the next, perhaps Aurora would have learned to savour; but the father dispatched the very good and ran to the and now?, and the girl learned to do the same, to dispatch her deeds and run to the next, without an interval, without enjoyment, because it was so the father received them and it was so she learned to receive them. The father's haste, more than the demand, was what marked her: the inability to stay, even for an instant, in the deed reached, before leaping to the next, and Aurora inherited that haste, that inability to stay, and carried it her whole life, always dispatching and leaping, never staying and enjoying.

And the girl Aurora learned, with that and now? repeated without end, the lesson that governed her life: that the arrival does not exist, that there is no place where one can stop and say I have arrived, that is enough, that the worth is always in the next step and never in what one already has, and that therefore one must always run, always conquer, always rise, because to stop is to fail and what one has already reached is never enough. She internalised the father's and now? so deep that it became her own voice, and even after the father died, even when there was no longer anyone asking her and now?, Aurora went on asking herself, at each victory, and now?, and then what?, without ever letting herself arrive, because the father's voice had become hers, and the finish line that drew back was now she herself drawing it away, at each step, without needing the father to do it.

There was, in Firmino, no conscious cruelty, and it is important to say it, because the story is not about a wicked father, it is about a wounded father who passed the wound to his daughter without knowing it. Firmino thought, sincerely, that he was doing his daughter good, that in pushing her toward more he prepared her for a life better than his, that the and now? was love and not poison. And perhaps the saddest is this: that the harm did not come from a bad intention, it came from a good intention ill-informed, from a father who wanted his daughter's good and who, for not knowing how to do better, for never having arrived himself, gave her the running instead of the arrival, thinking he gave her happiness when he gave her his own unhappiness. And Aurora, in understanding this, could not hate the father, because there is no hating one who did us harm in wanting to do us good; she could, yes, pity him, and forgive him, and free herself of him, which is different from hating him, and perhaps harder, because the harm done out of love comes wrapped in love and is hard to separate from the love that deserves to stay.

The mother, in this story, was a more faded presence, and that too marked Aurora. The mother, the one who had given her the name of the dawn, was a milder woman, who perhaps wanted for her daughter a lighter life, happier, less of a running; but she was a faded woman beside the father's ambition, and her milder voice always lost itself in the strong voice of the and now? Aurora remembered, vaguely, gestures of the mother that went against the running — a rest, daughter, a leave it be, a you don't have to do everything —, but they were weak gestures, smothered, that did not get to stand up to the father, and Aurora had followed the father and not the mother, because the father's voice was the one that ruled the house. Only much later would Aurora think of the mother with a new pity, understanding that the mother had perhaps seen what was being done to the daughter and had not had the strength to prevent it, and that there had been, in the house, a milder voice trying to pull her toward lightness, a voice she had not heard because the father's was stronger. And she would think that part of what she was learning, now, to arrive, to slow, to be enough, was, perhaps, to hear at last the mother's voice, the mild voice the house had smothered, and to give it, late, the rightness the father had never let it have.

And the cruellest is that Aurora spent her life running to please a father who, even dead, would never say what she awaited, because the and now? had no end, because there was no victory, however great, that would make the father's voice say at last you have arrived, that is enough, rest. Aurora ran after a pride that always drew back, after an enough that would never come, after an arrival that the father, in life or in memory, would never give her, because the father was not a man of enoughs, he was a man of and nows, and he had taught his daughter a world without arrival, a road without end, a race without a goal. And Aurora ran that race her whole life, trying to reach a line that was the father's pride, and never reached it, because that line did not exist, it had always been a mirage, the and now? disguised as a goal, and she had run after a mirage her whole life without ever understanding that she ran after nothing, or worse, after a thing that was not even hers, it was the wounded ambition of a father she carried on her shoulders without knowing it.

There was a single time, that Aurora kept her whole life, when the father nearly gave her what she awaited, and it was perhaps that time, more than all the others, that bound her to the running. Aurora, already grown, had reached a great thing, the greatest until then, and the father, on learning of it, had his eyes shine, and said a thing that was not the usual and now?, he said a thing resembling pride, resembling you have arrived. And Aurora felt, for an instant, what she had pursued her whole life, the full approval of the father, the rest, the arrival. But the instant lasted little, because right after, as though he repented of having stopped, the father added the and now?, pointed to the next step, and the instant of arrival shut as fast as it had opened. And it was cruel, that near-arrival, because it gave Aurora to taste, for a second, what she wanted, and at once took it from her, and a person who tastes for a second what they want and at once loses it is left even more attached to pursuing it, because now they know it exists, that it is possible, that it is one step away — and Aurora spent the rest of her life trying to reconquer that second of full approval of the father, running after that instant she had tasted once, without ever reaching it again, because the father never gave it to her again, and died without giving it, leaving her running forever after a lost second.

Only much later, with the story that was going to happen to her, would Aurora understand that the goal she pursued had never been hers, it had been the father's, and that she had spent her life running to satisfy the hunger to rise of a man who had put upon her what he had not managed for himself, and that therefore her running was not even for her own fulfilment, it was for his, it was a weight that was not hers and that she carried as though it were, running a road the father had traced for her and that had no end, after an arrival that was the father's and not hers, and that for that reason, even reached, would never satisfy her, because one does not kill a person's hunger by eating through another's mouth.
