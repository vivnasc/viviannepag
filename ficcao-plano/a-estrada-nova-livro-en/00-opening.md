## From the register of Véspera

In the record of the village, in the hand of the Keepers who have kept the book, the following is found:

Aurora, daughter of Firmino and of Véspera folk. She was the first at everything: the best of the village school, the first of her house to rise, the first woman of Véspera to reach places no woman had reached, the one who put her hand to a thing and did it better than everyone. She rose her whole life, step by step, without stopping, and was the pride of the family and of the village.

The name was a promise, of the Véspera kind: her mother wanted her the name of the dawn, of the breaking of the day, of a luminous beginning, dreaming of a daughter who would rise and go far. And life gave a woman who always rose, who always went far, and who was always in the dawn of a new goal and never in the noon of having arrived, because each victory, in the instant she reached it, became the step of the next, and the arrival she pursued her whole life always drew back, one more step, one more new road, without end.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs one who runs a whole life after an arrival that always draws back, nor what one pursues, without seeing it, that after all is not ours. There are those who climb every stair and never arrive anywhere, because the arrival they seek is not at the top of any stair. For those, books such as this one are needed.

This is a novel of the Fountain Square, like the other on this shelf, and it is for anyone who chases goals and never feels they have arrived. But it is not a story against ambition nor against wanting more; it is a story about the difference between rising and arriving, about the hunger we inherit without choosing, about the place where one finds, after all, the arrival pursued in vain upward. Whoever has ever reached what they wanted and felt nothing, whoever comes to the end of each conquest already running to the next, whoever can never say enough, that one will recognise themselves in Aurora, and will learn with her what she learned late: that the arrival is not a place reached by climbing, it is a decision to stop and to be where one is.
