## 5. The chorus of the Square

Aurora was not the only one in Véspera running after an arrival that did not come, nor the only one to have stayed, and it was in looking at others, in the Fountain Square where the village crossed paths, that she began, without knowing it, to see herself and the paths she had ahead.

Aurora began, in that time, to look at the people of the village in a new way, and to see things she had not seen her whole life because she had passed running. She had always seen the village from above, from the height of her conquests, and had divided the people into two categories: those who rose, like her, whom she respected, and those who stayed, whom she despised in silence. Now, stopped, she began to see a third thing that did not fit in that division: that many of those she had classified as stayers were, after all, arrived people, people at peace, people who lived, and that many of those who rose, like her, were, beneath, exhausted and dissatisfied people, people who ran without arriving. And her division of the world, that of those who rise and those who stay, began to come undone, and to give place to another, truer: that of those who arrive and those who do not arrive, and that division did not coincide with the first, because there were stayers who arrived and risers who never arrived, and Aurora, the riser who had never arrived, had to revise her whole hierarchy of the world, and to put herself, with humility, not at the top where she had always placed herself, but lower, behind many stayers who knew how to arrive and whom she, the riser, now envied.

There was Aníbal of the Shops — not to be confused with other Aníbals —, a man who had run like her, who had always wanted more, more business, more name, more than he had, and who had grown old bitter, because he had come to the end of a life of running and discovered that he had never had enough, that however much he amassed it never seemed to him to suffice, and who now, old, with all he had amassed, was a peevish and dissatisfied man, complaining of what was still lacking instead of enjoying the much he had. And Aurora looked at Aníbal of the Shops and saw, with a shiver, her own future if she went on running: an old man rich in things and poor in peace, dying dissatisfied in the midst of abundance, because he had never learned to say enough, and one who never learns to say enough dies hungry at a full table.

Aníbal of the Shops was, for Aurora, the warning of what she might come to be, and it was a frightening warning because Aníbal had risen less than she and was equally trapped. If Aníbal, with his lesser hunger, with his lower summits, had ended so dissatisfied, so bitter, so unable to enjoy what he had, what would become of Aurora, with her greater hunger, with her higher summits, if she went on in the same path? She would end up worse, she understood, because her hunger was greater, and the greater the hunger the greater the dissatisfaction, and Aurora saw in the bitter Aníbal a mirror of her future enlarged, and was afraid, and the fear was also an engine of her change, because a person changes sometimes from seeing, in another's mirror, what they do not want to be. And Aurora did not want to be Aníbal of the Shops, an old man rich and bitter complaining of what was lacking in the midst of abundance; she would rather be Conceição, the sister content with little; and to see the two, Aníbal and Conceição, the two paths side by side, helped her to choose, to understand where each one led, and to want the sister's and not Aníbal's.

There was Conceição, her own sister, who had stayed in Véspera and contented herself, and who was, for Aurora, the most uncomfortable mirror of all. Conceição had not risen, had not won, had no name nor position; she had a simple life, a modest house, some children, a husband, her even days. And for years Aurora had looked at her sister with the touch of contempt she had inherited from the father, the Conceição who contented herself with little, who had been nothing. But now, from the height of her exhaustion, Aurora began to look at her sister with other eyes, and to see a thing that disturbed her: that Conceição, with her small life, was happy, or at least content, at peace, arrived; that the sister who had stayed had arrived somewhere — at her life, at her contentment — while she, who had risen at everything, had arrived nowhere. And that comparison hurt, because it called into question the whole hierarchy Aurora had learned, the one in which she, who had risen, was above the sister, who had stayed; and she began to suspect that perhaps it was the contrary, that the sister, in her small and arrived life, was above her, in her large and arrival-less life, because the sister had arrived and she had not.

Conceição became, in that time, the most important person for Aurora, and there was in that a justice and an irony. A justice, because Conceição had been, her whole life, the despised sister, the one who had stayed, the one who had not risen, and it was she, after all, who had what Aurora needed to learn; an irony, because Aurora, who had run the whole world in search of the arrival, came to find it, in the end, in the sister who had never left Véspera, in the small life she had always despised. And Aurora had to swallow her pride to learn from her sister, to acknowledge that Conceição, in her simplicity, knew a thing that she, with all her greatness, did not know, and that she had to sit at the feet of the sister she had always looked down on to learn to arrive. But Aurora, at the bottom where she was, no longer had pride to prevent her, and she sat, and learned, and discovered in her sister not a failure who had stayed, but a master of an art she was ignorant of, the art of being enough, of arriving, of living what one has, and that discovery — that the small sister was, after all, the great one — was one of the most humble and most healing of her life.

The Fountain Square had, at its centre, an old fountain, and Aurora, in that time of beginning to stop, sat sometimes beside it watching the water, and the water taught her a thing. The water of the fountain arrived, filled the tank, and overflowed, mild, without hurry, without running anywhere; it was there, arrived, full, and its overflowing was not a running to another place, it was only the excess of a fullness, the surplus water of a full tank. And Aurora, watching the fountain, thought that she wanted to be like that water: to arrive, to fill herself, and to overflow from the fullness, instead of running always, dry, after a water that never filled her. The fountain was full and gave of what was left over to it; Aurora was empty and ran after what was lacking to her; and the difference between the two was the difference between arriving and not arriving, between the fullness that overflows and the hunger that runs. And Aurora wanted, watching the fountain, to learn to fill herself, to arrive at the fullness, to give of what is left over instead of running after what is lacking, and she understood that the fountain, stopped in the middle of the Square, full and overflowing, was a master as good as the sister, and that the whole village, without knowing it, lived around a lesson of arrival that she, her whole life, had crossed running without seeing.

And there were, in the Square, various people, each with their way of dealing with life, and Aurora, now that she began to see, noticed them in another way. She noticed those who knew how to be, sitting in the sun of the Square without hurry, talking, enjoying an afternoon, and whom she had always thought people without ambition and who now seemed to her, suddenly, people who knew a thing she did not know: to be, to enjoy, to arrive. She noticed that the village, which she had always looked at from above, from the height of her victories, had perhaps, in its simplicity, a wisdom she lacked, that of one who knows how to content themselves, to stop, to live what they have. And she understood a thing about the contempt she had felt for the stayers, that shamed her: that the contempt was, at bottom, envy disguised. She had despised her whole life those who stayed, those who contented themselves, those who knew how to be; and now she understood that that contempt hid an envy, the envy of their peace, of their contentment, of their capacity to arrive, that she lacked. She had despised in them what she could not have, like one who despises the fruit they cannot reach; and the contempt had been a way of not feeling the envy, of transforming the lack into a superiority, of telling herself that she did not arrive because she was superior to those who arrived, when in truth she did not arrive because she did not know how, and envied those who knew. And to acknowledge this, that her contempt for the stayers had been envy of their peace, was humiliating and was freeing, because in acknowledging the envy beneath the contempt Aurora could, at last, instead of despising those who arrived, learn from them, sit beside them, ask them to teach her what she did not know and they did, the art of arriving, that she had so despised and so envied.

And she began to ask herself, with a new humility, whether she had not spent her life despising, in others, exactly the thing she most lacked: the capacity to arrive.

The Fountain Square was, in Véspera, the place where the village crossed paths, where one stopped to talk, where one was without hurry, and it was significant that it had been there, in the place of stopping, that Aurora began to see. Because Aurora, her whole life, had crossed the Square running, in passing to somewhere, without ever stopping in it, and she saw those who sat there, talking, in the sun, as stopped people, people with nothing to do, people who went nowhere. And now, that she began to stop, Aurora sat sometimes in the Square, and saw that from within, and understood that those who were there were not people who went nowhere, they were people who had already arrived, who were where they wanted to be, who did not need to go anywhere else because they were well right there. And there was a wisdom in that Square, in the people who knew how to sit in the sun without hurry, that Aurora, from the height of her runnings, had always despised and that now seemed to her, suddenly, a wisdom higher than hers, that of one who knows how to be, that of one who has arrived, that of one who does not confuse moving with living.

It was Conceição, the sister, who said to her the thing that stayed. The two of them were together, one rare afternoon when Aurora stopped enough to be with her sister, and Aurora, in an unburdening that escaped her, said that she was tired, that she did not know what it was all for, that she came to the end of each conquest and felt nothing. And Conceição, who had neither studies nor victories but had a sure feeling, told her, simply: oh Aurora, you never stop to arrive. You run so much rising that you never stay any time anywhere to feel that you have arrived. I did not rise as you did, but I arrived, do you understand? I am here, in this small life, and I feel that I have arrived, that I am where I want to be, and you, with all you have, I have never heard you say that you have arrived. Perhaps it is not rising more that you lack, sister. It is stopping to arrive where you already are. The conversation with the sister had a second part, that Aurora also kept. Because Aurora, wounded in her pride, tried to defend herself, said that each was each, that she had not been made for a small life, that she needed more. And Conceição, without taking offence, said a thing that disarmed her: oh Aurora, I am not telling you to have a small life like mine, nor to stop doing what you do. I am telling you to arrive at what you already have, be it much or little. You can have a large life and arrive at it; what you cannot do is have a large life and never arrive at it, which is what you have. The question is not the size of the life, sister, it is whether you arrive at it or run always to the next. I arrived at mine, which is small; you did not arrive at yours, which is large. Arrive at yours, Aurora. Do not make it smaller; arrive at it. And Aurora understood, with that, that the sister was not inviting her to give up, to shrink, to content herself with less; she was inviting her to arrive at what she had, however much it was, and that to arrive was not to have less, it was to inhabit what one has, and that she could go on being the Aurora of the great deeds and, at the same time, learn to arrive at them, to inhabit them, to enjoy them, instead of running always to the next.

And Aurora stayed with that, and it hurt her, because the sister she had always thought had fallen behind had just told her that it was she, Aurora, who had never arrived anywhere, and that the arrival, after all, was not a place up there, it was a way of being where one is, and that Conceição, in her small life, had it, and she, in her large life, did not.
