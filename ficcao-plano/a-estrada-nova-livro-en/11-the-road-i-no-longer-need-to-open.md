## 11. The road I no longer need to open

It cost Aurora, at first, the idea of staying, because staying had, in her head, all the connotations the father had taught her: to stay was to stagnate, was to settle, was to give up, was to become one of the stopped ones the father despised. And Aurora had to dismantle, one by one, those connotations, to understand that to stay was not to stagnate — one could stay and grow within, in depth instead of in height —, that to stay was not to settle — one could stay by choice and not out of laziness —, that to stay was not to give up — one could stay because one had arrived and not because one had given up. She had to rehabilitate the word stay, that the father had poisoned for her, to separate it from the contempt he had linked it to, to discover that to stay could be a good thing, courageous even, the courage to stop fleeing, to inhabit a place, to deepen instead of always advancing. And it was in rehabilitating the staying that Aurora allowed herself, at last, to do it, and discovered that to stay, far from being the stagnation she had feared, was a form of deeper movement: not the horizontal movement of one who always advances to new places, but the vertical movement of one who deepens the place where they are, of one who instead of going anywhere else goes deeper where they are, and that that movement, that of deepening, was richer than that of advancing, because it gave roots instead of only giving kilometres.

There was a day, in that time of learning to arrive, when Aurora understood what the name her life had always had meant, the new road, and she understood it on deciding, for the first time, not to open one.

Because Aurora had been, her whole life, an opener of roads: each time she arrived at a place, instead of staying in it, she at once opened the road to the next place, traced the next goal, set off. The new road was her life: to be always opening a new one, departing anew, setting off toward one more place, without ever staying in any. And there was a day, in that time, when Aurora arrived at a good place — a contentment, a peace, a life that was beginning to taste good to her — and when the old Aurora would have, out of habit, begun at once to open the road to the next, to ask and now?, to trace the next goal. And Aurora, that time, stopped, and did a thing she had never done: she decided not to open the road. She decided to stay. She decided that that place, that contentment, that peace, were enough, were an arrival, and that she did not need to open any more road to anywhere, because she was already where she wanted to be.

And it was a decision of enormous strangeness, for her, that of not opening a new road, that of staying, that of not having a next place to run to. She felt herself, at first, almost naked without a goal ahead, without a road to open, because her whole life she had always had one, and to be without any was an unknown territory. But she went on getting used to it, and went on discovering that not having a new road to open was not the emptiness she feared, it was a liberation, because it meant that she had arrived, that she did not need to go anywhere else, that she could, at last, stay and live where she was instead of running to where she was not. And she understood that the true new road, the only one she lacked to open, was not one more to a next summit; it was the road of learning to stay, that of stopping opening roads, that of arriving — and that that one, the road of learning not to need more roads, had been the only one she had never opened, and was the one that took her, at last, somewhere, because it took her to herself.

The title of her life, the new road, had always been said as a praise, in the village: there goes Aurora, always opening a new road, always farther beyond. And Aurora had prided herself on that, on being the one who opened roads, the one who always went farther, the pioneer. Only now, in the time of the arrival, did she understand the reverse of that praise: that to open new roads without end was never to inhabit any, that to be always the one who goes farther beyond is to be never the one who stays, that a life of opening roads is a life without a house, without a resting place, without an arrival. The roads she had opened had not taken her to any place where she stayed; they had always taken her farther, to one more to open, in an advancing without end that seemed progress and was, at bottom, an inability to stop. And Aurora understood that her fame as an opener of roads, that the village admired, was the other face of her inability to arrive, and that perhaps the most courageous and hardest thing she could do, she who had opened so many roads, was the only one she had never done: to stop opening roads and to stay, at last, in one.

And she stayed. She decided to stay where she was, in a contentment, in a peace, in a life that was beginning to taste good to her, and not to open the next road, and it was the hardest and most freeing decision of her life, because it went against everything she had been, against the opener of roads, against the one who always went farther beyond. To stay was, for Aurora, the most radical act of a life of radical acts, because it was the only one she had never dared: that of stopping advancing, that of saying here I stay, here it is good, here I arrive. And in staying she discovered that to stay was not to stagnate, as the father had taught her to fear; it was to arrive, to inhabit, to live at last in a road instead of crossing it running to the next, and that there was more life in a place inhabited than in a thousand roads opened and never inhabited.

She learned, in that time of staying, a thing about time that changed her relationship with it. Her whole life, Aurora had treated time as an enemy to be defeated, a race against the clock, always wanting to do more in less time, always in a hurry, always feeling that time was short and that she had to make the most of it by conquering. And in learning to stop, to stay, to arrive, Aurora discovered another relationship with time: time as a place to be in and not a race to win, time as a space of life and not as a resource to maximise. She discovered that one could inhabit time instead of spending it, be in it instead of running through it, and that an inhabited hour was worth more than a run day, because the inhabited hour was lived and the run day was lost. And that new relationship with time was an immense liberation, because it took from her the hurry, the hurry that had pursued her her whole life, the sensation that time was short and was always fleeing; and Aurora, on ceasing to run against time and on beginning to inhabit it, discovered that time, inhabited, does not flee, it stays, it widens, and that a life that is inhabited, even short, is longer, within, than a life that is run, even long, because what makes a life long is not the years, it is how much of them one inhabits.

She drew near again, in that time, to the people the running had made her let fall, and it was one of the things that most filled her. She sought out old friendships that had withered because she was always elsewhere; she drew near again to her sister, Conceição, who became her greatest company; and she opened herself, slowly, to the possibility of new relationships, now that she was present, now that she did not run, now that she could be with someone without already thinking of the next step. It cost her, this drawing-near, because many of the bridges the running had burned do not rebuild easily, and Aurora had to accept that some relationships had been lost for good, that some people no longer came back, that the price of the running had been paid and could not be all undone. There were those she sought and who were no longer there, those she wanted to recover and who had drawn too far away; and Aurora had to mourn those losses, to accept that the running had cost her irrecoverable things, people who did not come back, time that was not replaced. But there were also those who came back, who forgave, who accepted beginning again, starting with the sister; and Aurora learned to give worth to what she could still recover instead of only weeping what she had lost, to rebuild the bridges that could still be rebuilt instead of only lamenting the burned ones. And in that rebuilding, in the effort to be present again for those who were still there, Aurora discovered a joy the running had never given her, the simple joy of being with those one loves without hurry, without a goal, only for the company, and she understood that that joy, which she had despised her whole life as lost time, was after all the fullest thing there was, fuller than any summit, and that she had had it always within reach and never reaped it because she always went in passing.

There was, in that time of drawing near, a reunion that moved Aurora particularly, and it was with the man who had loved her and who had gone, years before, tired of loving a woman always departing. Not to begin again what had been, for that time had passed; but to meet, already old, and for Aurora to be able to tell him what she had never told him: that he had been right, that she had always been elsewhere, that his love had deserved a presence she had never given, and that she was sorry. And the man, already with his own life, heard her without rancour, and there was between the two, in that late reunion, a peace, the settling of an old account, and Aurora left it with a sweet sadness and with a lesson: that the running had cost her loves that did not come back, and that to acknowledge it, to say it, to lament it, was a form of arriving, at last, at those lost loves, not to recover them, but to put them at peace. And she understood that to arrive included also this, to arrive at the loves one had lost, to acknowledge them, to take leave of them properly, instead of leaving them behind running as she had left everything; and that there was a possible arrival even at what one lost, the arrival that is to make peace with what no longer comes back.

And she discovered that people, whom she had always treated as stops in the running, as time that distracted her from the goals, were after all what gave savour to the arrival, that it was not worth it to arrive anywhere alone, and that the presence she had never given, now that she gave it, brought her a richness no victory had brought her. Aurora, who had collected victories and lost people, began, in the time of the arrival, to do the contrary: to leave the victories behind and to recover the people, and she discovered that she had made, her whole life, the wrong trade, and that she was still in time to invert it.

She discovered, in that time of staying, likings she had never had time to discover, because the running leaves no time for likings, only for goals. She discovered that she liked small and unimportant things — a flower, a conversation, an afternoon —, things she had despised her whole life as distractions from the running and that now seemed to her, suddenly, the very substance of life. She discovered that she liked to be with her sister, with her nephews, with people, without it being for anything, only for the company; she discovered that she liked to do nothing, in a way that would have horrified her years before; she discovered that she liked herself, the stopped Aurora, whom she scarcely knew, and who was better company than she expected. And each liking discovered was an arrival, because a liking is a thing one savours in the present, not a goal one pursues in the future, and Aurora, in discovering her likings, in allowing herself to have them and to savour them, went on filling the stopped Aurora that the bottom had revealed empty, went on discovering that beneath the runner there was, after all, a woman with likings and wishes, a person, and that that person, buried a whole lifetime by the running, was capable, at last, of living.

And she thought of the father, in that time, and made peace with him, in the possible way. She understood that Firmino had put his and now? on her back without malice, out of a wounded hunger that was his and that he had not known how not to pass to his daughter; and instead of hating him for it, she pitied him, the father who had run his life without ever arriving, who had died with his and now? in his mouth, without ever having allowed himself an enough, without ever having arrived anywhere. She visited the father's grave, in that time, a thing she did rarely because to visit graves was to stop and she did not stop, and she had, before the grave, a conversation with the dead father that she needed to have. She told him, in silence, what she had never told him in life: that she had run her whole life for him, for his pride, for his and now?, and that she was tired, and that she was going to stop. She told him that she forgave him, and that she hoped that he, wherever he was, had at last found the rest he had never had in life, the enough he had never known how to say. And she told him, above all, a thing that was her liberation: that she was not going to go on running his race, that she was going to stop, that she was going to arrive, and that if that disappointed him, wherever he was, she was sorry, but that the life was hers and that she was going, at last, to live it her way and not his. And it was a conversation that freed her, because it was to tell the father, at last, the no she had never told him in life, the I no longer run your race, and to say that no, even to a grave, even late, was to cut the last thread that bound her to the and now?, was to become, at last, the owner of her own road, free to travel it, or to stop in it, as she wished and not as the father had ordered.

And Aurora understood that in learning to arrive she did, also, what the father had never done, she broke in herself the and now? he had passed to her, and that perhaps that was the way to honour him and to free herself of him at the same time: not by fulfilling forever his wounded ambition, but by arriving, at last, at the place where he had never arrived, and showing, at least to herself and to whoever came after, that one could stop running, that there was an enough, that the road had a place where one could, at last, stay.
