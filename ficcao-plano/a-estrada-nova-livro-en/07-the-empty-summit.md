## 7. The empty summit

Aurora was seven years old when she climbed, once, with her father, the Hill of the Gallows, the high hill that could be seen from Véspera, and it was on that climb, without knowing it, that she learned everything that would come to lose her.

It was a summer Sunday, and Firmino, who rarely stopped, took her up a goat track, she climbing behind his big legs, scratching herself on the broom bushes, full of pride to be going with her father to that men's thing that was climbing the hill. They reached the top out of breath, and Véspera was down below, tiny, the Square, the church, the river glinting, the houses like boxes, and Aurora, with her heart leaping, turned to her father wanting to say look, father, look how lovely it is, look what we climbed. And Firmino, without looking at the view, without looking at the village down below, pointed with his finger at another hill, farther, higher, blue with distance, and said: that one is high, Aurora. That one is the one we should climb someday. This one is nothing beside that one.

And the girl, who had reached the top of the hill and wanted to enjoy it, was left looking at the next hill, and the hill she was on, conquered, with the lovely village at her feet, suddenly lost its savour, overshadowed by what was lacking. They went down, and during the whole descent Firmino spoke of the other hill, of the day they would climb it, and Aurora went down the Hill of the Gallows without having got to be on it, already on the next hill, at seven years old, and that scene was her whole life in miniature, and she would only understand it decades later, atop the greatest summit of all, when there was no longer a higher hill for the father to point to.

Because there was a goal, the greatest of all, the highest summit Aurora had ever pursued, and she reached it, and it was reaching it that took her to the bottom, because it was atop the greatest of her successes that she felt, with all clarity, the emptiness that had always accompanied her and that she had always managed to flee to the next goal.

Aurora had prepared her whole life for that summit, and the preparation had been, itself, a whole life: years of effort, of sacrifice, of renouncing, all aimed at that height, all justified by the promise of what she would feel on getting there. She had told herself, a thousand times, in the hard years, when I get there it will be worth it, when I get there I will rest, when I get there I will be happy; and that promise, the when I get there, had been what sustained her in the sacrifices, the carrot before the donkey, the deferred reward that justified the present effort. And now she had got there, to the when I get there, and discovered that there was no reward at all, that the when I get there had been a lie, that the there did not have what it had promised, and with it crumbled the justification of all the sacrifices, because the sacrifices only made sense by the promised reward, and the reward taken away the sacrifices were left without sense, in vain. Aurora looked back, from the empty summit, at the life of sacrifices she had made for the promise of that summit, and saw them all emptied of meaning, because the thing for which she had made them did not exist, and it was that vision, that of a whole life of sacrifices in vain, that made the summit not a triumph but the worst moment of her life.

It does not much matter what the goal was, and in Véspera these things are said without a name: it was the greatest recognition, the greatest rise, the summit of her life of conquests, that for which she had run, at bottom, her whole life, the goal that, if any, would be the one that would make the and now? fall silent at last, the one that would give her, at last, the arrival. Aurora reached it, after a life of climbing, and there was festivity, and the village celebrated, and those who admired her came to tell her you have arrived, you managed it, you are the greatest. And Aurora, atop that summit, awaiting to feel at last the arrival, the satisfaction, the enough, felt... nothing. Or worse than nothing: she felt the same emptiness as always, the same hunger, and, beneath them, a familiar voice, the father's voice within her, that, even before the greatest of successes, asked, softly, implacable: and now?

And it was that and now?, sounding atop the greatest summit of her life, in the moment that should have been that of the arrival, that broke Aurora in half, because it showed her, without appeal, the truth she had fled her whole life: that the arrival did not exist, that there was no summit, however high, where the and now? fell silent, where she at last arrived, where the hunger at last sated itself; that she could climb forever, reach the greatest of successes and then another greater still, and the and now? would always be there, and the emptiness would always be there, because they were not things that were filled by climbing, they were things the climbing would never fill, however high she got. Aurora had reached the top of her life and discovered that the top was empty, that the arrival they had promised her, that she had promised herself at each step, was not there, never had been, was a mirage, and that she had run a whole life after a mirage, and had arrived, exhausted, at the place where the mirage should be, and found the emptiness.

She had imagined, a thousand times, along the climb, what she would do when she reached that summit: how she would feel, what she would say, how at last she would rest, enjoy, be happy. She had imagined the arrival as a place of light, of fullness, the place where everything she had sacrificed would at last make sense, where she could say it was worth it. And she arrived, and it was none of that, there was no light at all, there was no fullness, there was no it was worth it; there was the same emptiness as always and the same and now?, and the disillusion was proportional to the imagination, that is, immense, because the more one imagines an arrival the more one is disillusioned who arrives and does not find what they imagined. Aurora fell from the height of her own imagination, from the image she had built of what arriving would be, and the fall was enormous, because she had imagined enormous, and the ground where she fell, that of the real emptiness of the summit, was so much lower than the height she had imagined that the fall broke her. And she understood, in that fall, that part of what had made her run her whole life was that image, the image of a glorious arrival up high, and that the image was false, that there was no glorious arrival at all atop any summit, that she had run her whole life after an image she had invented and that reality did not have, and that the reality of the summit, far from the image, was only emptiness.

It was the blackest moment of Aurora's life, that one, atop the greatest of her successes, because it was the moment in which the belief that had sustained her whole life crumbled: that it was worth running because one day she would arrive. That belief taken away, seeing that there was no arrival, that she had run for nothing, Aurora was left without ground, because her whole life had rested on that promise, that the arrival was coming, and to discover that it was not coming, that it would never come, was to discover that she had given her life to a senseless race, that she had sacrificed everything — the enjoyment, the rest, the presence, the people — for an arrival that did not exist. And that, therefore, all the sacrifices had been in vain, because the thing for which she had made them was a mirage, and she was left, atop everything, with her hands full of victories and her life empty of all the rest, understanding that she had traded everything that mattered for nothing.

There was, at that summit, a moment Aurora never forgot, and it was the moment when she understood that she had awaited her whole life for that instant and that the instant brought nothing. Her whole life had been a preparation for that arrival, all the steps had been to get there, to the greatest of summits, and Aurora had imagined a thousand times how it would be, the sensation of arriving, the fullness, the rest, the at last. And she arrived, and there was none of that, there was only the emptiness and the and now?, and the disproportion between what she had awaited and what she felt was of a cruelty that brought her down: a whole life of waiting and of sacrifice for an instant that, when it came, was empty. It was like one who walks years through the desert toward an oasis they see in the distance, and arrives, and the oasis is a mirage, it is sand, there is no water at all; and the thirst, that had been borne a whole life by the promise of the water, bursts all at once on discovering that there is no water, that there never was, that one walked a whole life toward a mirage. Aurora reached her oasis and found sand, and the thirst of a whole life, held back by the promise of that arrival, burst all at that summit, and it was the burst thirst, more than the emptiness, that threw her to the bottom.

At the dinner that was given on account of that summit, with important people at the table and speeches, there was a man beside her, a certain doutor Heitor, who was of those who rise and who took her for an equal, and who, on seeing her strange in the midst of all that, drew near and said to her, with the confidence of one who hands over a remedy they know: it is the sadness of the ending, dona Aurora. It happens to all those who reach high. One spends a life climbing a thing and when one has it this emptiness comes, this kind of and now. But it passes. The secret is not to stop. Find yourself another objective at once, a bigger one, and the emptiness disappears. It is only not to let it cool.

And Aurora nearly believed it, because it was exactly what she had always done, find a new objective to fill the emptiness, and doutor Heitor's mouth said to her her own remedy of always. She nearly answered him yes, that he was right, that she already had the next objective in mind. But that night, to hear the remedy said by another, to hear it from outside, made her understand, with a cold, that the counsel was wrong, that the emptiness would not pass with a new objective, that a new objective was exactly what had kept her running her whole life without ever filling the emptiness. That man was counselling her her own illness as though it were the cure, and the two of them there, at the table of the triumph, were the same thing: two empty summits giving each other counsel not to stop. The emptiness was not the sadness of the end of a conquest, to be cured with the next conquest; it was the deep emptiness of her whole life, that each next conquest had always smothered and never cured, and that now, at the greatest summit, with no next conquest of the same height to smother it, showed itself entire. And it was to refuse that counsel — find a new objective —, it was to understand that the new objective was the problem and not the solution, that marked Aurora's turning, because it was the first time that, instead of fleeing the emptiness forward, to the next goal, she stopped and faced it, and to face the emptiness instead of fleeing it was the beginning of curing it.

And there was, at that empty summit, an absolute solitude. Because Aurora was alone up there, at the height no one else had reached, and the solitude of the summit was literal and was symbolic: literal, because she had let fall along the way, in the running, all those who could accompany her; symbolic, because no one could understand her emptiness, no one imagined that the woman who had reached the top felt empty at the top, everyone thought her fulfilled, and she could not tell anyone that the top was empty, that she had arrived and felt nothing, because no one would believe it, everyone would think it madness or ingratitude of one who has everything. And so Aurora lived her emptiness alone, at the height, misunderstood, smiling for the festivity of others and feeling, within, the desert of one who reached the end of the climb and discovered that there was nothing up there, that the climb led nowhere, that she had run her whole life to an empty summit.

And there came back to her memory, at that empty summit, the Hill of the Gallows, the climb with the father at seven years old, Firmino's finger pointing the blue hill in the distance, that one is high, and the girl going down without having got to be on the top where she was. She understood, at the empty summit of her life, that the Hill of the Gallows had been her whole life in miniature: to reach a height and, instead of enjoying the view, be pointed at once to the next height, and to lose the enjoyment of what one has to the desire of what is lacking, always, on each hill, on each summit, until that last one, where there was no longer a higher hill for the father to point to and where therefore, at last, there was nowhere to flee. And there she was left, alone, with the view she had never learned to enjoy and with the emptiness of one who climbed a whole life without ever looking around, and with the certainty, new and terrible, that the father's finger pointing the blue hill went on pointing within her, even the father dead, even there no longer being any hill.

There is an expression that says that success does not fill, and Aurora, at the empty summit, understood it in her body, not as an idea, but as a lived truth. She had always thought the expression was of people who had no success, an excuse of those who did not arrive, a way for the failures to console themselves; and she discovered, at the summit, that it was true, that success indeed did not fill, that one could have all the success in the world and be empty, and that those who said that success does not fill were not failures consoling themselves, they were, some of them, people who had had success and discovered that it did not fill. And it was humiliating, for Aurora, to discover that the truth was on the side of the expression she had always despised, that the success she had pursued her whole life was, after all, unable to fill, as those said whom she had taken for envious; and she had to revise, from the empty summit, her whole belief, that success filled, that it was worth running after it, because she had it now, all of it, and was empty, and the proof was made, in her own body, in her own emptiness: success did not fill, never had filled, and she had given her life to filling it with a thing it did not give.

And it was from the bottom of that empty summit — because there are bottoms that are at the height — that Aurora, at last, stopped. Not by choice, at first, but from exhaustion and from despair: she stopped because there was nowhere left to run, because she had reached the greatest summit and it was empty, because the running had lost its meaning in losing the arrival. It was hard for her, at first, even to admit the emptiness, because to admit it was to admit that her whole life had rested on a mistake, and the pride resisted. She tried, in the first times after the summit, to do what she had always done: find a new goal, a new summit, tell herself that that one had not been the true one, that the true one was the next, and that that time she would feel the arrival. She tried, but she could no longer believe it, because the summit she had reached had been the greatest, the one she had always said would be the one that would suffice, and if that one had not sufficed, which would? There was no greater. And it was there being no greater, having reached the top of tops and found it empty, that took from Aurora the possibility of fleeing to the next summit, because there was no next summit, and without a next summit to flee to, Aurora was left, at last, without flight, face to face with the emptiness, forced to see it because there was no longer anywhere to run so as not to see it. And it was that lack of a next summit, that impossibility of fleeing to a higher place, that made that summit the bottom: because an emptiness one can flee is not a bottom, it is a postponement, and an emptiness one cannot flee, because there is nowhere to, that is a bottom, and Aurora, at the top of tops, with no higher summit to run to, touched at last the bottom of her emptiness, because for the first time she had no way of fleeing it.

And in that stopping, forced, desperate, atop everything and at the bottom of herself, Aurora began, without knowing it, the only thing that could save her, which was to look, at last, not at the next step, which there no longer was, but at herself, at her life, at what she had made of it, and to ask herself, for the first time without fleeing, whether there would be another way to live that was not to run, and where it would be, if not at the height, the arrival she had sought in vain her whole life.
