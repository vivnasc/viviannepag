## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a woman, in Véspera, who was the first at everything and who only at the end learned to arrive.

Aurora, daughter of Firmino, the one who rose more than anyone and never felt herself arrived, because each victory became the step of the next, and the finish line always drew back, one more step, one more new road. She spent a life running after an arrival that did not exist at the end of the climb, and it took reaching the greatest of summits and finding it empty, and a Keeper, and a sister who had stayed and contented herself, for her to understand what no one had taught her: that the goal she pursued had never been hers, it had been the father's; that her worth was written at birth and not at the top of any stair; and that the arrival is not a place reached by climbing, it is a decision to stop and to be where one is.

It was recorded too that she learned to distinguish the rising from the arriving, which she had taken her whole life for synonyms and which were after all contraries: that one can rise one's whole life and never arrive, and that one can arrive without rising anything more, stopping where one is; and that the arrival she had pursued upward had always been, stopped, down below, in the place from which she fled. And that she found again the sister, Conceição, who had stayed and contented herself, and whom the village and the father had held to be the lesser, and acknowledged in her the master who taught her what she, the first at everything, had never known.

It was recorded that she learned to arrive: to savour what she had instead of running to what was lacking, to stop in places, to silence the father's and now?, to say at last enough. And that she said, one ordinary morning, with nothing special about it, sitting in the sun, the phrase her whole life had lacked: I have arrived. I am here. And here is enough.

And it is set down, for whoever reads and recognises themselves, that this is not a story against ambition nor a praise of laziness, and it would be to misunderstand it to see it so. Aurora did not learn not to do; she learned to arrive at what she does. She did not trade the large life for a small one; she learned to inhabit the life she had, whatever its size. The lesson is not to stop rising, it is to stop making of the rising the only measure of worth and of arrival; it is to discover that one is worth something for being and not for winning, and that the arrival is not at the top of any stair, it is in the decision to be where one is and to find it enough. One who runs may go on running, if that is what they want; but let them learn, like Aurora, to stop now and then to arrive at what they have reached, to say I have arrived before going on, not to let the running eat their whole life without ever an arrival.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs one who ran their whole life to learn to stop, nor that the arrival one pursues upward had always been, stopped, down below, in the place from which one flees. There are those who climb all the stairs and never arrive anywhere, because the arrival is not climbed, it is decided. For those, books such as this one are needed. And of all of it a record was made.
