## 3. The house I came from

There are inheritances that are not of money nor of things, and the one Aurora received was of those: she inherited from her father a hunger, and hunger is the worst of inheritances, because it is not spent, it multiplies. One who inherits money can spend it and be free of it; one who inherits a hunger carries it a whole life, and the more they feed it the more it grows, because the inherited hunger is not sated by eating, it is sated, if at all, by understanding it and letting it go. Aurora inherited her father's hunger as one inherits an eye colour, without choosing it, without noticing it, and grew up thinking that the hunger was hers, part of her nature, when it was an inheritance, a thing that had been passed to her and that she could, if she knew how, give back. And it was that the great discovery of her life, toward the end: that the hunger that moved her was not hers, it was inherited, and that what is inherited can be given back, that she could lay down her father's hunger as one lays down a case carried without knowing it was another's, and discover, beneath it, her own hunger, or her own lack of hunger, the Aurora who existed beneath the inheritance and who perhaps had no hunger at all, perhaps only a wish to arrive, to stop, to live.

Aurora's running cannot be understood without the house she came from, because it was in that house, poor and full of a hunger that was not only for bread, that she learned that to rise was everything, and that to stay where one was was the worst of misfortunes.

Aurora's house was poor, with the poverty of Véspera of other times, and Firmino, the father, was a man whom that poverty had wounded. He had dreamed of rising, of leaving the poverty, of being someone, and had not managed it, had stayed where he was born, and that defeat, that of a life that did not get where it wanted, had embittered him, had made of him a man of wounded ambition, who looked upward with desire and at his own life with spite. And when Aurora was born to him, and the girl gave early signs of being brilliant, of being capable, Firmino saw in her his second chance, the chance to, through his daughter, climb what he had not climbed, reach where he had not reached, realise the ambition life had denied him. And he put upon his daughter, without saying it and perhaps without knowing it, all the weight of that unrealised dream.

There was, in the poverty Aurora came from, a lesson she drew and that marked her: that one rises or sinks, that there is no middle ground, that either one struggles to rise or one is dragged downward. Poverty teaches this to one who grows up in it with a hunger to leave it: that to stop is to sink, that to rest is to risk going back to the misery, that only constant rising keeps the person afloat. And Aurora learned this lesson of poverty, that to stop is dangerous, that one must always rise so as not to sink, and carried it her whole life, even long after the poverty had been left behind, even when there was no longer any danger of sinking, even at the height where she was. She went on rising as one who flees the misery, with the ingrained fear that to stop would make her return to it, without understanding that the misery had been left behind long ago, that she could stop without sinking, that the danger she fled no longer existed. The poverty, which had made her rise, had left her that fear, that of stopping and sinking, and it was that fear, as much as the father's and now?, that kept her running long after the running ceased to be necessary, because a person who learned in poverty that to stop is to sink does not unlearn it easily, even rich, even at the height, even safe.

And so Aurora grew up not only with her own ambition, but with the father's on her back, carrying the dream of rising of a man who had not risen, and that double load — her ambition and the father's — pushed her upward with a force few have, made of her the first at everything, but made of her also a girl who had no right to stop, because to stop was to betray not only her ambition but the father's dream, was to condemn him to a second defeat, that of seeing the daughter stay where he had stayed. Aurora rose, in part, so as not to disappoint the father, to realise what he had not realised, to lift him, with her rising, out of the bitterness of his defeat; and that motivation, to rise for the father, was even more enslaving than to rise for herself, because a person can content themselves with what they conquer for themselves, but hardly contents themselves with what they conquer to kill another's hunger, a hunger that is not theirs to kill and that is never killed.

There was, in the house Aurora came from, a word that was said much and another that was never said, and the two marked her. The one said much was more: more effort, more marks, farther, higher, always more. The one never said was enough: no one, in that house, said enough, it is good, that is enough, rest, because enough was a forbidden word, a word of defeat, the word of those who content themselves and do not rise. And Aurora grew up in a world of more without enough, and carried that world within, and made of it her life: always more, never enough, because it was what she had learned, it was the only grammar she knew. Only much later, with the Keeper and with the sister, would Aurora learn the forbidden word, the enough, and would discover that it was the word she lacked, the key of the arrival, because to arrive is, at bottom, to manage to say enough, and one who grows up without that word, in a world only of more, never arrives, because they lack the very word with which one arrives. And Aurora had to learn, old, the word she had lacked since she was a girl, the enough, and it was like learning a new language, the language of arrival, that the house she came from had never taught her, because in it only the language of more was spoken.

She grew up too hearing, at home, the father's contempt for those who stayed where they were born, for those who did not rise, for those who contented themselves. The neighbours who were satisfied with the simple life were, in Firmino's mouth, people without ambition, stopped people, people who were nothing; and Aurora learned to fear, more than anything, becoming one of them, staying stopped, contenting herself, not rising, because to be stopped was, in the father's house, the definition of failure, the worst thing one could be. And that fear, that of staying stopped, that of contenting herself, pursued her her whole life, and was one of the engines of her running, because to stop, for Aurora, was not rest, it was failure, it was to become one of the stopped ones the father despised, and she preferred to run to exhaustion than to risk becoming that.

It must be said that Firmino was not a monster, and that Aurora, even after understanding the harm he had done her, never managed to hate him, because she saw, beneath the demanding father, the wounded man he was. Firmino had given his daughter what he had: his hunger, his ambition, his refusal to content himself; he had not known how to give her what he did not have, which was the peace, the contentment, the knowing how to stop, because he himself had never had them. No one gives what they do not have, and Firmino did not have quiet to give his daughter, he had restlessness, and it was restlessness he transmitted to her, with the best of loves, thinking he pushed her toward happiness when he pushed her toward the same race without end that had consumed him. And Aurora, in understanding this, understood that she had inherited from the father not only the hunger, but the unhappiness of the hunger, and that the and now? that pursued her was the echo of the father's own unhappiness, a man who had died running, without ever arriving, and who had passed to his daughter, without wishing it, the curse of running and never arriving that had been his life.

And there was, in the house Aurora came from, an absence that weighed as much as the presence of the father's ambition: the absence of a place where one simply was, without having to deserve it. In Firmino's house, the love and the pride were earned, with deeds, with risings, with victories; there was no free love, a love for being, a lap where the girl could simply be without having to bring a victory to justify it. And Aurora grew up so, thinking that love was earned by rising, that she was worth something for what she conquered and not for what she was, and carried that conviction her whole life, always rising to earn a love and a worth that, at bottom, she sought in the wrong place, because the love earned by rising never suffices, it always has to be re-earned on the next step, whereas the love that is enough, the free one, that of being, that Aurora had never known, and so never knew it existed, and spent her life rising after a substitute for it that never sated her.

There was a sister, younger, Conceição, who did not leave that house with the same hunger, and whom the father, for not being brilliant like Aurora, left in peace, and that sister stayed in Véspera, made a simple life, married, had children, contented herself, and Aurora, for years, looked at her sister with a touch of the contempt she had learned from the father, the Conceição who stayed, who did not rise, who contented herself with little. Only much later, with the story that was going to happen to her, would Aurora look at her sister with other eyes, and see, in the Conceição who had stayed, not a failure, but a woman who knew a thing that she, with all her victories, had never learned: to arrive, to content oneself, to be where one is, to enjoy what one has. The sister the father had despised for not rising was, perhaps, the only of the two who had arrived somewhere, because she had arrived at herself, at her life, at her contentment, while Aurora, who had climbed everything, had never arrived anywhere, always running to the next step.

There was a difference in the way the father had treated the two daughters, and that difference, which had always seemed to favour Aurora, had after all been her misfortune and the salvation of the sister. To the father, the daughter who was worth something was Aurora, the brilliant one, the one who went far, and it was in her that he put the hunger, the demand, the and now?; Conceição, for not being brilliant, did not interest the father for his project, and he left her in peace, without hunger, without demand, without and now? And that difference, which made of Aurora the favourite and of Conceição the passed-over, was, at bottom, the contrary of what it seemed: because to be the father's favourite was to receive the hunger, the curse of the running, the inability to arrive; and to be the passed-over was to be spared all of that, was to grow up free, was to be able to arrive. Conceição gained in being passed over what Aurora lost in being favoured: the peace, the contentment, the arrival. And there was in this a bitter lesson about what it is to be the favourite child of a father with a hunger: that to be the one chosen to carry a father's hunger is no favour, it is a load, and that sometimes the less invested child is the more spared, and the more invested the more sacrificed, because it is in them that the father pours his hunger, and a father's hunger is the worst inheritance a child can receive.

Conceição, the sister who stayed, deserved that it be better told who she was, because it was she, in the end, the salvation of Aurora, and she was, of the two, the one the village and the father had held to be the lesser. Conceição was not brilliant like Aurora, did not get the best marks, gave no signs of going far, and so the father had left her in peace, had not put his hunger upon her, had not pushed her, and Conceição had grown up free of the and now?, free of the stairway, free of the running, and had made a life to her own measure: simple, small, but hers, chosen, content. She had married a man of Véspera, had had children, lived in a modest house, had her even days and her small joys, and was, in her discreet way, a happy woman, or at least at peace, arrived. And the father had died thinking that Conceição had been nothing, that she had fallen behind, that the daughter who was worth something was Aurora; and he had carried to the grave that error, without ever understanding that the daughter who had arrived somewhere was Conceição, and that Aurora, the one he had pushed and of whom he was proud, was the one who would never arrive anywhere, condemned by him to the running without end. It was an irony the father would never see, that of having despised the daughter who arrived and pushed the daughter who would never arrive, but Aurora, in the end, would see it, and it would be the despised sister who taught her what the father's daughter of pride, with all her victories, had never known.

But that was for the end. At the beginning, and during almost her whole life, Aurora carried the house she came from on her back as an engine and a curse: the engine that made her rise more than anyone, and the curse that prevented her from, at some time, on some step, feeling herself arrived, because she carried within the father's wounded hunger and the fear of stopping, and with those two things within there is no victory that suffices nor summit where one rests.
