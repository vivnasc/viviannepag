## 10. Learning to arrive

There was a particular difficulty in Aurora learning to arrive, and it was that she, used to being the first at everything, the best, the one who learned fast and did it better, was, in this, a terrible pupil, slow, awkward, full of relapses, and that humiliated her. The woman who had learned everything with ease, who had always been the first, found herself an awkward beginner in the art of arriving, behind simple people who knew it from birth, behind the sister who had never studied anything; and that inversion, that of being the last where she had always been the first, cost her pride. But Conceição taught her to accept being a beginner, not to demand of herself being the best at arriving — which would be, again, the running —, to accept learning slowly, with errors, as any beginner learns; and Aurora had to swallow the pride of being the first to allow herself to be the last, the slow apprentice of the art of arriving, and it was to swallow that pride, to accept being a beginner, part of the very learning, because to arrive requires humility, the humility of accepting that one does not know, that one is learning, that one is, in this, a beginner like any other, and Aurora, the first at everything, had to learn to be, at last, a beginner, and it was perhaps that the hardest and most healing lesson of all, that of coming down from the podium of first at everything to the ground of an apprentice of a simple thing that everyone knew and she did not.

To learn to arrive, Aurora discovered, was a trade like the others, and like the others it was learned slowly, with knack and with relapses, and she went on learning it, in that time of stopping, gesture by gesture, with Conceição for a master and the wish to stop running for a tool.

It did not go straight, this learning, and it would be a lie to say that Aurora learned to arrive from one day to the next. There were relapses, many, days when the old hunger came back with force, when she caught herself running again, inventing a goal, measuring herself by what was lacking; and at each relapse Aurora was discouraged, thought she had changed nothing, that she would always go back to the running. And it was Conceição who taught her patience in this too, not to make of the relapse a failure, not to demand of herself perfection in her very learning of not demanding perfection — which would be the greatest of ironies. You fall into the running again, and you go back to stopping, said the sister, and the next time you stop faster, and so one learns, little by little, with relapses, like everything that is hard. And Aurora, who had demanded perfection of herself her whole life, had to learn to be patient with herself, to forgive herself the relapses, to treat the learning to arrive not as one more goal to conquer perfectly, but as a way to travel by stumbling, and that patience with herself was, itself, an arrival, because it was the opposite of the father's demand, it was she giving herself the gentleness the and now? had never given her.

She began with the simplest and hardest thing: to savour what she already had. Aurora had a life full of things she had never savoured, victories that passed without festivity, conquests she had not enjoyed because she was already running to the next; and Conceição taught her to go back, to look at what she had done, and to let herself, at last, feel pride, feel joy, feel that it had been worth it, a thing she had never allowed herself because pride and joy were stops and she did not stop. Aurora learned to look at her life of deeds not as a stairway of steps already passed on the way to the next, but as a harvest, a rich harvest she had never reaped because she was always sowing the next field, and in reaping it at last, in letting herself feel what she had conquered, she discovered a satisfaction the running had never given her, the satisfaction not of winning, but of having won and of savouring it, which is a different thing and much greater.

She learned to celebrate, at last, what she had never celebrated. Conceição, in a gesture that moved Aurora, proposed to her a strange thing: to celebrate, belatedly, the victories Aurora had never celebrated. To take the great deeds of her life, one by one, and celebrate them now, years late, with a supper, with a toast, with a recognition she had not allowed herself at the time. And they did it, the two sisters, and it was of a sweet sadness, to celebrate decades late victories that had passed without festivity, to give at last to each one the moment of arrival that had been denied it; and Aurora wept, in those late celebrations, because it was like going to fetch, one by one, the joys she had left along the way, and giving them, at last, the place they deserved. And she understood that it is never too late to celebrate, that one can go back and give an old victory the festivity it did not have, and that to do so, to celebrate belatedly what was not celebrated at the time, is a form of arriving retroactively, of going to fetch the arrivals one wasted and living them at last, late but lived.

She learned to stop in places. Where before she passed running, always with her eyes on the next, Aurora learned to stop, to stay, to be. She learned to sit through an afternoon without filling it with objectives, to let an hour pass without producing anything, to be in a place without already planning the next place. It cost her, at first, because the head always fled to the next step, and Aurora had to bring it back, time after time, to the place where she was, like one who trains a dog to stay; but she went on training it, and the head went on learning to stay, and Aurora went on discovering the savour of being where one is, of inhabiting a moment instead of running over it, and it was as though, after a life of seeing the world from the window of a moving train, she at last got off the train and trod the ground and saw the things still, up close, with time, and discovered that they were beautiful, and that she had never seen them because she had always passed too fast.

She learned, with her sister, the worth of the small daily arrivals, that life is full of them and that she had never seen them. To finish a meal is an arrival; to see the sun set is an arrival; to end a good conversation is an arrival; to come to the end of a day is an arrival. Life, Aurora understood, is made of a thousand small arrivals every day, a thousand moments in which one completes something, in which one can say there, it is done, I have arrived; and she had spent her life crossing them all without seeing them, because she only recognised as arrival the height of a great stairway, and despised the small daily arrivals as nothing. And the sister taught her to see them, to recognise the small arrivals, to savour them: there, we have had supper, how good; there, the sun has set, how beautiful; there, it was a good day. And Aurora discovered that life, seen so, was not a single great stairway with a single arrival up at the top, it was a succession of small daily arrivals, within reach of everyone every day, and that one who learns to recognise them and to savour them arrives a thousand times a day, and one who only recognises the great arrival up at the top never arrives, because the top never comes. The arrival, she understood, was not rare and unique, it was common and daily; it was she who had sought it in the wrong place, up at the height, when it was everywhere, every day, to the hand of one who knew how to see it.

And she learned to silence the and now? Whenever she reached or had a good thing, and the father's voice within her asked, out of habit, and now?, and then what?, Aurora learned to answer it, with a new firmness: now nothing. Now I stay here. Now I savour this. That is enough. She learned to say enough, the word the father had never said, the word her whole life had lacked, and each time she said it, and felt it, she silenced a little more the father's voice, and arrived a little more at herself. Because the and now? was what prevented her from arriving, and to silence it, to tell it enough, was to open, at last, room for the arrival, for the being, for the feeling that one has enough. And Aurora went on learning to say enough, not as a giving-up, but as an arrival, because to say enough to what one has is to say I have arrived, is to say I am well where I am, is to stop running and discover that the place where one is is already sufficient.

She learned, too, to do things that led nowhere, and it was one of the hardest learnings, because everything in Aurora's life had always had to lead somewhere, to serve a goal, to be a step. To do a thing only for the pleasure of doing it, a thing that was not a means to anything, that rose nothing, that conquered nothing — that Aurora had never done, and she had to learn, with her sister, the worth of what serves for nothing. She learned to walk without a destination, to sew without it being to sell, to look at the river without an objective, to converse without the conversation having a useful end; and she discovered, with surprise, that those things that led nowhere were, after all, the ones that most made her feel arrived, precisely because they were not steps, they did not point to a next, they exhausted themselves in themselves, in the enjoyment of the moment. A thing that leads nowhere obliges us to be where we are, because there is no anywhere to which it points; and it was in doing useless things, in the good sense, things that were ends in themselves and not means to anything, that Aurora went on training the atrophied muscle of being, of arriving, of being enough in the present.

She learned, slowly, that there was a difference between walking and running, and that she had only known the running. To walk is to move with pleasure, at one's own rhythm, enjoying the way, without the hurry of arriving; to run is to move with the hurry of arriving, without enjoying the way, with one's eyes only on the goal. Aurora had run her whole life, had never walked, and the sister taught her to walk, literally, in unhurried walks through the village and the fields, and in those walks, in which for the first time she moved without running, Aurora discovered the pleasure of the way, of the things one sees when one does not go running, of the conversations one has when one is not in a hurry to arrive, and she understood that she had lost her whole life of enjoying the ways for always going running to the goals, and that now, walking, she recovered, slowly, what the running had robbed her of: the present, the way, the pleasure of moving without the distress of having to arrive.

"Do you know what is strangest, Conceição?" said Aurora, one afternoon, the two of them sitting in the sun of the Square, without hurry. "It is that I, now that I have stopped, that I no longer rise anything, that I conquer nothing new, feel myself more... " she searched for the word "more arrived than I ever felt atop everything. How can it be? I rose my whole life in search of arriving, and I only arrived when I stopped rising."

She learned, at last, to be present, which was the thing she had most lacked, and the presence brought her a world she did not know existed. Because in being present — where she was, with whom she was, in what she was — Aurora began to see things the running had always hidden from her: she saw the faces of the people she spoke with, instead of being already in the next place; she saw the beauty of the small things, the light of an afternoon, the savour of a food, that had always passed her by; she saw, at last, the life, that had always happened in the present and that she had always lost by being in the future. And the presence, she discovered, was the key of the arrival, because to arrive is no more than to be present where one is, and one who is present has already arrived, by definition, at the place where they are, and one who is absent, in the future, at the next goal, never arrives anywhere, because they are never where they are. Aurora, who had lived her whole life absent, in the future, learned to be present, and in being present she arrived, because the presence is the arrival itself, and the arrival she had sought in the future was, after all, in the present, awaiting only that she be there to find it.

There were relapses, even in that time of learning, and Aurora's greatest temptation, in the relapses, was to think she had failed, that she was not even good for arriving, and to go back to running out of despair. But Conceição taught her to see the relapses in another way: not as failures, but as part of the way, as the stumbles of one who learns to walk, that do not mean one is not going to learn, they mean only that one is learning. And Aurora had to apply to herself, in the relapses of the learning to arrive, the same arrival she was learning: that is, to arrive at the relapse, to accept it, to be in it without demanding of herself, instead of running to flee it toward perfection. To learn to arrive included learning to arrive at one's own failures, to be with them without transforming them into one more running to correct them; and it was perhaps that, the hardest arrival, that of arriving at one's own stumbles with gentleness instead of fleeing them with haste, and it was to learn it that made the learning possible, because one who demands perfection of themselves in the learning of not demanding perfection never learns, and Aurora had to allow herself to be imperfect at learning to arrive in order to, at last, arrive.

And Conceição laughed, with that simple laugh of one who always knew, and said: because the arrival was never up there, sister. It was always here, where you never stopped. You ran upward in search of it, and it was down here, stopped, awaiting you, in the place from which you always fled. You arrived when you stopped because the arrival was to stop, it always was. It took you a lifetime of rising to understand that one only had to stop. But you stopped. Better late.
