## 1. The first at everything

Aurora was the first at everything, and whoever saw her from outside envied her her life, and no one imagined that the most successful woman in Véspera was, within, one of the most tired and most empty, because she had spent her life running to an arrival that never arrived.

There are people who run because they like to run, for the pleasure of the movement, of the climbing, of the conquest, and there are people who run because they are afraid to stop, because to stop brings them an anguish that only the movement smothers, and Aurora, though she seemed of the first, was of the second. From outside, she seemed a woman moved by the liking for winning, by the healthy ambition of one who wants more; within, she was a woman moved by the fear of stopping, by the anguish that stillness brought her, and the running was, for her, less a pleasure than a flight, the flight from an anguish that awaited her whenever she slowed. So she could not stop: because to stop was not rest, it was a meeting with the anguish, it was to be face to face with the emptiness that the running smothered, and Aurora preferred to run exhausted than to stop and meet the emptiness, like one who prefers to go on walking, even dead with tiredness, than to stop and feel the cold. The running was her coat against the cold of the emptiness, and she did not take it off, even exhausted, because to take it off was to feel the cold, and the cold frightened her more than the exhaustion. And it took the empty summit to show her that the cold was there all the same, beneath the coat, that the running did not take it away, it only smothered it, for Aurora to dare, at last, to stop and to face the cold she had fled her whole life.

Since she was a girl she had been the first. The first of the village school, the best who had ever passed through there in living memory, the one the teacher pointed to as an example, the one who got the best marks with no apparent effort and who, when effort was needed, made it better than everyone. And she did not stop at the school: she was the first of her house, humble folk, to rise in life, the first to leave the poverty she came from, the first woman of Véspera to reach places no woman of the village had reached, the one who put her hand to a thing and made it yield, grow, make a name. She rose, Aurora, her whole life, step by step, and each step was higher than the previous, and the village looked at her from below, with pride and with envy, oh Aurora, that one went far, that one triumphed.

The village had for Aurora an admiration that was, at bottom, a way of not knowing her. They admired her for what she had done, for what she had climbed, for the name she had won, and that admiration, which seemed to know her, in truth kept her at a distance, because they admired the runner, the winner, the figure, and not the person, the Aurora within, whom no one saw because she did not show her and perhaps not even she herself knew her. Aurora was, in Véspera, much admired and little known, and there is a particular solitude in that, in being admired for a figure we are not within, in receiving the love of what we have done and not of what we are. Because the admiration for what one has done does not warm like the love for what one is; the admiration is cold, it is out there, on the pedestal, and the person, on the pedestal, is alone, admired and not loved, seen and not known. And Aurora, atop her pedestal of admiration, was one of the loneliest women in Véspera, because everyone admired her and almost no one knew her, and the admiration of many does not fill the solitude of one who is known by no one, rather it underlines it, because it shows that one is seen from outside and ignored within.

And she had triumphed, it was true. By the world's account, Aurora was a success, a life well lived, an example. She had a name, she had position, she had everything the village considered to be arriving in life. But there is an account the world does not make, which is the account of within, that of what a person feels, and by that account Aurora had arrived nowhere, because she had never stopped anywhere, never arrived, had always passed through everything running to the next. Each victory, in the instant she reached it, ceased to count, emptied itself, because Aurora's head was already on the next step, on the next goal, on what was still left to conquer. She won a thing and did not savour it, because she was already running to the next; she reached a summit and did not enjoy it, because she already saw, from there, the next summit, higher, to be conquered. And so Aurora lived, atop a life of successes, in a permanent dissatisfaction, in a hunger that no victory killed, because the hunger was not of victories, it was of a thing the victories promised and never gave: the sensation of having arrived, of being enough, of being able to stop.

There was a way the village had of speaking of Aurora that said everything, and that wounded her without anyone knowing. They said, when she passed, there goes Aurora, that one triumphed, that one arrived, and the arrived hurt her more than any other word, because it was exactly what she did not feel, and to hear others say that she had arrived, when within she had never arrived anywhere, was of a cruel irony. The village saw the height where Aurora was and called it arrival; Aurora, at the height, saw only what was still left to climb, and felt no arrival at all, only the tiredness of one who runs and the hunger of one who never sates. And that distance, between what the others saw — an arrived woman — and what she felt — a woman who never arrived —, was Aurora's solitude, that of one who is envied for what they have and cannot enjoy what they have, that of one who seems, from outside, to have everything, and within does not have the one thing they wanted, which was to be able to stop and feel that it was enough.

And there was a cruel thing in the way the world treated her, that fed the illness without knowing it: the world only saw her when she won. While she rose, while she conquered, the world looked at her, applauded her, gave her attention; and Aurora had learned, by that, that she only existed in the world's eyes while she won, that the attention, the recognition, the very being-seen, depended on going on climbing. And that was one more chain: because to stop was not only, in her head, to fail, it was also to disappear, to cease being seen, because the world ceased to look at one who ceased to win. And Aurora, who had learned as a child that she was only worth something if she produced, had found in the world the confirmation of that lesson, a world that only saw her while she won and that would ignore her if she stopped, and she ran, in part, so as not to disappear, to go on being seen, because to stop was to become invisible, and the invisibility frightened her almost as much as the failure. Only much later would Aurora discover that to be seen for what one wins is not to be truly seen, that it is an attention that is bought and that is lost, and that there was a way of being seen that did not depend on winning — that of being known, loved for what one is —, but that one she did not know, and so she clung to the only one she knew, that of being seen for winning, and ran so as not to disappear from the eyes of a world that looked only at those who climb.

The treadmill never stopped. That was how Aurora, later, would describe her life: a treadmill that was always moving, and she running on it, without ever arriving anywhere, because the treadmill always brought her more ground beneath her feet, one more goal, one more step, and however fast she ran she never reached the end, because the treadmill had no end. She ran, Aurora, and was exhausted from running, and did not stop, because to stop, in her head, was to fail, was to fall behind, was to cease being the first, and to be the first was all she knew how to be. And so she ran, even exhausted, even empty, even no longer knowing where to nor why, she ran because to stop was unthinkable, she ran because the running was she, was her identity, was what made her be Aurora, the first at everything.

There was a word Aurora never used, and it was the word enough, because for her nothing was ever enough, never, and the word did not fit in her vocabulary. The others said it is good, that is enough, no more; Aurora not, Aurora said it could be better, more is needed, not yet. Enough was, for her, a word of the weak, of people without ambition, of people who content themselves; and Aurora did not content herself, never, on principle, because to content oneself was to stop, and to stop was to fail. And so she lived in a world without enough, a world where nothing sufficed, where every conquest was at once insufficient before the next, and a world without enough is a world without rest, because rest comes from feeling that one has enough, and one who never feels they have enough never rests. Only much later would Aurora learn to say enough, and to feel it, and would discover that it was the most freeing word of the language, the word her whole life had lacked, the word that, said and felt, was the arrival itself: this is enough, I have enough, I am enough. But that word was, in the time of the running, so forbidden and so forgotten to her that Aurora did not even know it existed, and she lived without it, in a world of perpetual insufficiency, running after an enough she never let herself reach because she did not believe it existed.

And in running, she went on letting fall along the way everything that was not the running. She let fall the friendships, which had no time to grow because she was always departing for the next goal; she let fall the love, or the loves, which withered beside a woman who was always elsewhere, in her head, in the next conquest; she let fall the rest, the enjoyment, the small joys of life, because all of that were stops, and she did not stop. Aurora came to the height of her life of successes with her hands full of victories and her life empty of all the rest, alone, exhausted, surrounded by trophies and by no one, because she had traded everything that was not the running for the running, and the running, in the end, had not given her what it promised, had not given her the arrival, had only given her more running.

It must be said, so that Aurora be understood and her life not only envied, what it is to live so, within, because from outside it seems enviable and within it is a prison. To live so is never to rest, because rest is a stop and the stop is forbidden; it is never to celebrate, because celebration is a stop; it is never to be content, because contentment is the end of desire and the desire of the next step is the engine that cannot stop. To live so is to have always, beneath any joy, the shadow of what is lacking, the step to climb, the goal to reach, and that shadow robs all the joy, because there is no joy that survives the shadow of what is still lacking when what is lacking is infinite. Aurora lived in a dissatisfaction that no satisfaction cured, because scarcely had a satisfaction come than she already measured it against what was lacking, and what was lacking was always more than what she had, because the ruler of what was lacking had no end. And a life like that, within, is not the enviable life that it seems from outside; it is a race without a finish line, an effort without rest, a hunger without abundance, and Aurora, the most successful woman in Véspera, was, within, one of the unhappiest, caught in a prison she herself had built and that had the shape of a stairway with no top.

She was, when this story begins, a good fifty-some, perhaps sixty, a woman still at the height of her capacity, still rising, still winning; but it was a tired height, a winning without joy, because Aurora was beginning to feel, without yet admitting it, that there was something wrong, that all that climbing led her nowhere, that however much she won the arrival did not come. She was beginning to look back, at the road of victories that was her life, and to ask herself, with a tightening, where all that had taken her, and the answer, when she dared to see it, was frightening: nowhere, because she had never stopped anywhere, never arrived, always passed through everything running to the next. There was also, in that beginning of doubt, a new fatigue, a fatigue that was not of the body but of the soul, and that frightened Aurora because it was the first thing the running could not smother. Her whole life, when she grew tired, Aurora ran more, and the running smothered the tiredness, gave her a new aim, a new adrenaline, and the tiredness passed to the second plane. But she had come to a point where the running no longer smothered the tiredness, where to run more no longer gave any adrenaline, only more tiredness, and Aurora found herself, for the first time, without her usual tool, because the tool with which she had always fought the tiredness — to run more — no longer worked, it only worsened it. And it was that fatigue the running no longer smothered, that exhaustion of the soul that more running only worsened, the first clear sign that something had to change, that she could not go on, that the usual solution, to run more, had ceased to be a solution and had become the problem. Because there is a point, in the life of one who runs, when the running ceases to save from the tiredness and becomes the cause of it, and Aurora had come to that point, and it was there, without a tool, without the running that had always saved her, that she began to have to look for something else.

And it was that question, where has all this taken me, that began, slowly, to open in Aurora a chink through which there would enter, at last, the light that would show her what no one had told her: that the arrival she pursued her whole life was not at the top of any stair, and that she could run forever and never reach it, because the arrival was not a place, it was a thing she would have to learn to do, and that was called, simply, to stop.
