## 6. What I pursue is not mine

It was a slow work, this of Aurora distinguishing herself from the goals she pursued, because she and the goals were, in her head, the same thing: she was the first, she was the winner, she was her conquests, and to separate Aurora from the conquests was to separate a thing that seemed a single one. Who was Aurora without her victories? The question frightened her, because she suspected the answer was no one, that she was only her conquests and that, those taken away, nothing was left. And it took the Keeper, with the line of birth, to show her that there was an Aurora prior to the conquests, a girl who had existed before winning anything, and that that one, the Aurora of the line, was who she truly was, beneath the victories, and that the victories were things she had done, not things she was. Aurora had to learn to distinguish what she did from what she was, a distinction she had never made because she had always identified herself with what she did; and in making it, in understanding that she was a person and not a curriculum, that she was worth something for being and not for winning, she began to be able to let go of the goals without feeling that she let go of herself, because she understood that she was not the goals, that she was another thing, prior to and independent of them, a person, Aurora, who would exist even if she never won anything again.

Conceição's sentence worked in Aurora for weeks, and led her, slowly, to a discovery that changed the way she saw herself: that the arrival she had pursued her whole life, the summit where at last she could stop, had never been hers, it had been the father's, and that she had spent her life running after a thing that did not belong to her.

There was a scene, in that time, that crystallised the discovery. The two of them were shelling broad beans at Conceição's door, a slow task, of those that give time to talk, and Conceição, without lifting her eyes from the bowl, asked her a simple thing: oh Aurora, let me ask you a thing. If you could do tomorrow what you felt like, without having to prove anything to anyone, without it being to conquer anything, only for the pleasure, what would you do?

And Aurora was left with the bean half-shelled, in her hand, and did not answer. The sister waited, shelled one more, waited still. And Aurora found herself searching for an answer the way one searches for a thing in a house one has never entered. She did not know. Nothing occurred to her that she wanted to do only for the pleasure, without it being to reach something. Everything that came to her head was at once a means to a goal, and what was not a means to a goal was an emptiness, a room of the house with nothing inside. I do not know, she said at last, low, almost with shame. Conceição, I do not know. Nothing occurs to me. It is as if you asked me in a language I do not speak.

And Conceição set the bowl of beans on her lap, and looked at her sister with a pity that had nothing of superiority, and said slowly: there you are. You see, sister? I know at once. I, if they asked me, would say I would go to the river with the children, or I would plant the new rose bushes, or I would stay here at the door doing nothing in the sun. I have likings. You have no likings, you have goals. The running ate even your likings, Aurora. It did not eat only your rest, it ate the liking of wanting a thing only because. And that, sister, that is what hurts me most in you, more than the tiredness: it is to see you not knowing what it is you would want, if no one ordered you to want anything.

And it was that bean stopped in her hand, and that I do not know she had to say to her own sister, that showed Aurora, more than any reasoning, how much she had lost, how much the running had eaten even her capacity to have likings of her own. And she understood that to recover herself passed, also, through this: through relearning to want things only for the pleasure, to do something that led nowhere, and that that, the capacity to want without a goal, was the most atrophied of all, and the one she would most have to train.

She began with a simple question she had never asked herself: what do I want? Not what is the next step, not what is left to conquer, but what do I, Aurora, want, for myself, of my life? And she was left without an answer, because she had never asked herself that question, had never needed to, because the goal was always set, it was always the next step, and she only had to run to it. She had never chosen her goals; she had received them, one by one, from the stairway set before her as a child, the stairway of the father's and now?, and had climbed them all without ever asking whether they were hers, whether she wanted them, whether they took her to a place she herself had chosen. And in asking herself at last the question — what do I want? — Aurora discovered, with a fright, that she did not know, that she had never known, that her whole life of victories had been a life of goals she had not chosen, of a race whose direction had been given to her and that she had never questioned.

And she understood then whose the goals she had pursued were: they were the father's. Firmino's and now?, Firmino's wounded ambition, Firmino's unrealised dream of rising — it had been that Aurora had pursued her whole life, without knowing it, running to satisfy a hunger that was not hers, it was the father's, a hunger she had inherited as one inherits a debt, and that she had carried and tried to pay off her whole life without ever managing it, because one does not pay off another's hunger, however much one runs. And the question of the register, the question of the shelf, the one Aurora had never heard but that governed her life — what am I pursuing that is not mine? —, had, for her, a clear answer: she pursued the father's arrival, the father's rising, the father's enough, a goal that was his and not hers, and so, even reaching victory after victory, she never arrived, because the victories she reached were answers to another's hunger, and her own hunger, Aurora's, was left unsated, because she did not even know what it was.

She tried, in that time, to do a thing she had never done: to imagine her life if the goals had been hers. And she asked herself, what would I have wanted, if I had not inherited my father's hunger? What would Aurora, free of the and now?, have chosen for her life? And she discovered, with sadness, that she did not know, that it was too late to know, that she had never had room to discover her own desires because the whole room had been taken up by the father's. Perhaps she would have wanted the same things, perhaps not; but the point, she understood, was not that, the point was that she had never chosen, that she had lived a whole life without ever choosing what she pursued, receiving the goals ready-made and running to them, and that a life without choice, however successful, is a life that one does not get to live as one's own, it is a life lived in another's name, and that that was, at bottom, her greatest poverty, that of never having been the owner of her own goals, that of having given her life to pursuing what she had not even chosen to want.

It was a painful discovery, because it called into question the meaning of her whole life. If the goals she had pursued were not hers, then all her running, all her rising, all her successes, had been in the service of a thing that was not her, of a hunger that was not hers; and Aurora, who had prided herself her whole life on being the first, on having risen, had to face the possibility that all that rising was, at bottom, an obedience, the obedience of a daughter to a father's ambition, and not the realisation of a will of her own, because a will of her own she had never had room to have, always busy fulfilling the father's. And there was an anger in this, when Aurora let it come: the anger of understanding that she had given her life to a race she had not chosen, that she had worn herself out climbing a stairway that was not hers, that she had never lived her own life because she had lived the realisation of another's dream.

The anger, when it came, frightened Aurora, because it was anger at the father, and she had loved the father, and the father had died, and to be angry at a dead and loved father seemed to her a betrayal. But the anger was there, and it was just, the anger of one who understands that they have been robbed of their own life, that they were set running a race that was not theirs, that they were never let stop, that a hunger that was not theirs was put inside them and consumed them. And Aurora had to learn, with difficulty, that to be angry at the father did not cancel the love she had for him, that one could love and be angry at the same time, that to acknowledge the harm the father had done her was not to betray him, it was simply to see the truth, and that to see the truth was necessary to free oneself of it. She let herself, therefore, have the anger, instead of pushing it down as she had pushed everything down her whole life; she let herself feel the injustice of what the father had done her, without ceasing to love him, and it was that permitted anger, that faced truth, that began to loosen her from the father, because what is not acknowledged is not let go, and Aurora needed to acknowledge the anger to be able, at last, to let go of the running the father had put on her back.

But beneath the anger there was a liberation, and Aurora went on feeling it, slowly. Because if the goals she pursued were not hers, then she could let them go; if the stairway she climbed was the father's, then she could come down from it, or leave it, and look for her own; if the arrival that never came was the father's, then perhaps there was an arrival of hers, one of her own, that she could reach, not by climbing more, but by stopping, and asking herself, at last, what it was she wanted, and going after it, or discovering that what she wanted was not to climb more, it was to arrive, to stop, to be, to live. There was a difference, Aurora understood in that time, between an ambition born from within and one imposed from outside, and hers had always been imposed from outside, though she felt it as hers. An ambition born from within has an end, a point of satisfaction, because it comes from a true desire that, satisfied, rests; an ambition imposed from outside has no end, because it does not come from a true desire, it comes from another's demand that is never satisfied, and so one who pursues an imposed ambition runs without ever arriving, because there is no possible satisfaction of a desire that is not theirs. Aurora understood that her inability to arrive was not a defect of hers, it was the natural consequence of pursuing a goal that was not hers: others' goals never sate us, however much we reach them, because the hunger they try to kill is not ours. And it was freeing to understand this, because it took from Aurora the guilt of never arriving — she did not arrive not from being insatiable by nature, but from pursuing what was not hers — and it opened to her the chance of, pursuing at last what was hers, or stopping pursuing, finding the satiety that others' goals would never give her.

And it was that liberation, that of understanding that she could let go of a race that was not hers, that began to open in Aurora the possibility of a different life, a life in which she chose her goals, or in which she discovered that her goal was no goal at all, it was simply to arrive at herself, at her life, at the contentment the sister had and she did not, and that was not at the top of any stair, it was in stopping climbing stairs that were not hers.

There was a question that summed it all up, and Aurora asked it, at last, of herself, in that time: if no one saw me, if no one knew, if there were no one to impress nor any and now? to satisfy, what would I do with my life? And the question disarmed her, because she understood that she had never done anything only for herself, far from the eyes of others, far from the goal, far from the approval; that everything she had done had been, in some way, to show, to rise, to answer the and now?, and that if they took from her the eyes of others and the stairway of goals she would not know what to do, because she had never done anything only for the pleasure, only for herself, only because. And that question — what would I do if no one saw? — became, in that time, a compass, because it pointed her to what was truly hers, to the desires beneath the goals, to the Aurora who existed beneath the one who won; and Aurora began to ask it of each thing she did: am I doing this for myself or to show? am I wanting this because I want it or because it is the next step? And she went on distinguishing, slowly, what was hers from what was inherited, what she wanted from what they had set her to want, and in that distinction she began to find, at last, herself, beneath a whole life lived for the eyes of others and for the father's and now?

She could not, on understanding this, change at once, because between understanding and changing there is an abyss, and the running was in her bones, and the father's voice, the and now?, went on sounding within her even after she understood that it was not hers. There was a fierce resistance, within Aurora, to this discovery, and the resistance had the father's voice. Each time she thought of letting go of the running, of stopping, of choosing her own goals, the father's voice within her protested: are you going to give up? are you going to fall behind? are you going to be like those who are nothing? And the voice was strong, it was the voice of a whole life, and Aurora had to stand up to it, to contest it, to answer it that no, that to stop was not to give up, that to choose her goals was not to fall behind, that there was more life in arriving than in running. But the father's voice did not fall silent easily, because it was ingrained, it was almost her own voice, and Aurora understood that to free herself was not a single act, it was a daily combat against a voice that had decades of strength, and that she was going to have to wage that combat many times, perhaps for the rest of her life, answering the father's and now? with her own enough, time after time, until the enough gained strength and the and now? lost it. It was an unequal combat, at first, because the father's voice had decades and Aurora's enough was newborn, weak; but Aurora went on feeding her enough, giving it reason, and the father's voice went on losing strength as she ceased to obey it, because the voices within, like everything, weaken when one ceases to heed them and to obey them.

But the discovery was made, and it was great: Aurora could no longer believe, as she had believed her whole life, that the arrival was on the next step; she began to suspect that she had been pursuing it in the wrong place, in the wrong direction, after the wrong thing, and that the arrival she sought, if it existed, was not ahead, in what was left to climb, it was there, where she already was, awaiting only that she stop running enough to see it.
