## 11. The new entry

Saudade died at the end of May, with the poppies of the new bed open, and she died as she had lived the last while: slowly, for a while, until she should be right again.

There was no fall nor fright. There was a going-out of a lamp that lacks oil, day by day a nothing less, and the house, which had learned to be a house of three, went on learning without words to take its leave. The mother left first the table, then the window chair, then everything that was not the bed and the window seen from the bed, which Leal pulled from the wall without anyone asking him, so that she would have the square, as the other bed had been done, fifty-one years ago, in the house of the back street. Eufémia noticed that rhyme of the gestures and said nothing, and he too did not, and it was as though the family, at the third attempt, managed at last to do a thing like that in the light of day.

The village accompanied as the village accompanied: by invisible turns. More broth appeared at the door than three houses ate, Benvinda sent the rosemary without a message, and the old women of the square, those who still climbed stairs, came in turn to keep company an hour, sitting upright beside the bed with their sewing, scarcely speaking, for it was not to speak that one came, it was so that the house would not be alone with that. The doctor from away came twice, slow in her words as in her gestures, said what there was to say, which was little and was mild: the age, the tired heart, no suffering that could be seen, to leave her at her own pace. And on the good days, of which there still were some, tiny ones, the size of half a morning, Saudade wanted all the shutters open and asked after the useless flowers, and Eufémia brought her poppies in a glass of water, and the old woman was a long time looking at them with her eyes already far off, and said that her mother would scold, and laughed with a remnant of laughter, and it was the last thing resembling a conversation they had.

They divided the nights without ever dividing them by words. Eufémia made the first part, with the sewing; Leal appeared by midnight, in a jersey over his pyjamas, said go and lie down two hours, and sat down himself, and the house grew used to that changing of the guard as it had grown used to everything. One of those nights, Eufémia woke thirsty and came down, and stopped at the door of the back room without going in: inside, in the half-light, her husband was sitting beside the mother-in-law's bed talking softly. The words could not be made out, nor were they needed: it was the tone of one who tells a long and old thing, without hurry, to someone who sleeps. Eufémia went back up without making a noise and never asked him what he had been telling, nor to whom, whether to the old woman, to the other, or to himself. There are conversations that are of the one who has them, she had learned it that year, and each one of the house had now their own, with their dead and their almost, and the house bore them all, for that is what houses are for.

At the end, there stayed what stays: the nights of her own. Eufémia slept the last ones in the back room, on a pallet beside the bed, as one sleeps beside the sick and the children, and on the dawn when the mother went she was awake, by chance or by what seems chance, and noticed the change of the breathing and lit the candle and called Leal softly, and the two were with her the whole hour it took, each on their side of the bed, each with one of the small hands in theirs. Saudade neither woke nor suffered. Once, near the end, she furrowed her brow as one who searches, and Eufémia came near and said in her ear the two things she had kept for that hour, for weeks, written and learned by heart like the words of the east: that the pillowcases had come out the most beautiful she had seen in all her life of handling linen; and that she should go in peace, that she stayed, and stayed by choice. The brow unfurrowed slowly. Whether she heard, no one can swear. But Eufémia saw her compose herself, like one who orders their patience within for the last time, and depart composed, and that, coming from that woman, was an answer.

The burial was made on a Wednesday morning, with good weather, and people came from both banks.

The Keeper was there, as she is at everything, in her usual place, which is neither in front nor behind: at the side, at the distance of one who records. All the old women of the square came, those who could, and the little ones of the school came with their mothers, washed and combed, because the village takes the children to the burials of the houses it esteems, for them to learn early that this too is part of it. People came whom Eufémia did not expect: the miller's daughter with her new husband, the president's wife with no signs in her talk, Benvinda with the shop shut, in the old mourning of one who has shut the shop many times. And in front of everyone, on the cobbles of the back street, at the door of the old house by which the coffin came out, someone had put straw. Eufémia saw it in passing, and knew who had ordered it put there, because only one person in that village kept things instead of carrying them, and her eyes sought the Keeper over the people, and the Keeper held her gaze a moment, with no nod at all, and that was all, and it was the largest wreath of the whole burial.

Eufémia went behind the coffin on Leal's arm, and wept. She wept without shame and without astonishment, with the new trade of weeping learned on the slope, and the village saw Saudade's daughter weep her mother over the grave, in the light of day, and found it well, and two or three old women wept with her an old weeping, delayed, that perhaps was no longer only for that death. The earth was closed beside Bendito's grave, three rows below the other Eufémia, who that day had nearly the whole family on her slope in the sun, and the jonquils had ended long ago, and they were now the lilacs, and they would be the small June roses, for the calendar of the flowers, in that house, did not fail.

And after the burial, when the village came down and life took up its pace, there came the day that gives this chapter its name: the day of the entry.

Eufémia went to the House of the Register on a June morning, alone, with the papers of the death in the folder. The Keeper received her at the long table at the back, checked everything in her key-writing, and then did what Eufémia had come to see without knowing she came for it: she opened the great book of the deaths of the time of now, at the place where it went, set the ruler, wet the pen, and wrote the mother's entry.

And Eufémia watched, standing, on the other side of the table, the whole office.

She saw the Keeper write the name first, *Saudade*, alone, with space around it, because the name, she explained without raising the pen, is the beam of the entry and is set first, for the rest hangs from it. She saw her add, slowly, abbreviating nothing: daughter of whom, widow of whom, mother of whom. And here the pen stopped, and the Keeper raised her eyes, and asked, with her tremendous naturalness:

"Mother of whom, exactly? Tell me. You are the one who knows."

And Eufémia understood the whole question, because it came from a woman who had the two books on the middle shelf and knew exactly what she was asking. One could enter mother of Eufémia, and it was right, and it was the custom: the old dead are not repeated in the new entries. Or one could enter it whole. Eufémia looked at the open book, at the mother's name still fresh, with space around it, and said, slowly, dictating as one who teaches the first letters:

"Mother of Eufémia, who died at nineteen years and is on the eastern slope. And mother of Eufémia, who am I. In this order, which was their order."

The Keeper wrote. She wrote everything, word by word, in the order asked, and the name repeated itself in the entry as it had repeated itself in life, but now with the dates beside it, each with her own, the dead one with her death, the living one with her life, separate and together, sisters at last in writing. And when she finished, and dried the ink, and shut the book, she said, putting away the pen:

"Names need this, you know? Someone to write them whole and someone to say them aloud. It is my whole office, and it takes a lifetime to learn: to write whole, to say aloud. The rest are shelves."

Eufémia went back home by the square, and that afternoon, with Leal at the serra, she did the last tidying of the estate, which she had put off in order to be alone.

It was little and it was everything. The mother's clothes, washed and folded, went to those who needed them, by Benvinda's hand, who knew the village's needs without humiliating anyone. Bendito's portrait stayed on the corridor's chest, in its usual place, for there are things that are right where they are. And the tin box, Eufémia brought it to the kitchen table and opened it for the last time with ceremony, because from then on, she decided, that box would not have any ceremony again.

The sister's portrait she put a frame to, bought at the fair, simple, of pale wood, and set it in the parlour, on the middle shelf, between the portrait of her and Leal's wedding and a glass with poppies. The girl of the plait was left looking at the house that was to have been hers, from inside the frame, serious for the camera, and there was in that no grief at all, Eufémia verified on stepping back two paces to see her: there was a family, finally complete, with its dead in sight as is proper.

The wooden brush stayed. It was the brush with which the mother combed her as a girl, by the window, the brush that had been someone's, and Eufémia knew now, without needing to ask anyone, whose it had been. She took it up, passed her thumb over the bristles worn on the left side, the side of a thick plait, and was a moment with it in her hand, deciding. It could go to the village above, beside the rest. It could go to the buttons. It stayed in the room, on her chest of drawers, beside the comb of pale wood, and Eufémia went on using it every morning, in the usual plait, and the difference, which was not seen from outside, was the whole of it: until then she had used it because so; now she used it because so, but the because so was hers, chosen, with the two sisters and the mother inside it, in sight, as is proper.

The brown notebook stayed aside, on top of the chest, awaiting its place, which she already knew which it was, but that was a matter for another going-up to the village above.

And the letter she gave to the one who had written it.

She set it before him of an evening on the table, beside the small things, without a speech: take it, it is yours, it was lent to you fifty years. Leal wiped his hands on his knee before taking it up, as he did with things just varnished, and unfolded it, and read it from beginning to end, without hurry, by the light of the lamp, the letter of the lad he had been to the girl who did not arrive. Eufémia did not look at him while he read: she put away the sewing, wound the clock, left him his time. When he finished, he folded it by the old fold, was a moment with it in his hands, and said, with a voice that came from very far, half astonished, half tenderly moved, the voice of a man of seventy-two shaking the hand of the one of twenty-one:

"What a lad so sure of the world." He shook his head slowly. "Poor thing. He knew nothing."

"He knew how to choose windows," said Eufémia.

And Leal laughed, the dry laugh of the second year of the house, and got up, and went to put the letter in the small drawer of the workshop bench, where he kept the papers that were only his, and the matter was tidied away so, between the two, forever, with one phrase from each and nothing more, which was the right measure of that house.

The tin box, empty, stayed on top of the corridor's chest, uncovered, and Eufémia came to use it for the buttons, and there is no more complete way of unhaunting a relic than to fill it with buttons.

And on a June night, with the house of two again, and lighter and stranger for it, they had supper late, with the yard door open to the smell of the poppies and of the watered earth. Eufémia had gone the whole day with a question turning, since the morning of the entry: the Keeper's phrase, to say aloud, to write whole, had stayed working in her like leaven, and on setting the soup on the table she understood that the question was risen, ready, and that it would be there, at that table of press wood, before that man, and no one else.

"Leal," she said. "The question you asked me that night. Whether I had married you or come in the name. You asked me for a proper answer, and I asked to think. I am still thinking, do not be frightened. But I reached the middle, and the middle I want to tell you now, aloud, for that is how I learned this year that things are said." She sat, facing him, with her hands laid on the table as he laid his. "Mother is gone, the old house will be tidied, my sister has her name said and her grave with flowers. Everything is in its place, for the first time in my life. And I found myself today thinking: now I could. Now I could go away, if I wanted. No one holds me, I owe nothing now, the world is wide and I know how to read." She looked at him, straight, with her eyes dry and clear. "And it is for that that I tell you the middle of the answer, and the middle is this: if I stay, Leal, I no longer stay because it is arranged. That is over. It ended in this house this year. If I stay..." — and her voice did not tremble, it settled, as taught hands settle — "...I stay why? That is the question I lack. It is only that. And no one can answer it for me, not even you. Above all you."

Leal heard everything without moving, with his spoon forgotten beside his plate. And what he answered was the hardest and cleanest thing he answered in his life, and he said it looking at her, slowly, without blinking:

"No indeed. I do not answer it for you." And he took up the spoon, and before bringing it to his mouth added, low, with all the weight and all the lightness at the same time: "But know this, for your sums: I, if you go, will go on guarding your windows. And if you stay, I want you to stay as you say. For whatever reason. So long as the reason be yours."

Outside, in the yard, the poppies were shut for the night, awaiting the sun, as is their way, for they keep nothing from one day to the next and open again every morning on their own account. Eufémia ate the soup slowly. The answer that was missing did not come that night, and she did not force it: she had learned, in her apprentice year, that the true answers are like the old water beneath the square, that are not taken by force, one waits for them to remember the way. But she knew already one thing, and knew it there, with the spoon in her hand, in the kitchen of the house facing the fountain: the answer, when it came, came in the new writing. Crooked, alive, masterless. Hers.
