## 5. The letter

She opened it on a Wednesday in February, in the morning, alone in the house, and afterward she thought many times that she had chosen the hour badly, because there are things one should read at night, when the world is shut and no one can see one's face. But the truth is that she chose nothing: she had been circling the letter for weeks as one circles a wasps' nest, and that morning Leal went to the serra to help with a cousin's roof, and the mother slept the long sleep of the old in winter, and the house was suddenly so still, so hers, that her feet took her to the wardrobe before her head gave leave.

The morning had begun like all of them: the bread in the bread's place, the apron on the nail, Leal preparing his tools for the serra with half his thought already on the cousin's roof. Eufémia gave him the meal made and heard him recommend the fire and the mother-in-law, and at the door he stopped, as he always stopped when he was going to be away the whole day, and said what he always said: anything, send word by Benvinda's boy. It was the farewell phrase of that marriage, said a thousand times, and a thousand times she had answered go in peace. She answered go in peace. And she stayed at the window watching him cross the square with his tools on his shoulder, straight, without hurry, the most predictable man in Véspera, and she thought, with the letter already calling from up there: you sent me word by Benvinda's boy and you wrote the other two pages on both sides. The injustice of the thought tasted of vinegar to her, and she made it all the same, for the unjust thoughts too are part of a person waking.

She gave the mother her breakfast, slowly, by the spoon on the bad days, and that one was in-between. She sat her afterward in the window chair with the blanket and the sun, and the old woman was kept busy with the square, which was now her work of every morning: to watch the village pass, to take note of it for no one. Only then, with the whole house at its posts, did Eufémia go up.

She took the box from behind the linen. She sat with it at the kitchen table, in her place, and set the letter before her, on the scrubbed wood. *For Eufémia, by hand.* The young man's writing, laboured, with the E larger than it should be, as though whoever wrote it had put into that E more strength than the ink asked. By hand. The hand for which the letter had been written had been in the earth for fifty years. The hand that was going to open it trembled a little, and Eufémia looked at it with reproof, as she looked at the little ones who laughed in the row, and the hand obeyed like them: it composed itself, and did not correct.

She had circled the letter, in those weeks, with all the arguments of the two directions. Do not open it, said one voice, which was like the mother's: the letter is not yours, it was written for another, to read it is to open the window of another's room. Open it, said the other voice, which was new and did not yet resemble anyone: another's how, if the room is yours, if the house is this one, if the man sleeps on your side? Everything that is on that paper was your life, only promised to another: you have more right to it than to a will. And between the two voices Eufémia had gone for weeks, from one side to the other, as one goes in the corridors of houses with the sick, and what at last decided was neither of the voices: it was the morning. The still morning, her house, the hands with a will. There are decisions that are not taken, she knew it there: they ripen in the dark, like the late fruits, and one day they fall on their own, and one need only be beneath.

The paper crackled in unfolding, dry as a corn husk. They were two small pages, written on both sides, without a correction, which meant that they had been copied out fair, perhaps more than once. Eufémia knew that: the letters that matter are written first in draft, on the knee, on the workshop bench, and then copied with the tongue between the teeth, and what reaches the one who reads them is the version with no extra courage, only the right courage.

She read it slowly, from beginning to end, without stopping, as one reads a sentence.

It had no flourishes. It was the letter of a serious lad, and the serious lads of that time wrote as they set tile. But within the seriousness, line by line, went a whole life being promised, with dates and measures, and it was that that made it a terrible thing to read fifty years later, sitting in the kitchen where all the promises had been kept:

*The house will be ready for the autumn, if the weather lets it. The roof is done, and from the front windows one sees the fountain and the whole square, I chose them so on purpose, so that you would have somewhere to watch the village from when you are studying, for I know you like to watch the people pass.*

Eufémia raised her eyes to the kitchen window, above the sink, the window from which she had seen the square every day of her married life, and stayed a moment unable to go back to the paper.

*I will make you a big table, of wood from the old press, for your father promised me the plank. A table where your books and my suppers will fit, and those who come, if they come, and they will come.*

The table was beneath her elbows. It had sixty years of cloths passed over it and the marks of all the dishes, and it was, it had always been, the best table in the street, praised by the neighbours, the table where she corrected the little ones' notebooks and copied out the minutes fair. Your books and my suppers. Those who come had not come, neither for one nor for the other, and Eufémia discovered that it was possible to be jealous of a dead woman and to pity her at exactly the same time, in the same swallow.

*Do not be in a hurry with the course, nor heed what is said. I wait. I am not a man to wait little.*

There she had to get up. She went to the fire, stirred what did not need it, came back. I am not a man to wait little. No, he was not. He had waited the course, the City, the return. Then he had waited the mourning. Then he had waited twenty-four years more, until a girl with the right name was of an age to marry, and the village called that loyalty and had put the name on the man as one nails a plaque on a house. Eufémia had always said her husband's name without hearing it, as one says names. That morning she heard it whole, for the first time, and the name frightened her.

There was also, in the midst of the measured promises, a bit of a lad loose, and it was the bit that cost her most to read, because it was the most alive:

*The master says I am spoiling good wood with a betrothed's hurry, and he is right, I spoiled a plank from the press yesterday for having my head on the western window, arguing with myself whether to make it open in the middle or like a guillotine. The open-in-the-middle won. You will tell me if I did right, when you open it yourself.*

The western window was that of their room. It opened in the middle, in two leaves, and creaked on one side, and Leal adjusted its hinge every autumn for twenty-five years, and Eufémia had opened it every morning of her married life without knowing that she was answering a question asked of another woman, in a letter, before she was born. You did right, she said within, and was frightened, because she did not know to which of the two times of the house she had said it.

The rest of the letter was little: two more words about the work, and the farewell, which had no love written in it and was all of him: *Guard yourself from the cold, for you do not guard yourself. When you come, all is ready.* And beneath it, instead of the whole name, only the initial, that solitary and upright L, and Eufémia, who knew the letters as she knew faces, saw that the L had been made slowly, with the pen lifted twice, and that it was, in the whole letter, the only thing resembling a kiss.

She folded the pages by the old fold. The paper obeyed at once: fifty years of learning that fold, it knew it by heart.

And then she did what she should not have, or what she should, she never quite decided: instead of returning the letter to the box, she put it in the deep pocket of the apron, and went to live the day with it upon her body.

Before going out, she went to see to the mother, and the mother, from the window chair, looked at her face with the sudden attention she now gave to the wrong things, and said: you have wept. She had not wept. She had read, which was not yet the same thing, and she said it was from the wind, and the old woman accepted the wind as she accepted everything that winter, and went back to the square. But Eufémia was left with the question, going down the stairs: what more would that undone head be able to see, that the whole heads of the house had never seen? The mother's illness took the days and the dates, but what remained beneath, free of the schedules, seemed sometimes to see better: it saw weepings that had not yet been wept, people who were to come, girls at the window. It was as though the memory, in coming undone, left in view the oldest apparatus, the one from before the names, the one that knows things without knowing how it knows.

It was a common day, and never again did any common day seem to her the same thing. She went to the school and taught the numbers up to ten with another woman's love letter in her pocket. The little ones noticed nothing: the little ones never notice anything and notice everything, and the little one of the Serranos, the left-handed one, looked at her twice that morning with the narrow eyes of one who distrusts a sum. At the long break she came home, gave the mother her lunch, and the mother, on one of her in-between days, told for the third time the story of the kerchief carried off by the river, word for word as the other times, and that time Eufémia heard it with her heart leaping, awaiting the he of the long legs, and when the he came she pressed without meaning to the pocket of the apron, and the letter crackled softly, and the mother did not hear, and the world did not fall.

At the school's end, at midday, Benvinda made her stop at the door of the Shop, with a parcel in her hand: the sugar Eufémia had ordered and forgotten, because she was forgetting orders, she who had never forgotten anything. Benvinda handed it to her and kept it held a second more, looking at her with the counter eyes, which are the ones that see most in this life.

"You are sleeping badly," she said. It was not a question, in the manner of the land.

"I am with the house full, it is from that."

"That it is." Benvinda let go of the parcel slowly. "My mother used to say that full houses weigh less than full drawers. See what she meant by that, for I never knew."

And she went back to the counter, leaving Eufémia on the step with the sugar and the phrase, and the phrase weighed in the pocket of her apron the whole way, leaning against the letter, the two of them conversing with each other as though they had known each other forever.

In the afternoon, in the square, she crossed paths with the Keeper.

It was a crossing of nothing, by the fountain: the Keeper went with her papers under her arm, greeted her with her head, as always, and was going to pass. And Eufémia, who had decided nothing, found herself stopped, and heard herself say:

"One of these days I am going there. To the House. To see to some entries."

"The House is where it has always been," said the Keeper.

And she went off, without asking what entries, nor of whom, nor what for, and Eufémia stayed by the dry fountain with the strange sensation that she had just made an appointment for an illness she had not yet told anyone, not even herself, and that the doctor already knew.

Leal came back from the serra at dusk, smelling of resin and of cold, pleased with the day. The three of them had supper, for the mother wanted to come to the table, and it was a good supper, with the old woman ruling the soup and Leal telling of the cousin and the roof, and Eufémia laughing in the right places, with the letter in the pocket of the apron, hung on the door's nail, three steps from the table the letter promised.

It was on going to bed, with the house already in the dark, that he asked. Had he seen the box out of its place? Had he felt her the whole day in a place beside her own self? It was never known. Leal asked little and saw much.

"You have something on you," he said, in the dark, without reproach. It was an open door, in his way: whoever wished came in.

And Eufémia was a moment at the door. She saw inside the whole conversation: the box, the portrait, the notebook, the letter, the windows chosen on purpose, the table of press wood, the L of the pen lifted. She saw her husband listening to it all, with his hands still, and saw that he bore it, that he was a man for that and for more. It was not for him that she drew back.

"It is nothing," she said. "They are the mother's papers. Things of the old house, divisions, those things. I will see to it."

The lie came out round, even, sure as the hours, in her best writing. It was the first she had told him in twenty-five years of marriage, and Eufémia stayed awake long after he fell asleep, looking at the ceiling, learning a new and ugly thing about herself: that she lied well. That she lied with the natural knack of one who after all had always known, like one who discovers at fifty that they can swim, by falling into the river.

Before falling asleep, she got up without noise, took the letter from the apron and returned it to the box, unopened anew: the folds in their place, the L downward, the tin lid with its tired click. But to return it served no purpose now, and she knew it. There are letters that can be shut again; read, they cannot be put back unread. And that one, besides, did not fit in the box, because the box was small and the letter was the size of the house: Eufémia put out the light and lay down inside the letter, between the walls of the letter, beneath the roof of the letter, beside the man who had written it for another, and above her, in the dark, the windows chosen on purpose looked onto the square, awaiting the morning, to show again the fountain to one who was no longer there to see it.
