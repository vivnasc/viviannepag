## 10. Her own writing

To choose, Eufémia discovered that spring, was neither a gift nor a right: it was a trade. And like all trades, it had to be learned from the beginning, with small works, borrowed tools and much spoiled in the first weeks.

She began with the smallest thing she found: the word. There is a word that those who choose use at every hour and that she, she realised, never used, because it had never been needed: let me think. All her life she had answered at once. She answered at once because she knew at once, and she knew at once because the answers were all given beforehand, set on the table beside the bread, and her mouth was only the door by which they came out into the world. When the council president's wife came to ask her, as every year, for the signs for the spring festival, the same as last year's, which always looked so well, Eufémia opened her mouth to say the usual yes, and shut it, and said, with her heart beating as though she were robbing the Shop:

"Let me think. I will tell you tomorrow."

The president's wife was so astonished that she said of course, that she should think, and went off looking back. And Eufémia was left the rest of the day with that throbbing in her, astonished at how much it cost and how little it was, and the next day she said yes, that she would make the signs, but in another way, simpler, that she had a wish to try a new writing. And she made them. And the village found the signs different, and some liked them and others did not, and the world, again, did not fall.

The new signs had their small war, and the small war was a school. The president's wife found them too modern, and said so in the square, with that way of one who asks everyone whether it is not true; and one of the old women answered that a sign was a thing one read and that was that; and Benvinda hung hers in the Shop, high up, in plain sight, which was the Shop's way of voting. Eufémia learned of it all, as one learns everything, and bore three days of itching in the wish to go and explain herself, to mend, to promise that next year she would make the old ones. She did not go. She learned in those three days a rule of the new trade that no notebook teaches: whoever chooses, pays in others' talk. It was a price. It was paid. And by the fourth day no one spoke of the signs any more, because the village had passed to the mill plot and the marriage of the miller's daughter, and Eufémia understood, with astonishment hovering near laughter, that the bonfire of the village's opinion, seen from inside for the first time, was after all a small thing, that goes out on its own if no one blows on it.

The new writing was the second front of the trade, and that one was the deepest and the most secret.

It had begun by chance, or by what seems chance to one who is learning to see: on a Wednesday, alone in the school room after the little ones left, Eufémia took a draft paper and tried to write her own name with her left hand. A scrawl came out. She laughed alone, and was about to crumple the paper when she stopped: the scrawl was ugly, was crooked, was impossible, but it was new. It came from no notebook. It had no master. She stayed looking at it as one looks at a living thing, and from that day, every afternoon, in the empty room, she gave herself ten minutes of lesson: not of the left hand, for that had been only the door, but of the right hand freed, in search of a writing that was hers. She tried the t's taller, the e's narrower, the capital E of another family. She erred. She came back. The hand resisted as beaten paths resist one who wants to leave them, it always pulled toward the round, toward the even, toward the sure as the hours, and Eufémia scolded it without anger and brought it again off the track. She showed that to no one. It was, she understood it without knowing how to say it, the most naked thing she had done in her life: a woman of fifty, shut in a school room, learning to write.

At home, the new trade gave more visible fruits, and the first was a piece of furniture.

The corridor's chest of drawers had been in the same place since Eufémia had come into that house as a bride: to the left of the door, beneath the mirror. It was there because so, because so they had put it, because so it looked well. One April morning, Eufémia dragged it alone to the back wall, by the window, where it caught the afternoon light, and hung the mirror on the other side. Leal, on arriving, stopped in the corridor like one who comes into the wrong house, looked at the chest, looked at her, and Eufémia prepared herself within to defend the change tooth and nail, with reasons of light and of arrangement that she had rehearsed. But he only said:

"I always thought it looked bad there." And he hung up his coat, and added, already with his back turned, with the dry knack that was only heard well in the second year of the house: "It took you twenty-five years to notice. Next week you change me the whole room, if you like."

And in the yard, beside the rosemary the mother had ordered planted, Eufémia opened that April a new bed and sowed it with poppies and other flowers of no proper name, bought in paper packets at the fair, flowers of no use at all, that seasoned no pot nor cured any ailment nor served for anything except being beautiful in May. The mother, on a good day, was taken by the arm to see the bed and stayed a long time looking, with her lip trembling lightly, and Eufémia awaited the sentence, a house without governance, wasted earth. But the old woman said only, softly, with a strange admiration, from another bank:

"My mother never let us have flowers. She said that earth that gives a flower is a mouth that eats and does not work." And she pressed her daughter's arm, and said the phrase contrary to all the phrases of her life: "Let them come. Do not pull them up."

And there was the purchase. At the May fair, among the usual stalls, Eufémia stopped at the cloth one and saw a shawl. She did not need a shawl: she had hers, the one of a whole life, the one that waited for her on the nail, a gift from she knew not whom. This one was of fine wool, of a deep blue with a band of lighter blue, a blue not worn in Véspera, where the shawls of grown women were brown, grey, the colour of not being noticed. It stayed in her hands without her noticing the gesture. It weighed what good wool weighs and cost what it cost, and Eufémia had her money counted in her pocket for the things on the list, and the shawl was on no list of her life. She bought it. She bought it with her heart leaping from the robbed Shop, wrapped in brown paper, and brought it home almost hidden, and on taking it from the paper before the mirror she felt a ridiculous and warm shame, of a girl, and over the shame, stubborn, the absurd satisfaction of the apron strap, multiplied by a thousand. The village found the shawl too gay, of course, and said so by the usual means. Leal found in it another thing: you look more like yourself, he said, and neither of the two knew how to explain what that meant, and the two understood.

At the school, the harvest of the trade came by the hand of the little one of the Serranos.

It was at the end of a May morning. The little one, who was left-handed and stubborn and made her e's from the inside out, alive and crooked like vine shoots, finished her page and brought it to her, distrustful as always, awaiting the usual correction. And Eufémia took the page, and looked at all those e's, row after row, each with its own shape, unrepeatable, wrong according to the notebook and right according to some other thing she had been learning that whole year to see, and set the page down, and said:

"You know what? Leave them so."

"But you always say..."

"I know what I always say." Eufémia came near, and pointed to an e in the middle of the page, the most alive of all, the most crooked. "Can it be read? It can. Is it yours? It is. Well then. The writing has to be yours, little one. It has to be upright, it has to be clean, that is required. But yours. A borrowed writing" — and here her voice did a thing the little one did not understand and told her mother at lunch, it softened and grew harsh at the same time — "a borrowed writing is paid for dear, and is paid for a whole life."

And the little one of the Serranos went to her place understanding nothing, with the page uncorrected and the confused sensation of having won a thing, and from then on she made her e's as she wished, and became, years later, but that is no longer of this book, the woman who wrote what no one dared in Véspera, with a writing that was known among a thousand.

And then, with the trade already moving in her hand, Eufémia did the great work. She chose the night herself: a warm Friday, the mother asleep early, the evening only the two of them. She brought neither tea nor circling. She sat at the table of press wood facing her husband, waited for him to set down the small thing, and said:

"I read the letter, Leal."

The lamp made its little usual noise. Leal did not ask what letter. He stayed very still, with his hands laid on the table, and Eufémia went on, slowly, without letting go of him with her eyes:

"The one you wrote to my sister, with the house unfinished. It was in mother's chest, with the rest. I read it in February and I lied to you, and the lie is still here, between us, in the drawer where I put it. I come to take it from there." She breathed. "And I know about the east. I know about the flowers, I have known for months. I saw you with the watering can, hidden behind a cypress like a fool, and I kept silent. It is all I have kept silent, and I could go on keeping silent until I die, for to keep silent I was well taught. But this year I am learning another trade. So I ask you to your face, and I bear the answer that comes, whatever it be, because worse than any answer is the question rotting twenty-five years more beneath this house." And she made, at last, the question that crossed the book of her life from one end to the other, never said in these words, and said now in these very ones: "Whom did you marry, Leal? Me, or the promise?"

The room's clock struck some half hour. Leal looked at his own hands, open on the table, a long time. And when he spoke, he spoke as he set tile: slowly, piece by piece, without haste and without fault, and one could see that he was not inventing the answer, he was handing over an answer made years ago, waiting, like everything in that village, for someone to come and ask for it.

"I married the promise," he said. "I do not lie to you to spare myself, for that is no sparing at all. On the day of the yard, beside the rosemary, what I went to do was to keep my word. I had the house, I had the word given, I had you with her name, and the whole village pushing with that way the village has of pushing while standing still. I thought it was the right thing. I thought the heart was as they had told me, that it takes, like the vines." He raised his eyes then from his hands, and looked at her, and Eufémia saw that he was going now to the part that had never been said to anyone, not even perhaps to himself. "And it took. But hear me well, woman, for I only know how to say this once. It took where I did not expect. I expected that you would go on being her, and in the first times, forgive me, I looked for her in you: in the way, in the voice, in the plait. And you were alike, you were, in the drawing. But in the rest you were another, you were another in everything, and I went on noticing it slowly, year by year, as one notices the change of the seasons: you seasoned differently, you laughed at other things, you had patiences she did not have and readinesses she never had. And there was a day, I cannot tell you which, when I found myself thinking that I no longer remembered her voice. Just so, suddenly: I went to fetch it and it was not there. And I was frightened, and went to the east that Sunday to beg her pardon for it. And on the way back I understood that I came in a hurry. In a hurry for what, I asked myself, and the answer came down the slope with me: in a hurry to get home, to my home, where my wife was. You." His hands closed slowly one in the other, on the table. "The flowers, I take them to her because I owe it to her and because no one else was going to. That is mourning, and mourning is not taken from a man, nor does he want it taken. But married, Eufémia, married I am to you. For many years I have been only with you. If I did not tell you it was because to say this one had to speak of her, and to speak of her no one ever gave me leave in this land." And he added, lower, with the first harshness of the night, which was not against her: "You included."

The two of them stayed silent, with the lamp between them. And what Eufémia felt was not relief, or not only: it was the ground recomposing itself slowly beneath the house, pier by pier, and at the same time the notion, clear, that none of that answered the question that came next, the last, hers, which was not whom did you marry, but why do I stay. That one was no question for him. And he, who saw everything, saw that too:

"And you?" he asked, in his way, the open door, whoever wishes comes in. "Did you marry me, you? Or did you come in the name, as everything came?"

"I do not know," said Eufémia. And the word, said so, whole, at the table, was one of the greatest she said in her life. "I came in the name, that I did. Whether I stayed for more... I think so. But I want to answer you properly, and I do not yet know. Let me think." And for the first time in the night she smiled, a small and tired smile, of the two of them. "I am learning, Leal. To know things on my own account takes me time. I began late."

"You began when you could," he said.

And nothing more was decided that night, nor was it needed. They tidied the kitchen together, he washing, she putting away, in the usual choreography, which was suddenly no longer a beaten track: it was a thing one could choose, every night, and that perhaps he had been choosing for years, and that she was now going to learn to choose too, dish by dish, if that should be the answer. At the end, before putting out the lamp, Eufémia took from the pocket of her apron a folded paper and set it on the table, before him.

"What is this?"

"My name," she said. "Written in my writing. The new one. It is still crooked, and the E still flees from me to the old one. But I want you to be the first to see it."

And Leal unfolded the paper with both hands, with the care with which he lifted things just varnished, and read the woman's name written as no one had ever written it, in no writing of any book of the House of the Register, and stayed looking at it a long time, and what he said, when he said it, was in his way, dry and whole, and was all there was to say:

"This one I like better."
