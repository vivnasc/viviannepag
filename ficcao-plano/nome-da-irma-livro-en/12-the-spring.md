## 12. The spring

In Véspera it is said, of the women whose lives change late, that they are in their spring. It is said without mockery, or with the mild mockery the village reserves for what it respects: each one has their spring when it falls due, say the old women of the square, and there are those who have it in May and those who have it with the frost, and wretched is only the one who never has it. In that end of summer, a year after the mother's move, the village looked at the teacher of the first letters and said it of her, softly, without malice: Eufémia is in her spring. And for the first time in her life, what was said of Eufémia in the talk of the square was true before being a sentence.

It was in that end of summer that she went up to the east with words of her own.

She went alone, early in the morning, with the small August roses that Leal had left her cut by the door, without a word, in the new custom of the two: the flowers were now carried by whoever went, and the two went, sometimes together, sometimes each at their own hour, and the grave of the slope never again knew what it was to go a week without a visit or a conversation. The gate gave its creak. The village above was washed from the night, with the sun coming in low across the rows, and Eufémia climbed up to the eastern wall and changed the water and arranged the roses one by one, slowly, with her hands now knowing in the trade.

And then she spoke. She no longer needed to write first: the words came to her mouth on their own account, in the new writing.

"Good morning, Eufémia." She had found the tone, after months: the tone with which one speaks to a younger sister, because the sister, stopped at nineteen forever, was now the younger of the two, and would be more so each year. "I bring you the small roses and two pieces of news. The first: I sign today the papers of the old house. It is rented to the miller's youngest daughter, who married now and had nowhere to go. It will have new people, your house of the back street. It will have noise. I thought you would like to know." She straightened a rose that did not need it. "The second piece of news I will tell you later, if it goes well for me. Do not grow used to knowing everything before everyone, for you were always so, mother says... mother used to say." She corrected herself without hurry, with the new ease she had with the tenses of the verbs, that ease which is the only thing that well-made mournings leave as an inheritance. "Until next week, sister."

Sister. The word had come out for the first time in July, by chance, by way of farewell, and it had stayed. It was not a word of her family, for in that house no one had ever called anyone anything that was not the name or the role. It was, thought Eufémia going down the rows, the first word of kinship chosen in the history of the two: sisters they had always been, by paper and by misfortune; sisters by decision of hers, since July.

The House of the Register opened the big door to her at ten, for the signing of the papers of the old house.

The miller's daughter was there with her new husband, nervous and combed, and the Keeper at the end of the long table with the papers already laid out, and it was all that these things are: readings aloud, the usual questions, the inkwell passing from hand to hand. The newlyweds signed first, with the laboured writings of those who sign little, and then the Keeper turned the paper to Eufémia and held out the pen, and Eufémia took it, and signed.

She signed slowly, without trembling, in the new writing: the E tall and narrow, of another family, the t's upright as cypresses, the f with its loop beneath, crooked just enough, alive, unrepeatable, a name that read well and resembled nothing in the books of that house. The Keeper looked at the signature the time she looked at things, which was always the exact time, and said, drying the ink with the blotter:

"This writing is not in my books."

"No indeed," said Eufémia. "It is new. It is a year old."

"Hm." The Keeper raised the blotter and examined the result, and there was in her something that, in another face, would be called a smile. "The books are grateful. A writing that resembles none saves me confusions a hundred years from now."

And it was then, with the newlyweds already gone and the House silent again, that Eufémia took from the cloth bag the last tidying-away that she lacked: the brown notebook.

She set it on the long table, before the Keeper, with both hands.

"I would like to leave this here," she said. "If your office accepts it. It is neither an entry nor a paper of law. It is my sister's notebook, of the course she was going to take. It has her writing in it, which was mine for fifty years. At home it has already done what it had to do, and to throw it away one does not throw away, and for me to keep it again at the bottom of a chest would be to put it in the place where it did harm to everyone. I thought..." — and she searched for the words, and found them, for now she found them: — "I thought it ought to stay where the names stay: in sight, in order, beside hers. You are the one who knows whether it can be."

The Keeper drew the notebook to her and leafed through it, slowly, page by page, the rules and the lists and the sums, the *To take to the City* underlined twice. And what she said, when she shut it, was the thing Eufémia told Leal that night and that neither of the two forgot:

"I told you once that for what people live there is no book." She laid her hand on top of the brown notebook, fingers spread, as though she were entering it. "I was mistaken. There is coming to be one. It is being made little by little, with what people come here to leave when they learn the way. You have a place here for it, yes. Middle shelf. Beside hers."

And she got up, and went to put it there in that very moment, in sight of her, between the book of the births and that of the deaths of that old time: the brown notebook of the first Eufémia, lying down, in order, beside her own, in the other Véspera, the one that stays. Eufémia saw it stay and knew that the work was, now indeed, with the name put to it and tidied away, and she left the House of the Register into the sun of the square with her hands empty and light as one who has delivered the right parcel at the right address, at the end of a life of carrying it on her back without knowing of it.

The school began again that September as it always began again, with the smell of chalk and of slate varnish, and Eufémia received the new little ones at the door, one by one, as she had received them for thirty years. The little one of the Serranos came taller, browned from the summer of the serra, and brought, hung from her wrist by a string, a notebook of a brown cover that her mother had got her, and Eufémia, on seeing it, felt her heart give its old leap, and then laugh at itself: it was any brown notebook, new, empty, awaiting the writing its owner would wish to put on it. There are objects that unhaunt themselves on their own, with time, like the houses. On the first morning, she had the whole room write the name on the cover, each as they knew how, crooked or upright, and stayed watching them, bent over, tongues out, twenty heads inventing twenty ways of saying I, and she thought that this after all was her office, it had always been this, only now she knew: it was not to teach the writing. It was to guard the door while each one found their own.

The second piece of news departed on the Friday morning bus, with a cloth bag and a return ticket.

She had said it to Leal in August, at the table, with her common voice: I want to go to the City, to sleep there a night. Only one. To see what it is to wake there. And Leal, who that year had learned not to ask what for of the things that mattered, asked only whether she wanted company or wanted to go alone, and she said alone, this time alone, and he nodded and went to see about the bus timetables, which was his way of kissing the brow of a decision.

And it was so that Eufémia, teacher of the first letters of Véspera, fifty-one years old, married, did the thing that no woman of her line had ever done: she departed with no necessity at all. She was not going to an exam, not going to a doctor, not going to a funeral nor to papers. She was going because she wanted to, with a change of clothes and the money counted for a decent lodging house, and the whole village knew, of course, and this time what was said in the square Eufémia never came to know, because she no longer lived inside what was said in the square.

On the high curve of the New Road, where one sees at the same time the towers of there and the serra of here, her body did what it had done for thirty years: it began to hold its breath. And Eufémia noticed it in time, and did what she had trained a whole year in small things: she laid her hand upon it, as usual, as one calms an old animal, and breathed. She breathed on the curve, inside the curve, through the curve, with her eyes open, seeing the two lands at the same time without choosing either, and the bus passed, and nothing broke, and on the other side of the curve the world went on, wide, full of roads on which no one had ever awaited her nor ceased to await her.

Of the night in the City, which was the first of her life outside Véspera, Eufémia kept forever three things, and told them to Leal on Saturday, of an evening, with the soup between the two.

Of the afternoon, before the night, there was an hour she told no one, not even Leal, and that she kept for herself like a found coin: she came upon a school. She was on her aimless wandering, which was in itself a feast, a woman walking with no errand at all, and she turned a corner and there was a City school, large, of two storeys, at the hour of letting-out: the gate opening, the children pouring out, and behind them the teachers, young and less young, with their cases in hand, taking their leave of one another on the pavement. Eufémia stopped on the other side of the street and stayed watching, without shame, the whole time. It was that the sister had come to fetch, fifty-one years ago: that gate, that case, that pavement. One of them, the youngest, was laughing at something with her head thrown back, and Eufémia looked at her as one looks at portraits: with leave, from afar, without touching. Then the pavement emptied, and she went on, and did not feel what she feared to feel, which was the envy or the hole. She felt a thing resembling pride, with no sure owner: for that girl who laughed, for the one who did not arrive, for herself, who after all had always come, fifty years late and by her own hand, to make the City the visit that was left to make.

The first: that the room of the lodging house had its window onto a street where she knew no one, and that she stayed at the window until late, watching people pass of whom she knew nothing, neither name, nor house, nor wound, and that that, which should have been solitude, tasted to her of a river bath.

The second: that in the morning she woke with the bells of the trams, and that she stayed in bed listening to them, and that she remembered the sister, you take the alarm clock, mother, for in the City I wake with the bells, and that it did not hurt. She remembered her as one remembers the tidied dead: with company, not with weight. The sister had not got to hear the bells; she heard them for the two of them, a whole morning, and in each peal a tip of the mourning went on undoing itself into sound.

And the third thing Eufémia told with the spoon set down, choosing the words, because it was the whole news and the answer that had been owed since June:

"And then I dressed, and went to take coffee on the street, as they do there, and sat watching the City pass. And it was beautiful, Leal. It was big and washed and full of doors, and I sitting there, with the return ticket in my pocket, did all the sums again, from one end to the other. And I understood that I could. That I really could: I would find a school, find a room, the world had a use for me, it was not late. The door was wide open, and there was no one there pushing me either in or out, for the first time in my whole life." She looked at him over the soup, with clear eyes. "And it was there, sitting in that café, with the door all open before me, that I chose. I chose at leisure, point by point, like one who embroiders. And I came away on the afternoon bus, and when it made the high curve I was breathing like a delight, and when the serra won, and the village appeared down below, sure, waiting, I said within, with this new writing you like better: I am arriving. I did not say it is done, nor good, nor it was what was arranged. I said I am arriving home, because I want to, because it is mine, because I chose it today in a City café with the door open." And she took up the spoon again, and finished, dry and clear, in the manner of the house: "I stay, Leal. The question is answered. I stay because I want to. Now you see what you do with a woman who stays on purpose."

And Leal, on the other side of the table of press wood, was a moment silent, and then said, with the dry laugh coming slowly to his eyes:

"I make her supper tomorrow." And he got up to tidy, and on passing her laid his hand on her shoulder, a warm and exact weight, the hands that did not err, and added: "And in the morning I leave her shawl on the nail. Hung by whichever strap she wishes."

On Sunday morning, Eufémia crossed the square early, for her usual walk.

The summer was ending, and the square was as it had been for five hundred years: the houses shut in their sleep, the House of the Register with the big door unopened, the Shop smelling of bread inside the shutters. And in the middle, composed, the dry fountain, with its look of eternity. Eufémia went straight to it, as for a year she had gone straight to nothing, and laid her hand on the rim, on the warm spot where she had laid it in February, and stayed there a moment, hand on the stone, the woman and the fountain, the two dry and the two composed and the two, after all, with the old water down below, awaiting only one who knew how to ask it the way.

"Good morning," Eufémia said to it, aloud, in the deserted square. And she walked on, at her pace.

And whoever saw her cross the square that morning, and had seen her cross it a year before, would swear it was another woman, and would be mistaken, and the village, which knows everything, knew it better than anyone: it was the same. It was exactly the same, the one of the grey plait to the front of the left shoulder, the teacher, the one of the house facing the square. Only now she went by choice, step put by step put, and that, which is not seen and is seen, changes a person's gait in a way that no entry can record.

But there are books that can. This one, for example, that here closes, in the square of Véspera, on a Sunday morning, with a woman of fifty-one in the middle of her spring, on her way to the life she had always had and that had just begun.
