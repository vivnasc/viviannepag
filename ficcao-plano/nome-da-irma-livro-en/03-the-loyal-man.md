## 3. The loyal man

The roof of the old house was mended in the first week of November, before the rains, as Leal had said at the September breakfast it would have to be. The things Leal said at breakfast got done. It was a law of the house as sure as that of the bell, and Eufémia had lived inside it for twenty-five years without finding its weight, as one does not find a weight in the air.

She took him his midday meal halfway through the morning, by the back way. The day was clear and cold, and she saw him from far off, atop her mother's house, on his knees on the ridge, setting tiles with that exact slowness that was his signature. Leal was not a man of haste nor of force: he was a man of knack. His hands went straight to the place where things hurt, in the wood, in the stone, in the hinges, and the things ceased to hurt. The whole village asked him for the loan of his knack, and he went, on Sundays, to mend a widow's window or the step of a stair, and came back without telling anyone where he had been. Seventy-two years old and he climbed a roof as one climbs to the hayloft. Eufémia stayed at the gate watching him work, with the meal basket on her arm, and found herself thinking, with the new ideas of that autumn, that she had never seen her husband's hands be mistaken.

"Mind where you put your feet," she said, when he came down.

"I put them where I always put them." Leal sat on the door's step, uncovered the basket, broke the bread in half and gave her the larger half, all in the same sentence of the hands. "This roof will yet bury us all. Good tile, good wood. Wood like this is not made any more."

"So the house holds, then."

"The house holds another fifty years," he said, chewing slowly, looking up with the approval of a knowing man. "Houses, if one does not leave them to the rain, last longer than people. It was your father who praised it to me once, this wood. I was a lad."

That was how his past appeared: in slivers, by the door of the trade, without ceremony. I was a lad. Eufémia caught the sliver and kept it, as she had been keeping everything lately, and asked nothing, because she was afraid of the whole thread of the skein.

"And now?" she asked instead, with her head on the house. "It stays shut?"

"It stays shut until we see." Leal shook the crumbs to the sparrows, without hurry. "It is aired from month to month, the fire is lit twice a winter, and the house waits. Houses know how to wait, if one tells them one will come back."

Said by another man, it was a manner of speaking. Said by that one, with that house facing the square that had waited twenty-some years, it was a door ajar onto a room Eufémia had never entered, and she stayed a moment at the threshold, with her heart doing sums, and did not enter. They spoke of the mother. That she was smaller, he said, and that she ate better since her window gave onto the square: small people, he said, need to see the big ones pass. There was in him no impatience at all with the mother-in-law who upset his house and his hours. He had made her the handrail, he would make her the rest. Eufémia went back by the back way with the empty basket and all that wealth on her back, thinking that there were women in the village married to men to whom one had to ask everything twice, and that she, who would have thought it, had drawn the great prize in a raffle in which she had never bought a ticket.

But the thread existed, and the village knew it by heart. And that week, as though the autumn were conspiring with her sleepless nights, the thread came to her hand at the Shop.

It was because of a comb. Eufémia waited her turn at the counter, and ahead of her the oldest of the women of the square was buying pins and taking her time, because to buy, for the very old, is a way of conversing. The old woman saw her, measured her from head to foot with that right that ninety years give, and said, with no more ado nor wherefore:

"Your man made me a comb, you know? I was a girl." She laughed with her gums, delighted with her own memory. "Of pale wood, with the little rounded ends. Oh, the girls of that time died of envy of the combs he made. But he only made them for one."

"Grandmother, leave that be," said the granddaughter who accompanied her, with the distress of one who knows the way of her grandmother's conversations.

"Leave what?" The old woman shrugged, sovereign. "Old things. No one remembers any more."

And she paid for the pins and went off, on her granddaughter's arm, and Benvinda, on the other side of the counter, stayed winding the string of the parcel more slowly than the string asked, her eyes on the counter, waiting to see whether Eufémia wanted to take up the end the old woman had left on the wood. Eufémia asked for what she had come to ask, in the tone of one who had picked nothing up from the floor. But she carried the end home, as she carried everything now, and at night, in the dark, joined it to the rest of the skein: the combs he only made for one. In the small chest of small things that had come from the old house there was a comb of pale wood, of little rounded ends, worn on the left side; Eufémia had taken it up while tidying, without knowing what she had in her hand, and had put it in the drawer of the chest, beside her own.

Leal was the son of the old carpenter, and the old carpenter and Bendito understood each other as the trades understand each other that need one another: one made the doors, the other had the houses. The two families dined together at the festivals, lent each other tools and securities, and somewhere in those years, without anyone able to point to the day, it began to be said that the carpenter's lad and Bendito's elder girl were for each other. It was said first in jest, then in earnest of a plan. In Véspera many marriages began so, outside the betrothed, like the names: it was arranged over their heads and the heart was expected to come after, for the heart, it was said, is like the vines, it takes almost always, one need only set the stake straight.

What no one expected was that it would take in that way.

Because the lad, it was told, brought to the arrangement a seriousness that was no longer obedience. He appeared to her at the door of the sewing school with something made by his hands, a little box, a comb of pale wood. He learned no one knows where the names of the stars, because she had said one day that she wanted to know them. And when she was set to study for the City exam, he, who had a dead grandfather and a house of inheritance facing the square, half fallen, began to raise it. He told no one for whom it was, but he worked on it on Sundays in sight of the whole village, which is the highest way of saying.

The fever took her in nine days, in the spring. And what was told of the nine days was little, and what was told of the after was this: that the lad finished the roof of the house facing the square that same summer, alone, without speaking to anyone, and that then he shut the door and did nothing more in the house for years. He neither sold it, nor opened it, nor let it fall: he mended a tile when it was needed, and nothing more. The village, which marries everything, went on finding him brides in conversation, the daughter of this, the niece of that, and he let himself be married in conversation without helping nor denying, and married none of them. He worked. He had the most sought-after hands in the region and the most silent Sundays. A house for whom, the village asked, on seeing him, little by little, years later, go back to working on the house of the square, a window this year, a floor the next. A house for whom, and the question had no answer, and in Véspera the questions without an answer do not die: they stay waiting, like the fountain.

The answer was born in the winter of the great waters, on the other side of the square, and took twenty-four years to grow.

Eufémia knew the day the thing was decided, because the day was visible: a Sunday in September, she twenty-four years old, and Leal, washed and combed, in a dark suit that sat on him as a wardrobe sits on a man used to work clothes, knocking at the door of the house of the back street. He was shut with the mother in the good room the time of a measured conversation. Bendito was no longer living; the one who kept the door of the daughters was Saudade. Then the mother came to call her, with a face Eufémia had never seen, a face of one who closes a book that had been years open to the rain, and said: Senhor Leal wants to speak with you, he is in the yard.

He was standing beside the rosemary, turning the brim of his hat in his careful hands, and the speech he had prepared could be seen on his brow, and he did not use it. He looked at her a long moment, and what he said was:

"I know I am older than I ought to be. But the house of the square is ready, and I take good care of what is mine. If you want to come, you come. If you do not want to, I stay your friend all the same, and we speak no more of it."

He did not speak of love, and Eufémia thanked him for it for years, thinking it had been the modesty of an old-time man. Now, at fifty, with a sealed letter burning inside a wardrobe, she asked herself what other words he had burned on his brow that Sunday, before choosing those, the honest ones, the ones that promised nothing but what he could keep.

She said yes. Of course she said yes. The yes had been on the table for weeks, set there by the whole village, beside the bread, and she said it as she said the others: without cost.

They married the following spring, and the village made of the wedding a feast greater than the betrothed's account: a long table in the churchyard, the Shop with its doors open, the old women weeping with a feeling that was not quite for the betrothed and no one asked whose it was. They all said the same thing, with variations: it is right. It is so right. And it was, one could see that it was, right as a sum done: the ready house waited, the serious man waited, and the girl who had the right name went into the right house on the arm of the right man, and the whole village rested that day from a weight it had carried twenty-five years without knowing of it. The first year of marriage was of learning each other, as in all marriages, with the difference that Leal learned her as one learns a new thing, and she learned him as one memorises a lesson already half known, and neither of the two noticed the difference, because the difference made no noise. But let no one be mistaken in the account, and Eufémia, in the sleepless nights of that autumn, made a point of not being mistaken in it: she had not been unhappy a single day. Leal was better than the promised. He was patient, he was clean, he was funny in a dry way that was only heard well in the second year of the house. He had warmed her side of the bed on the frost nights before she lay down, twenty-five winters running, without ever mentioning it in the morning. If that was not love, it was a thing for which much love of words would be traded without loss. She liked her husband. That was the complication, thought Eufémia: if he were bad, the question was easy. He being so, the question was hers alone.

The tin box changed houses within the house on a Tuesday.

It had stayed in the corridor chest since the October night, without secret and without show, and Eufémia passed by it ten times a day and pretended it was a box. But that Tuesday, at the fall of the afternoon, she found the mother standing in the corridor, in her nightdress, with her hand laid on top of the tin lid, very still, with her back to her. She did not see her face. She made no noise. She stayed at the end of the corridor, suspended, with her heart in a strange place, waiting, and at the end of a long time the mother took her hand off the box, like one who gives up remembering what that gesture was for, and went back to her room dragging her slippers, and Eufémia was left not knowing what she had seen: whether a chance of an old woman lost about the house, or a visit that the hand still knew and the head no longer did.

That night, with Leal already in bed, she took the box to the room and put it away at the bottom of the wardrobe, behind the folded linen shirts. It was in doing so that she saw herself: the shirts lifted with the left, the box pushed into the dark with the right, the exact, millimetric gesture of the mother at the dowry chest. She was hiding it beneath the linen, as the mother had hidden it beneath the linen. Fifty years and a generation of distance, the same box, the same hiding place, and Eufémia drew her hands from the wardrobe as one draws them from the fire, and even so left the box there, because one thing was to see the gesture and another, still, was to know how to make a different one.

She nearly told Leal twice.

The first was that same night, in the dark, with the sentence already made: I found a box at mother's house, it has things of my sister's in it, it has a letter in it. She got as far as filling her breast to say it. What came out was a question about the roof. The second was on Sunday, in the yard, he pruning the rose, she beside him, and the sentence again whole in her mouth, shorter, more possible: Leal, did you remember her? And it did not come out either. It did not come out because Eufémia understood, with the small knife of that perception, that she had no fear at all of the answer that would hurt him. She had fear of the one that would upset her. For twenty-five years that house had rested on a question unasked, as certain bridges rest on a pier no one goes to see; and who goes, with their own hands, to strike the pier of the bridge where they live?

And meanwhile the chest of drawers gained a life of its own. Eufémia combed herself in the morning and her hand hesitated over the drawer as over an ember: in there, beside her combs, was the one of pale wood, worn on the left side, on the side of a thick plait pulled to the front of the shoulder. She could have moved it. She did not move it. There was in her a new stubbornness, still without a name, that would not let her: the things one hides command us, she was beginning to suspect, and the ones that stay in sight go on losing their poison little by little, like nettles picked.

December came in with the council minutes, as every year.

It was an old custom: on the nights before the winter assembly, Eufémia copied out the book of minutes, at the kitchen table, with the good pen and the blotter, and Leal sat on the other side with his small things, a handle to varnish, a lock to adjust, and they made the evening so, each at their trade, by the same lamp. The minutes of that year spoke of the school roof, of the serra path that the rains always took, of the Spinner's mill, which was again on people's tongues with its announced closures; the whole village passed through Eufémia's writing on its way to memory, and she liked to think that it was there, by her hand, that the years of Véspera were set straight. It was one of the hours she most prized in the week, and she had never said it to anyone, because to say spoils the thread of those things.

She was halfway through a page, the writing coming out round, even, sure as the hours, when she felt that he had stopped working. She raised her eyes. Leal was looking at her hand, at the way of the writing on the page, with an old rest in his face, and said, in his way, without knowing that he was saying anything:

"I could stay watching you write the whole afternoon. There are things I never tire of."

And he went back to the lock.

Eufémia finished the line. The hand finished it for her, round, even, sure, the hand practised line by line by a brown notebook through the winters of childhood, the hand that walked by the way of another hand. She even thanked him. She said something light, and he smiled, and the evening went on like the good evenings. Only within, and slowly, as the water rises in the deep lands, did his phrase unfold itself into the two questions she was left with, afterward, in the dark, long after he slept.

To whom had he said that for the first time, fifty years ago, leaning over what table, beside what lamp.

And to whom, to whom was he saying it now.
