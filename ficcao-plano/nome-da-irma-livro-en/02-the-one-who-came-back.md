## 2. The one who came back

The entry of the name was made in the winter, with the river still out of its bed and the village without a bridge.

Those who told it were the old women of the square, who in that time were young and were there: the House of the Register full as it had not been seen since no one knew when, the cold coming in through the big door, and the whole village pressed between the shelves of the books, wanting to see the girl. And it was not for the girl, for girls are born every year: it was for the name. The village knew what name it would be. It knew before the Keeper of that time, perhaps before the mother herself said it aloud, because in Véspera the important decisions are taken so, outside the people, in the air they breathe. And when the name was said over the open book, and the pen entered it, there ran through the house a deep sigh, of a work finished, and old Purificação, who had a hand for sentences, said the phrase that stayed: the river took the Bridge from us and brought us Eufémia.

The phrase stayed. The right phrases, in Véspera, stay: they pass from mouth to mouth until they lose their owner and seem age-old sayings. Eufémia grew up inside that one, as one grows up inside a house: without noticing the walls.

Saudade was at the front of everyone, in mourning beneath her good dress, with the nine-day-old daughter in her arms. She did not weep on hearing the name, as she had not wept at the burial, and the village found in her the strength of an old-time woman. Bendito, at her side, wept for two, which was the division of labour of that marriage, and no one held it against him: to men are forgiven the tears that to women are deducted.

Of this, Eufémia did not remember, of course. She remembered the rest.

She remembered the hands of the old women on her head. She was small, she went by her mother's hand to the Shop, to the churchyard, to the dry fountain where the women gathered even without water, and the old women bent down to her, laid an open hand on her head as one who checks a weight, and said: the very image. The very image, they repeated to one another, wagging their chins. They never said the very image of what, and Eufémia, for years, thought it was a greeting one made to all children, a kind of good day for little people. Only later did she notice that to the little one of the Shop they did not say the very image. Nor to the miller's daughter. It was only to her. It was a greeting only hers, and she thought that, at the time, a distinction.

Once, she would have been six, she asked. It was at the edge of the fountain, on a summer's late afternoon, the women sitting on the stone rim with their sewing on their knees, and the oldest of all had put her hand on her head and said the phrase, and the girl, that day, instead of receiving it as she received the rest, pulled her mother's skirt and asked aloud, with the clean voice of children who do not yet know where the walls are:

"The very image of whom, mother?"

The fountain made a silence the girl never forgot, because it was the first time she saw a silence be made: the needles all stopped at the same time, the faces all turned to the mother, and the mother, without hurry, settled her plait to the front of the shoulder, smoothed it with the palm of her hand, and answered to the sewing:

"Old women's things. Come, it is late."

And the oldest of all, on seeing them go, said to the others, low, but not so low that the girl did not hear, because in Véspera nothing is said so low:

"Poor little one. She does not even know what she carries."

The girl kept the phrase without understanding it, as one keeps seeds: by habit. And the phrase stayed in there doing what seeds do.

She remembered the plait. The mother combed her every morning, by the window, with a wooden brush that had been someone's, and the style was always the same: a thick plait, pulled to the front of the left shoulder. It was not the style of the other girls, who went about with loose hair or with two plaits behind, and Eufémia once asked for two plaits, like the miller's daughter. The mother went on brushing as though the phrase had fallen to the floor and were not worth picking up. One plait, she said afterward, and to the front of the shoulder, which is how it looks well. At eighteen, Eufémia wanted to cut her hair to shoulder length, which was beginning to be worn, and it was one of the rare times in her life that she heard the mother raise her voice. It was not a shout: it was a single word, not that, said in a tone that came from a place so deep that the daughter drew back as though she had trodden on grave-earth. She did not speak of hair again. The plait was there still today, grey, thinner, pulled to the front of the left shoulder, and Eufémia would come upon it in the mirror, in that autumn of her fifty years, and would hold it in her hand like one who holds a thing that after all was never hers.

And she remembered, more than anything, the notebook.

It was in the winter of her seven years. The mother took from a place she did not see a brown notebook, of worn corners, opened it on the kitchen table beside a new notebook, and said: you are going to learn to write properly. On the first line of the brown notebook there was a round writing, even, sure as the hours.

"Copy," said the mother.

The girl copied the first line, with her tongue out, and the letter came out as the first letters of everyone come out: alive and crooked, hers.

"It is crooked," said the mother, without any malice, as one says that it rains. "Again."

"But it can be read, mother."

"Read, it can be. It is not enough that it be read." The mother pulled the lamp more to the middle of the table, and for a moment looked at the brown notebook in a way the girl did not know how to read, and that she would take forty-three years to read. "Copy until it is the same."

"The same as what?"

The question had no answer, and it was for that that the girl asked it only that once. In Saudade's house one learned quickly which were the questions that had an answer and which were the others, the ones that fell to the kitchen floor and stayed there, and that no one swept up, because not even to the rubbish could they be taken. The same as the notebook, said the mother at last. And the girl copied. She copied that winter and the next and the next, evening after evening, the lamp in the middle of the table, the mother darning on the other side and raising her eyes now and then, without hurry, to say only it is crooked, or again, or, very rarely, it is better. One did not copy what the notebook said, which were rules and lists and grown people's sums the girl did not understand: one copied the writing. The way of the writing. The girl learned to make the e of that hand, the f of that hand, the loops of the g and the j of that hand, until one night, she would have been eleven, the mother took the day's page, held it to the lamp, was a long time silent, and said: it is done now.

It is done now, and she closed the brown notebook, and took it away, and the girl never saw it again.

The writing stayed with her. Eufémia's famous writing, Eufémia's gift, the writing that wrote the council minutes and the signs for the festivals and the serious letters of the whole village. Forty years of signing with it, of teaching with it, of being praised for it. And only now, in that autumn, with the tin box put away in the corridor chest, had Eufémia understood what the mother had meant that night with it is done now. It was not a praise. It was a work given as concluded. The writing had come back.

The school came to her by the same door by which all things came: without asking her anything.

She was seventeen. The old schoolmistress, who had taught the first letters for forty years, broke a hip in the January frost, and the village needed someone to hold the little ones until the end of the year. There was a conversation at the Shop, another at the churchyard, and on Sunday the schoolmistress sent word to Saudade: that she should send her the girl, who had good writing and good manners. The word went to the mother, not to her. The answer too: she goes Monday.

Eufémia was told the day before, at supper, between the soup and the rest.

"Tomorrow you go to help the schoolmistress, at the school," said the mother, serving her plate. "Until the end of the year, at least. You take the grey dress."

"Me?"

"You have good writing and good manners." The mother sat down, unfolded her napkin, and added, without raising her eyes, the phrase that shut all the doors through which a doubt might enter: "It is already arranged."

It is already arranged. Eufémia, years later, would think that her life had been made entire with those three words, said by various mouths, at various tables, always before she arrived: it is already arranged. And that night she said yes, of course, because it was the answer that waited for her on the table, beside the bread.

Of the first day at the school she remembered everything, and above all the beginning. Before the lessons, they sent her by the schoolmistress's house, who was in bed with the wedged hip and wanted to see with her own eyes what she was sending to her room. The old woman sat her down, asked her things of adding and of reading, set her to write a line on a paper she had by the bed, and studied the line a long time, glasses on the end of her nose, with a face that went on slowly coming undone toward a place that was not the face of assessing.

"You have the most beautiful writing that has passed through my school," she said at last. And she added, lower, with her eyes still on the paper: "Since..."

The next word did not come. The schoolmistress folded the paper in two, as though she were putting it away, and put away with it the phrase, and what she said in a voice of command was that the little ones are won by patience and lost by shouts, and that she should open the windows in the middle of the morning, rain or shine, for the sense needs air. Eufémia went off with the instructions and with the word that was missing. Since. For years she put it away as she put everything away: the schoolmistress was old, she tired, her phrases fell from her. Now she knew how to reckon better. The village was full of phrases like that, she understood now, phrases with a step missing in the middle, and everyone skipped the step with an agility so old that the skip was no longer even seen. Only she had gone fifty years climbing that stairway without asking why she always stumbled in the same place. And she liked it, let it be said. She liked the little ones, the slates, the smell of chalk and of school fire. She liked it still. Never, in thirty-three years, had she gone into that room unwillingly, and that, she thought, ought to count for something too. The course she did later, already married, when the New Road opened: she went and came back the same day, with her books in a cloth bag, without ever sleeping in the City. The first of the village to study away was Aurora of the Shop, years later, and the village still said it with pride and scandal. Of Eufémia they said another thing: that she had studied without leaving the side of her own. As who should say: she went and did not go. As who should say: she got as far as going and knew how not to go. It was a praise, in the mouth of the village. Eufémia had always heard it as a praise.

From the time of the course there had stayed with her a memory she never knew where to put, and that in that autumn came of its own to the light. It was on the returns. The afternoon bus made the New Road with the sun low, and there was a point, at the way out of the City, where one saw at the same time the towers of there and the serra of here, a single instant, on the high curve. And Eufémia, with her books in the cloth bag on her knees, found herself every day doing on that curve a strange thing: she held her breath. She did not know why. She held it on the curve and let it out when the serra won, and the village appeared down below, sure, waiting, and she let the air out slowly and said to herself good, it is done, I am arriving. For thirty years she had told that as relief. Now, without sleep, she was no longer sure what name to give the thing that is held and let out on a curve between two lives.

That October night, with the mother asleep in the back room and Leal sawing boards in dreams beside her, Eufémia, without sleep, set herself to making an inventory.

It was not a sad inventory. She made it with her eyes open in the dark, with method, as she made everything: from the beginning. The name had come first, before her, at the edge of a grave. The plait had come with the name. The writing had come from the notebook, practised until it was the other's. The office had come by word, from the schoolmistress to the mother. The husband had come... the husband had come by a conversation between houses she never heard, with an earlier betrothal inside it, like the dolls that have another doll inside them. The house had come with the husband, facing the square, its windows onto the fountain. Even the shawl that waited for her on the cold mornings had been a gift, and she no longer knew from whom.

And the friendships, she went on remembering, those too had come set on the table. The friend of a whole life had fallen to her for being the goddaughter of her mother's gossip: the two girls had been seated side by side at the festivals since before they knew how to speak, and the village decided that they were inseparable, and they, obedient, were so. She was a good friend, one did not say otherwise; but Eufémia, in the dark, asked the new question of that piece too, and the piece answered like the others: if they had seated her beside another girl, she would be the lifelong friend of another girl. Nowhere on the board did she find a piece that had been set there by her own hand. The board was complete, well played, won even. Only played by others.

She was looking for one thing. One only. One thing in her life, it could be small, it could be ridiculous, one thing that had entered her by her own choice, asked for by her, wanted by her before anyone.

The rosemary in the yard, she thought, with a beginning of relief. It was she who had planted it. And at once the memory, which on those nights was on duty, brought her the mother's voice, on an old Sunday, at the door of the freshly dug yard: plant a rosemary there, for a house without rosemary is a house without governance.

Eufémia laughed alone in the dark, softly, so as not to wake anyone. It was a dry laugh, without complaint, the laugh of one who understands the bill at the end of the month.

She found nothing, that night. She searched even in the smallest things, in those one does not confess for shame of their size: the way of taking the mug, the side from which she began to sweep, the stitch she preferred. It all came from somewhere, it all had a master, it all had a mould. She told herself that she would search better, that a life is many years and no one remembers everything, and that in some corner there must be a chosen thing, even if small, waiting to be found like the mother's needles. She fell asleep on that. And in the sleep, which in Véspera tells the truth, she went the whole night through a house that was hers but had all its doors in the wrong place, opening cupboards, asking faceless people whether they had seen a thing of hers, and no one knew anything, and the worst of the dream was not the not finding: it was that she too no longer knew how to say what the thing was, how it was, what colour, what size, hers in what way.

She woke before the bell, as always. The house received her already arranged: the bread in the bread's place, the apron on the nail, hung by the left strap. Eufémia stopped a moment in the middle of the kitchen, looking at the apron.

Then she hung it by the right strap, only to see, and the right strap chafed her neck the whole morning, and she bore it with an absurd, secret, disproportionate satisfaction, as the first men's must have been when they made the first fire. It did not count for the inventory, well she knew. An apron is not a life, and to chafe is not to choose. But it had been she, and it had been today, and one begins somewhere to learn a trade in which no one ever set us as an apprentice.
