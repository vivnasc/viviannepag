## 7. The flowers

The cemetery of Véspera lies on the eastern slope, turned to the morning sun, and the village likes to say that its dead wake early. One goes up by a path of flagstones between two low walls, passes the iron gate that is never locked, because from there no one flees and to there no one goes who should not, and within the graves are arranged in rows by the age of the ground: the old ones above, beside the old porch of the inner churchyard, the new ones below, and in the eastern corner, behind a hedge of box, the small plot of the little angels, where the village does not enter except for what it is, and from which it always comes out silent.

Eufémia went up on the Sunday, at the beginning of the afternoon, alone.

She had said at home that she was going for a walk, which was no lie nor out of character: she had gone for walks on Sundays forever, it was one of her sure things. Only the way was new. And the body noticed the new way before the head: in leaving the square by the eastern alley, instead of the river path, her legs grew heavy as on the great climbs, without the climb having begun, and Eufémia had to talk with herself as she talked with the little ones on the first days of school: come, it is not hard, come, I am going with you.

On the path of the flagstones, between the two low walls, she made the inventory of her burials, because the step of the climb drew sums out of her. She had been to all of them, since she was a girl: it was what was done, the whole village went up with each of its own, and she had gone up behind the coffins of neighbours and of schoolmistresses, of men of the fields and of children, of her own father, of old Purificação who had a hand for sentences. She knew the protocol by heart: the place of the relatives, the right step, what is said and what is not said at the edge of the graves. And in all those burials, dozens of them, fifty years of them, she had always been three rows away from her sister without ever turning her face that way. She remembered now, with a sharpness that made an impression, exact moments: a winter burial in which she had stood, by chance of the crowd, almost against the eastern wall, with her back to the third row, the whole time with her back turned, not knowing why and knowing. Her body had always known where the sister was. It had known it as bodies know: by avoidance, by the design of the detours, by the negative map that the steps of a whole life leave around the place where they never tread. It had not been necessary to forbid her anything. The good fences, she thought, finishing the sum with the breath of the climb, the good fences are made so: they are not raised around the field, they are raised within the person, and then the gate can be left open a whole life, for no one passes.

The iron gate gave its usual creak, which she knew from all the burials of her life. Within was the tidied silence of village cemeteries, which is not sad, it is domestic: swept beds, upright jugs, here and there a line of box to be trimmed. Eufémia stayed standing at the entrance, with the parcel of not knowing where to go. She knew, from hearing it said in fifty years of half-words, that the sister was in the upper rows, in the old ones. She knew nothing more. And there was no one to ask, nor did she want there to be, because that search, she understood on beginning it, had to be made just so: grave by grave, name by name, with her feet.

She went up by the middle row, slowly, reading.

And the reading did a thing to her that she had not expected: it calmed her. Because the names on the graves were the names of the village, the usual ones, those of the old women of the square and the men of the fields, Prudências and Bonanças, Custódias and Benvindas, Leais and Benditos, generations of them, and beneath each name the dates and above each name, often, a small porcelain portrait, and Eufémia knew half the faces and was known to half the names, and at a certain point she was no longer a woman looking for a grave: she was a neighbour reviewing the village above, greeting it with her eyes. Here the old schoolmistress, with her glasses on the end of her nose even in the porcelain. Here her father, Bendito, in the usual portrait, the one from the chest of drawers, and Eufémia stopped and straightened his jug, which was the least bit crooked, and said softly until later, father, for in a village cemetery one speaks with the dead for the same reason one speaks with the plants: because they are part of it.

Halfway through the search, without meaning to, she found herself beside the box hedge of the corner, and stopped.

On the other side of the hedge was the plot of the little angels. It could be seen over the box, which there was let grow taller on purpose: the small graves, almost all without a stone, with river pebbles set at the head, white, the size of small loaves. Those who did not get to have a name. Eufémia had never been so near that corner, because to that corner one does not go save for what it is, and she stayed a moment outside, with her hand on the box, looking at the white pebbles in the sun. The sister was not there; the sister had had a name, nineteen years of a name, and an entry, and a written stone. But Eufémia thought, without having summoned it, of the others: of those without even a name. And she thought, right after, for the first time in many years, with a mild and old pain that no longer shamed her, of her own, of those who had not come, of those whom she and Leal had awaited in silence the first years of marriage and then ceased to await also in silence, without ever a word, in the manner of the house. They had no grave nor pebble, hers: they had not got to be anything but hope. And Eufémia, with her hand on the box, did for them what she knew how to do: company, a moment, and went on, and that was not sadness, it was another thing, it was reviewing fully her house above, without skipping a room, as one does who wants at last to know the whole house where they have always lived.

She found her in the third row above, beside the eastern wall, with the sun full upon it.

She knew it was her before reading, because it was the only grave of that old row that looked of yesterday. The others had their dignified neglect of fifty years, the stone darkened, the name in need of paint. That one not. The stone was washed, the paint of the name fresh, the bed at the foot clean of weeds, and in the stone jug, upright, alive, with the water shining, there were fresh flowers. Jonquils. The first of the year, which opened only on the skirts of the serra, where there is sun and shelter, and which one had to know where to go and fetch.

*Eufémia*, said the stone. *Daughter of Bendito and of Saudade. Nineteen years.* And beneath it, in smaller letters, worn but repainted, faithfully repainted by whoever it was, a single line: *She was about to begin.*

Eufémia stood before her sister, in the February sun, a long time.

She did not weep, and it was not from holding herself in: it was because what she had within was not in a state of tears, it was in another form, a solid and awkward form that did not come out by the eyes nor by the mouth. She tried to speak, because she had come for that. She had rehearsed on the way phrases that seemed to her worthy, it is I, I have come at last, forgive the delay. Before the stone, the phrases all died on her, and she understood why, and the understanding was one of the hardest things of that whole winter: she had nothing of her own to say to that girl. All she could say to her was made of the other's things: your name I serve it, your house I dwell in it, your man, your table, your writing. It was like going to visit someone to give back to them the whole house and not having, beneath the house, anything that was ours to offer. She came with her hands overflowing and with empty hands. She sat on the rim of the grave beside it, which was of some Próspero of a hundred years ago and would not mind, and stayed there, silent, hands in her lap, keeping company. It was what she knew how to do. At least that, to keep company, she had learned on her own account, at her mother's bedside: to be there, with no usefulness at all, with nothing more. Perhaps it counted. Perhaps it was, seen rightly, the first thing of her own that she gave the sister: a Sunday afternoon, without a word, the two Eufémias in the sun.

The jonquils were what would not let her go. Who.

The mother had not left the house in half a year. The Keeper watered the memory of the whole village, she would not be watering jugs of that one alone. The old women of the square no longer climbed the slope. And the jonquils were of that week, two days at most, and the water of the jug was clean, and the paint of the *She was about to begin* was not ten years old, it had been put back time and again, by a hand that knew how to do things slowly and well.

The answer climbed the path of the flagstones at the end of the afternoon, when the sun was already low and Eufémia was getting up to go.

She heard the gate creak and drew back two steps without thinking, behind the big cypress of the corner, and was at once ashamed of the gesture and stayed in it, ashamed and hidden, because there were things stronger than shame. And she saw her husband come into the cemetery with a watering can in his hand and pruning shears in the pocket of his coat, saw him climb the rows with the step of one who climbs their own stair, without searching, without hesitating at any row, and stop before the washed grave, and set down the can, and take off his cap.

And she saw the rest, hidden behind a cypress like a wicked girl of a serial, with her heart beating in her ears: she saw Leal pour the old water of the jug to the foot of the box, with care, so as not to waste it; fill it fresh; arrange the jonquils one by one, with those thick fingers that did not err; pass his thumb over the stone, over the name, in a gesture that was not of cleaning anything, because there was nothing to clean, it was of another nature; and then stand, cap in hand, a short moment, shorter than she would have expected, the moment of one who has long since said everything and now only stops by to know whether all is well. Then he put on his cap, took up the can and went down the rows, and the gate creaked, and his steps went on fading down the path of the flagstones, downward, to the village, to the house facing the square where in an hour he would put the water on to heat for the supper of the three.

Eufémia came out from behind the cypress. The sun had set on the side of the serra and the cold came down fast, as it comes down on the slopes turned to the east, and she stayed a moment more before the grave, now with the long shadows, looking at the jonquils arranged one by one.

Fifty years, she thought. The whole village knew it, of course, as it knew everything: that was why the Keeper had said have you never been there, that was why no one had ever answered her question. The most serious man in Véspera had climbed that slope for fifty years, first a lad, then a man, then an old man, with flowers of the season and paint for the letters, and had never said a word, neither before marrying her nor after, and the whole village had kept the secret that was no secret at all, it was only a thing not spoken of, as the sister was not spoken of, as nothing that mattered was spoken of, in that land where everything was known and nothing was said, and where love and mourning both went about dressed in the same work clothes.

And there was more, and Eufémia forced herself to think it to the end, there in the cold, because she had learned that winter that the thoughts left half-made come back at night, with interest. If he climbed that slope since he was a lad, then he had climbed there during her betrothal. During the marriage. He had gone out of the house facing the square, countless Sundays, with the clean watering can, and she asking him good walk?, and he answering good, and the two knowing everything and neither saying anything. And when she took him his meal to the roof of the old house, the hands that broke the bread and gave her the larger half came from arranging flowers on the grave of the first. There was no betrayal in any part of it, and that was the strangest of all: there were two cares, side by side, for twenty-five years, each with its hour and its way, without robbing each other of anything, managed by a silent man with the exactness with which one manages a watering in August, so that the water reaches all the plots. It was she who had never known of the other plot. Or she had always known, in the manner of Véspera, and chosen not to know: and it was that second possibility, she understood, the one that hurt with most usefulness, because it no longer accused anyone but the old habit of her own face, that of composing itself.

She went down to the village with the first lights already lit. From the eastern alley one saw the window of her kitchen, yellow, with his shape inside it, in the sure life of six o'clock. And Eufémia stopped a moment to look, with the world turned over inside her breast, not knowing the name of what she felt. It was not jealousy, no longer. She had been beside jealousy in the morning, in thinking of the jonquils; now, after seeing him with his cap in his hand, she knew it was not that, nor near it. It was a thing for which she had no word, made of astonishment, of an almost unbearable tenderness, and of a fine envy, fine as the white thread of an embroidery: the man with whom she had slept for twenty-five years had done for the sister, in silence, without a single failing, for fifty years, the whole mourning that no one in her family had even known how to begin. He had given her a visited grave, a repainted name, flowers of the season. The mother had given her a silence and a dowry with the swapped letter. And she, Eufémia, had given her the life, in the sense in which one gives a thing one has taken.

She came into the house by the smell of the supper. Leal was at the sink, with his back turned, washing the watering can, which he set down in the usual corner, by the yard door, in the place where that can had stood all her life without her ever once asking him why a watering can of a house with a well went about always so clean.

"Good walk?" he asked, without turning.

"Good," said Eufémia. "I went up to the east."

His hands stopped at the sink, two seconds, the hands that never erred. Then they went on, and he said the supper was nearly ready, and she went to lay the table, and neither of the two said anything more, and the two knew everything, in the manner of Véspera. On the table of press wood, between the three plates, Eufémia laid her hands a moment and looked at them: they smelled of box and of cold stone. In eight days, she thought, the jonquils wither. And for the first time in her life it was she who did the sums of the calendar of the flowers, which was, she knew it there, the only calendar that in that house had never failed.
