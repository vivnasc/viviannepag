## 8. The sister's name

March came in with a south wind and with a new stubbornness in Eufémia's house: the daughter began to draw on the dead one, and the mother, who lost everything, did not lose the art of fleeing.

It was an art of fifty years, polished like the stones of rivers, and it worked even in the midst of the fog. Eufémia tried all the ways. She tried the broad way, on a good day, with the mother ruling the soup: mother, I found some things in the old house, I wanted to talk with you. And the mother, without changing her face, thought the soup needed one more bone, and after wanted to know about the roof, and after was sleepy. She tried the narrow way, of an evening, in half-light: mother, tell me about your life as a girl, before me. And the mother spoke, vaguely delighted, of the linen and the festivals, of the temper of her father, of the harvest songs, a whole hour of long-ago with a hole in the middle, a hole with the exact shape of a person, skirted by the conversation with the skill of one who dances in the dark in a kitchen they know by heart. She tried even the children's way, which was to take her hand and ask point-blank, in a lull of gentleness: mother, what was she like? And there the mother's hand did a thing Eufémia never forgot: it did not flee, did not draw back, stayed where it was, in the daughter's hand, but fell asleep, lost its strength and its sense on purpose, made itself warm stone, and the mouth said, she who, daughter, and the eyes went to the window, to the square, to far off, to a place where the question could not enter.

There was still the attempt of the portrait, which was the one that came nearest to going wrong. Eufémia took it from the box one morning of a good day, and left it, without saying anything, on the kitchen table, leaning against the sugar bowl, turned to the mother's chair: the girl of the plait, serious for the camera. And she waited, stirring the fire, with her heart in her mouth. The mother sat down, saw the portrait, and Eufémia, with her back turned, heard the silence change its quality, and turned slowly. Saudade was looking at the photograph without taking it up, with a smooth face, and what she said, at last, was: this is I, when young. Who fetched it for me? And it was not the illness speaking, or not only, and that was what froze the kitchen: it was a deception with fifty years of practice beneath it, a deception that knew the way. Eufémia put the portrait away without answering, and that night, in bed, she understood a thing that took her sleep: the mother and she had spent their lives in the same mirror, each one seeing inside it the wrong person. The mother looked at the living daughter and saw the dead one. And she looked at the dead one and saw herself. In that family no one had ever been seen by anyone, with a clean face, without overlay: they were three women in a row looking through one another, like panes of glass, and the first who managed to see someone, even once, broke the whole game.

Leal watched this without meddling, in his way. Only once, on seeing Eufémia give up another night, with the dishes in her hand, did he say, low, without raising his eyes from the small thing he had in his hands:

"One does not break down a door that has been shut fifty years. Either it opens from inside, or it does not open."

And Eufémia, who had already lied to her husband once and did not want to doubt him twice, heard the counsel and kept it, and stopped drawing. And it was, of course, when she stopped drawing that the door opened.

It opened on a Thursday at the end of March, at the beginning of the afternoon, with the south wind pushing high clouds and the first warmth of the year on the windowpanes. Eufémia had put the mother in the window chair, with sun on her knees, and had sat down herself beside her, with sewing: she was mending white linen, old pillowcases, and had on her lap the white thread and the pincushion. She did not choose the work on purpose. She never knew how to say, afterward, whether she had chosen it on purpose.

The mother was a long time silent, looking at the square. Then, without turning her face, with a voice that was not that of the bad hour nor that of the good days, a third voice, flat and clear as still water, she said:

"She did not get to finish the pillowcases."

Eufémia did not raise her head from the sewing. She knew, with an animal's certainty, that any movement was one movement too many, that this was like the birds that come to eat from the hand: to breathe was already a risk. She went on pulling the thread, slowly, and said, to the cloth:

"Which pillowcases, mother?"

"Hers. Of her dowry." The mother had her hands laid on her lap, very still, one inside the other. "She had the initials unfinished. I finished them afterward. It took me years. There is no slower embroidery than that, daughter, I would wish it on no one. Each stitch was a nail."

The south wind passed in the square and made a far shutter bang. Eufémia pulled another thread. The mother went on, with the same flat voice, without hurry, like one who dictates an entry:

"It was on a Sunday that she fell ill. She came from the fountain cold, in May, would you believe it, cold in May, and I scolded her, I even scolded her, that she went about with her head in the air with the City papers and did not wrap up. By the Tuesday she no longer knew her bed. The doctor came from away twice, your father went to fetch him at night with the mare borrowed from the Shop, and the doctor was young and was good, and there was nothing to be done, he said it to your father in the yard, I heard from the window, there are fevers that come with a name and an address, this one came so. Nine days. On the seventh she asked me to read her her lists, the things to take, and I read them to her, I read her all of them, and she corrected me two, she still corrected me, you take the alarm clock, mother, for in the City I wake with the bells. On the ninth she fell asleep in the middle of the morning and went with the day, by the window, for we had pulled her bed to the window, so that she would have the square."

"The village was good, in those nine days. That I never forgot." The flat voice made a pause, ordering the memory. "There was always a woman here in the house, with nothing arranged: they took turns, as in the watering. The Shop sent what was needed without anyone asking, and the old man of the shop, Benvinda's father, struck us off the ledger that whole month, he who struck nothing off for anyone, and it was never spoken of. And at the door, on the cobbles, they put straw." And as Eufémia did not understand, she explained, with an old patience: "Straw in the street, so that the ox-carts would make no noise in passing. It was done so, when there was grave illness in a house. The whole village treading lower because of a girl. Even today, when I see straw on the ground, it bends my heart."

The white thread stopped in the air, and Eufémia left it stopped. She no longer pretended to sew. The mother did not notice, or noticed and it no longer mattered: she was now in a place from which one tells, and the place had its laws, and the first was to go to the end.

"The burial was on a Friday morning. People came from both banks. I was seven months gone, it showed well by then, and I took your father on one side and my mother on the other, and at the churchyard door my mother stopped me and said in my ear the rule, which I already knew, everyone knew, but the rules are said in the ear in the hour, that is how it is done: a woman with child does not weep over a grave. The weeping calls. What you carry within hears everything, and if it hears you weep over the open earth, it is left called, and either it goes with the one you weep for, or it comes into this world with death already made its friend. So I did not weep." The hands on her lap pressed one against the other, slowly, with no drama at all, and it was the only gesture of the afternoon. "I stood over that grave which was the thing I most... I stood over that grave as one stands in a queue, daughter. With a composed face. The whole village weeping around me, your father undone, the other mothers hanging on me, and I dry, upright, with my hands so, holding you. Holding the weeping down, so that it would not rise, because if it rose, it called. You have no idea of the strength it takes not to make a mourning. To make one, anyone makes. It is not making one that takes a lifetime."

Outside passed the firewood man with his cart, and the sound of the wheels on the cobbles filled the kitchen a moment and went off. The mother breathed deep, and the voice went down one more tone, to the place where voices no longer tell, they confess:

"And at the edge of the earth, before they closed it, I made the promise. No one asked it of me. I put my hand on my belly, on you, and promised that the name would not die there. I thought it was love, daughter. In that hour, with all the weeping held in here forcing to come out somewhere, I thought it was the only thing I could do for her: not to let her end. And the weeping, since it could not come out by the eyes, came out by there. It came out by the promise. It was the only bit of mourning I made, and I made it into you, who were the place I had most to hand."

She fell silent. The sun had walked on her knees and was now on the floor, in a long lozenge. Eufémia, with the pillowcase forgotten on her lap, did not move. And then the mother turned her face, for the first time in that whole afternoon, and looked at her daughter, and her eyes were clean and present as they had not been in months, and she said the name.

"Eufémia," she said.

And it was not in calling. Eufémia understood it in the same instant, with her whole skin: the mother was not calling her. She was saying the other's name, for the first time in fifty years, aloud, in the light of day. She said it slowly, with its whole size, like one who sets down on the floor, bone by bone, a thing they have carried a whole lifetime on their back and that they no longer knew was a burden, so much was it their own.

"Eufémia. My first Eufémia." The voice did not break: it wore out, which is different. "How beautiful she was, daughter. How beautiful she was going to be."

The two of them stayed in the silence that followed, which was none of the old silences of the family: it was new, just born, and it was in the middle of the kitchen as a newcomer is, not knowing the arrangements, taking up space in a new way. Eufémia dared then what she never had: she set down the sewing, knelt beside the chair, as on the night of the linen, and asked, softly, with no accusation at all, only with all the rest:

"And I, mother? Where do I stand, in that story?"

The mother looked at her a long time. And the one who looked was still the present woman, the one with clean eyes, and one could see she was asking the question of herself in earnest, for the first time, with all the remnants of her lucidity gathered for that last work. And what she answered, she answered without begging pardon, without explaining herself, without a tear, with the tremendous and late honesty of those who have nothing more to lose nor to manage:

"You came after, and I never saw you arrive. I looked at you and saw her. My whole life, daughter. My whole life I looked at one and saw the other, and I called that taking care of you." She raised her small hand and laid it on the daughter's face, on the fifty-year-old face of the living daughter, and her eyes went over that face slowly, feature by feature, like one who reads a thing for the first time. "Let me see you," she said. "Let me see you, at least once, before this all goes out on me."

And she saw her. There, in the kitchen, at the end of a March afternoon, with the south wind on the panes, Saudade looked at the second daughter and saw her, the one who was, the one who had always been, and Eufémia let herself be seen, kneeling on the floor of her kitchen, with the mother's small and dry hand laid on her face, and there were no more words at all, because the ones there had been had all been needed to come there, and from then on what there was, was only this, which is not little, which is everything: two women looking at each other, in the light that remained, each one learning the other's face, in haste, slowly, before the day, outside, over the square and the dry fountain and the whole village of Véspera, finished going.

That night, in the back room, Eufémia tucked in her bedclothes and then stayed standing, in the dark, listening to her breathe. And she understood, at the door, before going out, what she had received that afternoon, and that it had not been a begging of pardon, because the mother had not begged, nor an explanation, because the mother had not explained. It had been an inheritance, the last and the first true one: the mother had handed her the mourning. She had handed it to her as one hands an unfinished work to one who has hands for it, the pillowcases unembroidered, the weeping unwept, the name unset down. Not out of cruelty, she understood it now once and for all, and not out of love, or not only: out of not having found, in fifty years, anywhere in the world a place where that fit, save inside the only person made to its measure.

The next morning, the mother remembered nothing.

Eufémia came down early, with her heart prepared for everything, and found her in the window chair on an in-between day of the common ones, scolding softly a sparrow that was on the sill. She looked at her daughter as she always looked, asked for the usual warm milk, asked after the hens that there were not. Of the previous afternoon, of the burial told, of the name said, of the hand on the face: nothing. The page had turned on its own during the sleep, as all of them turned now, and Eufémia served the milk with a deep and strange grief, that was not for the mother, it was for herself: the only witness of the greatest hour of her life no longer knew that the hour had existed. But on setting down the mug, the old woman did a small thing. She held the daughter's hand a moment, without reason, looked at her face with a clean attention, of a bird, and said, idly, pleased: you are here today. And she went back to the sparrow. And Eufémia stayed with that, which was neither memory nor was it forgetting, it was the remnant that stays when the two go: the mother no longer knew what she had said, but what she had said had been done. There are doors that, once opened, not even the fog shuts again.

Beneath the door of her and Leal's room one saw the line of light. Eufémia stayed a moment more in the dark corridor, between the two rooms, between what she had received and what she hid, with the house still around her and, in the chest of drawers, in the drawer, a comb of pale wood; and she said within, to no one, to the sister perhaps, trying for the first time how the name of the two sat in her living mouth: rest. Now I know the way. I will see to this, Eufémia. Leave it, I will see to this.
