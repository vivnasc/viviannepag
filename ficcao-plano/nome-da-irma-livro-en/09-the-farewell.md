## 9. The farewell

The idea of the farewell came neither from a book nor from a counsel, because for that there were no books in Véspera nor counsellors: it came from her office. It came on a school morning, in the middle of the first letters, when the little one of the Serranos finally finished her page of crooked and alive e's and asked, with the slate in the air, what one did now. And Eufémia answered what she had answered for thirty years without ever hearing what she said: now one finishes. One puts the name, one puts the date, and one tidies it away. A work is only done when one puts the name to it and tidies it away.

She was left with the phrase in her hand the rest of the morning, as she had been left with the he of the story of the kerchief. That was what the sister lacked, fifty years ago: no one had put the name to her and tidied the work away. The burial had been made, the earth had been closed, but what followed had been no tidying-away at all: it had been a hiding of a notebook in the chest, an embroidery by nails, a whole life of an open page. The mother, now, had said the name: the page had the name put to it. It remained to tidy it away. And to tidy away, in her family, no one knew how; but she knew. To tidy away was the talent of the house facing the square, her small and despised gift, and Eufémia decided that morning, among slates, that she was going to use it once in her life for a thing of the right size.

The words she wrote of an evening, alone, and it was the first time she used the new writing for a thing in earnest. She had practised it until then in drafts she tore up, in loose names, in trial phrases; now she set a clean sheet before her and wrote on it what she wanted to say to the sister, slowly, with the hand still unsure on the high E and the upright t's, crossing out and beginning again, and at a certain point she understood what she was doing and had to stop and look at the sheet: she was writing to the sister, for the first time, in a writing the sister had never had in her hand. All her life, any note of hers could have been written by the dead one, so alike was it. This one not. This one was, of all the things Eufémia would carry up the slope, the only one that came whole from a single person, and it was for that that she decided to read it aloud before the grave, though she knew the words by heart: because the sheet itself was the proof of life, the first, and the dead, the old women say, do not read papers, they hear voices.

She prepared the rest as she prepared the beginning of the school year: with a list. She sat of an evening with a paper and wrote, in her round writing: the notebook. The words. The day. Who goes. And she stayed looking at the who goes a long time.

To ask Leal to go with her was the hardest thing she did that year, harder than to open the letter, harder than to speak with the mother, and she never knew quite how to explain why, or knew and did not want to: because to ask that was to tell him I know, and to say I know was to show that she had been knowing in silence, with all that that dragged with it of doors and of piers. She chose the Saturday night, the kitchen tidied, he with his small things by the lamp. She set before him a bowl of tea he had not asked for, sat down, and said, with the most common voice she could fabricate:

"Tomorrow I would like to go up, to the east. To take some words to my sister." And as he stayed motionless, with the lock forgotten in his hands, she added, slowly, putting each word in its place as he put the tiles: "I would like you to come with me. To come with me this time."

The this time stayed between the two, on the table of press wood, saying all that was left to say. Leal set down the lock. He looked at his own hands a moment, those hands that never erred and that had been caught, at the end of fifty years, by a woman behind a cypress. When he raised his eyes, there was in them neither guilt nor relief: there was a thing more ancient, a lad's seriousness, and Eufémia saw for a second, with all clarity, the young man of the letter, the one of the windows chosen on purpose.

"I will go," he said. And nothing more, in his way. But when she was already getting up, he added, low: "I will take the flowers. I know where the first lilacs are, by the mill. She... " The phrase hesitated, fifty years of silence forcing against half a dozen words, and the words won: "She liked the lilacs better. The jonquils are because they come first."

There was missing from the list a line that Eufémia wrote and crossed out three times: the mother. To take the mother was impossible, the slope was no longer for her legs, and there was the greater fear, that of the journey upsetting for good the little that remained. But to go without telling her was not either, for secrets in that house were no longer made. So, on the Saturday afternoon, in a bit of a good day, Eufémia sat beside the window chair and said, simply: mother, tomorrow I am going up, to her grave. I am going to take her the notebook and some words. The mother was silent so long that Eufémia thought the phrase had been lost in the fog. But then the small hand sought hers on the blanket, and pressed it, and Saudade said, without taking her eyes off the square, with a firmness that came from the depths of the years:

"Tell her it was I who finished the pillowcases." And after a moment, lower: "It took me, but I finished them. She began everything and finished nothing, that head in the air. Tell her. She will understand."

And this was the message Eufémia carried up the slope, the first message between the two in fifty years, and she went thinking, on the way, that it was a mother's message like all the messages of the mothers of that land: on the outside it was about linen, and within it said rest, for I finished what was left unfinished of you, and it cost me my life, and I did it with love.

They went on the Sunday, at the beginning of the afternoon, the two of them by the eastern way, and the village saw them go and knew where they were going and no one asked anything, and for once Eufémia was grateful to that land for its genius of knowing everything in silence.

She carried the brown notebook in the cloth bag from the time of the course. She had taken it from the box in the morning, and had put in the box, in its place, the new copy of the entry, hers, the one asked for by her, and that silent exchange at the bottom of the wardrobe seemed to her, without knowing how to explain it, a right thing: the papers of the death tidied away, the living work out here, in the sun.

They went up slowly, at her pace, and Leal carried the lilacs wrapped in damp paper, with the care with which one carries a child in one's arms. At the iron gate, which creaked, he did a small thing she kept forever: he stopped half a second, took off his cap, and waited for her to pass first. In fifty years of visits, Eufémia understood, he had always been the only one, the owner of the custom, the nameless guardian of that bed. And on that Sunday, without a word, he was handing over the house: from then on, the one who went first was she.

Before the grave, what had to be done was done, and it was done slowly.

Leal changed the water, arranged the lilacs, gathered the withered jonquils of the week to the foot of the box. Eufémia waited for his hands to finish, and then took the notebook from the bag and said, aloud, in the empty cemetery and in the sun, the words she had written and learned by heart, because the important words, in her school, were written first and said after:

"I bring you a message from mother," she began, because the messages, in Véspera, go ahead of everything. "She sends word that it was she who finished the pillowcases. That it took her, but she finished them. She says you will understand." And she waited a moment, as one waits for a message to arrive, and only then passed to her words: "And I have come to give you back a thing that went with me fifty years without my knowing." The voice came out firm, classroom voice, and she let it go so, for it was hers. "I did not know you. They did not let me know you, or did not know how, it comes to the same. But you are within my writing, and your house was mine, and your name served me my whole life. I do not come to give it back, for I no longer know how to live without it: I come to give you back the rest. The notebook is yours. The course was yours. The first of the village was going to be you."

And she opened the notebook at the first page, and read. She read the heading underlined twice, *To take to the City*, and the whole list, item by item, the stockings, the books, the alarm clock, with her best reading-to-the-room voice, the voice that made the little ones raise their heads. Beside her, Leal stood with his cap in his hand and his eyes on the ground, very upright, and the wind stirred the lilacs.

And it was in the middle of the list that Eufémia heard herself. She heard herself reading as she read the minutes: round, even, sure as the hours, composed, without a break, doing well, once more, in the deepest hour of her life, the task that had been set before her. Even there, even in that, doing it in order. Even in the farewell of the sister she was being the diligent girl of the brown notebook, and the reading came out so perfect that it seemed, she understood with horror, that it seemed rehearsed by another, and she stopped in the middle of a line, with the alarm clock unread, not knowing how to go on.

It was Leal who noticed the silence and raised his eyes. And what he said, he said low, without coming near, with an exactness that only his hands usually had:

"Your sister never came up here to weep for anyone." And after a moment: "That part is yours. Do it as you wish, for no one taught it to you."

And Eufémia closed the notebook, and wept.

She wept badly, it was the first thing she knew of her own weeping: she wept in jolts, with no knack at all, with sobs that bent her in the middle and made her cling to her husband's arm, an ugly weeping, ungoverned, of one who never trained, and she understood, in the midst of it, with an astonishment that thickened it still more, that she was weeping for the first time since she knew not when, perhaps since she was a girl, and that she did not weep only for the sister: she wept by association all that was left unwept in that line of dry women, she wept the mother over the grave with the weeping held forcing, she wept the girl of the lists who woke with the bells, she wept for herself, for the one of the plait and the notebook, for the one who never chose anything, and the weeping came out all together, mixed, with no order at all, it the first work of her life that she did with no order at all, and perhaps for that, she thought much later, perhaps for that it was the first that came out well done.

Leal held her the whole time, without a word, as one holds a ladder.

When it ended, the sun was low and she had a swollen and light face, light as she had never felt it. She set the open notebook on the stone, by the jug, slowly, and then changed her mind and took it up again: not yet. She would find it its right place, and the stone, in the rain, was not it. But the first page was read, and the name was set down, and the work, for the first time in fifty years, was on its way to being tidied away.

They went down with the end of the afternoon. At the way out, the gate creaked for the two of them, and Eufémia went on her husband's arm, a thing they never did, of that generation that does not go about holding each other in the street, and the village that saw them go down so, she dishevelled and he serious, knew, as the village knew everything, that something had at last been tidied away in the village above, and the old women of the square, on seeing them pass, did not say the very image, said nothing, and one of them, the oldest, nodded, slowly, yes.

In the square, on arriving, Benvinda was at the door of the Shop gathering the fruit crates, and did what the whole village was doing at that hour behind the shutters: she saw them. She saw Eufémia's swollen face, the linked arm, the cloth bag, and said neither good afternoon nor asked anything. She went into the shop and came out again before they finished crossing, with a sprig of rosemary in her hand, of the good kind, the kind she sent to the sick, and held it out to Eufémia in silence, in passing, like one who gives change that is due. It was the Shop's way of saying what there was to say. Eufémia carried the rosemary home against her chest, and would put it in a glass of water in the kitchen, and its smell stayed that whole week, mixed with the rest, and years later that Sunday still came back to her so: by the smell of the rosemary, which is the smell of what is done without words.

At home, the mother was awake in the chair, on a remnant of a good day. She looked at her daughter's swollen face and did not ask where they had gone, because she knew, because all Véspera knew everything forever. She asked another thing, softly, with a girl's shyness, the only question that mattered to her and that she had awaited for fifty years for someone to ask it of:

"Was she beautiful?"

"She was, mother," said Eufémia. "She has fresh flowers and the name painted fresh. She is tended like the best on the slope. She always was."

The mother nodded slowly and turned her face to the window, to the square, and stayed so a long time, and whatever passed within her did not come out. Only at the end, when Eufémia was already going to put her blanket on her, did she say, to the pane, in a thread of voice where, for the second time that spring, the two whole daughters fit, but now each in her place:

"Good. Good that you go there now. They never let me go to weep, and to go dry I never could. " And she shut her eyes, and added, already half asleep, the phrase Eufémia carried with her from there for all the years she lived: "Now they may. Now the two of them may."
