## 4. The swapped name

The winter settled into the house facing the square like the mother: slowly, for a while, until it should be right again.

The house learned to be a house of three. It had its new clock, which was not the bell's but the back room's: the morning was measured by Saudade's waking, the afternoon by her sleep in the chair by the window, the night by the door ajar at the right angle, neither open nor shut. At the school, the little ones were on the big letters and the numbers up to ten, and Eufémia gave the lessons with half an ear on the door, awaiting a word that never came and could always come, which is the way of being of one who has an old person at home: the body in the room, the ear on the square. Eufémia went to the school and came back at the long break to set her at table; Leal, who was retired from the bulk of the trade but not from his hands, made, without anyone asking him, a new handrail for the corridor, to the measure of the mother-in-law's small hand, and sanded it to smoothness, and it was never spoken of again, which was his way of doing the big things.

There were good days. On the good days, Saudade scolded her daughter about the seasoning of the soup, ruled the kitchen seated, finger in the air, and told small things of sixty years ago with the sharpness of washed glass: the price of linen in the year of the marriage, the temper of her mother-in-law, a quarrel of neighbours in the time of the Floods. Eufémia brought her a sprig of rosemary from the Shop, with Benvinda's message, and the mother rubbed the needles between her fingers, smelled, and gave the verdict: Benvinda knows rosemary, she does, what she does not know is credit, and she laughed with the old laugh, and on that day one would say nothing was the matter.

On one of those good days she told the story of the kerchief, which Eufémia had never heard. It was apropos of nothing, as things came now: the old woman by the window, the winter sun on her face, and suddenly the time of the linen, when the girls went to bleach the cloth at the river and the bank was left white as though snowed. And there was a year, she told, when the wind took the kerchief off the head of one of them, and the kerchief went through the air, and alighted on the water, and went off downriver very straight, very sure of itself, as though it knew the way, and all the girls running along the bank shouting and laughing, after the kerchief, until the bend of the river. And there the story ended. Who caught the kerchief, asked Eufémia, fearfully, because the mother was laughing, and the mother never laughed like that. The one who was to catch it caught it, said Saudade, with a remnant of laughter and her eyes shut to the sun, he had the legs for it, that is what they were for. And she fell asleep in the middle of the next sentence, and Eufémia was left with that he in her hands the rest of the afternoon, not knowing whether she had just heard a story of the father or of a lad with a comb in his pocket, and without the courage to wake the mother for a question that, awake, she would no longer know how to answer.

And there were the other days. On the other days, the mother lost all the needles. She asked after the hens of the old house, which had not existed for ten years, and one had to tell her that they were seen to. She put the slippers by the fire to warm for Bendito, who came cold from the fields, and one had to leave them there until she forgot them, because to take them away was worse. One afternoon, Eufémia found her unmaking the made bed, sheet by sheet, with method, her brow furrowed with the look of one who fulfils an overdue obligation.

"Mother, the bed was changed yesterday."

"It has to be changed again." The small hands did not stop, they knew the trade by heart, folded the sheet against the chest in even pleats. "People are coming."

"People are coming? Who is coming, mother?"

The mother stopped then, with the sheet embraced, and looked at her daughter with a sincere astonishment, almost offended, as though the question were on purpose.

"Well, who would it be?" she said. "The one who is to come."

And she went back to the sheet, and there was no way to get more out of her, neither that day nor after, and the bed was made and unmade three times that afternoon, for the one who was to come, were she a forgotten visitor, a child of fifty years ago, or another thing that the house knew and did not say.

There was also the afternoon when she asked to go home. She was sitting in Eufémia's kitchen, in her chair, with her blanket on her knees, and she began to gather the ends of the blanket like one who prepares herself, and said, with the politeness of a visitor, that it was getting late and that she wanted to go home. The daughter explained that she was home. The mother listened with patience, agreed with everything, and asked again, lower, to be taken home. Eufémia learned that day, at her own cost, that the home the old ask for is not a dwelling: she took her by the arm for a turn around the square, slowly, in the dry cold, and when they came back in by the same door the mother sighed with relief and said, on settling in the corridor, that after all it was good to be back. They came from home to home, by the distance of a square. There are returns like that, Eufémia would think later, that are not made on the outside.

With the names, the illness had a special dealing. It ate them in its own order.

First were the far ones: the new neighbour, the man who brought the firewood. Then, the middle ones. One night, at supper, Saudade looked at Leal, who was reaching her the bread, and Eufémia saw her search, saw the search in her face, an anxious rummaging in drawers in the dark, and the mother gave up and asked, turned to her daughter, in a murmur of one who asks a favour:

"Your man... what is he called now?"

"Leal, mother. He is called Leal."

"Leal." The old woman repeated the name looking at him, and something in there must have fitted, because she nodded slowly, with a deep approval, and said: "That name always knew how to wait."

And she ate her soup, at ease, without noticing the silence she had put on the table. Leal stayed a moment with the bread in his hand. Then he set it down beside her plate, broken in small pieces, as she liked it, and said the soup was good, and the supper went on.

Later, in the tidied kitchen, with the mother already in bed, Eufémia found him standing by the fire, the dish-cloth forgotten on his shoulder, looking at the embers.

"Pay no mind," she said. "Today the names all fled her."

"I know." Leal took the cloth from his shoulder, folded it in three, hung it on the rail, all with the slowness of one who is elsewhere. "It is not that."

"Then?"

"It is that she knows more than she remembers," he said. And he went off to bed, and did not explain, and Eufémia stayed washing what was already washed, thinking that in that house, lately, everyone knew more than they said, beginning with her. But she came to know that night, for certain, one thing: the mother's head, in coming undone, did not come undone at random. It disarranged the upper drawers and left more and more in sight what had been fifty years at the bottom.

Outside, in the village, the mother's illness had its protocol, and Eufémia learned it quickly. Wherever she went, to the Shop, to the churchyard, at the school's end, there was the question: and your mother, how is she. It was not curiosity, or not only: it was the village doing what the village does, passing the illness from hand to hand as one passes a weight, so that no one should carry it alone. To the question one answered she gets by, and to the gets by one answered well she does, it is the age, and the weight was passed on. But sometimes the choreography brought one more step. One afternoon, at the school door, one of the little ones' mothers, a good woman, held her by the arm and said, with all the kindness in the world:

"At least she has you. You were always the best of her daughters."

And she went off consoled, leading her child by the hand, without knowing what she had said. The best of her daughters. In the singular there was no phrase: there was only one daughter. It was in the plural that the village spoke without noticing, in the hidden plural of its praises, and Eufémia stayed at the school door watching her go, thinking that the whole village was like the mother: it knew perfectly what it had forgotten, and forgot it every day with much labour.

Her own name, the mother never failed. It was the name she knew best. And it was for that that, when it happened, there was no mistake possible.

It was on a Thursday in January, at the bad hour. Eufémia had already learned the bad hour: the end of the afternoon, the light lowering, the hour when the old who get lost get lost more, as though the dark entered them before it entered the houses. At that hour the mother grew restless, asked for vague things, went and came within herself. Eufémia lit the lamps early, because of that. She was lighting them when the mother called her from the window chair.

"Eufémia. Come here."

And it was not the usual voice. The usual voice asked, or ordered, or complained. This was a low and tight voice, urgent, a voice of a secret between girls, and when Eufémia came near, the mother gripped her wrist with her small hand, with a strength she should no longer have had, and pulled her down, near her face, and what she said, she said very fast, with lively eyes, watching the door:

"Do not tell your father I told you. But do not go yet. The City does not run away. Go after the festival, go next month. Who helps me with the linen, if you go? Your dowry is nearly done, I am finishing your pillowcases, I embroidered your letter this week, it came out so well. And the lad..." The voice lowered still more, to almost nothing, and her eyes shone as the eyes of girls and of old women shine, which is the same shine at the two ends. "The lad is mending the house of the square for you. Mind that no one knows. See whether you can wait for him, for that one waits for you."

The room was warm and the soup boiled on the fire and outside, in the square, someone passed with a basket, slowly, in the sure life of six in the afternoon. And Eufémia, her wrist held in her mother's hand, was for a long time in two places at the same time, and in one of them she was a woman of fifty in a heated kitchen, and in the other she was a girl of nineteen with an exam set and a notebook of lists, a girl whom the mother asked in secret not to go yet, to stay one more month, to help with the linen, and the girl was going to die soon and neither of the two knew it, neither the one who asked nor the one who was there again, her wrist held, fifty years later, with no way at all to warn anyone.

Eufémia heard herself answer. She heard her own voice do what her voice knew how to do better than anything: what was needed.

"I will stay, mother," she said. "I will not go yet. I will stay for the linen."

The mother let go of her wrist. The urgency went out of her face all at once, as a lamp goes out, and in its place there came a total rest, of a child heard, and Saudade leaned back in the chair, shut her eyes, and said, already half asleep, with a thread of voice that served both daughters and chose neither:

"You were always a good daughter. You were always the best of all."

She fell asleep there, with an open hand on the arm of the chair. Eufémia stayed kneeling beside her longer than was needed, looking at her mother's open hand, and then got up with the care of one who leaves the side of a cradle, went to the fire, stirred the soup that did not need it, and wept she did not weep, it was a short dry thing that crossed her face and passed, because there was the supper, and the bad hour ends when the lamps are all lit, and hers already were.

At supper the mother was well, even funny. There was left in her no trace of the window.

"How was the day?" asked Leal, later, when the two of them stayed in the tidied kitchen.

"It was mild," said Eufémia.

And she did not lie, she said only half the day, and she noticed, in saying it, that she was making a collection of those halves, and that she kept them all in the same place, with the tin box, behind the linen. What she did not tell Leal was already enough to fill a drawer of the wardrobe. They were not lies. They were weights she measured alone first, she told herself, so as not to set them upon him without knowing how much they weighed. The mother, thought Eufémia, wiping the clean table, the mother must have begun so too.

Before shutting up the house, she went up to the room and did a thing she had not done since she was a girl: she looked at the bedclothes. The pillowcase of her side had, embroidered in the corner, small, old, washed by a thousand winters, a letter. An E of white thread on white linen, of that embroidery that is felt more than it is seen. Eufémia had known that letter forever: it was the E of her dowry, the dowry the mother had handed her ready on the day of the wedding, sheets, pillowcases, towels, all marked, all in order, all kept for years awaiting her. Awaiting her. Eufémia passed her finger over the E, slowly, and did the sum she had never done: at what age had the mother embroidered that letter, on what evening, for the bed of what house, for the body of what daughter. An E serves any Eufémia. A dowry is not embroidered twice. She had slept her whole life, she understood, with her face upon the letter of her sister, and called it, with all the innocence in the world, my dowry, as she called my name the name and my life the life.

It was that night that the idea came. Her ideas came to her as all things came to her, slowly and in order, but this one came from outside, came from the square.

Eufémia had put out the kitchen and was going to close the shutters when she looked, out of a habit of thirty years, at the square in the dark. The dry fountain, in the middle, composed. The shut houses. And at the door of the House of the Register, with a lantern set on the step, the Keeper, at that hour, turning the big key. It was not the hour for locking up: the House shut at the end of the afternoon, with the day. But there she was, in no hurry at all, shutting or opening, one could not tell, and to Eufémia it made no difference, because what stayed with her was not the doubt, it was the key. The sound that was not heard from there, and that she heard all the same: the sound of a deep lock, of a house where all the papers sleep, the right ones and the others, those of who was born and those of who died, hers and the other's, side by side, in the same writing, waiting for fifty years for someone to come and ask for them.

The Keeper picked the lantern off the step and went off, without looking at any window.

Eufémia closed the shutters. She went up, lay down beside her warm husband, listened to the breathing of the house: Leal on one side, the mother at the end, the wind over them all. The idea lay down with her, and it was simple, and was said in half a dozen words: to go to the House of the Register and ask for the papers. Hers and the other's. To set them side by side on the counter, in the light of day, with the Keeper for a witness, and to read with her own eyes what the whole village had read for fifty years over her head. She did not know what for. She did not know what changed by seeing them together, two old papers with the same writing, nor what question she would carry in her mouth when she asked for them. She knew only that it was there the thing went on, whatever the thing was, and that she had spent her life going into that house by the door of the right papers, and that there was another door.

She did not tell the idea to anyone, neither that night nor the following ones, and in that, at least, she knew well what she was doing and from whom she had learned it: in Véspera, the important ideas too are kept beneath the linen, until the day a woman opens the chest.
