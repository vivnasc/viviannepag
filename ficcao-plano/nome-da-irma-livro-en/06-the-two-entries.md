## 6. The two entries

The House of the Register smelled of old paper and of wax, and the smell did not change with the seasons, as the smells do not change of the things that are above time. Eufémia went in on a Saturday morning, with the papers of the old house in a folder under her arm, and the big door shut behind her with the deep sound she had heard from her window, weeks before, without hearing it.

Within, the House was larger than it seemed from the square. Shelves to the ceiling, and on the shelves the books, spine against spine, each the size of a table of generations: the births, the marriages, the deaths, by years, by houses, by streets. The whole village was there, lying down, in the writing of various hands, and Eufémia, standing in the middle, with her folder of a living woman, felt what all who stand there feel: that that was the other Véspera, the one that does not lunch nor quarrel, the one that stays.

She was not alone, at first. At the long table at the back, an old man from the lower fields was seeing to a division of property, with three papers before him and stubbornness set across his brow, and Eufémia waited her turn seated on the long bench of the entrance, as one waits in serious houses. She heard, without wishing to hear, the end of his conversation: that his father had always said the mill plot was for him, that the whole village knew it, that it was arranged. And she heard the Keeper answer, with the stone patience of her office, the phrase Eufémia took home for free, on top of the one she had come to fetch: what is arranged is not written, and what is not written, when those who arranged it fall silent for good, is left worth what the wind is worth. The old man went off, grumbling, with his papers in his hand, and there stayed in the air of the House, for a moment, the dust of all the things in Véspera that were arranged and not written, which were almost all of them, which were whole lives.

The Keeper went back then to the register she was copying out fair, and made a sign to Eufémia to come near. She did not raise her head at once, and Eufémia was grateful: it gave her time to set down the folder and to compose herself for the first half of the visit, the half true and lying, the papers of the old house.

They saw to that as one sees to those things. The house of the street behind the churchyard, in Saudade's name, widow of Bendito; the only daughter and heir; what needed to be entered now and what was only entered later, for the papers of houses, said the Keeper, always go a step behind the people and a step ahead of them, and the office is not to let them flee nor run people over. She had her usual voice, neither young nor old, and the cleanest hands Eufémia had ever seen, hands of one who handles what cannot be soiled. She checked the papers of the folder one by one, noted what was missing, wrote on a strip of paper the two things Eufémia still had to bring. Her writing was small and upright, with no ornament at all, a writing of a key.

"Anything more?" she asked then, putting away the strip.

And there was the door, opened with all simplicity, and Eufémia, who had brought the question rehearsed from home in three different fashions, used none of them.

"I would like to see my sister's entry," she said.

The Keeper did not ask what sister. She did not make the face of one who finds it strange, nor the worse one, of one who has long been waiting. She got up as though she had been asked for a weight of sugar, went to the middle shelf, ran her finger over the spines without hesitating, because her office was to know where each name slept, and came back with two books. Not one. Two.

She set down the first, opened it at the mark her fingers found at the first try, and turned it to Eufémia. Then she opened the second, further on, and set it down beside it. And she drew back a step, to leave the reader alone with the reading, for that too was office.

They were side by side, the two entries, in the light of the morning that came high through the windows of the House.

*Eufémia, daughter of Bendito and of Saudade.* And in the other book, with twenty winters of pages between them: *Eufémia, daughter of Bendito and of Saudade.* The same writing. Eufémia knew it would be the same writing, she had known it weeks before, in the old house, with the copy in her hand; but one thing is to know it and another is to see the two books open on the same table, the birth of one page and the birth of the other, written by the same hand of an old Keeper, with the same ink, with the same care, as though the village had ordered the same girl twice and the second order had come out right, without flaw, to replace the one that broke.

"Who was she?" asked Eufémia. She had not planned the question; it came out of the depths, ill-mannered from being so straight. "The Keeper of that time. The one who wrote this."

"The one who taught me," said the Keeper. "She wrote better than I do. She is over there." And she pointed with her chin, without ceremony, to one of the shelves at the back, where there would be, in one of the books of the deaths, the entry of the woman who had entered the two Eufémias. "We all stay here, sooner or later. It is the rest of this office: one knows the shelf."

Eufémia went back to the books. She read her sister's entry slowly, word by word, though she knew the copy by heart: the spring, the fever, the nine days, the nineteen years. And she read hers, which she had never read so, with both eyes at the same time: the winter of the great waters, the daughter of Bendito and of Saudade, and in the margin, in a tighter writing, the note that the entries sometimes carry and that she had never known hers carried: *She was given the sister's name, by the mother's wish.*

By the mother's wish. Four words in the margin of a book, fifty years ago, in sight of anyone who would go and read, and no one had ever told her, and she had never gone to read. The whole village knowing, the schoolmistress swallowing the since, the old women with the very image, and the margin of the book always there, still, waiting, with the infinite patience of paper. Eufémia laid her finger on the note, lightly, as she had laid her finger on the embroidered E of the pillowcase, and understood that she had been doing that for months: touching with one finger, over the top, the written things of her life by other hands.

"The entries say who was born and who died," said the Keeper, from her place, without coming near. "Who married, who was left of whom. The rest they do not say. They do not say who lived what. For the rest there is no book."

"There ought to be."

"There are those who go about making them," said the Keeper, and did not explain, and Eufémia did not understand, and only much later, in another year, would she remember the phrase.

Before asking for what she had come to ask, she did one more thing, which she had not planned: she asked to see her marriage. The Keeper went to fetch the book of the year, without asking anything, and opened it at the entry, and there it was: Eufémia, daughter of Bendito and of Saudade, with Leal, son of the carpenter, the date, the witnesses, the writing of the Keeper of that time, which was already another, more slanted. And there it was, at the bottom, her own signature, as a bride, twenty-five years ago: round, even, sure as the hours. Eufémia looked at her bride's signature a long time, as she had looked at the sister's portrait in the old house, with that recognition that does not recognise: the mouth was her mouth, the brow was her brow, and the whole was another person. She had signed her own life with the writing of the brown notebook. All the papers that were worth anything in the world said that she was she, and they were all signed by the other's hand, practised on hers. If proof were ever needed, she thought, with a fine and ridiculous shiver, there was none: there existed in no book, on no paper of Véspera, a single line written by her true hand, because her true hand had never been let exist.

She closed the book of the marriage slowly, and asked then for what she had come to ask from the beginning, and that only there, with the books open, she knew how to say:

"I want a copy. Of hers."

"Your mother asked for one, in her time," said the Keeper. "It is in your keeping, I reckon."

"It is. I want another." Eufémia searched for the words, and the ones that came shamed her and she said them all the same: "That one was the mother's. I want one that is mine. Asked for by me."

The Keeper looked at her, then, for the first time in the whole visit, a long look with no astonishment at all, and Eufémia felt herself read from head to foot like a page, and it was not unpleasant, and that was what was strange.

"It is the best reason I have been asked for one this year," was all the woman said.

And she made the copy there, before her, and Eufémia watched. She saw the Keeper choose the sheet, set the ruler, wet the pen, and write the sister's name from beginning to end, word by word, in the small and upright writing of the time of now. It was the third hand to write that name on the papers of the world. The first had written it twice, on a birth and on a death. The second, the mother's, had embroidered it on a dowry and kept silent fifty years. The third wrote it on a Saturday morning, without hurry, for a sister who had never said it aloud save to a kitchen in the dark. When she finished, the Keeper dried the ink with the blotter, folded the sheet in four, and handed it over, and Eufémia received it with both hands, as one receives the bread.

While the Keeper put away the books on the shelves, Eufémia stayed a moment looking at the whole House, with the copy in her hands, and dared the question that had been on her mind since the long bench of the entrance:

"Does it not weigh on you?" And as the other turned, she explained, with a gesture around: "This. The whole village in here, the living and the others, all the dates, all the margins. To know what you know of everyone. I carry one sister and I go about as I go about. You carry the whole earth."

The Keeper finished fitting the book into the middle shelf before answering, and answered with her back turned, straightening spines, in the tone of one who speaks of the weather:

"I carry nothing. That is the difference, and it took me years to learn it, for this is not taught, it is suffered." She turned, and leaned against the shelf, arms crossed, the first posture of rest Eufémia had seen in her. "I keep. To keep is not to carry. What is carried goes with us everywhere, on our back, and one bends. What is kept stays in its place, in order, and one knows where it is and goes there when it is needed. Your family, if you ask me, carried for fifty years what they should have kept: they went about with your sister on their back everywhere, in the plait, in the writing, in the dowry, in you, instead of putting her in her place, with her name in sight." She shrugged slowly. "It is not the temper of your house alone. It is the temper of this land. The whole village confuses the two things, and then is astonished to go about bent."

She was going out, already with the big door on her shoulder, when she turned. The question had stayed with her from the old copy, from the margins of the books, from all the still things waiting for a reader.

"Her grave," she said. "Who tends the grave? It is not the mother, for the mother does not go out. I know someone tends it."

The Keeper had gone back to her register, at the back table, and answered without raising her head, with the tremendous naturalness of one who says an obvious thing:

"Have you never been there?"

It was no answer at all and it was the whole answer. Eufémia stayed a moment with her hand on the big door, learning the whole lesson that fit in those four words: that the village knew she had never been there, as it knew the rest; that the grave had someone who went, and that the name of who went was also in no book; and that there were questions that were not answered out of respect, to oblige the one who asks to go and see with their feet.

At home, at midday, Leal had the table laid and the mother already seated, on an in-between day, folding and unfolding the napkin. Eufémia came in with the folder and with the face of one who comes from far, and he looked at her over the dish, a single look, of inventory, and asked nothing in front of the old woman. Only later, at the sink, with the mother already in the window chair, did he say, low, handing her a bowl:

"Did you get it done, at the House?"

"I got done what I went to get done." Eufémia wiped the bowl slowly. "And I brought back more than I took."

"That the House of the Register does," said Leal, and did not ask what, and she understood, by the way he scrubbed the pot that was already scrubbed, that her husband knew she went by new paths, and that he was, in his way, bearing the wish to ask her whether she needed company. She did not need it. She did, but not yet. And he waited, because to wait was what that name knew how to do.

She had crossed the square, before that, with the copy folded in four in the pocket of her coat, against her chest. Across the way, in her kitchen window, there was movement: Leal, for certain, putting the water on for midday. To the right, on the eastern slope, above the rooftops, one saw the low wall of the cemetery, with its cypresses black and upright, at the distance of always, in the small village where everything had been near everything for five hundred years. Eufémia stopped by the dry fountain and stayed looking at the wall as though it had just been built that morning.

Fifty years, she thought. Fifteen minutes from home, on foot, slowly. For fifty years that had been there, and she had gone to all the burials of the village, like everyone, and had passed all the graves, like everyone, and never, not once, had her feet made on their own the detour of three rows that needed making. It had not been a prohibition: no one had forbidden her anything. It had been another thing, deeper and better made, an invisible fence set so early that she had grown up thinking it was the natural shape of the ground.

On Sunday, she decided, by the fountain. And this time the decision did not come from outside, did not come by word nor by being arranged: it came from her, whole, with all the fear included in the price.

The fountain, dry as always, composed as always, said nothing. But Eufémia, on taking up her step home, did a thing she had never done in fifty years of the square: in passing, she laid her hand on the stone rim, lightly, as one lays a hand on an old animal, and the stone was warm from the February sun, and beneath the stone, deep, in the dark of the earth, there would still be the old water that had one day known the way there, and had lost it, or forgotten it, or was only, who knows, waiting for someone to remember to ask it.
