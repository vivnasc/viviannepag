## 5. The chorus of mothers

There were in Véspera, as everywhere, many houses emptied of grown children, and many women of Custódia's age living the same passage, that of the nest that empties, which is one of the most silent and least spoken of mournings, because it is not a mourning in earnest, no one has died, the children are alive and well, and so society does not give mothers of the empty nest the right to the mourning it gives them when someone dies; they are expected to be content, that the children grew, that everything went well, and the mother who suffers with the empty nest suffers often in silence, ashamed to suffer, because she feels she has no right to a pain over a thing that is, after all, the success of her mission. And Custódia, on coming to know these other women, went on understanding that she was not alone in that mourning without a name, that many lived it, each in her own way, and that perhaps it was for being a mourning without a name and without a right that so many lost themselves in it, because a mourning that cannot be wept in the light of day rots in the dark, and Custódia, like so many, had let hers rot, turning it, in her case, into vigil.

Custódia was not the only mother in Véspera whose children had grown and departed, and she knew others, women of her age with the house emptied, and it was in seeing them, each in her own way before the same empty nest, that Custódia began, without knowing it, to see herself, like one who sees themselves in a mirror they did not ask for.

They were three ways of answering the same question, the question the empty nest asked them all: what do I make of myself, now that I am no longer needed full-time? And each answered in her own way, and each answer was a path, and Custódia, in seeing them, saw the possible paths before her, like one who comes to a crossroads and sees where the roads lead by the example of those who have already taken them. Felizarda's road led to life; Idália's, to a slow death; and hers, that of the vigil, to a place that was neither one thing nor the other, a place of suspension, of waiting, of eternal watch, which was perhaps the worst of the three, because Felizarda at least lived and Idália at least felt, and she, Custódia, neither lived nor allowed herself to feel, she only kept watch, suspended between a life she refused and a pain she did not weep, mounting guard over an empty post so as not to have to do either.

There was Felizarda, who had freed herself. Felizarda had raised her children like Custódia, given them everything, and when they grew and departed Felizarda did a thing that scandalised half the village and that Custódia, at first, thought almost a betrayal: she began to live. She set to travelling, Felizarda, to going to places she had always wanted to see and never could because of the children; she got involved in things, made new friendships, found occupations, and went about more alive and more joyful than in the years when she had been only a mother. And there were those in Véspera who censured her, oh Felizarda, now that the children are gone she goes about enjoying herself, she ought to have more sense, a mother is a mother; and Custódia, at first, fell in with that censure, thought Felizarda frivolous, a mother who had let go of the mission as soon as she could. But with time, on seeing Felizarda happy, on hearing her speak of her travels with shining eyes, Custódia began to feel, without confessing it, a prick of envy, because Felizarda, freed, was a living woman, and she, on guard, was a woman wasting away, and Felizarda, frivolous or not, slept at night, and she did not.

Felizarda had not always been so, and it was that that gave Custódia most to think about. Felizarda told her, one day, that she too, when the children departed, had had a bad time, that she too had had her nights of empty house and of not knowing what to do with herself, that the empty nest had hurt her as it hurts all. The difference is that Felizarda, at the end of a time of mourning, had made a choice: she had decided that she was not going to spend the years left to her weeping for her gone children, that she was going to make of the second half of her life a thing of her own, and she had set to doing it, by force at first, without will, but she had set to it, and the will had come afterward, with the doing. It was not that Felizarda did not feel the lack of her children; it was that she decided not to let that lack govern the rest of her life. And Custódia, hearing this, understood that Felizarda was not of another stuff, was not a colder woman nor less a mother; she was a woman who had made a choice that she, Custódia, had not made, the choice to come down from the watch and live, and that that choice was still within her reach, Custódia's, if she had the courage to make it. Felizarda had not been born free; she had freed herself, by decision and by work, and what Felizarda had done, Custódia too could do, if she wished, if she dared, if she ceased to treat her vigil as a sacred duty and began to treat it as what it was, a prison whose key she had in her own hand.

Idália gave Custódia more fear than pity, and Custódia avoided her a little, without admitting it, because to see Idália was to see herself in a mirror that magnified the flaws. Idália spoke of her children as though they had died, though they were alive and well; she wept over everything; she had made of her house a sanctuary even more extreme than Custódia's; and she wasted away in plain sight of everyone, without caring for herself, without eating properly, like one who had decided, without saying it, that life had ended when the children departed. And Custódia, on seeing her, recognised in her her own path a step ahead, and that frightened her, because Idália was what Custódia might come to be if she did not change, was the fate toward which her vigil pushed her, slowly but without deviation. And perhaps it was also the fear of ending like Idália, as much as the example of Felizarda, that helped Custódia to change, because sometimes we need to see the two paths, the good and the bad, to choose: Felizarda showed her what she could gain if she came down from the watch, and Idália showed her what she could lose if she did not come down, and between the promise of one and the warning of the other Custódia went on finding the courage to choose the path of life.

There was Idália, who had fallen ill of longing. Idália was the opposite of Felizarda: when the children departed, Idália withered, let herself go, made of the longing for her children an illness, and spent her days weeping for the empty house, speaking of her children as of the dead though they were alive and well, wasting away from a mourning over a loss that was no loss at all, was only life following its course. And Custódia looked at Idália and saw, with horror, an exaggeration of herself, saw where the path of one who cannot accept that the children departed led, and Idália frightened her, because she was her own path taken to the extreme, the longing turned into illness, the mother dying of an empty nest. Custódia said to herself that she was not like Idália, that she at least governed herself, that she kept the house, that she did not let herself go; but deep down she knew that the difference between the two was small, that both lived turned toward a past that did not come back, that both made of their gone children an absence that ate their present.

The three had been mothers at the same time, raised their children in the same years, shared the same nights of fever and the same prides, and it was strange, and revealing, to see how the same point of departure — the full nest — had given three points of arrival so different when the nest emptied. Custódia thought of this, on seeing them, and understood that the empty nest did not make the mothers, it only revealed them: Felizarda, who had always had a life of her own beneath that of mother, flew to it when the nest emptied; Idália, who had made herself only a mother, withered when they took away her only role; and she, Custódia, who had made of motherhood a vigil, was left on vigil, because it was all she knew how to be. The emptying of the nest had been, for each one, the test that showed what there was beneath the mother, and Custódia had to admit, with some fright, that beneath her mother there was not a Felizarda full of life nor even an Idália full of longing, there was a sentinel, a function, a watch, and that it was easier to live again from a sleeping life, like Felizarda, or even from a longing, like Idália, than from a function, because a function has no desires to take up, has no life waiting, has only the emptiness of the post when the post empties.

And between the two, the Felizarda who had flown and the Idália who had withered, was Custódia, who had done neither one thing nor the other, who had stayed at the post, on guard, neither living like Felizarda nor dying like Idália, suspended, on watch, waiting. These women met sometimes, in the village, and talked, as the mothers of gone children talk, of the children, always of the children, of the visits, of the calls, of the grandchildren, and it was a conversation that both comforted and bound them, because in speaking only of the children they confirmed to one another that that was what a mother ought to be, only children, and none pulled the others toward another conversation, toward a life that was not the children's. Only Felizarda, when she appeared, brought another conversation, spoke of her own things, of her outings, and the others heard her with a mixture of censure and of envy, and Custódia, in the midst of them, began, slowly, to notice that difference: that to be with the mothers of gone children speaking only of children left her the same, bound, and that to be with Felizarda speaking of life pulled her outward, toward the possibility of a life of her own. And she went on choosing, without consciously deciding, to be more with Felizarda and less with the others, because Felizarda's company did her good and the others' kept her in the same place, and that choice of company was, itself, one of the first steps of the cure, because whom we go about with is where we go, and Custódia, in choosing to go about with one who had come down from the watch, chose, without yet knowing it, to come down too.

And perhaps it was that, she understood later, her particular way of not accepting the empty nest: neither to fly nor to wither, but to mount guard, to stay sentinel at the emptied post, as though by guarding it well she could pretend that it had not emptied, that the children were still there, that the mission still went on.

There was still, in the acquaintance Custódia went on making of these women, a lesson Felizarda gave her without meaning to, only by example, and that was about time. Felizarda said, of her travels and of her new things, a sentence that to Custódia seemed almost a scandal: I have so much good still to live. So much good to live, said Felizarda, with shining eyes, speaking of the years she had ahead as of a promise and not of a desert. And Custódia, on hearing her, realised that she herself never thought of the years left to her as good to live; she thought of them as time of waiting, time of guard, time to pass until the children's visits or until death, whichever came first. For Felizarda, the empty nest had been the opening of a new time, full of possibilities; for Custódia, it had been the end of the time that mattered, and the rest was only to last. And it was the same age, the same years ahead, the same situation; only the manner of looking changed, and that manner of looking made all the difference between an old age that was a promise and an old age that was a wait, and Custódia began to ask herself, hearing Felizarda, whether she too could not learn to see the years left to her as good to live, instead of as a guard shift to serve to the end.

It was Felizarda, the one who had flown, who said to her the thing that stayed. They met one day, and Felizarda, who saw deep, looked at Custódia, at her tiredness, at the dark circles of one who does not sleep, and said, without circling, with the frankness of one who has already freed herself: Custódia, you are guarding a house that no longer needs guarding. Your children are gone, and that is how it had to be, you raised them well, they flew, it is the proof that you did your work. The watch has ended, woman. You won the war. And you go on atop the rampart, weapons in hand, pointing at a field where there is no enemy any more. Come down from there. The war has ended. You have the right to come down from the rampart and to live what is left you, which is still much, if you do not spend it all on watch over a danger that has passed. The image of Felizarda's — the sentinel atop the rampart pointing at a field without an enemy — drove itself into Custódia, because it was exactly what she was and she knew it in the instant she heard it. She saw herself, suddenly, from outside, as Felizarda saw her: a stubborn old sentinel, alone on a rampart, weapons pointed at an empty field, for thirty years defending a fortress no one attacked, unable to lower her weapons because no one had given her the order to retreat and because she herself could not believe the war had ended. And the image was at the same time comic and tragic, a sentinel forgotten long after the end of the war, and Custódia, on seeing herself in it, felt a shame and a pity for herself that she had never felt, the pity of one who understands that they have spent years defending what no longer needed defence, the shame of one who sees themselves from outside doing a thing that, seen from outside, made no sense at all. But beneath the shame and the pity there was also, for the first time, a hope, because if she was a sentinel guarding an empty field, then the solution was simple, it was to come down from the rampart, and to come down from the rampart was within her reach, it was only a question of convincing herself that the war had truly ended, and Felizarda, alive and happy before her, was the proof that one could come down and live.

And Custódia stayed with that, and it hurt her, because Felizarda had said out loud what she did not dare to think: that the watch had ended, that she went on standing sentinel over a won war, and that she was spending the years left to her watching over an empty field.
