## 5. The chorus of mothers

Idália's house was at the edge of the village, on the shaded side, and Custódia went there to take her some cabbages from the garden, which was what one took to a woman one pitied and to whom one did not know what to say. She knocked, and it took a while, and when the door opened there came from inside a closed smell, of a room not aired, of food cooked and not eaten, and Idália appeared thinner than the last time, her hair uncombed and her housecoat on as though it were the only clothing she still had the trouble to put on.

Come in, Custódia, come in, said Idália, and the voice was of one who speaks low in a church. And Custódia went in, and the sitting room was a shrine. On the chest of drawers, on a white cloth, were the portraits of the children, the boy and the girl, in polished frames, and before each one, in a glass, fresh flowers, lilies and carnations, such as Custódia had seen only on altars and on graves, and she understood, with a shiver that climbed her spine, that it was exactly that, that Idália had put her living children on an altar of the dead. The kitchen table, at the back, was laid, a washed cloth, four plates, four glasses, the right cutlery, and on the plates food no one was going to eat, already cold, already forming that dull film of food that waited too long.

They sat, and Idália talked, and went on talking of the children like one who talks of those who have departed never to return. Joaquim, she said, Joaquim was my sun, Custódia, you remember him small, that laugh, and now he's far off, in that city, and telephones every fortnight, when he remembers, and I stay here with his voice in my ear three minutes and then it's the silence again, three minutes of son and a fortnight of nothing. And Liliana married, married well, I can't complain, but to marry is to die a little for the mother, Custódia, to marry is to go, now she has her house and her life and I'm the mother-in-law, I'm the grandmother of the visits, I'm the one to be seen at Christmas. I laid the table, said Idália, and her eyes went to the washed cloth and the four plates with the food untouched, I laid it again today, you know, I still lay the table for four as I laid it when they were here, and then I stay looking at the empty places and I don't even know any more if I should lay the table for one, Custódia, what is life for now, what is it for that a woman rises in the morning when there is no longer anyone to cook for.

Custódia listened and did not eat from the biscuit Idália had put before her, because the biscuit was from the box of the visits and the box of the visits had been open for months, and she was afraid to swallow it. And as Idália talked, Custódia felt growing inside her a thing that was not pity, or that was pity with horror inside it, because everything Idália said, Custódia too knew how to say, everything Idália felt, Custódia too felt, and the only difference between the two of them was a degree, was a step, was the distance that runs from one who still keeps the house clean to one who has it already to be cleaned. Idália was she, Custódia, farther along. It was towards there that the vigil was taking her, slowly but without swerve, towards that chair, towards that shrine, towards that table laid for four with the food souring on the plates, and Custódia got up earlier than courtesy ordered, left the cabbages on the counter, and went out into the street breathing like one who has escaped from underwater, with her own destiny leaning at her back.

At the end of the street, in the square, sitting on a bench in the sun with a bag at her feet, was Felizarda.

Felizarda was Custódia's age, they had raised their children in the same years, shared the same nights of fever and the same prides, and when Felizarda's children grew up and left Felizarda had done a thing that had scandalised half the village, she went about living. She travelled, Felizarda, went to places she had always wanted to see and never could because of the children, got herself into things, made new friends, and there she was, in the sun, more alive at sixty than many a woman at forty, with a postcard in her hand that she was showing to a neighbour. Oh that Felizarda, said half the village under its breath, now that the children are gone she goes about enjoying herself, she ought to have more sense, a mother is a mother. And Custódia, at first, had fallen in with that censure, had thought Felizarda frivolous, a mother who had let go the mission the moment she could.

Custódia, called Felizarda, come here, come and see this. And Custódia went, and Felizarda showed her the postcard, a city with a wide river and some bridges, I went there in May, she said, with Conceição and the other one, the one from the café, three old women on the loose, imagine, and we laughed like girls, we got lost in the metro and we laughed, and at night we ate in some taverns and I thought, Custódia, at fifty I'd never set a foot outside the serra. I'm going now to the north, she said, here in the bag is what I'm taking, you see, and beside the bench she had a half-packed bag, a jumper, a scarf, a folded guide. And she laughed, Felizarda, with her eyes shining, and Custódia, who came from Idália's house with the closed smell still on her clothes, looked at that woman in the sun and felt, without confessing it, an envy that hurt her.

You think I was always like this, said Felizarda, suddenly more serious, as if she had read her face. I wasn't, Custódia. When my youngest left, I shut myself in the house three months, you don't remember because no one noticed, but I know. I went from room to room at night, I went into theirs, I smelled the clothes, I cried for nothing, and there were days when I lay down in the afternoon because I didn't see what it was for to get up. The empty nest hurt me, woman, it hurt me as it hurts all of us, don't go thinking I'm of another paste, that I have no heart. But one morning, said Felizarda, and she lowered her voice, one morning I looked at myself in the mirror and I saw my mother, who died shrivelled with longing for us, I saw her there in my face, and I was afraid. And I said to myself, Felizarda, either you come down from here or you die here. And I began to go out. By force, at first, Custódia, with no will at all, I went to the square only so as not to be at home, I went with the women only to hear a human voice, and I had no pleasure in anything, I did it for the doing. But the will came afterwards, do you see, the will came with the doing, first I did it obliged and one day I caught myself doing it with pleasure, and now, now, said Felizarda, and she took the postcard and tapped it lightly on the palm of her hand, now I have so much good still to live, Custódia. So much good to live.

Custódia heard that sentence and it was almost a scandal to her, so much good to live, said like that, of the years that were coming, as of a promise and not of a desert, because she herself never thought of the years left to her except as time of guard, time to pass until the children's visits or until death, whichever came first.

And it was then, there in the square, that Felizarda looked well at her. She looked at her tiredness, at the dark circles of one who does not sleep, at the new thinness coming to her face, and the joy went out of her eyes and there was left a deeper thing, a frankness of one who has already freed herself and so can speak. Custódia, she said, and she took her hand over the bag, let me tell you a thing, because I look at you and I break with pity, and it's not pity at seeing you sad, it's pity at seeing you doing what you're doing. You are guarding a house that no longer needs guarding, woman. Your children are gone, and that's how it had to be, you raised them well, they flew, and that, Custódia, that is the proof that you did your work, it is not the proof that you failed, it is the proof that you won. The war is over. You won the war. And you go on atop the rampart, woman, weapons in hand, on sentinel, night and day, pointing at a field where there is no enemy any more, for thirty years defending a house no one attacks, alone up there guarding the void. Come down from there, Custódia. The war is over. You have the right to come down from the rampart and to live what is left you, which is still much, do you hear, it is still much, if you do not spend it all on watch over a danger that has passed.

And Custódia stood still in the square hearing herself described, and it was as if Felizarda had opened a door out of herself and set her to look at herself from afar. She saw herself suddenly as Felizarda saw her, an old stubborn sentinel, alone on a rampart, weapons pointed at an empty field, years and years defending a fortress no one attacked, unable to lower the weapons because no one had given her the order to retreat and because she herself could not believe the war had ended. And the image was at once comic and sad, a sentinel forgotten long after the end of the war, and Custódia, on seeing herself in it, felt rise to her face a shame she had never felt, the shame of one who sees herself from outside doing a thing that, seen from outside, made no sense at all. Her eyes filled, and she did not wipe them, because Felizarda had already seen all there was to see. But beneath the shame, and this was the strangest part, there was also, for the first time in a long while, something that resembled hope, because if she was only a sentinel guarding a field without an enemy, then the way out was simple, it was to come down from the rampart, and to come down was within her reach, it was only a question of believing that the war had truly ended. And the proof was there before her, in the sun, with a half-packed bag at her feet and a postcard in her hand, alive and happy, Felizarda, who had also been up there and come down.

You know, said Felizarda lower, letting go her hand, this isn't done from one day to the next. I didn't come down from the rampart in an afternoon. But one begins. Come with me to the north, come on, take a jumper, and Custódia laughed in spite of herself, a small, rusty laugh, of a thing not used in a long while, and said no, that she couldn't, that she had the house, and Felizarda looked at her and said, with a smile that was not of mockery, you have the house, yes, and that was the first lie of yours I didn't believe.

The mothers of gone children gathered sometimes, at the church door or at the house of one of them, and they talked, as such mothers talk, of the children, always of the children, of the visits, of the calls, of the grandchildren seen so seldom, and it was a conversation that comforted them as much as it bound them, because in talking only of children they confirmed to one another that that was what a mother should be, only children, and none pulled the others to another conversation, to a life that was not theirs. Idália, when she appeared at those circles, was the one who talked most and lowest, and the others listened to her with that respect one keeps for the greater grief. Only Felizarda, when she arrived, brought another conversation, talked of the outings, of the north, of the river with the bridges, and the others listened to her with a mixture of censure and envy, and there was always one who said, oh Felizarda, and Custódia, among them, began to notice a thing. She noticed that leaving that circle that talked only of children left her the same, bound, heavy, and that leaving Felizarda's side left her lighter, with her head turned outward. And she went choosing, without deciding it on purpose, to sit nearer Felizarda and less near the others, and it was to Felizarda she turned when the circle filled with gone children, and that choice of company was, without her noticing, one of the first steps out of the rampart, because whom we sit with is where we go.

And only later, alone at night in the kitchen, with the light on above the table and the whole house silent around that light, did Custódia put the three of them together and understand what she had seen. The three had been mothers at the same time, raised their children in the same years, and the same nest, in emptying, had given three women so different that it was hard to believe they had set out from the same place. The empty nest did not make the mothers, Custódia understood, it only revealed what there was beneath the mother. Felizarda, who had always had a life of her own hidden beneath the mother's, flew to it when the nest emptied. Idália, who had made herself only a mother and nothing else, withered when they took away her only role. And she, Custódia, who had made of motherhood a vigil, was left on vigil, because it was all she knew how to be. And she had to admit, by the kitchen light, with a small fright, that beneath her mother there was not a Felizarda full of life waiting to come back, nor even an Idália full of longing, there was a sentinel, a function, a guard, and that perhaps it was easier to live again from a sleeping life, or even from a longing, than from a function, because a function has no desires to take up, it has only the emptiness of the post when the post empties. But the war was over. Felizarda had said it aloud, in the square, in the sun, what she did not dare to think, and now it was said, and what is said is not unsaid. Custódia put out the kitchen light, and for once did not leave it lit awaiting anyone.
