## 12. The first whole night

It was the light of the morning that woke her, and not the three of the dawn, and it was for that that Custódia knew, before she knew anything else, that something great had happened.

She opened her eyes slowly, her face turned to the window, and the first thing she saw was the room full of brightness, that mild brightness of spring mornings that comes in between the curtains and lies on the floor like a tame animal, and she stayed an instant looking at it without understanding, like one who comes to a house she knows and finds it changed in its furniture. There was too much light. There was daylight where her body expected the pitch, and her body expected the pitch because her body, for thirty years, woke in the pitch, at three, with the house in darkness and her heart already in a start before her head knew why. But that morning, no. That morning it was day, and Custódia lay on her side, with her hand under her cheek, in the warmth of the bed, and the clock on the bedside table marked a little past eight, and she had not got up at three.

It took her a moment to put the two ends of it together. She lay there, still, letting the understanding rise slowly, as the water rises in an old well wheel, and as it rose she felt her chest tighten in a way she did not know, because in feeling out the night from within, in search of the usual, in search of the getting up at three, of the going about the house, of the listening at the door of the empty rooms, she found nothing. The night was smooth. The night had been a whole cloth, with no tears, from the first hours until the light, and she had crossed it all at once, asleep, and the world had not come down, and the children had not died, and the house was standing, and it was day.

She tried to remember the last time. Lying down, with her hand to her cheek, she strained to find in her memory a morning like this, a morning of sleeping into the day, and she had to go far back, back to before the night of the fever, back thirty years, to a girl who slept without knowing what it was to keep watch, and that girl was so far off she was almost another person, was almost a story someone had told her. And it was then, on understanding the distance she had walked to find that morning again, that Custódia set herself to looking for the word for what she felt, and the word did not come at once, so forgotten, so little used in thirty years, and when it at last came, it came slowly, almost spelled out, as though it returned from very far off: rested. She was rested. She said it in a low voice, to the pillow, rested, I am rested, and the word in her mouth had a foreign flavour, the flavour of a thing one thought lost and finds at the bottom of a drawer, and in saying it she felt her eyes fill without warning, and she let them fill, and cried there, in the bed, in the light of the morning, but it was a crying she did not know, a crying with no anguish at all beneath it, a crying that did not hurt, and she understood, in the middle of it, that this was how people cried when a thing too good for the body to bear in silence happened to them.

She stayed a while like that, letting the good crying run, and then stayed only lying down, breathing, feeling her whole body rested in a way she had forgotten was possible, her legs heavy and good, her back without the tightness of one who sleeps in waiting, and she thought that it had been the body, and not she, that had made that decision. She had lain down as on the other nights, without much hope, already used to it, already counting on the three of the dawn as one counts on the rain. But the body, at the usual hour, instead of raising her in a start, had turned over in the bed and gone on sleeping. She almost imagined it, at that hour of the dark, hesitating an instant, lifting its head, listening to the house, and the house at peace, and the day before full and good of going about with Felizarda, and the sitting room half transformed already, and Mercês on the telephone every morning, and the body laying its head back on the pillow and trusting. It had not been a decision, that night. It had been a surrender, in the best sense the word could have, the surrender of a body that at last, at the end of thirty years, believed it could lay down its weapons for one night and that the world would hold.

Before getting up, with the light already well into the room, Custódia stayed thinking of that other night, the one of thirty years ago, the night of the fever, the night that had founded everything. It was there, inside her, as it always had been, but that morning she looked at it in another way, without the fear, without the obligation, like one who visits a dear grave at the end of many years and can now look at it with tenderness instead of dread. And she spoke to it. In thought, low, she spoke to that night as one speaks to a person one loved and found hard to love. You saved my son, she said to it, and I will never forget you, and you made of me the guard I was, and in that time you did well, and I needed you, and I was strong because of you. But your time is over. I no longer need to relive you every night to honour what happened. You can be only a memory now, an old night in which I kept watch and won and saved what I loved, and not an order that commands me to keep watch forever. Go and rest, you too. And it was like setting down at last a weight she had carried without noticing, like putting away a letter one has read a thousand times in the drawer it belonged to, and Custódia knew, in doing it, that that was perhaps the last gesture of her cure, the closing of the circle that had opened, thirty years ago, on a daybreak of fever.

She got up. She put on her slippers, put on her dressing gown, and went to the kitchen to make the coffee, and made the coffee and took the cup to the new sitting room, and sat there, in the light of the morning, in her sitting room, in the room she had had changed and that still seemed to her to belong to another house, so bright it was. The light came flat through the window and fell on her hands, on the cup, on the washed floor, and Custódia drank the coffee slowly and thought of the children. But she thought of them in a new way. She thought of Mercês, in her house, with Aurélia; she thought of Tobias, in his; and instead of the usual tightness, instead of the vigil that gripped her chest whenever she named them within, she felt a thing wide and quiet, a thing that was almost pride and almost peace. They are well, she thought. I raised them, I kept watch over them while they needed it, and they grew up strong, and they went, and they are well, and this is not a loss, it is a victory, it is the proof that I did my work. And she stayed looking at the light in the cup, and to come down from the guard, she understood there, was at bottom this: it was to accept the victory, it was to accept that she had won the war she had fought for thirty years, the war of bringing two children safe to grown men and woman, and that to go on a sentinel after the war was won was no love at all, it was only not knowing how to accept that she had won.

It was with the coffee still in her hand that she telephoned Mercês, and the call was the lightest of many years. Mercês answered with the voice already on its guard, that voice the children put on for her calls, and asked is everything all right, mother, her heart on the far side already on the alert, and Custódia laughed, and Mercês heard her laugh and fell silent, because her mother had not laughed like that in a long while. I slept the whole night, child, said Custódia, and there was in her voice a girl's gladness, I slept from the first hours until morning, without waking a single time, without getting up at three, I woke with the sun already coming in at the window. There was a silence on the other end, and in that silence Mercês understood the size of it, understood that her mother was telling her much more than a night's sleep, she was telling her I came down from the guard, I am well, you can live at ease that I rest too. How good, mother, said Mercês at last, and her voice came hoarse, how good, and Custódia heard her breathe deep on the far side, heard her set down a weight she had carried for years, the weight of a mother alone and on watch, and understood that with that plain sentence she was giving the daughter back a great thing, the freedom to live without guilt.

It was the grandchildren who gave her the greatest surprise of that new life, and it was Aurélia who brought it to her. On one of the first times Mercês left her a few days at her grandmother's house, the girl, who before went about there quiet and respectful, as though in the house there were always a sick person asleep, leaned one afternoon on the arm of Custódia's armchair and asked her for a story. Tell one, grandma, one of those. And Custódia, who for thirty years had kept watch over the children in search of dangers instead of telling them anything, was a moment not knowing how, and then began, slowly, with one of when the girl's mother was small and ran away from school to go and see the kid goats, and Aurélia laughed, and asked for another, and another, and by the end of the afternoon she was perched on her grandmother's lap asking for one more, only one more, and Custódia, with the girl smelling of sun and milk leaning on her chest, understood that it was only that, to be the grandmother of the stories, without the weight of the whole life she had with the children, and that it was light, and it was good, and it was a joy the guard had hidden from her for thirty years. When Mercês came to fetch her, the girl asked to come back, and asked it in front of the grandmother, with her eyes shining, can I come again to the house of the grandmother of the stories, and Custódia had to go to the kitchen so they would not see her face.

There was a morning, of those first ones of whole sleep, when Custódia woke with the sun already high, higher even than the other time, and the old start raised her suddenly in the bed, with her heart racing and her mouth dry, and for an instant she was certain that she had failed, I slept too long, I failed the guard, something has happened, and she already had her legs out of the bed and her hand on the chest of drawers when she stopped. She stopped, and stayed there, sitting on the edge of the bed, barefoot, her heart still leaping, listening, and the house was silent, and the silence was only silence, and there was no guard at all to fail, and there was no danger at all, and there was no child at all crying in the dark, there was only an old woman sitting on the bed on a sunny morning frightening herself at her own rest. And Custódia laughed. She laughed alone, in the room, first low and then with a will, at the old sentinel that had given there its last kick before surrendering for good, and the laugh came out of her clean, and she heard herself laugh and understood, in the middle of the laugh, that that laugh was the exact measure of how far she had come, because one who laughs at her own fear is one who is no longer ruled by it, and she, who had taken thirty years of the vigil as the most serious thing in the world, could now laugh at it as at an old habit, and that meant that at last she ruled the sentinel instead of the sentinel ruling her.

It was so, morning by morning, that Custódia built a life that was hers, and she built it slowly, with method and stubbornness, as she had built everything, only this time for herself and not to guard the others. The mornings were of the call to Mercês and of her own things, of the coffee in the bright sitting room; the afternoons, often, were of Felizarda, of the outings, of the sewing, of the women; and the nights, at last, were of sleep. She visited the children instead of only awaiting them, took the coach and went herself to find them in the city, to see the grandchildren, to stay a few days, and came back, and the children received far more gladly the light mother who came to visit them than they had received the heavy mother who awaited them on watch at the window. And it was not only she who changed; it was the whole relationship of the three, because to a light mother one goes of good will, and the children, who for years had visited her out of duty and with weight, began to visit her out of pleasure, to stay longer, to laugh more, to discover beneath the sentinel a woman with opinions and humour they had barely known.

And that night, on lying down, Custódia did the thing that became her small rite of arrival. Before putting out the light, instead of listening to the house in search of a danger, she stopped in the middle of the kitchen and thought of the children, one by one, Mercês in her house, with Aurélia asleep beside her, Tobias in his, and said to them, low, in the dark, like one handing over a shift of guard to whoever now has it: you are safe, and I too can rest. Good night, my children. The guard is yours now, of your own lives, and you do it well. I have kept watch over what I had to keep watch over. Now I sleep. And then she made the gesture that was the most important of all, more even than the night of sleep: she put her hand to the switch of the kitchen light. That light was the symbol of her vigil, the beacon she had kept lit for thirty years so that no child should wake in the dark without seeing that their mother was at her post. She had lit it for the first time when the children were small and afraid of the dark, and had never put it out again, not even when they grew up and left, because to put it out was to admit that there was no longer anyone to wake afraid, that the beacon no longer guided anyone. And that night Custódia stayed an instant with her hand on the switch, in her light, in the silent kitchen, and then she put it out. She put it out on purpose, slowly, and the house went all dark, and silent, for the first time in thirty years. And she stayed there still in the dark feeling herself, awaiting the old fear, the reign of the danger she had always feared in the darkness, and the fear did not come, because the darkness turned out to be after all only what it had always been beneath her dread: the repose of the night, the rest of a house where everyone, at last, including the mother, slept in peace.

She went to bed in the dark, without stumbling, for the house knew her by heart, and lay down, and shut her eyes, and had learned, at the end of a whole life of guard, the hardest thing for a mother who loved too much: that to come down from the guard is not to cease loving the children, it is to trust them enough to let them live, and to love oneself enough to let oneself, at last, rest.
