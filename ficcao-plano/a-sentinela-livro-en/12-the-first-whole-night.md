## 12. The first whole night

Custódia's life, at the end of this story, did not become perfect, because lives do not become perfect, and she went on having her hard nights, her longings for her children, her moments of wanting to go back to the rampart. But it became a life, and that was everything, because before it was not a life, it was a guard; it became a woman, and that was everything, because before it was not a woman, it was a sentinel. Custódia went back to being a person, slowly, and a person has highs and lows, good days and bad, but lives, and that was what mattered, that Custódia had gone back to living instead of only watching, that the years left to her would be years lived and not years of waiting, and that, whatever those years brought her, she would cross them as a living person and not as a guard forgotten at her post.

There was a night, in that spring, when Custódia slept a whole night's sleep, from the first hours of the night to the light of morning, without waking at three, without getting up to mount guard, without listening to the house, and when she opened her eyes and saw that it was day, that she had slept the whole night, that the world had not crumbled, she understood that she had at last come down from the watch, and she wept, but that time from a good thing.

It had not been a decision, that night; it had been a surrender, in the good sense, the surrender of a body that at last trusted. Custódia had lain down as on the other nights, without great hopes, already used to waking at three; but on that night, by a conjunction of things — the good tiredness of a full day with Felizarda, the new peace of the house already half transformed, the sensation of not being so alone now that she spoke with Mercês every morning — her body, at the usual hour, instead of raising her in a start, turned over in the bed and went on sleeping. And it was only in the morning, on waking with the light, that Custódia understood what had happened, that she had slept the whole night, that she had not got up at three, that the body, for the first time in thirty years, had not mounted guard. And she lay in bed trying to understand what she felt, and what she felt was a thing so simple and so forgotten that it took her time to recognise it: she felt rested. Rested. The word seemed to her almost foreign, from having been able to apply it to herself so little in thirty years, and Custódia repeated it, lying down, rested, I am rested, and it was like finding again an old friend she had thought lost forever.

It was the first whole night in thirty years. Thirty years without sleeping a complete sleep, thirty years of waking at three, thirty years of sentinel; and on that night, without she herself quite knowing how, the body at last trusted, at last lowered the guard, at last let her sleep, and Custódia woke in the morning rested as she had not woken since before the night of the fever, since before she had made herself the eternal guard of her children. And she lay in bed, on waking, feeling that new rest, that body at last reposed, and understood what she had lost during thirty years: the simple rest of one who sleeps trusting, the peace of one who closes their eyes without fear, the whole sleep that is the right of one who trusts that the world can bear a night without their vigil.

It was not the last hard night, and Custódia knew it, because of these things one does not heal for good. There were, after that one, nights when the guard came back, when the old start raised her at three and she had to do all the work again, to lie down, to breathe, to tell herself that the silence was peace and not danger, to trust. But the difference, now, is that she already knew she could, because she had already managed it, because there had already been a whole night, and a whole night proves that another is possible, and Custódia clung to that proof on the hard nights, I slept a whole night, so I can sleep another, and she went on managing it, some nights yes, others no, but more and more yes than no, and it was so, night by night, that the sentinel went on at last coming down from the rampart to the bed, and staying there.

She thought, on waking from that first whole night, of the night of the fever, thirty years ago, the night that had founded her vigil, and she made her peace with it. She said to it, in thought, as one speaks with a memory dear and heavy: you saved my son, and I will never forget you, and you made me the guard I was, and you did well, in that time. But your time has ended, and I no longer need to relive you every night, I can now let you be only a memory, an old night in which I was strong and saved what I loved, and not an order that obliges me to keep watch forever. And it was as if, in making her peace with that founding night, Custódia freed it from the power it had had over her for thirty years, kept it in the place of memories instead of leaving it to command the present, and that tidying-away, to put the night of the fever in the past to which it belonged instead of leaving it to govern all the nights that followed, was perhaps the last gesture of her cure, the closing of the circle she had opened on that dawn of thirty years ago.

She got up, and made the coffee, and sat in her new room, by the light of the morning, and thought of her children, and thought of them in another way, without the tightening of the vigil, without the distress, without the weight. She thought of them as of two grown people, capable, living their lives far away, safe, and instead of the usual fear she felt a new thing, a thing that was almost pride and almost peace: they are well. I raised them, kept watch over them while they needed it, and they grew strong and capable, and they went, and they are well, and that is the proof that I did my work, it is not a loss, it is a victory. There was a morning, of those first ones of whole sleep, when Custódia woke with the sun already high and had, for an instant, the old start: I slept too much, I missed the guard, something has happened. And right after she laughed at herself, alone, in bed, because she understood the absurdity of the thought, because she understood that there was no guard to miss, that to sleep late was to miss nothing, it was only to rest, and that that start was the old sentinel giving her last kick before surrendering for good. And to laugh at that, instead of obeying it, was a sign of how far she had come, because a person who laughs at their own fear is a person who is no longer dominated by it, and Custódia, who for thirty years had taken her vigil seriously as the most serious thing in the world, was now beginning to be able to laugh at it, to treat it as an old mania, and that laughter was the measure of her cure, was the proof that the sentinel no longer commanded her, that she at last commanded the sentinel.

And she understood that to come down from the watch was, at bottom, to accept the victory, to accept that she had won the war she had waged for thirty years, the war of raising two children safe to adulthood, and that to go on standing sentinel after the war was won was not love, it was not knowing how to accept that she had triumphed, that the mission had been fulfilled, that she could, at last, lower her weapons and rest.

She noticed, with time, that it was not only she who had changed; it was the whole relationship of the three that had changed with her. The children, who for years had visited her out of duty and with weight, began to visit her for pleasure, and the difference was visible: they came more relaxed, stayed longer, laughed more, brought the grandchildren more often. Because a light mother is a mother one goes to willingly, and Custódia, in coming down from the watch, had become a light mother, a mother with whom it was good to be, a mother who asked after her children's lives with genuine interest and not with the distress of the vigil, who heard them without wanting to protect them from everything, who had her own things to tell, her outings, her friends, her life. And the children discovered, with joy and some astonishment, a new mother, or rather, a mother who after all had always been there beneath the sentinel and whom they had scarcely known, an interesting woman, with opinions, with humour, with life, and they liked her in a new way, not only with the love of duty one has for a mother, but with the liking one has for a person one likes to be near.

She telephoned Mercês, that morning, and the call was the lightest of many years. I slept the whole night, daughter, said Custódia, and there was in her voice a girl's joy, I slept the whole night, from the first hours to the morning, without waking once. And Mercês, on the other end, understood the size of that, understood that her mother was telling her much more than a night's sleep, she was telling her I have come down from the watch, I am well, you can live at ease for I too rest, and Mercês rejoiced as she had not rejoiced in a long while, because her mother gave her back, with that sentence, the freedom to live without guilt, the certainty that she did not have to carry the weight of a mother alone and on watch, that her mother was well, that her mother lived.

With time, Custódia built a life that was hers, and she went on building it slowly, as she had built everything, with method and with stubbornness, only that time for herself and not to guard others. The mornings were of the call to Mercês and of her own things; the afternoons, often, of Felizarda, of the outings, of the sewing, of the women; and the nights, at last, of sleep. One of the greatest changes, and one of those that gave her most joy, was that of the grandchildren. For years, Custódia had been for the grandchildren a distant and slightly frightening grandmother, the sad grandmother who lived far away and who put their mother, Mercês, in a bad mood after each call; a grandmother they visited out of duty and of whom they kept no great joy. And as Custódia came down from the watch, as she became a light and joyful woman, the grandchildren began to know another grandmother, a fun grandmother, with stories, with patience, with a liking for being with them, and the grandchildren began to like to visit her, to ask to go to grandmother's house, and Custódia, who had kept watch so much and been so little enjoyed, discovered at last, with the grandchildren, the simple joy of being a grandmother without being a guard, of being with children for pleasure and not out of a duty of vigilance, of telling them stories instead of watching over their dangers. And it was perhaps with the grandchildren that Custódia most savoured the new life, because with the grandchildren she did not have the weight of the whole history that she had with the children, she could be only the good grandmother, the grandmother of the stories, and that was light, and it was good, and it was a joy that the guard, for thirty years, had hidden from her.

She visited her children, now, instead of only awaiting them: she took the bus and went herself to them, to the city, to see the grandchildren, to stay a few days, and came back, and that, her going to them instead of living awaiting them to come, changed everything, because a mother who goes to see her children is an active mother, with a life, and a mother who only waits is a passive sentinel, and the children received much more willingly the mother who went to visit them, joyful and light, than they had received the mother who awaited them, heavy and on watch. Custódia's life, which for thirty years had been a guard post, became a person's life, with its occupations, its joys, its people, and the sentinel, which she had been, went on staying behind, an old version of herself that she remembered with some grief and some astonishment, no longer quite knowing how she had endured so many years without sleeping, on guard over a danger that did not exist.

Custódia did not forget, in this new life, where she had come from, and she went on becoming, in the village, a kind of voice for other mothers in the same place where she had been. When she saw a mother of a recently emptied nest beginning to make of her house a sanctuary, beginning to keep watch at a distance, beginning to waste away from longing, Custódia drew near to her, with the authority of one who had been there, and told her what Felizarda and the Keeper had told her: come down from the watch, woman, your children are safe, live your life, do not make yourself a sentinel of an empty post, for I did that thirty years and almost lost myself. And some heard her, and others did not, as she herself at first had not heard; but Custódia said it all the same, because she knew the price of not hearing, and because to transform her story into a warning for others was the way to give meaning to the thirty years she had lost, to make of her useless guard a new usefulness, that of sparing others what she had been through.

And that night, on lying down, Custódia did a thing that became her small rite of arrival: before putting out the light, instead of listening to the house in search of a danger, she thought of her children, one by one, Mercês in her house, Tobias in his, and said to them, softly, in the dark, as one who hands over a guard shift to those who now have it: you are safe, and I too can rest. Good night, my children. The guard is yours now, of your lives, and you do it well. I have kept watch over what I had to keep watch over. Now I sleep. That gesture, to put out the kitchen light, was perhaps the most important of all, more even than the night of sleep, because the kitchen light was the symbol of her vigil, the beacon she had kept lit thirty years so that no child would wake in the dark without seeing that the mother was at her post. Custódia had lit that light for the first time when the children were small and were afraid of the dark, and had never again put it out, even when the children grew and went, because to put it out was to admit that there was no longer anyone to wake afraid, that the beacon no longer guided anyone, that the guard had ended. And on that night, in putting it out on purpose, Custódia made at last that admission, and made it without pain, with peace, because she no longer needed the beacon lit to feel herself a mother, she already knew that she was a mother all the same, with the light out, sleeping, trusting; and the house went dark, all of it, for the first time in thirty years, and the darkness, which Custódia had always feared as the kingdom of danger, revealed itself after all to be only what it was: the repose of the night, the rest of a house where everyone, at last, including the mother, slept in peace.

And she put out the light, the kitchen light she had left lit thirty years, put it out on purpose, because she no longer needed to leave it lit for anyone, there was no longer a child who might wake frightened and need to see that the mother was at her post; and the house, for the first time in thirty years, went all dark, and silent, and at peace, a house that at last slept, with its sentinel at last sleeping within it, rested, trusting, free, after a whole life of guard, in the whole sleep of one who learned, at the end, the hardest thing for a mother who loved too much: that to come down from the watch is not to cease loving the children, it is to trust them enough to let them live, and to love oneself enough to let oneself, at last, rest.
