## 9. The bottom

It should be said that Custódia had never been a person to collapse. She had crossed hard things in life — the early death of her husband, the raising of her children alone, the straits, the difficulties — and had crossed them all on her feet, mounting guard, because the guard had always been her way of not collapsing: however bad it was, there were the children to keep watch over, there was the post to garrison, there was a duty that kept her on her feet. The vigil had been, throughout her whole life, the crutch that held her up in every misfortune, because while there was someone to guard Custódia had something to hold to, had a reason to get up, had a meaning. And what made that time so dangerous, more dangerous than all the previous misfortunes, was precisely that they had taken away her crutch: with the children grown and far away, there was no longer anyone to guard, there was no longer a post, there was no longer a duty, and Custódia was left, for the first time in her life, without her crutch, without the vigil that had held her up in all the previous falls, and it was for that that this time she fell, and fell deep, because she fell without the only thing that had always sheltered her, which was to have someone to be the guard of.

But between knowing and managing there is a path, and before Custódia came down from the watch there was the bottom, there was the night when she tried to sleep on purpose, to lower the guard by force, and could not, and it was on that night, in the panic of not managing to stop keeping watch, that she touched the lowest point of everything.

It should be said that no one saw this bottom of Custódia's, because, like all sentinels, she kept the appearance intact. By day, the village saw a woman on her feet, neat, who did her duty, who went to Mass, who cared for the house, and no one imagined her nights, the despair of the dawns, the war she waged alone against a body that would not let her sleep. Custódia hid her bottom as she had always hidden everything that was hers, because a guard does not complain, a guard endures, and to admit that she was sinking was, for her, a weakness unworthy of one who had been the strong one of the family. And it was that hiding, that refusal to show anyone what went on within her, that made her bottom deeper still, because hidden suffering has no relief, and Custódia suffered alone, at night, and pretended by day, and no one, not even the children, knew the size of the well she had fallen into, because she had made of the fortress a mask and behind the mask she came apart with no witnesses.

She had decided, after the House of the Register, that she was going to sleep. That she was going, that night, to lie down and sleep a whole night's sleep, to come down from the watch, as the Keeper had said. She prepared for it almost as for a battle: she lay down early, determined not to get up at three, not to go and see the rooms, to trust that the children were safe and to let herself sleep. And at three in the morning, punctual as for thirty years, she woke, with the usual start, with her whole body ordering her to get up, to go and see, to mount guard. And Custódia, that time, tried to resist. She stayed in bed, with her eyes closed, saying to herself all is well, they are safe, sleep, you don't have to get up, sleep. And she could not. The body did not let her. Her heart raced, the distress grew, and the more she forced herself to stay lying down the more the panic rose, the panic of a sentinel ordered to abandon the post, the ingrained terror that, if she slept, if she lowered the guard, some disaster would befall the children, as had nearly befallen Tobias on the night of the fever, thirty years ago.

She tried everything, that night and the following ones, to force herself to sleep. She tried reason, telling herself that the children were safe; she tried willpower, staying lying down with her eyes closed refusing to get up; she tried to tire herself by day so as to fall asleep at night. Nothing worked, because what kept her on guard was not within reach of reason nor of will, it was in a deeper place, in an ingrained fear that obeyed no orders. And it was to discover this, that she could not change just by wishing, that despaired her, because Custódia had always been a woman of getting what she wanted by force of will, and she saw herself now defeated for the first time by a thing within her that the will did not conquer, and that, for a woman who had made of strength her identity, was the most humiliating defeat of all: that of discovering that there was in her a thing stronger than her will, a fear of thirty years that did not let itself be commanded.

And it was there, in that panic, that Custódia understood the size of her prison: that it was not only a habit, it was a fear ingrained in the bones, it was a sentinel who could not come down from the rampart even if she were ordered to, because her body had learned, thirty years ago, that to come down from the rampart was to let a child die, and no reason, no word of a Keeper, no decision of the head managed, alone, to undo a fear so deep. And there was, in that panic of not managing to sleep, an almost physical dimension that frightened Custódia: the racing heart, the short breath, the sweat, the whole body on alarm, as though there really were a danger, though there was none. It was the body doing, alone, what it had done thirty years: preparing to attend, entering a state of guard, mobilising for an emergency that did not exist. And Custódia understood, in the midst of the panic, that her body no longer distinguished the real danger from the imagined danger, that it reacted to a silent and safe house as it would react to a child burning with fever, that thirty years of alarm had thrown out of order in it the bell of danger, to the point that it rang the alarm over nothing, over everything, over a calm night. And that disorder, that body that rang the alarm without cause, was perhaps the cruellest mark of a life of vigil: not only the soul tired, but the very body broken, trained for a permanent danger that had worn it out, and that went on sounding even when there was no longer anything to announce.

She got up, defeated, and went to the kitchen, and wept, wept from exhaustion and from despair, because she understood that she was trapped, that she wanted to come down from the watch and could not, that her own body kept her sentinel against her will, and that perhaps it was too late, that perhaps a whole life of vigil had marked her so deep that she no longer knew, no longer managed, to sleep as those sleep who trust that the world does not crumble if they close their eyes.

There was, in that kitchen, in the midst of the despair, a moment when Custódia saw herself from outside, as though it were another person looking at her, and what she saw was an old woman, alone, in a dressing gown, at four in the morning, weeping at the table of a kitchen with the light lit, in a big empty house where no one else lived, on guard over children who slept safe hundreds of kilometres away and who did not even know that their mother was awake weeping for them. And the image was so sad, and so alone, that Custódia barely recognised herself in it, and asked herself how she had come there, how the strong mother, the guard of all, the one who had held a whole house against the world, had come to this woman weeping alone at dawn, worn from a vigil that no longer served anyone. And she understood that she had come there one step at a time, one night at a time, without noticing it, that the vigil that had saved her when young had gone on consuming her slowly until it left her so, and that if she did not do something she would die there, on some dawn, on guard, alone, without her death saving or even being noticed by anyone in time.

It was Custódia's lowest night, because it was the night when she understood that it was not enough to want to change, that the fear was stronger than the will, and there was a moment, in that kitchen, when she thought she was going to stay so forever, on guard until she died, never again to sleep at ease, a sentinel condemned to an eternal post, and that idea, that of a whole life still ahead without ever being able to rest, was so heavy that Custódia, for the first time, felt a thing resembling not wanting to go on, a tiredness so deep of keeping watch that she came to wish not to wake again, only to rest at last, only for the guard at last to end.

What saved her from staying at the bottom was, by irony, the same thing that had brought her there: the love for her children. Because in the midst of the despair of that night, in thinking that perhaps it was easier not to wake, Custódia thought of what that would do to Mercês and Tobias, of the pain it would give them, and understood that she could not, that she had no right, that the children, whom she had kept watch over her whole life, did not deserve to lose their mother so, to giving up. And it was that same love, which had made her an eternal guard, that on that night prevented her from letting herself go, and that pushed her, in the morning, to the telephone, to the asking for help. There was a justice in this: the love that had imprisoned her was also the love that saved her, because at bottom Custódia's vigil, however crooked it had become, came from a good thing, from loving her children too much, and that good thing, at the bottom of the well, was still there, and it was to it that Custódia clung so as not to sink for good: to the love, which she had to learn to live in another way, but which was, in the end, what there was in her most true and most strong.

But it was also on that night, at the bottom of that despair, that Custódia did the first right thing, and she did it because she no longer had pride to prevent her: she asked for help. In the morning, she telephoned Mercês, and instead of telling her I get by, alone, as she had always said, she told the truth, she said daughter, I am not well, I cannot sleep, I cannot come down from the watch, I need help. Custódia hesitated before dialling the number, with the telephone in her hand trembling, because to ask for help was, for her, the hardest thing in the world, harder than any night of fever, than any vigil. To ask for help was to admit that she could not manage, that she was fragile, that she needed, and that went against everything she had been her whole life, against the guard, against the strong one, against the one who never gave trouble. But she was so at the bottom, so without strength, that for the first time the frailty defeated the pride, and she dialled the number, and when Mercês answered, with her voice still of sleep because it was early, Custódia, instead of the usual I get by, let the mask fall and told the truth, and it was as though a rampart she had been holding for thirty years finally crumbled, and on the other side of the ruin, instead of the emptiness she feared, was her daughter, saying I'm coming right now, mother.

And it was the first time in her life that Custódia asked a child for help, the first time she ceased to be the guard to be the guarded, the first time she admitted, to one she had kept watch over her whole life, that it was she, now, who needed someone to keep watch over her. And Mercês, on the other end of the line, instead of the usual weight and guilt, felt a new thing: she felt called, needed, capable of caring for the mother who had always cared for her, and she said I'm coming right now, mother, and she came, and it was that call, that asking for help, that coming-down of the sentinel's pride, the first thread of the stairway out of the bottom, because the bottom, Custódia understood, is not where one stays, it is where one sets one's feet, and she had set her feet at the bottom of that night, and in asking for help, in letting them at last care for her, she had taken the first step to climb.

What Mercês found, when she arrived, shook her, because she had never seen her mother so. The mother she knew was the strong one, the guard, the one who took care of everything, the one who never complained, and what she found was a worn woman, with sunken eyes, who did not sleep, who trembled, who wept, and Mercês understood, with a shock, that the strong mother of always was at the end of her strength, that behind the mask of strength she had always shown there was an exhausted and lonely woman, and that she, the daughter, had never known, because the mother had never let it be seen. And there was, in that reunion, an inversion that moved them both: Mercês taking her mother in her arms, the mother letting herself be embraced, the daughter doing for the mother what the mother had done for her her whole life, keeping watch, consoling, being there. And Custódia, in letting herself be embraced by her daughter, in letting them at last care for her, felt a thing she had not felt since she was a girl, since before she had made herself the guard of all: she felt cared for, safe, sheltered, and it was so good, and so strange, and so deserved, that she wept even more, but that time from relief, the relief of one who, at the end of a life of holding everything alone, finds at last some arms that hold her.
