## 9. The bottom

Custódia had never been one to break down. She had crossed hard things, the death of her husband early, the raising of the children alone, the tight straits, and had crossed them all on her feet, mounting guard, because the guard had always been her way of not breaking down: however bad it was, there were the children to keep watch over, there was the post to man, there was a duty that kept her standing. And now, with the children grown and far off, they had taken her crutch away, and she was left for the first time with no one to be the guard of, and it was for that that this time she fell, and fell deep. But before she came down from the rampart there was a night, and it is of that night that the book must speak, because it was there, and not in the Keeper's words, that the crossing was made or was lost.

She had come out of the House of the Register with a decision in her hand like one coming out with a parcel. That night she was going to sleep. She was going to lie down and sleep a whole sleep, come down from the guard, as they had told her she ought. She prepared for it almost as for a battle. She ate early and little. She went about the whole house putting out lights, closed the shutters, checked the gas, checked the latch, and then forced herself to stop checking, because to check was already to mount guard. She lay down with the sun still yielding behind the serra, pulled the blankets to her chin, shut her eyes with the force of a child who pretends to sleep so the father will leave it in peace, and told herself that she was resolved, that this night she would not get up, that this night she trusted.

At three in the morning, punctual as for thirty years, she woke.

It was not a gentle waking. It was the start of always, the body sitting up almost before she ordered it, the eyes open in the dark, the ears already opening the whole house in search of the sound that would justify the alarm. Go and see the rooms, said the body. Get up, go and see, go and make the rounds. And Custódia, that time, did not get up. She lay down, on her back, with her eyes shut, her hands on her chest, and began to talk to herself in a low voice, in a whisper that was almost a prayer, everything's well, Custódia, they are safe, sleep, you don't have to get up, there's nothing to see, sleep.

The heart did not hear her. The heart raced first, and beat in her throat, in her wrist, in her temples, a fast, uneven beating that she felt all over her body, as if there were someone running inside her. Her breathing shortened. She found herself breathing through her mouth, in small gulps, not reaching the bottom of the air. Everything's well, she repeated, louder, everything's well, and the sentence no longer believed in itself. A cold sweat was born at the root of her hair and ran down her nape, and she, lying down, motionless, resolved not to move, felt her whole body enter alarm, the same machine she had ordered to sleep turning itself on against her order, preparing to come running, mobilising for an emergency that existed nowhere in that silent house.

And it was then that the night of the fever came.

It was not memory, it was presence. The lying body turned, and instead of the dark room Custódia had suddenly the small Tobias burning in her arms, thirty years ago, his body on fire against hers, his breathing whistling, and she alone in the cold house not knowing whether the fever was yielding or whether it would take the boy before morning. The smell came too, the smell of a sick child, of a hot cloth, of fear. Her hands, on her chest, remembered exactly the weight, and closed over the void. Custódia opened her eyes to tear herself from there, and the fever came with her into the dark of the room, because the body did not distinguish, the body did not know that thirty years had passed, that Tobias was a grown man hundreds of kilometres away, that that house was at peace. For her body, in that instant, a child was burning in the next room, and she was lying there letting him burn.

The panic climbed to where it was no longer possible to stay lying down. The more she forced herself not to move, the more the terror grew, the ingrained terror of a sentinel ordered to abandon the post. She turned from one side to the other, gripped the sheet, counted the breaths and lost count, tried reason, they are safe, tried force, stay still, and neither of the two reached the place where the fear dwelt, which was too deep, below reason and below will. She held out perhaps an hour like that, she who had held out thirty years, and at a certain point she understood that she was trembling, and that she was not going to win.

She got up, defeated. Her feet found the slippers in the dark without her ordering it, from so many daybreaks. She put on the dressing gown. And instead of going to the rooms, because there were no longer rooms with anyone inside, she went to the kitchen, and turned on the light.

The kitchen light at four in the morning has a colour it has at no other hour, a raw colour, of a thing no one should be seeing. Custódia stood still at the entrance, blinking, and the whole house around was silent, a big silence, of a house where no one else sleeps. She leaned against the table, and then sat at the table, in that usual chair, and it was there, with both hands open on the cold top, that she at last cried. She cried of exhaustion and of despair, she cried because she understood that she was trapped, that she wanted to come down from the guard and could not, that her own body kept her on sentinel against her will.

And there was an instant, in the middle of the crying, when Custódia saw herself from outside, as though another person came into the kitchen and looked at her. And what that other saw was an old woman, in a dressing gown, alone, at four in the morning, crying at the table of a kitchen with the light on, in a big and empty house, on guard over two children who slept safe hundreds of kilometres away and who did not even know their mother was awake crying for them. The image was so sad and so alone that Custódia scarcely recognised herself in it, and asked herself aloud, at the empty table, how she had got there, how the strong one of the family, the one who had held a whole house against the world, had come to this woman worn out by a vigil that no longer served anyone.

And there came then, at the bottom of everything, a thought that frightened her more than the fever. She thought that perhaps it would be so forever, on guard until she died, never again sleeping at ease, and the idea of a whole life still ahead with not a single rest weighed on her so much that Custódia, for the first time, felt a thing resembling not wanting to go on, a tiredness of keeping watch so deep that she came to wish not to have to wake any more, only for the guard at last to end, only at last to rest. She did not think it with horror nor with drama. She thought it almost with sweetness, like one who thinks of lying down, and it was that sweetness that frightened her.

What saved her from staying there was, ironically, the same thing that had brought her to it: the love for her children. Because no sooner had the sweet thought passed than there came after it Mercês, there came Tobias, there came the face they would make, the pain that would give them, and Custódia understood, with her hands still wet on the table, that she had no right. That the children she had kept watch over a whole lifetime did not deserve to lose their mother like that, to giving-up, on some daybreak. And it was the same love that had made her an eternal guard that in that kitchen kept her from letting herself go, and that held her seated until the sky began to grey behind the serra, and that pushed her, when the morning at last came, to the telephone.

It was hours. Custódia stayed at the table watching the light change at the window, and when the clock struck an hour at which one may wake a daughter, she got up, and went to fetch the telephone, and sat down again with it in her hand. The hand trembled. She stayed looking at Mercês's number a long time, because to ask for help was, for her, the hardest thing in the world, harder than any night of fever, than any vigil. To ask for help was to admit that she could not, that she was frail, that she needed, and that went against everything she had been a whole lifetime, against the guard, against the strong one, against the one who was never a trouble to anyone. She almost hung up. She almost put the telephone away and decided she would wait another day, that she would manage alone, as always. But she was so at the bottom, so without strength, that this time the frailty beat the pride, and she pressed the number before she could repent.

Mercês answered at the fourth ring, with the thick voice of one torn from sleep. Mother. Is everything all right? What hour is this. Has something happened.

And Custódia, who had at her mouth the usual I'm getting by, the I'm well, child, it was only to hear your voice, the sorry to wake you, opened her mouth and let the mask fall. Child, she said, and the voice came out small, I am not well. I don't want to frighten you, but I am not well. I can't sleep. There are nights I don't sleep. I wake at three and I lie trembling and I can't come down from the guard, child, I know this makes no sense, you are safe, I know, but the body does not hear me, the body thinks something is going to happen to you if I close my eyes. And I can't do it alone any more. I needed help. I'm asking you for help, child, and I never asked you for anything in my life, I know.

On the other end there was a silence, but not the silence Custódia feared, not the void. It was the silence of someone who sits up in bed and turns on the light. When Mercês spoke, the voice had no sleep in it at all. Mother, she said. Oh mother. Why didn't you say it sooner. How long have you been like this and hiding it from me. A pause, and the sound of clothes moving, of a drawer. You're at home, you're there in the kitchen, are you. Don't hang up. Get dressed slowly and leave me the door on the latch. I'm coming, mother. Listen. I'm coming. I'm setting off right now and by lunchtime I'm there. Don't do anything, don't tidy anything, don't go pretending you're well for when I arrive. Stay there sitting that I'm coming.

Custódia said all right, child, in a thread, and she even said sorry, and Mercês said don't say sorry, mother, don't say that, and they hung up, and Custódia stayed with the telephone against her chest, at the table, in the kitchen with the light on, crying again, but no longer of the same water.

Mercês arrived a little after midday. She knocked and came in at the same time, and stopped at the kitchen door, and what she saw shook her, because she had never seen her mother like that. The mother she knew was the strong one, the one who saw to everything, the one who never complained, and what she found was a woman with sunken eyes, still in a dressing gown at lunchtime, worn, small in the chair. And Mercês said nothing. She crossed the kitchen, and knelt beside the chair, and took her mother in her arms, slowly, like one taking up a thing that can break.

And Custódia, who a whole lifetime had been the arms that held the others, let herself be held for the first time. She did not straighten herself, did not say it's over now, did not apologise for the house nor for herself. She leaned her face on her daughter's shoulder and let herself be held, and cried there what she had to cry, with Mercês doing on her head the exact gesture the mother had done on hers as a girl. It was the first time in her life that Custódia ceased to be the guard to be the guarded, the first time she admitted, to one she had kept watch over her whole life, that it was she, now, who needed one to keep watch over her.

The bottom, Custódia understood much later, in going back over that night, is not where one stays: it is where one plants the feet. And the love that had imprisoned her for thirty years was, in the end, the same that reached her the first hand out of the well, because it came from a good thing, from loving too much, and that good thing, down at the bottom, was still there whole. It was to it that Custódia clung. And when she at last let herself be held by her daughter's arms, she felt a thing she had not felt since she was a girl, since before she had made herself the guard of everyone: she felt cared for, safe, held, and it was so strange, and so deserved, that she cried even more, but this time of relief, the relief of one who, at the end of a life holding everything up alone, finds at last some arms that hold her.
