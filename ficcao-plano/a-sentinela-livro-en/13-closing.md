## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a mother, in Véspera, who kept watch over her children a whole lifetime and who only at the end learned to come down from the watch and to sleep.

Custódia, mother of Mercês and of Tobias, who on a night of fever, with her children small, learned that to keep watch saves and that to sleep kills, and who never again lowered the guard, not even when the children grew, were safe, and went to live far away. She stayed sentinel over a house that did not sleep, guarding empty rooms, awaiting a danger that had passed thirty years ago. It took a fall in the dark, with no one to keep watch over her, and a Keeper, for her to understand that the guard of her children had passed into their hands, that her shift had ended, and that to go on at the post was not love, it was a chain, for her and for them.

It was recorded too that, in coming down from the watch, she did not lose what she feared to lose. She went on being a mother, and a better mother; she went on loving her children, and loving them better, from nearer and lighter; and she gained what she had never had: sleep, a life of her own, the company of friends, the joy of the grandchildren, and the peace of one who trusts. The rampart she had thought was her motherhood fell, and beneath it was not the abandonment she feared, there was a living woman, the Custódia of the line, who went back to living the years left to her instead of spending them on vigil.

It was recorded that she learned to come down from the watch: to trust that her children were safe without her vigil, to transform a guarded room into a space of her own, to sleep, at last, a whole night. And that in coming down from the watch she did not lose her children, she gained them, because they, freed from the weight of her vigil, drew near again willingly. And that she said, on putting out for the first time in thirty years the light she had kept lit: they are safe, and I too can rest.

And it was recorded that she became, in the village, a voice for other mothers in the same passage, telling them what it had cost her to hear: that grown children are not guarded, they are trusted; that to come down from the watch is not to love less, it is to love by trusting; and that there is, after the children depart, a life waiting for whoever has the courage to come down from the rampart and go and live it.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who left home for their own life. It does not say what it costs a mother to come down from the watch after a life of vigil, nor that the best proof of a good mother's work is the day the children no longer need to be watched over. There are those who go on standing sentinel long after the war ends, without hearing that they have won, and that they may, at last, rest. For those, books such as this one are needed. And of all of it a record was made.
