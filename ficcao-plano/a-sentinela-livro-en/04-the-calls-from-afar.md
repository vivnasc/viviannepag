## 4. The calls from afar

The telephone was, in Custódia's house, an object of worship and of torture. It was always close by, always charged, always with the sound at maximum, because a call from a child could come at any moment and Custódia could not, on any account, miss a call from a child. And when the telephone did not ring, which was most of the time, Custódia looked at it, waited for it, and its silence weighed on her like an absence, like a proof that the children were too busy, too far away, too forgetful of their mother. She lived, in part, around that telephone, like a sentinel around a signal that may come from the horizon, and she measured her children's love by the frequency of the calls, and suffered with each day of silence, without understanding that her children's silence was not lack of love, it was only their life going on, and that to measure love by the calls was a reckoning that brought her only pain, because no frequency of calls would ever be enough to fill the hole she had and that was not a hole of calls.

The children telephoned, and it was in the calls, more than in anything else, that one saw the weight Custódia's vigil had placed on the relationship of the three, a weight she did not see and the children felt, and that made of the calls, which ought to have been a joy, a thing shot through with guilt.

The children loved their mother, it must be said out loud, so that neither they nor she be judged badly. Mercês and Tobias loved Custódia, and acknowledged all she had done for them, the life she had given them, the sacrifices, the nights of vigil they perhaps did not even know for certain but that they sensed. They were not ungrateful children, they had not let go of their mother out of selfishness; they had done what children do, and ought to do, which is to grow and live their lives, and they loved their mother at a distance, with the longing and the care of children who live far away. The problem was never lack of love in the children; it was the way Custódia's love, turned into vigil, into complaint, into weight, made it hard for the children to reach her with their love, because their love always collided with the guilt she put on them, and what ought to have been a meeting of three loves became a mismeeting, not for lack of love, but for an excess of weight on one side, the weight of a mother who loved too much and badly, who loved by suffocating, who loved in a way that drove away instead of drawing near.

Mercês telephoned with regularity, out of duty and out of love, and the calls always began well and always soured in the same way. Custódia asked after the grandchildren, after life, and listened, but at a certain point came the question that spoiled everything, and you, mother, how are you, and Custódia, instead of saying I am well, said things like I get by, alone, you know how it is, the house so empty, but don't worry about me, and each one of these sentences, said without malice, with the meek voice of one who does not complain, was in truth a small knife, because it put upon Mercês the weight of a mother alone and sad, made her feel guilty for being far away, for having her life, for being happy while her mother wasted away on watch over an empty house. And Mercês hung up those calls with a tightening, with the guilt of not doing more, of not coming more, of living far away, and that guilt, which Custódia did not put there on purpose but put there all the same, went on becoming a shadow over the calls, until Mercês began, without noticing it, to telephone with a weight in her breast, to put the calls off a little, to dread them somewhat, because each call gave her back the image of her mother alone and the bill for her own happiness far away.

And the worst is that Custódia did not do that out of malice, nor even on purpose; she did it because it was true, because she was indeed alone in an empty house, because the house indeed weighed on her, and in saying it to her daughter she was not manipulating, she was unburdening herself, sharing her pain with the only person to whom she could share it. But what for Custódia was an unburdening, for Mercês was a weight, because Mercês could not resolve her mother's solitude without leaving her own life and going back to Véspera, a thing she could not and should not do, and therefore each unburdening of the mother left the daughter with a problem without a solution and a guilt without a remedy. And it is this that makes so difficult the relationship with a father or a mother who makes themselves unhappy in old age: that their suffering is real, and their complaint is legitimate, and yet the child cannot resolve it without sacrificing themselves, and is caught between pity and powerlessness, between love and guilt, and ends up, often, by drawing away a little, not for lack of love, but for defence, because no one bears for long the weight of a guilt they cannot repair.

Tobias, farther still, telephoned less, and when he telephoned Custódia could not resist telling him how long had passed without news, oh son, how long it has been, I was already distressed, already thinking that something had happened to you, and Tobias, who had nothing wrong, who was only busy with life, felt in that his mother's vigil reaching out to him across hundreds of kilometres, the mother who went on keeping watch over him, the distress of the mother who made of each silence of his an imagined disaster. And Tobias loved his mother, but her vigil tired him, suffocated him a little, because a grown man does not want a distressed mother counting the days since the last call, he wants a mother who trusts that he is well, who lives her life, who does not make a drama of his silence. And without wishing it, Custódia's vigil drove Tobias away instead of drawing him near, because no one draws near willingly to one who makes them feel guilty for living.

There were still the birthdays and the celebrations, which for Custódia were mountains of waiting and which for the children had become sources of guilt. Custódia prepared the children's visits weeks in advance, cleaned the house from top to bottom, cooked too much, set everything at the ready, and counted the days, and on the days of the visit she was the happiest woman in Véspera. But the happiness lasted as long as the visit lasted, and when the children went away again, Custódia fell from an immense height, from the full house to the empty house, from the busy mother to the sentinel alone, and that fall, repeated at each visit, was so painful that the children, without saying so, began to feel it: they felt that each departure was a small mourning for the mother, that they left her worse than they had found her, and the guilt of that fall of the mother's joined the guilt of living far away, and the visits, which ought to have been joy, became heavy with anticipation and farewell. Custódia did not see that her excessive happiness with the visits was, itself, a weight, because it put upon the children the responsibility of being the only joy of a mother who had no other, and few things weigh more on a child than to know they are the only joy of a mother, because that turns each absence of theirs into a deprivation they inflict, and every child wants to be able to absent themselves without feeling that they are robbing their own mother of her light.

Custódia did not see this. Custódia thought she was being a good mother, present, attentive, concerned, and did not understand that her concern, instead of an embrace, was a weight, that her vigil at a distance, instead of protecting her children, drove them away, that each I get by alone and each I was already distressed was a brick in the wall of guilt that rose, without her wishing it, between her and her children. She thought the children came rarely because they were busy, because they lived far away, because life today is like that; it did not occur to her that they came rarely also because to visit their mother had become a heavy thing, laden with guilt, a plunge into her sadness, and that perhaps they would come more, and more willingly, if the mother did not make of each visit a reminder of how much she suffered alone.

Tobias, once, tried to tell her the same thing as Mercês, but in his way, drier, and it hurt her more. On a call in which Custódia told him, as always, that she was distressed about him, that she did not sleep thinking whether he was well, Tobias answered, with some impatience, mother, I am forty years old, you cannot spend your life distressed about me, that does not help me, it only makes me feel that you do not trust that I know how to live. And Custódia heard that as an ingratitude, as a son telling his mother that her concern bothered him, and she was hurt, and the call ended badly. But Tobias was right, and Custódia, deep down, knew he was, though the hurt did not let her admit it: her vigil, which she offered as love, was received by the children as lack of trust, as a weight, as a mother who would not let them be adults. And there are few things that drive a grown child farther away than a mother who insists on treating them as a child; the child flees, not for not loving the mother, but to be able to breathe, to be able to be the adult the mother will not let them be when she is near, and that flight, which Custódia lived as abandonment, was in truth a defence, the defence of one who needs distance to escape a vigil that suffocates them.

There was a call, with Mercês, that nearly opened Custódia's eyes, though she was not yet ready to see. Mercês, on a day of less patience, after hearing her mother say for the umpteenth time that she was alone in an empty house, answered, with a frankness that escaped her: but mother, you do not have to be alone in an empty house. Children grow and go, that is how it has to be, you raised us to be able to live our lives, and we are living them, and that ought to make you proud, it ought not to leave you on watch over empty rooms waiting for us. We are not going to come back to live there, mother. And you have to make something of your life that is not waiting for us, or you are going to spend the years left to you guarding a house instead of living it. There was a Christmas, in that time, that showed everything. The children came, with the grandchildren, and the house filled up, and Custódia was, for a few days, fully happy, the house full, the post garrisoned, the full-time mother once more. But even that Christmas the vigil did not let her go: she was distressed about everything, whether the grandchildren ate, whether they were cold, whether they did this or that, and the children saw their mother unable simply to enjoy their presence, always on guard, always anticipating dangers, always turning a joyful gathering into an operation of vigilance. And when Christmas ended, and the children went, Custódia fell into the usual emptiness, and called them to say how silent the house had become, how she already missed them, how hard it was, and the children, who had come willingly, felt again the guilt, the weight, and understood that even giving their mother a full Christmas they could not fill her, because the mother's hole was not of visits, it was of a life of her own, and no quantity of visits fills the hole of one who has no life of their own; only a life of their own fills it, and that the mother refused to build, waiting for the children to fill it, which no child can do, however much they come, however much they love.

Mercês regretted at once having said it like that, in that hard way, and in the days that followed she telephoned with more sweetness, trying to mend it, because she was afraid she had hurt her mother. But the truth is that that frankness, however much it had wounded her in the moment, was one of the things that most helped Custódia, because it was the first time anyone had told her, without circling, what everyone saw and no one said: that she had to make something of her life, that she could not go on on watch over empty rooms. The village pitied her and kept silent; the other mothers understood her and did not confront her; only Mercês, who was a daughter and who suffered the mother's weight up close, had the courage, or the lack of patience, to tell her the truth to her face. And sometimes that is what is needed, a truth said without sweetness by one who loves us, to pierce a stubbornness of years, and Mercês's, however much it had hurt, pierced something in Custódia, opened a chink through which, slowly, the light began to enter.

And Custódia was left silent, on the other end of the line, wounded, because she heard that as an accusation, as an ungrateful daughter telling her mother to fend for herself; but beneath the wound, very deep, a part of her also heard another thing, heard a truth, and it was that truth, planted on that call, that began, slowly, to work in her: that perhaps she was spending her life guarding a post no one asked her to guard, and making of her children, with her vigil, not a love, but a weight.

That night, after the call, Custódia did not sleep, but she did not sleep in a way different from usual: she did not sleep thinking of what Mercês had said, instead of thinking of a danger. And it was perhaps the first night in thirty years in which her insomnia was not of vigil, it was of thought, in which she lay awake not listening to the house but turning over a truth, and that change, however small, was already a beginning, because a sentinel who spends the night thinking of her own life instead of watching the perimeter is a sentinel who has already begun, without knowing it, to turn her eyes from the rampart inward, and to turn the eyes inward was what Custódia needed to do, at the end of a whole life turned outward, toward the children, toward the dangers, toward everything but herself.
