## 4. The calls from afar

The telephone was, in Custódia's house, an object of worship and of torture. It was always near, always charged, always with the sound turned up, because a call from a child could come at any moment and Custódia could not, on any account, miss a call from a child. And when the telephone did not ring, which was most of the time, Custódia looked at it on the kitchen table, beside the light she always left on, and waited for it, and its silence weighed on her like an absence. She lived, in part, around that device, like a sentinel around a signal that may come from the horizon.

That afternoon, it rang. It was Mercês.

Mother, said the daughter, and the voice came light, came from one who telephoned out of duty and out of love, regularly, never failing the week. Custódia gripped the telephone with both hands and went at once, as she always went, first to the children. And Aurélia, she asked, and the others, tell me, and Mercês told: Aurélia had got a good mark, she was learning the dividing sums and no longer cried over them, the youngest had lost a tooth on Thursday and put it under the pillow waiting for the mouse. One could hear, in the background, Aurélia calling for her mother about something, and Mercês covering the telephone and saying coming, child, wait, I'm talking to grandma. Custódia laughed at that coming, child, she recognised it, she had said it a thousand times in that same kitchen, and for a while the call was only that, was good, was two women talking of the life that went on.

And then it came. It came as it always came, with the best of intentions, the question that spoiled everything. And you, mother, asked Mercês, and you, how are you.

Custódia could have said I'm well. She knew, in some place, that it was what the daughter needed to hear. But what came out of her, in the mild voice of one who does not complain, was: I get by, child, I get by, you know how it is, here alone, the house very empty, but don't you worry about me, I manage. She said it with no malice at all, unburdening herself, sharing with the only person to whom she could share it. But each sentence like that, said so low, was in truth a small knife, and Custódia did not feel the knife, but Mercês felt it.

There was, on the other end of the line, a short silence, a tightness. The lightness was gone. Mercês lowered her voice, and what she said next had no gladness in it at all: oh mother, don't say that, you know I come as soon as I can, that if I could I'd come more, but with the children's school, with the work. I know, child, I know, Custódia cut in, quickly, I'm not charging you with anything, God forbid, I know you have your life. And it was worse that way, it was worse with the I'm not charging you with anything, because it put on the table exactly the charge it said it was not making. Mercês was left with the weight of her mother alone in an empty house, left with the guilt of being far off, of having her life, of being happy while her mother wasted away on watch over a house.

The call ended soon after, slowly, with nothing bad said, and that was the worst of it, that nothing bad had been said and Mercês still hung up different from how she had answered. She stayed a moment still with the telephone in her hand, looking at the screen going dark, and sighed, with that sigh of one who carries away a problem she cannot solve. Because Mercês could not solve her mother's loneliness without leaving her own life and going back to Véspera, a thing she neither could nor should do, and each of her mother's unburdenings left her with that in her hand, with a pain that had no remedy and a guilt that had no mending. She went to Aurélia, who was calling her, and carried that weight into the rest of the afternoon.

And the worst is that Custódia did not do it out of malice, nor even on purpose. She did it because it was true, because she really was alone in an empty house, because the house really did weigh on her. In saying it she was not manipulating anyone, she was unburdening herself. But what for Custódia was unburdening, for the daughter was a brick of guilt, and the bricks went on joining, call by call, until Mercês began, without noticing, to telephone with a weight in her chest, to put the calls off a little, to dread them a little, because each one gave her back the image of her mother alone and the bill for her own happiness far away.

With Tobias it was another thing, it was drier. Tobias, that one, lived farther still and telephoned less, and Custódia counted the days between his calls like one counting the nights of a vigil. When he at last telephoned, she could not resist. Oh son, she said at once, before anything else, how long it's been, I was already worried, I was already thinking something had happened to you, I spent the night without a wink. And Tobias, who had nothing the matter, who was only busy with his life and his work, heard in that his mother's vigil reaching out to him across hundreds of kilometres.

Mother, he answered that time, and in the voice came his impatience already, mother, I'm forty years old. You can't spend your life worried about me, that doesn't help me, do you see, that only makes me feel that you don't trust that I know how to live my own life. I'm well. I'm always well. You don't need to spend your nights awake on my account.

Custódia heard that and did not hear what he was saying, she heard what wounded her. She heard a son telling his mother that her worry troubled him, she heard ingratitude. So now a mother may no longer worry about a son, she answered, with the voice trembling a little, now worrying about you is troubling you, that's what I hear. That's not what I said, mother, retorted Tobias, tired already, and you know that's not what I said. And the call ended badly, ended at a slant, with the two of them hurt, and Custódia stayed a long time with the telephone in her hand chewing over her son's ingratitude.

But Tobias was right, and Custódia, deep down, knew he was, though the hurt would not let her admit it. Her vigil, which she offered as love, was received as lack of trust, as a mother who would not let him be the grown man he was. And the son fled, not for not loving his mother, but to be able to breathe, and that flight, which Custódia lived as abandonment, was after all a defence.

There came then the call that nearly opened Custódia's eyes, though she was not yet ready to see. It was with Mercês, on a day when the daughter had less patience, one of those days when one wakes already tired before the day begins. Custódia told her, for the umpteenth time, what she always told: that she was there alone, that the house was empty, that the children's rooms stayed made, ready, waiting, with the sheets washed, in case someone should turn up. And something, that time, came loose in Mercês.

But mother, she said, and it was no longer the mild voice of a daughter, it was a frankness that escaped her all at once, you don't have to be alone in an empty house. You don't. Children grow up and go, mother, that's how it has to be, it always has been, and you raised us precisely for that, to be able to live our lives, and we're living them, and that ought to make you proud, do you hear, it ought to make you proud and not on watch over empty rooms waiting for us. Don't leave them made, mother. Strip those beds. We are not going to come and live there again. We're not, mother, hear me well, I know it's hard but it's the truth and someone has to tell you: we are not coming back. And you have to make something of your life that isn't waiting for us, because otherwise you'll spend the years you have left guarding a house instead of living it, you'll spend what's left of you standing on a rampart watching for a danger that doesn't exist, waiting for a call, waiting for a visit, waiting, mother, always waiting, and the whole of life passing you by while you wait.

And she fell silent, breathless from what she had said, like one who let out all at once a thing that had been leaning there for years.

On the other end, Custódia was silent. She was wounded, mouth open over an answer that did not come, because she heard that as an accusation, as an ungrateful daughter telling her mother to fend for herself, to stop being a bother. All right, was all she managed to say at last, in a small voice, all right, child, I've understood, I'll say no more. That's not it, mother, Mercês began, but Custódia was already taking her leave, give the children a kiss, and hung up before her daughter could mend it.

And Mercês repented at once of having said it in that hard way, and was left herself too with the telephone in her hand, now on the far side of the guilt, afraid of having gone too far, of having hurt her mother who after all was only old and alone. In the days that followed she telephoned with more sweetness, more often, trying to mend it, asking after her mother with a new care. But the truth is that that frankness, however much it had wounded in the moment, was among the things that most helped Custódia, because it was the first time anyone told her, without circling, what the whole village saw and no one said. The village pitied her and kept quiet; the other mothers understood her and did not confront her; only Mercês, who was a daughter and suffered her mother's weight from near, had the courage, or the lack of patience, to tell her the truth to her face.

Custódia remembered, after this call, the last Christmas of the full house. The children had all come, with the grandchildren, and the house, which was always so silent, filled up all at once, with voices, with steps, with Aurélia running down the corridor, with the smell of the too-much food she had made. For a few days she was the happiest woman in Véspera, the post manned, the full-time mother once again. But even at that Christmas the vigil did not let go of her: she was anxious the whole time, whether the grandchildren ate well, whether they caught cold at the window, whether Aurélia would fall off that chair, and the children looked at her and saw a mother unable simply to be there, to enjoy it, always on guard, always anticipating a danger. And when Christmas ended and the children packed the bags and put the little ones in the car and went, Custódia fell from the height, from the full house to the empty house, into a void so deep that that very night she telephoned them to say how silent the house had become, how she missed them already, how hard it was to stay there alone after so much joy. And the children, who had come of such good will, felt again the guilt, the weight, and understood that even giving their mother a full Christmas they could not fill her, because the mother's hole was not a hole of visits.

That was Custódia's mistake, and she did not see it: she measured her children's love by the frequency of the calls, weighed it by the count of the visits, and no count was ever enough, because what she wanted to fill was not of calls nor of visits. The children loved their mother, it must be said loud. Mercês and Tobias loved Custódia, recognised all she had done for them. They were not ungrateful children; they were children who had done what children must do, which is to grow and to live. The problem was never lack of love. It was that Custódia's love, turned into vigil, into complaint, into weight, made it hard for the children to come to her with theirs, because their love always knocked against the guilt. And few things weigh more on a child than to know it is a mother's only joy, because that turns each of its absences into a deprivation it inflicts.

That night, after Mercês's call, Custódia did not sleep. But she did not sleep in a different way from the usual. She did not lie listening to the house, separating the creaks of the wood from the steps that never came, watching the perimeter like one on sentinel duty on a rampart. She lay chewing over what her daughter had told her. She heard it again, sentence by sentence, you have to make something of your life, you'll spend the years guarding a house instead of living it, we are not coming back, mother. It still hurt, hurt like an accusation. But beneath the wound, very deep, a part of her heard also another thing, heard a truth, and it was that truth, planted in that call, that began, slowly, to work on her from within.

And it was perhaps the first night, in thirty years, in which her insomnia was not of vigil, it was of thought. In which she stayed awake not guarding the children, but looking at herself. And a sentinel who spends the night thinking about her own life instead of watching the horizon is a sentinel who has begun, without knowing it, to turn the eyes of the rampart inward. To turn the eyes inward was what Custódia needed to do, at the end of a whole life turned outward, towards the children, towards the dangers, towards everything but herself.
