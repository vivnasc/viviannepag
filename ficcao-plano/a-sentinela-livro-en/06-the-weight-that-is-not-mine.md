## 6. The weight that is not mine

Custódia resisted Felizarda's sentence, at first, as one resists a truth that undoes us. She said to herself that Felizarda did not understand, that each mother was each mother, that some children needed more than others, that she knew her own, that she knew what she was doing. She armed against Felizarda's sentence all the defences of thirty years of vigil, and for a few days she managed to keep it at a distance, to go on guarding as though nothing were the matter. But truths, when they are truths, do not let themselves be kept away long, and Felizarda's went on coming back, at night above all, when Custódia was on watch and had no way not to think, and the question went on imposing itself, against her will: I am guarding what? And it was that question, repeated night after night, that ended by piercing the defences and leading Custódia to the discovery that changed everything, that of there being nothing left to guard, that of the war having ended, that of her mounting guard over emptiness.

Felizarda's sentence worked in Custódia for weeks, and led her, slowly, to a discovery that changed the way she saw herself: that she was carrying a weight that was no longer hers, the safety of her children, which had become theirs long ago, and which she insisted on going on carrying as though it still fell to her.

Felizarda's sentence — the guard of your children has long since passed to other hands — came back to her head at unexpected moments, and each time it came back it brought a new layer of understanding. Custódia thought: when Mercês married, I handed her, at the church door, to another person, and from that day the guard of her became hers and her husband's, and not mine. And she thought of Tobias, master of his house, of his life, and understood that the guard of Tobias too had passed, long ago, to Tobias himself. The guards had all passed, one by one, at their proper moments, as they always pass, and it was she who had not noticed, it was she who had gone on mounting guard over posts that had already changed hands, like an old soldier defending a border his country handed over by treaty years ago, with no one having told him that the war had ended and the map had changed. And there was a sadness in this, that of understanding that she had been guarding what no longer fell to her; but there was also a relief, because if the guard had already passed to other hands, then she could lay hers down, then there was no failure at all in coming down from the rampart, because the rampart already had others to guard it, the children themselves, and she could, at last, without guilt, without betraying anyone, withdraw from the post.

She noticed this one night, awake at three, as always, listening to the house. She was on watch, as for thirty years, with the old start waiting for a danger, and that night, instead of getting up to go and see the rooms as she always did, she stayed in bed thinking: watch over what? Mercês is hours from here, in her house, with her husband and her children, sleeping, safe, and if something happens to her at night it is not I, from here, who am going to save her; Tobias is farther still, master of his life, and if he needs something it is not my vigil, at this distance, that is going to give it to him. What am I on guard for, Custódia asked herself, lying in the dark. What danger am I watching, at this hour, in this empty house? And the answer, when it came, was an abyss: none. There was no danger at all to watch. The children were safe, and safe far from her, and her vigil protected them from nothing, because their life no longer passed through there, was no longer within reach of her guard. She was standing sentinel over an empty post, watching a field where there was no longer anything to watch, and her guard, which during the children's childhood had been a real rampart, was now a gesture without an object, a vigil over the nothing.

And she understood, that night, the thing Felizarda had told her in other words: that the safety of her children had ceased to be a weight of hers. When they were small, yes, their safety was in her hands, she was the rampart, and to keep watch was her duty. But the children had grown, and in growing they had taken on, as all children take on, the guard of their own lives; Mercês's safety was now Mercês's, Tobias's was Tobias's, they were the ramparts of their own houses, and Custódia, in going on wanting to carry their safety, was carrying a weight that no longer belonged to her, that had passed into their hands and that she refused to let go. And the question the village put to its mothers, without knowing it, the question of the register — what am I carrying that is not mine — had, for Custódia, a clear answer: she carried the guard of her children's lives, which were already theirs to guard, and in carrying it she protected them from nothing, she only exhausted herself and suffocated them with a vigil no one asked of her.

She thought, on that night of revelation, of the word that gave name to her name: Custódia, the one who has the custody, the guard. Her mother had given her that name dreaming of a daughter who would know how to watch over her own, and Custódia had fulfilled the promise of the name better than anyone, had made herself a whole guard, a perfect custody. But custody, she understood now, is a thing one has for a time and then hands over: one has the custody of a child while it is a child, and then one hands over to it the custody of itself when it grows, because an adult has the custody of their own life. And Custódia had not handed over the custody. She went on holding the guard of people who were of age to guard themselves, refused to make the handing-over that all mothers have to make, that of returning to the grown child the custody of their own life. And in not making that handing-over, not only did she carry a weight that was no longer hers, she also prevented the children from fully taking on the weight that was theirs, because it is hard for a child to feel themselves entirely master of their life while the mother insists on mounting guard over them at a distance.

There was a resistance, of course, because to let go of that weight was to let go of the only thing that made her feel herself a mother. If I am not on guard, thought Custódia, then what am I? A mother who lowered the guard, who ceased to keep watch, who sleeps at ease while her children go about the world, is she not a relaxed mother, a mother who no longer cares? And it took her time to understand that no, that to come down from the watch was not to cease loving her children, it was to love them in another way, it was to trust them, it was to acknowledge that they were grown and capable, it was to honour the work she herself had done in raising them strong. To go on guarding was, at bottom, not to trust them, was to treat them still as children unable to guard themselves, was a lack of faith in what they had become. She thought much, in that time, of the difference between protecting and trusting, and understood that they had been, in her life, almost opposites, when they ought to have been phases of one same thing. To protect is the love of the first times, the love of one who cares for a fragile child; to trust is the love of the later times, the love of one who acknowledges a capable adult. And a sound mother makes the transition from one to the other as the child grows, she protects less and trusts more, until, at the end, she trusts almost entirely and protects almost nothing, because the child already protects themselves. Custódia had not made that transition. She had stayed stopped in protection, in the love of the first times, and had gone on protecting children who were no longer fragile, refusing to pass to the love of trusting, and so her love, instead of maturing with the children, had stayed frozen in a time that had passed, a love of protection applied to those who no longer needed protection and needed, rather, trust. And to trust, Custódia understood, was after all the hardest love, because to protect is to do, is to act, is to be on guard, it gives a sensation of usefulness; and to trust is not to do, is to draw back, is to let the other walk, and it gives no sensation of usefulness, it even seems, to one who does not understand, a form of abandonment. But it was that the love the children needed now, the love that does not do, that trusts, that draws back, and Custódia was going to have to learn to give it, against all her nature of a mother who protects by doing.

And a mother who truly loves her grown children has to make this act of faith: she has to believe that they manage, that they are safe without her vigil, that they can live their lives without the mother standing sentinel, and she has to come down from the rampart not out of giving up, but out of trust.

And there was, beneath everything, a truth harder still that Custódia was forced to face: that the vigil, besides fear for the children, was also a flight from herself. While she was on guard, while she had a post, while she worried about the children, Custódia did not have to ask herself what she wanted for herself, what she would make of her life, who she was beyond a mother; the guard filled her days and her nights and spared her those questions. To come down from the watch was to come face to face with those questions, with the emptiness of a woman who had made herself only a guard and who did not know what else she was, and that face to face frightened her, perhaps, more than the imagined danger to the children itself. The vigil was, at bottom, also a hiding place: while she watched over others, she did not have to look at herself, and to look at herself, after a life turned wholly outward, was frightening, because she might find no one there. And Custódia understood that to come down from the watch was going to require of her not only to let go of the fear for her children, but also to face the question the guard had let her avoid her whole life: and I, who am I, what do I want, now that I no longer have to watch over anyone?

There was, in Custódia's case, an added difficulty, which was the widowhood. A mother who is widowed early and raises her children alone puts upon her children, without wishing it, not only a mother's love but also the place that would be the husband's, makes of her children her whole family, her company, her reason, everything. And when those children depart, the widowed mother is left not only with the empty nest but with the house empty of everything, without children and without husband, alone with a double solitude, and so perhaps she clings even harder to the vigil, because to let go of the children is, for her, to be left with absolutely no one. Custódia did not have, as others had, a husband to grow old with after the children departed, a companion for the empty nest; she had only the children, and so the children were everything, and to let go of them was to be left with nothing, and that was one of the deepest roots of her vigil, that of a woman alone who had made of her children her whole family and who had, to retreat to when they departed, not even a companion, only a big empty house and the echo of her own solitude.

And it was also for this that to come down from the watch was, for Custódia, so frightening: because to come down from the watch was to be left alone with that double solitude, without the vigil to fill it, and she had to find, in coming down, a way to people her life that was not the vigil of her children, friends, occupations, company, a life of her own, or the solitude would swallow her. And it was for that that Felizarda's friendship and Mercês's presence were so important: it was not enough for Custódia to let go of the guard, she had to replace it with life, with people, with bonds, because an emptiness is not let go of, it is filled, and to come down from the watch with nothing to fill its place would be only to trade the prison of the vigil for the prison of bare solitude.

But to know this with the head and to manage to do it with the body were two distinct things, and Custódia, who had understood with her head that she ought to come down from the watch, discovered that the body did not let her: she went on waking at three, went on listening to the house, went on awaiting a crying from thirty years ago, because the vigil was not a decision, it was a habit of a whole lifetime, ingrained in the bones, and the habits of a lifetime are not let go of by understanding that they are wrong, they are let go of slowly, by force, with relapses, as one lets go of a vice. She began, even so, to try small things, because she had understood that she could not change all at once but perhaps could change in bits. She tried, one night, not to go and see the rooms when she woke at three; she managed to stay in bed a few minutes, with her heart leaping, before yielding and getting up, but it was a few minutes, and the next night it was a few minutes more. She tried to leave the kitchen light off one night; she did not manage, she lit it halfway, but she tried. She tried to tell Mercês, on a call, a thing that was not a complaint; and it cost her, but she said it. They were tiny steps, almost invisible, and Custódia, used to doing everything with force and all at once, grew impatient with their slowness, wanted already to be cured, already to sleep, already to be another. But she went on learning, slowly, that these things are not changed with force, they are changed with patience, tiny step by tiny step, and that each tiny step counted, that to come down from a rampart of thirty years was done stair by stair and not in a leap, and that the haste, which had always been her ally, was there her enemy, because what she had to do was not done fast, it was done slowly, with the patience she had never had and that she was going to have, too, to learn.

And Custódia understood that she had ahead not a decision, but an unlearning, the hard task of teaching a sentinel's body to lower the guard after thirty years of mounting it, and that this was going to be slow, and hard, and that perhaps she needed, to manage it, a thing that would shake her enough to force her to change, a thing that, without her knowing it, was already on its way, in the form of a night in which the sentinel, from so much keeping watch over others, would discover that no one kept watch over her.
