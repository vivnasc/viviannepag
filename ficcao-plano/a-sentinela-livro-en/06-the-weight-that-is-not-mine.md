## 6. The weight that is not mine

It was on a night at the end of October, with the house cold and the kitchen clock striking three, that Custódia, for the first time in thirty years, did not get up.

She woke as she always woke, suddenly and whole, without that wet interval between sleep and waking that other people have. Her eyes opened in the dark and the body was already listening, the heart beating in a way she knew better than she knew her own face, that hurried, on-post beating that was hers since Belarmino died and she was left alone on guard over the whole house. Her hand already sought the dressing gown laid at the foot of the bed, the dressing gown that was her uniform, and her feet already turned to the floor awaiting the order to come down and make the rounds, to go to Mercês's room, to go to Tobias's room, to lean her head against the doors of the rooms that had been empty for years and listen, within them, for the silence of children who no longer slept there.

And that night, she does not know to this day why, perhaps from tiredness, perhaps because Felizarda's sentence had been going around her head for weeks like a stone inside a shoe, Custódia stayed. She stayed lying down, with the dressing gown in her hand and her heart leaping, and let herself ask, there in the dark, the question that for thirty years she had never had the leisure to ask because she was always busy obeying it the other way round: keep watch over what.

She said it in a low voice, to the empty house, and the house did not answer. Keep watch over what. Mercês is hours from here, said Custódia to the ceiling, in her house, with her husband, with her children, asleep, safe, and if something happens to her this night it is not I, from here, from this bed, who save her; Tobias is farther still, master of his house and his life, and if he needs something it is not my rounds at three in the morning that give it to him. What is it I am on guard over, asked Custódia of the dark, lying down, her heart slowing against her will. What is this danger that I watch, at this hour, in this house where no one sleeps any more but I.

And she waited for the answer as a soldier waits for the enemy on the rampart, eyes on the field, and the field was empty. The answer, when it came, was an abyss opened beneath the bed: none. There was no danger at all to watch. The children were safe, and they were safe far from her, and her vigil covered them from nothing because their lives no longer passed beneath that roof, no longer fell within the reach of her guard. She was on sentinel over an empty post. She was standing on a rampart watching a field where there was no longer any war, weapon pointed at the nothing, and the house around her was big and cold and smelled only of her.

It was then that she remembered, lying down, with a sharpness that made her close her eyes, the day she gave Mercês away.

She saw the church, the light coming in from the side through the stained glass and falling in dust on the stone floor, and she saw herself coming down the nave with her daughter hanging on her arm, Mercês in white, her hand trembling on top of hers. Custódia had kept that hand a whole lifetime. She had wiped its nose and trimmed its nails and held it crossing the road and in the fever of the bad nights, that hand was a post she had mounted and never left. And at the door of the altar she stopped, as she had to stop, and the son-in-law was there waiting, in his suit, serious, with his own hand outstretched, and Custódia made the gesture every mother makes and no mother forgets: she took her daughter's hand, which was hers, and laid it in the hand of another man, and withdrew her own. It was an instant. Mercês's hand passed from one guard to another before the whole village, and no one saw anything extraordinary in it because it is what one does, one gives the daughter away, but Custódia, on that October night, twenty years later, lying in the dark, understood what she had done with her hands without understanding it with her head: that day she had handed over the guard of Mercês, given it to other hands, and from then on Mercês was to be guarded by her husband and by herself, and not by her. The guard had passed. She herself had passed it, with her own hands, at the church door, and had gone back home and gone on a sentinel as though she had handed over nothing.

And with Tobias it had been deafer still, with no ceremony at all, no stained glass nor music, only a son who one day bought his house and took his things and became master of his life and of his guard, and she had not noticed, had gone on keeping watch over him at a distance as she kept watch over a six-year-old child with fever. The guards had all passed, one by one, in their proper times, as they always pass, and it was Custódia who had stayed at the post defending a frontier her country had ceded by treaty years ago, with no one having told her the war had ended and the map had changed. Felizarda's sentence, lying there, made sense to her at last in the flesh and not only in the head: the guard of your children has already passed to other hands. The war is over.

There was an enormous sadness in this, and Custódia cried a while, low, in the dark, not quite knowing whether she cried of grief or of something else. Because beneath the sadness, hidden, there was also a relief she scarcely dared to feel: if the guard had already passed, then she could set hers down. There was no fault at all in coming down from the rampart if the rampart had others to guard it, the children themselves, and she could, at last, without guilt, without betraying anyone, withdraw from the post. The weight she had carried for thirty years, the safety of the children, was no longer a weight of hers. It had passed to their hands, and it was she who refused to open her fingers.

Her mother had given her that name, Custódia, dreaming of a daughter who would know how to watch over her own, and she had fulfilled it better than her mother had dreamed, had made herself an entire guard, a perfect custody. But custody, she understood lying down, is a thing one has for a time and afterwards hands over; one has the custody of a child while it is a child, and when it grows one gives it back the custody of itself, because an adult guards itself. And she had not made the handing-over. She went on holding what was no longer hers, and in holding it she covered the children from no danger, only squeezed them from afar with a guard no one had asked of her.

Beneath everything, in that bed, with the dressing gown still in her hand, there was a memory she did not want to let in and that came in all the same. Belarmino, alive, reaching his hand in the dark to her side of the bed when she got up at night to keep watch over the little ones, and saying to her, in the thick voice of sleep, come to sleep, woman. Come to sleep. In that time there was a hand in the bed that called her back, there was a man telling her that the guard was not hers alone, that one could sleep, that the world held together without her one night. Then Belarmino died, and the hand ceased to be in the bed, and there was no one else to say come to sleep, woman, and Custódia, alone, made of the children the whole family, put in them the place of the husband and the place of herself, all in one bundle, and kept watch over them with a doubled force, her own and that of the missing man. That was why to let go of them was worse for her than for the others: to let go of them was to be left with the bed empty on both sides, with no children and no husband, alone in a doubled loneliness, and perhaps that was the deepest root of the vigil, the fear that, the guard lowered, there would be left in the house absolutely no one, not even a shared sleep.

And there was still, beneath that, the truth that cost her most to look at, and that she looked at there in the dark because she no longer had anywhere to flee: that the vigil, besides fear for the children, was also a hiding place from herself. While she was on guard she did not have to ask herself what she wanted for herself, who she was beyond a mother; the rounds at three filled her nights and spared her the question. To come down from the guard was to come face to face with a woman who had made herself only a guard and knew nothing else of herself, and that frightened her more than all the imagined dangers of the children together, because she might look inward and find no one there.

To protect and to trust, she understood, had been in her life almost opposites, when they should have been two ages of the same thing. To protect is the love of the first times, it is to do, to act, to be on guard, and it gives a good sense of usefulness. To trust is the love of the later times, it is to not do, to step back, to let the other walk, and it gives no usefulness at all, it even seems, to one who does not understand, abandonment. And it was that one, the not-doing, the harder love, and it was that one the children now asked of her, and she was going to have to learn to give it against all her nature of a mother who protects by doing.

She did not get up that night. She stayed in bed until the window began to grey, and it was the first time in thirty years that she did not make the rounds. But let no one think that from one night to the next the sentinel came down from the rampart and went to sleep at ease, because that woman does not exist in Véspera nor anywhere. The next night the body woke her again at three, and the body knew nothing of what the head had understood, the body went on on guard, awaiting a cry from thirty years ago.

She began then to do small things, because she understood that all at once she could not, but perhaps in bits. One night she tried again to stay in bed; she held out a few minutes, with her heart leaping and her feet already on the floor, and then gave way and got up and went to see the rooms, but it was a few minutes, and the next night it was a few minutes more. She tried to leave the kitchen light off; she did not manage it, she lit it halfway down the stairs, but she noticed that she had hesitated, and the hesitation was new.

And one afternoon she telephoned Mercês and forced herself to a thing that cost her more than any rounds. Custódia, who in a call to her daughter knew only how to pour out the complaint, the unease, the do take care and the watch out and the you tell me nothing, that time bit her tongue against all of those and looked in her chest for a thing that was not complaint, and almost did not find it, so little practised was she at it. Mercês on the other end, awaiting the usual, the litany of the anxious mother, and Custódia swallowing all of it and saying, at last, with her voice trembling from an effort Mercês could not guess, that everything was well there, that the daughter should not worry, that she had even gone today to the churchyard to talk with Felizarda. It was a small sentence and it cost her as it costs to lift a piece of furniture alone, and Mercês fell silent a moment on the other end, with that silence of one who hears a familiar voice say a thing it never said, and answered, slowly, that it was a good thing, mother, a good thing. And Custódia hung up and was left with the telephone in her hand feeling strangely light and strangely exposed, like one who lowered the shield for an instant and discovered that the air did not kill her.

They were minute steps, almost invisible, and Custódia, used to doing everything with force and all at once, grew impatient at their slowness, wanted already to be cured, already to sleep, already to be another. But she went on learning, against all her nature, that these things are not changed with force, they are changed with patience, step by step, and that haste, which had been her ally a whole lifetime, was here her enemy. To come down from a rampart of thirty years was not done in a leap. It was done slowly, with relapses, as one gives up a vice, and she, who had never had any patience, was going to have to learn that too.

She had ahead of her, she understood, not a decision but a breaking of habit, the slow and hard task of teaching a sentinel's body to lower the guard after thirty years of mounting it. And that was going to need time, and perhaps more than time; perhaps it needed a thing that would shake her enough to force her to change for good. A thing that, without her knowing, was already on the way, in the form of a night in which the sentinel, from keeping watch over the others so long, was going to discover that there was no one in the house keeping watch over her.
