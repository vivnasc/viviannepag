## 7. The fall with no witness

The things that change our lives often come small and banal, and Custódia's came in the form of a badly laid rug and of eyes tired from not sleeping in thirty years. It was not a grave illness, it was not a hospital drama; it was a stumble, a fall, an aching hip, things that happen to one of her age and that normally come to nothing. But by the way it happened — at night, alone, in the dark, with no one — that banal fall opened in Custódia a window onto the truth of her life that no word of Felizarda's or of the Keeper's had managed to open, because one thing is to hear oneself told that one is alone, another is to feel it in the body, fallen on the cold floor, awaiting a help that does not come because there is no one to bring it.

It happened at night, of course, because it was at night that Custódia lived, and it was the thing that shook her enough to force her to look at what she had become: she fell, alone, in the dark, and there was no one keeping watch over her, and it was that absence of a witness, more than the fall, that wounded her to the depths.

She had got up at three, as always, for the rounds of the house, to go and see the empty rooms, to listen to the silence, and in the dark of the corridor, with the delayed sleep of one who never sleeps well, she stumbled, and fell, and struck herself hard, and stayed on the floor of the corridor, alone, aching, unable to get up at once. And it was there, fallen on the floor of the corridor of her silent house, at three in the morning, that Custódia had the cruellest perception of her life: that she could stay there, fallen, and no one would know, no one would come, no one kept watch over her. She, who had kept watch over her children a whole lifetime, who had spent thirty years standing sentinel so that they would never lack someone attentive in the hour of danger, was now herself in danger, fallen on the floor, and there was no sentinel at all keeping watch over her, there was no one awake listening for her fall, there was no Custódia for Custódia, because she had spent her life being the guard of others and no one had stayed on guard over her.

She stayed on the floor some time, unable to get up at once, and it was a time that seemed to her a lifetime, there, face down in the dark of the corridor that smelled of the wax of the rooms she so cared for, with her hip throbbing and the silence of the house upon her like a lid. And in that time on the floor, Custódia had thoughts she had never had, because the position of one who is fallen and alone forces thoughts that the position of one who is standing and busy keeps away. She thought of the quantity of nights she had spent on her feet, on watch, so that her children would never be alone in danger; and she thought that now it was she who was alone in danger, fallen, and that there was no one, and the injustice of that, the irony of that, ran through her like a knife: the guard of everyone, fallen, without a guard. She thought that if she cried out no one would hear, because the nearest house was far and it was night; she thought that the telephone was in the kitchen, far; she thought that she was, there, as helpless as she had never let a child be, and that that helplessness was the harvest of a life of being she who shelters and never the sheltered.

To get up was hard, and slow, and each movement hurt, and Custódia had, in trying to raise herself alone from the floor, a new humility, that of a body that is no longer the one of before, that no longer gets up as it used to get up, that is entering the age in which one falls and does not get up easily. And that humility of the body joined that of the spirit, because Custódia understood that she was entering a phase of life in which she herself was going to need care, vigil, someone to keep watch over her, and that she had organised her life exactly contrary to what that phase asked: she had made herself alone, with no one near, refusing care, being the guard of others, when what was coming was her needing a guard. She had spent her life keeping watch, and came to the age of needing to be watched over with no one at her side, because she had driven everyone away with her self-sufficiency, with her refusal to need, with the mask of strength she had worn her whole life.

She managed to get up, at the end of a time, aching but whole, and sat in the kitchen, in the kitchen of the lit light, trembling, and it was not from the cold, it was from what she had just understood. She thought: if I had broken a hip, I would have stayed here on the floor until when? Until Mercês wondered that I did not answer the telephone, and that could be days. Until someone from the village noticed that I did not go out, and that could be days too. I, who never left a child alone a minute in danger, could stay here fallen days without anyone knowing, because I mounted guard over everyone and no one mounts guard over me. And that perception, that of the sentinel alone at her post, that of the guard who has no one to guard her, was the beginning of Custódia's bottom, because it showed her, in a way that could not be ignored, the price of a whole life of keeping watch over others: the absolute solitude of one who has spent herself in caring and who, in the hour of needing care, discovers that there is no one, because she never let there be, because she was always the one who cared and never the cared-for.

She telephoned Mercês in the morning, but did not tell her of the fall, hid it, as she hid everything, said that all was well, because a guard does not worry others, a guard endures. And only on hanging up did she understand the absurdity of that: that she had just fallen alone at night, that she had been in real danger, and that her first reaction had been to hide that from her daughter so as not to worry her, so as to go on being the strong one, the one who gives no trouble. And she understood that it was that mechanism that had left her so alone: she always hid her frailties, always showed herself well, never asked anything, and so she had taught everyone, her whole life, that she did not need, that with her there was no need to worry, and the harvest of that mask of strength was the solitude, because no one keeps watch over one who never shows that they need to be watched over. In hiding the fall from Mercês, Custódia was doing again, on automatic, the gesture of a lifetime, that of not letting anyone care for her; and it was in noticing that gesture, in catching herself hiding it, that it showed her that her solitude was not a chance, it was her work, built hidden frailty by hidden frailty, across a life of playing the strong one.

And she understood a thing deeper still, there in the kitchen, trembling: that her vigil, which she had always taken for love, had also, at bottom, a root of fear and of pride that she had never wanted to see. Fear of not being needed, of ceasing to have a post, of discovering, on coming down from the watch, that without the vigil she did not know who she was. And pride, the pride of being the one who keeps watch, the one who never fails, the one who endures, the indispensable one, the pride that had made her always refuse to be cared for, that had made her always be the strong one, the guard, and never the guarded. And it was that pride, she understood, that had now left her alone on the floor of a corridor at three in the morning, because a woman who never let anyone care for her, who made a point of always being the sentinel and never the protected, comes to old age with no one on guard, because she taught everyone, her whole life, that she did not need, that she endured, that she was the one who took care of others.

She thought, in that kitchen, of Idália, the mother who had withered from longing, and of Felizarda, the one who had flown, and understood that she was at a crossroads and that the fall had been the warning that she had to choose a path. She could go on as she was, and she would be Idália, or worse, she would be a fallen sentinel who one day would not get up; or she could change, come down from the watch, and perhaps still reach some of Felizarda's life, some good years, some rest, some company. And she understood that the fall, however much it had frightened her, had been perhaps a stroke of luck, a warning in time, because it had shown her the end of the path she was walking before she got there for good, had let her see, in one night, the absolute solitude toward which her vigil pushed her, and sometimes one needs to see the abyss up close to find the courage to change course, and Custódia had seen the abyss, fallen on the floor of the corridor at three in the morning, and she was going, from that vision, to draw at last the strength to do what Felizarda, Mercês, and the Keeper had been telling her for so long.

Still on the floor, before she managed to get up, Custódia had a memory that came to her from the depths of the years and that tightened her heart: the memory of herself, young, attending to fallen children. How many times had she not run, in quick steps, to a child who had fallen, who cried, who had hurt themselves, and raised them, and consoled them, and cleaned their knee, and given them her lap. A thousand times. It had been her life, attending to those who fell. And now it was she who had fallen, and there were no quick steps running, there was no lap, there was no one to raise her, there was no mother at all for the mother. The image of the two moments side by side — she young attending to her children, she old fallen with no one to attend to her — was of a sadness that ran through her whole, because it summed up the fate of one who gives themselves without keeping anything for themselves: they spend their life attending, and when they fall, there is no one to attend, because they taught the world to count on them and never to count on themselves.

And she thought, there on the floor, of a thing she had never thought: that perhaps she had done wrong in always being so strong. That perhaps, if she had let the children care for her sometimes, if she had shown weakness, if she had asked, if she had allowed the relationship to be from two sides and not only her giving, perhaps now she would not be so alone. Her strength, which she had always taken for a virtue, showed itself to her now, there on the floor, also as a mistake, because the strength that lets no one in is a strength that leaves one alone, and Custódia, the strongest of mothers, the one who never needed anyone, was now fallen and alone precisely for never having needed anyone, for having made of self-sufficiency a rampart that kept everyone on the outside, including those who loved her and who would have wanted, if she had let them, to care for her.

That night, Custódia did not lie down again. She stayed in the kitchen until morning, thinking, and it was one of the most important nights of her life, because it was the night in which the vigil turned against her and showed her its own face. She had kept watch her whole life against the danger to her children; and the danger that caught her, in the end, was not the children's, it was hers, it was the danger of a woman alone who had spent herself guarding others and who had kept nothing for herself, not even a person who would stay on guard over her. She noticed, there on the floor, a small thing that stayed with her: that no one knew where she was at that hour, and that no one would notice her absence until the next day, perhaps. Mercês counted on news from her mother, but not at fixed hours; Tobias, far away, could go days without calling; the village slept. Custódia was, on that floor, in a hole of time in which she existed for no one, in which she could disappear without the world noticing at once, and that perception, that of being able to disappear without anyone noticing in time, was perhaps the most solitary of her life. She, who for thirty years had guaranteed that no child of hers was ever alone in danger, was now exactly in that situation she had always kept away from her children: alone in danger, with no one attentive. She had given her children what she had never kept for herself, and the bill of that generosity without measure came to her there, on the cold floor of the corridor.

She thought, afterward, already seated in the kitchen, of Belarmino, of the husband dead so long, and had, for the first time in years, a longing for him not as the father of her children but as her companion, as the man who told her come and sleep, woman, who reached his hand to her in bed, who wanted to care for her and whom she had never let care for anything because she was always on guard. And she understood that she had lost, with his death, not only a husband, but the only person who had ever wanted to keep watch over her, and that after him she had never again let anyone take that place, because she had made herself the guard of all and the guarded of none. She thought that perhaps, if Belarmino were alive, she would not be there, fallen and alone at dawn, because there would be someone in the house, someone to whom she would be missed at night, someone who would notice her; and she understood that her solitude was not only the children's having departed, it was of a whole life of driving away from her whoever wanted to care for her, beginning with her own husband, whom she had never let into the intimacy of her vigil.

The sentinel had fallen at her post, and there had been no one to notice it, and that image — the guard fallen alone in the dark, with no one to keep watch over her — was the one that finally forced her to admit what Felizarda and Mercês had told her and she had not wanted to hear: that she had to change, that she could not go on like this, that a life of pure vigil had led her to a bottom of pure solitude, and that if she did not come down from the watch, and soon, she was going to end as that night had ended, fallen and alone, with the difference that the next time perhaps she would not manage to get up.
