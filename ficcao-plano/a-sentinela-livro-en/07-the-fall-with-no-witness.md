## 7. The fall with no witness

The things that change our lives often come small and trivial, and Custódia's came in the form of a badly laid rug. It was not a grave illness, it was not a hospital drama, it was a stumble in the dark, an aching hip, things that happen to one of her age and that by day, in the light, with people in the house, would have come to nothing. But that night had no light, nor people, nor witness, and it was for that, and only for that, that a fall of nothing opened her a window that neither Felizarda nor the Keeper, with all their words, had managed to open.

She got up at three, as on every night, because it was at three that her body woke for the rounds even with no one left to do the rounds over. She put her feet on the floor, sat a moment on the edge of the bed, and from the empty side, from his side, there came to her, without her calling it, Belarmino's voice, come to sleep, woman, stay where you are, as he used to say, with his hand reaching across the sheets in search of hers. Thirty years later he still reached his hand to her in the dark, and she, thirty years later, still got up. She put on her slippers and went out into the corridor.

The corridor smelled of the wax of the rooms. She waxed them every fortnight, the empty rooms, Mercês's, Tobias's, always ready, always clean, as though they were going to arrive that night drenched from the road, and it was that smell, sweet and closed, that received her in the dark of each rounds. It was in that smell that she stumbled. The rug was badly laid, its corner had been lifting for months and she said every week that she had to fasten it, and never fastened it, because the house was full of things she kept for an afterwards that did not come, and the corner of the rug caught her slipper, and the floor came to meet her faster than her body knew.

She struck her hip first and her shoulder after, and the sound of the fall crossed the whole house and came back, and then nothing came back any more, and it was the after that frightened her. She lay on her side, on the cold floorboards, her face turned to the skirting board and the smell of the wax now flush with her nose, and for an instant she thought of nothing, only felt, the cold of the floor climbing her back, the hip throbbing with a deep, old throb, of bone, and the silence. The silence was the worst part. It was not lack of noise, it was a thing that weighed, that came down on her like a lid on a pot, and beneath the lid was she, fallen, and nothing more.

Come on, she said low, get up. She said it to the house, or to the dark, or to no one, and she put her hand on the floor and pushed, and the hip answered with a stab that made her stop and groan between her teeth. She tried again, with the other side, holding to the wall, and the wall was far and smooth and had nowhere for the hand to take hold, and she slipped and went back to the floorboards, and stayed. She panted. Her nightdress was twisted and cold against her leg, and her heart beating in her ears, and it was there, lying on her side on the corridor floor of her own house at three in the morning, that the thought came to her, not in big words but as a thing one feels in the belly: I can stay here.

I can stay here, and no one comes.

She lay still letting the idea settle, and the idea went on growing in the dark, cold as the floor. The telephone was in the kitchen, at the end of the corridor, on the wall, far, as far as if it were in another house, and she could not reach the kitchen even crawling, for the hip would not allow it. The nearest neighbour's house was at the end of the street and had had its lights off for hours. If she cried out, and she was not going to cry out, for a woman does not cry out at night fallen in her nightdress, but if she cried out, no one would hear. She was there, and the world went on outside sleeping, and between her and the world there was the corridor in darkness, the rug that betrayed her, and a distance that standing was ten steps and fallen was a whole lifetime.

And then the irony came to her, and it came embodied, inside the body, not as a pretty sentence one thinks but as a tightness in the throat: she, the sentinel. She, who for thirty years had mounted guard on the rampart so that no child of hers should ever be alone in danger, who had slept with one ear awake, who had got up a thousand times in the night to go and see if they breathed, she was now fallen at her own post, in real danger, and there was no sentinel at all on her rampart. There was no Custódia for the Custódia. The guard of everyone, fallen in the dark, with no guard. She laughed, a dry laugh that hurt her ribs, because there was nothing else to do with it but laugh, and the laugh came out by itself into the corridor and no one heard it either.

It was on that floor, unable to get up, that the memory came to her, and it came clear as though it had been the day before. Tobias, small, perhaps five, running in the yard and sprawling on the stones, and his cry tearing the afternoon, and she already running before the cry ended, already in the yard, already on her knees beside him, lifting him to her lap, turning him to the light to see the grazed knee, blowing on the wound, saying there there mother is here, mother is here. She still felt his weight on her lap, the smell of his sweaty hair, the run down the corridor with him clinging to her neck to sit him on the kitchen bench and wipe the blood. It had been her life, that. To come running. The quick steps down the corridor whenever one of them fell, the lap always ready, mother is here.

And now it was she who was fallen in the same corridor, and there were no quick steps coming, there was no lap, there was no one saying there there, I'm here. It was the same house, the same corridor down which she had so many times run to come running, and it was deserted. There is no mother for the mother, she thought, and the sentence went all through her, because it was exact. She had given the lap a thousand times and never kept one for herself. She had taught the world to count on her and never to count on her own self. And the bill for all that generosity reached her there, against the skirting board, on the cold floor that smelled of the wax of the rooms where no one slept any more.

Getting up took her a long time and cost her everything. It went in parts, like dismantling an old piece of furniture in reverse, first getting on all fours biting the pain, then reaching the latch of Mercês's door and hauling herself up by it trembling, then standing leaning against the wall breathing until the house stopped spinning. Each movement was a negotiation with the body, and the body was no longer the one of before, no longer obeyed, no longer got up as it used to get up, and Custódia knew there, in the dark, a humility she had never had, that of one who understands she has entered the age in which one falls and no longer gets up easily, in which the next time perhaps one does not get up at all.

She reached the kitchen holding to the walls and turned on the light, and the kitchen light hurt her eyes and did her good all the same, because it was light, it was the end of the dark. She sat in her usual chair, at the table, and stayed trembling. It was not from the cold, though she was cold. It was from what she had just seen of herself. She put her hands on the table to watch them stop trembling and they did not stop, and she stayed looking at her own hands, the hands that had raised so many from the floor, now trembling alone on the oilcloth, and she made the count, the true count, the one she never let herself make. If I had broken the hip, she thought, I'd have stayed there on the floor until when? Mercês counts on news from me but not at fixed hours, she might wonder after a day, after two. Tobias goes days without calling, far off as he is. The village does not notice my absence from one day to the next. I could have stayed in that corridor two days, three, without anyone knowing, I, who never left a child alone in danger a single minute. I mounted guard over everyone and no one mounts guard over me.

In the morning, when the light was already coming grey through the kitchen window and she was still in the same chair, not having lain down, the telephone rang. She got up slowly, with the hip complaining at each step, and went to answer, and it was Mercês, with the voice still of one just woken, and the granddaughter chirping in the background, Aurélia asking for the bread. Good morning, mother, said Mercês, is everything all right there?

And Custódia, with the hip throbbing, with the whole night on her back, with the bruise already darkening on her thigh beneath her nightdress, heard herself answer, everything's fine, child, everything's grand, I slept like a stone, and you, and Aurélia, is the little one well, I hear her there, tell her grandma sends a kiss, and she carried the talk on ahead, quickly, lightly, asking after the school and the weather and the little one's soup, filling the air with well-being so that not a chink should be left through which the truth could pass. She said everything was fine three times in two minutes. And it was in the middle of the third that she caught herself.

She caught herself hiding. She heard her own voice lying, sweet and firm, the same voice that her whole life had said don't worry about me, the same that so many times had said to the children mother holds out, mother is well, and she stopped in the middle of the sentence, with the truth in her mouth, I fell tonight, Mercês, I fell alone in the dark and couldn't get up and I was afraid, and she did not say it. She swallowed it. She said instead that she had to go put the water on, and sent another kiss to the granddaughter, and hung up.

She stayed with her hand on the telephone, on the kitchen wall, looking at it. And she understood, all at once, with a clarity that made her sit down again, that that, that small gesture of hiding, was the whole mechanism of her loneliness laid bare. It had not been the fall that left her alone. It had been that, repeated over a whole lifetime. Each time she had shown herself well when she was unwell, each time she had said I need nothing when she needed everything, each time she had hidden a weakness so as not to be a trouble, she had taught one more person not to worry about her, to count on her holding out, to leave her on guard and never guarded. Her loneliness was no chance fallen from the sky. It was her own work. She had built it with her own hands, hidden frailty by hidden frailty, and had just laid another brick in the wall without noticing, on the telephone, in the morning, with the aching hip, saying everything's fine.

And, beneath everything, sitting in the kitchen by the lit light, trembling, she saw the root, and the root was not pretty. She had always taken her vigil for love, and there was love, but there was also, beneath it, what she had never wanted to look at, fear and pride. Fear of not being needed, of coming down from the rampart and discovering that without the post she did not know who she was, that a woman who ceases to keep watch is left without a face. And pride, the pride of being the one who holds out, the one who never fails, the one who is no trouble, the indispensable one, the pride that had made her refuse, always, over a lifetime, that anyone should care for her. It was that pride that had put her there, alone on the floor of a corridor at three in the morning. One who never lets anyone in reaches old age with the whole house only for herself, and the whole floor only for herself, and the whole fall only for herself.

She thought, as the day cleared, of Idália and Felizarda, the two set side by side like two ends of a road. Idália, who had withered with longing, shut in, in want, awaiting one who did not return. Felizarda, who had flown, who had come down from the guard in time and gained good years, company, rest. And Custódia understood that she was at a crossroads, and that the fall, however much it had frightened her, had perhaps been a stroke of luck, a warning in time, because it had shown her, in a single night, the end of the road she was on before she got there for good. She could go on as she was, and she would be Idália, or worse, she would be a sentinel who one day falls and does not get up. Or she could change, come down from the rampart, fasten the rug, let in whoever wished to come in, and perhaps still reach something of Felizarda.

She thought again of Belarmino, and this time not as the father of the children but as her man, the one who reached his hand to her in the bed, the one who said to her come to sleep, woman, the only one who had ever wanted to keep watch over her and whom she had never let keep watch over anything, because she was always on guard, always standing, always seeing to everything but to letting herself be loved. She had lost with him, she understood, more than a husband. She had lost the only person who had asked, for years, only to keep watch over her, and whom she had sent off to the edge of the bed with a coming, lie down, I'll be there in a minute, and never came. If he were alive, perhaps she would not have lain fallen until daybreak, because there would be someone in the house she was missed by, someone who would notice her in the empty bed and come to the corridor with the light in his hand. But she had sent him off first, and then the children, and was left, in the end, exactly as she had made herself, alone on the rampart, guard of all and guarded by none.

The sentinel had fallen at her post, and there had been no one to notice. And it was that image, of the guard alone in the dark with her hip on fire, with no witness, that finally forced her to admit what Felizarda and Mercês had been telling her for so long and she had not wanted to hear, that she had to change, and soon, that a life of pure vigil had brought her to a bottom of pure loneliness, and that if she did not come down from the rampart she was going to end the night as she had ended that one, fallen and alone on the cold floor, with the difference that the next time perhaps she would not reach the kitchen light.
