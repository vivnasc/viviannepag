## 2. The night keeping watch saved

The fever does not knock at the door. It comes in without asking, and Custódia knew it was in the house before she knew it with her head, the same way one knows a child is crying before it cries: she went to a room that, when she had put Tobias down, was only warm, and was now a small ember inside a blanket.

He was still a baby, Tobias, of the age when a child does not speak its trouble, it whimpers. Belarmino was away, on a job far off, the kind that took a man for weeks, and that that night Custódia wished she could undo with her hands, bring him out of the dark along the road, come to sleep, woman, he always said, his hand reaching across the cold side of the bed, and his side was empty and cold and the whole night ahead. In the other room Mercês slept, small too, mouth open, oblivious, and it was good that she slept: one child to keep watch over was enough.

Custódia took her son in her arms and her son was burning. It was not the heat of one wrapped too warm, it was a fire from inside, a fire that came from his temples to her hand as though the boy were a roof tile in the August sun, and his chest rose and fell fast, too fast, in a short breath she counted without noticing, one two, one two, and each time she counted it her own heart went after, out of step. She lit the lamp with one hand only, the match trembling, and by the yellow light she saw her son's face flushed and his eyes half closed, glazed, looking at a place that was not the room.

You're hot, my love, she said to him low, in the voice that did not want to frighten him nor to frighten herself. You're very hot. Mother is going to cool you, do you hear, mother is here.

She set to work like one with no right to think. She went to the kitchen barefoot and the floor bit her soles with that cold of old houses that climbs through the night, and the water in the jug was not enough, it was warm from the house, and she knew she had to go to the well. She wrapped the boy in a clean cloth, laid him in the middle of the big bed, in his father's place, with bolsters on either side so he would not turn, and went out to the yard with the basin under her arm, barefoot, in her nightdress, and the night out there was pitch and crickets and the bucket went down with a splash that seemed very loud to her in the silence. She hauled the rope with her hands burning from the cold, filled the basin from the deep water of the well, water that cut, and came back inside spilling along the way, leaving a wet track on the floor that would still be there in the morning.

She wetted the cloth, wrung it, laid it on her son's forehead. The boy shuddered at the cold touch and whimpered, and she almost took it off out of pity, but did not, she left it, because a mother's pity at that hour is a traitor and Custódia already knew it without anyone ever having taught her. She changed the cloth the moment it warmed. She put a fresh one on his forehead, another on his neck, opened his clothes, blew on him slowly, counted his breath, and between one thing and another she talked, talked to him without stopping, because her voice was the thread by which the boy knew he was not alone in the fire: here I am, she said, I won't leave you, mother does not sleep, you can be resting that mother's eyes are open for the two of us. She looked at the clock on the chest of drawers, the hands seemed nailed, it was two, then it was a little past two, then it was still a little past two, and the fever did not yield, and inside Custódia prayed a prayer that was not of the church, it was of a mother, it was a please repeated to a God of whom at that hour only one thing interested her, that he not take this one from her.

And it was when she thought the worst had passed, it was when the boy seemed to calm and she allowed herself the luxury of resting her head against the wood of the bed for a second, it was there that it happened.

Tobias gasped in another way. His eyes rolled back, only the white was left, and the small body stiffened all at once, hard, arched, trembling all over in a trembling that was not of the cold nor of the fever, a trembling of a machine, and from his lips came a little foam, and the breath locked. Custódia did not think. What thought was her body. She already had him on his side before her head told her put him on his side, already had her finger wiping his mouth before she knew why, already held him against her with a firmness that did not squeeze, turned over, his face down, her cupped hand catching his forehead, and she said in his ear in a voice that came from a deep, calm place she did not know she had, that's it, let it out, let it come, mother has you, mother does not let go, breathe for me, Tobias, breathe, and she counted the seconds of the trembling that seemed to her a whole life and were perhaps twenty, and at the end of the twenty the little body softened suddenly, all of it, like cloth, and for an instant that stopped the world the boy did not breathe at all.

No, said Custódia. Only that. No.

And she blew on his face, and patted him lightly on the back with her palm, no, you don't do this to me, and the boy tore in an enormous breath, a sob of air that filled his whole chest at once, and coughed, and cried, and the crying was the most beautiful thing Custódia heard in her life, more beautiful than any music, because a child that cries is a child that is, and she pressed him to her chest and cried too, in silence so as not to frighten him, with her face buried in his wet hair, trembling now herself, all of her, who had held his trembling whole and only fell apart afterwards.

There is no neighbour one calls at such an hour, but Custódia called. She took the boy in her arms, wrapped up, crossed the yard and knocked at Rosenda's door, the neighbour from the house next door, three raps and the name said low so as not to wake the village, Rosenda, oh Rosenda, it's me, and the light came on inside and Rosenda came to the door in a headscarf and a sleepy face, and it was enough to see Custódia's face to wake her for good. He had a turn, Rosenda, said Custódia, and her voice trembled now that it could tremble. He had a fit, the fever climbed and he had a fit, but it has passed now, he's breathing now, but I won't stay alone, come with me, stay with me until it's day.

Rosenda came. She put water on the fire, made her a tea Custódia did not drink, and stayed near, in the chair in a corner, saying the things old women say at such hours, that this business of children's fevers frightens much and kills little, that hers had had the same and there she was, a grown woman, that the worst has passed now, neighbour, see how he already breathes deep, and Custódia heard and did not hear, because her eyes were where they had been the whole night, on her son's chest rising and falling, slower now, fuller, and on the forehead that hour by hour grew less ember and more skin.

By daybreak the fever yielded for good. The grey came to the window, first the cockerel of some house, then that of another, and Tobias slept at last a true sleep, loose, a baby's, his mouth making that gesture of one suckling in dreams, and he breathed in peace, long and deep, and would not even remember, that one, anything. For him that night had never existed. Rosenda went off, with her hand on Custódia's shoulder and a now you sleep too that she did not hear, and Custódia was left alone with her saved son, leaning against the wall of the room, sitting on the floor at the foot of the bed with her knees against her chest, emptied of everything, without strength even to lie down.

And it was there, on the cold floor, at the first grey, that the thing came to her that never again let go. It was not relief only. It was a thing beneath the relief, warmer and more dangerous, a thing that rose through her chest like a tide: she had stood between her son and death, she, with her hands and her eyes, and had won. If she had slept the one second too many in which she rested her head, if she had lowered her eyes at the moment of the trembling, the boy would have gone. It was not God, it was not luck, it was no doctor, for there was none there. It was she. And that, Custódia discovered without knowing she discovered it, felt good. It felt good in a deep and secret way and almost with shame at being so good, because a woman who holds a life in her hands once like that knows forever after that she is worth something, that she is needed, that without her someone dies, and nothing in the world gives a certainty so great nor so hard to let go.

She leaned over the cradle of the bed, over her sleeping son, and made him, low, with her mouth almost in his hair, the promise that was to govern her whole life: never again, do you hear. Never again does mother lower the guard. As long as you are under this roof, mother's eyes do not close at ease, I swear to you. And the boy slept, and did not hear, and was not to remember, and she said it all the same, more to herself than to him, like one driving a nail into a wall for the rest of her life.

In the morning she got up, washed her face, fed Mercês, who woke oblivious to the whole night, asking for papa, and the kitchen light, that one, stayed lit. She noticed while sweeping that she had forgotten to put it out and did not put it out. She left it burning in the broad day, and was to leave it burning many and many nights after, years and years, without noticing what she was doing or why, because a lit light, since that daybreak, was for Custódia the way for the guard never to sleep.

And life proved her right, or she became convinced that it did, which for the matter is the same. There was, some years later, a winter night when Mercês, already a grown girl, woke coughing a dog's cough, locked, that closed off her air and put a thin whistle in her throat at each breath, and Custódia, who slept her straw sleep at the end of the corridor, heard her at the third fit and was at the side of her daughter's bed before she had finished waking. Sit up, child, sit up straight, open your mouth, she said, and she opened the window to the cold against all the sayings of the village, and put water to boil for the steam, and held the girl sitting against her chest until the whistle eased and the air passed clean again, and when Mercês fell asleep at last, slumped in her arm, Custódia did not lie down: she stayed in the chair at the foot of the bed watching her breathe, as she had stayed with Tobias, because she had already learned, and the lesson told her it was in the minute the mother lay down that the cough closed off again. You see, the guard said to her within, you see how it is needed. And the guard was right, that night and on a thousand nights like it, and it was from being right for so many years that Custódia never came to suspect the day when it would cease to be.

And it must be said plainly, before anyone judges Custódia with today's eyes: that was love, and it was true love, and it was right. In her time and in her village, a fever like this killed by night what by day would have been treated, there was no telephone, there was no car, there was no hospital a few minutes away, there was a mother alone against the dark, and a mother who kept watch was indeed, without metaphor, the only rampart between the child and death. Custódia kept watch well. She kept watch over the two of them well, all her life, and how many fevers she caught in time, how many frights she heard before they were disaster, because there was always the mother awake. Her error was not to keep watch that night. The error, that one, was still far off, and was of another nature, and was to take thirty years to show itself: it was not to understand that the night had ended. It was to go on a sentinel on the rampart long after the enemy had gone, mounting guard over a fever of which only she kept the memory, against a danger the saved son himself never knew he had run, faithful to a battle she had won and that no one else in the world remembered having been fought. The fever had passed thirty years ago. It was Custódia's body that never knew, never, that the war had ended.
