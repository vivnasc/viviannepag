## 2. The night keeping watch saved

The mothers of Custódia's age, and the generations before her, raised their children in a time and a world that asked for vigilance in earnest, and this must be said so that Custódia's vigil is not judged with the eyes of today. They were times of more danger and less safety net: illnesses that today are treated with a telephone call then killed a child in a night; there were not the means, the safeguards, the quick aid of now; and a mother was, far more than today, the true and only line between her child and a thousand dangers. Custódia's vigilance, seen in its time, was no exaggeration at all; it was what was expected of a good mother, it was the necessary thing, it was what saved children. Custódia's mistake was not to keep watch in those times; it was not to understand that the times had changed, that the world of her grown children was no longer the dangerous world of their childhood, that the safety net that had not existed had come to exist, that the vigilance that had been necessary had ceased to be. Custódia went on keeping watch as though it were still the old time, the time without aid, when the children already lived in a time of telephones and hospitals and safeguards, and that lag, between the time in which her vigil was formed and the time in which she went on applying it, was the root of the absurdity of her watch.

To understand Custódia one must go back to a night, far back, when the children were small, because it was on that night that she learned the thing that made her a sentinel for the rest of her life: that to keep watch saves, and that to sleep kills.

Tobias would have been still a baby, and he fell ill, with one of those childhood illnesses that climb suddenly, a fever that in an instant went from nothing to a fire, and it was at night, as these things happen, always at night, when there is no doctor near and the village sleeps and a mother is alone with a child burning in her arms. Belarmino was still living, but he was away, at a job far off, and Custódia was alone with the two children, and Tobias burned, and Mercês, small too, slept in the other room, and Custódia, that night, did not sleep, she kept watch.

One must understand what it was, in that time and that place, a night like that, to grasp what it stamped on Custódia. There was no doctor a telephone call away, no hospital a few minutes off, none of the safety one has today; there was a mother alone, in a house, with a child burning, and the village asleep, and the long night ahead, and the terrible awareness that everything depended on her, on what she knew how to do, on her attention, on her resistance to sleep. A mother, on those nights, was indeed the only rampart between her child and death, not by metaphor, but in earnest, and Custódia lived several of those nights, and in each she learned the same lesson, engraved ever deeper: that sleep was the enemy, that to lower the eyes was to risk, that a child's life could fit in the interval of a mother being distracted. No wonder that a woman who lived nights like that came out of them convinced that to keep watch was love and to sleep was danger; what is a wonder, and what is Custódia's tragedy, is that she went on living by that law long after the world had changed, long after the children had the doctor a telephone call away and their lives safe in their own hands.

She kept watch over her son the whole night. She put cold cloths on him, gave him water, held him, watched his breathing, counted his sighs, did not take her eyes off him for a second, because she was afraid, a mother's fear that is the greatest fear there is, the fear that her child would die in her arms if she lowered her eyes. And at a certain point, at dawn, in the worst of the fever, Tobias had a thing, a convulsion, a fright, and it was because Custódia was awake, because she was keeping watch, because she had not taken her eyes off him, that she attended to him in time, that she did what had to be done, that she held him and cooled him and saved him, or that she believed she had saved him, which for the purpose is the same. There was a moment, at daybreak, with her son at last breathing in peace, when Custódia, exhausted, leaning against the wall of the room, felt a thing she had never forgotten: a mixture of total relief and absolute power, the sensation of having been between her son's life and death and of having won, of having been she, her vigil, that made the difference. And that sensation, however terrible the night that had preceded it, was addictive, in the deep sense of the word, because it gave Custódia a certainty about her worth that she never again wanted to let go: the certainty of being indispensable, that her children's lives depended on her, that without her they could die. There are mothers who come out of a night like that relieved and able to forget it; Custódia came out marked, with the vigil engraved as a sacred duty, and perhaps because she needed, more than others, to feel herself indispensable, to have a worth no one could take from her, and the vigil gave it to her, made her the rampart, the saviour, the one without whom the children would die, and that identity, born on that dawn, was the one she wore for the rest of her life and the one it most cost her to take off.

In the morning the fever broke, and Tobias was well, and Custódia, exhausted, with her son sleeping at last in peace in her arms, made herself a promise that governed her life: never again would she lower the guard. Never again would she sleep at ease while she had a child under her roof. Because she had learned, that night, that a child's life could depend on the mother being awake, and that to sleep, to lower the eyes, to rest, was a luxury a mother could not allow herself, because it was in the instant she lowered her eyes that disaster could come.

Custódia recalled every detail of that night, even thirty years later, with the sharpness with which one recalls the nights that change our lives. She recalled the weight of Tobias burning in her arms, the smell of the fever, the well water she went to fetch for the cloths, the cold of the floor on bare feet, the distress that tightened her chest at each of her son's panting breaths. She recalled the solitude of that night, alone with two small children and one burning, with no doctor, with no one, only she against the illness, and she recalled above all the instant of the fright, the convulsion, the moment when she thought she was losing him, and the way her body reacted before her head, how her hands knew what to do, how she saved him. And she recalled the morning, the fever breaking, the son falling asleep at last in peace, and she exhausted, emptied, but with a new certainty driven into her breast: that she had been between her son and death, and that it had been her vigil that won. How does a woman forget a night like that. She does not. And Custódia did not forget, and she did well not to forget; the mistake was not to remember, it was to let the lesson of that night govern all the nights that came after, even those that had no fever at all to keep watch over.

It was a love, that, and it was a true love, of the greatest. Custódia kept watch over her children her whole life with the devotion of one who knows she is the only thing between them and danger, and she kept watch over them well, and thanks to that vigil the children crossed childhood and youth safe, because there was always the mother awake, the mother attentive, the mother who heard everything, who saw everything, who attended before the disaster happened. How many times will Custódia's vigil not have kept away a real danger, caught a fever in time, heard a child going out at night, smelled the gas, seen what a distracted mother would not see. Many. Custódia's vigil saved her children times without number, and she knew it, and that was her pride and her proof of love.

It must be said too where it came from, in Custódia, this readiness for eternal vigil, because it was not born only on that night of fever; the night of fever only gave form to a thing that was already in her. Custódia had grown up in a house where she learned early that the world was dangerous and that safety was won by force of attention. She had lost things when young, frights, perhaps one of her own that had marked her, and had brought to motherhood a distrust of the world, a certainty that, if one lowers the guard, disaster enters. The night of fever did not invent that certainty; it confirmed it, gave it the trial by fire, transformed an anxious tendency into a law of life. And that was why Custódia's vigil was so hard to let go of: it was not only a habit of thirty years, it was the expression of a way of seeing the world of a whole lifetime, that of one who believes that safety depends on her vigilance and that to relax is to invite tragedy. To change this was not to change a habit; it was to change a faith, the faith that the world does not crumble only because she holds it with her eyes, and to change a faith is one of the hardest things there is, because a faith is not argued with reasons, it is lived with the body, and Custódia's body believed, to the bone, that if she slept the world would fall.

But the promise she made that night — never again to lower the guard — had a hidden poison, and the poison was the word never. Because a promise of vigilance that has an end is love; a promise of vigilance that is forever is a prison. And Custódia had promised forever, had promised never again to sleep at ease, and she kept it, kept it even after the children grew up, even after they ceased to need a vigil, even after they went away, because a promise of forever does not know when to end, has no sign to tell it now you may stop, and Custódia stayed on guard long after the guard had ceased to be needed, bound to the promise of a dawn of fever that no one remembered any more but her.

Tobias, for his part, did not even remember the night that had saved him, because he was a baby, and had grown up without ever knowing that there had been a dawn in which he was between life and death and that it was his mother's vigil that brought him back. For Tobias, that night did not exist; for Custódia, it was the founding night of everything. And there was something moving and sad in this asymmetry: the mother living a whole life hostage to a night of which the saved son himself had no memory, mounting guard because of a danger the son never knew he had run. Children, in general, do not know the weight of the nights their parents kept watch over them; they live their light lives, without imagining the vigils that brought them there, and it is well that it be so, it is how it has to be. But there are parents who are left alone with that weight, with the memory of nights the children forgot, and who go on keeping watch against dangers that now exist only in their memory, and Custódia was one of those, a sentinel faithful to a battle of which only she kept the record.

There had been, in truth, more than one night like that, across the years of the children's childhood, and each reinforced the promise. There had been the night Mercês had a fit of coughing that seemed to drown her, and it was the awake mother who sat her up and saved her; there had been the time Tobias, an adolescent, went out and did not come back at the hour, and it was the mother on watch at the window who noticed and went after him and pulled him from bad company; there had been a thousand small times when Custódia's attention caught in time a thing that, without her, would have gone wrong. And each one of those times was, for Custódia, a proof to add to the file: you see, the vigil told her, you see how it is needed, you see how if I were not attentive things would happen. And she was right, while the children were children and adolescents at home. Custódia's mistake was never to have kept too much watch in those years; it was not to have understood that those years ended, that the vigilance that was necessary at twelve and at fifteen ceased to be at thirty and at forty, and that to go on applying to a distant adult the vigilance one applies to a child at home is not love too much, it is love out of its time, love that no longer finds its object and that therefore turns, from protection, into weight.

What she never learned, and what the story was going to teach her, is that there is a difference between keeping watch over a sick child and keeping watch over a healthy one, between mounting guard against a real danger and mounting guard against an imagined one. On the night of the fever, the vigil saved, because there was a danger to watch. But thirty years later, with the children grown, safe, far away, masters of their lives, there was no longer any danger to watch, and Custódia's vigil had become a guard against phantoms, a sentinel pointed at an empty field, a not-sleeping that no longer saved anyone because there was no longer anyone in danger. The fever had passed thirty years ago. But Custódia's body went on keeping watch as though it were the height of every night, because she had learned so deeply, on that dawn, that to sleep kills, that she could never again believe that, after all, at that point of her life, to sleep no longer killed anyone, and that the only person to whom her vigil did harm, now, was she herself, the exhausted sentinel who did not know the war had ended.
