## 3. The untouched rooms

A house always says who dwells in it, and Custódia's said a woman stopped in a time already past. Whoever entered saw an impeccable house, cared for to the smallest detail, but saw too, if they knew how to look, a museum house, a house where nothing changed, where everything was in the place of twenty years ago, where time had stopped at the point at which the house had been full. There were no new things, in Custódia's house, because new things would be of a new life, and she had no new life, she had the watch of the old life; there was no disorder, because disorder is of those who live, and she did not live, she kept watch; there was not, anywhere, a sign of a present Custódia, with present likings, with a life going on, there was only the careful conservation of a life that had ended. And the two children's rooms were the heart of that museum, its most sacred pieces, and it was in them that one saw, most clearly, what Custódia did with her life: she conserved it, instead of living it, kept it, instead of continuing it.

Custódia's house had two rooms that were two temples, and the way she cared for them said everything about what she had become: Mercês's room and Tobias's room, untouched, ready, waiting, guarded with the devotion with which one guards a sacred thing, or a dead thing one does not want to bury.

There was an irony in these guarded rooms, and it was that the children themselves, when they came, barely noticed them, or noticed them with a certain discomfort. Mercês, sleeping in her old room on visits, felt herself strange among the girl's dolls, she who was a mother of children; Tobias found it a little stopped in time, almost ghostly, the room of a boy he no longer was. The children had moved on, had changed, were other people, and the rooms kept them as they had been twenty years ago, as they no longer were, and so the rooms did not comfort them, but rather reminded them, with some unease, that the mother wanted them stopped in a time in which they no longer were. And there was here a truth Custódia did not see: that in keeping the children as they had been, she refused, at bottom, to know the children as they had become, refused to update their image, to accept that they had changed, to know the adults they were instead of the children they had been. The untouched rooms were, also, this: a refusal to let the children grow within the mother's head, an insistence on keeping them children, and the children felt it, and it cost them, because everyone wants to be seen as what they are and not as what they were, and Custódia's children felt that their mother still saw them as children, and that those rooms were the proof of it.

Mercês's room was as Mercês had left it on the day she had gone from home to marry, years before. The girl's dolls still on the shelves, though Mercês was already a mother of children; the school notebooks in a drawer; the bedspread she had chosen as a girl; everything in its place, everything clean, everything waiting for a Mercês who now had her own house and who would never again live in that room. Tobias's room the same, stopped in time, in the time when Tobias was a boy who lived there, a time that had ended long ago and that that room insisted on not letting end.

Custódia cared for those rooms as for a mission. Every week she changed the sheets of the beds where no one slept, made the beds with care, smoothed the bedspreads, dusted the shelves, aired the rooms, left everything ready, always ready, as though the children might arrive at any moment and need to find their post just as they had left it. And there was in this an immense tenderness, the tenderness of a mother who keeps the house open to her children, who says, without words, this is always your house, there is always a place here for you. But there was also, beneath the tenderness, a thing deeper and sadder, which was Custódia's inability to accept that the children had gone, to let those rooms cease being children's rooms to become something else, to admit that the life in the house had changed and that those spaces, kept like reliquaries, were after all the proof that she could not come down from the watch, could not accept that her post had emptied.

There was a difference between the two rooms that said something about the two children and about Custódia. Mercês's room was more untouched, more a museum, because Mercês had left home to marry, all at once, on an appointed day, and the room had stayed as in a portrait; Tobias's had something more unfinished, because Tobias had left bit by bit, going and coming back, until one day he came back no more, and the room kept that gradual departure, some things taken and others left, like a sentence interrupted halfway. And Custódia, who knew every detail, read in the rooms the two ways the children had left her, and both hurt her, Mercês's clean departure and Tobias's drawn-out one, because both, in the end, came to the same: an empty house and a mother guarding the traces of those who had departed. And she cared for the two rooms equally, with the same devotion, because for a sentinel there are no posts more important than others, there is the duty to guard all, and Custódia guarded those two temples with the equity of one who makes no difference between her children, giving each empty room the same useless and loving care.

When the children came to visit her, rarely, they slept in their rooms, and Custódia had then a few days of full happiness, the rooms at last occupied, the house at last full, the sentinel at last with someone to guard. But they were days, and then the children went away again, and the rooms went back to being empty, and Custódia went back to making the beds where no one would sleep, and each time she smoothed a bedspread over an empty bed it was like smoothing it over a small death, the death of the time when that house had been full, the death of the full-time mother she had been and who no longer had anyone to need her full-time.

There was, in the details of those rooms, an archaeology of Custódia's love that moved and hurt at the same time. In Tobias's room, on the wall, still the pencil marks where she had marked her son's height each year, a stairway of lines that stopped at the height of a boy of eighteen and had never climbed again, because the boy who had grown beyond those lines had grown far away, in houses Custódia did not measure. On the shelf, the school books, the trophies of small things, a notebook with a boy's handwriting. In Mercês's room, the mirror where the daughter had made herself up as a girl, the photographs tucked into the frame, the sewing box the daughter had used to learn to sew on her mother's lap. Custódia knew every object, and each object was a memory, and she moved through those rooms as through an album of a life that had been full and that now was only hers to recall. And there was tenderness in this, much of it; but there was also the refusal to accept the present, the clinging to a past kept in objects, and Custódia, without seeing it, lived more in the museum-rooms of her gone children than in her own present life, like one who prefers the memory of a full house to the silence of an empty one, and so embalms the memory instead of living the silence.

Mercês, once, on visiting her, suggested to her with care, with the care of one who touches a sensitive thing, that perhaps she could change the room, mother, make of it a sewing room, or a proper guest room, or whatever she liked, that she no longer lived there, that it made no sense to keep all that stopped in time. It was a dangerous subject, that of the rooms, and the village knew it, and so no one touched it; only Mercês, who was a daughter and had that right, had dared, and barely had she dared than she retreated, on seeing her mother's reaction. Because the rooms were not, for Custódia, rooms; they were the proof that she went on being on duty, the material proof that the house was ready, that the mother had not abandoned her post, and to touch the rooms was to touch that, was to suggest to her that the duty had ended, that she no longer needed to be ready, that she could come down from the watch, and that was, for her, at that time, unthinkable, intolerable, almost an assault.

And Custódia heard that as one hears a profanation, and answered badly, answered that the room was Mercês's, that it would always be Mercês's, that while she was alive that room stayed as it was, and Mercês, seeing that she had touched where she should not, retreated, apologised, changed the subject. That night, after Mercês's suggestion, Custódia went to her daughter's room and stayed there a long time, looking at everything, trying to imagine that room transformed, and could not, because each object seemed to her irreplaceable, each thing seemed to her a part of her daughter she could not take away. And she understood, in that moment, without yet admitting it entirely, that the problem was not the daughter needing the room — the daughter did not need it, she had said so herself —, the problem was she, Custódia, needing to keep it, needing that room untouched as proof that motherhood had not ended, as an anchor to a time that did not come back. To take Mercês's things out of that room would be, for Custódia, to admit that Mercês no longer lived there, that she had grown, that she had gone, that the time of the full house had ended, and that admission was exactly what Custódia could not make, and the untouched rooms were her way of not making it, of keeping the past alive by force of conserving it in objects, of refusing the present by guarding the past.

But the suggestion stayed with Custódia, and troubled her, not because it was bad, but because it touched a truth she did not want to see: that to keep the rooms untouched was not for the children, that the children did not need those rooms, that they had their houses; it was for her, it was for her to go on feeling herself a mother on watch, it was for her post not to empty for good, and that, therefore, the rooms she guarded in the name of her children were, in truth, guarded in the name of her own fear of ceasing to be needed.

There was a thing Custódia did and that she did not even confess to Mercês: sometimes, at night, when the solitude was great, she went into the children's rooms and sat there, in the dark, on the made bed where no one slept, and stayed, alone, smelling the room, remembering when that room had been full of a child, and it was a thing between comfort and torture, because the room brought her the presence and the absence at the same time, the child who had been there and the child who was no longer there. And she came out of those rooms more alone than she had entered, but she went back there, night after night, like one who visits a grave, because it was the way she had of being close to her gone children, and she did not understand that to be close to the children through empty rooms was to be ever farther from life, more attached to an absence, more unable to accept that the children were alive and well elsewhere and that the way of being close to them was not to keep watch over their empty rooms, it was to telephone them, to visit them, to live, and to let life, and not the mourning of an emptied house, be the bridge between her and them.

She did not want to see it, then. She went on making the empty beds, guarding the temples, keeping the post, and when she lay down, at night, in her own bed, she heard the silence of the two untouched rooms at the end of the corridor, and that silence was the sound of her life now, the sound of a guarded house where no one lived any more but the guard. There was still, in those rooms, a thing Custódia did and that was perhaps the most revealing of all: she left, in each one, small signs that the mother had been there, that the mother cared, a bunch of fresh flowers on the bedside table of Mercês's room, a shirt of Tobias's ironed and laid on his bed, as though the children might arrive at any moment and notice the presence of the mother who had cared for everything in their absence. They were gestures of love, and they were gestures of one who speaks with the absent, of one who keeps up a conversation with children who are not there, of one who refuses to accept the absence and so peoples it with small cares, like one who sets the table for guests they know will not come. And there was in this a solitude so deep that it moved one, that of a woman caring for empty rooms as one cares for people, speaking with the absence through flowers and ironed shirts, keeping alive, by force of small gestures, a presence that had gone years ago and that did not come back except for a visit.

And Custódia fell asleep badly, with the light sleep of a sentinel, with one ear on the silence of the empty rooms, awaiting a child's crying that had not come in thirty years, and that her body, faithful to the post, went on listening for, night after night, without rest, with no end in sight, with no one to tell her that she could, at last, stop listening.
