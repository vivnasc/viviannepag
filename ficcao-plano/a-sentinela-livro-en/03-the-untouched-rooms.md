## 3. The untouched rooms

A house always tells who dwells in it, and Custódia's told a woman stopped in a time that had passed. Spotless, tended to the last detail, and at the same time a museum house, where nothing changed, where everything was in the place of twenty years ago, at the point where the house had been full. There were no new things, because new things would belong to a new life, and Custódia had no new life, she had the guard of the old one. And the two rooms of the children, at the end of the corridor, were the heart of that museum, two temples kept with the devotion with which one keeps a sacred thing, or a dead thing one does not want to bury.

Mercês arrived on a Saturday afternoon, with her children and her husband and the bags, and slept, as she always slept when she stayed, in her old girlhood room, at the end of the corridor. Custódia had had it ready since morning: the bedspread pulled smooth without a crease, the dolls lined up on the shelf like small soldiers, the school notebooks in their usual drawer, the bunch of fresh flowers on the bedside table, all clean, all in the place of twenty years ago. It was the best night of the month for Custódia, the house full, voices in the rooms, water running in the bathroom at hours when the house had not heard water run in years, a grandchild running down the corridor whom she had to hush so as not to wake his brother. She kept watch over them all from within, lying in her own bed, with an ear at each door, glad with that gladness that came to her only when there were people to guard, the house at last with its posts manned, the sentinel at last with someone to keep watch over.

It was in the morning, at breakfast, with the coffee steaming and the children still asleep, that Mercês said the thing.

Mother, I sleep in there and it's as if I were ten years old again, she said, laughing, but it was a laugh with a bottom to it. I wake at night and don't know where I am, I see the dolls in the dark and I get a tightness, I swear to you I do. I'm forty-two, mother, I have three children, and I sleep in a girl's bed under my own dolls. It's strange. Don't you find it strange?

Custódia set down her cup. She found nothing strange about it. She found it the most natural thing in the world, a room that waited for a daughter, a daughter who came and found it as she had left it, and she said so, said the room was as Mercês liked it.

As I liked it, mother. As I liked it at sixteen. Mercês stirred the sugar slowly, choosing, and Custódia knew that way of choosing the words, it was her own, inherited in that same kitchen. I'm not the one of sixteen any more. And I thought, look, I thought, and don't be angry, that perhaps you could make something else of that room. A sewing room, mother. You who sew so well, who spend your life with the sewing box on your lap in the sitting room, in the awkward position, with the dim light. You'd put a good table there at the window, the machine, all the threads tidy, it would be a treat. Or a proper guest room, with a grown person's bed, for when we came. Whatever you wanted. I don't live here any more, mother. It makes no sense for all that to stay stopped in time just because of me.

There was a silence in the kitchen the size of a seam, as Mercês's mother also knew how to make, and the morning light came in from the side and struck the table, and Custódia felt a thing rise from her chest that was not anger yet but was on the way to being.

The room is yours, she said, and the voice came out harder than she meant. The room is yours and will always be yours. As long as I'm alive that room stays as it is. The dolls are not touched, the bedspread is not touched, nothing is touched. There must always be, in this house, a place that is yours, for the day you need it. A person never knows when they'll need to come back to their mother's house, Mercês. One never knows.

The matter of the rooms was dangerous, and the whole village knew it, and so no one touched it; only Mercês, who was a daughter and had that right, had dared, and barely had she dared than it could be seen she had repented. She looked at her mother over the cup, in that way one looks at someone one loves and has just hurt without meaning to. She opened her mouth to say something more, and did not say it. Forgive me, mother, was all she said, low, it was an idea, it was nothing, forget I spoke. And she changed the subject, asked after Benvinda, after the grocery, after some neighbour, and the morning went on, and by lunchtime no one spoke of it any more. At the end of the afternoon they went off, the bags, the children, the husband, the grandchild who ran down the corridor again and whom this time Custódia did not hush, because the silence that came after did not need sparing.

She stayed at the window watching them go down the path, the car taking the bend, the hand of a grandchild waving through the glass. Then the house closed over her like a hand.

That night, alone, she did what she did every week. She went to Mercês's room, took off the bedspread, changed the sheets of the bed where no one would sleep again so soon, pulled the bottom sheet until it had no fold, folded the top one with the crease in the right place, two hands' breadth turned over the bedspread, as her mother had taught her, as she had taught Mercês, on her lap, with the sewing box open between the two of them. She put the bedspread back, the one her daughter had chosen as a girl, and smoothed it with both hands open, slowly, top to bottom, and it was like smoothing it over a grown person's bed that was not there. No one sleeps here any more, she said low, to the room, to the dolls, to the daughter who was at that hour halfway down the road. But someone will. When you need it, it's here.

She took out the previous day's flowers, already drooping, and put in their place the fresh bunch she had brought from the shop, on the bedside table, turned towards the door, for the daughter to see first if she came in. She straightened the dolls the grandchild had tousled in his running, one by one, seated, hands in their laps, turned towards the bed. She opened the drawer, saw the school notebooks with Mercês's girlhood writing, closed it again. She dusted the frame of the mirror where her daughter had done herself up as a girl, the photographs tucked in the corners, Mercês's sewing box that she never took from there, because to take it would be a fault, that box in which the daughter had learned to sew on her mother's lap. Each object was a memory, and she moved through the room as through an album of a life that had been full and was now only hers to recall. She closed the door with care, like one closing the door of a room where a child sleeps.

She passed to Tobias's room, which was another thing, more unfinished, with its gaps and its things left half-done, because Tobias had not left all at once like his sister; he had left bit by bit, going and coming, taking some things and leaving others, until one day he came back no more, and the room kept that dragged-out departure like a sentence broken off halfway. On the shelf still the school books, a small trophy from some race, a notebook with a boy's writing. She made the bed the same way, smoothed the bedspread, and then went to the wardrobe and took down a shirt of her son's, of those that still remained there, washed and pressed by her the day before, and laid it on the bed, spread out, with the sleeves folded over the chest, ready to put on. It was here waiting for you, Tobias, she said, and the voice did a strange thing in her throat. I pressed it for you today. There's not a crease in it. If you arrived now it would be here.

Then she went to the wall, the wall beside the window, where the pencil marks were. She had marked them year by year, putting her son with his back to it, barefoot, leaning, no cheating, stand straight Tobias, and drawing above his head with the pencil and writing the year beside it, small. It was a ladder of marks that climbed the wall and stopped at one, higher than the others, with a number beside it, eighteen. There it had stopped. The boy who had grown beyond that mark had grown far off, in other houses, at heights Custódia no longer measured. She put her finger on the last mark, on the one of eighteen years, and stayed like that, with her finger on the wall, a long time, like one touching the forehead of someone asleep. You were here, she said. You were this big.

When she finished the two rooms, when everything was ready, the beds made, the flowers set, the shirt spread out, the dolls straight, she did not turn on the light to leave. Instead she went back to Mercês's room, in the dark, and sat on the edge of the made bed. She stayed there. The house was silent, that deep silence of houses where one person alone lives, and from the kitchen came, down the corridor, the thread of light she always left on, which entered through the chink of the door and gave the room a milk-coloured brightness. Custódia breathed. The room smelled of her daughter, of a remnant of daughter that twenty years had not taken, of a smell she could not name and that was the room's and nothing else, and to breathe it was at once the best and the worst of the night, because it brought Mercês back and said, in the same breath, that Mercês was not there. She sat on the bed of the daughter who had gone off to marry all at once, on a fixed day, and her body, in that dark, could as easily be that of a mother rocking a child as that of a woman at a grave. She did not cry. She was beyond that. She only stayed, breathing the smell, with her hands in her lap, like a sentinel who sits without leaving the post.

She came out of there more alone than she had gone in, and knew it, and was to come back the next night, and the next, like one who visits a grave that has no name because the dead are alive and living well elsewhere.

She lay down at last in her own bed, and at the end of the corridor stayed the two untouched rooms, silent, ready, awaiting no one. That silence was now the sound of her life, the sound of a guarded house where nothing lived any more but the guard. And there was a truth in it that Custódia would not let herself see, though Mercês's suggestion had grazed it that morning and had stayed, troubling her, the way a crumb stays in a bed: that those rooms were not for the children. The children had their houses, their rooms, their lives, and had no need of that place stopped in time. It was she who needed it. She needed the smoothed bedspread and the fresh flowers and the pressed shirt to go on feeling herself a mother on watch, for her post not to empty for good, so as not to have to admit that the time of the full house had ended and that the way to be near her children was not to keep watch over their empty rooms, it was to telephone them, to go and see them, to live. She kept the children as they had been twenty years ago and, without seeing it, refused to know the men and the woman they had become, held them children inside her own head, and they felt it, and it cost them, and that was why Mercês slept badly among the dolls and Tobias found it ghostly, a room of a boy he no longer was.

She did not want to see it, that night. She fell asleep badly, in a light sentinel's sleep, with an ear on the silence of the two rooms at the end of the corridor, awaiting a child's cry that had not come in thirty years and that her body, faithful to the post, went on listening for, night after night, without rest, with no end in sight, with no one to tell her that she could, at last, come down from the rampart and stop listening. But the daughter's suggestion had stayed in there, small and stubborn, beneath everything, awaiting its night.
