## 8. The Keeper of the Register

The paper was a pretext and Custódia knew it as she pushed open the door of the House of the Register, still with the night of the fall in her bones, still with the kitchen light lit inside her eyes. She had a certificate to see to, a thing of half an hour, a signature, a stamp, and she could have sent it by someone else, or left it for another day, but she had got up with her hands needing somewhere to go and her feet had brought her there, to the house where the registers of the village were kept, where there was, it was said in Véspera, a woman who saw deep and did not judge, and to whom people went to see to papers and from whom they came out with something else.

The house received her with the calm of old and certain things, the smell of paper and waxed wood, the light that came in at a slant and rested on the spines as though it too had learned not to hurry. Custódia, who went about with her nerves frayed, felt the calm climb her legs like one entering still water, and stood by the big table, with the certificate in her hand, not quite knowing why she had come.

The Keeper looked at her over the open book. She did not look at her with pity, which was how the village had looked at her since she was widowed, nor with the admiration they put on the Custódia who held everything up on her feet. She looked at her with a clean attention, that of one who has seen much pass, much sorrow, and is no longer frightened nor moved from outside, only listens. Sit, woman, she said, and reached her hand towards the chair on the other side of the table. The paper is seen to in a moment. It's you who don't seem to me to come only for the paper.

And it was the calm of the house, or it was the attention without judgment, or it was the badly slept night that had taken from her the last strength to pretend, but Custódia, who had kept herself composed before all Véspera for thirty years, sat down, laid the certificate on the table like one setting down an excuse that no longer serves, and her tongue came loose. She told. She told of the house that does not sleep, the children's rooms untouched with the beds made and no one to unmake them, the nights when she found herself on her feet in the corridor at three in the morning listening for a breathing that was not there. She told of the fall, the tumble in the dark on the way to a watch no one asked of her, the cold of the floor, the time she lay there until she could get up, and no one in the house to hear her fall. She told of the loneliness of the sentinel who keeps watch over all and over whom no one keeps watch, and she went on telling, and her voice grew hoarse, and she herself marvelled at telling that woman things she had never told Mercês nor Tobias nor herself.

The Keeper heard without hurry, without interrupting her, her hands still on the book. And when Custódia stopped, breathless from what she had let go, the Keeper put to her a question no one had put to her. And whose is the guard of your children now, Custódia? Who keeps watch over them, today, at night, in their houses?

Custódia went still. She opened her mouth and what came out was the truth, because she no longer had strength for anything else. They do, I suppose, she said. They and whoever lives with them.

Then your guard has already passed to other hands, woman, said the Keeper, and she said it low, with no hardness at all, like one entering a fact in a book. Your children now have their own sentinels, which are themselves. Your shift is over. And you go on at the post, weapons in hand, mounting a guard that is no longer yours.

Custódia shifted in the chair like one trying to straighten herself under a weight. The guard of a child does not pass, she said, and the voice trembled from wanting to be right. A mother is a mother forever. That does not pass to any hands.

The Keeper did not contradict her head on. She leaned a little, and her gentleness was the firmest thing in the house. To be a mother forever, yes, Custódia, that you are, and will be until you die, and they your children until they die, she said. No one takes that from you, not even you yourself, even if you wished it. But to be a mother is not to be a sentinel. They are two things, and you swapped one for the other and were left thinking they were the same. You can be a mother without mounting guard. Motherhood does not end when you come down from the rampart; it changes shape. You pass from a mother who protects to a mother who accompanies, from a mother who keeps watch to a mother who loves from afar, from a mother who carries to a mother who trusts. You do not cease to be a mother. You cease to be a sentinel, which is another thing, and that one, yes, that one has an hour to end.

Custódia fell silent digesting that, because it was the first time she saw the two things apart, set side by side on the table like two different certificates. Her whole life she had thought that to be a mother was to mount guard. And the woman was telling her that she could let go of one without letting go of the other, and there was in that, beneath the fright, something that breathed.

The Keeper got up without saying anything more and went to the shelves at the back. She ran her finger along the spines, read years in a low voice, took down a heavy book, bound in worn leather, and brought it to the table and opened it slowly, turning the thick pages with the care of one who handles a living thing. It was the book of births of the year Custódia had been born. She ran the lines with her finger, stopped, and turned the book towards Custódia.

Look, she said. Here you are.

And she put her finger on a line, and Custódia bent over the book and saw the old ink, brown from age, and saw her name written by a hand long dead, Custódia, daughter of whose daughter she had been, born on such a day of such a year. The Keeper did not take her finger from the line. This line was written when you were born, she said, long before you were anyone's mother, long before you had a post to guard. When this ink dried, you were the sentinel of no child. You were only Custódia, a girl with a life of her own ahead and no rampart to keep watch over. That Custódia existed before the guard. And she will exist after it, because the guard was a mission that fell to you for a time, a big and good time, but a time, and times pass. The Custódia of the line, that one, does not pass. She is still there, beneath the sentinel, waiting for you to come down from the rampart and let her go back to living.

Custódia stayed looking at the line of her birth. She stayed a long time. The Keeper's finger drew away and she went on looking, at the ink, at the name, at the date, and it was like looking at a person she had known as a child and lost sight of, and who after all was there, waiting for her, patient, from the other side of more than half a century. There had been a Custódia before everything. A girl who had been born with a name and a life ahead and who had not been born to be anyone's sentinel, who had been born only to live, as everyone is born. The guard had come afterwards, with the children, and had been a big part of her life. But it had not been from the beginning. And what had not been from the beginning did not have to be until the end.

The Keeper let her look as long as she needed and only then spoke again, and what she said was what stayed most. There is a thing you are not hearing well, Custódia, she said. You kept watch over your children as a sentinel keeps watch over a fortress at war. And while there was war, you did well, and you saved them, and you were a great guard, of the best these books have seen pass. But wars end. And there is a moment when the sentinel has to understand that the silence in the field is no longer the calm before the assault. It is the peace after the victory. And you confused the two. For thirty years you have heard the silence of your field as a threat, and for thirty years it has been only peace.

Custódia raised her eyes from the book.

That silence of your house at night, the Keeper went on, that you listen to with dread awaiting the danger, that silence is the sound of a house where everyone is safe. It is the sound of a war won, woman. Learn to hear it in another way. Learn to hear it as peace, and you will at last be able to sleep in it.

And it was then that Custódia cried. It was a rare crying, because she was not one to cry, a guard does not cry, a sentinel cannot be rubbing her eyes when she should be watching the line. Perhaps for that, for being so seldom, that crying came to her from so deep, without warning, climbing her chest like a thing that had been held beneath many stones. She cried with her hand to her mouth first, trying to hold it, like one stopping a chink, and then she let herself go, because there was no longer any rampart to defend there, at that table, before that woman. She cried the thirty years of sleep she had not slept, the thirty years of keeping watch over a field that had emptied without her noticing, she cried the girl of the line who had been lost from sight. And she cried too, in the same crying, without being able to part one water from the other, a thing that was not only grief, that had within it a relief, as though to cry were to set something down.

The Keeper said nothing. She did not put her hand on her shoulder, did not tell her to be calm, did not try to sew up the chink through which everything came out. She stayed seated on the other side of the table, with the book open between the two of them, and let her cry, let her cry it all, with the patience of one who knows there are cries that are mournings put off for years on end and that are crossed no other way but to the end.

When Custódia calmed, the house was darker, the slanting light had already moved its place. The Keeper closed the book of births slowly, but did not put it away at once, left it there within reach, like one leaving a door ajar. I see many mothers pass through these books, she said, and I see what happens to them when the children grow up. Some come down from the guard and go back to living, and those have a good old age, and the children come to see them of good will, because it is light to be with them. Others do not come down. They stay on sentinel at the empty post, and they waste away, and the children come to see them out of duty and with weight, because it is heavy to be with one who makes us feel guilty of having lived. You are at the crossroads, Custódia. And do not deceive yourself in one thing. To come down from the guard is not to love your children less. It is to love them in the way they need now, which is not to be watched over, it is to be trusted. The greatest love you can give them is to show them that you are well, that you have a life, that they need not carry the guilt of having left you alone at the post. Free them of that. It is the last good thing your vigil can still do for them. To end.

Custódia took up the certificate that was still on the table, forgotten, and the Keeper stamped it and signed it in a quick gesture, dispatching the pretext, giving it back to her like one giving back what was small after having given what was great. At the door, with the light of the street already coming in cold, Custódia stopped. Her face was washed from the crying and her eyes burned, and she carried in her chest two words the woman had put there inside her and that she did not yet know how to use. To come down from the guard. She repeated them within, at the door, and for the first time they did not taste to her of defeat. They tasted of a freeing, hers and the children's, of a door that opened onto a place where one could sleep.

And she went out into the afternoon of Véspera understanding everything with her head and nothing with her body. The head had understood. The head knew, there, in the street, with a clarity that hurt, that the guard had passed to other hands, that Mercês and Tobias were safe in their houses, that the silence of her house at night was peace and not danger, that there was a Custódia of the line prior to all the guard and that that Custódia waited for her still. It knew everything. And the body nothing. The body, which went down the cobbles with its feet planted as always, already knew, without saying it, that that night it would wake at three in the morning all the same, with its heart leaping, listening for the danger in the empty room, mounting the guard the head had just learned to let go. Because the guard was not in the head. It was in the bones, in the thirty years of habit, in the fear of a whole lifetime, and the body does not obey the head when what is at stake is a fear of that age. Custódia came down the street with the two words germinating in her chest, to come down from the guard, still trembling, already cast, and she carried them as one carries a seed to a ground that does not yet know whether it will accept it, not knowing that the path between knowing and managing is the longest there is, and that she still had almost all of it ahead.
