## 8. The Keeper of the Register

The House of the Register was, in Véspera, the place where the memory of those who had passed through was kept, and the Keeper was the woman who tended the books, and there was in the village an old wisdom about that house and that woman: that people went there to see to papers and came out with another thing, with questions they did not know they had, with truths they had been avoiding. Custódia, who was of feet planted on the earth, had never given much credit to such things, but she was so shaken from the night of the fall, so without ground, that she went to the House of the Register half out of a need to see to a paper and half out of a deeper need she did not know how to name, that of being with someone who would not judge her, who would listen to her, who perhaps would tell her what she herself could not tell herself. And the Keeper, who saw deep, perceived it as soon as Custódia entered, perceived that that woman did not come only for the paper, she came for a thing she did not even know she came to fetch, and received her with the time and the attention of one who knows she has before her not a matter to dispatch, but a person to untie.

It was to the House of the Register that Custódia ended up, after the night of the fall, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that began to loosen the knot, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, had over mothers a wisdom that came from centuries of seeing them raise children, see them depart, and stay.

The House of the Register had, for whoever entered, a quiet of a thing old and safe, and Custódia, who went about with her nerves frayed, felt it on entering, and perhaps it was that quiet that loosened her tongue and let her unburden what she unburdened to no one. Because the Keeper, unlike the village, did not look at her with pity nor with admiration; she looked at her with a clean attention, without judgement, the attention of one who has seen much people and much pain pass and is no longer frightened nor moved from outside, only listens. And there was in that listening a permission Custódia had never had: the permission not to be strong, not to be the guard, to be only a tired woman telling the truth. And Custódia, who had kept herself standing and composed before all Véspera for thirty years, sat in the House of the Register and, for the first time, let herself be fragile before another person, and it was such a great relief that she understood, by it alone, how much it had weighed, all those years, the obligation to be always the strong one.

She had gone to see to a paper, and the Keeper, on seeing her with that face of one who has not slept in thirty years, asked her how she was, and Custódia, who went about shaken from the night of the fall and already without the strength to pretend, told her. She told of the house that did not sleep, the untouched rooms, the nights of vigil, the fall, the solitude of the sentinel with no one to keep watch over her. And the Keeper listened, without hurry, and at a certain point asked a question no one had asked Custódia: and whose is the guard of your children now, Custódia? Who keeps watch over them, today, at night, in their houses? And Custódia, surprised, answered the truth: they, I suppose. They and whoever lives with them. And the Keeper said, softly: then your guard has already passed to others, woman. Your children now have their own sentinels, who are themselves. Your shift has ended. And you go on at the post, mounting a guard that has already passed to other hands.

The Keeper's question — whose is the guard of your children now — was the first thing to shake Custódia truly, because it was a question she had never answered entirely. She had always thought of the guard of her children as a thing of hers, hers forever, as though to be a mother were to be an eternal guard; and the Keeper put before her the idea that the guard changed hands, that there was a moment when it passed from the mother to the child, and that that moment, in her case, had passed long ago. Custódia tried to answer that the guard of a child never passes, that a mother is a mother forever, but the Keeper, with gentleness, dismantled her argument: to be a mother forever, yes, Custódia, that you are, and will be until you die, and they your children until they die. But to be a mother is not to be a guard. You can be a mother without mounting guard. Motherhood does not end when you come down from the watch; it changes form. You pass from a mother who protects to a mother who accompanies, from a mother who keeps watch to a mother who loves from afar, from a mother who carries to a mother who trusts. You do not cease to be a mother; you cease to be a sentinel, which is another thing. And Custódia, who had confused the two things her whole life, who had thought that to be a mother was to mount guard, was left digesting that distinction, that she could go on being a mother without going on being a sentinel, and it was perhaps that distinction, more than any other, that opened the door to her, because it took from her the fear that to come down from the watch was to cease being a mother.

And the Keeper went to fetch a book, that of the births of the year Custódia had been born, and opened it, and showed her the line. Look, she said. Here you are. Custódia, daughter of whom you were daughter, born on such a day. This line was written when you were born, long before you were mother of anyone, long before you had a post to guard. When this line was written, you were no child's sentinel; you were only Custódia, a girl with a life of her own ahead. That Custódia existed before the guard, and will exist after it, because the guard was a mission you had for a time, a time that has passed, but the Custódia of the line, that one, has not passed, she is there still, beneath the sentinel, waiting for you to come down from the rampart and let her live again.

Custódia looked at the line of her birth, written more than half a century ago, and stayed a long time looking at it, because it was the proof of a thing she had forgotten completely: that there had been a Custódia before the guard, a girl born with a name and a life ahead, and that that girl had not been born to be anyone's sentinel, she had been born only to live, as everyone is born. The guard had come afterward, with the children, and had been a part of her life, a large and good part; but it had not been the whole life, had not been from the beginning, and therefore did not have to be to the end. There had been Custódia before the sentinel; so there could be Custódia after her. And that idea, so simple, was for Custódia a liberation, because it showed her that to come down from the watch was not to fall into the nothing, it was to come back to a Custódia who had existed before, who was there still, beneath thirty years of vigil, waiting for the post to vacate so that she could, at last, live again.

And she said more, the Keeper, and it was what stayed most with Custódia. She said: I see many mothers pass through these books, and I see what happens to them when the children grow. Some come down from the watch and live again, and those have a good old age, and the children come to see them willingly, because it is light to be with them. Others do not come down, they stay sentinel at the empty post, and those waste away, and the children come to see them out of duty and with weight, because it is heavy to be with one who makes us feel guilty for having lived. You are at the crossroads, Custódia. You can come down from the watch, and have a good old age, and be a light mother to whom the children come willingly; or you can stay at the post, and spend what is left you on watch over the emptiness, and drive your children away with the weight of your vigil. But do not be mistaken in one thing: to come down from the watch is not to love your children less. It is to love them in another way, the way they need now, which is not to be watched, it is to be trusted. The greatest love you can give them now is to show them that you are well, that you have a life, that they do not need to carry the guilt of having left you alone. Free them of that, Custódia. Come down from the watch. It is the last good thing your vigil can still do for them: to end.

The Keeper, before letting her go, said to her one more thing that Custódia took home and repeated to herself many times on the hard nights. She said: you kept watch over your children as a sentinel keeps watch over a fortress at war. And while there was war, you did well, and you saved them, and you were a great guard. But wars end, Custódia, and there is a moment when the sentinel has to understand that the silence in the field is no longer the calm before the attack, it is the peace after the victory. You confused the two things. For thirty years you have heard the silence of the field as a threat, when it has long been only peace. Learn to hear the silence in another way. That silence of your house at night, which you listen to with dread awaiting the danger, is the sound of peace, woman, it is the sound of a house where everyone is safe, it is the sound of a won war. Learn to hear it as peace, and you will at last be able to sleep in it. And Custódia, who had spent thirty years hearing the silence of the house as a threat to watch, stayed with that new idea, that the silence could be peace and not danger, and it was perhaps that, more than any other, the idea that began to let her sleep, because what kept her on guard was not the silence, it was the way she heard it, and to change the way of hearing it was to begin to be able to rest in it.

There was, in the conversation with the Keeper, a moment when Custódia wept, and it was a rare weeping, because she was not one to weep, a guard does not weep, and it was perhaps for that, for being so rare, that that weeping relieved her so much. She wept on understanding, by the Keeper's word, the size of what she had lost: thirty years of sleep, thirty years of a life of her own, thirty years on watch over a field that had emptied, and she wept for the Custódia who had lost herself in those thirty years, the woman she could have been had she come down from the watch sooner. But she wept also, in the same weeping, from relief, because the Keeper had opened her a door, had shown her that she was still in time, that to come down from the watch was possible and was even an act of love, and that therefore the years left to her did not have to be like those that had passed. And the Keeper let her weep, without interrupting her, because she knew that that weeping was necessary, that it was the weeping of a mourning put off thirty years, the mourning of the empty nest that Custódia had never allowed herself to weep and that she had to weep at last to be able to go on, because what is not wept is not let go of, and Custódia needed to weep the loss to be able, finally, to come down from the guard she had raised so as not to feel it.

And Custódia heard that, and it was as if a door opened, because the Keeper had given her a way to come down from the watch that was not a betrayal, it was a last act of love: to come down from the watch to free the children from the weight of her vigil, to give them a light mother instead of a wasting-away mother, to let them live without guilt. And she understood that the thing she had taken for love — the eternal vigil — had become the contrary of love, a weight, a guilt, a chain; and that true love, now, was exactly what seemed to her hardest: to let go, to trust, to come down from the rampart, and to show her children, by living, that they could live. The word the Keeper gave her, to come down from the watch, stayed with Custódia like a key, but a key she did not yet know how to use, because one thing is to receive the right word and another is to manage to live it. Custódia left the House of the Register understanding everything with her head — that the guard had passed to other hands, that the children were safe, that the silence was peace and not danger, that she had a line of her own prior to motherhood — and managing nothing with her body, which went on waking at three, listening, watching. And it was that distance, between the knowing and the managing, that made the months that followed so hard, because it is a particular torture to know what one ought to do and not manage to do it, to see the way out and not manage to walk to it, and Custódia, who had always been a woman of doing what she decided, found herself unable to do what she most wanted, to come down from the watch, because the watch was not in the head, it was in the body, in the bones, in the thirty years of habit, and the body does not obey the head when what is at stake is a fear of a whole lifetime.

She left the House of the Register with the Keeper's word in her breast, to come down from the watch, and for the first time that idea did not seem to her a defeat, it seemed to her a liberation, hers and her children's, and it was with that new idea, still trembling but already germinating, that Custódia began, slowly, the path of becoming again, beneath the sentinel, the Custódia of the line, the woman who had existed before the guard and who could, still, come to exist after it.
