## 10. Coming down from the watch

The first night Mercês stayed in the house, Custódia could scarcely believe what she felt. She woke at three, of course, with the usual start, but on waking she heard, coming from the next room, her daughter's breathing as she slept, and that breathing, that sound of another living person in the house, did to her a thing she had not expected: it calmed her. She lay listening to her daughter's breathing, instead of listening for the danger, and her daughter's breathing told her there is someone here, you are not alone, you can rest, and Custódia, for the first time in she did not know how long, fell asleep again after three, lulled by the breathing of another person in the house. And she understood, in the morning, a thing that shook her: that part of what kept her on guard, all those years, was the solitude, was being alone in the house, with no one, because a sentinel alone cannot lower the guard, there is no one to relieve her; and that it had been enough for there to be another person in the house, only one, for her guard to slacken, because the guard could, at last, be shared. And that taught her a thing about what she needed to do: it was not enough to convince herself that the children were safe; she needed also not to be so alone, to have people in her life, because solitude fed the vigil and company calmed it.

To come down from the watch, Custódia discovered, was not done all at once, with a decision; it was done slowly, by force, with relapses, as one dismantles a rampart stone by stone, and she went on doing it, in that late spring of her life, with Mercês's help, who came, and with the stubbornness of one who had decided, at the bottom, that she did not want to die a sentinel.

Mercês had to make an arrangement in her own life to come and stay a few days with her mother, and she did it willingly, but Custódia, at first, did not leave her in peace with the guilt: oh daughter, you leaving your life because of me, I do not want to be a weight, go away, I will manage. They were the words of always, the refusal of always of care, the guard not wanting to be guarded. And Mercês had to be firm, to tell her mother, I want to be here, it is no weight to me at all, stop this and let me care for you, and it took Mercês insisting, day after day, for Custódia to begin to accept the care without spoiling it with guilt. Because there was in Custódia, as ingrained as the vigil, the inability to receive, and the two things were the same: a woman who only knows how to give and does not know how to receive is a woman who keeps watch over others and does not let them keep watch over her, and Custódia had to learn the two things at the same time, to come down from the watch and to let herself be cared for, because they were the two faces of the same coin, the coin of a whole life of being only the one who protects and never the protected.

Mercês came, and stayed a few days, and the first thing she did was the thing Custódia needed and had never had: she kept watch over her mother. At night, when Custódia woke at three with the usual start, Mercês was there, in the next room, and Custódia, on hearing her daughter's breathing in the house, felt a thing she had not felt in thirty years: that she was not alone on guard, that there was another person in the house, that she could, for once, not be the only one awake. And it was strange, and it was hard, but it was also an immense relief, that of a sentinel who, for one night, has someone to relieve the shift, and Custódia, with her daughter in the house, managed, for the first time in a long while, to sleep again after three, because there was another person on guard, because she could lower her eyes without that meaning to abandon the post.

It was strange, at first, for the two of them, this inversion of roles, the daughter caring for the mother who had always cared for her. Custódia had difficulty letting herself be cared for, accepting that Mercês did things for her, that she kept watch over her, because her whole life it had been she who did, she who kept watch, and to receive care instead of giving it was an unknown and uncomfortable terrain. She felt useless, felt that she was giving trouble, apologised at all hours, do not bother with me, daughter, you have your life. And Mercês had to teach her, with patience, that to let herself be cared for was not to give trouble, it was to give the daughter the joy of repaying, of caring for the one who had cared for her, and that in refusing the care the mother was, without wishing it, refusing the daughter that joy. Mother, let me do this for you, said Mercês, you did everything for me my whole life, let me now do a little for you, do not take that from me. And Custódia, slowly, went on learning to let herself be cared for, and it was a learning as hard as that of coming down from the watch, because it was the same thing seen from the other side: to come down from the watch was to cease being only the one who protects, and to let herself be cared for was to accept being, also, protected, and the two things asked of her the same humility, that of not being always the strong one, that of accepting to need others, that of coming down from the solitary pedestal of the guard to the shared ground of those who give and receive care.

And Mercês, with tact, helped her mother to understand a thing that changed everything: that to come down from the watch was not to be left without a guard, it was to entrust the guard to those who now had it, who were the children themselves, masters of their lives. Mother, said Mercês, you can sleep at ease because I care for myself, and Tobias cares for himself, and we are no longer the children you kept watch over, we are grown people with our own lives at the ready. You are not abandoning us if you sleep; you are trusting us. And the greatest gift you can give us is to trust us, is to show us that you believe we are capable, because a mother who does not come down from the watch is, without wishing it, telling her children that she does not trust that they can manage, and we can, mother, you raised us to manage. Trust us. Come down from the watch. It is the proof that you did a good work.

Mercês helped her too with the rooms and with the house, with tact, without forcing. She did not tell her throw this out, change everything; she helped her, slowly, to understand that she could let the house change, that she could transform the silence of the empty rooms into something else, and she proposed small steps to her, mother, what if we put here a good chair for you to sit and read by the light of this window, which is so beautiful. And Custódia, who would have refused outright a great change, went on accepting the small steps, and each small step slackened her guard a little, because to change the house was to admit that life had changed, and to admit that life had changed was the beginning of accepting it, and to accept that the children had departed and that the house was now hers, and not a post awaiting them, was the true coming-down from the rampart, more even than the sleep. Mercês understood this, and so she worked on the house at the same time as the sleep, because she knew that her mother's guard was in everything, in the nights and in the rooms and in the surplus food and in the empty places at the table, and that to come down from the watch was to change all of that, slowly, piece by piece, until the house ceased to be a post and became a house again, the house of a living woman and not the temple of a departed family.

And Custódia, slowly, went on doing it. She went on forcing herself, night after night, not to get up at three, to stay lying down, to say to herself they are safe, I can rest, and at first she could not, and got up all the same, and went to see the empty rooms; but with time, with the stubbornness, with the memory of her daughter keeping watch over her, she went on managing more and more to stay lying down, went on managing to fall asleep again, went on teaching her body, slowly, that to sleep no longer killed anyone, that to lower the guard was no longer to abandon a post, that she could close her eyes and the world did not crumble, the children did not die, the house did not burn, everything went on in its place while she, at last, rested. There helped her, in that time, an image she herself went on building and that became her way of calming herself on the hard nights. She imagined the guard of her children as a shift she had served, long, honoured, of thirty-some years, and she imagined that now came the hour of the changing of the guard, the hour when the soldier who has kept watch the whole night hands over the post to the one who comes to relieve the shift and goes at last to rest. And those who came to relieve the shift were the children themselves, grown, capable, ready to guard their own lives; and Custódia imagined herself handing over the guard to them, formally, as one hands over a post, telling them the shift is yours now, and drawing away, and going to rest, with the clear conscience of one who has done their part and handed it over into good hands. This image, that of the changing of the guard, helped her much, because it transformed the coming-down from the watch from a thing that seemed giving-up or abandonment into a thing that was honourable, natural, right: the relief of a shift served, the handing-over of the post to those who now had it, the deserved rest of one who has kept watch over their part and handed it on.

And each night in which she managed to sleep a little more was a stone fewer in the rampart, a small victory of a sentinel learning to come down, and Custódia went on collecting them, those victories, night by night, with the patience of one who dismantles slowly a fortress that took thirty years to build.

Mercês could not stay forever, of course, she had her life, and there came the day when she had to go back to her house, and Custódia was afraid that, alone again, everything would go back to the beginning, the guard, the insomnia, the post. And it went back, in part; but not entirely, because something had changed, and Mercês had left her mother two things that held her. One was practical: they agreed that they would speak every morning, a short call, and that so, if something happened to the mother at night, the next day it would be known quickly, and that, to know that there was someone counting on her morning, gave Custódia a thing she had never had, the sensation that there was also someone keeping watch a little over her, and that sensation, however small, calmed her guard, because part of what kept her sentinel was knowing herself alone, with no one to whom she would be missed at night. There was, before Mercês departed, a conversation between the two that was one of the most important of the whole story, and that happened one night, the two of them in the kitchen, at the table, as they had not been in a long while. Mercês told her mother things she had never told her: she told her how, as a child, she had sometimes felt the weight of her vigil, how she had grown up with the sensation that her mother was always awaiting a disaster, and how that, though it came from love, had weighed on her, had put fears in her body, had taught her to see the world as a dangerous place. And Custódia heard that with pain, because it had never occurred to her that her vigil, which she took for pure love and pure protection, could have weighed on her children, could have transmitted to them her own fear of the world. But Mercês said it to her without rancour, with love, to help her understand, and she said also the reverse: and it is for that, mother, that it gives me such joy to see you coming down from the watch now, because in seeing you trust, in seeing you sleep, in seeing you live, it is as though you gave me, late but you give it, the permission for me too not to live on guard, for me too to trust, for me not to do to my children what without wishing it you did to me. And Custódia understood, with that conversation, that to come down from the watch did not free only her; it freed also her daughter, and perhaps her grandchildren, from an inheritance of fear, because fears pass from mother to daughter as the colour of the eyes passes, and a mother who learns to trust teaches her daughter to trust, and breaks, for the following generations, the chain of the vigil.

The other thing Mercês left her was a sentence, said in farewell: mother, you kept watch over us your whole life; now let us keep watch a little over you. Do not take that from yourself or from us. And Custódia, who had always refused to be cared for, understood that to refuse her children's care was also a way of keeping them at a distance, of going on being only the guard and never the guarded, and that to let herself be cared for was, after all, a way of letting them love her back, after a life in which only she had loved.

It did not go straight, of course. There were relapses, nights of panic, nights when the old fear came back with all its strength and she got up distressed to mount guard as always. But the difference, now, is that Custódia already knew what that was, already put a name to it — it is the fear of thirty years ago, she said to herself, it is the night of the fever, it is not today, today my children are safe —, and to put a name to it took half its strength away, and Custódia went on learning to treat her vigil as an old stubborn sentinel who insisted on mounting guard long after the end of the war, and to tell her, with firmness and with tenderness, the war has ended, soldier, you may rest. She learned, in that process, to be patient with herself, a thing she had never been, because a guard has no patience, she has discipline, she demands of herself and of others immediate perfection. But to come down from the watch was not done with discipline, it was done with patience, with the acceptance that there would be good days and bad days, relapses and advances, and that this was normal, that it was not failing, that it was the way of any deep change. And Custódia, who her whole life had demanded perfection of herself, who would have reproached herself harshly for each relapse, went on learning to treat herself with the gentleness with which one treats those healing from a long illness: without haste, without demand, one day at a time, content with each small progress and without punishing herself for each retreat. And that gentleness toward herself was, in itself, a cure, because a woman who demanded harshness of herself her whole life, who was always the strong one, the one who does not allow herself weakness, to find at last gentleness toward herself is to find a peace that the harshness had never given her, and Custódia, in learning to be gentle with herself, learned also, without noticing it, to be happier, because a great part of her unhappiness had always come from the harshness with which she treated herself, from the demand to be always strong, always on guard, always perfect, and to let go of that demand was to let go of a weight as great as that of the vigil itself.

And the sentinel, slowly, went on obeying her, went on lowering her weapons, went on coming down from the rampart, and Custódia, behind her, went on finding again a thing she had lost thirty years ago and that she scarcely remembered: sleep, rest, the peace of one who trusts that they can close their eyes without the world crumbling.
