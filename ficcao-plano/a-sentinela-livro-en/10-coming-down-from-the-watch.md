## 10. Coming down from the watch

The first night Mercês stayed in the house, Custódia lay down expecting not to sleep, as she had lain down for thirty years, and fell asleep without noticing, which was already a miracle, but the true miracle came at three. She woke at three, as always, with the start of always, her body already half sitting up in the bed before her head knew why, her hand reaching for the switch, her ear open to the danger. And then, in the dark of the house, instead of the danger, she heard another thing. It came from the next room, the room that had been Mercês's as a child and had become hers again for a few days: the breathing of her daughter sleeping, slow, deep, steady, a small warm bellows rising and falling on the other side of the wall. Custódia stayed motionless listening to it. She was not listening to the danger, she was listening to her daughter's breathing, and her daughter's breathing told her things that no word had told her in thirty years: there's someone here, you are not alone, you can lower your eyes that there is another living person in this house. And Custódia, who for thirty years had not fallen asleep again after three, who got up and went to see the rooms and made the rounds of the house like a guard on the rampart, let herself stay lying down, lulled by that warm bellows on the other side of the wall, and fell asleep again. It was the simplest thing in the world, and it was enormous.

In the morning, at the breakfast table, with the light coming in and Mercês breaking her bread for her as though it were the most natural thing, Custódia had a jolt she did not tell her daughter. She understood, slowly, biting the bread, a thing that left her without ground for an instant: that part of what had kept her on guard all those years was not only the fear that the children might die. It was the loneliness. It was being alone in the house, with no one, because a sentinel alone cannot lower the guard, there is no one to relieve her, and her body knew it, and so it did not sleep. It had been enough for there to be one more person in the house, only one, breathing on the other side of the wall, for the guard to ease. The guard could, at last, be shared. And Custódia, with the cup halfway through the gesture, understood that it was not enough for her to convince herself that the children were safe, far off; she needed also not to be so alone, because loneliness was firewood for the vigil and company was water.

To come down from the guard, Custódia was going to discover that late spring of her life, was not done all at once, with a decision at the door. It was done slowly, by force, with relapses, as one dismantles a rampart stone by stone, and it was done with Mercês at her side, and with the dull stubbornness of one who has decided, at the very bottom, that she did not want to die a sentinel.

But there was a beginning in which Custódia almost spoiled it all, and spoiled it in the only way she knew, with guilt. Mercês had unmade her own life to come and stay those days, and Custódia would not leave her in peace. Child, she said to her at the table, you leaving your whole life on my account, I don't want to be a weight to anyone, go home that I'll manage, I always managed. They were the words of always, the refusal of always, the guard not wanting to be guarded. And Mercês set down the cloth she had in her hand and looked at her straight and was firm, firmer than Custódia expected of a daughter of hers. Mother, I want to be here, did you hear me, I want to, you're not weighing on me at all, and stop sending me away that I'm not going. You did everything for me a whole lifetime, everything, you got up a thousand nights on my account, and now that it's me wanting to do a little for you, you come with the story of the weight. Let me take care of you. Don't take that from me. And Custódia, who had prepared to win the argument as she won them all, was left without an answer, because the daughter was right and the daughter's rightness hurt her in a good way, in a way she did not know.

It was strange, and it was hard, this inversion, the daughter caring for the mother who had always cared for her. There was in Custódia, as ingrained as the vigil, the inability to receive, and she went on discovering that the two things were the same seen from two sides: a woman who knows only how to give and not how to receive is a woman who keeps watch over others and does not let them keep watch over her. She felt useless when Mercês made her food, apologised at each gesture, don't trouble yourself with me, child, let me do it, you have your life. And Mercês, with the patience of one who teaches a stubborn child, went on telling her that to let herself be cared for was not to be a trouble, it was to give the daughter the joy of repaying the one who had raised her, and that in refusing the care the mother was, without meaning to, refusing her that joy. Custódia, slowly, went on accepting it. And it was a learning as hard as coming down from the rampart, because it demanded of her the same humility, that of not always being the strong one, of accepting that she needed others, of coming down from the lonely pedestal of the guard to the shared ground of one who gives and receives.

Mercês helped her with the house too, with tact, forcing nothing. She did not say to her throw this out, change everything, open those windows that this looks like a wake. She sat with her one afternoon in the still sitting room, looked around, and said only, slowly, mother, and what if we put a good chair here, an armchair, right in this corner in the light of this window, for you to sit and read in the afternoon, that this afternoon light is so beautiful and is going to waste here. Custódia, who would have refused a great change out of hand, did not know how to refuse a chair. And she went on accepting the small steps, and each small step eased her guard a little, because to change the house was to admit that life had changed, and to admit that life had changed was the beginning of accepting it. To accept that the children had left and flown as Felizarda had flown, and that the house was now hers and not a post awaiting them, was the true coming-down from the rampart, more even than the sleep. Mercês understood this, and so she worked the house at the same time as the nights, because the mother's guard was in everything, in the three in the morning and in the closed rooms and in the empty places at the table, and to come down from the guard was to go changing all of it, piece by piece, until the house ceased to be a post and became a house again, that of a living woman and not the temple of a parted family.

But the conversation that stayed, the one Custódia was to go back over many nights after the daughter left, happened one night, the two of them in the kitchen, at the table, with the kitchen light on only above them and the whole house dark around, as they had not been in a long while. Mercês had her tea cooling in her hand and was slow to begin, and Custódia understood, by the slowness, that something deep was coming. Mother, said Mercês at last, I never told you this and I want to tell you while I'm here. I, as a child, felt the weight of your vigil. I felt you always awaiting a disaster, always with your ears pricked, and I was small and didn't know the name of it, but I knew the smell, and I grew up with the sense that the world out there was a place where bad things lay in wait, ready to fall. That put fears in my body, mother. Fears I still carry today. And Custódia heard that with a pain she had not imagined possible, because never, not once, had it occurred to her that her vigil, which she gave as pure love and pure protection, could have weighed on the children, could have passed on to them her own fear of the world as one passes on the colour of the eyes. She wanted to defend herself, wanted to say it had been out of love, and Mercês, as though reading her face, went ahead. I know it came from love, mother, I always knew, I don't tell you it with any rancour, I tell you it with love. And I tell you because there is a reverse, and the reverse is the best thing I have to give you. It is for this, mother, that it gives me such joy to see you come down from the guard now. Because seeing you trust, seeing you sleep after three, seeing you live, it is as if you gave me, late but you give it, the permission for me too not to live on guard. For me too to trust. For me not to do to my children, without meaning to, what without meaning to you did to me. And Custódia heard that with pain and with light at the same time, the two things in the same chest, and understood that to come down from the guard freed not only herself; it freed the daughter, and perhaps the grandchildren still to come, from an inheritance of fear, because fears pass from mother to daughter as the colour of the eyes passes, and a mother who learns to trust teaches the daughter to trust, and breaks, for those who come after, the chain of the vigil.

Mercês could not stay forever, of course, she had her life, and the day came to go back to her house. Custódia was afraid, a child's fear, that alone again everything would go back to the beginning, the guard, the insomnia, the post awaiting no one. And Mercês, at the parting, with the bag already on her shoulder and her hand on the door handle, left her two things. One was an agreement: we'll talk every morning, mother, a short call, even if only for you to tell me everything's well and that you slept. And that, to know that there was someone counting on her morning, awaiting her voice, gave Custódia a thing she had never had, the sense that there was also someone keeping watch a little over her, and however small it was it calmed her guard, because part of what had made her a sentinel was knowing herself alone, with no one she was missed by at night. The other thing was a sentence. Mercês stopped at the door, turned, and said it to her slowly, for it to stay. Mother, you kept watch over us a whole lifetime. Now let us keep watch a little over you. Don't take that from us, from yourself and from us. And rest easy, mother, sleep, that I out here go well, and Tobias goes well, we are not the children you kept watch over any more, we are grown people with our lives in order, you raised us for this. Trust us. Come down from the guard. It is the proof that you did a good job. And she went, down the cobbles, and Custódia stayed at the door watching her go, and understood, with the house empty behind her again, that to refuse the children's care had always been a way of keeping them at a distance, of going on being only the guard and never the guarded, and that to let herself be cared for was, after all, to let them love her back, after a life in which only she had loved.

It did not go straight, of course not. There were relapses. There was a night, a few days after Mercês left, when the old fear came back with all its force, and Custódia woke at three drenched, her heart at a gallop, already standing in the middle of the room mounting guard as always, the house again a post and the night again full of disasters lying in wait. But the difference, now, was that Custódia already knew what it was, already knew its name. All right, she said to herself, standing in the dark, breathing. This is the fear from thirty years ago. This is the night of the fever, it is not today. Today my children are safe, each in their bed, in their life. And to name it took half its force, and Custódia lay down again, and stayed in the dark treating her own vigil like an old stubborn sentinel that insisted on mounting guard long after the war was over, and said to it, with firmness and with tenderness, from the depth of the bed, the war is over, soldier, you can rest.

She learned, in that back-and-forth of advances and retreats, a thing she had never known: to be patient with herself. A guard has no patience, she has discipline, demands of herself and of others immediate perfection, and Custódia, who a whole lifetime had charged herself with hardness, who had always been the strong one, the one who allows herself no weakness, would, before, have beaten herself for each relapse. But to come down from the guard was not done with discipline, it was done with patience, with the acceptance that there were going to be good days and bad days, and that this was not failing, it was the way of any deep change. And she went on learning to treat herself with the mildness with which one treats one healing from a long illness, without haste, without charging, glad of each small progress and without punishing herself for each step back. And that mildness towards herself was, in itself, a cure, because a great part of her unhappiness had always come from the hardness with which she treated herself, from the demand to be always strong, always on guard, always perfect, and to let go that demand was to let go a weight as great as that of the vigil itself.

And on the harder nights, when not even the name of the fear was enough, Custódia had an image she clung to, and it was that of the relief of the guard. She imagined the vigil over the children as a long shift, an honourable one, of thirty-odd years, and she imagined that the hour came at last when the soldier who had kept watch the whole night hands over the post to the one who comes to relieve him and goes, at last, to rest. And those who came to relieve the shift were the children themselves, grown, capable, ready to guard their own lives. Custódia imagined herself handing them the guard as one hands over a post, saying the shift is yours now, and stepping away from the rampart without guilt, because she handed it over into good hands. And so, slowly, the sentinel went on obeying her, went on lowering the weapons, went on coming down from the rampart, and Custódia, behind her, went on finding again a thing she had lost thirty years ago and that she could scarcely remember any more: sleep, rest, the peace of one who trusts that she can close her eyes without the world coming down.
