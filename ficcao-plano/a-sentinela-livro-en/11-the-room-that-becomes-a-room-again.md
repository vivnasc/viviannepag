## 11. The room that becomes a room again

It was on a spring morning, one of those when the light comes in from the side and seeks out things, that Custódia opened the door of Mercês's room with an intention that months before would have seemed to her a profanation, and stood still at the entrance, with her hand still on the latch, letting the light do the work of showing what she came to dismantle. The room was as it had always been, the temple untouched, the bedspread pulled smooth without a crease, the dolls seated on the shelf looking at the door like those who have waited years for someone to come in and not leave at once, the closed smell that things kept and unused have. It was the first time in her life, she understood right there, at the entrance, that she was going to make a space only for herself, and the idea, which months before had offended her when Mercês had sown it and she had refused it with sharpness, seemed to her now not only good but necessary, urgent even, as though the room had long been asking her to let it breathe.

She began with the bedspread, because it was the largest thing and the easiest to take hold of and the hardest to set down. She pulled it slowly from the bed, folded it in half and then in four, and in her hands the bedspread was light, was only cloth, but it weighed on her arms a whole night of the small Mercês's fevers, the one in which she had kept watch over her daughter until morning with this same bedspread at her feet, changing the cloths on her forehead, and Custódia stood in the middle of the room with the folded bedspread against her chest, smelling it without noticing, and let herself stay like that a while, taking her leave, before setting it in the box she had brought, with care, smoothing it inside the box as though it were still needed. She did not throw it away. None of that was thrown away. It was a matter of putting away, said to her a voice within that was her own voice already come down from the guard, it was a matter of putting the past in its place as past, and not of erasing it.

Then came the dolls, one by one, and those cost more. She took the first, the one with the plait, the one Mercês had dragged through the whole house until its face wore away, and held it with both hands, lingered, remembered her daughter rocking it on her lap in a corner of the kitchen while she cooked, and said goodbye to it low, goodbye, and put it away lying in the box with the same tenderness with which she had once laid it in the girl's bed. It was at that doll that the tears came, and Custódia let them come, because she noticed, with surprise, that they were not the others. They were not the tears of the vigil, those dry and angry ones that tore her throat at three in the morning and eased nothing. These ran loose and almost warm, hurt in another way, hurt as a good thing that has ended and that one is grateful to have had hurts, and she understood, crying with a doll in her hand, that that was mourning, but mourning of the good kind, the kind one does in order to be able to go on, and not the kind one refuses and that rots within. She cried over each doll she put away, and each one left her lighter, not heavier, which was the reverse of all she knew of crying.

At last the girl's sewing box, the little one, with the spools of coloured thread and the make-believe thimble, and that one she closed with the lid and set on top of the dolls, and stayed looking at the full box, a whole life of a full house put away with tenderness inside a box, and instead of the tightness she expected she felt, beneath the sadness, a deep relief, the relief of one who at last sets down a weight she had carried for years without knowing she could set it down. The room, around her, went on emptying of the traces of the past, went on becoming wall and floor and light again, and Custódia, in the middle of it, with the box at her feet, breathed in a way she had not breathed in thirty years.

It took her days to do the rest, without hurry, because what is done with ceremony is not done in haste. She brought up from the store room the good chair, the armchair, the one no one used, and put it by the window, in the light, turned outward; she found a small table for the side, for the sewing basket and the glasses and whatever she fancied; and when she finished, and the room that had been a reliquary became a room again, a living room, a used room, she sat in the new chair, in the light of the window, and had the strange and new sensation of having, at last, in her own house, a place that was not a guard post. A place to be. A place of hers.

She telephoned Mercês that night, and telephoned with apprehension, her heart beating like a girl caught out, because she was afraid of hurting her, of the daughter thinking the mother had erased her room, her childhood, the proof that she had lived there. Mercês, she told her, slowly, I changed your room, child, I made of it a little sitting room for myself, I put your things away with care, I kept everything, I threw nothing out, but I took those things off the shelves and put a chair of mine there in the light of the window, and I don't know if I did well, I don't know if I hurt you. On the other end there was a short silence, and Custódia held her breath, and then came Mercês's voice, and it came glad, came truly glad, mother, good, good that you did that, I hardly even remembered that room and I don't need it stopped in time, I have my house, mother, what I always wanted was for you to use yours, for you to live. And she added, lower, that it was the best news they could give her, better than a room kept like a museum of her childhood, because it was the sign that the mother was coming down from the guard, coming back to herself, getting better, and that she, Mercês, had been waiting years to hear her mother speak like that, light, of a liking of her own. And Custódia, with the telephone in her hand, understood all at once what she had never understood: that she had kept the rooms thinking it was for the children, and that the children did not even want that, that it was she who needed to keep the temples in order to go on feeling herself on guard, and that in changing the room she took nothing from anyone, she gave herself a thing she had never had.

Tobias's room, that one, she did not touch, and it was on purpose, and it showed her to herself that she had learned to do things in their time, without swinging from one extreme to the other. She left it as a guest room, tidy but alive, ready to receive whoever came, no longer as a reliquary. The marks of her son's growth on the door jamb, those pencil lines with the dates beside them, those she did not erase, she stayed looking at them a while with the rag in her hand and decided to leave them, because they were beautiful, they were memory, and memory is kept; but she left them there with no solemnity at all, as one leaves a dear mark on a wall, and not as an altar to which one lights a candle. Each thing in its place, she thought: the memories as memories, dear but light, and the rooms as rooms, and not as posts.

She began then to use the little sitting room, to sit in the chair in the light, and it was there, in the afternoons alone and in the afternoons with Felizarda, that she discovered herself again in bits. On one of those afternoons she took up the sewing for the taking, not to mend anything of anyone's, and found herself embroidering the border of a kitchen cloth only because she fancied the colour of the thread, and stayed like that, in the light, with her hands busy at a beautiful and useless thing, and the calm it gave her was a calm she did not know, that of doing a thing for pleasure and not out of duty. She put some plants in the window, three small pots, and watered them in the morning with the old jug, and peeped at them every day in search of a shoot, and when the first came, a green knot opening on one of the stems, Custódia called no one to see it and stayed alone looking at it an absurd while, glad as a child, because it was a small and living thing to care for that did not enslave her as the guard of the children had enslaved her, a thing that grew on its own and only asked of her water and a person who noticed.

Felizarda, the one who had flown, the one she had for years thought frivolous and judged from above, was the one who pulled her out of the house. She turned up one Sunday afternoon with a scarf on her head and some plan of going to see the cherry trees on the upper road, come on, Custódia, leave the dust that the dust stays, and she took her in the car with the windows down, and the two of them went talking along the way of things that were not the children, of an old neighbour, of a dress Felizarda had bought and already regretted, of some girlhood folly they still remembered having done. And at a certain point Felizarda told a tale so wonderfully clumsy that Custódia laughed, laughed aloud, mouth open, and the sound of her own laughter caught her unawares, she marvelled at it the way one marvels at a voice heard recorded, because it had been so long since she used it that she scarcely recognised it. And she understood, laughing beside Felizarda on the cherry-tree road, that a sentinel does not laugh, she is on duty, and that a woman who laughs again is a woman coming down from the rampart. Felizarda, who knew the way, gave her no lessons at all; she went on only showing her, by example, without saying it, that one could have a good life after the children left, that on the other side of the empty nest there was a life waiting, if one had the courage to go and fetch it.

And then there was Tobias's visit, and it was the most beautiful proof of all. Tobias came seldom and, when he came, came tense, shoulders raised, awaiting the weight of his mother; he came that time for the weekend and went on finding, as the hours passed, a woman he did not know, a mother who talked to him of her friends and the cherry trees and the plants in the window instead of talking to him of her aches and her loneliness, a mother who asked after his life with interest and not with anxiety. And at the parting, on the Sunday afternoon, with the bag already in the car, the thing happened. Tobias braced himself inside for the usual, for the oh son when are you coming back that I'm here so alone, the sentence that pursued him down the motorway for years; and what his mother said to him, at the door, with her hand on his arm and the greatest of calms, was go easy, son, I'm well, live your life, come when you can that I have mine here. And Custódia saw, in her son's face, his jaw drop and his shoulders lower all at once, saw the relief pass over his face like one setting down a load he had carried so long he no longer even felt it, and Tobias embraced her longer than was his custom, with gratitude and almost with regret at going, and went off that time without the weight and without the guilt. And he began to come more, after that, and to telephone more, because it no longer cost him, because the mother had become a good place to go back to and not a weight to carry.

And that was the irony Custódia went on understanding, slowly, in the calls that became light and the visits that became easy: that for thirty years she had mounted guard so as not to lose the children, and that it had been the guard that drove them off, and that it had been enough to come down from it, to let go of them, to trust, to see them come back of good will. She had held them with a closed hand, and they had fled the closed hand; she opened the hand, and they alighted on it, free, and for that very reason nearer than ever. To hold drives off, she understood at last. To let go draws near.

It was a lesson she would have liked to learn sooner, and sometimes, in the chair in the light of the window, she thought of the years she had lost with some grief, of the heavy visits, of the calls of guilt. But Felizarda, to whom she confessed this one afternoon, would not let her linger there. Don't think of what you lost, Custódia, she said to her, think of what you still have, you have years ahead, you have the children, you have the granddaughter, you have a life to live; you learned late, but you learned, and there are those who never learn and die on watch over an empty field. And Custódia clung to this, to the woman prior to the guard who was coming back bit by bit, the Custódia of the line, and stopped crying over the lost years to begin living the ones she had, which is, in the end, the only sensible thing one does with the time that remains.
