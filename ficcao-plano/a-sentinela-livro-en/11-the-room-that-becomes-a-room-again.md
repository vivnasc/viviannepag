## 11. The room that becomes a room again

There was a day, in that spring, when Custódia did a thing that months before would have seemed to her a profanation and that was, after all, the clearest sign that she had come down from the watch: she changed one of the rooms. She took Mercês's room, the untouched temple she had guarded for years, and transformed it, with her own hands, into something else, and it was like burying at last a dead one who had not died, and breathing, and letting in air where for years there had been only waiting.

The idea was not hers, at first; it was Mercês's, who had sown it months before and met with her mother's refusal, and who came back to it with more tact on one of her visits, mother, and that light so beautiful from the window of my old room, it would be so good for you to have there a little corner of your own to sit in of an afternoon. And that time Custódia, who had already come down a little from the watch, who already slept better, who no longer saw the suggestion as a profanation, stayed thinking, and the idea, which months before had offended her, began to seem to her good, and then desirable, and finally necessary, because Custódia had understood, meanwhile, that she had not in the house a single place that was hers, that the whole house was the absent children's and the guard's, and that she deserved, at last, a corner that was hers, to sit in, to read, to be, to exist as a person and not as a sentinel.

It was the first time in her life, Custódia understood in deciding it, that she was going to make a space only for herself. The house had always been the children's, and before the children it had been the parents', and never, anywhere, at any moment, had Custódia had a corner that was only hers, a place made to her measure, for her likings, for her rest. Her whole life she had settled herself in the corners that were left over, after giving the best to others, as mothers do, who keep the back part of the house and the chair nearest the kitchen and the place from which one gets up most often. And to make now, at her age, a space only hers, was an almost revolutionary act for a woman like that, it was to affirm that she deserved a place, that she existed, that she had a right to a corner of her own house that was for her and not to serve others. And Custódia, in planning that room, in choosing the chair, the light, the things, felt a new and timid thing, resembling pleasure, the pleasure of doing a thing for herself, of giving herself a treat, of treating herself, for the first time, as someone who also deserves care and not only as one who cares.

She decided to make of it a room of her own, a thing she had never had, a space only hers in the house that had always been the children's. She put away Mercês's girl's things with care, without throwing them out, kept them in a box, with tenderness, because they were memories and memories are kept; but she took them off the shelves, freed the room from the stopped past, and put there things of her own, a good chair to sit and read by the light of the window, a table, her things, and the room, which had been a reliquary, became a room again, a living room, a used room, a room of hers. And when she finished, and sat in the new chair, by the light of the window, in the space she had made for herself, Custódia felt a thing she had not felt in thirty years: that she had a place in her own house that was not the guard post, a place to be, to live, to be herself, and not anyone's sentinel.

It took her days to do it, and she did it with an inner ceremony that no one saw but that for her was important, almost a rite of passage. She took each of Mercês's girl's objects with both hands, lingered over it, remembered, and took her leave of it, and put it in the box with care, saying goodbye to it without throwing it out, because it was not a matter of erasing, it was a matter of tidying, of putting the past in its place as past to give room to the present. And there were tears, of course, because each object was a piece of a life in which she had been a full-time mother of a full house, and to tidy them away was to accept, for good, that that life had ended; but they were tears different from those of the vigil, they were tears of healthy mourning, of the mourning one does in order to be able to go on, and not of the mourning that is refused and that rots. Custódia wept those objects as one weeps a good thing that has ended and that one is grateful to have had, and on finishing, on seeing the room empty of the traces of the past and ready for a new life, she felt, beneath the sadness, an enormous relief, the relief of one who lays down at last a weight they had carried for years without knowing they could lay it down.

She telephoned Mercês to tell her, with some apprehension, because she was afraid her daughter would be hurt, that she would think the mother had erased her room, her childhood. But Mercês, on hearing, instead of being hurt, was glad, deeply glad, mother, good, good that you did that, I do not need that room stopped in time, I have my house, what I want is for you to use yours, for you to live. And Mercês understood, and said it to her mother, that that was the sign that her mother was getting better, that she had come down from the watch, that she was coming back to life, and that that was the best news she could receive, much better than a room kept as a museum of her childhood. And Custódia understood, with that conversation, that she had kept the rooms untouched thinking it was for the children, and that after all the children did not even want it, that it was she who needed to guard the temples to go on feeling herself on guard, and that in transforming the room she took nothing from the children, she gave herself a thing she had never had: a space to exist.

Tobias's room, that one, Custódia did not transform, yet, and it was a conscious decision, and it showed that she had learned to do things in their time, without extremes. She kept Tobias's room as a guest room, tidy but living, ready to receive whoever came, but no longer as a reliquary, no longer as a museum stopped in time, but as a normal room of a normal house where visitors sometimes stay. Did she take the growth marks off the wall? No, she left them, because they were beautiful, they were memory, and memory is kept; but she left them without solemnity, as one leaves a dear mark in a house, and not as an altar. And she understood, in making these choices, that to come down from the watch was not to erase the past nor to renounce motherhood, it was to put each thing in its place: the memories as memories, dear but light; the rooms as rooms, and not as posts; and the present life as what mattered now, her life, which was beginning again.

And she went on using the new room, sitting there, reading, being, living, and she discovered, slowly, likings she had forgotten she had, or had never had time to discover, because her whole life had been guard and the guard leaves no time for likings. She went back to going out, too, to being with Felizarda, the one who had flown, who went on pulling her toward life, toward outings, toward conversations, toward things that were not keeping watch; and Custódia, who had always thought Felizarda frivolous, discovered that Felizarda was not frivolous at all, she was only a woman who had come down from the watch in time, and who had much to teach her about how one lives after the children depart, how one makes of the years that remain a good thing instead of a vigil.

She discovered, in that new room and in the afternoons with Felizarda, likings she did not even know she had, because her whole life of guard had not left her time to discover what she liked. She discovered that she liked to sew, not out of necessity, but for pleasure, for the quiet of having her hands busy with a beautiful thing; she discovered that she liked to be in conversation with women of her age, to laugh, to speak of things that were not the children; she discovered that she liked to care for some plants she put at the window, to see them grow, a small and living thing to care for that did not enslave her as the guard of her children had enslaved her. And each liking discovered was a little of Custódia coming back, the Custódia of the line, the woman prior to the sentinel, and Custódia was astonished to have, at her age, new likings, to be capable, so late, of discovering things she liked, and she understood that it is never too late for a person to come back to themselves, that even after a whole life of guard there is still, beneath the sentinel, a woman with likings and wishes waiting for room to exist.

There was a visit from Tobias, in that time, that was the most beautiful proof of the change. Tobias, who came rarely, and who when he came came tense, awaiting the weight of his mother, came that time and found a mother he did not know: a mother who had things to tell him that were not complaints, who spoke to him of her friends, of her outings, of her life; a mother who asked after his life with interest and not with distress; a mother who, in farewell, instead of the usual oh son, when will you come back, I am so alone, told him go in peace, son, I am well, live your life, and come when you can, for I have mine here. And Tobias went away that time without the usual weight, without the guilt, and Custódia saw, on his face in farewell, a relief and a joy that told her everything: that in freeing her son from the weight of her vigil she had drawn him near, that Tobias, without the guilt suffocating him, could at last love his mother willingly, and that that light farewell was worth more, for the two of them, than all the heavy visits of the previous years. And Tobias began to come more, after that, and to telephone more, because it no longer cost him, because his mother had become a good place to come back to and not a weight to carry, and Custódia understood, with a deep joy, that she had recovered her son by letting him go, exactly as the Keeper had told her.

And the most beautiful was what happened with the children. Because as Custódia came down from the watch, as she had a life of her own, as she slept, as she was well, the calls with the children went on changing: they ceased to be laden with guilt, ceased to have the weight of the mother alone and sad, and became light, joyful, conversations between a mother who lived and children who lived. Mercês began to telephone more, and more willingly, because she no longer dreaded the call, no longer came out of it with guilt; Tobias began to come more, because it was no longer heavy to be with his mother, there was no longer the vigil suffocating him, there was a light mother, a mother with a life, a mother with whom it was good to be. Felizarda was, in this phase, a company that was worth more to Custódia than she expected. The two, who had been only acquaintances, became friends, and Felizarda took Custódia into her world, into her outings, into her occupations, and Custódia, who for thirty years had done nothing that was not for the children or the house, discovered, at her age, the joy of a friendship, of a chosen company, of doing things for pleasure with one one likes. The two went out walking, went to places, talked, laughed, and Custódia, who had forgotten the sound of her own laughter from so little using it, went back to laughing, and each laugh was a stone fewer in the rampart, because a sentinel does not laugh, she is on duty, and a woman who goes back to laughing is a woman who is coming down from the watch, coming back to being a person. And Felizarda, who knew the path, went on showing Custódia, without lessons, only by example, that one could have a good life after the children, that the years that remained could be good, that everything was not over when the nest emptied, that there was, on the other side of the empty nest, a life waiting to be lived, if one had the courage to go and fetch it.

And Custódia understood, watching the children draw near as she came down from the watch, the greatest irony of all: that for thirty years she had mounted guard so as not to lose her children, and that it had been the guard that had driven them away, and that it had been enough to come down from it, to let them go, to trust, to see them come back willingly. She had held them with a closed hand, and they had fled the closed hand; she opened the hand, and they alighted on it, free, and for that very reason nearer than ever.

It was a lesson Custódia would have liked to learn sooner, and sometimes she thought of it with some grief, of all the years she had lost, of all the heavy visits, of all the calls of guilt, of all the estrangement her vigil had caused and that could have been avoided had she understood sooner that to hold drives away and to let go draws near. But Felizarda, to whom she confessed this grief, said to her a thing that consoled her: do not think of what you lost, Custódia, think of what you still have. You have years ahead, you have your children, you have grandchildren, you have a life to live. What is past is past; you learned late, but you learned, and better late than never, and there are those who never learn and die on watch over an empty field without ever having understood. You understood in time to enjoy what is left you, and that is a stroke of luck, not a misfortune. And Custódia clung to this, to the idea that she had learned in time, that she was still in time to enjoy, and she stopped weeping the lost years to begin to live the years she had, which is, in the end, the only sensible thing one can do with the time that remains to us: to live it, instead of spending it weeping for what does not come back.
