## 1. The house that does not sleep

There was in Véspera a house that did not sleep, and whoever passed at night saw it: the kitchen light lit at two, at three in the morning, and behind the window the shadow of a woman standing, listening, like a sentinel atop a rampart.

On a night in October, Custódia woke at three, as always, without an alarm. It was not a sound that raised her, because there was no sound at all, it was the body, the old start of one who hears a child crying thirty years after the crying has stopped. She sat up in bed in the dark, her heart still beating for a danger that did not come, and waited a moment to hear it again, the crying, the cough, the small step in the corridor, anything that might justify the start. It did not come. It never came. But Custódia got up all the same, because to lie listening was worse than to go and look, and she put on the three-in-the-morning rounds dressing gown, the thick wool one she kept hung behind the door for this purpose, so as not to light lamps in search of a wrap.

The floorboards were cold underfoot. There was the smell of the wax she had waxed the corridor with the day before, a deep, sweet smell of a tended house no one trod. She went first to the kitchen. The light was on, as she had left it, but Custódia stopped on the threshold to confirm it, to confirm the beacon, and only then went on, because a kitchen in darkness was, to her, a house surrendered. She crossed the corridor with her hand grazing the wall, slowly, and reached the two rooms at the end. At the door of Mercês's room she stopped and listened. Nothing. At the door of Tobias's room she stopped and listened. Nothing. She pushed this last one, which creaked faintly, and went in.

The bed was made, as she had made it on Sunday, the sheets pulled straight, the bedspread even. Even so Custódia drew near, smoothed the top sheet with the palm of her hand, in a gesture that had nothing to correct, and pulled straight a corner of the bedspread that was already straight. On the wall, at the bed's head, were the pencil marks, Tobias's height marked each year, a line and the age beside it, in her writing: five, six, seven, and the marks climbing the wall until they stopped all at once at eighteen, where the boy stopped growing and began to go away. Custódia ran her finger along the last one, like one closing a coffin slowly, and stayed there, listening for a breathing that did not come, the breathing of a boy asleep that she had hoped to hear every night for thirty years and that had not, for a long, long time, filled that room.

Then she went back to bed, and lay with her eyes open in the dark until the light came through the chink of the window. She got up tired, with the tiredness of one who has kept vigil, and it was her own face, in the mirror, that charged her for the night: she was fifty-and-some, and the village gave her more, because the lack of sleep of a lifetime hollows a woman, and she had the sunken eyes of one who has not slept a whole night in thirty years. It was not the age. It was the vigil. It was having aged before her time from giving herself too much, having spent her health keeping watch over her children, and coming to old age worn not by the years but by the giving, with her body charging her, in aching bones, the price of a life atop the rampart.

That morning she went down to the village, because the pantry was asking. She came down the cobbles with the basket on her arm and went into Benvinda's grocery, which was the centre of the world for half of Véspera. Benvinda was at the counter, pencil behind her ear, the book of debts open, and raised her eyes when she saw her come in. Custódia brought no list, never brought one, knew by heart what it took to fill a house, and went on asking: a kilo and a half of salt cod, Benvinda, and of the good; all the eggs you have fresh; that whole ham; the big sack of flour; and milk, plenty of milk, that goes quickly. Benvinda set it all down on the counter, slowly, and the pile grew until it was almost a siege, food for a week of people, and at a certain point she stopped, pencil in the air, and looked at the mountain of things and then at Custódia.

Oh Custódia, she said, without hardness, with that mildness of one who notices, this here is food for six, and you are one. Who is all this for, woman.

Custódia straightened at the counter. She felt the small blow and put it away as she put everything away, inside, without a change of face. It is to have on hand, she answered. One never knows when they come. Mercês turns up any weekend with the little ones, and Tobias, if he does come, mustn't find the house empty. And Benvinda lowered her eyes to the book, and noted down what she was taking, and said no more, because she had already said what there was to say and Custódia had not heard it; but she stayed watching her go, the basket weighing on her arm, and felt for her, without saying it, a pity of equal for equal, the pity of one who recognises in another a tightness of the same family. At the far end of the square someone said, on seeing her pass, what a mother she was, she gave everything to those children, and Custódia heard, and straightened herself again, because it was the only thing left to her, to have been a great mother, to have kept vigil, to have given everything. But there came at once the poor thing, alone in that house, and that one hurt, because beneath the rampart of the watch there was a woman alone in a big house, a sentinel forgotten at her post long after the garrison had gone.

At noon she ate. She laid the big kitchen table, the one that seated six, and sat in her place, the mother's, at the head, with the other five places empty around her. She ate soup from a pot that fed a family, and in front of her, in the place where Mercês had sat as a girl, and beside her, where Tobias had sat, there was no one, only the clean wood and the echo of the spoon against the plate in a kitchen too large for the sound of a single person eating. She ate quickly, like one fulfilling a duty, and the rest of the soup stayed in the pot, and the whole ham went to the pantry, and the milk, and the eggs, to keep company with what was already there from the week before and had not got eaten.

It was while putting the milk away that she came upon the other pot, the Thursday boiled stew, forgotten at the back of the shelf. She lifted the lid and the smell rose, sour, turned, food gone bad from being too much for one mouth alone. Custódia took it to the sink and threw it out, and it hurt her, not for the food, though that too, but for what the surplus food was telling her there, at the sink, running down the drain: that she went on cooking for a full house that had emptied, that she set the table for six and scraped the pot of one, that she filled the pantry as though there were many mouths and then threw away the leftovers because there were no mouths at all but hers.

It was that afternoon that Aurélia, the granddaughter, Mercês's daughter, who had stayed in Véspera with her grandmother while her mother saw to some papers in the village, dropped the question on her. She was going about the house opening doors, as children do, to see what was behind each one, and stopped at the door of uncle Tobias's room, that tidy, quiet room, with the bed made and the pencil marks on the wall, and asked, from the height of her seven years, not knowing what she carried:

Grandma, why do you make uncle Tobias's bed if he doesn't sleep here?

Custódia, who was coming from the corridor with an armful of towels, stopped halfway through the gesture, like one struck by a stone she did not see coming. Aurélia had already gone on ahead, was already opening another door, had already forgotten having asked, with that lightness of children who drop the big questions the way they drop a toy. But Custódia was left with the question in her hand, not knowing how to set it down. I do it in case he comes, she said, low, more to herself than to the granddaughter who was no longer listening. So he finds the bed ready, so he doesn't arrive to a surrendered house. And even saying it she felt the hole in the answer, because Tobias did not come, he telephoned when he could, sent news, made his life even farther off than his sister, and that bed made every week, those sheets changed where no one slept, were not for him: they were for her, they were the proof that she was still on duty, that the mother had not abandoned the post.

And there, towels held to her chest at her son's door, Custódia remembered Belarmino. It came like that, without warning, the way the dead come. She remembered him alive, in bed, with the children still small on the other side of the wall, watching her rise for the umpteenth time that night. Come to sleep, woman, he would say, in the thick voice of sleep, the little ones are well, you don't have to get up at their every breath. And he would reach his hand to her across the sheet, the big warm hand, come here, rest, stay here. Custódia saw the hand in the dark, saw it open, waiting for her, and even so she got up, went to look, because the fear was stronger than her husband's hand, and she heard behind her Belarmino's sigh, letting the hand fall in the empty place where she should have been, and perhaps he too felt himself a little outside that vigil that had no place for anyone else. He died early. And with him died the only voice that had ever called her back from the rampart to the bed, and the watch, with no one to contest it, grew, took over everything, became the whole house.

Because that was what Custódia did not see, neither that afternoon nor yet: that she mounted guard over a post no one attacked any more. She had raised two children alone in that house, Mercês and Tobias, had been the only rampart between them and the world, and had been a good rampart, the children grew up safe, fed, watched. But a rampart that grows used to being on guard does not know how to stop being so when the war ends, and Custódia's war had ended long ago. The children had left for their own houses, as children leave when a mother has raised them well, and that, which ought to have been the proof of her success, was the wound she could not heal, because to keep the post, to keep the rooms ready, to leave the light on, was her way of refusing that time had passed, of pretending the house was still full. To let go the watch was to accept that her life as a full-time mother had ended, and that was what she feared most in the world.

Felizarda, her own age, had come down from the watch when her children left, had mourned the empty house and gone back to living, travelled, laughed, came to tell the village the places she had been. Idália, on the contrary, had withered with longing for her gone children, had let herself go, dried up at the window. Custódia had done neither one thing nor the other: she had stayed atop the rampart, eyes on the horizon, awaiting an enemy who did not come because he no longer existed, without even knowing that she was choosing, every night, not to come down.

Mercês came to fetch Aurélia at the end of the afternoon, and the little girl went off telling her mother the doors she had opened in grandma's house, and Custódia stayed at the door to watch them go down the street, the granddaughter hopping from stone to stone, the daughter holding her by the hand, until they turned the corner. Then she went into the house, which was once again the size of one person alone, with the echo of her steps in a space made for many, and she went lighting the lamps as it darkened, and left the kitchen one lit, as always, for the night.

She lay down early, not to sleep, to rest the body while the ear went on serving, and fell asleep lightly, like one who dozes at a post, awaiting the three in the morning when she would return to the rounds, to the kitchen, the corridor, the two rooms listening. She had made herself so wholly the sentinel of her children that, the sentinel taken away, she did not know if anyone was left over, did not even remember having been, at any time, a woman who did not live on watch over others. And so she went on, night after night, standing on her rampart, guarding empty rooms, keeping vigil over a house that did not sleep, without hearing, in the sleeping village outside, the voice the story was going to bring her, slowly, and that would tell her what no one had told her in thirty years: that the watch had ended, that the children were safe, that she could, at last, come down from the rampart and sleep.
