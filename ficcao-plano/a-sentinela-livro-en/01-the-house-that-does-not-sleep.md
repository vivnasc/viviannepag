## 1. The house that does not sleep

There was in Véspera a house that did not sleep, and whoever passed at night saw it: the kitchen light lit at two, at three in the morning, and behind the window the shadow of a woman standing, listening, like a sentinel atop a rampart. It was Custódia's house, and the woman standing was she, and for years that house had not slept, because for years Custódia had mounted guard over a post no one attacked any more.

Custódia was fifty-and-some when this story begins, though she looked more, because the lack of sleep of a lifetime marks a face, hollows it, ages it, and she had the face of one who has not slept in thirty years, the sunken eyes, the air of one who is always in the middle of a vigil. People, on seeing her, thought her older than she was, and it was not the age, it was the accumulated tiredness of thirty years without a whole night, the wearing of a body always on duty, always alert. And there was an injustice in this, that of a woman who had aged before her time from giving herself too much, who had spent her own health in the vigil of her children, and who came to old age worn not by the years but by the giving, with her body charging her, in lost nights and aching bones, the price of a life of keeping watch.

Custódia had raised two children in that house, Mercês and Tobias, and had raised them alone much of the time, because her husband, Belarmino, had died early, and the vigil she mounted over her children was that of one who knows she is the only rampart between them and the world. It was a good rampart. The children grew up safe, fed, watched, protected from all the dangers a mother can keep away by night and by day, and Custódia had of this a deep pride, the pride of one who held a house alone and kept it standing against everything. But a rampart that grows used to being on guard does not know how to stop being so when the war ends, and Custódia's war had ended long ago, and she went on atop the rampart, eyes on the horizon, awaiting an enemy who did not come because he no longer existed.

The routine of Custódia's nights had the precision of a guard's shift, and it was always the same. She lay down early, but not to sleep, more to rest the body while the ear went on serving. She fell asleep lightly, like one who dozes at a post, and at two, at three in the morning, without an alarm, the body raised her, with the old start, and Custódia got up, put on her dressing gown, and made the rounds: the kitchen, where she relit the light if it had gone out; the corridor; and the two rooms at the end, where she stopped at the door, listening, like a sentinel checking the perimeter. There was nothing to hear, there was never anything to hear, but Custódia listened all the same, because to listen was what she knew how to do, to listen was her way of existing, and a night without rounds was, for her, a night in which she had failed in her duty. Then she went back to bed, and lay with her eyes open in the dark until the light came, and got up tired, with the tiredness of one who has kept vigil, and crossed the day counting the hours to the next night, when she would return to the post.

The children had grown, it is what children do. Mercês had married and gone to live far away, in a city hours from Véspera, and had her own life, her work, her children; Tobias had followed his path even farther, and came rarely, telephoned when he could, sent news. The two were well. The two were safe. The two had left Custódia's house for their own houses, as children leave when a mother has raised them well, and that, which ought to have been the proof of Custódia's success, was the wound she could not heal, because a mother who made of vigil her life does not know what to do with herself when there is no longer anyone to watch over.

The first weeks after the last child left home had been, at the time, bearable, because Custódia still hoped, still thought the children would come back often, that the house would fill up again at weekends, that the leaving was not so definitive after all. But the weeks turned to months, and the months to years, and the visits, instead of increasing, spaced out, as is natural to happen when children make their lives, and Custódia went on understanding, slowly and with pain, that the leaving had indeed been definitive, that the house would not fill up again except for a visit, that the time of the full house had ended for good. And instead of mourning that loss and going on, as Felizarda had done, Custódia refused to accept it, and her refusal took the form of the watch: if she kept the post, if she went on standing vigil, if she kept the rooms ready, then in a way the house went on being full, the time had not ended, she went on being the full-time mother she had always been. The watch was, at bottom, a way of refusing time, of pretending nothing had changed, of keeping alive, by force of vigil, a life that had ended, and that was why Custódia clung to it so hard, because to let it go was to accept that time had passed and that her life as a full-time mother had ended, and that was what she feared most in the world.

And so she went on guarding. She went on waking at night, at two, at three, with the old start of one who hears a child crying, and got up, and went to look, and the rooms were empty, and the house was silent, and there was no crying, only the memory of a crying from thirty years ago that her body still listened for. She went on leaving the kitchen light lit, as she had left it when the children were small and she wanted them, if they woke frightened, to see that their mother was at her post. She went on not sleeping a whole night's sleep, because to sleep a whole night's sleep was to lower the guard, and to lower the guard, in the logic she had learned thirty years before, was to abandon the post, was to risk that the disaster only the vigil kept away would befall her children.

Belarmino, her husband, had been the only one who had ever tried to make her come down from the watch, and Custódia remembered that now, sometimes, with a wounded tenderness. While he still lived, when the children were small, Belarmino would say to her, on seeing her get up at night for the umpteenth time, come and sleep, woman, the little ones are well, you don't have to get up at every breath of theirs, and he would reach his hand to her in the bed, come here, rest. And Custódia could not, she got up all the same, went to look, because the fear was stronger than her husband's hand, and Belarmino sighed and let her go, and perhaps he too had felt himself a little outside that vigil that had no place for anyone else. Belarmino died early, and with him died the only voice that still called Custódia back from the rampart to the bed, to rest, to the life of two; and from his death Custódia was left alone with her watch, with no one to tell her come and sleep, and the watch, with no one to contest it, grew, took over everything, became the whole house.

She kept their rooms intact. Mercês's room as she had left it on leaving home, with her girl's things still on the shelves; Tobias's room the same, the bed made, waiting, always waiting, as though the children might come back at any moment and need to find the post just as they had left it. Custódia made those beds every week, beds where no one slept, changed the sheets of empty beds, dusted rooms where no one lived any more, and did it with the devotion of one who tends a temple, because for her those rooms were not empty rooms, they were guard posts, they were the proof that she went on being on duty, that the house was ready, that the mother had not abandoned her place.

There was a way Custódia had of speaking of her children that said everything: she always spoke of them in the present tense of vigilance, as though they were still hers to guard. She did not say Mercês lives far away, she said my Mercês, poor thing, with that life so full, I worry so; she did not say Tobias is well, she said oh Tobias, I never know if he eats as he should, if he rests, I always have him on my mind. She spoke of two adults as of two children she went on watching over at a distance, and whoever heard her understood that for Custódia the children had never truly grown, that she went on seeing them through the eyes of the mother who had watched over them small, unable to see them as what they were, grown people, capable, masters of themselves. And that inability to see them grown was the root of her vigil: one does not mount guard over a capable adult; one mounts guard over a fragile child; and Custódia, to go on mounting guard, had, without knowing it, to keep the children children in her head, not to let them grow within her, even though they had grown in the world.

Custódia's days were as organised as her nights, and as empty of herself. In the morning, after the badly slept night, she did the work of the too-large house, cleaned what was already clean, cooked portions that were left over, and all with the care of one who keeps a house ready for the children who might arrive. In the afternoon, she went to Mass, or saw to some thing in the village, and came back to the empty house, and the afternoon dragged, long, with nothing truly her own to fill it, only the wait for the night, when she would return to the post. She had no pastimes, Custódia, had no real friends, had nothing that was only hers, because her whole life had been her children's and the watch's, and now that the children had gone she was left a whole day to fill and nothing to fill it with but the house and the wait. And the days so, empty of herself and full of waiting, were perhaps as heavy as the nights of vigil, because they were the other face of the same coin: a woman with no life of her own, suspended between the watch of the night and the wait of the day, passing the time instead of living it.

The village looked at Custódia with that mixture of respect and pity that one has for the mothers who gave themselves wholly to their children. They said, oh Custódia, poor thing, alone in that house, with her children so far away, but what a mother she was, she gave everything to those children. And Custódia heard the what a mother she was and straightened herself, because it was the only thing left to her, to have been a great mother, to have kept vigil, to have guarded, to have given everything; but she also heard the poor thing, alone, and that poor thing hurt, because beneath the rampart of the watch there was a woman alone in a big empty house, a sentinel forgotten at her post long after the whole garrison had gone, and that woman, at night, listening for a silent house, felt a solitude she did not know how to name and that came, at bottom, from a simple thing: that she had spent her life guarding others and that no one had been left to guard her.

The house, too large for one person alone, was also a character in this story, because it had been thought and built for a family and went on set up for a family that no longer dwelt in it. There was the big kitchen table, where six fit and where one ate; there were the places each had had at the table, and Custódia always sat in hers, the mother's, leaving the others empty, as though the children might arrive and take theirs; there was the pantry that she still filled as though there were many mouths, and then the food spoiled, because it was too much for one person, and Custódia threw away food and it hurt her, not for the food, but for what the surplus food said, that she went on cooking for a full house that had emptied. Everything in that house was sized for a life that had ended, and Custódia moved through it like the last inhabitant of a too-large house, a small woman in a space made for many, and the echo of her steps in an empty house was perhaps the most exact sound of her life: that of a single presence in a space made for a multitude that had departed.

But Custódia did not complain, and did not come down from the watch, because to come down from the watch was, in her head, to cease being a mother, and to be a mother was all she knew how to be. There had been, before all this, a Custódia who was not only a watch, and Custódia, on the nights of vigil, sometimes went to fetch her, far back in memory, in search of a proof that she had been, at some time, something else. She vaguely remembered a girl, herself, before Belarmino and before the children, a girl who had had likings, who had laughed, who had dreamed of things she no longer even remembered which. But the memory of that girl was so old, and so buried under thirty years of watch, that it seemed more the memory of another person, of a character in a story heard in childhood, and Custódia could no longer feel her as being herself, could no longer recognise herself in that girl of likings and laughter. It was as if the watch had erased not only Custódia's present but also her past prior to motherhood, as if thirty years of sentinel had eaten the whole woman, before and after, leaving only the watch, only the post, only the vigil, and no Custódia in any part of time.

She had made herself so wholly the sentinel of her children that, the sentinel taken away, she did not know if anyone was left over, did not know who the Custódia was who was not on guard, did not even remember having been, at any time, a woman who did not live on watch over others. And so she went on, night after night, standing on her rampart, guarding empty rooms, keeping vigil over a house that did not sleep, awaiting a danger that had passed, without hearing, in the sleeping village outside, the voice the story was going to bring her, slowly, and that would tell her what no one had told her in thirty years: that the watch had ended, that the children were safe, that she could, at last, come down from the rampart and sleep.
