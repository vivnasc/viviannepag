## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Tranquilina — the quiet one — daughter of people of the village, born on a night of thunderstorm, and the name was a promise and an irony, of the kind life plays on houses without their noticing: the mother, who lived with the father a love of shouts and of reconciliations, of partings and returns, of storm upon storm, set on her daughter, born in the middle of a storm, the name of the quiet one, begging of the word the calm the house did not have; and the girl grew up with the name of calm and the heart of the storm, and made of her life the opposite of the name, because what is learned in a house is stronger than what is promised at a christening.

The register says on what night each one was born, and there are those who are born on nights that stay in their blood: Tranquilina was born on the night of one of the greatest storms remembered in Véspera, with the sky tearing itself and the river rising, and one would say, seeing the life she had, that she brought the storm within, that she came to need it, that she learned, before she learned to speak, that love makes noise, that what makes no noise is not love, and that peace, the true, the silent, was for her not the clear sky after the storm, but the desert, the emptiness, the place where one is no longer loved because no one shouts any more.

This is the seventh book of the shelf of the Bridge, the shelf of those who lose themselves when they love, and within it, it is the book of the one who confuses intensity with love. It tells the story of a woman who only feels loved in a storm, who needs the quarrels to feel the reconciliations, the partings to feel the returns, and who has now, for the first time, a serene love, a gentle man who loves her without storm, and whom that calm love, instead of soothing, terrifies, because it tastes to her of death, of abandonment, of desert, and she is on her way to blowing it up, to provoking the storm she lacks, only to feel, in the din, that she is still loved. It is not a book against passion; it is a book about one who learned, as a child, to read love by the noise, and who must learn, as a grown woman, to tell it from the calm, before she destroys, with her own hands, the first quiet love life has given her.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Tranquilina who only feels alive in a storm, who confuses peace with the lack of love and the din with the truth, who blows up what is good because the good tastes to her of too little, let her know, from the first line, what it took her a whole life not to know: that peace is not absence, it is love without a wound; that intensity is not proof of love, sometimes it is only the echo of a house where there was shouting; and that there is a night, in every life like that, the first calm night, on which the greater courage is not to blow up the peace to feel something, it is to cross it whole, without fleeing and without setting fire, and to discover, on the far side of the fear, that the calm was not the desert: it was, after all, the place where, for the first time, one could rest from being loved.
