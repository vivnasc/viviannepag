## 3. The love without noise

Brando loved Tranquilina in a way she had never seen, and so she did not know how to read it: he loved her without noise, and Tranquilina, who had learned to measure love by the noise, did not know what to do with a love that made none.

It was a love of small and sure gestures, like everything in Brando, who lived on small and sure things. He noticed things. Once, weeks before, Tranquilina had said in passing, looking at his window, that as a little girl she had had a wristwatch of her mother's, with a round face and some old numerals, and that it had been lost, and she said it the way one says a thing of no importance, and changed the subject at once. And Brando said nothing. But a month later, on a Sunday, he put into her hand, wrapped in a cloth, a lady's watch, old, with a round face, with numerals like those, that he had gone searching for in the junk fairs of the big village, and that he had cleaned and set going with his tweezer hands. And he said only, I like to see the time on you in this one, and he made no scene of it, did not wait for thanks on bended knee, went back to the café as if he had passed the salt. And Tranquilina was left with the watch on her wrist, moved and disconcerted, because it was the deepest gesture a man had ever had with her, and it was a gesture without noise, and she did not know how to receive gestures without noise.

He made her no declarations of fire, did not swear her eternal love in the middle of a quarrel, did not tell her he would die without her. He told her, with simplicity, I like you, Tranquilina, and he said it on calm days, without drama, without a storm being needed to wrench it out, and it was an I-like-you so serene, so sure, that Tranquilina, used to the I-love-yous shouted in the middle of the storms, did not know how to receive it, found it too little, found in it a lack of fire, without perceiving that that calm I-like-you was worth more, was deeper, was more to be trusted than all the shouted I-love-yous of the storm-men, because the shouted I-love-yous were wrenched out by the fever and passed with it, and Brando's I-like-you depended on no fever, it was there every day, calm, sure, like the clock in the middle of the window, that told the time to those who passed without needing anyone to notice it.

There was an afternoon when this was seen, and it was small and deep, and it was at the old station. Brando had to go to the big village to see to some pieces he had ordered, and said to Tranquilina I'm coming on the six o'clock train, will you wait for me at the station?, and she said yes. And Tranquilina, deep down, without wishing it, hoped he would be late, that he would fail, that he would leave her waiting, because the waiting, the distress of the waiting, was what she knew of love. She sat on the stone bench of the platform, the bench where her parents, courting, according to Felismina, had quarrelled and loved each other more times than anyone in the village, and saw the half-past-five come in, and Brando was not on it, of course, he had said at six; and Tranquilina's heart, even knowing, began to do what it had always done, to tighten, to ask and what if he doesn't come, and what if there was a problem, and Tranquilina caught herself almost liking that tightening, feeding it, because it was known, it was the taste of love according to her house, the heart leaping on the platform waiting for a man who perhaps will not come.

And the six o'clock train came in at six, and Brando got down, with the bag of pieces and his hat in his hand, and searched for her with his eyes on the platform and smiled on finding her, and came straight to her, sure, punctual, without any drama. And Tranquilina, on seeing him get down like that, instead of the enormous relief she expected, felt an absurd thing, an almost-disappointment, because there had been no true distress, there had been no uncertainty, there had been no dramatic return of one who had been lost and come back; there had been only a man who said he was coming at six and came at six. And Brando, who read something in her face, asked:

"Were you waiting long? You should have come on the six-ten, you needn't have bored yourself here alone."

And Tranquilina perceived, with shame, that she had been there an hour boring herself on purpose, feeding an invented distress, wishing the man who loved her would fail her, only to be able to feel, in the failure and the forgiveness, the fever she called love. And she said no, I've just arrived, and it was a lie, and they walked back on foot down the lower street, and Brando went on telling her about the pieces, calm, and she went at his side thinking she was mad, that she had before her a man to be trusted and wanted him to be one not to be trusted, that she had the harbour and longed for the shipwreck.

And it was that the horror of Tranquilina in those days: to perceive that her sickness made her wish for the bad of love. That she, without wishing it, wished for the quarrels, the delays, the jealousies, the partings, because without them she did not feel the love, and that she was, therefore, rooting for Brando, the good man, to fail, to lose his temper, to give her drama, and that this was unjust and was cruel, because Brando gave her nothing bad, gave her only the good, and she wanted the bad, and was going, if she did not mend, to punish him for being good, to provoke him so that he would give the bad she needed, and to spoil, with her sickness, a man who was not to blame for loving her in peace.

And there was a Saturday when Tranquilina saw Brando be who he was with another person, and that taught her more than everything he said to her. She was in the shop, sitting in a corner watching him work, when in came an old woman, aunt Custódia — keeping — from the upper street, with a wall clock wrapped in a towel, an old pendulum clock that had belonged to her dead husband. She brought it because it had stopped, she said, stopped on the night the man died, and she wanted it going again, and she had already been to two clockmakers in the big village who had told her it was not worth the trouble, that she should buy a new one. And Brando unwrapped the clock, and opened it, and looked at it a long time, and instead of telling the old woman it was not worth the trouble, which is what it was worth, he said only, with his mildness: this one goes, dona Custódia. It is tired, but it goes. Leave it with me a few weeks. And the old woman went off, and Brando set himself to work on that clock that was not worth the trouble it was going to give, night after night, by the light of the lamp, with the patience of one who lays out tiny pieces on a black cloth; and when Tranquilina asked him why he wasted so much time on a thing that was worth nothing, Brando raised his eyes, and said: it is not the clock she wants going, Tranquilina. It is the night it stopped. She wants to set going the night the man died. That is what I am mending. And Tranquilina was left with that lodged in her, because it was the most tender thing she had heard in her life, and it was said by a man bent over a clock, without looking at her, without making a scene of it, and she perceived, with a tightening, that that man had more tenderness in his way of taking up an old clock than all the storm-men had had in the declarations of fire they shouted at her, and that she, mad, found it too little.

Brando, who was mild but was not a fool, felt the restlessness in Tranquilina. One night, in the shop, after closing, with all the clocks making their low ticking around the two of them, and it was a sound that filled the shop without making noise, dozens of clocks marking the time each in its own way, he set down the tweezers, and asked her, with his serenity:

"Tranquilina. Are you well with me? Sometimes it seems to me you go about anxious, as if something were lacking. And if it is, tell me, for I want you to be well."

And Tranquilina, caught, did not know what to answer, because the truth, the storm is lacking to me, was impossible to say, was absurd, was sick, and she was ashamed of it; and she said:

"No, Brando. Everything is well."

And it was a lie, and Brando perceived that it was a lie, because a man who works with the smallest pieces sees at once when a thing does not sit right; but he did not insist, because he was a man who did not force, and he took up the tweezers again, and said only, when you want to tell me, I am here, in no hurry, as I say to the clocks. And Tranquilina was left with the lie lodged in her, and with his question hurting her, because Brando's question, are you well with me, was the question of a man who truly cared, who noticed her, who wished her well, and it was a calm question, without drama, and it was, in his way, a declaration of love greater than any drama, and Tranquilina did not know how to receive it, because it did not come wrapped in storm, and she only knew how to open the presents that came wrapped in storm.

And there was, still, a night of rain that put everything to the test without her perceiving it. Tranquilina fell ill, of a sudden fever, of the kind that pin a person to the bed from one day to the next, and she was alone at home, and sent word to Brando that she could not come to meet him as they had arranged, and she asked for nothing, because asking was not in her nature. And Brando, without her asking, closed the shop early, and came through the rain, and knocked at her door with a bundle of things, and came in, and made her a tea, and put a cold cloth on her forehead, and stayed. And he made no drama of it, did not say oh what a worry, did not rush about, did everything with that serenity of his, and at a certain point he sat by the bed with a watch in his hand, working low by the light of the lamp, keeping her company without drawing attention to it, and Tranquilina, in the fever, half asleep, heard the ticking and the rain outside, and felt a thing she did not know how to name that night and that was, she would perceive, the first time she felt it: she felt cared for without having to fall more ill than she was. Because in the parents' house, the caring too came in storm; her mother, when she fell ill as a girl, cared for her with great distress, with drama, with weeping, as if the fever were a calamity; and Tranquilina had learned that to be cared for was to be the object of distress. And Brando cared for her without distress, with calm, and that, at first, almost disappointed her, she thought he did not care enough because he did not fret; but in the fever, half asleep, she perceived that Brando's calm caring settled her, while her mother's anxious caring would have frightened her more, and that a man who cares for an ill person without making a tragedy of them is a man who gives security and not fear, and that she had spent her life confusing the two things.

And she perceived, that night, in the midst of the clocks, a thing that moved her and frightened her: that Brando loved her more, and better, than all the storm-men had loved her, and that she could not feel it, because his love made no noise, and she only felt the love that made noise. That it was like a person who went deaf from living beside a waterfall, and who no longer hears the low voices, only hears the crash, and that Brando spoke to her of love in a low, sure voice, in the ticking of his clocks, and she did not hear it, deafened by the crashes of the old loves, and was going, if she did not learn to hear the low, to lose the one man who spoke to her of love in a voice that did not need to shout.

And there was, too, in those days, a small thing that stayed hurting her, because it showed her the size of the damage of her learning. She was with Brando at a café table, and at the next table a young couple were quarrelling, low but with fire, hissing at each other, with that tension of quarrels in public; and Tranquilina, without realising, stayed watching them, and felt, underneath, a thing that shamed her: she felt a barb of envy. Envy of a quarrel. Because that couple, in the midst of the venom, had a thing that seemed to her, to her mad head, intensity, importance, in-earnest love; and she and Brando, there, calm, taking their coffee in peace, seemed to her, for an instant, lukewarm beside that. And Brando, who followed her look, perceived, because he was a man who perceived, and said only, low, with a serene sadness: you envy that, don't you? And Tranquilina did not know how to lie, and lowered her eyes, and Brando did not get angry, did not take it ill, said only: I know. I know that to you that seems more than this. But look well, Tranquilina; look at their faces. That is not too much love, it is love with fear. They are hurting each other. And you envy those who hurt each other, because they taught you that the hurt is the measure. And he fell silent, and drank his coffee, and Tranquilina was left with that driven in, because it was true, she envied those who hurt each other, and pitied those who were at peace, who were they, and she perceived the size of the crookedness she carried within, which was to prefer the venom to the honey only because the venom made more noise going down.

That night, lying down, Tranquilina wept, and it was a new weeping, not of drama, but of a deep sadness: the sadness of having before her a good love and not being able to feel it, of having the harbour and wanting the storm, of being, herself, the problem, the crooked one, the one who spoils the good for not knowing how to receive it. And for the first time she did not lay the blame on Brando, did not say he lacks fire, did not find him too little; she perceived that the lack was not in Brando, it was in her, in her deafened ear, in her head built on the storm, and that if she wished to stay with that love, if she wished to stop blowing up the good in search of crashing, she was going to have to do the hardest thing of her life: she was going to have to learn to hear love in a low voice, to feel the peace without confusing it with the desert, to tell Brando's calm from the lukewarm silence of the house, and to rest, at last, in a love that made no noise, which was, she perceived slowly, the only noise worth the trouble, that of the silence of one who loves us in peace.
