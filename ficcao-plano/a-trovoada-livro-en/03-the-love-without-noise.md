## 3. The love without noise

Brando loved Tranquilina in a way she had never seen, and so she did not know how to read it: he loved her without noise, and Tranquilina, who had learned to measure love by the noise, did not know what to do with a love that made none.

It was a love of small and sure gestures. Brando noticed things: he knew what she liked, remembered what she had said in passing, brought her without fuss the thing she needed before she asked for it. He made her no declarations of fire, did not swear her eternal love in the middle of a quarrel, did not tell her he would die without her; he told her, with simplicity, I like you, Tranquilina, and he said it on calm days, without drama, without a storm being needed to wrench it out, and it was an I-like-you so serene, so sure, that Tranquilina, used to the I-love-yous shouted in the middle of storms, did not know how to receive it, found it too little, found in it a lack of fire, without perceiving that that calm I-like-you was worth more, was deeper, was more to be trusted than all the shouted I-love-yous of the storm-men, because the shouted I-love-yous were wrenched out by the fever and passed with it, and Brando's I-like-you depended on no fever, it was there every day, calm, sure, like the sun that needs no storm to rise.

And Brando had a thing Tranquilina had never known in a man and that disconcerted her: he was to be trusted. When he said he was coming, he came; when he said a thing, it was true; he did not leave her waiting, did not put her in distress, did not play with her the game of partings and returns the others played. And Tranquilina, instead of resting in that to-be-trusted, which was gold, felt strangely disappointed, because Brando's to-be-trusted took from her the distress of waiting, and the distress of waiting had been, in the old loves, a part of the love, the part of the uncertainty, of the will-he-come, of the leaping heart, and without that distress the love seemed to her lukewarm, too sure, without the seasoning of uncertainty that she had learned to confuse with passion.

There was an afternoon when this was seen, and it was small and deep. Brando had to go to the city to see to a thing, and said to Tranquilina I'll come on the six o'clock train, wait for me at the station. And Tranquilina, at bottom, without wishing it, hoped he would be late, that he would fail, that he would leave her waiting, because the waiting, the distress of the waiting, was what she knew of love, and she almost wished for the delay to feel the known distress, to have her heart leaping, so as then, on seeing him arrive, to have the enormous relief of the return. And Brando came on the six o'clock train, sure, as he had said, and Tranquilina, on seeing him get down punctual, instead of the relief, felt an absurd thing, an almost-disappointment, because there had been no distress, there had been no uncertainty, there had been no dramatic return, there had been only a man who said he was coming at six and came at six, and that, which was the proof of a love to be trusted, tasted to her, to her mad head, of a lack of passion, and Tranquilina perceived, with shame, that she was wishing the man who loved her would fail her, only to be able to feel, in the failure and the forgiveness, the fever she called love.

And it was that the horror of Tranquilina in those days: to perceive that her illness made her wish for the bad of love. That she, without wishing it, wished for the quarrels, the delays, the jealousy, the partings, because without them she did not feel the love, and that she was, therefore, rooting for Brando, the good man, to fail, to get angry, to give her drama, and that this was unjust and was cruel, because Brando gave her nothing bad, gave her only the good, and she wanted the bad, and was going, if she did not mend herself, to punish him for being good, to provoke him so that he would give the bad she needed, and to spoil, with her illness, a man who was not to blame for loving her in peace.

Brando, who was gentle but was not a fool, felt the restlessness in Tranquilina, and one day, with his serenity, asked her: Tranquilina, are you well with me? Sometimes it seems to me you go about anxious, as if something were wanting. And if something is wanting, tell me, for I want you to be well. And Tranquilina, caught, did not know what to answer, because the truth, the storm is wanting, was impossible to say, was absurd, was sick, and she was ashamed of it; and she said no, Brando, all is well, and it was a lie, and Brando perceived that it was a lie, but did not insist, because he was a man who did not force, and Tranquilina was left with the lie stuck in her, and with his question hurting her, because Brando's question, are you well with me, was the question of a man who truly cared, who noticed her, who wished her well, and it was a calm question, without drama, and it was, in his way, a declaration of love greater than any drama, and Tranquilina did not know how to receive it, because it did not come wrapped in storm, and she only knew how to open the presents that came wrapped in storm.

And she perceived, that afternoon, a thing that moved her and frightened her: that Brando loved her more, and better, than all the storm-men had loved her, and that she could not feel it, because his love made no noise, and she only felt the love that made noise. That it was like a person who went deaf from living beside a waterfall, and who no longer hears the low voices, only hears the crash, and that Brando spoke to her of love in a low voice, sure, and she did not hear it, deafened by the crashes of the old loves, and was going, if she did not learn to hear the low, to lose the one man who spoke to her of love in a voice that did not need to shout.

That night, lying down, Tranquilina wept, and it was a new weeping, not of drama, but of a deep sadness: the sadness of having before her a good love and not being able to feel it, of having the harbour and wanting the storm, of being, herself, the problem, the crooked one, the one who spoils the good for not knowing how to receive it. And for the first time she did not lay the blame on Brando, did not say he lacks fire, did not find him too little; she perceived that the lack was not in Brando, it was in her, in her deafened ear, in her head built on the storm, and that if she wished to stay with that love, if she wished to stop blowing up the good in search of crashing, she was going to have to do the hardest thing of her life: she was going to have to learn to hear love in a low voice, to feel the peace without confusing it with the desert, to tell Brando's calm from the lukewarm silence of the house, and to rest, at last, in a love that made no noise, which was, she perceived slowly, the only noise worth the trouble, that of the silence of one who loves us in peace.
