## 12. The first calm night

There was a night, at the end of this book, that was the night the whole of it walked towards, and it was the most ordinary night in the world, and it was, for that very reason, the most important of Tranquilina's life: it was the first calm night she crossed whole, without fleeing and without setting fire.

She had been preparing for that night her whole life without knowing it, in each crossed itch, in each silence she had not broken, in each hand Brando had held for her at the bench; but none of the others had been quite that one, because the others had always had a reason near by, a difficulty, an eve, a thing that justified the staying still. This one had nothing. This one was the pure peace, without cause, without help, without a disaster to sustain it, the peace that was only peace, and it was that, she would perceive, the hardest to cross, because the calm without reason is the desert most like the desert, and Tranquilina, her whole life, had fled it more than anything.

It was a summer night, serene, and the two of them were in the new house, the one with the yard, married a short time. They had dined, and had tidied up, and now Brando was sitting at the kitchen table with a clock before him, one of those works he brought home, and he was cleaning the pieces by the light of the lamp, in no hurry, with the serenity of always; and Tranquilina was sitting on the other side of the table, doing nothing special, watching the night through the window, the same window that gave onto the yard where there was buried, under the rose bush, the heap of the shards of her childhood. There was nothing special, no disaster, no feast, no drama, only a calm night of a calm marriage, of the kind that fill the greater part of a good life and that Tranquilina, her whole life, had feared, because they were the nights on which, in the house of the Ladeira, one either made a storm or entered the desert.

And on that night, as on so many, in the middle of the rest, there rose in Tranquilina the old itch, the old fear of the desert, the old wish to break the calm, to provoke, to light a spark, to feel, in the crash, that she was alive, that she was loved, that this was not the lukewarm silence of childhood. It came in the usual clothes, dressed as a reason: she caught herself watching Brando bent over the clock, silent for an hour, and the old voice woke within her, and said, he doesn't even speak to me, he's more with that clock than with me, I don't count, this is my father's hearth again, I'm on his lap and he looks over my head. And the itch rose, and Tranquilina's hand nearly moved towards the spark, nearly found the sentence that was going to break the night, you're always with your clocks, to you I'm a bench.

And she did not say it. Tranquilina held herself, and breathed, and did what she had learned, itch by itch, hand by hand: she stopped, and looked at the itch head on, and asked herself, is this desert or is it peace? Is this silence the one of my father's hearth, or is it the one of Brando who loves me rested? And she looked at Brando, there, by the light of the lamp, with the loupe in his eye and a tiny piece between his fingers, and noticed a thing: that he, feeling her look, without raising his head, reached out his free hand across the table and laid it an instant on hers, only an instant, without words, without letting go of the work, and went back to the clock. And in that small gesture, in that hand that reached out without her asking, Tranquilina saw, at last, with clarity, that it was not desert, that it was peace; that Brando, silent over the clock, was not absent as the father was absent before the hearth; he was present, there, with her, keeping her company without noise, and had told her so with his hand, without needing to raise his eyes. The silence between the two was not the absence of the parents' house; it was the presence of a love that did not need to make noise.

And the itch, seen so, without fuel, without the desert that justified it, with nothing on the other side to answer it, went on rising to a crest, and there it trembled, and then, slowly, began to come down, and to drop, and went out, and passed. And on the far side of the crossed itch, for the first time in Tranquilina's life, there was neither storm nor desert: there was the peace.

And it was there, on the far side of the crossed itch, in that kitchen, with the ticking of Brando's clock starting up again low over the table, that Tranquilina discovered the thing the whole book had been keeping for her: she discovered that peace was not the desert. That she had spent her life fleeing the calm thinking it the desert of childhood, and that after all the calm, when one crosses the fear of it, is not the desert, it is the place where, at last, one rests. She felt, on that calm night, beside Brando, a thing she had never felt: she felt herself at home. Not in the house of the storm, not in the house of the desert, but in a third house, which she did not know, the house of peace, where one can be in silence without fear, where the love is present without making noise, where one need not provoke to confirm that one is loved, because one knows, in the calm, without proof, without crashing, that one is. And it was a discovery so great, so deep, so new, that Tranquilina, on that ordinary night, with Brando mending a clock beside her, wept, low, from a gladness that had no name, the gladness of resting, at last, from being loved, instead of fighting, a whole life, to be.

Brando, feeling her weep, set down the loupe, and set down the clock, and asked, with his calm:

"What is it, Tranquilina?"

And Tranquilina, with brimming eyes, told him the truest thing she had said in her life:

"Nothing bad, Brando. It's that I'm at peace. And it's the first time I'm at peace and have no fear. My whole life, the calm frightened me, tasted to me of desert, and I made a storm to flee it. Do you remember the cup, that night, what you said to me, that the glue is the mark of a thing that was broken? I spent my life keeping shards, Brando. Thinking they were love. And now I'm here, with you, in silence, and I have no wish to break anything, and I have no fear, and there are no shards to keep, and I'm only well. It's the first time. The first calm night of my life that I cross without fleeing and without setting fire."

And Brando, who understood what that was, said nothing, because it was not needed, took her hand, and the two stayed so, in silence, on Tranquilina's first calm night, two lovers in peace, without crashing, without fever, without storm, only the love, in a low voice, and the silence between the two full of that love, and the clock on the table marking, low, the time that passed carrying nothing off, which is what time does in the houses where one is at peace: it passes, and carries nothing off, because there is no longer a storm to carry things off, there are only the days, one after another, mild, joining together into a life.

And they stayed so, silent, and at a certain point the clock that Brando was mending, forgotten on the table, struck an hour, low, and Tranquilina heard it strike the hour, and remembered aunt Custódia, the clock of her dead husband that had stopped on the night of the death, and how Brando had set it going to set going the night the man had died. And she perceived, in that kitchen, that Brando had done the same to her: that she carried, since the night of thunderstorm on which she was born, a clock stopped within, stopped at the hour of the desert, at the hour of the hearth her father looked at over her head, and that that man, slowly, with the patience of one who lays out tiny pieces on a black cloth, in no hurry, without forcing, had set it going for her; that she was there, on that first calm night, going for the first time, marking a time that carried nothing off, and that the thing Brando had mended, in all those months, had not been a clock, it had been her. And she did not tell him this, because it was not needed, and because there were things the low voice says better in the silence; but she kept it, and it was the deepest truth she carried from that night: that she had been, herself, a clock stopped at the wrong hour, and that the calm love of a mild man had set her, at last, to striking the right hours.

And out there, on that summer night, there was no storm. Tranquilina drew near to the window, before lying down, and looked at the sky, and the sky was clear, serene, without a cloud, and below, in the yard, the rose bush she had planted over the shards of the Ladeira made a dark shape, and was giving its first buds, and Tranquilina stood looking at it, and thought of Aurora, and of Vasco, and of the glued cup, and of the shard with the painted flower she had kept apart, in a little box, at the bottom of the chest of drawers; and she perceived that she had made peace with the house she came from, without throwing it out and without repeating it, which was the only way to make peace with a house like that. And she fell asleep, at last, on a night without a storm, under the clear sky, in the peace, beside Brando, and it was the first of many nights so, calm nights that she went learning, one by one, to inhabit, without fleeing and without setting fire; and each calm night she crossed whole was a victory over the storm she carried in the cradle, and was a brick in the house without a storm she was making with Brando, and was the proof, given every night, that peace was not the desert, that one could love in a low voice, that the greater courage, for one who was born in the storm, is not to make the thunder, it is to cross the calm whole, and to discover, on the far side of the fear, that the quiet was not the lack of love: it was, after all, the love that did not need to make noise to be, and where, for the first time in a life of crashes, Tranquilina could, at last, do the one thing the storm had never let her do, which was to rest.
