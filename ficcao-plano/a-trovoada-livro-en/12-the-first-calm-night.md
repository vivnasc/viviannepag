## 12. The first calm night

There was a night, at the end of this book, that was the night the whole of it walked towards, and it was the most ordinary night in the world, and it was, for that very reason, the most important of Tranquilina's life: it was the first calm night she crossed whole, without fleeing and without setting fire.

It was a summer night, serene, and the two of them were at home, married a short time, and there was nothing special, no disaster, no feast, no drama, only a calm night of a calm marriage, of the kind that fill the greater part of a good life and that Tranquilina, her whole life, had feared, because they were the nights on which, in the parents' house, one either made a storm or entered the desert. And on that night, as on so many, in the middle of the calm, there rose in Tranquilina the old itch, the old fear of the desert, the old wish to break the calm, to provoke, to light a spark, to feel, in the crash, that she was alive, that she was loved, that this was not the lukewarm silence of childhood.

But Tranquilina, that night, made the whole crossing. She felt the itch rise, and instead of following it, instead of provoking, she stayed with it, and crossed it, slowly, as she had learned, and it was hard for her, because the itch was strong, and the memory of the desert was near, and there was a moment when she nearly gave in, when the old Tranquilina nearly won, when her hand nearly reached for the spark. And it did not reach. Tranquilina held herself, and breathed, and asked herself, as she had learned, is this desert or is it peace?, and looked at Brando, there, beside her, calm, reading, keeping her company without noise, and saw, at last, with clarity, that it was not desert, that it was peace, that Brando was there, that he loved her, that the silence between the two was not the absence of the parents' house, it was the presence of a love that did not need to make noise; and the itch, seen so, without fuel, without the desert that justified it, went out, slowly, and passed, and on the far side of the itch, for the first time in Tranquilina's life, there was neither storm nor desert: there was the peace.

And it was there, on the far side of the crossed itch, that Tranquilina discovered the thing the whole book had been keeping for her: she discovered that peace was not the desert. That she had spent her life fleeing the calm thinking it the desert of childhood, and that after all the calm, when one crosses the fear of it, is not the desert, it is the place where, at last, one rests. She felt, on that calm night, beside Brando, a thing she had never felt: she felt herself at home. Not in the house of the storm, not in the house of the desert, but in a third house, which she did not know, the house of peace, where one can be in silence without fear, where the love is present without making noise, where one need not provoke to confirm that one is loved, because one knows, in the calm, without proof, without crashing, that one is. And it was a discovery so great, so deep, so new, that Tranquilina, on that ordinary night, with Brando reading beside her, wept, low, from a gladness that had no name, the gladness of resting, at last, from being loved, instead of fighting, a whole life, to be.

Brando, feeling her weep, set down his book, and asked, with his calm, what is it, Tranquilina, and Tranquilina, with brimming eyes, told him the truest thing she had said in her life: nothing bad, Brando. It is that I am at peace. And it is the first time I am at peace and am not afraid. My whole life, the calm frightened me, tasted to me of desert, and I made a storm to flee it. And now I am here, with you, in silence, and I am not afraid, and I do not want to make a storm, and I am only well. It is the first time, Brando. The first calm night of my life that I cross without fleeing and without setting fire. And Brando, who understood what that was, said nothing, because it was not needed, took her hand, and the two stayed so, in silence, on Tranquilina's first calm night, two lovers in peace, without crashing, without fever, without storm, only the love, in a low voice, and the silence between the two full of that love, and Tranquilina, for the first time since a night of thunderstorm on which she was born, rested.

And out there, on that summer night, there was no storm. The sky was clear, serene, without a cloud, and it was a calm night like so many, and Tranquilina, who was born on a night of storm and who had learned to need it, fell asleep, at last, on a night without it, under the clear sky, in the peace, beside Brando, and it was the first of many nights so, calm nights that she went learning, one by one, to inhabit, without fleeing and without setting fire, and each calm night she crossed whole was a victory over the storm she carried from the cradle, and was a brick in the house without a storm she was going to make with Brando, and was the proof, given every night, that peace was not the desert, that one could love in a low voice, that the greater courage, for one who was born in the storm, is not to make the thunder, it is to cross the calm whole, and to discover, on the far side of the fear, that the quiet was not the lack of love: it was, after all, the love that did not need to make noise to be, and where, for the first time in a life of crashes, Tranquilina could, at last, do the one thing the storm had never let her do, which was to rest.
