## 8. Telling peace from absence

Tranquilina's cure began with a distinction, the one the Keeper had given her, and that she had to learn not with her head, which already knew it, but with her body, which is where the illness dwelt: to learn to feel the difference between the two calms, that of peace and that of absence, which in the parents' house came confused and which her whole life she had seen as the same.

She went to seek Brando, after days at the bottom, and it was the hardest thing she had done, because to seek is to be on the side of the one who asks, and Tranquilina, proud, raised in the storm, did not know how to ask, she knew how to provoke; but she lowered her pride, and went to him, and told him the truth, the whole truth, which was the first time she told the whole truth to anyone: Brando, I am ill. I was born in a house of storms, and I learned that love is the crash, and that peace is the desert, and so your peace, which is love, tastes to me of a lack of love, and I provoked storms to flee a desert that is in my head and not in you. And I punished you for being good, and that was the most unjust thing of my life, and I beg your pardon. I do not want to lose you. And I know I have no right to ask you anything, after what I did to you. But I ask you for a chance. Not to go on as we were, for so I will blow you up. A chance to learn. To learn to love you in peace. Slowly. With relapses, for there will be some. But learning. Will you give me that chance?

And Brando, who was gentle but was not a fool, heard her, and stayed silent, and then said, with an honesty that honoured her: Tranquilina, I love you. And what you tell me now, that truth, is more than you ever gave me. But I am afraid. I am afraid of coming back and of you, a month from now, going back to the storms, and of your wearing me out, and of my becoming a bitter man living with a woman who punishes me for being good. I do not give you storms, Tranquilina, it is not my nature, and if you do not cure yourself in earnest, this is not going to work. So I give you the chance, yes, but on one condition: that you treat yourself in earnest, that you look at that illness of yours head on, that each time the itch of the storm comes to you, you stop, and see it, and do not follow it. I will help you. But the cure has to be you. And at the first punishment, at the first Bravo you throw in my face, I will go, because I do not want to wear myself out. Do you accept it so?

And Tranquilina accepted, and began, with Brando, the hardest work of her life: to learn to tell the two calms apart, and to rest in the good one. And it was a work of every day, and it was made of small things. Each time she was with Brando on a calm night and felt rising the fear of the desert, the itch of the storm, the wish to provoke, Tranquilina, instead of provoking, stopped, and did the thing the Keeper had taught her: she asked herself, is this desert or is it peace? Is this silence the one of my worn-out parents, or is it the one of Brando who loves me rested? And she learned to see, slowly, that it was peace, that Brando was there, that he looked at her with love, that the silence was not absence, it was presence without noise, and that the fear of the desert was a memory, was the house, was the old storm, and not the reality of the calm night with a man who loved her.

And she went on discovering, in making this distinction day after day, things that moved her. She discovered that peace had a taste, when one learned to feel it, that was not the desert: it was a taste of rest, of safety, of not having to be on alert, of being able to drop her guard, and that that taste, at first almost imperceptible to her, deafened by the crashes, became, slowly, audible, like a low voice one begins to hear when the noise drops. She discovered that the body, after a calm night with Brando, did not come out exhausted as it came out of the storms, it came out rested, and that that rest, which she before read as boredom, was in truth what love should give, the rest, and that she had spent her life confusing the exhaustion of the storm with the intensity of love, and fleeing the rest thinking it a lack of love.

And there was a moment, in that learning, that was the hinge of the cure. One night, calm, with Brando, Tranquilina felt rising the itch of the storm, strong, and almost gave in, almost provoked; but she stopped, and instead of following the itch, did a new thing: she told the truth to Brando. She told him, Brando, the itch is coming to me. I am feeling the wish to make a quarrel. It is not of you, it is of my illness. Help me not to follow it. And Brando, instead of getting angry, instead of taking it ill, did the most beautiful thing: he took her hand, and stayed silent, with her, holding her hand in the itch, and Tranquilina felt the itch of the storm rise, and rise, and Brando there, calm, holding her hand, without feeding the storm, without taking the spark, and the itch, without fuel, without a crash on the other side, went out, slowly, and passed, and on the far side of the itch, instead of the desert she feared, was Brando, his hand in hers, the peace, and Tranquilina perceived, in that moment, in her body, the thing that was going to cure her: that if she crossed the itch without following it, without provoking, on the far side was not the desert, was the peace, and that each time she crossed the itch without giving in, the illness weakened, and the peace gained taste, and she learned, gesture by gesture, night by night, itch by crossed itch, the thing she had been born without knowing and was going, at last, to learn as a grown woman: that one can love in peace, that the calm is not the desert, and that the greater courage, for one who was born in the storm, is not to make the storm, it is to cross the calm whole, without fleeing and without setting fire, until one discovers that on the far side is, not the absence, but the love, rested, waiting.
