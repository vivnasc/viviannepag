## 8. Telling peace from absence

Tranquilina's cure began with a distinction, the one the Keeper had given her, and that she had to learn not with her head, which already knew it, but with her body, which is where the sickness dwelt: to learn to feel the difference between the two calms, that of peace and that of absence, which in the parents' house came confused and which her whole life she had seen as the same.

She went to seek Brando, after days at the bottom, and it was the hardest thing she had done, because to seek is to be on the side of the one who asks, and Tranquilina, proud, raised in the storm, did not know how to ask, she knew how to provoke. But she lowered her pride, and went down the lower street, and stopped at the shop door, and saw him inside, at the bench, bent over a clock, and he saw her through the window and did not come to the door, he stayed where he was, waiting, and she had to go in, and it was, to go into that shop, with all the clocks making their low ticking around her, the hardest thing of her whole life. Brando set down the tweezers, but did not get up. He stayed looking at her, without hardness and without sweetness, waiting. And Tranquilina, standing in the middle of the shop, told him the truth, the whole of it, which was the first time she told the whole truth to anyone:

"Brando, I am ill. I was born in a house of storms, on the night the river nearly carried off the bridge, and I learned there, before I could speak, that love is the crash, and that peace is the desert. I have kept my whole life a jar of shards of the crockery my parents broke, thinking I kept love, and what I kept was damage. And so your peace, which is love, tastes to me of a lack of love, and I provoked storms to flee a desert that is in my head and not in you. And I punished you for being good. And I threw Bravo in your face, which is the most unjust thing I have done in my life, and which was a lie, because I do not want Bravo, I never wanted him after meeting you, I only wanted to wound you so you would give me the crashing. I beg your pardon. I do not want to lose you. And I know I have no right to ask you anything, after what I did to you. But I ask you for a chance. Not to go on as we were, for so I will blow you up. A chance to learn. To learn to love you in peace. Slowly. With relapses, for there will be some. But learning. Will you give me that chance?"

And Brando, who was mild but was not a fool, heard her to the end, without interrupting her, and stayed silent, with the clocks marking the silence around the two of them. And then he said, with an honesty that honoured her:

"Tranquilina, I love you. And what you tell me now, that truth, is more than you ever gave me. But I am afraid. I am afraid of coming back and of you, a month from now, going back to the storms, and of your wearing me out, and of my becoming a bitter man living with a woman who punishes me for being good. I saw what this did to my father, you know? My father was like me, and my mother was not storm, but she was of complaint, of bitterness, and she wore him out, year by year, until he became a quiet man who had no gladness left at all. I do not want to wear myself out so. I do not give you storms, Tranquilina, it is not my nature, and if you do not cure yourself in earnest, this is not going to work." And he was a moment silent, and then: "So I give you the chance, yes, but on one condition. That you treat yourself in earnest. That each time the itch of the storm comes to you, you stop, and see it, and do not follow it. I will help you. But the cure has to be you. And at the first Bravo you throw in my face, at the first punishment for my being good, I will go, and that time I will not speak. Do you accept it so?"

And Tranquilina accepted, and began, with Brando, the hardest work of her life: to learn to tell the two calms apart, and to rest in the good one. And it was a work of every day, and it was made of small things. Each time she was with Brando on a calm night and felt rising the fear of the desert, the itch of the storm, the wish to provoke, Tranquilina, instead of provoking, stopped, and did the thing the Keeper had taught her: she asked herself, is this desert or is it peace? Is this silence the one of my worn-out parents, or is it the one of Brando who loves me rested? And she learned to see, slowly, that it was peace, that Brando was there, that he looked at her with love, that the silence was not absence, it was presence without noise, and that the fear of the desert was a memory, was the house, was the old storm, and not the reality of the calm night with a man who loved her.

And there were relapses, as she had warned there would be, and the first came early, and nearly threw everything away, and it was important because it showed her that the cure was not not to fall, it was to get up quickly. It was on a night, a few weeks after they had come back, when the itch caught her off guard and she, before she saw it, had already said a crooked thing to Brando, a barb, of the small ones, all right, never mind me, I am nothing, I will stay here alone as always. And she saw, the instant after, Brando's face close, and saw him set down what he had in his hands and go to fetch his coat, slowly, without a word, because he had warned her, at the first punishment I will go. And Tranquilina felt the panic, the true panic of losing him again, and did a thing she had never done in her life: she ran after a storm she herself had lit, to put it out. Brando, wait, she said, and her voice trembled. What I said was the sickness. It was the itch. I saw it late, I had already let it out, but I saw it. Do not go. Let me set it right. I did not mean that, I wanted to light a spark, and I saw it after lighting it, and I am putting it out in front of you. Please. And Brando stayed with the coat in his hand, at the door, measuring her, seeing whether it was true; and it was; and he set down the coat. And he said only, all right. But next time, see the itch before you say it, not after. And Tranquilina perceived, that night, that the cure was not never again to feel the itch, it was to see it earlier and earlier, first after saying it, then in saying it, and at last before, and that each time she caught it earlier it was a step, and that the whole road was to learn to see it in time, as one learns to feel the rain in one's knees before the sky gathers.

And she went on discovering, in making this distinction day after day, things that moved her. She discovered that peace had a taste, when one learned to feel it, that was not the desert: it was a taste of rest, of safety, of not having to be on alert, of being able to drop her guard, and that that taste, at first almost imperceptible to her, deafened by the crashes, became, slowly, audible, like a low voice one begins to hear when the noise drops. She discovered, too, that peace had time, which was a thing the storm had never had. In the houses of storm, time is all short and all in jolts, one lives from peak to peak, from quarrel to peace, and the lukewarm days in between, the calm ones, do not count, they are only the wait for the next crash; and Tranquilina, raised so, had never learned to inhabit the long, mild time, the afternoon when nothing happens, the week with nothing special, which she had always thought dead time, time to waste. And now, with Brando, she went on discovering that it was in that time, in the mild, in the time when nothing happens, that life was truly lived; that the best hours of her life came to be the ones she would before have thought the emptiest, an afternoon sewing beside him at his work, a supper without a subject, a walk with no purpose; and that she had spent her life despising the time of the peace, skipping it, in a hurry to reach the next crash, and that the crash lasted a minute and the time of the peace lasted a life, and that she had traded a whole life for a succession of minutes, and that she was, at last, learning to make the trade the other way, to live the long time instead of the short minutes.

She discovered that the body, after a calm night with Brando, did not come out exhausted as it came out of the storms, it came out rested, and that that rest, which she before read as boredom, was in truth what love should give, the rest, and that she had spent her life confusing the exhaustion of the storm with the intensity of love, and fleeing the rest thinking it a lack of love.

And there was a small sign, in the body, that told her something was changing before her head knew it. Tranquilina noticed, one morning, that she had slept the whole night through. It was nothing at all for most people; for her it was a miracle, because her whole life she had slept badly, with a start, with one ear half open awaiting the crash, as those sleep who grew up in houses where the night could break at any hour. The little girl of the Ladeira had learned to sleep as soldiers sleep, with one foot out of the trench, and the woman had carried that broken sleep into all the beds of her life. And now, beside Brando, with no crash possible, with no threat in the dark, her body began, slowly, to let itself sleep whole, to release the guard it had kept mounted thirty years, and Tranquilina caught herself waking in the morning rested, a thing she barely knew, and perceiving that the alert with which she had lived her whole life had not been her way of being, it had been the mark of a house, and that she, in a room in peace, beside a man who did not blow up, was learning a thing the body had forgotten if it had ever known: to learn to drop the guard, which is the thing the peace asks and the desert never allows.

And there was a moment, in that learning, that was the hinge of the cure. One night, in the shop, after closing, with the two of them sitting at the bench and the clocks around them, Tranquilina felt rising the itch of the storm, strong, without cause, out of nothing, as it always came. And she nearly gave in; she already had the sentence ready, already had the thread chosen to pull until the knit came undone. But she stopped. And instead of following the itch, she did a new thing, the hardest thing, which was to tell the truth in the exact moment when it hurt:

"Brando," she said, and her voice trembled. "The itch is coming to me. Now. I am feeling the wish to make a quarrel, to say a bad thing to you, to break this. It is not of you. It is of my sickness. Help me not to follow it."

And Brando, instead of getting angry, instead of taking it ill, instead of asking but why, what did I do, did the most beautiful thing: he reached out his hand across the bench, and took hers, and stayed silent, with her, holding her hand in the itch. And he said nothing, and did not let go, and they stayed so, and Tranquilina felt the itch of the storm rise, and rise, and her heart beat, and the old little girl of the Ladeira pushing her towards the spark; and Brando there, calm, holding her hand, without feeding the storm, without taking the spark, without giving her the other side of the crash that the crash needs to exist. And the itch, without fuel, with nothing on the other side, went on rising to a crest, and there it trembled, and then, slowly, began to come down, and to drop, and went out, and passed. And on the far side of the itch, instead of the desert she feared, was Brando, his hand in hers, the clocks marking the time low, the peace.

And Tranquilina perceived, in that moment, in her body, and not in her head, the thing that was going to cure her: that if she crossed the itch without following it, without provoking, on the far side was not the desert, was the peace; and that each time she crossed the itch without giving in, the sickness weakened, and the peace gained taste, and she learned, gesture by gesture, night by night, itch by crossed itch, and hand by held hand, the thing she had been born without knowing and was going, at last, to learn as a grown woman: that one can love in peace, that the calm is not the desert, and that the greater courage, for one who was born in the storm, is not to make the storm, it is to cross the calm whole, without fleeing and without setting fire, until one discovers that on the far side is, not the absence, but the love, rested, waiting.
