## 11. The house without a storm

The itch cured, or nearly, and the peace proven in disaster, Tranquilina and Brando decided to make a life together, and it was a calm decision, without the drama of the engagements she had known, and it was that calm, precisely, that told Tranquilina she was doing the right thing.

There was no great scene, no proposal on bended knee in the middle of a storm, no rapture she had learned to confuse with the importance of decisions. It was on a Sunday afternoon, in the shop, with him finishing the assembly of a clock he had been mending for months, a pocket watch of a customer who had already given up on it. Brando tightened the last screw, wound it, held it to his ear, and the watch began to go, and he smiled that smile of one who has done a small and sure thing, and held it out to Tranquilina for her to hear the ticking, and said, without further ceremony, with his simplicity:

"Listen. It's going again." And then, in the same tone, without changing his voice: "Tranquilina, I want to make a life with you. Will you marry me?"

And Tranquilina said yes, and it was a calm yes, without fever, without crashing, with a watch just mended ticking in her hand. And for an instant the old Tranquilina nearly grew disappointed at the lack of drama, nearly found too little that proposal without fire, without knees, without tears, made the way one says the watch is going again; but the new Tranquilina, the one curing herself, perceived, in the same instant, that it was precisely so, calm, without drama, that a life should be decided, and that the drama, which she had learned to put into everything, was not proof of importance, it was only noise. The most important decision of her life was being taken in the low voice, in the peace, with the ticking of a watch that had gone again, and it was so it should be, because a calm life is begun with a calm decision, and not with a storm.

And in deciding to make a life with Brando, Tranquilina thought of the house they would make, and thought of it, for the first time, as the opposite of the house where she was born. She thought of a house without a storm, a house where love made no noise, where there was no shouting, where the difficulties were faced with calm and not with war, where the silence was the peace and not the desert, and where the children, if they had any, grew up learning that love is the rest and not the crash, that peace is not a lack of love, that one can love without wounding. And she perceived, in imagining that house, that she was doing the thing the Keeper had told her, she was cutting the chain, she was not passing the storm on, she was being, in her family, the woman who stopped the storm.

And she did, in those days, a thing she needed to do, and that was the farewell to the old house. She went to the chest of drawers, and took from the bottom drawer the jar of shards, the bits of the crockery of the Ladeira, those of Aurora's glued cup, the wreckage she had kept her whole life thinking she kept love. And she thought of throwing them out, of going to the river and throwing them in the water, but she did not do it, because to throw them out was to pretend that house had not existed, and she did not want to pretend; she wanted to forgive. So she took a single shard, the biggest, one that still had a remnant of a painted flower, and kept it apart, in a little box, that one only, to remember where she came from, Aurora at the window, Vasco gluing the cup a whole night, the in-earnest love those two had had and had not known how to keep without breaking it. And the rest, the heap of shards, she took and buried at the bottom of the yard of the new house, under a rose bush she planted over it, and told no one why, and it was her way of making peace with the Ladeira: not to throw out the parents' house, but to put it under a thing that flowered.

And she thought of the parents, and it was with pity and with forgiveness. She perceived that the parents had not been bad, that they had loved each other, in their way, in storm, but that they had been, themselves, children of houses of storm, that they had inherited the storm as she had inherited it, and that they had worn out their life shouting not from not loving each other, but from not knowing how to love any other way, from no one ever having taught them, from never having had, as she had had, the luck of a Brando, of a calm love that showed them there was another way. And she had pity on Aurora, who had died without ever having a single settled day, as Felismina had said; and on Vasco, who glued cups in the middle of the night because it was the only tenderness he knew how to give; and she forgave them the storm they gave her, because they gave what they had, and what they had was the storm, and not the peace. And in forgiving them, Tranquilina freed herself from them, took her leave of the house of the storm, and was left, at last, free to make her own, the house without a storm, with Brando, with the peace she had learned.

And she went, before marrying, to Felismina, on purpose, to tell her a thing she needed to say. She found the old woman at her door, taking the sun, already bent, with the hands that had borne a life, and she sat beside her on the step, as she used to sit as a little girl, and said to her: Felismina saved me. That soup, that day, what she said to me, that I was still in time. And what the Keeper told me about her, that Felismina cut the chain, that she came from worse than I and made peace. I am going to marry Brando, Felismina. And I am going to make a house without a storm. And I wanted Felismina to know that it was she who showed me it could be done. And the old woman stayed silent, and her eyes filled, and she took Tranquilina's hand with that hand of hers all knots, and said only: then there are two of us now, child. And it is so these things are done; one at a time, one showing the other that it can be. Your mother had no one to show her. You had me, and the one who comes after you will have you. It is so a chain like this is ended; not all at once, but one by one. And Tranquilina perceived, on that step, in the sun, with Felismina's hand in hers, that her cure was not an end, it was a link, that she came to be, in the village, what Felismina had been for her, the living proof that it is cut, and that one day some young woman, from a house of shouting, would see her calm with her clockmaker and would think, without knowing why, that perhaps it could be done.

And the village, which saw from outside, was surprised at the calm of Tranquilina, whom it had known tempestuous. A friend of hers, Rosalina, from the days of girlhood, of the loves of fire of both of them, met her one day and looked her up and down, and said, with the best of intentions and a barb of pity:

"Oh Tranquilina, you who were so full of fire, always in a drama, always in a love that was a gale. And now this, so calm, with the clockmaker, without a scene, without a jealousy. Don't you miss the passion, woman? That was life."

And Tranquilina answered a thing she herself heard with surprise, so clear it was, and that came out of her without effort, because it was no longer an idea, it was a thing known in the body:

"I miss the fever sometimes, Rosalina, for the fever is addictive, I won't lie to you. But the fever left me exhausted and alone, and the peace leaves me rested and accompanied. I saw my mother die of what you call life. That wasn't life, it was a fire, and she burned in it. I no longer want the fever. I want the love. And love, I discovered late, is calm. The fever is what makes noise. And what makes noise is not the strongest; it's only the loudest."

And Rosalina stayed silent, because she had no answer to that, and she went, and carried the sentence with her, and perhaps, who knows, it would serve her one day.

And they married, on an autumn morning, without pomp, in the small church of the village, with Felismina in a front pew and Brando's old mother with her stick and a few more, and it was a calm wedding, without the rapture she would, as a girl, have demanded of a day like that. And there was an instant, at the church door, when the old Tranquilina gave her last sign: she looked at it, the village coming out, the mild sun, Brando at her side in his suit, everything so serene, and felt, for a second, the old disappointment, is this a wedding?, where is the fire, where is the great thing?; and right after, the second after, she laughed at herself, laughed within from a new gladness, because she perceived that that disappointment was the sickness giving its last kick, and that she now recognised it, and no longer obeyed it, and that a calm wedding was exactly what she wanted, and that the great thing was there, all of it, in the mild man at her side who took her hand without gripping it too hard, and that she had learned, at last, to see the great in the calm, which is the hardest thing to learn for one who only knows how to see the great in the crash. And on leaving the church, arm in arm with Brando, Tranquilina passed before the fountain of the square, the same fountain that had spoken to her on the day of the Keeper, and the fountain ran mild, and she heard it, and smiled at it, as at an old acquaintance who had told her the truth.

And at night, lying down, on the first night of a calm life with Brando, Tranquilina felt a thing she had never felt, and that was the exact opposite of the girl who provoked storms so as not to be in the desert: she felt peace, and was not afraid of it. She did not confuse it with the desert, did not feel the itch to break it, did not await the crash; she rested in the peace, let herself be in it, and fell asleep calm, in the calm, without needing a storm, and it was a sleep different from all those of a life, because it was the sleep of one who had learned, at last, to rest from being loved, and not to fight to be it, the sleep of one who had cut the chain of the storm and lay down, for the first time, on a night without it, in a peace that was no longer the desert, it was, at last, the house without a storm that she was going, from then on, with Brando, to learn to inhabit.
