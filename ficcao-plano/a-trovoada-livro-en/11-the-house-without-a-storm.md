## 11. The house without a storm

The itch cured, or nearly, and the peace proven in disaster, Tranquilina and Brando decided to make a life together, and it was a calm decision, without the drama of the engagements she had known, and it was that calm, precisely, that told Tranquilina she was doing the right thing.

There was no great scene, no proposal on bended knee in the middle of a storm, no rapture she had learned to confuse with the importance of decisions; there was a calm conversation, at the door, on a serene night, in which Brando said to her, with his simplicity, Tranquilina, I want to make a life with you, will you marry me?, and Tranquilina said yes, and it was a calm yes, without fever, without crashing, and for an instant the old Tranquilina nearly grew disappointed at the lack of drama, nearly found too little that proposal without fire; but the new Tranquilina, the one curing herself, perceived, in the same instant, that it was precisely so, calm, without drama, that a life should be decided, and that the drama, which she had learned to put into everything, was not proof of importance, it was only noise, and that the most important decision of her life was being taken in the low voice, in the peace, and that it was so it should be, because a calm life is begun with a calm decision, and not with a storm.

And in deciding to make a life with Brando, Tranquilina thought of the house they would make, and thought of it, for the first time, as the opposite of the house where she was born. She thought of a house without a storm, a house where love made no noise, where there was no shouting, where the difficulties were faced with calm and not with war, where the silence was the peace and not the desert, and where the children, if they had any, grew up learning that love is the rest and not the crash, that peace is not a lack of love, that one can love without wounding. And she perceived, in imagining that house, that she was doing the thing the Keeper had told her, she was cutting the chain, she was not passing the storm on, she was being, in her family, the woman who stopped the storm, who learned to love in peace, and who was going to give her children, if she had any, a house without the crash she had inherited, and to cut, in a calm marriage, the chain of generations that came from a night of thunderstorm on which she was born.

And she thought of the parents, and it was with pity and with forgiveness. She perceived that the parents had not been bad, that they had loved each other, in their way, in storm, but that they had been, themselves, children of houses of storm, that they had inherited the storm as she had inherited it, and that they had worn out their life shouting not from not loving each other, but from not knowing how to love any other way, from no one ever having taught them, from never having had, as she had had, the luck of a Brando, of a calm love that showed them there was another way. And she had pity on the parents, who loved each other so and wore themselves out so, and forgave them the storm they gave her, because they gave what they had, and what they had was the storm, and not the peace; and in forgiving them, Tranquilina freed herself from them, took her leave of the house of the storm, and was left, at last, free to make her own, the house without a storm, with Brando, with the peace she had learned.

And the village, which saw from outside, was surprised at the calm of Tranquilina, whom it had known tempestuous. Her friends, used to her dramas, to her loves of fire, saw her now calm, with Brando, without dramas, and some, raised like her in the idea that love is crashing, almost pitied her, thought Tranquilina had grown too calm, that she had lost the passion, that she had settled for a lukewarm love; and they said to her, with the best of intentions, oh Tranquilina, you who were so full of fire, now so calm, do you not miss the passion?, and Tranquilina answered a thing she herself heard with surprise, so clear: I miss the fever sometimes, for the fever is addictive. But the fever left me exhausted and alone, and the peace leaves me rested and accompanied. And I no longer want the fever. I want the love. And love, I discovered late, is calm. The fever is what makes noise. And what makes noise is not the strongest; it is only the loudest.

And at night, lying down, on the eve of a calm life with Brando, Tranquilina felt a thing she had never felt, and that was the exact opposite of the girl who provoked storms so as not to be in the desert: she felt peace, and was not afraid of it. She did not confuse it with the desert, did not feel the itch to break it, did not await the crash; she rested in the peace, let herself be in it, and fell asleep calm, in the calm, without needing a storm, and it was a sleep different from all those of a life, because it was the sleep of one who had learned, at last, to rest from being loved, and not to fight to be, the sleep of one who had cut the chain of the storm and lay down, for the first time, on a night without it, in a peace that was no longer the desert, it was, at last, the house without a storm that she was going, from then on, with Brando, to learn to inhabit.
