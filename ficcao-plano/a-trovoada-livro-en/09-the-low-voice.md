## 9. The low voice

As Tranquilina learned to cross the itch without following it, a thing happened she did not expect: she began to hear Brando's love, which had always been there, and which she, deafened by the crashes, had never heard. She began to hear the low voice.

Because Brando's love spoke low, it had always spoken low, and Tranquilina, raised in the crash, had never heard it, in the way one who lived beside a waterfall stops hearing the low voices. And now that the noise of her own storm dropped, that she stopped making crashing, Tranquilina began to hear what was beneath the crash, the low voice of Brando's love, and it was a voice that said much, that said, every day, in small gestures, I like you, I am here, you can rest, and Tranquilina, who had spent months finding that love too little, for making no noise, began to hear it, and in hearing it discovered that it was not too little, it was much, it was more than all the shouted loves she had had, only it was a much that did not shout, a calm much, and that to hear it one had to make silence within, to quiet one's own storm, and it was that Tranquilina went doing, and the more she quieted the storm, the more she heard the love, and the more she heard the love, the less she needed the storm.

And she went relearning to read Brando's gestures, which before tasted to her of too little and which now, read in the right language, that of peace, tasted to her of much. The Brando who always came when he said he would come, whom she before found dull for not leaving her in the distress of waiting, was now, she perceived, a man to be trusted, a man one could rest on, and to rest on a man, she discovered, was better than distress over him, it was adult love and not fever. The Brando who had no jealousy, whom she before thought did not love her enough, was now, she perceived, a man who trusted, who left her free, who loved her without wanting to bind her, and to be loved without being bound, she discovered, was better than to be loved with jealousy, it was love that respects and not love that possesses. And each gesture of Brando, reread in the language of peace, revealed itself to Tranquilina as what it had always been and she had never seen: a love greater, deeper, more to be trusted, than all the storms she had confused with love.

And she began to let herself be loved in peace, which was a new thing for her, who only knew how to be loved in storm. She let herself be cared for by Brando without provoking; she let herself be with him on a calm night without fleeing; she let herself receive his I-like-you without finding it too little; and each time she let herself be loved in peace, without blowing it up, she felt a thing she had never felt and that was the opposite of the desert: she felt safe. She felt, in Brando's calm love, a safety the parents' house had never given her, the safety of one who is loved without having to fight for it, without having to provoke to confirm it, without having to live on alert awaiting the next crash; and that safety, which she before would have called boredom, was after all what she most needed, what had been wanting her whole life, the rest of being loved in peace.

There was a night, calm, when this was seen in a way that moved her. The two of them were there, doing nothing special, in silence, a silence that before would have put Tranquilina on alert, awaiting the desert; and Tranquilina, instead of the alert, instead of the itch, felt, in that silence, a thing she recognised, at last, as what it was: she felt peace. She felt well, calm, safe, beside Brando, in the silence, without needing to break it, without needing crashing, and perceived, with a gladness that almost made her weep, that she was feeling the peace, the good one, that of love without a wound, and that it was not desert, it was the opposite of the desert, it was the fullest place she had ever been, full of a calm love that made no noise and that was, for that, deeper than any crash. And she said to Brando, low, in the silence: I am well, Brando. I am really well. Without a storm. And it is the first time. And Brando, gentle, pressed her hand, and said nothing, because it was not needed, and the silence between the two, which before would be the desert, was now the fullest thing in the world, two lovers in peace, silent, resting in each other, without crashing, without fever, without storm, only love, in a low voice, which is the voice in which in-earnest love, when it is love and not fever, always speaks.

And Tranquilina perceived, that night, that she was curing herself, not because she no longer had the itch of the storm, for that she would have still, more spaced out, weaker, but she would have it, because the illness is not cured entirely, only weakened; but she was curing herself because she now knew how to hear the low voice, now knew how to tell the peace from the desert, now knew how to rest in the calm love without blowing it up, and that this, to know how to rest in the peace, was the cure, was what had been wanting her since a night of thunderstorm on which she was born in a house of crashes, and was, now, at the end of a life of confusing love with noise, the simplest and the hardest thing she had learned: that love, when it is in earnest, does not need to make noise, and that peace, when it is love, is not the desert, it is the place where, at last, one can rest.
