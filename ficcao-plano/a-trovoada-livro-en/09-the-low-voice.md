## 9. The low voice

As Tranquilina learned to cross the itch without following it, a thing happened she did not expect: she began to hear Brando's love, which had always been there, and which she, deafened by the crashes, had never heard. She began to hear the low voice.

Because Brando's love spoke low, it had always spoken low, and Tranquilina, raised in the crash, had never heard it, in the way one who lived beside a waterfall stops hearing the low voices. And now that the noise of her own storm dropped, that she stopped making crashing, Tranquilina began to hear what was beneath the crash, the low voice of Brando's love, and it was a voice that said much, that said, every day, in small gestures, I like you, I am here, you can rest.

And she went on relearning to read Brando's gestures, which before tasted to her of too little and which now, read in the right language, that of peace, tasted to her of much. There was a morning, for example, when she woke at his house, and he had already got up, and she heard him in the kitchen making the least noise possible, setting down the kettle with care, moving on tiptoe so as not to wake her; and before Tranquilina would not even have noticed, or would have found it too little, a man who makes coffee is not a declaration; but that morning, lying down, hearing a man walking on tiptoe through his own house to let her sleep, Tranquilina perceived that she was hearing, live, the low voice of love, and that that, a man making himself silent for her, was worth more than all the men who had made noise for her, because those who made noise made it for themselves, and that one made silence for her.

The Brando who always came when he said he would come, whom she before found dull for not leaving her in the distress of waiting, was now, she perceived, a man to be trusted, a man one could rest on, and to rest on a man, she discovered, was better than distress over him, it was adult love and not fever. The Brando who had no jealousy, whom she before thought did not love her enough, was now, she perceived, a man who trusted, who left her free, who loved her without wanting to bind her; and to be loved without being bound, she discovered, was better than to be loved with jealousy, it was love that respects and not love that possesses. And each gesture of Brando, reread in the language of peace, revealed itself to Tranquilina as what it had always been and she had never seen: a love greater, deeper, more to be trusted, than all the storms she had confused with love.

And there was a Sunday, the same as the one of the river at the start of the book, that measured the road she had travelled. They went back to the same gentle spot, to the same low, clear river, spread the same cloth in the same shade, and Brando, lying on his back with his hat over his face, said, as he had said months before, this is what living is, Tranquilina, a Sunday like this, with nothing more. And Tranquilina, that time, instead of the anguish, instead of the wish to set fire to the perfect afternoon, felt the perfect afternoon be perfect, and did not want to set fire to it, and lay down beside him, and stayed listening to the gentle river running over the stones, and thought, with an astonishment that almost made her laugh, that it was the same river of the night she was born, the same river that had then risen and nearly carried off the bridge, and that now ran low and clear and gave her rest; and that she had been the two things, the flood and the bed, and that she was, at last, learning to be bed. And she said to Brando, low, it is, it is living; and it was the first time she said it truly, and he did not answer, because he was half asleep, and the not-answering, that time, did not taste to her of desert, it tasted to her of trust, of a man who can fall asleep beside her because he is at peace, and Tranquilina let him sleep, and watched over his sleep in the shade, and it was the first time in her life she kept someone's rest instead of wanting to break it.

And she began to let herself be loved in peace, which was a new thing for her, who only knew how to be loved in storm. She let herself be cared for by Brando without provoking; she let herself be with him on a calm night without fleeing; she let herself receive his I-like-you without finding it too little; and each time she let herself be loved in peace, without blowing it up, she felt a thing she had never felt and that was the opposite of the desert: she felt safe. She felt, in Brando's calm love, a safety the parents' house had never given her, the safety of one who is loved without having to fight for it, without having to provoke to confirm it, without having to live on alert awaiting the next crash; and that safety, which she before would have called boredom, was after all what she most needed, what had been lacking her whole life, the rest of being loved in peace.

And she learned, she too, to speak the low voice, which is a thing one learns slowly when one grew up shouting. Because Tranquilina, raised in the storm, only knew how to say love loud, in drama, and Brando's calm love taught her, little by little, to say it low. There was a small gesture that was the first time she spoke his language. Brando had a pocket watch that he always carried with him, inherited from his father, and that had been stopped for years, and that he never mended, he who mended the clocks of the whole village, because, he said, there are things one does not mend in oneself, and he left it stopped at the hour his father had died, like aunt Custódia, after all, without knowing it. And Tranquilina, one afternoon, without saying anything to anyone, took that watch secretly to a clockmaker in the big village, and asked him to set it going, and paid for it out of her own, and brought it back, and put it in Brando's hand one night, without ceremony, without drama, in his way, and said only, listen, it's going again; it's time it went, Brando. And Brando was left with his father's watch ticking in his hand, and his eyes filled, he who never wept, and he said nothing, because it was not needed, and Tranquilina perceived that she had just said I love you in his language, in the low voice, without noise, in a small and sure gesture, and that he had heard her full on, better than he had ever heard anyone's shouted I-love-yous, because she had set going for him the time he had let stop, and that, in a man who lives by clocks, was the greatest declaration of love there was.

There was an afternoon when Felismina saw her, and said a thing to her that stayed. It was at the washing place, the same washing place where the old woman had told her months before to take hold of the clockmaker with both hands, and Felismina looked at her, and narrowed her eyes, in that way of old women who see what does not yet have a name.

"You're different, child," she said. "You're more settled. I knew you your whole life with that fire of yours, always in a back-and-forth, like your mother. And now you're like the river on the good days." And she lowered her voice, and said the thing: "Your mother never got here, you know? She died without ever having a single settled day. You've got here. Don't throw it away."

And Tranquilina took that home, you've got where your mother didn't, and it was a sweet weight, because she perceived that her cure was not only hers, it was also Aurora's, it was to redeem, in herself, the settled day the mother never had.

And there was a night, calm, when the low voice was heard in a way that moved her. The two of them were at her house, and Brando had brought a wall clock he was mending for a customer, and he set it going there in the kitchen while he worked on something else, and the clock made its slow ticking, and they did not speak, each at their own thing, in a silence that before would have put Tranquilina on alert, awaiting the desert. And Tranquilina, instead of the alert, instead of the itch, felt, in that silence with the clock's ticking underneath, a thing she recognised, at last, as what it was: she felt peace. She felt well, calm, safe, beside Brando, in the silence, without needing to break it, without needing crashing. And she said to Brando, low:

"I'm well, Brando. I'm really well. Without a storm. And it's the first time I'm in silence with you and have no fear of it."

And Brando, mild, raised his eyes from what he was doing, and looked at her, and pressed her hand, and said nothing, because it was not needed, and went back to the work; and the silence between the two, which before would be the desert, was now the fullest thing in the world, two lovers in peace, silent, resting in each other, with the low ticking of a clock that was going again, without crashing, without fever, without storm, only love, in a low voice, which is the voice in which in-earnest love, when it is love and not fever, always speaks.

And it was in those days that Tranquilina caught herself doing a thing she had never done: not putting noise in the house when she was alone. She noticed, one afternoon, that she was at home alone, sewing, and that she had not turned on the radio, and that she was not singing, and that she was not searching for any noise to cover the silence; she was only, in silence, sewing, and she was well. And she remembered the night she had discovered, with the jar of shards in her hand, that she was afraid of the silence in herself, that she always covered the silence with noise out of fear that it was the desert; and she perceived that now no, that the silence of the empty house no longer tasted to her of desert, it tasted to her of rest, and that she could be alone and silent without distress, which was a new thing, because one who learns to be at peace with oneself learns to be at peace with the other, and one who is afraid of one's own silence will be afraid of the silence of two. And it was so, from the silence she stopped covering at home to the silence she stopped breaking with Brando, that Tranquilina went on measuring the road, and perceiving that the cure was not only of the love, it was of the whole of her, it was to learn to bear the rest everywhere, within and without, alone and accompanied.

And Tranquilina perceived, that night, that she was curing herself, not because she no longer had the itch of the storm, for that she would have still, more spaced out, weaker, but she would have it, because the sickness is not cured entirely, only weakened; but she was curing herself because she now knew how to hear the low voice, now knew how to tell the peace from the desert, now knew how to rest in the calm love without blowing it up, and that this, to know how to rest in the peace, was the cure, was what had been lacking her since a night of thunderstorm on which she was born in a house of crashes, and was, now, at the end of a life of confusing love with noise, the simplest and the hardest thing she had learned: that love, when it is in earnest, does not need to make noise, and that peace, when it is love, is not the desert, it is the place where, at last, one can rest.
