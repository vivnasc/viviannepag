## 1. The peace that frightens

Tranquilina had a courtship the whole village envied her, and it was that courtship, precisely, that was making her ill, because it was a courtship without a storm, and Tranquilina did not know how to live without a storm.

Brando was a gentle man, and the name said it. He was calm, he was steady, he was good; he loved Tranquilina in a tranquil and deep way, without dramas, without jealousy, without those storms Tranquilina had known in her old loves; he treated her well, spoke to her low, never raised his voice to her, never left her waiting, always appeared when he said he would appear, and loved her with a serenity that was, for any sensible woman, the dream, the harbour, the rest. And Tranquilina, who had no sense in these things, or who had a crooked sense, built in a crooked house, instead of resting in that serene love, went about with it anxious, restless, feeling that something was wanting, without knowing what, and the thing that was wanting, she would discover through this book, was the storm.

Because love, for Tranquilina, had a shape, and the shape was that of the thunderstorm. The loves she had had, as a girl, and she had had them intense, had all been so: quarrels and reconciliations, shouts and kisses, partings in the middle of the night and returns at dawn, jealousy, drama, reconciliations that were worth all the quarrels, and Tranquilina had lived them as one lives a fever, spending herself, suffering, but feeling herself, in each crash, intensely alive, intensely loved, because for her the crash was the proof: if he shouted, it was that he cared; if there was drama, it was that there was love; if he left her and came back, it was that he could not live without her. Intensity was the measure of love, and the more it hurt, the more it was worth, and Tranquilina had come out of those loves shattered and, at bottom, with a kind of longing, the longing for the fever, the longing to feel as alive as only the storm made her feel.

And now she had Brando, and Brando gave no storm at all, and Tranquilina, instead of thanking heaven, was wasting away of calm. She had been with him some months, and the months had been good, gentle, without a single drama, and it was precisely that which frightened her: there was no drama. Brando had no jealousy, and Tranquilina, instead of resting, asked herself whether he loved her enough, because in her head, built in her house, whoever loves is jealous, and whoever is not jealous does not love, and Brando's serenity, which was sure love, seemed to her, by the crooked light of her learning, a lack of love. Brando did not leave her waiting, he always appeared, and Tranquilina, instead of liking it, missed the distress of waiting, the heart leaping asking whether he would come, because that distress, in her house, had been the taste of love, and without it love seemed to her tasteless, lukewarm, like food without salt.

There was one night, at the start of the book, that showed it to her in a way that frightened her. The two of them were talking, at the door, on a calm night, and it was a good night, of the kind anyone would want, Brando speaking to her low of the two of theirs' plans, with that serene tenderness, and Tranquilina, instead of being well, felt rising in her a restlessness she did not know what it was, an itch, a wish to break that calm, to say a thing that would provoke, to find a cause for a quarrel, and she caught herself, with horror, seeking in Brando's talk a thing to take offence at, twisting a sentence of his so as to be able to hold it against him, trying, without knowing why, to light a spark in that too-calm night. And Brando, gentle, did not bite, did not get angry, answered with the gentleness of always, and the spark went out, and Tranquilina was left with the restlessness unsatisfied, and went home that night feeling ill, without understanding why on earth she felt ill on so good a night, with so good a man, in a love the whole village envied her.

Because this is what Tranquilina could not understand about herself: that she felt ill in the good. That a calm night with Brando left her more restless than a night of quarrel had left her, as a girl, with the tempestuous loves. That peace, instead of soothing her, put her on alert, as if calm were a threat, as if the quiet hid a danger, as if, in the storm-less silence, she awaited at any moment the crash, and the crash did not come, and the waiting for the crash that did not come was worse than the crash itself. Tranquilina did not know how to rest in peace; she only knew how to rest after the storm, in the truce, in the interval between two crashes, and a love that had no crashes had, for her, no truces, because a truce is the rest of war, and where there is no war there is no truce, there is only a continuous peace that Tranquilina, raised in war, did not know how to inhabit.

The village, which saw from outside, did not understand. Her friends said to her, Tranquilina, you don't know the luck you have, a man like that, calm, good, who treats you like a queen, hold on to him, marry him, for men like that don't exist; and Tranquilina said yes, that she knew, that she was going to hold on to him, and inside she thought but something is wanting, and told no one what was wanting, because she was ashamed, because she knew, at bottom, that what was wanting was a wrong thing, that the storm was wanting, and that a woman who misses the storm in a serene love is a woman with something crooked inside, and Tranquilina felt the crookedness, and was ashamed of it, and did not know how to set it straight.

And the worst, what frightened her most, was a thing she was beginning to perceive and did not wish to perceive: that she was going to blow up that love. That if that restlessness went on, if that wish to provoke a storm went on growing, she was going, sooner or later, to make the storm, was going to provoke a real quarrel, was going to make Brando lose his calm, or was going herself to do a thing that would blow it up, only to feel, in the crash, that she was alive, that she was loved, that this was an in-earnest love and not a lukewarm calm; and that in doing it she was going to destroy, with her own hands, the first good love life had given her, and was going to destroy it precisely for being good, for being calm, for giving no storm, and that this, to blow up the good for being good, was a thing so absurd, so against herself, that Tranquilina, glimpsing it, was afraid of herself, of what she was capable of doing to Brando, for nothing, only for not knowing how to live in peace.

That night, lying down, Tranquilina did a thing she did not do: she tried to understand herself. Instead of pushing the restlessness away, as she always pushed it, she stayed with it, and asked herself, in the dark, why on earth the calm frightened her, why on earth she, who had the dream of any woman, a man who loved her in peace, was wasting away of peace and dreaming of a storm. And she did not know how to answer, that first night, because the answer came from far off, from the house, from the storm in which she was born, from the shouts and the reconciliations of her parents, and Tranquilina had not yet gone down to that depth; but she stayed with the question, for the first time, instead of covering it, and fell asleep with it, and it was a restless sleep, the sleep of one who senses she is going to have to look at a thing she has avoided for thirty-one years, which is the place where she learned, before she could speak, that love is a thunderstorm, and that peace, the silent kind, Brando's kind, is the silence that came, in her house, after the storms, and that she learned to fear more than the crashes themselves, because it was the silence in which the parents, worn out from shouting, did not speak, and the house became a desert, and the girl, in the desert of the silence, felt more alone and more unloved than in the midst of the worst shouts.
