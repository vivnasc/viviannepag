## 5. The storm in person

It was in the midst of the days when Tranquilina was beginning, with effort, to wish to cure herself, that life played on her the trick it always plays on those who are trying to change: it set before her, in person, the sickness she wished to let go. Back to the village came Bravo — the fierce one — the storm-love of her youth, the tempest made man.

Bravo had been, years before, Tranquilina's most intense love, the one who had best given her the fever she called love: a man of fire, of jealousy, of partings and returns, of nights of quarrel and dawns of passion, a man who had made her suffer like no other and feel herself alive like no other, and who had gone away from the village, years before, at the end of one of their storms, without taking his leave, leaving her only a message by the mouth of another person, leaving Tranquilina shattered and, deep down, with the longing one has for the fevers that nearly kill us.

And now he came back, without warning. Tranquilina saw him at the door of the café of the old station, leaning against the jamb with that air of his, smoking, watching the village pass as one who appraises it, and it was enough for her to see him to feel her whole body respond, her heart fire off, the old fever wake, and to perceive, with a mixture of terror and of desire, that Bravo still did to her what Brando never had: he gave her the crash, without saying a word, only by appearing. And Bravo, who knew the effect he had on her, crossed the street and came straight to her, and was what he had always been, intense, hot, full of drama.

"Tranquilina," he said, and he said it like one who bites the name. "I knew I'd find you the very first day. You and I have this, there's always a current pulling us one to the other. You know there is." And he lowered his voice, and said the thing that was the mother tongue of her heart: "I never forgot you. These years away were a desert. I came back for you."

And Tranquilina heard him, and it was to her like hearing known music after months of hearing a foreign language, that of Brando's calm love; Bravo spoke her language, that of the storm, and she understood it all, felt it all, and for some instants, dangerous, intoxicating, she almost forgot Brando, almost threw herself again at the fever, because Bravo's fever was what she knew, was what tasted to her of love, and Brando's calm love, beside that fever, seemed to her, again, lukewarm, too little, without fire. And she agreed to take a coffee with him, there, at the outside table, in sight of Brando's shop, which was three doors away, and it was a thing she knew, on sitting down, that she should not have done.

And it was there, in the temptation of Bravo, at the café table, that Tranquilina had the proof of all she was learning, and had it in her body, which is where the proofs count. Because with Bravo she felt the crash, yes, the fever, yes, but she began to notice, now that she was trying to cure herself, things she had not seen before. She noticed that he talked a great deal and asked nothing; that he told half an hour of himself, of the rounds he had made, of the women he had left, of the men he had quarrelled with, and not once asked her how her life had been, what she did, whether she was happy. She noticed that, halfway through, a young woman passed in the street and Bravo followed her with his eyes and made a remark, there, in front of her, and right after took her hand across the table with a possessive intensity, like one who marks what is his. She noticed that at the end of that half-hour she came out tired, sucked dry, on alert, exactly as she came out, as a small girl, of the storms of the Ladeira; that Bravo made her feel alive but did not make her feel at peace, that it was all emotion and no rest, all crash and no truce. And she noticed, above all, a thing that shook her: that beneath the fever, the passion, the drama, Bravo was a man who hurt her, who played with her, who made her suffer, and that she, before, had read that suffering as love, had thought the suffering the proof of the loving, when the suffering was only suffering, and the love to be trusted, the one that did not hurt, was Brando's, the calm one, the one she found too little.

And there happened, in those days, a small thing that put the two men side by side for Tranquilina to see, and that she had never had thus, the comparison before her eyes. She was coming from the café with Bravo, one afternoon, and Bravo, over a thing of nothing, over her having greeted a man in the street, made her a scene right there, gripped her arm hard, with who's that, where do you know him from, and there was jealousy and there was too much force in his fingers, and Tranquilina felt the old fear, that fear she had learned, as a girl, to call passion. And that same afternoon, later, she passed by Brando's shop, and told him, without thinking, that she had met an old acquaintance in the street; and Brando, instead of jealousy, instead of who's that, asked only, with genuine interest, and were you glad to see him? And it was the difference between the two men said in a sentence: Bravo asked where do you know him from to control her; Brando asked were you glad to know her. One wanted to know in order to bind her; the other wanted to know in order to keep her company. And Tranquilina, that night, laid the comparison before her and looked at it a long time, and perceived that her whole life she had read the gripping-of-the-arm as love and the were-you-glad as indifference, and that she had it all backwards, that the gripping was sickness and the asking was love, and that she, raised in the storm, had her thermometer broken, marked fever where there was health and marked health where there was fever.

And there was a moment, days later, that was the hinge. Bravo learned, because in the village everything is learned, that she was courting the clockmaker, and sought her out at the washing place, and made her a scene of jealousy there on the path, a scene of fire.

"The clockmaker?" he said, and he laughed, but laughing badly. "You, with that lukewarm man, who counts seconds the whole day long? You, who are fire, with a man who's never going to make you boil? You're mine, Tranquilina, you always were, and I won't leave you with that man who doesn't even know what he has in his hands. Drop him. Come with me. I'm the one who loves you in earnest."

And before, Tranquilina would have read that jealousy as love, would have melted, because he cares so much that he's jealous, he won't leave me, he fights for me. But that day, with her eyes a little more open, Tranquilina heard that you're mine and saw Bravo's jealousy for what it was: not love, but possession; not care, but control; a man who wanted her not for her own good, but for himself, and who confused, like her, the crash with the love. And she compared, without wishing it, with Brando, who had never been jealous, who left her free, who had never told her you're mine, who said I like you, which is a different thing from you're mine; and she perceived, suddenly, that what she had read in Brando as a lack of love, the absence of jealousy, was after all the highest form of love, the love that trusts, that does not bind, that wants the good of the other and not the possession of them, and that Bravo's jealousy, which she had read as proof of love, was after all the proof of a sick love, equal to hers, a love that needs the storm because it does not know how to love in peace.

And she remembered, with new eyes, an old night with Bravo, from that time, a night she had kept her whole life as the proof of the great love they had had, and that now, reread, told her another story. They had quarrelled, one of the thousand times, and Bravo had left her house at dawn, and she had stayed at the window weeping, exactly like the mother, and at four in the morning he had come back, soaked, throwing little stones at the glass, and had come up, and they had made peace with an intensity that left her trembling. And Tranquilina had told that night to half the world, for years on end, with a shine in her eyes, see what in-earnest love is, he came back at dawn in the rain for me. And only now, at the café table, with Bravo before her no longer enchanting her, did she see the whole night: she saw that he had made her weep until four in the morning before coming back; that the glorious return had only been glorious because the parting had been cruel; that she had spent five hours in suffering to have ten minutes of ecstasy, and called that love, and the man who never made her weep so as then to console her, Brando, she had called lukewarm. And she perceived, with a cold shudder, that she had spent her life admiring arsonists for putting out the fires they themselves lit, and finding too little the man who simply set fire to nothing.

It was not with Bravo. There was an instant when she was on the edge, when the fever nearly took her, when she nearly blew up everything with Brando to throw herself at the known storm; but what she had learned, with effort, held her. She remembered Brando's sadness, the you-need-storms-and-I-do-not-give-them; she remembered the parents' house, the lukewarm silence she called desert; she remembered Felismina at the cemetery gate, those two died of longing for each other, and while still alive; she remembered the exhaustion Bravo gave her, and the rest Brando gave her and that she did not know how to receive. And she perceived that to throw herself at Bravo was to choose the sickness, was to choose the house, was to choose the fever that was going to kill her as it had nearly killed her, and that to stay with Brando, and learn to love him in peace, was to choose the cure, however much the cure, at first, tasted to her of lukewarm, because everything that cures a fever tastes lukewarm to one who has the fever.

And there was, before the no, a night when she nearly said the yes, and it must be told, because the cure is not made of one who was never on the edge, it is made of one who was on the edge and came back. Bravo had set her a meeting at the café door, at night, and Tranquilina went, who knows with what intention, perhaps not even she knew, with her heart whirling, with the old fever calling her and the new reason pulling her back. And on the way, going down the lower street, she passed before Brando's shop, closed, dark, and stopped. And she saw, through the window, by the street light, all the clocks inside, silent, stopped in the darkness, waiting for the morning for the mild man to set them going; and she saw, at the back, the bench where he bent over his work, and the stool where she had so many times sat watching him work; and she felt, before that dark window, a thing that Bravo's fever had never given her: she felt home. She felt that that calm shop, with the clocks waiting and the worn bench, was the place where she wanted to be, and that the café where Bravo waited for her was a place of passing, of crashing, of smoke; and she stood before Brando's window a long time, and then turned about, and did not go to the café, and did not go to Bravo that night, and went home, and it was the first time in her life she chose a dark, silent window against a café door full of fire, and she perceived, on lying down, that she had begun to cure herself on the night when a closed shop of clocks tasted to her more of love than a man who waited for her.

She told Bravo no. She told him so right there, on the washing-place path, with a firmness she herself did not expect, and that surprised her:

"You were the most intense love of my life, Bravo. That was true. But the most intense was not the best, and it took me thirty years to perceive that. You did not love me. You wanted me. It is different. You never once asked me, in all these days, whether I was happy. Brando asks me every day. You call me yours. He calls me Tranquilina. I no longer want fever. I want love. And the love, the kind to be trusted, the kind that does not leave me exhausted, I have it with him, in the calm I learned to fear and am, at last, learning to love."

And Bravo, who was not used to a no, made the usual storm: he shouted, there on the path, in front of those who passed; he threatened; he said she would regret it, that she would go back to the lukewarm and die of boredom, that a year from now she would come knocking at his door on her knees. And Tranquilina, hearing Bravo's storm, instead of melting, instead of feeling the fever, felt a new thing: she felt weariness. She felt that that, the crash, the drama, was wearying, was poor, was always the same, the same thunder for the same reasons, and that she now knew it by heart, and no longer wanted it. And she turned her back on him, and left Bravo to make his storm alone in the middle of the path, and went down the street, and passed before the fountain of the square, and stopped, and breathed, and felt, for the first time, that to say no to the storm was not to lose love, it was to choose it, it was to choose Brando's calm love against Bravo's fever, it was the first step, painful and enormous, of the cure of a sickness she had carried since a night of thunderstorm on which she was born, in a house where love and shouting always came in together.
