## 5. The storm in person

It was in the midst of the days when Tranquilina was beginning, with effort, to wish to cure herself, that life played on her the trick it always plays on those who are trying to change: it set before her, in person, the illness she wished to let go. Back to the village came Bravo, the storm-love of her youth, the tempest made man.

Bravo had been, years before, Tranquilina's most intense love, the one who had best given her the fever she called love: a man of fire, of jealousy, of partings and returns, of nights of quarrel and dawns of passion, a man who had made her suffer like no other and feel herself alive like no other, and who had gone away from the village, years before, at the end of one of their storms, leaving Tranquilina shattered and, at bottom, with the longing one has for the fevers that nearly kill us. And now he came back, without warning, and it was enough for Tranquilina to see him, at the café door, with that air of his, to feel her whole body respond, her heart race, the old fever wake, and to perceive, with a mixture of terror and of desire, that Bravo still did to her what Brando never had: he gave her the crash, without saying a word, only by appearing.

And Bravo, who knew the effect he had on her, drew near, and was what he had always been, intense, hot, full of drama, told her he had never forgotten her, that he had come back for her, that the years away had been a desert, and he said it with that tempestuous passion that was the mother tongue of Tranquilina's heart, and she heard him, and it was like hearing known music after months of hearing a foreign language, that of Brando's calm love; Bravo spoke her language, that of the storm, and Tranquilina understood it all, felt it all, and for some instants, dangerous, intoxicating, she almost forgot Brando, almost threw herself again at the fever, because Bravo's fever was what she knew, was what tasted to her of love, and Brando's calm love, beside that fever, seemed to her, again, lukewarm, too little, without fire.

And it was there, in the temptation of Bravo, that Tranquilina had the proof of all she was learning, and had it in her body, which is where the proofs count. Because with Bravo she felt the crash, yes, the fever, yes, but she began to notice, now that she was trying to cure herself, things she had not seen before: she noticed that Bravo's fever left her exhausted, that at the end of a conversation with him she came out tired, sucked dry, on alert, as she came out, as a girl, from the storms of the house; she noticed that Bravo made her feel alive but did not make her feel at peace, that it was all emotion and no rest, all crash and no truce; and she noticed, above all, a thing that shook her: that Bravo did not treat her well. That beneath the fever, the passion, the drama, Bravo was a man who hurt her, who played with her, who made her suffer, and that she, before, had read that suffering as love, had thought the suffering the proof of the loving, when the suffering was only suffering, and the love to be trusted, the one that did not hurt, was Brando's, the calm one, the one she found too little.

And there was a moment, with Bravo, that was the hinge. Bravo, in his intensity, made her a scene of jealousy, there, over Brando, a scene of fire, of you are mine, I do not leave you with that lukewarm man, and before Tranquilina would have read that jealousy as love, would have melted, because he cares so much that he is jealous; but that day, with her eyes a little more open, Tranquilina saw Bravo's jealousy for what it was: not love, but possession, not care, but control, a man who wanted her not for her own good, but for himself, and who confused, like her, the crash with the love. And she compared, without wishing it, with Brando, who had never been jealous, who left her free, who loved her without wanting to bind her, and perceived, suddenly, that what she had read in Brando as a lack of love, the absence of jealousy, was after all the highest form of love, the love that trusts, that does not bind, that wants the good of the other and not the possession of them, and that Bravo's jealousy, which she had read as proof of love, was after all the proof of a sick love, equal to hers, a love that needs the storm because it does not know how to love in peace.

She did not go with Bravo. There was an instant when she was on the edge, when the fever nearly took her, when she nearly blew up everything with Brando to throw herself at the known storm; but what she had learned, with effort, held her. She remembered Brando's sadness, the you-need-storms-and-I-do-not-give-them; she remembered the parents' house, the lukewarm silence she called desert and that after all was not the calm love; she remembered the exhaustion Bravo gave her, and the rest Brando gave her and that she did not know how to receive; and she perceived that to throw herself at Bravo was to choose the illness, was to choose the house, was to choose the fever that was going to kill her as it had nearly killed her, and that to stay with Brando, and learn to love him in peace, was to choose the cure, however much the cure, at first, tasted to her of lukewarm, because everything that cures a fever tastes lukewarm to one who has the fever.

She told Bravo no. She told him so with a firmness she herself did not expect, and that surprised her: she told him he had been the most intense love of her life, and that it was true, but that the most intense was not the best, that she had learned, late, that intensity is not love, it is fever, and that she no longer wanted fever, she wanted love, and that love, the kind to be trusted, the kind that did not leave her exhausted, the kind that treated her well, she had with Brando, in the calm she had learned to fear and was, at last, learning to love. And Bravo, who was not used to a no, made the usual storm, shouted, threatened, said she would regret it, that she would go back to the lukewarm and die of boredom; and Tranquilina, hearing Bravo's storm, instead of melting, instead of feeling the fever, felt a new thing: she felt weariness, felt that that, the crash, the drama, was wearying, was poor, was always the same, and that she no longer wanted it, and she went off, leaving Bravo to make his storm alone, and went down the street, and breathed, and felt, for the first time, that to say no to the storm was not to lose love, it was to choose it, it was to choose Brando's calm love against Bravo's fever, it was the first step, painful and enormous, of the cure of an illness she had carried since a night of thunderstorm on which she was born, in a house where love and shouting always came in together.
