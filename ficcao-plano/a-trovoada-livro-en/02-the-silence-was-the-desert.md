## 2. The silence was the desert

Tranquilina learned what love was in a house where love and shouting came in together, and it was for that she, her whole life, confused them: she heard them always at the same time, and in a child's head what comes together stays together forever.

Tranquilina's parents loved each other in a tempestuous way, and they loved each other truly, and that was the confusion, because it was not a false love, it was an in-earnest love made of storms, and the girl, watching, learned that it was so that in-earnest love was made. They quarrelled over everything and nothing, shouting, with doors slamming, with plates breaking sometimes, with the father leaving the house in the middle of the quarrel and the mother weeping at the window; and then, the storm past, they reconciled with the same intensity, with embraces, with tears of joy, with an enormous tenderness that seemed to be worth all the quarrels, and the days after a great quarrel were the sweetest days of the house, the ones of most love, of most laughter, as if the storm cleansed the air and left, after, a warmer sun.

And the girl Tranquilina learned, in that to-and-fro, a lesson that governed her life: that love is this, it is the storm and the peace that comes after, and that the peace only tastes good after the storm, and that without storm there is not the good peace, there is only the other silence, the bad one. Because there were, in the house, two silences, and the girl learned to tell them apart as one tells the sky of good weather from that of bad: there was the sweet silence, that of the reconciliations, that of after the quarrels, in which the parents spoke to each other low and touched each other and the house was a nest; and there was the bad silence, that of the lukewarm days, in which the parents, worn out from so much storm, did not quarrel but also did not love each other, went about the house each to their own side, without shouts but without tenderness, and that silence, the lukewarm, the one without storm and without peace, was, for the girl, the worst of all, was the desert, was the place where she felt most alone, because it was the place where there was no love at all, neither that of the storm nor that of the peace, only the absence, only the emptiness, only two tired adults who no longer had the strength to love each other or to quarrel.

And it was that, the bad silence, that of the desert, that stayed in Tranquilina as the greatest fear, greater than the fear of the shouts. Because the shouts, however bad they were, were love, were proof that the parents cared, that there was fire, that the house was alive; and the lukewarm silence was the opposite, was the proof that love was over, that the house had died, that it was no longer worth even shouting, and the girl, on the days of lukewarm silence, went about the house anxious awaiting a storm, and when the storm came, when the parents went back to shouting, she felt, strange as it seems, a relief, because the shout was better than the desert, the shout said there is still love here, and the desert said there is no longer.

And she learned, without knowing she was learning, to provoke the storm when the desert lasted too long. As a small girl, on the days of lukewarm silence, she did some mischief, broke a thing, said an insolence, anything that would set the parents shouting, at her or at each other, because the shout, even directed at her, even scolding her, was better than the silence, was attention, was proof that she existed, that they cared about her, and the silence was indifference, was the desert, was not existing. And so Tranquilina learned, a girl, the thing that was going to govern her loves her whole life: that when the calm lasts too long, one provokes a storm, because the storm is love and the calm is abandonment, and the crash of love is better than the silence of the desert.

The mother had died some years before, and the father soon after, worn out the two of them, perhaps, of a life of storms, and Tranquilina had been left an orphan with her heart already formed, formed in that house, formed for storm-love, and she had carried that form into her loves, and had always chosen, without knowing why, storm-men, men who gave drama, who gave jealousy, who gave the to-and-fro of the partings and the returns, because those tasted to her of love, gave her the known fever, filled her desert with crashing; and she had always refused, or always found too little, the calm men, the gentle ones, the Brandos, because their calm tasted to her of the lukewarm silence of the house, of the desert, of the absence of love, and she fled the calm as one flees a thing that does harm, without perceiving that she was fleeing not the calm, but the desert of childhood, and that Brando's calm was not the desert, it was another thing, it was the clear sky without needing a storm to clear it, and that she, raised in the storm, did not know how to read the clear sky, confused it with the emptiness.

It was of this that Tranquilina began to remember in those days, after the night she caught herself wishing to provoke Brando, and she did not remember in these words, for she was not one to explain herself, she remembered in images, in the mother weeping at the window, in the father going out into the night, in the sweet days after the quarrels, and above all in the lukewarm silence, in the desert, in the fear of that silence, and there came into her, slowly, an understanding that frightened her: that what she felt with Brando, the restlessness, the something-is-wanting, was the fear of the desert, and that she was confusing Brando's peace with the lukewarm silence of the house, and that for that the peace frightened her, because her body, formed in the storm, read the calm as absence of love, as desert, and wished to provoke the storm to flee a desert that after all was not there, that was in the memory, in the house, in childhood, and that she brought into the serene love of Brando without it being there.

And she perceived, with a shudder, that if she did not tell one thing from the other, Brando's peace from the desert of the house, she was going to do to her love what she did as a child to the silence of the house: she was going to provoke the storm, was going to blow up the calm, was going to destroy the good to flee a desert that existed only in her head, and was going to lose Brando, and was going, after, to miss him, and was going to seek another storm-man to fill her desert with crashing, and was going to repeat, with the faithfulness of those who do not know themselves, the life of the parents, the house of the storms, and was going perhaps to leave it, one day, to a daughter, if she had one, the chain of the storm passing from generation to generation, making of each one a woman who confuses love with noise and peace with abandonment, and who blows up, her whole life, the calm that could give her rest, in search of a storm that gives her the fever she learned to call love.
