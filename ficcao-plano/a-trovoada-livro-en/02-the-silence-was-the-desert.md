## 2. The silence was the desert

Tranquilina learned what love was in a house where love and shouting came in together, and it was for that reason that she, her whole life, confused them: she heard them always at the same time, and in a child's head what comes together stays together forever.

The house was on the Ladeira, halfway up the slope, and it had a kitchen with a window that gave onto the path, and it was to that window that the first memory of Tranquilina stayed fastened: her mother, Aurora — dawn — leaning against the glass, weeping, watching her father go down the path in the rain after a quarrel. Aurora wept in a way that was not of one who loses love, it was of one who loves it too much; she wept and said to the glass, low, he'll come back, he always comes back, and Tranquilina, sitting on the floor behind her, four or five years old, learned at that window the first law of love according to her house: that love is a thing that leaves and comes back, and that between the leaving and the coming back there is the window, and the weeping, and the distress, and the waiting, and that it is the waiting that makes the return worth the while.

The father, Vasco, was a man of easy thunder and of easy sun too, and that was the confusion of everything, because he was not a bad man, he was a man of storm. He came in from the upper fields, red from the labour and the wine, and one word at the table was enough, a word of nothing, for the sky of the kitchen to gather. Aurora was not a woman to bow her head; she answered back, and the tone rose, and Vasco rose higher, and the storm made itself in three minutes, out of nothing, with the same speed with which there makes itself, in summer, over the serra, a storm that in the morning could not have been foretold. Crockery was broken. Tranquilina remembered the sound, which no other sound of her life ever equalled: the sound of a plate cracking against the wall, dry, final, and right after the silence of a second, and right after the mother weeping and the father going out, and the little girl looking at the shards on the floor the way one looks at fallen stars.

And then, and this was the part that lost her forever, came the sweet. The father came back, days later or hours later, with a bunch of flowers from the path or with a paper twist of sweets, head bowed, and Aurora made out she was angry for three minutes and gave way to the bedroom, and the two shut themselves in, and the house filled with a new tenderness, with low laughter, with a newlyweds' gladness that was more intense, far more, than the gladness of the houses that never quarrelled. The days after a great quarrel were the sweetest days of the Ladeira, the ones of most love, of most laughter, as if the storm cleansed the air and left, after, a warmer sun, and Aurora sang in the kitchen, and Vasco carried her from the stove to the table in his arms in play, and Tranquilina, a small girl, in the middle of that sweetness, engraved the lesson that governed her life: that peace only tastes good after the storm, and that without storm there is no good peace.

There was a cup that ended up telling everything. It was a cup of Aurora's, of good china, with a fine little stem, that Vasco had given her when they were courting; it broke in one of the storms, thrown or dropped, that no one any longer knew. And Vasco, in the reconciliation, instead of throwing it out, sat at the table one whole night gluing it, shard by shard, with a joiner's glue, and it ended up a cup with scars, with threads of glue crossing it like rivers, but whole, able to hold the coffee. And Aurora kept it, and used it, and said, with pride, this is the best one I have, because this one has fallen and come back. Tranquilina heard that a thousand times, and came to know, without knowing she came to know it, that a thing that has fallen and come back is worth more than a thing that has never fallen, and she carried that cup within her her whole life, and measured by it, without realising, all her loves: the one that had not fallen and come back, the one that had no glue crossing it, seemed to her new china, without history, without proof of love.

Because there were, in the house, two silences, and the little girl learned to tell them apart as one tells the sky of good weather from that of bad. There was the sweet silence, that of the reconcilings, that of after the quarrels, in which the parents spoke to each other low and touched each other and the house was a nest; and there was the bad silence, that of the lukewarm days, in which the parents, worn out from so much storm, did not quarrel but also did not love each other, went about the house each to their own side, without shouts but without tenderness, and that silence, the lukewarm, the one without storm and without peace, was, for the little girl, the worst of all, was the desert, was the place where she felt most alone, because it was the place where there was no love at all, neither that of the storm nor that of the peace, only the absence, only the emptiness, only two tired adults who no longer had the strength to love each other or to quarrel.

And Felismina saw, from the house next door, because the houses of the Ladeira were stuck one to another and the walls thin, and everything was heard. Felismina told it years afterwards to Tranquilina, already a woman, one afternoon at the washing place, wringing a sheet, without malice, like one who gives back to the daughter a piece of the house she could not see from outside. I used to hear them, child, she would say. I heard your father going out into the night, and I heard your mother weeping against the wall, for the wall of her bedroom was the wall of mine, and sometimes I laid my hand to the wall, on my side, like one who wants to take her hand through the lime, and I said within, hold on, woman, he'll come back, for he always comes back. And he came back. I saw him going up the path in the morning, with the flowers gathered at the path's edge, with that dog's face that begs, and your mother opened the door to him, and for a week I heard them laughing through the wall, and it was a good laughter, child, it was the laughter of people who love each other. And I, on my side, with my man who never gave me a flower nor a shout, who never left and never wept, I, sometimes, God forgive me, envied them. I envied their storm. Only much later did I understand that there was nothing to envy, that that envy was the same sickness that was going to catch you, and that my quiet man, who never gave me a flower, gave me something better, gave me fifty years of peace, and that I was a fool not to know how to see it. And Tranquilina kept this, Felismina's envy of Aurora's storm, because it was her own sickness seen from outside, in the mouth of an old woman who had cured herself of it in time to recognise it.

Tranquilina knew exactly the taste of that silence, because there was a winter when it lasted. It was the winter when the father was ill in the chest and lost the work up above, and stayed at home, and there was no money nor wine nor strength for storms, and the house entered a lukewarmness that lasted months. The parents did not quarrel; worse, they hardly spoke, they gave each other the good-mornings and the pass-me-the-bread and nothing more, and Aurora stopped singing, and Vasco spent the afternoons looking at the hearth, and Tranquilina, who would then have been eight, went about the house as about the house of other people. One afternoon, that winter, she sat on her father's lap, beside the hearth, in search of some thing, of a caress, of a word, and the father let her stay, but did not hold her close, did not say anything, stayed looking at the hearth over her head, and the little girl felt, there, on her own father's lap, a loneliness greater than she felt on the days of the shouting. Because the shouts, at least, were directed; the hearth was not. And it was that afternoon, on the lap of an absent father, that Tranquilina learned what the desert is: it is not to be far from the one you love, it is to be near and not be seen. And she concluded, with the crooked logic of a child, a thing she was going to carry her whole life: that it was better to be shouted at than to be nothing, better the storm than the hearth.

It was that winter that the little girl began to keep the shards, and the first shard had a story that she carried without understanding it. One morning, after one of the rare storms that broke that lukewarm winter, Aurora was sweeping the broken plates onto the dustpan, silent, with a closed face, and the little girl, sitting on the floor, saw a small shard escape the broom and come to rest in a corner, under the dresser, a shard with a little blue flower painted on it. And the little girl waited for the mother to go out, and went on all fours to fetch the shard, and slipped it into her pocket, and could not have said why, and carried it to her room, and hid it in a matchbox. And at night she took it out, and looked at it by the light that came through the door, and felt, in holding the shard, a thing she only much later understood: she felt safe. The shard was the proof that in that house, that morning, someone had cared enough to break a plate; and as long as there were shards, there was love; what frightened her was not the broken plate, it was the mother's silent broom sweeping up the silence. That was why the little girl kept the shards, and never kept the silences, because the shards were the proof that the house was alive, and the silence was the proof that it was dying; and it was so, shard by shard, that there formed in Tranquilina the jar she was going to carry through her life, the jar of the kept storms, the museum of a little girl who was afraid of the quiet.

And she learned, without knowing she was learning, to provoke the storm when the desert lasted too long. That same winter, one night, with the lukewarm silence weighing on the house like damp, the little girl Tranquilina went to the shelf and took down on purpose the glued cup, the parents' cup, and let it fall on the kitchen floor, in front of the two of them, and made out it was carelessness. And the cup, which had already fallen and come back, broke again, and this time for good, and Aurora gave a cry, and Vasco got up, and there made itself, over a cup, the first storm of that whole winter, shouts, tears, the father saying there was no peace in that house, the mother weeping over the cup that was the thing she most had. And the little girl, in the corner, against the wall, in the middle of the crash she herself had made, felt, strange as it may seem, a deep, warm relief, almost a guilty happiness, because the house was alive again, the parents were there again, present, whole, caring about some thing, even if it was about rage, and the desert had been broken, and the shout said there is still someone in this house, and the silence said there is no longer anyone. And Tranquilina, that night, picked up from the floor the shards of the glued cup, on the sly, and put them in the jar with the others, and never told anyone, in thirty-one years, that the last storm of that whole winter had been the work of the hands of a little girl who preferred the storm to the hearth.

So Tranquilina learned, a little girl, the thing that was going to govern her loves her whole life: that when the calm lasts too long, one provokes a storm, because the storm is love and the calm is abandonment, and the crash of love is worth more than the silence of the desert.

There was one reconciliation, of the many, that stayed in Tranquilina as the master-lesson of all she was going to confuse her whole life. The little girl would have been about ten. There had been a great storm, of the kind that lasted days, with Vasco away from the house and Aurora wasting away, and the little girl in the middle, making herself small. And one night the father came back, and Tranquilina, from her bed, heard the door, and heard the low voices, and got up on tiptoe and went to peer through the crack of the kitchen door. And she saw a thing she never forgot: she saw her father on his knees on the kitchen floor, yes, on his knees, before the mother seated, with his head in her lap, weeping, and Aurora passing her hand through his hair, slowly, and the two saying to each other things the little girl did not hear but understood by the tone, and there was in it a tenderness so enormous, so deep, so in earnest, that the little girl, at the crack of the door, felt her heart fill, and thought, with the absolute certainty of children: this is it, love is this. And it never occurred to her, neither that night nor in thirty years, the question that would have saved her: and for there to be this, what had to be before? There had to be the storm, there had to be the father away from the house for days, there had to be the mother wasting away; the kneeling was only so beautiful because it came after the crash. Tranquilina kept the beauty of the kneeling and forgot its price, and went after that beauty her whole life, without perceiving that she was buying whole storms only to have, at the end, the man on his knees, and that a man who never needs to kneel, because he never hurt you, is worth more than all those who kneel after breaking you.

Aurora died first, of a weariness to which the doctors gave another name, and Vasco went after her less than two years later, and Felismina, who buried them both with the village, said the sentence that Tranquilina never forgot, said at the cemetery gate, buttoning her coat: those two did not die of the illness, child. They died of longing for each other, and while still alive. They spent themselves loving each other so, rough, and did not know how to do it any other way. Tranquilina was left an orphan with her heart already formed, formed in that house, formed for storm-love, and she had carried that form into her loves, and had always chosen, without knowing why, storm-men, men who gave drama, who gave jealousy, who gave the to-and-fro of the partings and the returns, because those tasted to her of love, gave her the known fever, filled her desert with crashing; and she had always refused, or always found too little, the calm men, the gentle ones, the Brandos, because their calm tasted to her of the lukewarm silence of the house, of the desert, of the absence of love, and she fled the calm as one flees a thing that does harm, without perceiving that she was fleeing not the calm, but the desert of childhood, and that Brando's calm was not the desert, it was another thing, it was the clear sky without needing a storm to clear it, and that she, raised in the storm, did not know how to read the clear sky, confused it with the emptiness.

It was of this that Tranquilina began to remember in those days, after the night she caught herself wishing to provoke Brando, and she did not remember in these words, for she was not one to explain herself, she remembered in images: in the mother at the window weeping, in the father going down the path, in the glued cup with its rivers of glue, in the hearth her father looked at over her head, in the shards on the floor like fallen stars, and above all in the lukewarm silence, in the desert, in the fear of that silence. And there came into her, slowly, an understanding that frightened her: that what she felt with Brando, the restlessness, the something-is-lacking, was the fear of the desert, and that she was confusing Brando's peace with the lukewarm silence of the house, and that for that reason the peace frightened her, because her body, formed in the storm, read the calm as absence of love, as desert, and wished to provoke the storm to flee the desert that after all was not there, that was in the memory, in the house, in childhood, and that she brought into Brando's serene love without it being there.

And she perceived, with a shudder, that if she did not tell one thing from the other, Brando's peace from the desert of the house, she was going to do to her love what she did as a child to the silence of the house: she was going to provoke the storm, was going to blow up the calm, was going to destroy the good to flee a desert that existed only in her head, and was going to lose Brando, and was going, after, to miss him, and was going to seek another storm-man to fill her desert with crashing, and was going to repeat, with the faithfulness of those who do not know themselves, the life of the parents, the house of the storms, and was going perhaps to leave it, one day, to a daughter, if she had one, the chain of the storm passing from generation to generation, making of each one a woman who confuses love with noise and peace with abandonment, and who blows up, her whole life, the calm that could give her rest, in search of a storm that gives her the fever she learned to call love.
