## 7. The bottom

The Keeper's clarity did not cure Tranquilina, because the deep sicknesses are not cured by clarity, and Tranquilina, who had understood it all with her head, relapsed with her body, and the relapse was the bottom of the book: she provoked the last storm, the greatest, and nearly lost Brando, and it was on that edge of losing him that she saw herself, at last, whole.

It happened in some weeks when the love with Brando was calm, good, and it was precisely the calm that made her relapse, because the calm, in Tranquilina, still woke the fear of the desert, still gave her the itch of the storm, and however much her head knew the distinction of the two calms, her body, formed in the storm, felt the peace and wanted the crash. And there was, beneath, a worse thing, that she would only understand afterwards: that Bravo was about in the village, and that Bravo was the chain calling her, and that Tranquilina, without confessing it, went about frightened of her own cure, and one who is afraid of curing themselves clings to the sickness with more force. And on a too-calm night, with the fear of the desert rising, with the chain pulling, Tranquilina did what all the Keeper's clarity did not prevent her from doing: she provoked. And she provoked worse than ever.

It was at night, in her kitchen, and the two of them were finishing supper, and there was no cause at all, and it was for that reason that she had to invent a great one. Brando was telling her, with his tranquillity, that he had decided to stay in the shop where he was, that the house with the yard was dear after all, that there was no hurry to change anything. And Tranquilina, with the itch rising, caught on that, but took it to a place she had never gone.

"Of course, there's no hurry for anything with you," she said, and her voice came already crooked. "No hurry for the house, no hurry to marry, no hurry to live. You have no fire, Brando. You never had. With you life is spent counting seconds behind a glass, and I'm rotting here at your side."

"Tranquilina," he began, and there was hurt, but still calm. "Where does this come from now?"

And she, seeing him still calm, went to fetch the biggest knife she had, the one she knew cut deep, and used it, and it was the cruellest thing she did in her life:

"It comes from my having seen Bravo. There. I've said it. I saw Bravo, and he loves me, Brando. He has fire. He fights for me. He's jealous, he cares, he doesn't treat me like a bench of clocks. Maybe this was a mistake. Maybe I needed a real man, and not a mild man who lets me rot in the peace."

And she threw Bravo in his face the way one throws a plate at the wall, knowing where it struck, and Brando, wounded where it most hurt, lost his calm for good. He got up, and the chair scraped on the floor, and he shouted, he who never shouted, and they said things, and the storm was the greatest of the two, and it lasted, and Tranquilina, in the middle of it, felt the old terrible relief, the old fever, her heart leaping, and for an instant she was full. But Brando, at the end, stopped. He shouted no more. He stood, white with grief, and said a thing that was not in his nature and that was, for that, terrible, said low, more terrible for being low:

"Then go to Bravo, Tranquilina. Go to your fire. I don't give you storms, and you don't know how to live without them, and I've wearied. I've wearied of being good and of your punishing me for it. I've wearied of loving you in peace and of your wanting war. Go to Bravo, who'll give you the crashing I don't give you. I'm leaving. And this time I'm not coming to knock at this door in two days. I'm not your parents' cup, Tranquilina. I don't let myself be broken and glued, broken and glued, until I'm all glue and no china. Goodbye."

And he went, and closed the door behind him with care, without slamming it, and it was that care, more than a crash, that terrified Tranquilina, because a man who slams the door comes back, and a man who closes it with care has decided. And this time he went in earnest, with no door opening the next day, and Tranquilina was left alone, and it was there, in the silence that fell after that storm, that she touched the bottom.

Because the silence that fell after the storm was not the sweet silence of the reconcilings, which she expected, which she had provoked the storm to have; it was the other, the bad one, the desert. Brando did not come back. And Tranquilina was left in the empty house, with the crash echoing and no peace coming after, and went to the kitchen, and sat at the table, and stayed looking at the new cup, the one Brando had brought her, the one she had wanted to break, untouched, there, useless; and she perceived, in the desert she herself had made, the most terrible thing of all: that her sickness did not even give her what it promised. That she provoked the storms to have the reconcilings, the sweetness of after, but that the storms, taken too far, did not give reconcilings, they gave deserts, they gave losses, they gave the bad silence, the true one, the one of those who are left alone; and that she, in wishing to flee the desert by provoking the storm, had created the desert in earnest, the greatest, the one of having lost, with her own hands, the man who loved her in peace, and of having been left alone, without the crash and without the peace, in the desert that the storm, when it passes a certain point, always leaves.

And there was a night, of the worst, when the sickness made her its last offer, and she was a step from accepting it. Alone in the house, in the desert she had made, with Brando gone, Tranquilina thought of Bravo. She thought that Bravo was in the village, that it was enough to go to him, that he would receive her with open arms, that he would give her the crashing, the fever, the forgetting, that he would fill the desert with storm. And she got up, and put on her coat, and even opened the door to the night, resolved to go and fetch the one thing she knew how to do to the desert, which was to throw wood on it. And she stopped at the door. She stopped because she saw, with a sharpness that froze her, what she was going to do: she was going to flee a desert by throwing herself at a fire; she was going to betray the man who loved her in peace to run to the man who hurt her in fire; she was going, for not bearing the silence of one night, to repeat the house of the mother point by point, the window, the waiting, and now the run through the rain into the wrong arms. And she closed the door, from the inside, and leaned against it, and slid to the floor, and stayed there sitting against the closed door half the night, trembling, holding on, not going, and it was the hardest thing she did in her life, to stay sitting against a door doing nothing, because the doing-nothing, for one who only knows how to make a storm, is the greatest effort there is. And when the sky began to lighten behind the serra, Tranquilina was still sitting against the door, frozen, exhausted, but on the inside, and she perceived that she had crossed the first whole night of the desert without setting fire to it, and that if she had managed to cross that one, the worst, alone, perhaps she could cross the others, with Brando, if he would have her back.

And she stayed there, at the bottom, for days, with the empty house, without Brando, and it was the worst time of her life. She did not eat at the hours, did not open the windows, went from one side of the house to the other as the mother had gone on the days of lukewarm silence, and one night she caught herself at the kitchen window, leaning against the glass, in the same posture as Aurora, looking at the path waiting for a man who did not come, and she had such a fright of herself that she drew away from the window as one draws away from a mirror that shows too much. Because she saw, in that glass, the mother; she saw that she was being the mother; she saw that she had repeated the whole house, point by point, the storm, the door, the silence, the window, the waiting; and that she was now, as Aurora had been so many times, alone in a house in silence after a storm, awaiting a return that, this time, perhaps would not come.

She ate badly and slept worse, and one night, around the third or fourth of the absence, she sat at the table with the new cup before her, Brando's, the one she had wanted to break, and stayed looking at it a long time, by the faint light of the kitchen. And she perceived, in that untouched cup, the whole injustice of what she had done. There was a man who had brought her a cup out of kindness, a cup she had only to accept and use and that would last her whole life without ever needing glue; and she, instead of using it, had wanted to break it, so as to glue it, so as to make it like the mother's, the one with the rivers of glue, because her crooked head thought a cup was only worth something after being broken. And there she was now, with the whole cup in her hands and the man lost, and she perceived that she had done to Brando what she had tried to do to the cup: she had wanted to break him so as to glue him, to break the mild man so as to make him a storm-man, and that she had broken him in earnest, and that perhaps there was no longer glue to glue him. And she raised the cup to her mouth, and drank a sip of water from it, only to use it, at last, as it should have been used from the start, whole, without breaking it, and it was the first right thing she had done in days.

On one of those nights, she went to fetch the jar of shards, and tipped it out on the table, all the shards of her life, those of the crockery of the Ladeira, those of the glued cup she herself had broken as a little girl, and looked at it, at that heap of bits of broken things she had kept her whole life as if they were relics of love, and she saw, at last, what it was: they were not relics of love. They were wreckage. They were the bits of everything that had broken in a house where two people who loved each other spent themselves shouting and died wearied. She had kept the storms thinking she kept the love, and what she had kept was only the shards, the proof not of the love, but of the damage. And she saw that she had wished, without knowing it, to repeat that house, and that she had repeated it, and that she was going, if she did not stop, to fill another jar, her whole life, with the shards of everything she blew up in search of a fever that was not love.

On the third or fourth day of the bottom, there was a knock at her door, and it was Felismina, with a plate covered with a cloth, soup, because Felismina was of the old women who think distress is fought with soup, and who perhaps are right. She came in without asking leave, set down the plate, sat at the table, saw the shards scattered, saw the new cup, saw Tranquilina's face, and asked nothing, because she knew everything, for in the village everything is known. She stayed a while silent, and then said, slowly: I know what is happening to you, child, because it happened to me. You do not know, but I too had your sickness. I came from a house worse than your mother's, and I married a mild man, and I was, at the start, where you are now, finding him too little, provoking him, wanting crashing. And once I took the thing too far, as you have taken it, and he was on the point of going, as yours went. And do you know what saved me? It was to see myself in the mirror doing to my house what my father did to his. It was to perceive that I, who swore a thousand times as a girl that I would never be like him, was being just the same, only with words instead of hands. And it was that fright that stopped me. And she looked Tranquilina in the eyes, and said the thing that was a gift: you are still in time, child. I was. Fifty years of peace I had with my quiet man, fifty years that no one envied me and that were worth more than all your mother's storms, God rest her. Your clockmaker did not go from having grown tired of you; he went from fear of wearing himself out. Go to him, and tell him the truth, all of it, without pride. The mild men forgive, child, it is what they do best. But do not make them forgive too many times, for mildness too has a bottom. And she went, leaving the soup, and Tranquilina ate it, and it was the first thing she ate in days, and while she ate it she perceived that Felismina was what the Keeper had told her, the living proof that the chain is cut, and that she had come, with a plate of soup, to tell her that from the bottom one comes out.

And she saw, above all, the thing that broke her and saved her: that Brando was right, that she had punished him for being good. That she had had before her a man who loved her in peace, to be trusted, without hurting her, who had searched the fairs for a watch like her mother's, who asked her every day whether she was well; and that instead of thanking him, instead of resting in that rare love, she had punished him, provoked him, wounded him, thrown Bravo in his face, only because his love made no noise, only because he did not give her the fever, only because his peace tasted to her of the desert of childhood. And that this, to punish a man for loving in peace, was the most unjust and the sickest thing one can do, and that she had done it, and that she had lost Brando because of it, because of the sickness, because of the storm she carried from the cradle.

And it was at the bottom of that desert, with Brando lost, with the shards scattered on the table, that Tranquilina made the decision that changes books: she decided that, if Brando came back, if there were a chance, she was going to cure herself in earnest, was going to learn to love in peace, was going to tell the two calms apart, was going to stop provoking storms, whatever it cost; and that if Brando did not come back, if it was too late, she was going to cure herself all the same, so as not to repeat, so as not to do to another man what she had done to Brando, so as to cut the chain, so as not to be, her whole life, the woman who blows up the good in search of crashing. And she gathered all the shards, slowly, and put them back in the jar, but this time not to keep them as love; she kept them as a warning, like one who keeps the mark of a flood on the wall to remember how high the water rose. And that decision, made at the bottom, in the desert she herself had made, was the beginning of the cure, because the cure of a sickness always begins at the bottom of it, when one sees, at last, without fleeing, that the sickness does not give what it promises, that the storm does not give love, it gives desert, and that the only road out of the desert is not more storm, it is the peace she had spent her life confusing with the very desert she fled.
