## 7. The bottom

The Keeper's clarity did not cure Tranquilina, because the deep illnesses are not cured by clarity, and Tranquilina, who had understood it all with her head, relapsed with her body, and the relapse was the bottom of the book: she provoked the last storm, the greatest, and nearly lost Brando, and it was on that edge of losing him that she saw herself, at last, whole.

It happened in some weeks when the love with Brando was calm, good, and it was precisely the calm that made her relapse, because the calm, in Tranquilina, still woke the fear of the desert, still gave her the itch of the storm, and however much her head knew the distinction of the two calms, her body, formed in the storm, felt the peace and wanted the crash, and Tranquilina, on a too-calm night, with the fear of the desert rising, did what all the Keeper's clarity did not prevent her from doing: she provoked. And she provoked worse than ever, because this time there was, beneath, the fear of curing herself, and one who fears curing themselves clings to the illness with more force, and Tranquilina clung to the storm with the force of one who feels she is going to lose it.

She made a quarrel without cause, as on the other times, but took it further, said harder things, threw at Brando the mistrust, accused him of not loving her enough, of being lukewarm, of having no fire, and when Brando, hurt, tried the calm, she spat at him the cruellest thing, told him perhaps it had been a mistake, perhaps she needed an in-earnest man, a man of passion, and threw Bravo in his face, said Bravo loved her truly, that Bravo had fire, and used Bravo as the lightning of the storm, to wound Brando deep, to make him explode, to get the great crash. And she succeeded. Brando, wounded where it most hurt, lost his calm for good, and said things too, and the storm was the greatest of the two, and at the end Brando, white with grief, said to her a thing that was not in his nature and that was, for that, terrible: then go to Bravo, Tranquilina. Go to your fire. I do not give you storms, and you do not know how to live without them, and I have wearied. I have wearied of being good and of your punishing me for it. I have wearied of loving you in peace and of your wanting war. Go to Bravo. I am leaving. And he left, and this time he left in earnest, with no door opening the next day, and Tranquilina was left alone, and it was there, in the silence that fell after that storm, that she touched the bottom.

Because the silence that fell after the storm was not the sweet silence of the reconciliations, which she expected, which she had provoked the storm to have; it was the other, the bad one, the desert, because Brando did not come back, and Tranquilina was left in the empty house, with the crash echoing and no peace coming after, and perceived, in the desert she herself had made, the most terrible thing of all: that her illness did not even give her what it promised. That she provoked the storms to have the reconciliations, the sweetness of after, but that the storms, taken too far, did not give reconciliations, they gave deserts, they gave losses, they gave the bad silence, the true one, the one of those who are left alone; and that she, in wishing to flee the desert by provoking the storm, had created the desert in earnest, the greatest, the one of having lost, with her own hands, the man who loved her in peace, and of having been left alone, without the crash and without the peace, in the desert that the storm, when it passes a certain point, always leaves.

And she stayed there, at the bottom, for days, with the empty house, without Brando, and it was the worst time of her life, and it was, she would perceive, the necessary time, because it was at the bottom, in the desert she herself had made, that Tranquilina saw, at last, without fleeing, the whole truth of her illness. She saw that the storm was not love, that the storm destroyed love, that she had, with her storm, destroyed the best love she had had. She saw that the fever she pursued, that of the crash, was the fever of the parents' house, and that the parents' house had not been a happy house, it had been a house of storms in which two people who loved each other wore themselves out shouting and died wearied, and that she had wished, without knowing it, to repeat that house, and that she had repeated it, and that she was now, as the mother had been so many times, alone in a house in silence after a storm, awaiting a return that, this time, perhaps would not come.

And she saw, above all, the thing that broke her and saved her: that Brando was right, that she had punished him for being good. That she had had before her a man who loved her in peace, to be trusted, without hurting her, and that instead of thanking him, instead of resting in that rare love, she had punished him, provoked him, wounded him, thrown Bravo in his face, only because his love made no noise, only because he did not give her the fever, only because his peace tasted to her of the desert of childhood. And that this, to punish a man for loving in peace, was the most unjust and the sickest thing one can do, and that she had done it, and that she had lost Brando because of it, because of the illness, because of the storm she had carried from the cradle.

And it was at the bottom of that desert, with Brando lost, that Tranquilina made the decision that changes books: she decided that, if Brando came back, if there were a chance, she was going to cure herself in earnest, was going to learn to love in peace, was going to tell the two calms apart, was going to stop provoking storms, whatever it cost; and that if Brando did not come back, if it was too late, she was going to cure herself all the same, so as not to repeat, so as not to do to another man what she had done to Brando, so as to cut the chain, so as not to be, her whole life, the woman who blows up the good in search of crashing. And that decision, made at the bottom, in the desert she herself had made, was the beginning of the cure, because the cure of an illness always begins at the bottom of it, when one sees, at last, without fleeing, that the illness does not give what it promises, that the storm does not give love, it gives desert, and that the only road out of the desert is not more storm, it is the peace she had spent her life confusing with the very desert she fled.
