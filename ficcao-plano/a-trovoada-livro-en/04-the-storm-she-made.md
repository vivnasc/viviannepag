## 4. The storm she made

What Tranquilina feared happened: the restlessness grew until she could not bear it, and, without deciding it on purpose, but without preventing it either, she made the storm. She provoked the quarrel she had been wishing for, and had, in the crash, the relief she sought, and it was that relief, more than the quarrel, that terrified her.

It was over a thing of nothing, as the storms one makes on purpose always are: they need a small cause to hide that they have no cause at all. It was a Saturday night, and Brando had come to dine at her house, and he carried, wrapped in newspaper, a present, which was a thing he did, to bring small things without occasion. It was a cup. He had seen in a shop in the big village a cup of good china, with a fine little stem, like one Tranquilina had once described to him, of her mother's, and he had bought it, and brought it, and put it on the table with a smile, with the innocence of one who has got it right.

And Tranquilina looked at the cup, and instead of the tenderness, felt rising in her a dark thing. Because Brando's cup was new. It was whole. It had no glue crossing it, it had no scars, it had not fallen nor come back. It was a cup that had never been broken, and so, to Tranquilina's crooked head, it was a cup without history, without proof, without love; and the one of her father, the one of Aurora, the glued one, was worth more, because that one had fallen and come back. And Tranquilina, who went about with the spark awaiting a place to catch, caught on that, without quite knowing what at, only knowing that the new cup hurt her and that she needed to break it, and she said, with a sharpness that came from nowhere:

"You buy things thinking you know me. It's nothing like my mother's. My mother's had a history. This one has nothing."

Brando stood looking at the cup, without understanding.

"I can change it," he said, mild. "It was only to please you."

"With you everything is always easy," she threw at him, and the spark was already catching, and she already felt the air gathering as in the kitchen of the Ladeira. "Everything right, everything mild, everything without a problem. Sometimes it seems you don't care about anything. That it's all the same to you."

"I care about you," said Brando, and there was already a beginning of distress in his voice, because he did not understand where this came from.

And Tranquilina, seeing that he did not explode, raised her tone, and said things, and searched his face for the sign that he was going to explode, that he was going to shout, that he was going to give the storm. She threw in his face invented hurts, complaints that had no foundation, that he left her alone, that he never defended her, that perhaps he did not love her enough because whoever loves suffers and he did not suffer for her, and all this to make him lose his calm, all to light the storm. And she lifted the new cup, Brando's, and for an instant the old little girl of the Ladeira was on the brink of letting it fall, there, in front of him, as she had let the glued one fall in front of the parents; but she did not let it fall, she set it down hard on the table, and said more things, harder.

And Brando, at a certain point, lost his calm. He was a mild man, but he was a man, and there is a point at which mildness gives way, and Tranquilina, pushing, pushing, led him to that point, and Brando got up from the table, and the chair scraped on the floor, and he raised his voice, for the first time since she had known him:

"That's enough, Tranquilina! I said nothing! I brought you a cup, that's all, and you've made a war of it! What do you want from me? Tell me what you want, because I no longer know!"

And there was rage in his voice, true rage, and one hears badly what follows, they shouted at each other, said things, and it was the first storm of the two. Brando, at the end, white, snatched up his coat, and went off into the night, and slammed the door, and the house shook, and Tranquilina was left alone, with the house trembling from the crash, with the new cup still whole on the table.

And it was then that Tranquilina felt the terrible thing: she felt herself alive. She felt, in the middle of the quarrel, with her heart beating, with the adrenaline of the storm, a thing she had not felt in months of calm with Brando, and that thing was the fever, was the crash, was the intense sense of living an in-earnest love. And alone in the house after the quarrel, with her heart still leaping, she caught herself feeling, beneath the hurt, strangely satisfied, strangely full, as if the storm had given her a thing she had been starved of. She went to the kitchen, opened the drawer, took out the jar of shards, and sat with it at the table, beside the new cup, and looked at the two, the jar of the kept storms and the cup that had never been broken, and perceived, with horror, that it was that, that it was exactly that, that she had provoked the quarrel to feel that, to fill the desert with crashing, and that she felt more alive after a quarrel than she had felt in months of peace, and that this was her sickness, to see in the storm the life and in the peace the death.

And the two days when Brando was away, Tranquilina lived in a way that should have warned her, and did not warn her, because she still read everything backwards. She lived them intensely. She did not eat, did not sleep, went about the house with her heart leaping, thinking of him at every hour, rehearsing the reconciliation, imagining the return; and all that intensity, that not-eating and not-sleeping and thinking-of-him-at-every-hour, Tranquilina read as love, as proof that she loved him very much, see how I suffer for him, it is because I love him. And she did not see what was underneath: that she only felt the love with that force when she had put it at risk, that she needed to almost-lose it to feel it whole, that love, for her, only kindled in the fear of losing it, and that for that reason she went about, without knowing it, always putting herself at risk of losing it, provoking the storms, making the threats, because it was the only way she knew to feel the love with that intensity. A sure love, that was not at risk, made her feel nothing; only the love in danger kindled her; and so she put it in danger, without perceiving that it was the sickness, and not the love, that needed the danger to burn.

And there came, after the relief, the other part of the cycle, the one she also knew from the house: there came the wish for the reconciliation, the desire for Brando to come back, to make peace, to live the sweetness of after the storm, which was, in her memory, the most intense part of the love, the warmer sun after the storm, Aurora singing in the kitchen, Vasco carrying her in his arms. And Tranquilina perceived, in wishing for the reconciliation, the whole mechanism of her sickness: that she needed the quarrel to have the good peace, that without storm she did not know how to feel the tenderness, that love, for her, was this cycle, quarrel and peace, crash and truce, and that a love without quarrel, as Brando's had been until then, did not give her the good peace because it did not give her the storm that preceded it, and that she, without knowing it, was trying to install in Brando's love the cycle of the parents' house, the storm so as then to have the peace, because the continuous peace, the storm-less one, Brando's, did not taste to her of peace, it tasted to her of the desert.

And there was, in those two days when she waited for Brando, a moment when Tranquilina saw herself in the mirror of her mother for the first time without knowing how to explain it. She was at the kitchen window, at the end of the second day, looking at the path waiting to see him come up, and she caught herself in the exact posture of Aurora, leaning against the glass, with the same distress, saying within he'll come back, he always comes back; and she had a shiver, but did not understand where it came from, because she still read that posture as love, see how I wait for him, it is because I love him, and not as what it was, the inheritance, the learned stance of a woman who had spent her life at that window waiting for a man she herself made leave. The little girl who picked up the shards from the floor had become a woman and was now at the window like the mother, and the jar of shards was in the drawer, and the house of the Ladeira repeated itself in her point by point, and she still did not see it, because what is learned in a house is worn like skin and is not felt, it is felt only when someone from outside points it out to us, or when life, by force, wrenches it from us.

Brando came back, two days later, and knocked at the door in the morning, before opening the shop, and Tranquilina lived the reconciliation with the intensity she expected, and it was sweet, and it was strong, and she felt herself loved as she had not felt in the months of calm, and for some days she went about well, went about full, because she had had her dose of storm and of peace, her cycle, her fever. And there was, in those sweet days of the reconciliation, a moment that Tranquilina still kept as happiness before perceiving what it was. One afternoon, Brando brought her flowers from the path, gathered by hand, a bunch of field flowers tied with a stalk of grass, and put them on the table, and Tranquilina, on seeing them, felt her heart fill as it had never filled in the calm afternoons; and only much later did she perceive why. It was the scene of Vasco. It was the father going up the path with the flowers gathered at the roadside after the storm, it was the house of the Ladeira repeating itself there, in her kitchen, with another man; and her heart had filled not because the flowers were worth more than the watch he had given her on a calm day, but because they came after a storm, and what came after a storm had, for Tranquilina, a taste that nothing that came in peace would ever have. And it was that the trap she was slow to see: that she did not love the Brando of the flowers-after-the-quarrel more than the Brando of the watch-on-a-calm-day; she loved them the same; she only felt the first more, because the feeling, in her, needed the contrast of the crash, and a love without crash, however great, she barely felt, as one does not feel the air one breathes, and she only noticed it, as with the air, when she put it in want.

But Brando, in the reconciliation, sat at the table, in the chair facing the cup, and said to her a thing that stayed with her, and that was the beginning of the cure. He said to her, with the mildness coming back, but with a new sadness:

"Tranquilina, I came back, and I love you, but I have to tell you a thing. That quarrel had no cause. You made it. I saw you making it, saw you searching for where to catch, like one who looks for a loose thread to pull until the whole knit comes undone. And I do not know how to live like this, quarrelling without cause so as then to make peace. I do not need storms to love you. I love you in peace. And if you need storms, Tranquilina, if you only feel loved when there is drama, then this is going to be hard, because I am not going to give you dramas, it is not my nature, and if that is what you need, I am not the man. Think about it. Not for us to quarrel. For you to perceive what you need, and whether I can give it to you."

And he fell silent, and then, lower, looking at the new cup, which was still on the table, untouched:

"And that cup, Tranquilina. I know what you meant. That your mother's was worth more because it had fallen and come back, because it had the glue crossing it. But your mother broke that cup, child. Someone broke it. The glue is pretty, but it is the mark of a thing that was broken. I do not want to give you a cup with glue. I want to give you one that need not be glued."

And Tranquilina heard this, and it was hard for her, because Brando had put his finger on the exact wound, with his calm: you need storms, and I do not give them to you; and the glue is the mark of a thing that was broken. And she perceived that he was right, that she had needed the storm, that she had made it, and that if she went on like that she was going to wear Brando out, was going to make him give dramas that were not his, or was going to lose him, because a mild man does not bear a life of manufactured storms, he wearies, he goes, and Tranquilina was going to be left without him, and was going to seek another storm-man to give her the crashing, and was going to repeat the parents' house. And for the first time, instead of wanting the storm, Tranquilina was afraid of it, afraid of her own sickness, afraid of what she was capable of doing to Brando, for nothing, only for not knowing how to live in peace, and wished, for the first time, not the storm, but the cure, not the crash, but the capacity to rest in the peace, and it was that wish for the cure, born of Brando's sadness and of a sentence about the glue of cups, the beginning of her crossing, because no one is cured of a sickness they still think is love, and Tranquilina, in that reconciliation, began, at last, to see that her storm was not love, it was sickness, and that love, the in-earnest one, could be that calm thing Brando gave her and that she did not know how to feel.
