## 4. The storm she made

What Tranquilina feared happened: the restlessness grew until she could not bear it, and, without deciding it on purpose, but without preventing it either, she made the storm. She provoked the quarrel she had been wishing for, and had, in the crash, the relief she sought, and it was that relief, more than the quarrel, that terrified her.

It was over a thing of nothing, as the storms one makes on purpose always are: they need a small cause to hide that they have no cause at all. Brando said some thing, an innocent remark about a thing Tranquilina had done, said without malice, in his gentleness, and Tranquilina, who went about with the spark awaiting a place to catch, caught on that. She twisted the sentence, held it against him, answered ill, and when Brando, surprised, tried to explain himself, with calm, Tranquilina raised her tone, and said things, and sought, in his face, the sign that he was going to explode, that he was going to shout, that he was going to give the storm; and as Brando did not explode, as he answered with the gentleness of always, Tranquilina raised more, said harder things, threw in his face invented grievances, complaints with no foundation, all to make him lose his calm, all to light the storm.

And Brando, at a certain point, lost it. He was a gentle man, but he was a man, and there is a point at which gentleness gives way, and Tranquilina, pushing, pushing, led him to that point, and Brando raised his voice, for the first time, and told her she was being unjust, that he had said nothing bad, that she was making a quarrel without cause, and there was anger in his voice, true anger, and one hears badly what follows, they shouted at each other, said things, and it was the first storm of the two, and Brando, at the end, left, slammed the door, went off into the night, leaving Tranquilina alone, with the house trembling from the crash.

And it was then that Tranquilina felt the terrible thing: she felt herself alive. She felt, in the midst of the quarrel, with her heart beating, with the adrenaline of the storm, a thing she had not felt in months of calm with Brando, and that thing was the fever, was the crash, was the intense sense of living an in-earnest love, and Tranquilina, alone in the house after the quarrel, with her heart still leaping, caught herself feeling, beneath the grievance, strangely satisfied, strangely full, as if the storm had given her a thing she had been starved of, and perceived, with horror, that it was that, that it was exactly that, that she had provoked the quarrel to feel that, to fill the desert with crashing, and that she felt more alive after a quarrel than she had felt in months of peace, and that this was her illness, to see in the storm the life and in the peace the death.

And there came, after the relief, the other part of the cycle, the one she also knew from the house: there came the wish for the reconciliation, the desire for Brando to come back, to make peace, to live the sweetness of after the storm, which was, in her memory, the most intense part of the love. And Tranquilina perceived, in wishing for the reconciliation, the whole mechanism of her illness: that she needed the quarrel to have the good peace, that without storm she did not know how to feel the tenderness, that love, for her, was this cycle, quarrel and peace, crash and truce, and that a love without quarrel, as Brando's had been until then, did not give her the good peace because it did not give her the storm that preceded it, and that she, without knowing it, was trying to install in Brando's love the cycle of the parents' house, the storm so as then to have the peace, because the continuous peace, the storm-less one, Brando's, did not taste to her of peace, it tasted to her of the desert.

Brando came back, two days later, and Tranquilina lived the reconciliation with the intensity she expected, and it was sweet, and it was strong, and Tranquilina felt herself loved as she had not felt in the months of calm, and for some days she went about well, went about full, because she had had her dose of storm and of peace, her cycle, her fever. But Brando, in the reconciliation, said to her a thing that stayed with her, and that was the beginning of the cure. He said to her, with the gentleness coming back, but with a new sadness: Tranquilina, I came back, and I love you, but I have to tell you a thing. That quarrel had no cause. You made it. And I do not know how to live like this, quarrelling without cause so as then to make peace. I do not need storms to love you. I love you in peace. And if you need storms, Tranquilina, if you only feel loved when there is drama, then this is going to be hard, because I am not going to give you dramas, it is not my nature, and if that is what you need, I am not the man. Think about it. Not for us to quarrel. For you to perceive what you need, and whether I can give it to you.

And Tranquilina heard this, and it was hard for her, because Brando had put his finger on the exact wound, with his calm: you need storms, and I do not give them to you. And she perceived that he was right, that she had needed the storm, that she had made it, and that if she went on like that she was going to wear Brando out, was going to make him give dramas that were not his, or was going to lose him, because a gentle man does not bear a life of manufactured storms, he wearies, he goes, and Tranquilina was going to be left without him, and was going to seek another storm-man to give her the crashing, and was going to repeat the parents' house. And for the first time, instead of wanting the storm, Tranquilina was afraid of it, afraid of her own illness, afraid of what she was capable of doing to Brando, and wished, for the first time, not the storm, but the cure, not the crash, but the capacity to rest in the peace, and it was that wish for the cure, born of Brando's sadness, the beginning of her crossing, because no one is cured of an illness they still think is love, and Tranquilina, in that reconciliation, began, at last, to see that her storm was not love, it was illness, and that love, the in-earnest one, could be that calm thing Brando gave her and that she did not know how to feel.
