## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Tranquilina, daughter of people of the village, born on a night of thunderstorm, crossed, at last, the first calm night of her life without fleeing and without setting fire, and made a life, in peace, with a gentle man, and cut in herself the storm she had inherited from a house of shouts. Be it set down that she learned to tell the two calms apart, that of peace and that of absence, which in her house came confused, and that she discovered, on the far side of the fear, that the quiet was not the desert, it was the love that does not need to make noise.

The register keeps the night on which each one was born, and there are those who are born on nights that stay in their blood. This Keeper saw, in the book, the night of thunderstorm on which Tranquilina came into the world, and saw, pages back, the storms of her parents, generation shouting at generation; and understood that Tranquilina had inherited the storm, and that she confused, like all the children of houses of storm, the crash with the love and the peace with abandonment. And she had the rare luck to meet a calm love, and the greater courage not to blow it up, and to learn, with effort, to love it in peace, and in doing so she cut the chain, and will give her children, if she has any, a house without a storm.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Tranquilina who only feels loved in a storm, who confuses intensity with love and peace with its lack, who blows up what is good because the good tastes to her of too little, let her know what she learned and what this house records: that peace is not absence, it is love without a wound; that intensity is not proof of love, sometimes it is only the echo of a house where there was shouting; and that the greater courage is not to make the storm to feel something, it is to cross the calm whole, without fleeing and without setting fire, and to discover that the quiet is the place where, at last, one can rest from being loved.

Tranquilina traded the fever for the love, the crash for the low voice, the inherited storm for the house she chose. And on the calm nights of Véspera, since that first one, there is one more woman who no longer provokes the storm to flee the desert, because she learned that between the two there is a third place, that of peace, where one rests, and which is what this village, book by book, is slowly learning.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
