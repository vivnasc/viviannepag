## 6. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to a paper at the House of the Register, a certificate of her age that they asked her for I do not know what, and it was so, for a thing of bureaucracy, that Tranquilina sat before the Keeper, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps the nights on which each one was born, and where, she would perceive, the storms one inherits are kept too.

The House of the Register was cool and smelled of old paper and of ink, and the Keeper was at her table, with her spectacles slid down her nose, with a great book open before her and a blotter at her side. She was a woman of settled hands, who saw people as she saw the entries, slowly and whole. She saw to the paper, wrote what she had to write in her upright hand, dried the ink with the blotter, and then, instead of sending her off, stayed looking at Tranquilina over her spectacles, with that look that reads between the lines, and said:

"Tranquilina. You were born on the night of the great storm. I remember. It is here, in my book."

And she rose, and went to a tall shelf, and ran her finger along the spines until she found the year, and took down a heavy book, and brought it to the table, and opened it, and turned the pages until she stopped at one, and turned the book towards Tranquilina, and pointed. There was the entry of her birth, the date, the hour, the names of the parents, Aurora and Vasco, in the hand of an old Keeper; and beside it, in the margin, in smaller writing, a note, for the old Keepers sometimes noted the weather, like ones who do not want the village to forget anything. And the note said: night of a great storm, the river rose to the bridge, an oak fell on the upper path.

"Look at this," said the Keeper, low, with her finger on the note. "You were born in the middle of a storm, Tranquilina, on the night the river nearly carried off the bridge. And they gave you the name of the quiet. Your mother wished to take the storm from you with the name. I knew her, Aurora; she came here to register you, she could barely walk yet, and she said to me, I named her Tranquilina so this girl will have the peace I never had." The Keeper raised her eyes. "She did not manage it, did she? You brought it within. The name stayed on the outside, like the ink on this page, and the storm stayed in your blood."

And Tranquilina, who expected only to see to a paper, felt the Keeper touch a deep thing in her, and her eyes filled without her bidding them. And the Keeper, seeing her shaken, did a thing she did not do for everyone: she turned the pages back, years back, and went to the entries of the parents.

"And look," she said. "Here is your parents' marriage, Aurora and Vasco. And do you see this date, and this, and this?" She pointed to entries in the margin, in the hands of different Keepers, along the years. "Here a separation was set down that was entered and not carried through. Here, two years later, a return. Here a complaint that reached the judge and that your mother withdrew on the eve. And here, you see, in the middle of all this, you were born. Your parents, Tranquilina, were a byword in the village. Everyone knew of their storms. They quarrelled, separated, came back, ten times, twenty. And they loved each other, that they did, no one can say they did not love each other. But they loved each other so, in storm. And you grew up in the midst of those storms, and learned, before you could speak, that love is this, it is the crash. And now you do not know how to love any other way."

And the Keeper closed the book slowly, with both hands, like one who closes a thing that weighs, and said to Tranquilina a thing she never forgot:

"I have watched the families of the village pass by this table for many years, and I learned to see the chains. Do you see all these books? They are houses. And I, from so much reading of them, learned that there are houses of storm, like yours, in which love is made by shouting, generation after generation; and the children of those houses, almost all, choose storms, marry storms, make storms, and raise children in the midst of them, and the chain passes, and passes, and no one cuts it, because no one sees that it is a chain, all think it is love. And now and then, rarely, there is a child of a house of storm who has the luck to meet a calm love, a man or a woman of peace, and who has then a choice the others did not have: either they blow up the calm love, because they do not know how to feel it, and go back to the storm, to the chain, to the house; or they learn, with effort, to love in peace, and cut the chain, and give their children, if they have any, a house without a storm. You, Tranquilina, are one of those children. You have met the calm love, have you not? The clockmaker. The whole village knows. You have before you that choice. The question is whether you are going to blow it up or whether you are going to learn it."

And the Keeper turned still a few more pages, to the last entries of the parents, and her finger came to rest on the two dates of death, that of Aurora and that of Vasco, so near one to the other, less than two years between them. Do you see? she said, low. They went almost together. It was not chance. I have seen many couples in these books, and the storm ones go almost always so, one after the other, quickly, because they spent themselves, because a life burning consumes all the wood, and when one goes, the other, who knew no living but in that fire, goes out at once, having no one left to burn with. The peace ones last longer, child, not because they love each other more, but because they spend themselves less; the calm bed does not dry up, the flood runs itself out. Your mother and your father loved each other their whole life and killed each other their whole life in the same manner, and it was the same thing, the love and the death, because fire is so, it is the warmth and it is the ash in the same gesture. And Tranquilina looked at the two dates so near, and perceived, with a tightening, that the house she had grown up in was not only a house of shouting, it was a house that had consumed itself, and that she, in wishing to repeat it, was asking not only for the storm, but for the ash the storm leaves.

Tranquilina heard this, and it was to her like the light that comes suddenly into a dark room: she saw, all at once, the whole chain, the house of the Ladeira, the inherited storm, her own sickness, Bravo who had come back to pull her once more into the chain, and Brando as the rare luck, the calm love that had fallen in her path, and the choice she had before her, to blow up or to learn. And she perceived that she had been, with Bravo, with the manufactured storms, with the new cup she had wanted to break, trying to pull Brando into the chain, trying to make of the calm love a storm love, because it was the only one she knew, and that this was to blow up her luck, was to go back to the house, was not to cut the chain; and that the other choice, the hard one, that of learning to love Brando in peace, was the only one that saved her, and the children she might have, from the storm of generations.

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a keeper of books, sat down again, and said to her still the thing that gave her the key, slowly, so the sentence would not be lost:

"Your error, Tranquilina, is to confuse two things that in your house came together and that are different. In your house, the calm only came after the storm, and so you think the calm without storm is the other calm, the bad one, the desert one, the one of when your parents, worn out, did not speak. But there are two calms, child, and you only know one, the bad one. There is the calm that is a lack of love, the desert, and there is the calm that is love without a wound, the peace. And they are as different as night and day, but you, raised in the storm, see the two as the same, see all calm as desert, and so the clockmaker's calm, which is peace, which is love without a wound, tastes to you of desert, of a lack of love, and you want to blow it up to flee a desert that is not there. Learn to tell them apart, Tranquilina. His peace is not your parents' silence. One is love rested. The other was love over. And until you tell them apart, you are going to flee the first thinking it is the second, and you are going to lose the best that has happened to you."

And Tranquilina, on the point of rising, asked the Keeper the question that burned her, the question of one who needs to know whether there is a way out: and has anyone ever cut it, ever? Has anyone of these houses of storm ever managed it? Or is it all the same chain to the end? And the Keeper looked at her, and for a moment softened, and went to the book of another year, and showed her an entry, and said: Felismina. You know her, she was a neighbour of your mother's. Felismina came from a house worse than yours, from a father who beat, from a chain of generations of men who beat and women who endured. And Felismina married a quiet man, with no fire at all, a man the village thought too little for her, and everyone said, poor thing, she's gone and buried herself with that lukewarm one. And she had, at the start, your sickness, child; she found him too little, she missed the crashing she had grown up used to, she was on the edge of leaving him more than once. But she held on. She crossed the calm, without knowing she called it that, and cut the chain, and raised her children in a house without a blow and without a shout, and her children raised their children in the same peace, and the chain stopped in her, fifty years ago, and the village did not even notice, because the chains that are cut make no noise, only those that are kept. That is why Felismina told you, at the washing place, to take hold of the clockmaker. She knows what she speaks of. She is the proof that it is cut. Go and speak with her, Tranquilina, for she crossed what you have before you.

Tranquilina left the House of the Register with the key in her hand, the distinction between the two calms, and crossed the square, and stopped by the fountain, and stood looking at the water. And the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her: that the water runs better in the calm bed than in the flood; that the flood makes noise and carries everything off and passes, and the calm bed makes no noise and lasts, and gives to drink. And she remembered the river that had risen on the night she was born, the river that had nearly carried off the bridge, all noise and force and danger, and the same river on the mild days, low, clear, when the women of the Ladeira went there to wash and the children to gather cockles; and she perceived that it was the same river, and that she had spent her life confusing the flood with the river, the noise with the water, and thinking that a calm river was not a river because it made no noise, when it is precisely the calm river that quenches the thirst, and the flood that drowns.

And she went home, that afternoon, with the truth of the Keeper and that of the fountain, and looked at her love with Brando in another way: no longer as a lukewarm love that lacked fire, but as a calm bed, a peace that was love and not desert, and perceived that she had been trying to turn her river into a flood, to put storms into it, and that if she went on she was going to dry the river by dint of wanting it a flood, and that her crossing, from then on, was to learn to drink from the calm bed without confusing it with the drought, to rest in the peace without confusing it with the desert, to love Brando in a low voice without needing the crash that the parents' house had taught her to confuse with love.
