## 6. The keeper of the register

She went to see to a paper at the House of the Register, and it was so, for a thing of bureaucracy, that Tranquilina sat before the Keeper, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps the nights on which each one was born, and where, she would perceive, the storms one inherits are kept too.

The Keeper, who saw people as she saw the entries, saw to the paper, and then stayed looking at Tranquilina, with that look that reads between the lines, and said: you were born on the night of the great storm. I remember. It is here. And she rose, and went to a shelf, and took a book, and opened it, and showed Tranquilina the entry of her birth, with the date, and beside it, in the hand of the old Keeper, a note, for the old Keepers sometimes noted the weather, and the note said: night of a great storm, the river rose. And the Keeper said, low: you were born in the middle of a storm, Tranquilina. And they gave you the name of the calm. Your mother wished to take the storm from you with the name. She did not manage it. You brought it within.

And Tranquilina, who expected only to see to a paper, felt the Keeper touch a deep thing in her, and the Keeper, seeing her shaken, turned the pages back, to the entries of Tranquilina's parents, and said: and look. Here is your parents' marriage. And here, see, the dates of your brothers, and yours. And do you see how between one and another there are years in which quarrels that reached court are set down, separations that were not carried through, returns? Your parents, Tranquilina, were a byword in the village. Everyone knew of their storms. They quarrelled, separated, came back, ten times, twenty. And they loved each other, that they did, but they loved each other so, in storm. And you grew up in the midst of those storms, and you learned, before you could speak, that love is this, it is the crash. And now you do not know how to love any other way.

And the Keeper said a thing Tranquilina never forgot: I have watched the families of the village pass by this table for many years, and I learned to see the chains. There are houses of storm, like yours, in which love is made by shouting, generation after generation; and the children of those houses, almost all, choose storms, marry storms, make storms, and raise children in the midst of them, and the chain passes, and passes, and no one cuts it, because no one sees that it is a chain, all think it is love. And now and then, rarely, there is a child of a house of storm who has the luck to meet a calm love, a man or a woman of peace, and who has then a choice the others did not have: either they blow up the calm love, because they do not know how to feel it, and go back to the storm, to the chain, to the house; or they learn, with effort, to love in peace, and cut the chain, and give their children, if they have any, a house without a storm. You, Tranquilina, are one of those children. You have before you that choice. You have met the calm love. The question is whether you are going to blow it up or whether you are going to learn it.

Tranquilina heard this, and it was to her like the light that comes suddenly into a dark room: she saw, all at once, the whole chain, the parents' house, the inherited storm, her own illness, and Brando as the rare luck, the calm love that had fallen in her path, and the choice she had before her, to blow up or to learn. And she perceived that she had been, with Bravo, with the manufactured storms, trying to pull Brando into the chain, trying to make of the calm love a storm love, because it was the only one she knew, and that this was to blow up her luck, was to go back to the house, was not to cut the chain; and that the other choice, the hard one, that of learning to love Brando in peace, was the only one that saved her, and the children she might have, from the storm of generations.

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a keeper of books, said to her also the thing that gave her the key: your error, Tranquilina, is to confuse two things that in your house came together and that are different. In your house, the calm only came after the storm, and so you think the calm without storm is the other calm, the bad one, the desert one, the one of when your parents, worn out, did not speak. But there are two calms, child, and you only know one, the bad one. There is the calm that is a lack of love, the desert, and there is the calm that is love without a wound, the peace. And they are as different as night and day, but you, raised in the storm, see the two as the same, see all calm as desert, and so Brando's calm, which is peace, which is love without a wound, tastes to you of desert, of a lack of love, and you want to blow it up to flee a desert that is not there. Learn to tell them apart, Tranquilina. Brando's peace is not your parents' silence. One is love rested. The other was love over. And until you tell them apart, you are going to flee the first thinking it is the second, and you are going to lose the best that has happened to you.

Tranquilina left the House of the Register with the key in her hand, the distinction between the two calms, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain, and the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her: that the water runs better in the calm bed than in the flood, that the flood makes noise and carries everything off and passes, and the calm bed makes no noise and lasts, and gives to drink, and that she, Tranquilina, had spent her life confusing the flood with the river, the noise with the water, and thinking that a calm river was not a river because it made no noise, when it is precisely the calm river that quenches the thirst, and the flood that drowns.

And she went home, that afternoon, with the truth of the Keeper and that of the fountain, and looked at her love with Brando in another way: no longer as a lukewarm love that lacked fire, but as a calm bed, a peace that was love and not desert, and perceived that she had been trying to turn her river into a flood, to put storms into it, and that if she went on she was going to dry the river by dint of wanting it a flood, and that her crossing, from then on, was to learn to drink from the calm bed without confusing it with the drought, to rest in the peace without confusing it with the desert, to love Brando in a low voice without needing the crash that the parents' house had taught her to confuse with love.
