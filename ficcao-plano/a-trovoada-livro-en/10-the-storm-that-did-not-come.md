## 10. The storm that did not come

The proof that Tranquilina was curing herself did not come on a calm night, for those she already knew how to cross; it came on a day of true difficulty, on a day that, in the parents' house, would have given the greatest of storms, and that with Brando gave another thing, and it was in that other thing that Tranquilina saw the difference between the house she had inherited and the house she was learning to make.

There came a small disaster, of the kind life brings, a serious problem, of those that put a couple to the test: an illness in Brando's family, a worry, money that was wanting, a string of bad things that fell together, as they always fall, and for some weeks the life of the two was hard, was heavy, was of distress. And in the house of Tranquilina's parents, a disaster like this would have been the perfect pretext for the storm: they would have quarrelled over it, would have accused each other, would have shouted, because in her house difficulty did not unite, it divided, it gave a cause for the crash, and the disaster was always, in the end, one more storm among the many.

And Tranquilina, at the start of the difficulty, felt her body want the storm. She felt the itch, stronger than ever, because distress feeds the itch, and fear, and weariness, and Tranquilina caught herself wanting to unload on Brando, wanting to quarrel, wanting to make of the disaster a storm, as she had learned at home; and there were one or two moments when she nearly gave in, when the itch nearly won, when she nearly threw at Brando the distress in the form of an accusation. But she stopped. She did what she had learned: she stopped, and saw the itch, and asked herself, is this love or is it illness, is this going to unite us or divide us, and perceived that the storm, in that moment of difficulty, instead of helping them, was going to sink them, was going to add war to the disaster, and that what they needed, in that moment, was not crashing, it was peace, it was to hold to each other, it was to face the difficulty together, and not to turn it into one more reason to shout at each other.

And she did not make the storm. She crossed the itch, harder than ever, because the difficulty fed it, and instead of unloading on Brando, she did the opposite: she drew near to him. She told him, Brando, this is hard, and I am afraid, and the itch to quarrel with you is coming to me, but I do not want to quarrel, I want to be with you in this. And Brando, gentle, with his calm that now, instead of irritating her, sustained her, embraced her, and told her let us solve this together, Tranquilina, with calm, for with calm everything is solved, and the two faced the difficulty together, without a storm, and Brando's calm, which she had spent months finding lukewarm, revealed itself, in that difficulty, as what it was: a strength. An enormous strength. Because in the disaster, a calm man is a rock, and Tranquilina, who before thought Brando's calm a lack of passion, discovered, in the difficulty, that his calm was the most valuable thing a man could have, that it was on him she could lean, that his calm held her, and that if Brando were a storm-man, like Bravo, the disaster would have been double, because to the difficulty would have been added the drama, and the two would have sunk shouting.

And it was so that Tranquilina learned, in practice, in the difficulty, the greatest truth of the book: that peace is not weakness, it is strength; that a calm love is not a lukewarm love, it is a strong love, able to bear the disasters without breaking; and that the storm, which she had confused with passion, with life, with in-earnest love, was after all, in the hours that count, in the hours of disaster, the most useless and the most destructive thing, because in the disaster one does not need crashing, one needs a rock, and Brando was a rock, and Bravo would have been a flood, and she perceived, at last, which of the two was the in-earnest love and which was the fever.

The difficulty past, solved with the calm of the two, Tranquilina looked back, at those hard weeks, and perceived, with a grateful astonishment, that they had been through a disaster and that there had not been a single storm. That for the first time in her life she had crossed a difficulty without turning it into war, that she had faced a disaster with peace, and that peace not only had not sunk them, but had saved them, had united them, had made of them, in the difficulty, a team and not two enemies. And it was there, in perceiving that they had been through a disaster without a storm, that Tranquilina knew she was cured, or on the way to being so for good, because the proof of a cure is not what one does on the calm days, it is what one does on the days of disaster, and she had spent her day of disaster in peace, and the storm that the parents' house would have sent her, the one her body nearly sent, that one, for the first time in a life, did not come, because Tranquilina, at last, did not let it come, and discovered, in the storm that did not come, that she was stronger than her illness, and that peace, which she had feared her whole life, was after all the place from which the storms of life are faced, and not the lack of them.
