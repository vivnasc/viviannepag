## 10. The storm that did not come

The proof that Tranquilina was curing herself did not come on a calm night, for those she already knew how to cross; it came on a day of true difficulty, on a day that, in the parents' house, would have given the greatest of storms, and that with Brando gave another thing, and it was in that other thing that Tranquilina saw the difference between the house she had inherited and the house she was learning to make.

There came a small disaster, of the kind life brings. Brando's mother, an old woman of few words who lived in the upper village and to whom he went every Sunday, fell and broke her hip, and had to be operated on in the big village, and was months in bed and in need of care; and at the same time a great customer of Brando's, a gentleman of the city watch shops who gave him half his work, closed his doors and was left owing him three months of service, and Brando found himself suddenly with his mother ill, with an expense he had not foreseen, and with less than half the money he counted on. And for some weeks the life of the two was hard, was heavy, was of distress, of comings and goings to the big village, of sums made at the table, of short nights.

And in the house of Tranquilina's parents, a disaster like this would have been the perfect pretext for the storm. She knew exactly how it would have been, because she had seen it: the father arriving loaded with the distress and unloading it in shouts, the mother answering back, the blame flying from one to the other, it's you who, no, it's you who, and the disaster becoming, in the end, one more storm among the many, because in her house difficulty did not unite, it divided; it gave a cause for the crash. When the father had lost the work up above, that time of the chest, the house had not gathered around the distress; it had broken in it.

And Tranquilina, at the start of the difficulty, felt her body want the storm. She felt the itch, stronger than ever, because distress feeds the itch, and fear, and weariness; and there was a night, at Brando's house, with him arriving late and silent from the big village, exhausted from the mother and the sums, when Tranquilina caught herself wanting to unload on him, wanting to catch on his silence, wanting to tell him you don't even speak to me now, I count for nothing, and to make of his exhaustion a cause for war, as the mother would have done. The sentence was already in her mouth. And she stopped.

She did what she had learned: she stopped, and saw the itch, and asked herself, is this love or is it sickness, is this going to unite us or divide us, and perceived that the storm, in that moment of difficulty, instead of helping them, was going to sink them, was going to add war to the disaster, and that what they needed, in that moment, was not crashing, it was peace, it was to hold to each other. And instead of unloading, she did the opposite: she went to Brando, who was sitting at the table with his head bowed over the papers, and drew near to him, and said:

"Brando, this is hard, and you're tired, and do you know what I felt like? I felt like quarrelling with you. The itch to quarrel with you is coming to me, in the middle of all this, God knows why. But I don't want to quarrel. I want to be with you in this. Tell me what I can do."

And Brando raised his head, and looked at her, and there was in his eyes a great weariness and, beneath, a greater gratitude, because he perceived what she had just done, which was to choose not to be storm on a day when everything pushed her to it. And instead of the calm irritating him, as it had irritated her months before, it was his calm, now, that sustained her. He reached out his hand, and said:

"Sit down here. Let's look at this together, with calm. With calm everything is solved, Tranquilina. My mother will get better, and the money will sort itself out, it has to. What I couldn't bear was having to solve this and you against me. Like this, with you on this side, I can bear it."

And the two of them sat down to the papers, and made the sums, and divided what there was, and Tranquilina put in of her own, and they went to the big village together to see the old woman, and Brando's calm, which she had spent months finding lukewarm, revealed itself, in that difficulty, as what it was: a strength. An enormous strength. Because in the disaster, a calm man is a rock, and Tranquilina, who before thought Brando's calm a lack of passion, discovered, in the difficulty, that his calm was the most valuable thing a man could have, that it was on him she could lean, that his calm held her, and that if Brando were a storm-man, like Bravo, the disaster would have been double, because to the difficulty would have been added the drama, and the two would have sunk shouting.

And there was an afternoon, at the hospital of the big village, that showed her all at once the house she was learning to make. Brando's mother, that old woman of few words, was in the bed, with her operated hip, in pain, and ill-tempered, as old people become when the body betrays them; and she said to her son an unjust thing, a sour complaint, that he had put her in that hospital to be rid of her, which is what old people say when they are afraid. And Tranquilina saw Brando take that, the injustice, said by his own mother, and saw him not answer with bitterness, not take it ill, only take the old woman's hand and say, with his calm, you know it isn't true, mother, I come here every day, and I'll keep coming until you're well and scolding me at home. And the old woman fell silent, and pressed his hand. And Tranquilina, in the corner of the room, perceived where Brando's calm came from: it came from a house where the complaint was borne without returning the complaint, where the injustice was taken and not thrown back, and that was the exact opposite of her house, where everything that came in as a barb went out as a knife-thrust. And she perceived that she was learning, with that man, not only to love in peace, but to do what he did: to receive a barb and not return it, to let it fall instead of throwing it, which is the secret of all calm houses, and that she, come from a house of mirrors where each hurt is reflected and multiplied, was now, slowly, learning to be wall instead of mirror, to let the hurt strike and stay, instead of sending it back with more force.

And there was a moment, in the midst of those hard weeks, when the old sickness beckoned to her, because it is in the difficulties that it beckons most. On a night of weariness, Tranquilina caught herself thinking of Bravo, not with desire, but with a kind of crooked longing: with Bravo, she thought, all this would be higher, more felt, there would be shouting, the disaster would be lived in grand style, and there was in that, in her head, a false promise that the drama made the pain more true. And she stopped, and saw the thought head on, and perceived the deceit: that drama does not make pain more true, it only makes it noisier; that the pain of Brando's mother being ill was the same with crashing or without it, and that the crashing would only add more pain, that of the war on top of that of the disaster; and that she no longer wanted the pain in grand style, she wanted the pain shared, which is a different and better thing, the pain that is carried by two in silence and not the one shouted one against the other. And the thought of Bravo passed, as the itches pass when they are given no fuel, and Tranquilina did not even come to tell it to Brando, because she caught it early, before it turned into words, which was what he had asked of her.

And it was so that Tranquilina learned, in practice, in the difficulty, the greatest truth of the book: that peace is not weakness, it is strength; that a calm love is not a lukewarm love, it is a strong love, able to bear the disasters without breaking; and that the storm, which she had confused with passion, with life, with in-earnest love, was after all, in the hours that count, in the hours of disaster, the most useless and the most destructive thing, because in the disaster one does not need crashing, one needs a rock, and Brando was a rock, and Bravo would have been a flood, and she perceived, at last, which of the two was the in-earnest love and which was the fever.

And there was, at the end of those weeks, a night that stayed. Brando's old mother, already better, went to spend a few days at her son's house to convalesce, and Tranquilina, who barely knew her, found herself caring for her, making her bed, giving her her soup, and she was afraid, because ill-tempered old people frightened her, reminded her of the father of the hearth. And one afternoon the old woman, from the bed, said a thing to her, in that dry way of hers: my boy had luck with you, you know? I was afraid he'd fall in with one who'd wear him out, for he's too mild, and the mild ones either fall into good hands or let themselves be worn to the bone. You, at the start, I don't know what you were doing to him, for he went about a while with a face I didn't know on him. But now no. Now he goes about in peace. And one sees, in a son's face, whether his woman gives him peace or war. And Tranquilina heard that, from the mouth of the mother of the man she had nearly worn out, and it was to her at once a shame and a forgiveness, because the old woman knew, in her way, what had happened, and even so told her now he goes about in peace, and it was the greatest thing they could tell her, that she gave peace to a man, she who had been born to give storm, and who was, at last, learning the trade opposite to the one the house had trained her for.

The difficulty past, the old woman on her feet with her stick and the money coming in again, Tranquilina looked back, at those hard weeks, and perceived, with a grateful astonishment, that they had been through a disaster and that there had not been a single storm. That for the first time in her life she had crossed a difficulty without turning it into war, that she had faced a disaster with peace, and that peace not only had not sunk them, but had saved them, had united them, had made of them, in the difficulty, a team and not two enemies. And she remembered the house of the Ladeira, and the winter when the father fell ill, and the broken house, and perceived that she had just done, with Brando, the exact opposite of what the parents had done: they had given her the disaster that divides, and she had just lived the disaster that unites.

And it was there, in perceiving that they had been through a disaster without a storm, that Tranquilina knew she was cured, or on the way to being so for good, because the proof of a cure is not what one does on the calm days, it is what one does on the days of disaster, and she had spent her day of disaster in peace, and the storm that the parents' house would have sent her, the one her body nearly sent, that one, for the first time in a life, did not come, because Tranquilina, at last, did not let it come, and discovered, in the storm that did not come, that she was stronger than her sickness, and that peace, which she had feared her whole life, was after all the place from which the storms of life are faced, and not the lack of them.
