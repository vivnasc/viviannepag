## 9. Who am I without the weight

There came then the strangest time of Perpétua's life, stranger than that of the fourteen years in which she learned to carry. Because at fourteen she at least knew what she was, she was the one who held the house; and now, at fifty-two, with the house holding itself up without her, she did not know what she was. And she discovered that not to know what one is, when one has spent a life being it, is an orphanhood worse than that of fourteen, because at fourteen she had been orphaned of her mother, and now she was orphaned of herself.

Because the weight, Perpétua discovered, was not only weight. The weight was also, without her ever having noticed, what gave her her place, her name, her reason to get up in the morning. When she carried, she knew what she was for: she was for holding the house. Every morning had a meaning, which was the weight of that day, the old ones, the papers, the decisions, the mouths; and Perpétua got up and knew what she was going to, and at the end of the day, however tired she was, she could say to herself I was needed, I was wanted, I held. And now, with the siblings carrying their part, with the house reorganised, the uncle buried, Perpétua got up in the morning and, for the first time in her life, did not know what she was going to, because there was no weight waiting for her, and the day opened before her empty. And the emptiness, which she had thought would be rest, was after all anguish, because it was a day in which she was not needed, and a woman who grew used to being needed does not know how to be in a day in which she is not.

And it was this that no one had told her, neither the doctor nor the Keeper, who had told her set down, but had not told her what comes after setting down, which is the hardest: the emptiness of the place the weight occupied. Because a column does not have only the weight on top; it has also, around the weight, the identity of the one who carries it, and when it sets the weight down, it sets down too, without meaning to, the identity, and is left not knowing who it is, because it was the weight that told it who it was. And Perpétua, free of the weight, instead of light, felt herself lost, felt an enormous lack, and the lack was the lack of the very thing that was killing her, which is the most treacherous of all, to miss the weight that was cracking us, to want to take it up again not because the house needs it, but because without it we do not know who we are.

And there was a Sunday, in that time, that gave her the measure of the emptiness. It was a Sunday like the ones that used to fill her with tasks, and Perpétua woke, and there was nothing to do. No old one to change, for the uncle had died and the aunt had gone to Felismina's house for a while. No feast to prepare. No sibling needing her. She stayed in bed longer than she had ever stayed, not out of idleness, out of not knowing what to do with a Sunday that was only hers. She got up, and walked through the mother-house, the house where there had always been people needing her, now silent, and the silence, which ought to be peace, was a haunting, was the lack of all the voices that had always called her. She stood at the window, and did not know how to stand at the window. She took up a piece of sewing, and put it down. And she felt, in the midst of that empty Sunday, a thing that shamed her: she felt a longing to be needed. A longing for the voices that called her, oh Perpétua, oh sister. A longing even for the weight. And she understood, with horror, that she preferred the weight to the silence, that she was capable of going to fetch the weight only to flee that Sunday; and that it was that, and not the tiredness of the body, the deepest addiction of the column, the need to be needed, the not knowing how to exist except by being called.

And there were days, in that time, on which Perpétua almost took up the weight again, no longer because the house creaked, for the house no longer creaked, but because she herself could not bear up in the emptiness. She went to Felismina's house to see whether they needed her. She telephoned Belmiro inventing reasons to see to things. She offered to carry what was no longer hers to carry. And she perceived, with shame, that it was not out of love for the family that she did it, it was out of not knowing how to be without the weight, it was because the emptiness was unbearable to her, it was that she was trying to take up the column again not to hold the house, but to hold herself, to know again who she was. And the siblings, already grown, already carrying their part, began to say to her, with affection, oh sister, we have already seen to that, rest; and the rest, which ought to be a relief, was a knife, because it was the confirmation that they no longer had need of her, that her place in the house, that of column, had ceased to exist, and that she was left without a place.

One afternoon, in the emptiness, Perpétua tried to remember who she was before. Before the February dawn, before the column, when she was only the girl Perpétua, the best in the class, the one the teacher said could go far. She tried to remember what she liked at thirteen, what she dreamed of being, what made her laugh, and it was like searching for a person in a faded photograph: there was a girl there, but Perpétua could hardly see her face any more. She remembered vaguely liking to sing, a wish to learn, a curiosity about the world out beyond the mountain; but all that had stayed on the far side of February, in a life she had not got to live, and the girl who liked those things had been buried so early, and by so much weight, and for so long, that Perpétua no longer knew whether she still existed beneath the column, or whether the column had crushed her for good. And it was that, perhaps, the saddest thing of the emptiness: not only not to know who she was now, but no longer even to be able to find who she had been before, the girl left unlived whom the weight had buried at fourteen, and whom she would have, now, at fifty-two, to try to dig up, without even knowing whether she was still there.

And the village, which had always called her the saint, began to call her another thing, or to call her nothing. Before, in the square, on the way out of mass, they would still say it is a good thing we have Perpétua, that one bears everything, she is a saint; and the title, for all that it was a sentence, was also a place, was being someone in the eyes of the village. And now, with Perpétua sharing out the weight, with the family seeing to everything, the village no longer had reason to say it, and the it is a good thing we have Perpétua was heard less and less, and Perpétua, who ought to have rejoiced to cease being the village's martyr, found herself, with shame, longing for the title. Because the saint, at least, was someone; and the woman who had shared out the weight and had time for herself was no one in the eyes of a village that knows only how to praise those who spend themselves. Perpétua discovered that the village does not praise one who learns to live, it praises one who sacrifices himself; that a woman who sets the weight down the village does not call free, it calls selfish, or worse, it calls nothing, it forgets her; and that the price of setting the weight down was also to lose the applause of a village that applauds only columns, and to have to learn to be worth in her own eyes without the praise of others, which was a thing that she, used to living off being needed, did not yet know how to do.

There was an afternoon that was the image of all this. Perpétua went to Felismina's house, with a pretext, to take a thing, but in truth to see whether they needed her, to find a way to be useful. And she arrived, and the house was seen to, the supper made, the children settled, everything going, without her. And Felismina, contented, said, oh sister, why have you come, all is well, there was no need, sit down, rest. And Perpétua stood in the middle of her sister's kitchen, not knowing what to do with her hands, with the hands that had always known what to do in any kitchen, and that there, in that kitchen seen to, had nothing to hold, nothing to solve, nothing to carry. And it was an emptiness in her hands before it was an emptiness in her soul, because her hands, hands of forty years of chores, did not know how to stay still, and stayed there, hanging, useless, in search of a weight that was no longer there. She sat, because her sister bade her, and drank a coffee she did not want, and went away early, and on the way back understood, with a deep sadness, that she had gone to her sister's house not to give anything, but to receive a place, and that her sister, without malice, had refused it to her, with a sit down, rest, that was the most loving thing in the world and that Perpétua, in that time, did not know how to receive, because it took from her the only thing she knew how to be, the needed one.

And it was here, in this emptiness, that Perpétua touched the bottom of the book, which was a hard truth. That she had spent her life carrying the others, in part out of love, yes, but in part also out of fear, the fear of stopping and having to meet herself. That the weight, besides killing her, had spared her a thing she feared more than the weight, which was herself, the question of who am I, what do I want, what would I make of my life if it were only mine. While she carried, she never had to ask that question, because the weight filled her days and spared her the meeting with herself. And to set the weight down was, for that reason, more frightening than to carry it, because carrying kept her far from herself, and setting down put her, for the first time in forty years, before the stranger that she was, before the Perpétua left unlived beneath the column, whom she did not know, and was afraid to discover.

And even sleeping became hard, for a reason the opposite of before. Before, Perpétua fell asleep organising the next day in her head, the uncle at such an hour, the papers, the food, the errands, and the list of tomorrow's obligations was the prayer that made her fall asleep, because a head full of duties has no space for anything else. Now, lying down, she would go to organise the next day and there was no next day to organise: there was no uncle, there was no weight, there were no obligations waiting for her. And the head, used to falling asleep making lists, was left without lists, and instead of quieting, grew agitated, because the place the obligations occupied filled now with the question, and the question did not let her sleep. Perpétua discovered that it was easier to fall asleep tired and full of duties than rested and empty of them, and that the emptiness, which she had thought would be the rest, took from her even her sleep, because her sleep, for forty years, had been the sleep of exhaustion, and the exhaustion now was missing to her, and with it the sleep.

Who am I without the weight, Perpétua asked herself, lying at night, with her body still aching from the forty years, but without the weight of tomorrow waiting for her. And she had no answer. She knew who she was holding the house: she was Perpétua, the strong, the trusty, the one who bears. But without that, she did not know. She did not know what she liked, because she had never had time to like anything. She did not know what she wanted, because she had never asked herself, she had always gone about answering what the others needed. She did not even know what she would do with a free afternoon, because she had never had one; and now that she had them, she stood at the window not knowing how to be in them, like one who holds in her hands a thing she always wished for and discovers she does not know how to use it.

And she understood that this was the true crossing, the one beneath all the others, the one the weight had hidden from her. It was not to learn to set down, for that she had already learned. It was to learn to live after setting down. It was to find herself. It was to discover, after a life of being for the others, who she was for herself. And that this crossing had no Keeper to make it for her, nor doctor to give her the truth ready-made; this one she had to make alone, in the emptiness, without the weight to spare her from herself. And it was the hardest thing they had ever asked of her, harder than holding the house at fourteen, because holding the house she knew how to do, and living for herself she did not, no one had ever taught her, she had never had anyone to learn it with. And Perpétua, at the bottom of that emptiness, afraid, but now without the weight to flee from herself, understood that she had, at last, at fifty-two, to begin from the beginning, like a girl, to learn the thing everyone learns young and that she had never learned: to be a person, and not a column.
