## 9. Who am I without the weight

There came then the strangest time of Perpétua's life, stranger than that of the fourteen years in which she learned to carry, because at fourteen she at least knew what she was, she was the one who held the house, and now, at fifty-two, with the house holding itself up without her, she did not know what she was, and she discovered that not to know what one is, when one has spent a life being it, is a form of orphanhood worse than that of fourteen, because at fourteen she was orphaned of her mother and now she was orphaned of herself.

Because the weight, Perpétua discovered, was not only weight. The weight was also, without her ever having noticed, what gave her her place, her name, her reason to get up in the morning. When she carried, she knew what she was for: she was for holding the house. Every morning had a meaning, which was the weight of that day, the old ones, the papers, the decisions, the mouths, and Perpétua got up and knew what she was going to, and at the end of the day, however tired she was, she could say to herself I was needed, I was wanted, I held. And now, with the siblings carrying their part, with the house reorganised, Perpétua got up in the morning and, for the first time in her life, did not know what she was going to, because there was no weight waiting for her, and the day opened before her empty, and the emptiness, which she had thought would be rest, was after all anguish, because it was a day in which she was not needed there, and a woman who grew used to being needed does not know how to be in a day in which she is not.

And it was this that no one had told her, neither the outside doctor nor the Keeper, who had told her set down, but had not told her what comes after setting down, which is the hardest: the emptiness of the place the weight occupied. Because a column does not have only the weight on top; it has also, around the weight, the identity of the one who carries it, and when it sets the weight down, it sets down too, without meaning to, the identity, and is left not knowing who it is, because it was the weight that told it who it was. And Perpétua, free of the weight, instead of light, felt herself lost, felt an enormous lack, and the lack was the lack of the very thing that was killing her, which is the most treacherous of all, to miss the weight that was cracking us, to want to take it up again not because the house needs it, but because without it we do not know who we are.

And there was a Sunday, in that time, that gave her the measure of the emptiness. It was a Sunday like the ones that used to fill her with tasks, and Perpétua woke, and there was nothing to do, no old one to see to, no feast to prepare, no sibling needing her, and she stayed in bed longer than she had ever stayed, not out of idleness, out of not knowing what to do with a Sunday that was only hers; and she rose, and walked through the mother-house, the house where there had always been people needing her, now silent, and the silence, which ought to be peace, was a haunting, was the lack of all the voices that had always called her, and Perpétua, in the midst of that silence, felt a thing that shamed her: she felt a longing to be needed, a longing for the voices that called her, a longing even for the weight, and she understood, with horror, that she preferred the weight to the silence, that she was capable of going to fetch the weight only to flee that empty Sunday, and that it was that, and not the tiredness of the body, the deepest addiction of the column, the need to be needed, the not knowing how to exist except by being called, the fear of the silence in which, with no one needing her, she had at last to meet herself.

Who am I without the weight, Perpétua asked herself, lying awake at night, with her body still aching from the forty years, but without the weight of tomorrow waiting for her. And she had no answer. She knew who she was holding the house: she was Perpétua, the strong, the trusty, the one who bears. But without that, she did not know. She did not know what she liked, because she had never had time to like anything. She did not know what she wanted, because she had never asked herself, she had always gone about answering what the others needed. She did not even know what she would do with a free afternoon, because she had never had one, and now that she had them, she stood at the window not knowing how to be in them, like one who holds in her hands a thing she always wished for and discovers she does not know how to use it.

And she understood that this was the true crossing of the book, the one beneath all the others, the one the weight had hidden from her: it was not to learn to set down, for that she had already learned; it was to learn to live after setting down, it was to find herself, it was to discover, after a life of being for the others, who she was for herself. And that this crossing had no Keeper to make it for her, nor doctor to give her the truth ready-made; this one she had to make alone, in the emptiness, without the weight to spare her from herself, and it was the hardest thing they had ever asked of her, harder than holding the house at fourteen, because holding the house she knew how to do, and living for herself she did not, no one had ever taught her, she had never had anyone to learn it with. And Perpétua, at the bottom of that emptiness, afraid, but now without the weight to flee from herself, understood that she had, at last, at fifty-two, to begin from the beginning, like a girl, to learn the thing everyone learns young and that she had never learned: to be a person, and not a column.
