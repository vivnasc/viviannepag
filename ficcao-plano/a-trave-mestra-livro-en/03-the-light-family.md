## 3. The light family

Perpétua's siblings were light, and their lightness was a fine thing to see, and it was her weight, and Perpétua took fifty-two years not to do the sum that the lightness of some is always, somewhere, the weight of another.

Belmiro, from the city, telephoned on Sundays, and the call was almost always the same. He complained. He complained of the work, of the traffic, of the bills, of the tiredness, of those ordinary troubles of one who has an ordinary life, and Perpétua listened to him, and agreed with him, and consoled him, oh Belmiro, that passes, take it easy. And Belmiro hung up relieved, and never, in years of Sundays, asked his sister how she was, she who carried, alone, the old ones and the house and everyone's crises; because to Belmiro, as to all of them, Perpétua had no problems, Perpétua was the one who solved the problems, and the one who solves the problems is asked by no one about her own.

Felismina, from the next village, came to the House of the Mill to rest from her troubles, which were small, and she rested well, because at the House of the Mill there was Perpétua to see to everything. She arrived with her grievance of the week, a falling-out with the husband, a vexation, and Perpétua listened to her at night, in the kitchen, and sheltered her, and Felismina went away relieved, with her little problem tipped into her sister, and never noticed that she tipped it into a woman who carried everyone's great problems, nor did it occur to her to ask, and you, sister, and yours, into whom do you tip them?

One Saturday, Felismina would come with a bag of clothes for Perpétua to sew, and settle in the kitchen, and begin to tip out the week. My man this, my neighbour that, my son does not study, I am so tired, sister, you do not know what it is. And Perpétua, who knew very well what it was to go about tired, who went about tired in a way Felismina could not imagine, listened to her, and agreed with her, and consoled her, and set the coffee before her, and the cake, and sewed her clothes while she listened, and Felismina talked, talked, unburdened herself, and at the end of the afternoon got up another woman, light, rested, with her clothes sewn and her soul tipped out, and said, oh sister, how well I always feel when I come here, with you is where I rest. And she went away, and Perpétua was left with the kitchen to tidy, and with Felismina's problems still ringing in her head on top of her own, and with the sewn clothes that were not hers, and never, in years of Saturdays, did Felismina ask her, and you, sister, where do you rest? Because the mother-house was the place where the others went to rest, and no one thinks of the one who holds up the place where the others rest, as no one thinks of the mattress on which one sleeps.

And Tó, the youngest, he was the masterpiece of the damage. At forty he still turned to Perpétua for everything, with the naturalness of a child, because Perpétua had been his mother since the months of his age. When he got himself into a blunder, it was Perpétua he called, and Perpétua got him out of it. When money ran short, Perpétua gave it. When he needed advice, or a face to tell him do not worry, Perpétua was there. And Tó had never grown up, because no one grows up while there is someone to carry him, and Perpétua had carried him forty years, with love, and with that love had kept him a child.

One night, Tó telephoned in tears, at forty years old, like a child. He had got himself into a debt, into an ill-thought-out thing, and was in distress, and called Perpétua, who was the one called when the world fell. Perpétua, I have done something foolish, I do not know what to do, I owe money to people who do not play games, you have to help me, you always know what to do. And Perpétua, at one in the morning, with the whole day on her back, set herself to thinking how she would get him out of it, and got him out, as she had always got him out, gave the money, spoke to whoever was needed, undid the mess, and Tó, saved, fell asleep at ease, with his sister seeing to his damage; and Perpétua was the one who did not sleep, thinking from where she would take the money she had given. And it never occurred to her, nor to Tó, that to save him was to keep him a child, that each time she got him out of it she took from him the chance to learn not to get into it, that the love with which she saved him was the same love with which she kept him from growing. Tó was forty years old and had the age of a child, because a child is someone they have never let bear the consequences of his acts, and Perpétua, by catching them all, had kept her brother a child for forty years without knowing it.

And the three loved Perpétua, in their way, and were grateful to her, and said so. But their love and their gratitude had a blindness, and the blindness was this: they did not see the weight. They saw Perpétua strong, capable, always available, and took that for her nature, for temperament, for vocation, as though she liked to carry, as though she were happy doing it, as though it did not cost her. And by not seeing the weight, they carried her without guilt, without measure, without noticing they bent her, because a beam that does not complain looks like a beam that does not suffer, and the siblings, seeing her bear without a single complaint, concluded that bearing did not cost her, and carried her more.

There was a summer, some years before, that Perpétua never forgot, and that was the image of the whole thing. The siblings arranged among themselves a few days at the beach, the three of them, with their families, a rented house, and no one asked her in earnest, because it was obvious Perpétua could not go, that she had the old ones, that someone had to stay; and Perpétua said go, go, enjoy yourselves, I will stay here with the old ones, and she stayed. She stayed that whole August at the House of the Mill, with the heat, with the aunt who could not govern herself, with the bedridden uncle, changing him in the heat, making food, alone; and at the end of the week Belmiro sent her by telephone some photographs, the three siblings at the beach, laughing, the children in the water, with a caption, we miss you, sister, next year you come with us. And Perpétua looked at the photographs in the hot kitchen, with the uncle calling from the room, and felt no envy, for envy was not in her nature; she felt a thing deeper and sadder, she felt that life happened to the others and passed her by, that the siblings had summers and she had Augusts tending old ones, and that the next year you come with us was a sentence said every year and that never, in twenty years, was fulfilled, because there was never anyone to stay with the old ones but her, and while she stayed, no one needed to find anyone to stay.

And the deepest thing is that, when they offered her help, Perpétua was the first to refuse it. A neighbour, seeing the House of the Mill so full of people in the charge of one woman alone, offered one day, oh Perpétua, would you like me to come and keep the uncle company an afternoon, so you can breathe? And Perpétua, instead of accepting the afternoon of rest that fell to her from heaven, said what she always said, oh no, thank you, do not trouble yourself, I can manage, I will see to it. I will see to it. It was the sentence of her life, said out of pride disguised as courtesy, and it was that sentence, more than the siblings, that had put her alone beneath the weight, because a woman who always says I can manage teaches everyone around her to leave her to manage alone. The siblings leaned on her, yes; but it was she who never truly let them carry, who refused the neighbour's hand, who said I will see to it to whoever offered, because to admit she needed help was, for her pride, harder than to carry the whole weight. And she understood, much later, that her loneliness had not been only the work of the others who leaned on her, it had been also her own work, who let no one come near to relieve her, who held the weight with both hands and even slapped the hand of whoever wished to take it from her.

There was an occasion, that winter, that showed her everything at once, and that opened, beneath, a new thing.

Uncle Aníbal grew worse. The doctor came, and said it was necessary to decide, that the man needed more care than could be given him at home, that one had to think of a place, or of paid people, or of proper turns, and that money was needed and arms were needed and a decision was needed, of the kind that weighs. And Perpétua, as always, called the siblings. Not to ask them to carry, for that she had already given up asking; to inform them, so they would know, because it was their uncle in question.

They came. Belmiro on purpose from the city, Felismina from the next village, Tó who was already there. They sat at the kitchen table, the same at which, in childhood, Perpétua had warmed the milk for Tó, and Perpétua set out the matter, the care, the money, the options. And the siblings listened, serious, worried, and when she finished, a silence fell, and then Belmiro said what is said in such cases in a family with a beam:

"Oh, Perpétua, but you are the one who knows about that. You are the one who is here, you are the one who knows the man, you have the knack for these things. Do as you think best, for we trust you."

"Just so," said Felismina. "You decide, sister, and then you tell us what is needed, and we will help with what we can."

And Tó said nothing, for Tó never said anything at such times, he sat looking at the table waiting for the conversation to end.

And the we will help with what we can was the formula with which they had always given her back the weight, seeming to lend a hand and in truth leaning again on the column. They got up, kissed her, oh sister, you are a saint, I do not know what would become of this family without you, and went away light, Belmiro to the city, Felismina to her village, leaving Perpétua at the kitchen table with the great decision, and the great care, and the great money, and the uncle dying in the back room, all of it set on her back, with a kiss on top.

And Perpétua, that time, instead of accepting as she had always accepted, felt, beneath the acceptance, a new thing, a thing she never let herself feel. She felt anger. A small anger, dull, ashamed, the anger of one who carries everything and to whom they say you are the one who knows and go away light. She sat at the table, alone, with the coffee cups still to clear, and the anger rose in her, and she was frightened of it, because anger was not in the nature of a column, and she pushed it away, as she pushed everything away. But the anger, once felt, did not go altogether. It stayed, beneath, dripping.

And it revealed to her, slowly, a thing she had never admitted: that she did not carry the family only out of love. She carried it also out of a kind of pride. The pride of being the column, the important one, the one who can, the indispensable. The pride of being the saint the village praised, the one without whom the family did not go on. And that pride, which gave her her place, was also what kept her from setting down, because to set down would be to come down from the place of the one who bears everything to the place of one who also needs; and Perpétua had so much pride in being the one who bears that she would sooner break than admit that she too could not bear. And she understood that the prison of pride was as strong as that of love, perhaps stronger, because love one lets go of when one sees that it does harm, but pride clings on to the end.

That night, alone, after clearing the cups, Perpétua looked at her own hands, set on the table. The hands that had held the house for forty years, since the night they warmed the milk for Tó. They were old, marked, with the thick knuckles, with an old scar from the press. And she thought that never, in forty years, had those hands held a thing that was only hers, a liking, a dream, a life. They had always held the others, and they would, if she did not change, hold the others until they cracked, and die full of everyone's weight and empty of anything that was hers.

And it was the first time Perpétua felt pity for herself. Not the pity that softens, the other, the one that frees, the pity of one who sees a whole life given to holding a house that never asked her how she was. And she understood that she was in a dead end: that she could not go on carrying everything, because her body no longer let her, because she was going to crack; but that she did not know how to stop, because to stop was to let go of the place, was to come down from the pride, was to discover herself a stranger; and that the siblings were not going to help her stop, because their lightness depended on her weight, and they, without malice, needed her to go on being the column so they could go on being light. If she wished to set the weight down, she was going to have to do it alone, against the will of the siblings, against her own pride, against forty years of habit; and it was going to be the hardest thing of her life, harder than carrying, because carrying was to go on being who she was, and setting down was to risk ceasing to be it, and no one lets go without fear of the only place she has known since the age of fourteen, not even when that place is killing her.
