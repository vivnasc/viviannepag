## 3. The light family

Perpétua's siblings were light, and their lightness was a fine thing to see, and it was her weight, and Perpétua took fifty-two years not to do the sum that the lightness of some is always the weight of another, somewhere.

There were three of them, the siblings, and they had their lives, and had them light: one lived in the city, with a job and some children and some travels, and came to the village for the feasts, and complained of his little things, the tiredness of work, the bills, those ordinary troubles of one who has an ordinary life; the other had married into a neighbouring village, and had her house, and her small problems, and came to the House of the Mill when she needed to rest from her small problems, and rested, because in the House of the Mill there was Perpétua to see to everything; and the third, the youngest, had always been the spoiled one, the one who had never taken up any weight, and who at forty still turned to Perpétua for everything, for money, for advice, for the untangling of his blunders, with the naturalness of one who turns to a mother.

And the three loved Perpétua, in their way, and were grateful to her, and said so, but their love and their gratitude had a blindness, and the blindness was this: they did not see the weight. They saw Perpétua strong, capable, always available, always holding everything, and took that for her nature, for temperament, for vocation, as though Perpétua liked to carry, as though she were happy doing it, as though it did not cost her; and by not seeing the weight, they carried her without guilt, asked of her without measure, leaned on her without noticing they were bending her, because a beam that does not complain looks like a beam that does not suffer, and Perpétua's siblings, seeing her bear without a single complaint, concluded that bearing did not cost her, and carried her more.

And there was, in the lightness of the siblings, an injustice no one saw, neither they nor Perpétua, and which was the true wound of the book: the siblings could have their small problems, could complain of their little things, could live their lives with the ordinary troubles, precisely because Perpétua took the great problems from them. The old ones, the bedridden uncle, the aunt going slowly mad, the ends of the month of those who fell short, the real crises, those Perpétua absorbed, all of them, and the siblings were left only with the little things, and complained of the little things to Perpétua, to the woman who carried all their great problems, without seeing the absurdity of complaining of their small troubles to the one who carried their great ones, and Perpétua heard their complaints of the little things, and consoled them, and did not tell them the truth, which was and I, who carry what truly weighs on you, to whom do I complain?, because a column does not complain, and Perpétua swallowed, and carried, and the siblings went away light, consoled of their little things by the woman whom they never asked about her great things.

There was, in that time, an occasion that showed it to her in a way that wounded her. The bedridden uncle grew worse, and it was necessary to decide what to do, and it was a great thing, of the kind that weighs, money, care, hard decisions, and Perpétua, as always, called the siblings, not to ask them to carry, for that she had already given up asking, but to inform them, so that they would know; and the siblings came, and heard, and said what they always said, oh Perpétua, you are the one who knows, you have the knack for that, do as you think best, we trust you, and went away, light, leaving Perpétua with the great decision and the great care and the great weight, and Perpétua, that time, instead of accepting as she had always accepted, felt, beneath the acceptance, a new thing, a thing she never let herself feel: she felt anger. A small anger, dull, ashamed, the anger of one who carries everything and to whom they say you are the one who knows and go away light, the anger of a beam that, for an instant, felt the weight and wanted to throw it onto those who ought to share it.

And she was frightened by the anger, because anger was not in the nature of a column, and she pushed it away, as she pushed everything away, but the anger, once felt, did not go altogether, it stayed, beneath, and revealed to her, slowly, a thing she had never admitted: that she did not carry the family only out of love, she carried it also out of a kind of pride, the pride of being the column, the important one, the one who can, the indispensable; and that this pride, which gave her her place, was also what kept her from setting down, because to set down would be to come down from the place of the one who bears everything to the place of one who also needs, and Perpétua had so much pride in being the one who bears that she would sooner break than admit that she too could not bear, and that this, the pride's, was a prison as strong as the love's, perhaps stronger, because love one lets go of when one sees it does harm, but pride clings on to the end.

And she understood, in those days, with the anger beneath and the tiredness above, that she was in a dead end: that she could not go on carrying everything, that her body no longer let her, that she was going to crack; and that she did not know how to stop, because to stop was to let go of the place, was to come down from the pride, was to discover herself a stranger, and the siblings were not going to help her stop, because their lightness depended on her weight, and they, without malice, but without wanting to see it, needed her to go on being the column, so that they could go on being light. And Perpétua understood that if she wanted to set the weight down, she would have to do it alone, against the will of the siblings, against her own pride, against forty years of habit, and that it was going to be the hardest thing of her life, harder than carrying, because carrying was going on being who she was, and setting down was risking ceasing to be it, and no one lets go without fear of the only place she has known since the age of fourteen, not even when that place is killing her.

At night, alone in the mother-house, Perpétua looked at her own hands, the hands that had held the house for forty years, and they were old, tired, marked, and she thought that never, in forty years, had those hands held a thing that was only hers, a liking, a dream, a life; they had always held the others, the house, the weight, and they would, if she did not change, hold the others until they cracked, and die full of everyone's weight and empty of anything that was hers. And it was the first time Perpétua felt pity for herself, not the pity that softens, the other, the one that frees, the pity of one who sees a whole life given to holding a house that never asked her how she was, and it was that pity, born of the dull anger, that was the beginning of her crossing, because a column only sets the weight down when, at last, she pities herself enough to prefer the risk of falling to the torment of going on holding everything alone.
