## 1. The column

Perpétua was the column of the House of the Mill, and the column has this about it: that the whole house leans on it, and no one notices it, because a column is only noticed when it cracks.

It was she who was called when there was a trouble, and there was always a trouble, for in a large family there is always someone in difficulty. It was she the brother telephoned when the money ran short at the end of the month; it was to her the sister-in-law brought the children when she needed to go out; it was to her the old ones of the house were handed, the aunt who could no longer govern herself, the bedridden uncle, because Perpétua is the one who knows, Perpétua is the one with the knack, Perpétua is the one who can; it was to her the feasts fell, the Christmases, the weddings, the funerals, the full house, the long table laid and cleared by her, because the mother-house was hers, and it is in the mother-house that the family gathers, and whoever has the mother-house has its labour. And Perpétua did everything, and did it well, and did it without complaint, with a quiet competence the whole family took for granted, as one takes for granted the beam that holds the roof: no one thanks it, no one asks it how it is, because a beam does not tire, a beam is there, and the house rests on it without thinking twice.

And Perpétua let herself be rested upon. She not only let it, she sought it, because being the column was, for her, what she was, it was her place in the world, it was the way she knew how to exist. If a Sunday passed without anyone needing her, she felt strange, almost useless, and found a way to be needed, telephoned to ask if all was well, offered to see to something, went to look in on the old ones, because to be holding the house was the way Perpétua felt herself someone, and a day on which the house did not need her was a day on which she did not quite know who she was. The others leaned on her out of convenience; she let herself be leaned on out of need, the need to be the column, because to take the weight from her was to take her place, and Perpétua did not know how to live without the place.

The family, seeing from the outside, thought Perpétua a saint, a woman of a rare fibre, and said so: it is a good thing we have Perpétua, what would become of this family without Perpétua, she is the one who bears everything. And they said it with gratitude, but it was a gratitude that did not see what it cost, that took Perpétua's strength as inexhaustible, as though she were truly made of timber and not of flesh, and that therefore, without malice, kept loading the beam more and more, because the beam bore it, and what bears, one loads further, and no one in the House of the Mill reckoned what Perpétua carried, because the reckoning was invisible, made in pieces, a request here, a crisis there, an old one beyond, and each piece seemed little, and the sum of all the pieces was a woman of fifty-two holding a whole family alone.

And the siblings, those, lived light. And it was that, more than the weight, that was beginning to hurt Perpétua without her yet admitting it. Because the siblings could live light precisely because she was the column: they did not have to worry about the old ones, for Perpétua saw to them; they did not have to worry about the feasts, for Perpétua made them; they did not have to run to one another's crises, for Perpétua ran to them; and so the siblings made their lives, travelled, rested, complained of their little things, settled in the lightness the column paid for, without seeing, or without wanting to see, that their lightness was her weight, that each thing they were free of was a thing she carried, and that the whole family went light on the back of a woman who went more and more bent.

In that time, Perpétua had begun to feel her body. She did not say it to anyone, for to say it would be to complain, and complaining was not in the nature of a column; but she felt, for some months, a pain in her back that did not pass, a tiredness sleep did not take away, a shortness of breath climbing the stairs, signs she pushed aside, put off, excused with age, with work, with anything but the truth, which was that her body, after a life of holding the house, was beginning to give way, as the timber gives way that has borne too much weight for too long, slowly, with a creak no one yet heard but that she already felt, within.

There was a Sunday, at the start of the book, that showed it to her. It was a Christmas, and the mother-house was full, and Perpétua had spent days preparing everything, alone, the food for thirty people, the house, the old ones dressed and seated, the children, everything, and in the middle of the meal, with the long table full of the family laughing and eating, Perpétua, on her feet, serving, felt the world turn, a shortness of breath, a pain in her chest, and had to lean against the wall so as not to fall, and stood there, leaning, looking at the full table of the family she sustained, and no one saw her, no one noticed that the column was leaning against the wall trying not to fall, because no one looks at the beam, all of them were looking at the table, at the feast, at the food she had made, and Perpétua, leaning against the wall, with the full table of the light family before her, had, for the first time, a thought that frightened her: and if I fall? And if I fall, now, here, who holds all this?

And what frightened her was not that she might fall; it was the answer she did not have to the question of who would hold the house if she fell. Because Perpétua understood, leaning against the wall, that the whole family rested on her, and that if she fell there was no other column, there was no one prepared to hold, because she herself, her whole life, by holding everything, had taken from the others the need and the practice of holding anything at all, and that the House of the Mill was a house with a single column, and that a house with a single column is a house that falls whole on the day the column cracks, and that she, by being the main beam, by letting no one share the weight, had made the family dependent on her in a way that was not strength, it was fragility, the fragility of a house that rests wholly on a single old timber that was beginning, in silence, to creak.

The dizziness passed, and Perpétua straightened, and went back to serving, because the feast could not stop, because the column does not fall in front of the house, and no one knew what had happened; but on that Christmas something changed in Perpétua, because the thought, once made, did not unmake itself: and if I fall, who holds this? And the question, which seemed to be about the family, was after all about her, it was the first chink of a larger question the book was to open in her, the question of what it was for, to be the column, if the price was to go bent and alone holding a house that did not see her, and of who Perpétua would be, of what would remain of her, if one day, instead of cracking, she had the courage to do the thing a beam never does and that she had never done: to set down.

At night, after the table was cleared, the dishes washed, the old ones put to bed, gone home the last light and grateful brother, Perpétua stayed alone in the mother-house, in the kitchen, with the tiredness of fifty-two years upon her, and sat down, at last, for it was one of the few times she sat, and stayed looking at the empty house, the house she held, and felt a thing she never let herself feel, beneath the tiredness, a thing she always pushed away: she felt alone. Alone in a deep way, the loneliness of the column, the loneliness of one who holds everyone and whom no one holds, and made, for the first time, without meaning to, the question that was to change the book and her life: and me, who holds me? Everyone leans on me. And I, on whom do I lean? And she did not know how to answer, because the answer, she saw it with a tightening, was on no one, was the cold wall of an empty kitchen at the end of a feast, and she went to bed with the question, and it was the first night, in many years, on which the tiredness did not bring her sleep, because beneath the tiredness there was now, awake, the question of who holds the column, and the column, Perpétua discovered that night, had no one, held herself, and was running out of strength to do it.
