## 7. The fear that the house will fall

Perpétua's greatest trial was not setting down the first weight, for that she set down with the doctor and the Keeper fresh in her head; it was resisting taking it up again when the house creaked, when it seemed it would fall, when the fear that everything would fall pushed her, with all the force of forty years, to go back to being the column.

Because a house that was always shored on a single column, when that column sets down, creaks, and the creaking frightens, and it seems it will fall. And that is what happened in the House of the Mill. The siblings, set to carry their part, carried it badly at first, as one carries what one never learned. Belmiro, with the papers, fell behind, missed a deadline for something of the uncle's, made a muddle that took work to undo. Felismina neglected a turn, did not appear one night when she should have, and uncle Aníbal passed a worse night, alone, and the following morning was low. And Tó, set to see to a thing, saw to it in his own way, badly, and there was a small misfortune, nothing grave, but enough for the house to creak, for it to seem that without Perpétua holding everything things came undone, to give reason, in appearance, to Perpétua's fear that the house did not hold up without her.

And the fear rose, and it was terrible, because it was the deep fear, that if she set everything down everything would fall. Each failure of the siblings was like a proof that she had been right, that only she could hold the house, that the siblings were not capable, that setting down had been a mistake, and the house was going to fall, and the fault would be hers, for having set down. And the column in Perpétua, the old one, that of the fourteen years, cried out to her take up the weight again, you see that without you it falls, you see that only you can, go back to being the column, save the house, that was your function, it was your place. And Perpétua, seeing the house creak, seeing the siblings fail, felt an almost irresistible desire to do what she had always done, to take everything up again, to see to everything herself, to save the house from the creaking; and there was a moment, long, in which she almost gave in, in which she almost went back to being the beam that bears everything.

But she had now, against the column, the truth of the doctor and that of the Keeper, and she had, above all, a question she had learned to ask. Instead of taking up the weight, Perpétua stopped, and asked herself: is the house falling, or is it creaking? Is it collapsing, or is it reorganising itself? Are my siblings incapable, or are they learning, and learning has its failures, as mine had at fourteen? And she remembered herself at fourteen, learning to hold the house, and how she had failed at first, how she had made muddles, how she had neglected things, because no one learns to carry without dropping a few times. And she understood that the creaking of the house, the failures of the siblings, were not the proof that they were going to fall, they were the proof that they were learning; and that if she took up the weight again now, at the first creak, she would never let them learn, she would make them incapable again, and the house would go back to having a single column, and the problem would stay the same, put off to the day she fell.

And it was an agony to her, each failure. When she learned that Belmiro had let the deadline of a paper of the uncle's pass, and that it was going to mean work and perhaps a fine, Perpétua felt her hands wish to go to the telephone, to see to it herself, to ring the offices, to undo the muddle, to save Belmiro from the error as she had saved everyone from every error for forty years. She already had the telephone in her hand. And she set it down. She forced herself not to ring, to let Belmiro discover the fine for himself, to let him go to the offices, to let him see to what he had spoiled, even at the cost of money, even at the cost of time, because she understood that if she saved him from that one, Belmiro would never learn not to let the deadlines pass, and she would go back to seeing to the uncle's papers for ever. It was as hard for her as bearing a pain. To see badly done what she would do well, to see spoiled what she would save, and not to move, to hold her hands, to let the house creak. It was easier to carry a weight than to bear to see the house tremble without holding it; but that, she understood, was the new work of the column that learns to set down: not to carry, and to bear the fear that everything would fall, which is a form of courage higher than carrying, because carrying gives us the illusion that we are in control, and setting down obliges us to trust.

And there was a night, in the midst of that creaking, on which Perpétua almost gave in for good, and which was the greatest trial. Tó had made a real blunder, of the kind that brings loss, and telephoned her in the middle of the night, in distress, with the child's voice she had known since the cradle.

"Perpétua, I have got myself into a bad thing, I do not know what to do, you have to help me, you always know what to do."

And the whole column, within Perpétua, cried out to her take it, save, see to it, he is your brother, he is your boy, how can you leave him. And she, with the telephone in her hand, in the middle of the night, was for long minutes on the verge of doing what she had always done, of getting up and going to solve it, of getting Tó out of it as she had got him out a thousand times. But she breathed, and asked herself the new question: if I save him from this, does he learn? Or does he only learn if he bears it? And she understood, with pain, that each time she had saved him she had kept him from growing, that she had kept him a child at forty by dint of getting him out of things; and that the hardest and most loving thing she could do that night was the one that seemed to her the harshest.

"Tó," she said, and the voice was hard for her to bring out, "I am not going to see to this for you. You did it, you solve it. I am here, on the telephone, if you need to talk, to think with me. But I will not untangle you. You are able. You have only to discover that you are."

And Tó grew angry, and accused her, you have changed, you do not love me any more, before you always helped me; and he hung up the telephone in her face. And Perpétua stayed sitting on the bed, in the dark, with the dumb telephone in her hand, and it was the hardest night of her crossing, harder than carrying forty years, because carrying was easy, it cost only the body, and this cost her the soul: it was to hear a brother in distress, a child she had raised, accusing her of abandoning him, and not to run to save him, by knowing that to save him was to keep him a child. She did not sleep. But she did not take up the weight. She let Tó solve his own blunder. And Tó, after some bad days, solved it. Badly, with difficulty, but he solved it, and it was the first thing he had solved alone in his life, and Perpétua saw him come out of it a little more a man than he had gone in.

And what she saw, after some weeks of creaking, was the beginning of the proof: the house did not fall. The siblings, after failing, corrected. Belmiro learned not to fall behind on the papers. Felismina came to care better, ashamed of the turn she had neglected. Tó, after the blunder he solved alone, began, for the first time in his life, to take a thing seriously. The house creaked, took fright, reorganised itself, and the timbers that had been leaning on the column began, with difficulty, to bear their part. And Perpétua saw, with an astonishment that was relief and was mourning at the same time, that the doctor had been right, that the house did not fall without her holding everything, that it creaked and reorganised itself, and that the siblings were after all capable, and that they had only not been capable because she had never let them, and that the problem, the true one, had never been their incapacity, it had been her incapacity to set down.

There was a day that proved it to her at one stroke. Perpétua, at the doctor's advice, went to spend a day away, in the larger town, alone, and it was the first time in years that she absented herself a whole day from the House of the Mill. And she went with her heart tight, imagining the worst, seeing in her head the house collapsing in her absence, the uncle without care, the siblings lost. And she came back at night, afraid of finding the house in ruin. And she found it going. Felismina had done the uncle's turn, and the uncle was well cared for, more even than she cared for him in haste between a thousand things. Belmiro had seen to a paper. The house was standing, clean, in order, without her. And Perpétua, at the door, seeing the house working without her having been there the whole day, felt the world turn inside out, because her whole life she had believed that if she were missing a single day everything fell, and there was the proof that no, that the house bore a whole day without the column, and bore it well, perhaps better, because many hands care better than one pair of tired hands doing everything in haste. And it was relief, and it was, beneath, the first pang of the mourning that followed.

There was a thing she heard, by chance, that marked her. She was in the kitchen, and the siblings, in the parlour, were speaking on the telephone with one another, arranging the uncle's turns, settling who took which day, without consulting her, without asking her to decide, without her at the centre. Oh Belmiro, this week you take the Thursdays, for I cannot, said Felismina; and Belmiro, all right, and Tó takes the weekends, and the aunt we take turn by turn. They arranged among themselves, alone, a system, shared out the weight by agreement, without the column in the middle distributing everything. And Perpétua, in the kitchen, hearing the siblings organise themselves without her, felt the two things at once: the relief of no longer having to be the one to decide everything, and the small mourning of seeing that they already decided without her, that they no longer put her at the centre, that the house had learned to work without needing to ask her leave. It was strange to her to hear a family organise itself without her, she who had organised that family alone for forty years; strange and good and sad at the same time, as is everything that has to do with setting down.

And it was relief and it was mourning at the same time, and the mourning was the part Perpétua did not expect. Relief, because the house did not fall, because she could set down, because she was not condemned to carry everything until she cracked. But mourning, because if the house did not fall without her, then she was not so indispensable as she had thought, and her indispensability, which had been her whole life her pride, her place, her identity, revealed itself, in the creaking that did not turn to a fall, less true than she believed. There was a family lunch, about that time, in which the mourning hurt her in a small thing. It had always been Perpétua who carved the meat, at the head, which was the place and the gesture of the one who rules the table, of the one who serves the house. And at that lunch, without anyone arranging it, it was Belmiro who took up the knife and carved the meat, with naturalness, because he had learned to do it on the times she had not been able. And Perpétua, seated, saw the meat carved by another, saw her gesture, her place, pass into her brother's hands, and felt an absurd pang, a sadness out of all proportion to a thing as small as carving meat; and she understood that it was not for the meat, it was for everything, it was to see her place taken, her function shared out, her indispensability undoing itself before her eyes in a knife that no longer needed her hand. No one noticed. It was a silent mourning, seated at the table, watching another do what had always been hers, and having to pretend she was glad, that she was, and that it hurt all the same.

And to discover that the house held up without her was to discover that the place she had occupied for forty years was not so necessary as she thought, and that, however much it freed her, hurt, because it took from her the only thing she knew how to be, the indispensable, and left her before the question the book had been keeping for her: if the house holds up without me, if I am not the indispensable column I thought, then who am I? What is left of Perpétua, when the house no longer needs her not to fall?
