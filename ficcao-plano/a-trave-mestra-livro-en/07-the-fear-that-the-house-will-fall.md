## 7. The fear that the house will fall

Perpétua's greatest trial was not setting down the first weight, for that she set down with the doctor and the Keeper fresh in her head; it was resisting taking it up again when the house creaked, when it seemed it would fall, when the fear that everything would fall pushed her, with all the force of forty years, to go back to being the column.

Because a house that was always shored on a single column, when that column sets down, creaks, and the creaking frightens, and it seems it will fall, and that is what happened in the House of the Mill. The siblings, set to carry their part, carried it badly at first, as one carries what one never learned: the one with the papers fell behind, missed a deadline, made a muddle; the one with the care of the uncle neglected a turn, and the old man passed a worse night; the youngest, set to see to a thing, saw to it in his own way, badly, and there was a small misfortune, nothing grave, but enough for the house to creak, for it to seem that without Perpétua holding everything things came undone, to give reason, in appearance, to Perpétua's fear that the house did not hold up without her.

And the fear rose, and it was terrible, because it was the deep fear, that if she set everything down everything would fall, and each failure of the siblings was like a proof that she had been right, that only she could hold the house, that the siblings were not capable, that setting down had been a mistake, and the house was going to fall, and the fault would be hers, for having set down. And the column in Perpétua, the old one, that of the fourteen years, cried out to her take the weight up again, you see that without you it falls, you see that only you can, go back to being the column, save the house, that was your function, it was your place, and Perpétua, seeing the house creak, seeing the siblings fail, felt an almost irresistible desire to do what she had always done, to take everything up again, to see to everything herself, to save the house from the creaking, and there was a moment, long, in which she almost gave in, in which she almost went back to being the beam that bears everything.

But she had now, against the column, the truth of the doctor and that of the Keeper, and she had, above all, a question she had learned to ask. Instead of taking up the weight, Perpétua stopped, and asked herself: is the house falling, or is it creaking? Is it collapsing, or reorganising itself? Are my siblings incapable, or are they learning, and learning has its failures, as mine did at fourteen? And she remembered herself at fourteen, learning to hold the house, and how she had failed, at first, how she had made muddles, how she had neglected things, because no one learns to carry without dropping a few times; and she understood that the creaking of the house, the failures of the siblings, were not the proof that they were going to fall, they were the proof that they were learning, and that if she took up the weight again now, at the first creak, she would never let them learn, she would make them incapable again, and the house would go back to having a single column, and the problem would stay the same, put off to the day she fell.

And she did the hardest thing of her life, harder than carrying for forty years: she did not take up the weight. She let the house creak. She let the siblings fail, and correct, and learn. She bore the fear that everything would fall without doing anything to prevent it, which is the highest form of a column's courage, because it is easier to bear a weight than to bear the fear that the house will fall without one holding it. She helped the siblings correct their failures, taught them, but did not do it for them, let them carry their part even trembling, even failing, and it was an agony to her, each failure, each creak, because the instinct of forty years cried out to her save, hold, carry; but Perpétua held herself instead of holding the house, and waited, and watched.

And there was a night, in the midst of that creaking, on which Perpétua almost gave in for good, and which was the greatest trial. The youngest had made a real blunder, of the kind that brings loss, and the whole house looked to Perpétua expecting her to do what she had always done, to take up the problem and solve it and spare the brother the consequences; and the brother, in trouble, telephoned her in the middle of the night, anxious, asking her to see to it, to untangle it, as so many times. And Perpétua, with the telephone in her hand, in the middle of the night, felt the whole column cry out to her take it, save, see to it, he is your brother, how can you leave him; and she was, for long minutes, on the verge of doing it, on the verge of getting up and going to solve it, as always. But she breathed, and asked herself the new question: if I save him from this, does he learn? Or does he only learn if he bears it? And she understood, with pain, that each time she had saved him she had kept him from growing, and that the hardest and most loving thing she could do that night was the one that seemed to her the harshest: to tell the brother I am not going to see to this for you, you did it, you solve it, I am here if you need to talk, but I will not untangle you. And she said it, and it was like tearing out her heart, and the brother was angry, and accused her of abandoning him; and Perpétua bore the accusation without giving in, and it was the hardest night of her crossing, harder than carrying, because carrying was easy, it cost only the body, and this cost her soul, it was to see a brother in trouble and not save him, knowing that to save him was to keep him small.

And what she saw, after some weeks of creaking, was the beginning of the proof: the house did not fall. The siblings, after failing, corrected. The one who fell behind on the papers learned not to fall behind. The one who neglected a turn came to care better. The youngest, after the small misfortune, began, for the first time in his life, to take a thing seriously. The house creaked, took fright, reorganised itself, and the timbers that had been leaning on the column began, with effort, to bear their part, and Perpétua saw, with an astonishment that was relief and was mourning at the same time, that the doctor had been right, that the house did not fall without her holding everything, that it creaked and reorganised itself, and that the siblings were after all capable, and that they had only not been capable because she had never let them, and that the problem, the true one, had never been their incapacity, it had been her incapacity to set down.

And it was relief and it was mourning at the same time, and the mourning was the part Perpétua did not expect. Relief, because the house did not fall, because she could set down, because she was not condemned to carry everything until she cracked. But mourning, because if the house did not fall without her, then she was not so indispensable as she had thought, and her indispensability, which had been her whole life her pride, her place, her identity, revealed itself, in the creaking that did not turn to a fall, less true than she believed; and to discover that the house held up without her was to discover that the place she had occupied for forty years was not so necessary as she thought, and that, however much it freed her, hurt, because it took from her the only thing she knew how to be, the indispensable, and left her before the question the book had been keeping for her: if the house holds up without me, if I am not the indispensable column I thought, then who am I? What is left of Perpétua, when the house no longer needs her not to fall?
