## 4. The outside doctor

Perpétua's body said what she would not let herself say, and said it through the mouth of an outside doctor, a young woman who did not know the House of the Mill nor Perpétua's place, and who for that reason could look at her as a person and not as a column.

It was the body that forced her to go. The pains in her back, the tiredness, the shortness of breath had grown worse, and Perpétua, who put off everything that was hers, put off the going to the doctor too.

It was on a winter morning, still dark, in the back room. Uncle Aníbal was wet, and had to be changed, and Perpétua, alone at six in the morning, took hold of the old man to turn him, as she took hold every day, and the old man was heavy, and she pulled, and it was in that pull that she felt something give way within her chest, a pain that tore through her left side, and she stayed bent over the uncle's bed, with the old man half-turned in her arms, without breath, her sight darkening and a cold sweat upon her. She stayed there, clinging to the bed, bearing the old man she could not let go and the heart that was failing her, alone, in the dark, with no one to call, because at six in the morning there was no one in the House of the Mill but her and a bedridden old man she was holding while her own body failed her. And she thought, in that instant, bent over the bed with the pain gripping: if I die now, here, the uncle is left alone with my hand on top of him, and no one comes until the afternoon. That was the thought that frightened her in earnest: not the dying, but the dying while holding, with an old man in her arms and no one to find her for hours. The pain passed, after a few seconds that seemed to her long, and Perpétua finished changing the uncle, with her hands trembling, and went down to the kitchen, and sat, and stayed a long time sitting, feeling her heart, and understood that this had been a warning, and that the next time it might not pass; and it was that fear that finally defeated the postponing.

She went first to the one in the village, which was the natural thing. And the village doctor, who had known her for years, who had seen her carry the whole house her whole life, listened to her complaints with half an ear, took her blood pressure, and said what one says to Perpétua, with the haste of one whose waiting room is full: it is the age, dona Perpétua, it is the work, you slow down a little, rest more, and that passes. Rest more. As though resting were a thing one prescribed to a woman who held a whole house. The village doctor treated her as the whole village treated her, as the saint who bears, of whom nothing is asked in earnest because she always bears, and sent her home with a piece of advice that was a fine way of not seeing her. And Perpétua understood, on leaving, that there she was not going to be seen, because whoever knew her as a column did not know how to see her as a person. And so she went to the outside one, to the health centre of the larger town, to a young doctor who did not know her from anywhere, without great hope, only because the pain in her chest had frightened her in earnest.

To go to the larger town, she had to find someone to stay with the uncle that morning, and she went to ask the neighbour, with difficulty, with shame, oh neighbour, would you mind seeing to the uncle just one morning, for I have to go to the larger town; and the neighbour, of course, with all my heart, go and rest easy. And Perpétua caught the nine o'clock bus, and sat by the window watching the mountain go past, and found herself, halfway, with a strange feeling, almost guilty: that of going somewhere, alone, not to take anyone, not to see to anyone's affairs. It was for the doctor, true, it was no outing; but even so it was the first time in she did not know how long that she went to the larger town only for a thing that was hers, her own body, and there was in that, beneath the fear of the consultation, a strange lightness, that of going, for once, to see to herself. And she felt guilty of the lightness, because a column does not go riding about on buses while there is an uncle at home; and it was that guilt, that of going to see to herself, that showed her the size of the damage, because a woman who feels guilty of going to the doctor is a woman who no longer even allows herself the right to her own body.

In the waiting room of the larger town's health centre, seated among strangers, Perpétua felt a thing she felt nowhere in her own village: she felt herself nobody. There she was not the Perpétua of the House of the Mill, the saint, the column, the one who bears; she was any woman with a paper in her hand waiting her turn, the same as the others, without weight, without place, without a known name. And instead of troubling her, that being nobody gave her a strange rest, because to be nobody is, sometimes, to be, at last, a person, free of the place the others have put us in. And she understood that it was for this that there she was going to be seen: because no one there knew her as a column, and so they could look at her as a person. At home, she was the saint, and of the saint nothing is asked; there, she was a patient like the others, and a patient is asked everything. Sometimes it is only away from our own land that they can see us, because in our own land we are the place we occupy, and the place covers the person.

The doctor examined her without haste. She listened to her chest, told her to breathe deeply, felt her back, asked about the pains. And it was in the questions that the thing happened, because the doctor, instead of asking only about the symptoms, asked about her life, and it was the first time in forty years that anyone asked Perpétua about her life.

"Whom do you live with?" she asked.

"I live alone. Well, alone, I have a bedridden uncle in my charge, and the old ones of the house."

"In your charge." The doctor noted it down. "And how many people do you have in your charge, all told?"

And Perpétua began to count, and got muddled, because there was the uncle, and the aunt who could no longer govern herself, and the siblings who turned to her, and the house, and the feasts, and she lost the count, and said only, a fair few.

"When was the last time you rested?" asked the doctor. "Truly. A whole day, with no one needing you."

Perpétua fell silent. She searched her memory and did not find. She did not remember. She opened her mouth to answer and had no answer, and the lack of an answer was the answer, and the two of them understood.

"And who cares for you, dona Perpétua?" asked the doctor, more slowly.

And Perpétua fell silent again, and this time the silence lasted longer, because the answer was no one, and she did not want to say it aloud, because said aloud the answer hurt. The doctor waited, and saw the silence, and understood more in that silence than in all the examinations, and set down her pen, and said to Perpétua a thing she never forgot.

"You have the body of one who has carried too much weight for too long. This that you have is not really an illness, it is a warning. The heart, the back, the shortness of breath, it is the body crying out to you a thing you do not want to hear, which is: enough. And I am going to speak to you frankly, because it is my trade and because you are no acquaintance of mine and I have no need to spare you: if you do not set that weight down, and I am not speaking of pills, I am speaking of the weight of your life, of all the people you carry, if you do not learn to set down, the body will set it down for you. And then you will no longer choose how. You will fall. And when a beam falls, it falls all at once, and the house falls with it. You must learn to set down before you crack, because cracking gives no warning, and setting down is still in time."

And Perpétua heard from the outside doctor, from a stranger, the word that no one in the family, who had seen her carry for forty years, had ever said to her: set down. Because the family needed her to carry, and so would never say to her set down; and the outside doctor needed nothing of her, did not lean on her, had no interest in her weight, and so could tell her the truth that those who leaned on her hid. It is the luck columns sometimes have, Perpétua thought afterwards: that of meeting someone who does not need them, and who for that reason can see them, and tell them the truth that those who need them keep silent.

And the doctor said more, and it was what most shook her, because it touched the deep fear.

"You are going to tell me that if you set the weight down, your family falls. That there is no one to hold it. And perhaps you really believe that. But I have seen many Perpétuas, many women who hold whole families, and I will tell you a thing they all learn by force and that I will spare you if you will hear it: the house does not fall. When the column sets down, the house creaks, takes fright, reorganises itself, and the other timbers, which were leaning because the column bore everything, discover all at once that they know how to bear, because they always knew, they had only never had to do it. Your family does not fall if you set down. Your siblings grow up. They have only to grow up. And while you carry them, they do not grow, because no one grows while there is someone to carry him. You, by carrying them, are keeping them from learning to hold themselves up. Set down, and see. The house does not fall. You fall, if you do not set down."

Perpétua left the outside doctor shaken as never before. Because the doctor had given her, in half an hour, what forty years had not given her. The truth of the body, told by one who had no interest in silencing her. The truth of the house, which did not fall if she set down. And the truth of the siblings, who did not grow because she carried them. And she took with her, on the way back, on the bus, no longer the timid question of and if I fall, who holds, but its new version, the one the doctor had opened in her: and if I set down, and the house does not fall? And if my siblings, whom I think incapable, are after all capable, and only do not hold because I never let them? And if the weight I have carried for forty years is not a weight that only I can carry, but a weight I let no one share, out of love and out of pride, and that is killing me for want of my knowing how to set down?

And on the way back, on the bus, looking at the mountain through the window, Perpétua found herself doing the thing of always, without thinking: hiding. She was already planning to say nothing to anyone. Not to tell the siblings she had gone to the doctor, not to tell them her heart had failed lifting the uncle, not to tell them she had been told to set the weight down or fall. Because a column does not give news that she is cracking; she gives news that all is well. And Perpétua understood, in the middle of that plan of silence, that the hiding was the disease itself. That she hid the tiredness as she hid the weight, as she hid the anger, as she hid everything that was hers, so that the family would go on seeing her whole, strong, the saint, the one who bears. And that if she went on hiding, no one would know, and no one would change, and she would carry alone until she fell, and the family would discover the truth only on the day the beam cracked, too late, with the remorse on top. And it was then that she decided, there on the bus, that she was going to do the opposite of what she had always done: she was going to tell. She was going to tell the siblings that she was cracking, she was going to show the weakness she had hidden for forty years, because the first thing a column has to set down, before setting the weight down, is the secret that she is tired.

She went home that afternoon with the doctor's truth hammering, and however much she turned it over, the thing came down to a choice, the one the whole book had been preparing for her. To go on being the column, faithful to the place of the fourteen years, faithful to the pride of bearing everything, until she cracked, and fell, and took the house with her. Or to set down. To risk. To come down from the place. To let the house reorganise itself without her bearing everything. To let the siblings grow. And to discover, on the far side of the set-down weight, who Perpétua was when she was holding nothing.

And the second choice frightened her more than the first, because the first she knew, it was her whole life, and the second was the unknown. But the outside doctor had put into her hand a thing she did not have, which was the certainty that, if she did not set down, the body would set her down, and that cracking gives no warning, and setting down was still in time. And Perpétua, for the first time in forty years, instead of putting off what was hers, took with her, home, the decision not to put off any longer the one thing she had never done: to relieve herself, at last, of the weight, before the weight relieved her of it, in the worst way, all at once.
