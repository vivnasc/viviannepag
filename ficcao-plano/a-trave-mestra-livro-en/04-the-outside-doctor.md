## 4. The outside doctor

Perpétua's body said what she would not let herself say, and said it through the mouth of an outside doctor, who did not know the House of the Mill nor Perpétua's place, and who for that reason could look at her as a person and not as a column.

It was the body that forced her to go. The pains in her back, the tiredness, the shortness of breath, had grown worse, and Perpétua, who put off everything that was hers, put off the going to the doctor too, until one morning, lifting an old man from his bed, she felt something give way within her chest, a pain that left her doubled over, and she was afraid, the fear that at last defeats the postponing, and she went to the doctor, not the one in the village, for the one in the village knew her and treated her as everyone treated her, in a hurry, for Perpétua bears it; she went to the outside one, to the health centre of the larger town, a young doctor who did not know her from anywhere.

And the outside doctor examined her, and listened to her chest, and asked her questions, and it was in the questions that the thing happened, because the doctor, instead of asking only about the symptoms, asked about her life, and it was the first time in forty years that anyone asked Perpétua about her life. She asked her how many people she had in her charge, and Perpétua began to count and lost count. She asked her when was the last time she had rested, truly, with no one needing her, and Perpétua did not know how to answer, because she did not remember. She asked her who cares for you, and Perpétua fell silent, because the answer was no one, and the doctor, seeing the silence, understood more in that silence than in all the examinations, and said to Perpétua a thing she never forgot.

She said to her: you have the body of one who has carried too much weight for too long. It is not an illness, it is a warning. Your heart, your back, your tiredness, it is the body crying out to you a thing you do not want to hear, which is enough. And I will tell you frankly, because it is my trade: if you do not set that weight down, and I am not speaking of pills, I am speaking of the weight of your life, of all the people you carry, if you do not learn to set down, the body will set it down for you, and then you will not choose how. You will fall. And when a beam falls, it falls all at once, and the house falls with it. You must learn to set down before you crack, because cracking gives no warning, and setting down is still in time.

And Perpétua heard from the outside doctor, from a stranger, the thing that no one in the family, who had seen her carry for forty years, had ever said to her: set down. Because the family needed her to carry, and so would never say to her set down; and the outside doctor needed nothing from her, did not lean on her, had no interest in her weight, and so could tell her the truth that those who leaned on her would never tell, which is the luck columns sometimes have, that of finding someone who does not need them and who for that reason can see them, and tell them the truth that those who need them hide.

And the doctor said more, and it was what most shook Perpétua, because it touched the deep fear: you are going to tell me that if you set the weight down, your family falls, that there is no one to hold. And perhaps you believe it. But I have seen many Perpétuas, many women who hold whole families, and I will tell you a thing they all learn by force and that I will spare you if you will hear it: the house does not fall. When the column sets down, the house creaks, takes fright, reorganises itself, and the other timbers, which were leaning because the column bore everything, discover all at once that they know how to bear, because they always knew, they had only never had to do it. Your family does not fall if you set down. Your siblings grow up. They have only to grow up, and while you carry them, they do not grow, because no one grows while there is someone to carry him. You, by carrying them, are keeping them from learning to hold themselves. Set down, and see. The house does not fall. You fall, if you do not set down.

Perpétua left the outside doctor shaken as never before, because the doctor had given her, in half an hour, what forty years had not given her: the truth of the body, told by one who had no interest in silencing her; and the truth of the house, which did not fall if she set down; and the truth of the siblings, who did not grow because she carried them. And she took with her, to the House of the Mill, no longer the timid question of and if I fall, who holds, but its new version, the one the doctor had opened in her: and if I set down, and the house does not fall? And if my siblings, whom I think incapable of holding, are after all capable, and only do not hold because I have never let them? And if the weight I have carried for forty years is not a weight that only I can carry, but a weight I let no one share, out of love and out of pride, and that is killing me for want of my knowing how to set down?

And she understood, with the doctor's truth hammering, that she had before her the choice the whole book had been preparing for her: to go on being the column, faithful to the place of fourteen, faithful to the pride of bearing everything, until she cracked, and fell, and took the house with her; or to set down, to risk, to come down from the place, to let the house reorganise itself without her bearing everything, to let the siblings grow, and to discover, on the far side of the set-down weight, who Perpétua was when she was holding nothing. And the second choice frightened her more than the first, because the first she knew, it was her whole life, and the second was the unknown; but the outside doctor had put into her hand a thing she did not have, which was the certainty that, if she did not set down, the body would set her down, and that cracking gives no warning, and setting down was still in time, and Perpétua, for the first time in forty years, instead of putting off what was hers, took with her, home, the decision not to put off any longer the one thing she had never done: to relieve herself, at last, of the weight, before the weight relieved her, in the worst way, all at once.
