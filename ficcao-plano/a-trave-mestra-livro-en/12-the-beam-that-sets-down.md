## 12. The beam that sets down

And the day came, after all the settings-down and all the setbacks, on which Perpétua set the weight down no longer at the warning of the doctor, nor at the truth of the Keeper, nor at the plea of the brother, but from within, by her own choice, by having at last understood with her whole body and not only with her head that she was not the column, she was the woman, and that the woman had a right to a life; and that day was the climax of the book, and it was without fanfare, as the true climaxes of the houses of Véspera are, a small thing that changes everything within.

It was at a family feast, of the many Perpétua had always organised alone, seeing to everything, serving everyone, never sitting down, being, at the feasts as in life, the column that bears so that the others may enjoy. And at that feast, for the first time in forty years, Perpétua did not see to everything. She shared it out beforehand: this one brings this, that one makes that, the other sees to the table; and when the feast came, instead of going about serving, instead of being in the kitchen while the others were at the table, Perpétua did the simplest and hardest thing of her life: she sat at the table, with the others, and let herself be served.

And it was a moment small and immense. Perpétua seated at the table, in the midst of the family, with nothing to carry, with no one needing her in that instant, eating what others had made, being served by those she had always served, being, for the first time at a feast, not holding the feast up, but a part of it; and she looked around, and saw the family working without her at the centre, saw the siblings seeing to things, saw the house holding itself up without the column holding it, and instead of the mourning she had felt at first, on discovering herself dispensable, she felt that time another thing, she felt peace, the deep peace of one who understands that the house does not need her to hold itself up and that this, which at first hurt, is after all the greatest proof of love a column can give, to leave the house able to hold itself up without her, so that on the day she fails the house does not fall.

And it was there, seated at the table, served, in the midst of the family that had learned to share the weight, that Perpétua understood for good what the Keeper had told her: that a house of a single column is fragile, and that a house of many timbers is strong; and that she, by setting down, had not weakened the house, she had strengthened it; that the greatest good she had done the family was not the forty years carrying it, it was that time teaching it to carry itself; and that her beam, the main beam, by setting down, had not left the house without a beam, it had made of the whole house a single structure, in which each timber was a little a beam, and so none had to be the whole beam, and so the house did not fall. And she understood that to set down had not been to betray the function she had received at fourteen, it had been to fulfil it at last in full, because the true function of a main beam is not to hold the house up alone for ever, it is to hold it up the time necessary for the house to learn to hold itself, and then to set down, and to leave the house standing, which is the only way for the house not to fall when the beam, one day, fails.

And there came to her, in the middle of the feast, the youngest niece, a girl of fourteen, the exact age at which Perpétua had become a column, and the girl, who was helpful and serious and did not cry in front of the others, was that day going about seeing to everything, serving the old ones, clearing the plates, being the one who helps, and the family was already beginning to say of her what they had said of Perpétua, oh this one is like the aunt, this one is the one who will hold the house, this one has the knack; and Perpétua, on hearing that, on seeing the niece of fourteen being pushed, with praises, towards the place that had pushed her at fourteen, felt a tightening in her chest, and did a thing that changed the feast: she rose, went to the niece, took the plates from her hands, and said to her, in front of everyone, not today, my dear, today you sit at the table with us and let yourself be served, for there are grown people enough here to clear plates. And she sat the niece at the table, beside her, and understood, in doing it, that she was doing for the girl what no one had done for her at fourteen, that she was cutting, in that small gesture, the chain that makes of houses machines for manufacturing columns, and that perhaps that, and not another, was the greatest entry she was going to leave in the book, that of not letting the niece repeat her life, that of telling her, in time, before the place stuck to her skin, you do not have to be the beam, my dear, you can be only a girl.

And at that feast, seated at the table, Perpétua set down the last thing she had left to set down, which was not a weight from outside, it was one from within: she set down the idea that she was worth what she carried. Her whole life she had believed, without knowing it, that her worth was the weight, that she was worth by being useful, by bearing, by being needed, and that if she did not carry she would be worth nothing, would be dispensable, they would stop wanting her; and at that table, served, wanted, in the midst of the family, carrying nothing, Perpétua felt herself wanted all the same, felt that the family wanted her and not the weight she carried, and she set down, at last, the belief that lay beneath the whole column, that she was worth only by carrying, and discovered, in the warmth of that table, that she was worth without carrying, that she had always been, that her worth had never been the weight, it had been her, and that she could, at last, set everything down, the weight from outside and the one from within, and go on being wanted, having a place, being Perpétua, not the column, the woman. And it was that the beam that set down, that day, at the table: not the timber that bore the house, but the belief that forced her to bear it, and on setting it down she became, for the first time in her life, light, and the lightness, she discovered, was not the emptiness she had feared, it was the life, at last, beginning.
