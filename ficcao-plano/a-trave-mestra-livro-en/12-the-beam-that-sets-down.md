## 12. The beam that sets down

And the day came, after all the settings-down and all the setbacks, on which Perpétua set the weight down no longer at the warning of the doctor, nor at the truth of the Keeper, nor at the plea of the brother, but from within, by her own choice, by having at last understood with her whole body and not only with her head that she was not the column, she was the woman, and that the woman had a right to a life. And that day was the climax of the book, and it was without fanfare, as the climaxes of the houses of Véspera are, a small thing that changes everything within.

It was at a family feast, of the many Perpétua had always organised alone, seeing to everything, serving everyone, never sitting down, being, at the feasts as in life, the column that bears so that the others may enjoy. And at that feast, for the first time in forty years, Perpétua did not see to everything. She shared it out beforehand: Belmiro brought this, Felismina made that, Tó saw to the table, the sisters-in-law the dishes. And when the feast came, instead of going about serving, instead of being in the kitchen while the others were at the table, Perpétua did the simplest and hardest thing of her life: she sat at the table, with the others, and let herself be served.

And it was a moment small and immense. Perpétua seated at the long table, in the midst of the family, with nothing to carry, with no one needing her in that instant, eating what others had made, being served by those she had always served, being, for the first time at a feast, not holding the feast up, but a part of it. She looked around, and saw the family working without her at the centre. She saw Belmiro carving the meat, a thing that had always been hers. She saw Felismina ruling the serving dishes. She saw Tó filling the glasses, laughing, a man. She saw the house holding itself up without the column holding it. And instead of the mourning she had felt at first, on discovering herself dispensable, she felt that time another thing, she felt peace, the deep peace of one who perceives that the house does not need her to hold itself up, and that this, which at first hurt, is after all the greatest proof of love a column can give: to leave the house able to hold itself up without her, so that on the day she is missing the house does not fall.

And, seated, Perpétua noticed a thing: that she was, for the first time at a feast, hearing the feast. In forty years of feasts, she had never heard one, because she had always been in the kitchen, or serving, or clearing, or watching whether anything was missing to anyone, with her head on the next serving dish, never on the conversation, never on the music, never on the joy. The feasts, for her, had always been work, seen from behind, from the kitchen; never feast, seen from the table. And at that one, seated, with nothing to watch, Perpétua heard, at last, a feast: she heard the conversations, the peals of laughter, the music, the stories that repeated themselves every year and that she had never heard to the end because she was always getting up; she heard the nephews laughing, the wine running, the joy of a family together, and understood, with an astonishment that filled her eyes, that she had made forty feasts and never been at any one of them, that she had given forty years of feasts to the others and never had one for herself; and that that one, in which she sat and listened, was the first feast of her life, at fifty-two years old, the first one she did not make, but lived.

And there came to her, in the middle of the feast, the youngest niece, Felismina's daughter, a girl of fourteen, the exact age at which Perpétua had become a column. The girl was helpful and serious, and did not cry in front of the others, and was going about that day seeing to everything, serving the old ones, clearing the plates, being the one who helps; and the family was already beginning to say of her what they had said of Perpétua, oh this one is like the aunt, this one is the one who will hold the house, this one has the knack, this one is no trouble. And Perpétua, on hearing that, on seeing the niece of fourteen being pushed, with praises, towards the place that had pushed her at fourteen, felt a tightening in her chest, and did a thing that changed the feast. She rose, went to the niece, took the plates from her hands, and said to her, in front of everyone:

"Not today, my dear. Today you sit at the table with us, and you let yourself be served, for there are grown people enough here to clear plates." And she bent down, and said to her in her ear, only for her, the thing no one had said to her at fourteen: "Hear one thing, that your aunt learned late and wants to spare you. You do not have to be the beam of this house. However much knack you have, however much they praise you. You can be only a girl, and then a woman with your own life. Do not let them put the house on your back only because you bear it. Do you promise me?"

And she seated the niece at the table, at her side, and the girl, astonished, let herself be seated. And Perpétua understood, in doing it, that she was doing for the niece what no one had done for her at fourteen, that she was cutting, in that small gesture, the chain that makes of houses machines for manufacturing columns; and that perhaps that, and not another, was the greatest entry she was going to leave in the book: that of not letting the niece repeat her life, that of telling her, in time, before the place stuck to her skin, you do not have to be the beam, my dear, you can be only a girl.

And in the middle of the feast, Tó rose, with his glass in his hand, and tapped it lightly with his knife, and a silence fell, and Tó, the youngest, the spoiled one, the one who had never said anything at family gatherings, did a thing no one expected: he made a toast to Perpétua. He said, with his voice trembling a little, I want to say a thing, that should have been said long ago and that no one ever said in this family. Perpétua held us all up our whole lives. Since the mother died, we were all raised, sustained, got out of trouble, loved, by her, and we never thanked her, because we thought it was her duty, that she liked it, that it did not cost her. And it cost her. It cost her her health, it cost her her own life, which she never had, because she went about living ours. And now that she has set the weight down, and that we have learned to carry our part, I want to raise this glass to her, to our sister, to our second mother, who held us up when we did not know how to hold ourselves, and who taught us, in the end, the hardest thing, which was to let her set down. To Perpétua. And the whole family rose, and raised their glasses, to Perpétua, to Perpétua; and Perpétua, seated, served, in the midst of the siblings standing to toast her, with the niece at her side, wept, but it was a new weeping, not that of the empty kitchen at the end of the feast, it was that of being seen, at last, and thanked, and wanted, at the table, in front of everyone, after forty years of serving without anyone rising to thank her.

And it was there, seated at the table, served, in the midst of the family that had learned to share the weight, with the niece saved at her side, that Perpétua understood for good what the Keeper had told her: that a house of a single column is fragile, and that a house of many timbers is strong; and that she, by setting down, had not weakened the house, she had strengthened it; that the greatest good she had done the family had not been the forty years carrying it, it had been that time teaching it to carry itself. Her beam, the main beam, by setting down, had not left the house without a beam; it had made of the whole house a single structure, in which each timber was a little a beam, and so none had to be the whole beam, and so the house did not fall. And she understood that to set down had not been to betray the function she had received at fourteen, it had been to fulfil it at last in full, because the true function of a main beam is not to hold the house up alone for ever, it is to hold it up the time necessary for the house to learn to hold itself, and then to set down, and to leave the house standing, which is the only way for the house not to fall when the beam, one day, fails.

And at that feast, seated at the table, Perpétua set down the last thing she had left to set down, which was not a weight from outside, it was one from within: she set down the idea that she was worth what she carried. Her whole life she had believed, without knowing it, that her worth was the weight, that she was worth by being useful, by bearing, by being needed, and that if she did not carry she would be worth nothing, would be dispensable, they would stop wanting her. And at that table, served, wanted, in the midst of the family, carrying nothing, Perpétua felt herself wanted all the same, felt that the family wanted her and not the weight she carried. And she set down, at last, the belief that lay beneath the whole column, that she was worth only by carrying, and discovered, in the warmth of that table, that she was worth without carrying, that she had always been worth, that her worth had never been the weight, it had been her. And it was that the beam that set down, that day, at the table: not the timber that bore the house, but the belief that forced her to bear it; and on setting it down she became, for the first time in her life, light, and the lightness, she discovered, was not the emptiness she had feared, it was the life, at last, beginning.

And she began, in those months, to make plans, a thing she had never done, because a column does not make plans, a column is available for the plans of others. She remembered what the Keeper had told her, there is not a single entry that is only yours, not a journey, not a thing of your own; and she decided that she was going to have one. She decided, at fifty-two years old, that she was going to make a journey, a journey of hers, not to take anyone to the doctor nor to see to anyone's papers, a journey only to see, to know, for the curiosity about the world out beyond the mountain that the girl of thirteen had had and that the weight had eaten. She did not yet know where, nor when; but the decision, in itself, was a revolution, because it was the first time in life that Perpétua planned a thing that was only hers; and when she said it to the siblings, at the table, I am going to make a journey, one of these days, alone, a silence of astonishment fell, and then Tó laughed, and said, and you do very well, sister, it was high time, and Felismina, go, go, for we will stay here with everything; and Perpétua, on hearing the we will stay here with everything, said with naturalness, without her having to ask it, understood that the house was, at last, able to let her depart, and that her journey was going to be that entry that was missing from the Keeper's book, the first, in forty years, that would be only Perpétua's.

At the end of the feast, when the family had gone and the house was left in silence, Perpétua did not clear the table. She left it laid, with the leftovers, with the joyful disorder of a feast, and went to sit at the door, in the cool, to watch the night fall on the mountain. Another woman would have cleared the table first; the column would have cleared the table. But Perpétua left it, and stayed at the door, with nothing to carry, looking at the mountain, and it was the first night of feast, in forty years, on which the dishes waited for her instead of her waiting for the dishes. And it did not weigh on her conscience. For the first time, the leaving-undone did not weigh on her, because she understood that the house was not going to fall for the dishes being left for the next day, as it had not fallen for her setting the weight down; and that she had, at last, a right to a night, to a mountain, to the cool, to a life. And at the door, in the cool, with the mountain darkening, Perpétua did a thing she had never done in life: she imagined the future. Not the future of the house, nor of the siblings, nor of anyone; her own. She imagined the years she had ahead, and for the first time she did not see them as more of the same, more weight, more chores, more bearing until she fell; she saw them as a thing open, to be filled, hers. She imagined the journey she was going to make. She imagined the afternoons at the café, the books at night, the conversations with Benvinda, the Sundays with nothing to do that no longer frightened her. She imagined herself growing old not as the column that spends itself holding the house until it cracks, but as a woman who, after a life of holding the others, had, in the end, a few years to hold herself, and lived them. And she understood that those years, the ones left to her, could be, if she wished, the best of her life, the first that would be hers; and that it had been necessary to reach fifty-two, and almost to crack, and to set down the weight of forty years, for the Perpétua left unlived on the February dawn to have, at last, a life ahead. Better late, she thought, at the door, in the cool. Better late than never to have been.

She cleared the dishes the next day, without haste, and as she washed them she went humming, a thing she had not done since she was a girl, since before the February dawn on which she became the beam of the House of the Mill.

And the following Christmas, the first true Christmas of her life, the family shared out the work, and Perpétua, instead of going about three days in the kitchen alone and serving on her feet without sitting down, did her part and nothing more, and sat at the long table with the others, and ate, and laughed, and at midnight she was not alone in the kitchen weeping over the dishes, as she had been forty Christmases; she was at the table, with the family, with Benvinda invited at her side, with the shawl of a warm colour on her shoulders, hearing the feast instead of serving it. And no one leaned against the wall trying not to fall, because there was no longer a single column holding the house up; there was a house of many timbers, and a woman in the midst of it who, after forty years, had at last, in the book of Véspera, an entry that was only hers: that of one who set the beam down in time, and discovered, on the far side of the weight, that she was still in time to live.
