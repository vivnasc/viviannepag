## 5. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to uncle Aníbal's papers at the House of the Register, one more weight she carried alone, and it was thus, by a piece of bureaucracy, that Perpétua found herself seated before the Keeper, among the books where Véspera keeps the houses and those who held them.

The House of the Register was cool and smelled of paper and wax. The Keeper was a woman of a certain age, with spectacles on the tip of her nose, with that stillness of one who spends her life writing down what people bring. She saw to the uncle's papers, slowly, and as she wrote she looked at Perpétua over the spectacles, and noticed a thing, that it was always Perpétua who came, to see to everything, alone.

"Your family is large, Perpétua," she said, without lifting her face from the paper. "And it is always you who comes here. To the papers of the old ones, to the feasts, to the deaths. I have seen you here for forty years, always you. Where are your siblings?"

And Perpétua gave the answer of always, the one she gave the whole village without ever thinking about it.

"They have their lives. I am the one who is left with these things. I am the one with the knack."

The Keeper set down her pen. She looked at her a moment, with those eyes that read between the lines, and instead of answering, rose, went to a shelf, and took down a thick book, with a black cover, and leafed through it, and searched for a page, and turned it towards Perpétua, and put her finger on a line.

"Look."

It was the page of the death of Perpétua's mother, forty years back. The Keeper ran her finger along the entry.

"Here it is. Your mother died, and you were fourteen. And do you see what is set down after? Everything your mother held passed into your name. The old ones in your charge, the papers of the house, everything. At fourteen, Perpétua. I remember seeing you come in here, a girl, to see to the papers of a house of grown people, and I thought, poor thing, what a weight for shoulders so young. And you carried it." She leafed forward, slowly, year by year, and her finger went stopping at entries. "And do you see how, page by page, down the years, everything that is of the House of the Mill goes on appearing in your name, and never, not once, in the name of your siblings? The old ones, in your name. The feasts, in your name. The deaths, in your name. This house, Perpétua, in my book, has a single person holding it up for forty years. And a house that has a single person holding it up is not a strong house. It is a house that falls on the day that person falls."

Perpétua looked at the pages, her name repeated, year after year, always hers, and the Keeper let her look, and then said the thing she took home and never again let go.

"I see the families pass by this table, Perpétua, and I have learned to see those that have a main beam and those that do not. Those that have a beam, like yours, are houses that look strong and are fragile, because they all rest on a single timber, and it is enough for that timber to give way for everything to fall. And those that have no beam, those that share the weight among many timbers, are houses that look more fragile, because no one alone holds them, and are the strongest, because if one timber gives way, the others bear up. Your family, Perpétua, was made fragile by you, without your wanting it. By holding everything, you kept it from learning to share the weight, and made of it a house of a single column, which is you, and which is tired. The good you can do this family now is not to hold it more. It is to stop holding it alone, so that it may learn, while the column is still standing, to share the weight, before the column falls and there is no longer time to learn."

And it was hard for Perpétua to hear that, because it turned forty years of good inside out. She had always thought that to hold the family was the greatest good she could do it; and the Keeper was telling her that the greatest good was to stop holding it alone. That her strength had been, without her knowing, the weakness of the house. That by wishing to spare the siblings the weight she had made them incapable of carrying it. And that a house of a single column is a house on the verge of falling.

And so that Perpétua would see that she did not speak idly, the Keeper went to another shelf, and brought another book, older, and searched, and showed her an entry.

"Look at this house, Perpétua. The house of the Ferreiros, which you did not know, which went before your time. It had a beam like you, a woman who held everything, Conceição, God rest her. A whole life bearing the house alone, like you, never letting anyone learn to bear. And she was proud of it, as you are proud. And one day she fell, suddenly, of the heart, in the middle of a chore, at fifty-odd years, as you will fall if you do not set down. And do you know what happened to the house, Perpétua? It fell with her. The children, who had never learned to hold anything, did not know how. They quarrelled over the inheritance, lost the house, scattered, and today the house of the Ferreiros is a ruin there at the edge of the village, which you have seen without knowing whose it was. A house that had too strong a beam, and that fell on the day the beam fell, because the beam never let the other timbers learn to bear." She closed the book. "I do not want to write your entry as I wrote Conceição's, Perpétua. I do not want to have to write, some years from now, fell suddenly, and the house with her. I want to write another thing. I want to write: set down in time, and the house learned to bear itself up, and stayed standing."

Perpétua looked at that entry, Conceição's, the beam that cracked and took the house, and saw herself in it, and shivered, because it was her future written in a hand of thirty years ago, if she did not change.

And the Keeper, seeing her shiver, turned the page, and showed her another house, so that she would not leave only with the fear.

"And look at this one, Perpétua, which is the opposite, so you may see that it can be done. The house of the Soares, up there. It too had a beam, Bonança, a woman like you, who held everything. But Bonança, at a certain point, fell ill, and had to set down, and instead of carrying until she fell, she shared the weight among the family, with difficulty, with creaking and all, and the house, instead of falling, learned to bear itself up. And when Bonança died, years later, old, the house of the Soares did not fall, because it no longer depended only on her; it reorganised itself, and went on, and is still there, standing, full of people. Bonança's entry, in my book, does not say fell suddenly and the house with her; it says set down in time, and the house stayed." She tapped lightly on the page. "You can choose which of these two houses you are going to be, Perpétua. The Ferreiros', which fell, or the Soares', which stayed. The difference is not luck. It is setting down in time."

Perpétua looked at the two entries, side by side, the house that fell and the house that stayed, the beam that cracked and the beam that set down, and understood that she had there, before her, the two possible futures of the House of the Mill, and that it was she who chose which.

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a book-keeper, showed her yet another thing, which was the one that most hurt her and most freed her. She leafed through the book again, slowly, and said:

"And look at your name, Perpétua. It appears on every paper of the house for forty years. On those of the old ones, on those of the feasts, on those of the deaths. But see one thing." She turned the pages. "It appears on none that is yours. There is not, in forty years, a single entry that is only Perpétua's. There is no marriage, for you did not marry, you had no time. There is no journey, there is no thing of your own. You are in this book whole, but always holding the others, never living. You are the only person of the House of the Mill who, in my book, in forty years, never did anything for herself. And that, Perpétua, is not the entry of a life. It is the entry of a column. And you are not a column. You are a woman. And you are still in time to put in the book, before the end, an entry that is yours."

Perpétua stayed a moment silent, with her hand resting on the open book, on her name repeated for forty years, and felt a thing that caught her by surprise: she wept. She had not wept in front of anyone for decades, for the column does not weep, but there, before the Keeper, before that book that was her life told in entries, the others', never hers, the tears came to her without her being able to hold them.

"How is it that you know all this?" she asked, wiping her face, ashamed. "Of my life, without my telling you."

"You are not the first beam to sit in this chair, Perpétua," said the Keeper, low, with the tenderness of one who has seen many pass. "I have kept the register of Véspera for forty years, and I have learned to see, in the letters, the lives. And there is a kind of woman who always appears, in every generation, the one who holds everything, the saint, the column, who comes here to see to everyone's papers and never to her own, and whom I recognise at the door, by the way she sets her hands on the table, tired, and by the way she says, when I ask her how she is, all is well. You said all is well to me when you came in. And I know what that means. It means that it is not, and that no one has asked you in earnest for so long that you no longer even know how to answer anything else." She laid her hand on her hand. "I have only told you what you already know and have never let yourself say."

Perpétua left the House of the Register with that weighing on her and freeing her at the same time. She crossed the square slowly, with the uncle's papers in her hand, and stopped beside the fountain. And the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her, in the mute way fountains say things to one who is ready to hear them. A fountain holds the water of the mountain and gives it to whoever passes; but the fountain does not hold itself up alone, it rests on a structure, it is held by the stone, by the earth, by many things; and if the fountain had to be, itself, the structure and the water at the same time, to give and to hold itself up alone, it would run dry. And Perpétua understood that she had spent her life being the water and the structure at the same time, giving to the family and holding herself up alone, with no one to hold her; and that it was for this that she was running dry, cracking, giving way, because not even a fountain holds itself up alone, and a woman still less.

She stayed sitting on the rim of the fountain a long time, that afternoon, watching the village pass. A woman came to fill her pitcher, and another helped her lift it to her head, and two old women stopped to talk, and a man wet his face coming from the fields, and Perpétua watched that, the coming and going of the people around the water, the helping one another lift the pitcher to the head, and understood a thing she had never noticed: that to the fountain no one went alone, that to the fountain one went in company, and that they helped one another, and that the weight of a pitcher, shared, was light. And she thought that she, her whole life, had been the only one of the family to carry the pitcher alone, without anyone putting a hand to lift it; not because there were no hands, but because she had never asked, because the column does not ask, and whoever does not ask carries the pitcher alone even in the midst of a fountain full of people who would help. And it was there, on the rim of the fountain, watching the village help one another around the water, that Perpétua decided, for good, that she was going to learn to ask for the hand that had always been missing to her, not because there was no one to give it, but because she had never reached out to ask.

And she went home that afternoon with the truth of the Keeper and that of the fountain, and looked at her weight in another way. No longer as a duty that only she could fulfil, but as a weight she let no one share, and that was killing her. And she understood, with the truth of the Keeper and that of the two houses, which beam she wished to be. Not the Ferreiros', the one that bore alone until it cracked and took the house with it. The Soares', the one that set down in time and left the house standing. She wished that her entry, in the book of Véspera, would say what the Keeper had wished to write, set down in time, and the house stayed; and not the other, fell suddenly, and the house with her. And for that she had to do the thing a main beam most fears, which is to cease to be the only one who holds; she had to accept being a timber among timbers, and not the beam on which everything depends, because a beam on which everything depends is a beam that kills the house on the day it falls, and a timber among many is a house that does not fall for any one being missing.

And she understood that her crossing, from there on, was the hardest she could imagine, harder than carrying: it was to set down. It was to let the house creak and reorganise itself. It was to force the siblings to grow. It was to risk discovering herself a stranger. And it was, at last, after forty years, to put in the book of her life, before the end, an entry that was, for the first time, only hers.
