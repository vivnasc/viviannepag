## 5. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to the papers of the sick uncle at the House of the Register, and it was thus, by one more weight she carried alone, that Perpétua found herself seated before the Keeper, among the books where Véspera keeps the houses and those who held them.

The Keeper, who saw people as she saw the entries, saw to the papers, and noticed that Perpétua saw to them alone, as she saw to everything alone, and said: your family is large, Perpétua. And it is always you who comes here. To the papers of the old ones, to the feasts, to the deaths. I have seen you here for forty years, always you. Where are your siblings? And Perpétua gave the answer of always, they have their lives, I am the one who is left with these things, I am the one with the knack; and the Keeper, who had heard that answer from many Perpétuas, looked at her with those eyes that read between the lines, and rose, and went to a shelf, and took down a book, and searched, and turned it towards Perpétua, and said: look.

And it was the page of the death of Perpétua's mother, forty years back, and the Keeper ran her finger along the entry, and said: here it is. Your mother died, and you were fourteen. And do you see what is set down after? Everything your mother held passed into your name. The old ones in charge, the papers of the house, everything. At fourteen, Perpétua. I remember seeing you come in here, a girl, to see to the papers of a house of grown people, and I thought, poor thing, what a weight for shoulders so young. And you carried it. Forty years. And do you see how, page by page, down the years, everything that is of the House of the Mill goes on appearing in your name, and never, not once, in the name of your siblings? This house, Perpétua, in my book, has a single person holding it up for forty years. And a house that has a single person holding it up is not a strong house. It is a house that falls on the day that person falls.

And the Keeper said a thing Perpétua never forgot: I see the families pass by this table, and I have learned to see those that have a main beam and those that do not. Those that have a beam, like yours, are houses that look strong and are fragile, because they rest entirely on a single timber, and it is enough for that timber to give way for everything to fall. And those that have no beam, those that share the weight among many timbers, are houses that look more fragile, because no one alone holds them, and are the strongest, because if one timber gives way, the others bear up. Your family, Perpétua, was made fragile by you, without your wanting it: by holding everything, you kept it from sharing the weight, and made of it a house of a single column, which is you, and which is tired. The good you can do this family now is not to hold it more. It is to stop holding it alone, so that it may learn, while the column is still standing, to share the weight, before the column falls and there is no longer time to learn.

Perpétua heard this, and it was hard for her, because it turned forty years of good inside out: she had always thought that to hold the family was the greatest good she could do it, and the Keeper was telling her that the greatest good was to stop holding it alone, that her strength had been, without her knowing, the weakness of the house, that by wanting to spare the siblings the weight she had made them incapable of carrying it, and that a house of a single column is a house on the verge of falling. And she understood that she had to do a thing that seemed to her the opposite of love and that was after all its highest form: she had to set down, not only to rest, but to force the house to learn to hold itself, to share the weight, so that the family, on the day she failed, would not fall whole, because it had learned, with her still alive, to bear up without the beam.

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a book-keeper, showed her yet another thing, which was the one that most hurt her and most freed her. She leafed through the book, and said: and look at your name, Perpétua. It appears on every paper of the house for forty years. On those of the old ones, on those of the feasts, on those of the deaths. But see one thing: it appears on none that is yours. There is not, in forty years, a single entry that is only Perpétua's. Not a marriage, for you did not marry, you had no time. Not a journey, not a thing of your own. You are in this book whole, but always holding the others, never living. You are the only person of the House of the Mill who, in my book, in forty years, never did anything for herself. And that, Perpétua, is not the entry of a life. It is the entry of a column. And you are not a column. You are a woman. And you are still in time to put in the book, before the end, an entry that is yours.

Perpétua left the House of the Register with that weighing on her and freeing her, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain, and the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her: that a fountain holds the water of the mountain and gives it to those who pass, but that the fountain does not hold itself up alone, that it rests on a structure, that it is held by the stone, by the earth, by many things, and that if the fountain had to be, itself, the structure and the water at the same time, to give and to hold itself up alone, it would run dry; and that she, Perpétua, had spent her life being the water and the structure at the same time, giving to the family and holding herself up alone, with no one to hold her, and that it was for this that she was running dry, cracking, giving way, because not even a fountain holds itself up alone, and a woman still less.

And she went home, that afternoon, with the truth of the Keeper and that of the fountain, and looked at her weight in another way: no longer as a duty that only she could fulfil, but as a weight she let no one share, and that was killing her, and that her family, to be truly strong, needed not the column bearing everything, but many timbers sharing the weight; and she understood that her crossing, from there on, was the hardest she could imagine, harder than carrying, it was to set down, it was to let the house creak and reorganise itself, it was to force the siblings to grow, it was to risk discovering herself a stranger, and it was, at last, after forty years, to put in the book of her life, before the end, an entry that was, for the first time, only hers.
