## 2. The fourteen years

Perpétua became the column of the House of the Mill in a single day, at fourteen, and the cruellest thing about that day is that no one decided it, no one asked it of her in words; the house needed a beam, looked around, and the eyes settled on her, and she went under the roof, and there she stayed forty years.

Her mother died on a February dawn, suddenly, of the heart, in bed, without time for farewells. Perpétua was fourteen and was the first to know it, because she slept in the next room and heard her father calling his wife in a way she never forgot, a way that was not of calling, it was of already knowing. She got up, and went, and saw her mother in the bed, and saw her father kneeling at the side of the bed with his face against the sheet, and the house, in a few minutes, filled with that silence only deaths bring, a silence with weight, that works its way into the walls.

And then, at daybreak, the house had need. That was what she learned, the girl of fourteen, that morning: that death does not stop the house, that the house, for all its mourning, is hungry in the morning, and the old ones need to be changed, and the little ones need to eat, and the fire must be lit, and someone must see to the burial, and someone must go to the village to say that the mother had died, and someone must hold everything while the father is kneeling at the side of a bed unable to rise.

And there was no such someone. The father was undone, and stayed undone a long time, a man who buried his wife and buried with her the will to govern the house. The siblings were small: Belmiro was ten, Felismina seven, and Tó, the youngest, was a babe of months, who cried in the cradle with hunger and did not know he had been left without a mother. And the old ones, the grandmother already gone simple, an uncle, were old, were to be tended, not to tend anything.

Perpétua remembered an exact scene from that first morning, and it was the foundation stone of all that came after. Tó cried in the cradle, with hunger, for some time, and no one went to him, because the father was in the dead woman's room and the others were small and afraid. And Perpétua, fourteen years old, went to the kitchen, and did what she had seen her mother do a thousand times, warmed the milk, tested it on her wrist, and took the brother into her arms, and gave it to him. And Tó fell quiet, and suckled, and fell asleep in her arms, warm, heavy, alive. And Perpétua, there, standing in the cold kitchen, with the brother asleep in her arms and the mother dead in the next room, felt two things at once that never again let her go, her whole life. She felt an enormous pride, that of being able, of soothing, of holding, of filling, at fourteen, the empty place of the mother. And she felt, beneath the pride, an enormous weight, that of seeing that this, from then on, was hers. That there was no one else. That the brother, and the house, and the little ones, and the old ones, and the undone father, and everything, was now hers to hold.

It was in that scene, with the brother asleep in her arms, that the girl Perpétua died and the column was born. And it was so early, and so without choice, that she never again knew how to separate the pride of holding from the weight of holding, because the two were born to her together, in the same arms, on the same morning, and were, her whole life, the same thing.

She left school that year, without anyone deciding it in words, as nothing was decided. She was among the best in the class, the teacher was fond of her, said that girl could go far if she studied; but a house without a mother, with little ones and old ones, has no way to set the ablest of the children to studying, and the ablest, instead of going far, stays near, stays at home holding what there is. Perpétua remembered the last day of school, though no one had told her it was the last; she remembered putting away her notebooks, and the teacher laying a hand on her shoulder at the door and saying to her, with pity, a thing she understood only years later: it is a pity, Perpétua, a pity. And it was. The other children of her age went on growing as children grow, playing, studying, courting, having a time that was theirs; and Perpétua, at fourteen, passed over to the other side, to the side of those who tend, and never again had a time that was only hers, and went on forgetting, with the years, that she had ever had one. The girl who could go far stayed to hold the house, and the house was left owing her a life it never paid.

The village helped, in the village's way, which is with the mouth. They said, in the square, on the way out of mass, that what a mother those children had lost, but what a mother Perpétua had become. That girl, poor thing, at fourteen, raising her brothers and sister, tending the old ones, holding the house. She is a saint. And each praise was one more brick on the place she had taken, and the place, taken on an emergency, became, year after year, the very person, the Perpétua who holds the house, until one no longer knew where Perpétua ended and the column began.

The father was never the same again, and went on fading at the side of the daughter who had replaced him. In the first years he still went to the fields, still did a few things, but the helm of the house he never took up again, because the daughter had taken it, and an undone man gladly clings to a daughter who holds what he no longer has the strength to hold. And so the father went on living in Perpétua's shadow, like the siblings, leaning on her, letting her decide, letting her see to things, and died years later, slowly, in the back room, cared for by her, without ever, in his whole life, having said to her a word of thanks, because the men of that house did not know how to thank those who carried them, and because to the father, perhaps, it hurt too much to thank the daughter for what should have been his to do. Perpétua cared for her father as she cared for everything, in silence, and buried him, and at the grave, at her mother's side, she understood without knowing how to say it that she was now even more alone in holding the house, because the last adult had died who, for all that he was only a presence, still kept her company at the top of the house; from then on, it was only she and the weight.

And here lay the error, which was no one's error, it was the error of the emergency: a place one takes at fourteen out of a calamity ought to be let go when the calamity passes. And Perpétua's calamity passed. The siblings grew up. The father recovered, in his half-way fashion, and went on living, and died years later without ever having taken back the helm. The house steadied. But Perpétua did not let the place go, because the place was no longer a charge, it was she, and to let it go would be to cease to be who she was. She went on holding the house long after the house needed to be held by a girl; went on mothering the siblings when the siblings were already men and women with their own houses; went on being the column when the house could already have had others, but did not develop them, because where one column holds everything, the other timbers never learn to bear their own weight, they lean, and the column carries them too.

Because this is what happens in families with a main beam, and Perpétua was the living proof: the beam, by holding everything, keeps the others from gaining strength. Her siblings never had to learn to hold anything, because Perpétua held. Belmiro never learned to manage his money, because when it ran short there was Perpétua. Felismina never learned to bear her bad days, because there was the mother-house to flee to. Tó never grew up, at all, because they never let the weight of a single day fall upon him. And Perpétua, by wishing to spare them, by wishing to hold everything so that they would not have to, had made of them, without knowing, adults who did not know how to hold anything, and had made of herself the single column of a house that, without her, had no one to bear it up.

The first Christmas without the mother was that same year, ten months later, and it was Perpétua, fourteen years old, who made it. No one ordered her to; the house looked at her, and she understood that it was hers to make the Christmas, so that the little ones would have Christmas, so that the father would not spend the night looking at the empty chair of his wife. And the girl of fourteen killed the hen, and made the supper as she had seen her mother make it, and laid the table, and seated the siblings, and served them, and to Tó, who was a baby, she gave the milk in her arms in the middle of the supper; and the father, seeing the daughter of fourteen make the Christmas of the dead mother, wept in silence at the head of the table, and was not able to say to her thank you, because the men of that house never knew how to say thank you to those who held them. And Perpétua, fourteen years old, standing to serve the first Christmas without a mother, did not cry, because she had already learned, in ten months, that the column does not cry in front of the house; she kept the crying for after, for the kitchen, alone, at the end of the night, washing the dishes of a Christmas she had made with her girl's hands. And it was the first of forty Christmases the same, all made by her, all served by her, all wept, if there was weeping, alone in the kitchen at the end of the night; until that one, forty years later, on which she leaned against the wall and no one noticed her, which was the same Christmas, the same kitchen, the same loneliness, only with a woman of fifty-two in the place of the girl of fourteen, and forty years of weight in between.

The mother, in dying, had left her the name and the place. Perpétua. The one who lasts, the one who endures, the one who stays. The name had been the mother's promise, who had wished for a daughter who would last; and the daughter fulfilled it in a way the mother did not foresee, lasting beneath a roof for forty years, bearing, without anyone, in forty years, asking the beam how it was. But the mother had not left her, because she could not, the permission one day to let the weight go. And to let the weight go, Perpétua understood much later, was going to be harder than to carry it, because carrying cost the body, but letting go cost the soul, the who-am-I, the only place she had known since the age of fourteen. A person lets go more easily of a weight that breaks her back than of a weight that gives her her name, because what breaks the back is only pain, and what gives the name is the person herself.

And there was the name, which was the whole thing in a single word. Perpétua. The mother had given it to her thinking of lasting, of enduring, of not ending, which is what every mother wants for a daughter, that she last, that she live long; and the daughter had fulfilled the name, had lasted, had endured, but not as the mother had wished. She had endured in the same place for forty years, beneath the same roof, bearing the same house, perpetual as a column is perpetual, motionless, fixed, holding always the same weight. The name, which should have been a blessing of long life, had been a sentence of immobility, because to endure can mean to live long, or it can mean never to leave the spot, and Perpétua had fulfilled the name in the second sense, the sad one, that of one who never changes, that of one who stays perpetually where she was set. And she understood, in those nights, that if she wished to honour the name in the good sense, the one the mother had wished to give her, that of lasting, of living, she had precisely to betray it in the other, the one of staying motionless; she had to cease to be perpetual as a column is and become perpetual as a woman who lasts because she lives, and not who lasts because she is held fast.

It was of this that Perpétua began to remember in the nights that followed that Christmas on which she had leaned against the wall. She remembered in images: the mother in the bed, the father kneeling, Tó suckling in her arms in the cold kitchen, the looks of the village settling on her at fourteen. And there came into her, slowly, an understanding that shook her: that she had not chosen to be the column. She had been chosen, at fourteen, by a calamity, and never again had anyone asked her whether she wished to go on being it, and she herself had never asked, because there was no time, because the house had need, because it was easier to go on than to stop. She had spent her whole life fulfilling a place that had fallen upon her on a February dawn forty years before, without ever having chosen it, and without ever having asked who she would be if she did not fulfil it.

And she understood, with a shiver that was not of the cold, that she was fifty-two years old and had never been anything but the column. That she did not know who Perpétua was without the weight. That she had never had time to discover what she liked, what she wanted, what she dreamed, because since fourteen her time had been the others'. And that if she one day set the weight down she would find herself before a stranger, herself, a woman of fifty-two who had never lived for herself; and that this stranger frightened her more than the weight, because the weight she knew, and the stranger she did not, and it was easier to go on carrying the house, however much it broke her, than to stop and have to know, at last, at fifty-two, the woman left unlived beneath the girl who made herself a beam on a February morning.
