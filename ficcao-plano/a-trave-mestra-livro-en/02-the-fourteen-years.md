## 2. The fourteen years

Perpétua became the column of the House of the Mill at the age of fourteen, in a single day, and did not cease to be it for forty years, and it was for that reason that she confused, her whole life, holding the house with being herself: it was the thing she became at fourteen, and she never again had time to become another.

Her mother died when Perpétua was fourteen, and died suddenly, leaving a house full of younger children, an undone father, some old ones in her charge, and the emptiness a mother leaves in a house, which is the emptiness of one who held everything without anyone seeing. And someone had to hold, because a house cannot be left without a column, and the father could not, undone as he was, and the siblings were small, and the old ones were old, and one looked around, in that house in mourning, in search of someone to hold, and the eyes settled on Perpétua, who was fourteen and had the knack, who was the most capable of the children, who did not cry in front of the others, who already helped her mother with the chores, and without anyone deciding it in council, without words, only with looks and with the force of circumstance, the House of the Mill handed Perpétua, at fourteen, the place of the dead mother, and Perpétua accepted it, because there was no one to accept it, and held the house, and never again let it go.

And at fourteen it was heroic, and it was, in a way, good for her, because it gave her a place in the midst of the mourning, gave her something to be for when everything was collapsing, and Perpétua, who had lost her mother, found in the column a way not to sink with the house, to be needed, to be someone at a moment when she might have been only one more lost orphan. She held the small siblings, all but raised them, made herself their mother at fourteen; cared for the old ones; held the father; set the house going; and the village, which saw, admired that girl who held a house of grown people, and said that a mother Perpétua had lost, but what a mother Perpétua had become, and each praise was one more brick in the place she had taken, and the place, taken at fourteen out of necessity, became, year after year, her identity, the thing she was, the Perpétua who holds the house.

And there lay the error, which was no one's error, it was the error of circumstance: the place one takes at fourteen for an emergency ought to be set down when the emergency passes, and Perpétua's emergency passed, the siblings grew, the father recovered or died, the house steadied; but Perpétua did not set the place down, because the place was no longer a charge, it was she, and to set it down would be to cease to be who she was, and so she went on holding the house long after the house needed to be held by a girl, went on mothering the siblings when the siblings were already men and women with their own houses, went on being the column when the house already had, could have, other columns, but she did not develop them, because Perpétua held everything, and where one column holds everything, the other walls never learn to bear their weight, they stay decorative, they lean on the column, and the column carries them too.

Because this is what happens in families with a main beam, and Perpétua was the proof: the beam, by holding everything, prevents the other timbers from gaining strength. Perpétua's siblings never had to learn to hold anything, because Perpétua held; never had to care for the old ones, because Perpétua cared; never had to run to one another's crises, because Perpétua ran; and so they grew, the siblings, without developing the capacity to bear weight, dependent on the column, light not because they were strong but because they had a beam that carried all their weight, and Perpétua, by wanting to spare them, by wanting to hold everything so that they would not have to, had made of them, without knowing, adults who did not know how to hold anything, and had made of herself the single column of a house that, without her, had no one to bear it up.

The mother, in dying, had left Perpétua the name and the place, but had not left her, because she could not, the permission one day to let it go. And Perpétua, who had become the column on a day of mourning at fourteen, now carried, at fifty-two, not only the weight of the house, but the weight of a whole identity built upon holding, and to let the weight go would be, for her, harder than to carry it, because carrying cost her body, but letting go cost her soul, her who-am-I, the only place she had known since the age of fourteen, and a person lets go more easily of a weight that breaks her back than of a weight that gives her her name, because what breaks the back is only pain, and what gives the name is the person herself.

She remembered an exact scene from that time, the one that had stayed as the first stone of the column. It was a few days after her mother's burial, and the house was still full of the stunning of grief, and the youngest of the siblings, who was still almost a baby, was crying with hunger, and no one was going to him, because the father was shut in his room and the others were small and the old ones were old; and Perpétua, fourteen, went to the kitchen, and did what she had seen her mother do, warmed the milk, and took the brother into her arms, and gave it to him, and the brother fell quiet, and fell asleep in her arms; and Perpétua, there, with the brother asleep in her arms, at fourteen, felt two things at once that never again left her: she felt an enormous pride, that of being able, of soothing, of holding, of taking the place of the mother who was missing; and she felt, beneath the pride, an enormous weight, that of seeing that this, from then on, was hers, that there was no one else, that the brother, and the house, and the old ones, and everything, was now hers to hold. And it was in that scene, with the brother asleep in her arms, that the girl Perpétua died and the column was born, and it was so early, and so without choice, that she never again knew how to separate the pride of holding from the weight of holding, because the two were born to her together, in the same arms, on the same day, and were, her whole life, the same thing.

It was of this that Perpétua began to remember in those days, after the Christmas on which she had leaned against the wall, and she remembered it in images, in the dead mother, in the house full of little ones, in the looks that settled on her at fourteen, in the weight she took up that day and never again set down, and there came into her, slowly, an understanding that shook her: that she had not chosen to be the column, she had been chosen, at fourteen, by an emergency, and that no one ever again asked her whether she wished to go on being it, and that she herself never asked, because there was no time, because the house needed it, because it was easier to go on than to stop, and that she had spent a whole life fulfilling a place assigned to her on a mourning afternoon forty years before, without ever having chosen it and without ever having asked who she would be if she did not fulfil it.

And she understood, with a shiver, that she was fifty-two and had never been anything but the column, that she did not know who Perpétua was without the weight, that she had never had time to discover what she wanted, what she liked, what she dreamed, because since fourteen her time had been the others', the house's, the weight's, and that if one day she set the weight down, if one day she ceased to be the column, she would find herself before a stranger, herself, a woman of fifty-two who had never lived for herself, and that this stranger frightened her more than the weight, because the weight she knew, and the stranger she did not, and it was easier to go on carrying the house, however much it broke her, than to stop and have to know, at last, at fifty-two, the woman left unlived beneath the column she became at fourteen.
