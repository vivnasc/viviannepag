## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Perpétua, daughter of the House of the Mill, the one who was the column of the family from the age of fourteen, learned, after forty years, to set down the weight that was cracking her, and did not set down the family, she set down the weight; and be it set down that the house, at first, creaked, and took fright, and seemed it would fall, and did not fall, it reorganised itself, and the other timbers, which leaned on the beam, discovered that they knew how to bear, because they had always known, they had only never had to do it. And be it set down that Perpétua, after the column, found at last the woman left unlived beneath the weight, and learned, late and clumsy, the thing everyone learns young and that she had never learned: to be a person, and not a beam.

The register keeps those who hold the houses, and there are names that appear on every paper of a family and on none that is their own. This Keeper saw, in the book, the name of Perpétua across forty years of deaths, feasts, cares and decisions of the House of the Mill, always holding the others, never living; and saw, at last, appear the first entry that was only hers, not of a weight she carried, but of a life that was beginning, and it was a tomato planted out of pleasure, and an afternoon at the window doing nothing, and a table at which, for the first time, she sat down and let herself be served. And the Keeper understood that Perpétua had fulfilled, at last, the name her mother had given her, but the opposite of what the mother had wished: she did not last by holding the house, she lasted by learning to set it down, and that was her permanence.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Perpétua who holds everyone and whom no one holds, who is the column the whole house leans on and whom no one asks how she is, who confuses being needed with being loved and holding with existing, let her know what she learned and what this house records: that a beam too needs to rest; that to set down is not to let fall, it is to let the house learn to hold itself up; that by carrying the others with love one keeps them small, and that the greatest good of a column is not to carry better, it is to set down, and to let them grow; and that on the far side of the weight is the question the beam never had time to ask, who am I carrying nothing, and that the answer, when at last it comes, is not the emptiness one feared, it is the life, which was waiting, on the far side, to be, for the first time, lived.

Perpétua traded the weight for the life, the column for the woman, the house of a single beam for the house of many timbers. And in the houses of Véspera, since the one in which she sat at last at the table and let herself be served, there is one more woman who no longer confuses her worth with the weight she bears, because she learned that she was worth without carrying, that she had always been worth, and that it is never too late, not even at fifty-two, to set the beam down and begin, at last, to be a person.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
