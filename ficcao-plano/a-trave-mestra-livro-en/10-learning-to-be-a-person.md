## 10. Learning to be a person

And Perpétua began to learn to be a person, and she was clumsy at it, as is everything begun late, and there were those who laughed, and those who found it strange, and she herself felt, many times, ridiculous, a woman of fifty-two doing the things of one beginning to live, but she did them, because she had understood that either she began there, clumsy, or she would never begin, and would die a column, having never been a person.

She began with the small things, which were, for her, enormous. One afternoon, instead of going to see whether anyone needed her, she sat at the window and did nothing, and it was hard for her as a penance, because her body, used to being useful, cried out to her get up, do something, there is always something to do; and Perpétua had to force herself to stay, not to do, to bear the discomfort of being useless, and she discovered, after many afternoons like that, that the not-doing, which at first hurt her, began slowly to suit her, and that there was a thing, in staying at the window watching the mountain with no weight waiting for her, that she had never had and that was, she understood with astonishment, quiet, the quiet she had never known, because she had never stopped long enough to feel it.

Then she went to the things she might like, and she went by touch, because she did not know what she liked, she had never had time to know. She tried. She planted some things in the yard, not because it served the house, but only because, only to see, and she discovered that she liked to see what she planted grow, and it was the first thing she did in life that served nothing and no one, that only gave pleasure to her, and Perpétua, on picking the first tomato from a plant she had set out of pleasure and not out of need, felt a small and new joy, the joy of a thing that was only hers, that she did not carry, that she owed to no one, that was only pleasure, and understood, in that small joy, how much life owed her, how many small joys the weight had eaten, and how much there was still, perhaps, to be picked.

And there was an afternoon that stayed, one of those that mark, and that was, seen from outside, the smallest thing in the world. Perpétua took the bus and went to the larger town, alone, not to see to anything, with no papers to deliver, no old ones to take to the doctor, no errands from anyone; she went only to go, only to walk, and sat in a café, alone, a woman of fifty-two seated in a café in the middle of the afternoon with nothing to do, and at first she was ashamed, she felt everyone was looking at her, that she was there wasting time, that she ought to be being useful somewhere; and then, slowly, with the coffee before her and the afternoon passing and no one needing her, she felt a thing she had never felt, that of being in a place only out of pleasure, that of her time being hers, that of not owing that hour to anyone; and it was so strange to her and so good that tears came to her eyes, there in the café, not of sorrow, of a kind of astonishment, the astonishment of one who tastes at fifty-two a thing everyone tastes young, which is an afternoon of one's own, and perceives, in the pleasure of it, how many afternoons life took from her, and how much still, perhaps, was left her to live.

She learned to say no, which was the thing that cost her the most, because a column does not say no, a column bears; and Perpétua had to learn, slowly, with setbacks, to say I cannot, to say not today, to say you see to that, and each no cost her like tearing skin, because behind each no was the fear of ceasing to be needed, of disappointing, of being no longer wanted if she did not carry; but she went on saying it, and went on seeing that the world did not fall when she said no, that the siblings did not stop wanting her, that they went on wanting her, and that after all they had never wanted her for the weight she carried, they had wanted her, and that it was she, and not they, who had confused being wanted with being useful, and that by learning to say no she was discovering that she was wanted even without carrying, which was a thing that she, deep down, had never believed.

And there was a greater learning, harder than all of them, which was to learn to let herself be cared for. Because Perpétua, who had cared for everyone for forty years, did not know how to be cared for, and when the siblings, grown, began to want to care for her, to offer to see to her things, to ask her Perpétua, and you, what do you need, she did not know how to receive, she was flustered, she said I need nothing, I will see to it; and she had to learn, by force, to let herself be cared for, to say yes when they offered, to receive, and it was one of the hardest things, because to receive, for one who only knew how to give, is a humility that costs, it is to admit that one also needs, that one is not only a column, that one is also, like everyone, someone who needs to be held sometimes; and Perpétua, by letting herself be cared for by the siblings she had carried, by receiving from them what she had always only given, discovered a new tenderness, that of being cared for by those one has cared for, which is perhaps the highest form of a family's love, that of the care turning round, of the one who carried being in turn carried, and so closing the circle that a column, by carrying everything alone, never lets close.

And Perpétua understood, in this clumsy learning of being a person, a deep thing: that the life left over to her, after setting the weight down, was not a lesser life, it was a greater one, because it was, for the first time, a life of her own; that the years left to her, after the column, were not the remainder, they were the best, because they were the first in which she was going to live for herself; and that it was never too late, she discovered, to begin to be a person, that even at fifty-two, even after a life of being a column, there was still time to learn to live, to discover what one likes, to pick the small joys the weight had eaten, and to be, at last, before the end, not the one who holds the house, but a woman, with likings, with quiets, with a life that was only hers. And she went on learning, slowly, late, clumsy, and each small thing she learned was a joy, and Perpétua, who had spent her life feeling the weight, began, for the first time, to feel the life, and it was good, she discovered, the life, when one sets it down off one's back and puts it, at last, before one's eyes, to live it.
