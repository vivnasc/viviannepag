## 10. Learning to be a person

And Perpétua began to learn to be a person, and she was clumsy at it, as is everything begun late. There were those who laughed, there were those who found it strange, and she herself felt, many times, ridiculous, a woman of fifty-two doing the things of one beginning to live. But she did them, because she had understood that either she began there, clumsy, or she would never begin, and would die a column, having never been a person.

She began with the small things, which were, for her, enormous. One afternoon, instead of going to see whether anyone needed her, she sat at the window and did nothing. And it was hard for her as a penance, because her body, used to being useful, cried out to her get up, do something, there is always something to do. Perpétua had to force herself to stay, not to do, to bear the discomfort of being useless. And she discovered, after many afternoons like that, that the not-doing, which at first hurt her, began slowly to suit her; and that there was a thing, in staying at the window watching the mountain with no weight waiting for her, that she had never had and that was, she understood with astonishment, quiet, the quiet she had never known, because she had never stopped long enough to feel it.

And she learned, slowly, the difference between being lonely and being alone, which are different things and that she had confused her whole life. To be lonely, the loneliness of the column, that of one who holds everyone and whom no one holds, had been her company of forty years, and it hurt. But to be alone, simply alone, in an afternoon of her own, with no one needing her and without needing anyone, was another thing, and she discovered that it could be good. That there was a quiet in being alone by choice, at the window, with the mountain, without the distress of one who is lonely bearing the world. That the loneliness of the column was to be alone with a weight, and the new aloneness was to be alone with no weight at all, and that the first crushed and the second rested. She learned to be alone without feeling lonely, which is one of the hardest arts and that she, paradoxically, could only learn after setting the weight down, because while she carried she had been always lonely and never alone, always surrounded by people needing her and always with no one for herself; and now, without the weight, she could be alone with no one around and, for the first time, not feel lonely, because she was no longer waiting for someone to hold her, she had learned to hold herself.

Then she went to the things she might like, and she went by touch, because she did not know what she liked, she had never had time to know. She tried. She planted some things in the yard, not because it served the house, but only because, only to see; and she discovered that she liked to see what she planted grow, and it was the first thing she did in life that served nothing and no one, only gave pleasure to her. And on picking the first tomato from a plant she had set out of pleasure and not out of need, Perpétua felt a small and new joy, the joy of a thing that was only hers, that she did not carry, that she owed to no one. And she understood, in that small joy, how much life owed her, how many small joys the weight had eaten, and how much there was still, perhaps, to be picked.

She bought, one day, a thing for herself, without its being needed. She went to a fair, and saw a shawl, a fine shawl, of a warm colour, and stood looking at it, and the seller said to her it suits you, take it, and Perpétua was about to say no, I do not need it, which was what she always said to everything that was for herself; and she stopped, and thought, and understood that she did not need it, it was true, but that she liked it, and that liking was reason enough, that her whole life she had bought only what was needed, for the house, for the others, for the old ones, and never a thing only because she liked it, only for herself. And she bought the shawl. And it was strange to her, and it was almost sinful, to spend money on a thing she did not need, only because she found it fine and liked it; and she took it home almost in secret, like one who carries a forbidden luxury, and put it on, and looked at herself in the mirror with the shawl of a warm colour, a woman of fifty-two, and found herself, for the first time in she did not know how long, fine to look at, and was ashamed to find herself fine, and liked being ashamed, because it was a new shame, that of one who allows herself, at last, a thing only for herself.

And there was an afternoon that stayed, one of those that mark, and that was, seen from outside, the smallest thing in the world. Perpétua took the bus and went to the larger town, alone, not to see to anything, with no papers to deliver, no old ones to take to the doctor, no errands from anyone. She went only to go. And she sat in a café, alone, a woman of fifty-two seated in a café in the middle of the afternoon with nothing to do. At first she was ashamed, she felt everyone was looking at her, that she was there wasting time, that she ought to be being useful somewhere. And then, slowly, with the coffee before her and the afternoon passing and no one needing her, she felt a thing she had never felt: that of being in a place only out of pleasure, that of her time being hers, that of not owing that hour to anyone. And it was so strange to her and so good that tears came to her eyes, there in the café, not of sorrow, of a kind of astonishment, the astonishment of one who tastes at fifty-two a thing everyone tastes young, which is an afternoon of one's own, and perceives, in the pleasure of it, how many afternoons life took from her.

And she made a friend, a thing she had not had since she was a girl. Her name was Benvinda, she was of her age, a widow, she lived alone in a house nearby, and they began to speak to one another at the fountain, and then to take coffee one at the other's house, and Perpétua discovered a thing she had not had since the age of fourteen: a relationship that was not a duty. Benvinda asked nothing of her, did not depend on her, did not want her for what she carried; she wanted her for the company, for the conversation, for the pleasure of being with her, and that was so new to Perpétua that at first she distrusted it, that at first she sought, without noticing, a way to be useful to Benvinda, to carry something of hers, because she did not know how to be in a relationship without carrying. And Benvinda, who was sharp, perceived it, and said to her one day, at the table, oh Perpétua, you do not have to do anything for me, you do not have to bring me anything, you do not have to solve anything of mine; I am fond of you, it is enough for me that you come and that you stay and that you tell me your things. And Perpétua, on hearing that, that you tell me your things, was left not knowing what to say, because no one, in forty years, had asked her to tell her own things; they asked her to solve theirs. And it was Benvinda who taught her, slowly, coffee by coffee, the thing the family had never taught her: to be loved without carrying, to be without serving, to receive a friendship that charged her nothing, which was, Perpétua discovered, the first friendship of her life that was only hers, and not a post of service.

She learned to say no, which was the thing that cost her the most, because a column does not say no, a column bears. She had to learn, slowly, with setbacks, to say I cannot, to say not today, to say you see to that; and each no cost her like tearing skin, because behind each no was the fear of ceasing to be needed, of disappointing, of being no longer wanted if she did not carry. But she went on saying it, and went on seeing that the world did not fall when she said no, that the siblings did not stop wanting her, that they went on wanting her; and that after all they had never wanted her for the weight she carried, they had wanted her, and that it was she, and not they, who had confused being wanted with being useful. And on learning to say no, she discovered that she was wanted even without carrying, which was a thing that she, deep down, had never believed.

And she went back to reading, a thing she had not done since school, since she left it at fourteen. She bought a book, in the larger town, on one of those afternoons she now had for herself, and it was strange to her to buy a book, a woman of her age, what a thing, people would say, what does she want with books; and she took it home, and at night, instead of falling into bed exhausted as she had fallen for forty years, she read. She read slowly, for she had lost the habit, and it was hard for her at first, but the liking took hold of her, and she discovered, in the book, the thing the girl of fourteen had had and that the weight had eaten: the curiosity about the world out beyond the mountain, the wish to know, to learn of other lives that were not her own. The teacher had said, forty years before, it is a pity, this girl could go far; and Perpétua, at fifty-two, with a book in her hands by the lamplight, understood that perhaps she was still in time to go far, not with her feet, for that was already late, but with her head, with reading, with the curiosity that after all had not died, had only fallen asleep beneath the weight, and that woke now, page by page, as the song had woken in the yard. The girl who could go far had not gone far; but the woman she was could still, she discovered, go far seated, at night, with a book, which is a way of travelling that does not need to ask anyone to stay with the old ones.

And there was a greater learning, harder than all of them, which was to learn to let herself be cared for. Because Perpétua, who had cared for everyone for forty years, did not know how to be cared for; and when the siblings, grown, began to want to care for her, to offer to see to her things, to ask her oh sister, and you, what do you need, she did not know how to receive, she was flustered, she said I need nothing, I will see to it. She had to learn, by force, to let herself be cared for, to say yes when they offered, to receive, and it was one of the hardest things, because to receive, for one who only knew how to give, is a humility that costs, it is to admit that one also needs. One day, Felismina arrived unexpectedly at the House of the Mill with the lunch already made, brought from her house, and said, today I cooked, sister, you sit down and eat, for it is my turn; and Perpétua, who was about to refuse out of habit, who was about to say do not trouble yourself, held herself back, and sat down, and let her sister serve her, and ate what her sister had made for her, and it was the first time in forty years that she ate a meal another had set before her in her own house. And it was strange to her, and it was sweet, the new sweetness of being cared for by those one has cared for, which is perhaps the highest form of a family's love, that of the care turning round.

And one morning, hanging the washing in the yard, Perpétua found herself singing. It was not a decision; it came out of her, by itself, an old song, of the kind she knew as a girl, and she sang it low, hanging the sheets in the sun, and only halfway did she notice what she was doing, and stopped, astonished, because she had not sung for forty years, since before February. The girl who liked to sing, the one she had thought buried for good beneath the column, was there, after all, alive, at the bottom, and it had been enough to set the weight down for her to breathe and open her mouth again. And Perpétua, in the yard, with the sheets shaking in the wind and the song on her lips, wept and laughed at the same time, because it was like meeting again a person one had thought dead, and the person was she, the Perpétua of before, the girl left unlived, whom the weight had buried and whom the lightness, slowly, was digging up. She went back to singing, from then on, without being ashamed, and the village, which had always seen her grave and bent, began to hear Perpétua humming at the chores, and found it strange, and liked it, because a beam that sings is a beam that has set the weight down.

And she began to occupy herself with her niece, Felismina's daughter, the girl of fourteen in whom the family was beginning to find a knack for holding the house. Perpétua, who recognised in that girl the danger that had been her own, set herself to taking care of her in another way: not to carry her, but to push her towards life. She took her with her to the larger town, showed her the world out beyond the mountain, gave her books, said to her study, my dear, go far, do not stay bound to this house as I stayed. And when the family set the niece a weight that was not hers, oh you go, for you have the knack, Perpétua was the first to say no, leave the girl, she has her school, she has her life, that we do who are grown. Because she had understood that the greatest good she could do with the life left over to her was to keep the niece from repeating hers, was to cut, in one generation, the chain that had made of her a column at fourteen; and that to spare the niece the weight was a way of redeeming herself, retroactively, of the weight no one had spared her, was to give the girl of fourteen of now what no one had given the girl of fourteen she had been.

And she understood, in this clumsy learning of being a person, a deep thing: that the life left over to her, after setting the weight down, was not a lesser life, it was a greater one, because it was, for the first time, a life of her own. That the years left to her, after the column, were not the remainder, they were the best, because they were the first in which she was going to live for herself. And that it was never too late, she discovered, to begin to be a person; that even at fifty-two, even after a life of being a column, there was still time to learn to live, to discover what one likes, to pick the small joys the weight had eaten, and to be, at last, before the end, not the one who holds the house, but a woman, with likings, with quiets, with a life that was only hers. And she went on learning, slowly, late, clumsy, and each small thing she learned was a joy; and Perpétua, who had spent her life feeling the weight, began, for the first time, to feel the life, and it was good, she discovered, the life, when one sets it down off one's back and puts it, at last, before one's eyes, to live it.
