## 6. Setting down the first weight

To set down, for a column, is not done all at once, for to set everything down at once would be to let fall; it is done weight by weight. And the first weight Perpétua set down was small and cost her as if it were the greatest, because it was not the weight that cost, it was letting go of the place of the one who carried it.

She decided to begin with uncle Aníbal, who was the heaviest and most urgent weight. And instead of seeing to everything alone, as she had always done, she did a thing she had never done: she called the siblings again, and this time it was not to inform them. She seated the three of them at the kitchen table, Belmiro, Felismina, Tó, and instead of telling them I will see to it, as she always said, she said to them a word she had never said in forty years.

"I need."

It was as hard for her as pulling a tooth. Because a column does not need, a column bears, and to say I need was to come down from the place, was to admit she did not bear, was to expose her weakness to those who had always seen her strong. But she said it.

"I need you to see to the uncle with me. I can no longer alone. My body no longer lets me. I went to the doctor, and the doctor told me that if I do not set down, I fall. And if I fall, everything falls, because you never learned to hold anything, because I never let you. So now you are going to learn. You, Belmiro, take the papers and the uncle's accounts. You, Felismina, take the care, the turns watching him. And you, Tó, are going to have to put your hand to it for the first time in your life. I will help, I will teach, but I will not do it alone."

And the siblings were disconcerted, because they did not know that Perpétua, the one who asks; they knew the other, the one who sees to it, the one who bears, the one who says leave it with me. And their first reaction was the predictable one, of those who grew used to a column and find it strange that she should ask: they tried to give her back the weight.

"Oh, Perpétua," said Belmiro, "but you are the one who knows about that, you have the knack, I cannot be at this from the city. You do it as always, and I will send the money, I will send whatever is needed."

"Sister," said Felismina, "I have my house, my own, I cannot come here to do turns. You are here, you live here, it is easier for you. We will help with what we can."

And there it was again, the we will help with what we can, the sending money from afar, the excuse of it is easier for you, the fine way of leaning on the column without seeming to. And the Perpétua of before would have accepted, would have said all right, leave it with me, and would have carried. But the Perpétua of that afternoon, with the doctor fresh in her head, did not accept. She said a thing she had never said, and that changed everything.

"No. I am not going to see to this alone. And it is not easier for me, Belmiro, it is that I am the one who pays for the ease with my body. You have your lives, I know. I too had mine. I never lived it, because I went about carrying yours." Her voice trembled, but did not give way. "I did not know how to see to a house of grown people at fourteen, and I learned, because I had to learn, because there was no one else. Now there is. Now you learn. I do not ask you everything. I ask you your part. Which is just."

There were protests, there were those who were hurt, there was Belmiro saying that his sister had grown odd, that she was not the one of always, and Felismina weeping a little, that the sister had never spoken to them like that. And Perpétua had to bear that hurt of the siblings, which was perhaps the hardest thing of all, because a column that sets the weight down disappoints those who leaned on her, and those who lean, on feeling the column draw away, feel abandoned, and are hurt, and make the column feel guilty. And guilt is the last weapon the house uses to set the beam carrying again.

Felismina, hurt, was the one who handled the guilt best, without knowing that she handled it. She telephoned her one night, after a bad day with the uncle, and said to her, with her voice trembling: oh sister, I do not know if I am able to do this, I have never tended an old man, and you force me, and I have my house, my own, and I go about in distress, and you, who always did this so well, now leave me here alone with the uncle in my arms. You have changed, Perpétua. Before you were not like this. Before you helped. And each word of Felismina's was a needle in the right place, in the place of the guilt, and Perpétua, on the other end of the line, felt her heart tighten, felt herself bad, felt the wish to say all right, leave it, I will come, I will see to it, as she had said a thousand times. She had the sentence in her mouth. And it was the hardest thing of that night to swallow it, not to say it, because to say it was to draw back, was to carry again, was to solve the guilt at the cost of cracking herself again.

And Perpétua felt the guilt. She felt herself bad for not carrying, felt herself selfish, heard within the voice that said they need you, how can you refuse, what woman are you who does not help her family. And she had to answer that voice, with the doctor's truth: I am not refusing to help. I am refusing to carry everything alone. I am letting them learn to carry their part. And that is not selfishness. It is the greatest good I can do them, which is not to let them fall on the day I fall, because they will have learned, with me alive, to hold themselves up.

And a thing happened that Perpétua did not expect, and that was the beginning of the proof that the doctor and the Keeper were right: the siblings, set before the I need, before the I will not do it alone, before the necessity of carrying their part, began, with difficulty, to grumble, but to carry. Belmiro, set to the papers, saw to the papers, badly at first, fell behind, made muddles, and then, by force, better. Felismina went to do a turn watching the uncle, and discovered that she knew how, that she had always known, she had only never had to do it. And Tó, the spoiled one, set for the first time in his life to see to a thing in earnest, got muddled, complained, but did it. And Perpétua, seeing the siblings carry, slowly, their part, understood, with an astonishment that was almost joy, that they were capable, that they had always been, that they did not hold not because they did not know how, but because they had never had to know, because she had carried them; and that it was enough for her to set down for them to discover that they knew how to hold themselves up.

And to set down, Perpétua discovered, gave more work at first than to carry, because to set down is to teach, and to teach is slower than to do. She had to show Felismina how to change the uncle without hurting her back, how to turn the old man in the bed, how to give him the broth slowly so he would not choke, how to know, by the smell and by the face, when he was worsening. Things she had done for two years without thinking, and that she had to put into words, slowly, with patience, while Felismina got muddled and said oh sister, I am not able, and almost let the old man fall, and Perpétua, within, with the instinct of forty years, had to hold herself back so as not to say leave it, leave it, I will do it, it is quicker. Because it was quicker, yes. To do it herself was always quicker than to teach it. But quicker was not better; quicker was the column's trap, the excuse with which the beam never lets the other timbers learn. And Perpétua, biting her tongue, let Felismina get muddled, and err, and learn, even though it cost her to see badly done what she did well, because she understood that to let it be done badly is the price of teaching, and that a column that only lets be done what is well done is a column that never sets anything down.

And there was, on that first night of weight set down, a detail that moved her and that she kept. Felismina, who had taken the care of the uncle, telephoned her at the end of the day. Not to give her back the weight, as she had always done; for a thing she had never done. To ask her how Perpétua's day had gone.

"So, sister, how are you?" said Felismina. "Today was a hard one, the uncle's. Did you bear up?"

And it was the first time in forty years that a sibling telephoned her to ask about her, and not to ask of her. And Perpétua, on the other end of the line, did not know how to answer, because she was not used to being asked, and got muddled, and said she was well, as she always said. But within, on hanging up, she understood a thing that shook her: that by setting the weight down, by saying I need, by ceasing to be only the one who bears, she had opened in the siblings a space to see her, to care for her, which while she had been only the column had never existed, because one does not ask a column how it is. And that perhaps, by setting the weight down, she was not only relieving herself; she was teaching the family to see her, at last, not as the beam that bears, but as the woman who also needs, sometimes, to be asked how her day was.

And there was the first night on which it was not she who went to the uncle. It was Felismina's night, and Perpétua lay in her bed knowing that, at such an hour, the uncle was going to call, and that it was not going to be she who got up, it was going to be Felismina. And it was an enormous strangeness to her, lying in the dark, to hear, far off, the uncle call, and not to get up. Her body, used these two years to getting up at the old man's first moan, got up by itself, by instinct, and Perpétua found herself already sitting up in bed, already with her feet on the floor, on her way to the uncle's room; and she had to force herself to lie down again, to let Felismina go, not to go to him, and it was one of the hardest things she had done, to stay lying down hearing the old man call and knowing it was not hers to go, because the instinct to care, after forty years, is stronger than the will, and to disobey it costs like disobeying one's own body. She heard Felismina get up, go, see to the uncle, and the house settle; and she lay there, with her eyes open, learning the most unnatural thing a column can learn, which is to hear the house have need and not go to it, to let another go, to stay still while the instinct cries go.

And she set down, in those days, a second weight, which was that of the money. When Belmiro telephoned, the following Sunday, with the request of always, oh sister, this month was tight, can you save me, Perpétua, for the first time in forty years, said a different thing. She said, Belmiro, this month I cannot. And it is not only this month. I went about my life taking from my reserve to plug your holes, and I never said anything to you, and you never learned not to make holes, because there was always Perpétua to plug them. Well, now there is not. You earn, you have a life, you do your sums and govern yourself, like everyone. I will help you think, if you like, but I will plug your holes no more with my money. And Belmiro fell silent, offended, oh sister, but I am in distress, and Perpétua bore the silence, and the offence, and did not give in, and hung up with her heart tight, thinking herself bad; and she went to bed feeling selfish, hearing the voice that said he is your brother, he is in distress, how can you. But the following month Belmiro managed alone, did his sums, tightened his belt, did not make the hole, because there was no one to plug it; and Perpétua understood that by plugging his holes for forty years she had taught him to make them, and that by ceasing to plug them she was, at last, teaching him not to make them, which was a greater good than all the money she had given him.

It was small, this first setting down, and it did not solve everything, and there were to be setbacks, and days on which the siblings tried again to give her back the weight, and days on which she, out of habit, almost took it up again. But the beginning was made. The column had set the first weight down, and the house, instead of falling, had done what the doctor and the Keeper had said: it had creaked, had taken fright, had reorganised itself, and the other timbers had begun, with difficulty, to bear their part. And Perpétua, at night, after setting the first weight down, felt a thing she had not felt for forty years: she felt her body a little lighter. Not much. Only one weight less. But enough to perceive, in the small relief, the size of the weight she carried, and to want, for the first time, to go on setting down, weight by weight, until she discovered, on the far side of them all, the woman left unlived beneath the column, and who began, in that first relief, to breathe.
