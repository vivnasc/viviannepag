## 6. Setting down the first weight

To set down, for a column, is not done all at once, for to set everything down at once would be to let fall; it is done weight by weight, and the first weight Perpétua set down was small and cost her as if it were the greatest, because it was not the weight that cost, it was letting go of the place of the one who carried it.

She decided to begin with the sick uncle, who was the heaviest and most urgent weight, and instead of carrying him alone, as she had always done, she did a thing she had never done: she called the siblings, and instead of telling them I will see to it, as she always said, she told them I need you to see to it. And it was the first time in forty years that Perpétua said to the siblings I need, and it was as hard for her as pulling a tooth, because a column does not need, a column bears, and to say I need was to come down from the place, was to admit that she did not bear, was to expose her weakness to those who had always seen her strong.

And the siblings, on hearing I need, were disconcerted, because they did not know that Perpétua, the one who asks; they knew the other, the one who sees to it, the one who bears, the one who says leave it with me. And their first reaction was the predictable one, of those who grew used to a column and find it strange that she should ask: they tried to give her back the weight. They said what they always said, oh Perpétua, but you are the one who knows, you have the knack, we do not know how to see to that, you do it as always, and we will help with what we can. And the we will help with what we can was the formula with which they had always given her back the weight, seeming to lend a hand and in truth leaning again on the column, and Perpétua, before, would have accepted, would have said all right, leave it with me, and would have carried; but that time, with the doctor's truth and the Keeper's, she did not accept.

She said a thing she had never said, and that changed everything: no. I am not going to see to this alone. I can no longer. My body no longer lets me. And you are going to have to learn, because I will not always be here. You, she said to one, take the papers and the accounts. You, she said to the other, take the care, the turns watching the uncle. And you, she said to the youngest, you are going to have to put your hand to it for the first time in your life. I will help, I will teach, but I will not do it alone. And the siblings protested, oh Perpétua, but we do not know, but we have our lives, but you are the one who always did it; and Perpétua, holding herself so as not to give way, so as not to take up the weight again only to end the protest, said: I did not know either, at fourteen, and I learned, because I had to learn. Now you learn. And your lives, my siblings, I too had mine, and I never lived it, because I went about carrying yours. It is time you carried your part. I do not ask everything of you. I ask your part. Which is just.

And it was hard, and there was resistance, and there were those who were hurt, and those who thought Perpétua had changed, that she had grown odd, that she was no longer the one of always; and Perpétua had to bear that hurt of the siblings, which was perhaps the hardest thing of all, because a column that sets the weight down disappoints those who leaned on her, and those who lean, on feeling the column draw away, feel abandoned, and are hurt, and make the column feel guilty, and guilt is the last weapon the house uses to set the beam carrying again. And Perpétua felt the guilt, felt herself bad for not carrying, felt herself selfish, heard within the voice that said they need you, how can you refuse, what woman are you who does not help her family; and she had to answer that voice, with the doctor's truth: I am not refusing to help. I am refusing to carry everything alone. I am letting them learn to carry their part. And that is not selfishness. That is the greatest good I can do them, which is not to let them fall on the day I fall, because they will have learned, with me alive, to hold themselves up.

And a thing happened that Perpétua did not expect, and that was the beginning of the proof that the doctor and the Keeper were right: the siblings, set before the I need, before the I will not do it alone, before the necessity of carrying their part, began, with effort, to grumble, but to carry. The one who took the papers saw to the papers, badly at first, then better. The one who took the care went to do the turns, and discovered that she knew how, that she had always known, she had only never had to do it. The youngest, the spoiled one, put his hand to it for the first time in his life, and it was a shock to him, and he complained, but he did it. And Perpétua, seeing the siblings carry, slowly, their part, understood, with an astonishment that was almost joy, that they were capable, that they had always been, that they did not hold not because they did not know how, but because they had never had to know, because she had carried them, and that it was enough for her to set down for them to discover that they knew how to hold themselves up.

And there was, on that first night of weight set down, a detail that moved her and that she kept. The sister from the neighbouring village, the one who had taken the care of the uncle, telephoned her at the end of the day, not to give her back the weight, as she had always done, but for a thing she had never done: to ask her how her day, Perpétua's, had gone. And it was the first time in forty years that a sibling telephoned her to ask about her, and not to ask of her, and Perpétua, on the other end of the line, did not know how to answer, because she was not used to being asked, and was flustered, and said she was well, as she always said; but within, on hanging up, she understood a thing that shook her: that by setting the weight down, by saying I need, by ceasing to be only the one who bears, she had opened in the siblings a space to see her, to care for her, which while she had been only the column had never existed, because one does not ask a column how it is, and that perhaps, by setting the weight down, she was not only relieving herself, she was teaching the family to see her, at last, not as the beam that bears, but as the woman who also needs, sometimes, to be asked how her day was.

It was small, this first setting down, and it did not solve everything, and there were to be setbacks, and days on which the siblings tried again to give her back the weight, and days on which she, out of habit, almost took it up again; but the beginning was made, the column had set the first weight down, and the house, instead of falling, had done what the Keeper had said, had creaked, had taken fright, had reorganised itself, and the other timbers had begun, with effort, to bear their part. And Perpétua, at night, after setting the first weight down, felt a thing she had not felt for forty years: she felt her body a little lighter, not much, only one weight less, but enough to perceive, in the small relief, the size of the weight she carried, and to want, for the first time, to go on setting down, weight by weight, until she discovered, on the far side of them all, the woman left unlived beneath the column, and who began, in that first relief, to breathe.
