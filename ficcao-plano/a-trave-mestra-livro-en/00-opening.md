## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Perpétua — the lasting one — daughter of the House of the Mill, the cousin who was left with the mother-house, and the name was a promise, of the kind that are kept the wrong way round: the mother wanted her daughter to be lasting, to endure, to hold; and the daughter kept it in a way the mother did not foresee, holding the whole family on her back her whole life, as a main beam holds a roof, without anyone, in fifty years, asking the beam how it was.

The register says who was left with whose house, and there are names that appear on every paper of a family: on the death entries seeing to the burials, on the marriage entries making the feasts, on the birth entries running to the labours; and Perpétua was one of those names, she was on every paper of the House of the Mill, not because she was the eldest, which she was not, nor the heir, for the inheritance was shared, but because she was the one who held, the column, the beam; and when a family has a beam, the whole of it leans on her, and forgets, by dint of the house not falling, that the beam too is timber, and that timber, however strong, creaks, and cracks, and one day gives way, if no one relieves it of the weight.

This is the first book of the shelf of the Long Table, the shelf of those who ask what their place is among the others, and within it, it is the book of the one who carries the family. It tells the story of a woman who was, from the age of fourteen, the column of the House of the Mill: who pays for the repairs, who takes in everyone's crises, who keeps the old ones, who sustains the feasts, and on whom the family rests as the roof on the beam, without ever asking her how she is. It is not a book against caring for others, for caring is good; it is a book about the one who cares for everyone and for whom no one cares, about the weight that is not seen because it looks like strength, and about what happens to a beam when, after a life of holding the house, it finds that it must set the weight down, or crack.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Perpétua who holds everyone and whom no one holds, who is the column the whole house leans on and whom no one asks how she is, who confuses being needed with being loved and holding with existing, let her know, from the first line, what it took Perpétua a whole life not to know: that a beam too needs to rest; that to set down is not to let fall, it is to let the house learn to hold itself; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to bear one more weight, it is to set it down, and to discover, with astonishment, that the house does not fall, it creaks, it reorganises itself, and the others, all at once, grow up; and that on the far side of the weight is the question the beam never had time to ask, which is who am I when I am holding nothing.
