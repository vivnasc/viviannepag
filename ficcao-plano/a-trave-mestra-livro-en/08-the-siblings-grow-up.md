## 8. The siblings grow up

There happened, as Perpétua set the weights down one by one and did not take them up again, the most surprising thing of the book, the one the Keeper had promised her and she had not believed: the siblings grew up. After a lifetime of being light, they grew twenty years in a few months, because they were forced to, and no one grows while there is someone to carry him.

It was seen little by little, in small things. Belmiro, from the city, who had always been the one who complained of his little things and left the great ones to Perpétua, set to carry his part of the uncle's papers, of the accounts, of the decisions, began to change. He stopped complaining of the little things, because now he had great things to see to, and the great things, he discovered, gave him a seriousness, a dignity, a place in the family that the little things had never given him. Once, on the telephone, instead of complaining, he told Perpétua, with a pride she had never heard in him, that he had solved alone a hard matter of the uncle's papers, that he had gone to the offices, that he had seen to everything. Look at me, sister, me seeing to bureaucracy, who would have thought it. And the light, complaining man was becoming, by carrying, a more whole man, more grown, and Perpétua saw him grow, and it hurt her and filled her at the same time, because she perceived that she had kept him small for forty years by carrying him, and that it had been enough to set him down for him to grow.

And there was a Sunday on which Belmiro telephoned, and Perpétua, on hearing his voice, prepared herself, out of habit, for the request, for the this month was bad, save me. And no request came. There came another thing, which she had never heard from Belmiro in forty years. Oh sister, said Belmiro, I rang only to know how you are, in earnest, and to tell you a thing: I have been thinking, and I realised that you paid out, over the years, a great deal of my money, many times that I asked and you gave and never charged. And now I am better off, and I want to begin to give back to you, little by little, what you gave me. And I want to help you with the things of the house, which I never helped with. Do not tell me no, sister, for this time it is I who insist. And Perpétua, on the other end of the line, was left without speech, because it was the first time in life that Belmiro rang her to give and not to ask, to give back and not to take; and she understood, in that telephone call, that the brother had truly grown, that to set him down had not pushed him away, it had made of him a man capable of repaying, and that the reckoning of forty years, always in her favour, always in credit, was beginning, at last, to turn round, not because she charged it, but because the brother, grown, wished to pay it.

Felismina, who came to the House of the Mill to rest from her small problems, set to do the uncle's turns, to share the care, discovered in herself a capacity to care she had never known she had, because she had never had to use it. And by caring for uncle Aníbal, by watching him at night, by changing him, by feeding him from her hand, she gained a firmness, a competence; and gained too, she discovered, a closeness with the old man she had never had, because whoever cares stays near. And Perpétua perceived, seeing her sister, that by caring for everything alone, without knowing it, she had taken from the siblings not only the weight, but also the closeness, the love that is gained by caring; and that now Felismina, by caring, gained that love, and wept the uncle when he died, some months later, with a true grief, the grief of one who had cared for him, and not the distant grief of one who had only paid for another to care for him.

Uncle Aníbal died that winter, slowly, one night, with Felismina watching him, for it was her night. And the uncle's death showed, better than anything, what had changed. Because if the uncle had died a year before, he would have died with Perpétua alone watching him, and the siblings would have come to the burial from afar, in black, to give condolences as one gives them to a misfortune of others, and would have gone back to their light lives without the death touching them in earnest. But the uncle died now, after months cared for by all, and it was Felismina who closed his eyes, and it was Belmiro who saw to the papers of the death without Perpétua telling him how, and it was Tó who helped dress the dead man, a thing he had never done, and who came out of the room white as chalk but having done it. And at the burial, on the mountain, the three siblings wept the uncle with a true grief, the grief of those who had cared for him, who had known him sick, who had wiped his chin and heard the muddles of an old man; and Perpétua, on seeing them weep in earnest an old man who, a year before, was only a weight in her charge, understood that by sharing the weight she had shared the love too, because care and love go together, and that she, by carrying everything alone for forty years, without knowing it, had not kept only the weight for herself, she had kept for herself alone the love that is gained by caring, and deprived the siblings of it. Now no. Now the uncle had been everyone's, in life and in death, and the grief was shared, like the weight, and a shared grief is a family, and a grief of one alone is a column.

Perpétua saw it, one night, passing by chance the house where Felismina watched the uncle. She looked in through the half-open door, without being seen, and saw her sister seated at the side of the old man's bed, in the dead hours, giving him the broth from her hand, slowly, spoonful by spoonful, speaking to him low, wiping his chin, with a tenderness and a knack Perpétua had never imagined in her, the Felismina who came to the House of the Mill to flee her little problems, there, caring for an old man in the dead hours, with love. And Perpétua, at the door, without entering, without showing herself, let her sister care for the uncle alone, and went away with her heart full, because she saw, in that scene, the proof of everything: that Felismina had always had that capacity to care, that tenderness, that knack, and had never used them because she had never had need, because there was Perpétua to care for everything; and that it had been enough for Perpétua to set down for her sister to discover, in herself, a caregiver who had been there for ever, waiting to be needed. She did not enter to help. For the first time in her life, she saw a scene of care in the family and did not enter to do it herself; she left it to whoever was doing it, and went away, and it was that, the not entering, a victory greater than many entrances.

And Tó, the youngest, the spoiled one, the one who had never taken up any weight, was the one who grew the most, because he was the one who had been kept the smallest. Set, for the first time in his life, to carry a thing in earnest, to see to his own affairs alone, not to turn to Perpétua for everything, he passed through a time of distress, of complaint, of rage even, because it is hard to grow up at forty, it is hard to find oneself capable when one has lived a life being carried. But he passed through it. And on the far side of it appeared a man no one knew, not even himself, a man who held himself up, who had a place. And Perpétua, seeing the youngest brother become, at last, at forty, a man, understood the harm she had done him by spoiling him, by carrying him, by never letting him grow; and understood that the greatest good she had done him in life was not all the times she had got him out of trouble, it was that one, the single time she said no to him, get yourself out of it, I can no longer, and forced him, by force, to become a man.

And there was a moment that sealed this truth, and that moved Perpétua to tears. Tó, already carrying his part, already another, came one day to her at the House of the Mill, and sat in the kitchen, at the table of always, and said to her a thing she did not expect.

"Perpétua, I was angry with you. For making me see to my own things, for telling me no that night, I thought you had grown hard, that you no longer wanted me." He fingered the tablecloth, without looking at her. "And now I have understood a thing. All my life you helped me. And none of those helps did me as much good as this one, the one of making me hold myself up. I was forty years old and I was a boy, because you never let me be a man, from so much carrying me. And now I am. And I owe it to you. Not to the times you carried me. To the time you set me down." And he looked at her, at last. "Thank you, Perpétua. For letting me fall, which was the only way for me to learn to get up."

And Perpétua, on hearing this from the brother she had raised from the cradle, wept, because it was the confirmation of everything. The brother thanked her not for the weight she had carried, but for the weight she had set down. And she understood, for good, that to set down had not been to abandon them, it had been to love them better, it had been to give them what carrying them had never given them, which was themselves.

And there was a thing that happened among the siblings, and that Perpétua had not foreseen, and that was perhaps the greatest proof that setting down had been good. On beginning to carry, each his part, the siblings, who had always lived each in his own lightness, without crossing much, began to need one another. To speak to one another. To arrange who did what, who took the turn, who saw to the paper. And in that sharing of the weight they discovered among themselves a closeness they had never had, because the weight, they discovered, besides tiring, unites, and those who carry a thing together are bound in a way that those who live each light in his own never are. And Perpétua, seeing the siblings draw near one another by dint of carrying together what she had always carried alone, understood that, by being the single column, she had deprived the siblings not only of the weight, but also of the closeness among themselves, because a family in which one person carries everything is a family in which the others do not have to unite; and that by setting the weight down she had given them, without foreseeing it, the gift of becoming, at last, brothers and sister to one another, and not only the light children of a column-sister.

And the Sundays changed, which is where one sees what changes in a family. Before, on Sundays, the family came to the mother-house and Perpétua served, and the siblings sat down to be served, and she in the kitchen and they at the table. Now, on Sundays, they came and put their hands to it: Belmiro carved, Felismina brought a pot made at home, Tó laid the table, the sisters-in-law washed the dishes, and Perpétua, for the first time, could sit down a while, in the midst of them, without being the one to do everything. And the old aunt, the one who could not govern herself, ceased to be only Perpétua's weight and came to be visited turn by turn, taken walking turn by turn, cared for turn by turn, and the old woman, who lived forgotten in her room, began to have company, because an old one shared among many has more visits than an old one in the charge of one alone. And the House of the Mill, on Sundays, instead of the silence of a woman serving and a family being served, filled with the noise of a family working together, which is a different noise, more joyful, the noise of a house where the weight is everyone's and so crushes no one.

And the House of the Mill, after those months, was another. It was no longer a house of a single column, with a tired beam bearing everything and three timbers leaning and light; it was a house of many timbers, each bearing its part, and it was, for that reason, a stronger house, a house that no longer fell on the day Perpétua was missing, because it had learned, with her still alive, to share the weight. And Perpétua, seeing the house reorganised, the siblings grown, the weight shared, the uncle buried with the true grief of those who had cared for him, felt the enormous relief of one who has set down, after forty years, a weight that was killing her. And she felt, beneath the relief, the question that was missing, the greatest, the one the book had kept for the end: now that the house does not need me to hold itself up, now that I have set down the weight that gave me my place, my name, my identity, now that I am no longer the indispensable column, who am I? What do I do with the life left over to me, I who never had a life that was only mine? And it was that, the question of who Perpétua is without the weight, the last crossing of the book, and the hardest, because to set the weight down had been hard, but to discover herself, after a life of holding the others, was harder still, it was to find oneself before a stranger of fifty-two who had, at last, for the first time, to learn to live for herself.
