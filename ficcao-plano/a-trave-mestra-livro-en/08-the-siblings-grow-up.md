## 8. The siblings grow up

There happened, as Perpétua set the weights down one by one and did not take them up again, the most surprising thing of the book, the one the Keeper had promised her and she had not believed: the siblings grew up. All at once, after a lifetime of being light, they grew twenty years in a few months, because they were forced to, and no one grows while there is someone to carry him.

It was seen little by little. The brother from the city, who had always been the one who complained of his little things and left the great ones to Perpétua, set to carry his part of the old ones, the papers, the decisions, began to change: he stopped complaining of the little things, because now he had great things to see to, and the great things, he discovered, gave him a seriousness, a dignity, a place in the family that the little things had never given him; and the light, complaining man became, by carrying, a more whole man, more grown, and Perpétua saw him grow, and it hurt her and filled her at the same time, because she understood that she had kept him small for forty years by carrying him, and that it had been enough to set him down for him to grow.

The sister from the neighbouring village, who came to the House of the Mill to rest from her small problems, set to do the uncle's turns, to share the care, discovered in herself a capacity to care she had never known she had, because she had never had to use it; and by caring for the uncle, by carrying her part of the weight of the old ones, she gained a firmness, a competence, and gained too, she discovered, a closeness with the old ones she had never had, because whoever cares stays near, and Perpétua, by caring for everything alone, without knowing it, had taken from the siblings not only the weight, but also the closeness, the love that is gained by caring, and now the sister, by caring, gained that love, and Perpétua saw that she had deprived them, by carrying everything, not only of the weight, but also of the good that is in the weight, which is the love that is gained by carrying it.

And the youngest, the spoiled one, the one who had never taken up any weight, was the one who grew the most, because he was the one who had been kept the smallest. Set, for the first time in his life, to carry a thing in earnest, to see to his own affairs alone, not to turn to Perpétua for everything, he passed through a time of distress, of complaint, of rage even, because it is hard to grow up at forty, it is hard to find oneself capable when one has lived a life being carried; but he passed through it, and on the far side of it appeared a man no one knew, not even himself, a capable man, who held himself up, who had a place, and Perpétua, seeing the youngest brother become, at last, at forty, a man, understood the harm she had done him by spoiling him, by carrying him, by never letting him grow, and understood that the greatest good she had done him in life was not all the times she had got him out of trouble, it was that one, the single time she said no to him, get yourself out of it, I can no longer, and forced him, by force, to become a man.

Because this is what the column does not see while it carries, and that Perpétua only saw on setting down: that by carrying the others, with the best of intentions, with love, she keeps them small, dependent, incapable, and that the carrying, which looks like kindness, is also, without meaning to, a way of keeping them from growing, of having them bound to the column, of being the indispensable one; and that the greatest good a column can do for those it carries is not to carry them better, it is to set them down, to let them learn to hold themselves up, to give them the chance, hard but necessary, to discover that they are capable, that they always were, they had only never had to be. Perpétua, by setting the siblings down, made them the gift that forty years of carrying them had never made: she gave them themselves, gave them the capacity, the dignity, the grown life that she, by carrying them, had denied them without knowing.

And there was a moment that sealed this truth, and that moved Perpétua. The youngest brother, already carrying his part, came to her one day, and said a thing she did not expect: Perpétua, I was angry with you for forcing me to see to my own things, I thought you had grown hard, that you no longer wanted to help me. And now I have understood a thing. All my life you helped me, and none of those helps did me as much good as this one, the one of forcing me to hold myself up. I was forty and I was a boy, because you never let me be a man, from so much carrying me. And now I am. And I owe it to you, not to the times you carried me, but to the time you set me down. Thank you, Perpétua. And Perpétua, hearing this from the brother, wept, because it was the confirmation of everything, because the brother thanked her not for the weight she had carried, but for the weight she had set down, and she understood, for good, that to set down had not been to abandon them, it had been to love them better, it had been to give them what carrying them had never given them, which was themselves.

And there was a thing that happened among the siblings, and that Perpétua had not foreseen, and that was perhaps the greatest proof that setting down had been good. On beginning to carry, each his part, the siblings, who had always lived each in his own lightness, without crossing much, without sharing anything heavy, began to need one another, to speak to one another, to coordinate, to divide the uncle's turns, to arrange who did what, and in that sharing of the weight they discovered among themselves a closeness they had never had, because the weight, they discovered, besides tiring, unites, and those who carry a thing together are bound in a way that those who live each light in his own never are; and Perpétua, seeing the siblings draw near one another by dint of carrying together what she had always carried alone, understood that, by being the single column, she had taken from the siblings not only the weight and the closeness with the old ones, she had deprived them also of the closeness among themselves, because a family in which one person carries everything is a family in which the others do not have to unite, and that by setting the weight down she had given them, without foreseeing it, the gift of becoming, at last, brothers and sister to one another, and not only the light children of a column-sister.

And the House of the Mill, after those months, was another. It was no longer a house of a single column, with a tired beam bearing everything and three timbers leaning and light; it was a house of many timbers, each bearing its part, and it was, for that reason, a stronger house, a house that no longer fell on the day Perpétua failed, because it had learned, with her still alive, to share the weight. And Perpétua, seeing the house reorganised, the siblings grown, the weight shared, felt the enormous relief of one who has set down, after forty years, a weight that was killing her, and felt, beneath the relief, the question that was missing, the greatest, the one the book had kept for the end: now that the house does not need me to hold itself up, now that I have set down the weight that gave me my place, my name, my identity, now that I am no longer the indispensable column, who am I? What do I do with the life left over to me, I who never had a life that was only mine? And it was that, the question of who Perpétua is without the weight, the last crossing of the book, and the hardest, because to set the weight down had been hard, but to discover herself, after a lifetime of holding the others, was harder still, it was to find oneself before a stranger of fifty-two who had, at last, for the first time, to learn to live for herself.
