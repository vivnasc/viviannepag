## 11. The relapse

But one does not undo a column of forty years without setbacks, and Perpétua had hers, and it was a great one, and it came when she least expected it, in the way relapses always come, under cover of a good reason, dressed as love, so that they are almost unrecognisable as a setback and look rather like a duty.

The uncle fell ill, the same as before, and this time in earnest, and the house needed it, and needed it urgently, and Perpétua, at the first sign that the uncle was worsening, felt the old column wake within her with all its force, and before thinking, before asking herself anything, she was already taking everything up again, seeing to everything herself, doing turns, seeing to the papers, deciding, carrying, and the siblings, seeing Perpétua take up the weight, did what those grown used to a column always do the moment she offers, they leaned again, relieved, let Perpétua see to it, she has the knack, she knows, and in three days the house was back to what it was, a single column bearing everything and the others leaning, as though the months of learning had not existed.

And the terrible thing was that Perpétua, on relapsing, felt good, at first, and it was that which was most dangerous. She felt herself needed again, in her place again, knowing who she was again, and the emptiness of the months of learning to be a person, which had so anguished her, filled all at once with the weight, and Perpétua, instead of taking fright at the relapse, rested in it, because carrying was what she knew, and being a person cost her, and the weight, however much it cracked her, was her zone of comfort, was the known, was the lap of the old identity, and she, in the fright of the uncle's illness, ran to that lap, to the weight, as one runs to the known when one is afraid, even when the known does one harm.

It took her weeks to see that she had relapsed, because the relapse came dressed as duty, and who would reproach a woman for caring for her sick uncle, and she herself said but he is my uncle, he is ill, it is my duty, what was I to do, leave him; and behind the duty, which was true, hid the setback, which was that she had taken up everything alone again, had pushed the siblings away, had gone back to being the single column, and had done all that not only for the uncle, but for herself, out of not knowing how to be in the emptiness, out of the weight being more comfortable to her than the freedom. And it was the body, again, that warned her, because after weeks of carrying, the pains came back, the shortness of breath came back, the doubling over to lift the old man came back, and it was in that pain, in the exact repetition of the pain that had taken her to the doctor, that Perpétua woke, and understood, with a tightening, that she had relapsed, that she was again on the road to cracking, that the months of learning to set down had gone down the drain at the first emergency, and that the column, after all, did not undo itself like that, that it was within her, lying in wait, ready to wake at the first good reason.

And it was one of the siblings, the youngest, the one who had grown the most, who told her the truth, and it was a fine turn of the book, because it was the brother she had carried her whole life who now held her up. He came to her and said: Perpétua, you have relapsed. You took up everything again. I know it is the uncle, I know it is your duty, but look at yourself, you are cracking again, and we are leaning again, and this is not because of the uncle, this is because you do not know how not to carry, and we do not know how not to lean, and we have both gone back to what it was. And he said to her further, and it was what saved her: let me carry my part, Perpétua. You taught me. Do not take from me now what you taught me. When you take up everything, it is not only that you crack yourself; it is that you make me small again, you make me again the boy who does not carry. Set down, for I bear it. Trust me, for it was you who taught me to bear.

And there was, on the brother's lips, a phrase Perpétua did not expect and that disarmed her for good. He said to her: do you know what the difference is between now and before, Perpétua? Before, when there was a trouble, you took it up and we did not even notice it, the trouble passed over us without touching us, because you were the roof. And now the trouble wets all of us, and that frightens, but it makes us grow, because a person only grows in what wets her. You, by taking up everything, sheltered us from the rain, and we thanked you, but we stayed children, because a child is someone they have not yet let get rained on. Let us get our part of the rain, Perpétua. Not to punish you, not because we do not want to help you. Because it is the only way for us to grow, and because you, by getting all the rain alone, are catching cold until you die. And Perpétua heard that about the rain, and understood that the brother, whom she had carried her whole life, had learned, by carrying his part, a wisdom that she, so busy holding the roof, had never had time to learn.

And Perpétua, on hearing from the youngest brother, the one she had most spoiled, the one who had grown the most, that set down, for I bear it, wept, because it was the sign that the learning had not been in vain, that even with her relapse something had stayed, that the brother had truly grown and was now the one holding her up; and she understood, with humility, that the relapse was part of it, that no one sets down a weight of forty years without taking it up a few more times, that the learning to set down was not a straight line but a coming and going, a setting down and drawing back and setting down again, and that what mattered was not never to relapse, for that was impossible, it was to recognise the relapse when it came, and to set down again, and that to have at her side one who would tell her Perpétua, you have relapsed, and one who would tell her set down, for I bear it, was the difference between cracking alone and rising up accompanied. And she set the weight down again, this time with the brother's help, and it was less hard for her than the first time, because she already knew the way, and because she was no longer alone in doing it, and she understood that that was, after all, the strong house the Keeper had spoken of: not the one that has no falls, but the one that, when a timber gives way, has the others to bear it up, and to set it on its feet again.
