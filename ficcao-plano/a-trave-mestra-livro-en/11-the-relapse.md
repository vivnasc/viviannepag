## 11. The relapse

But one does not undo a column of forty years without setbacks, and Perpétua had hers, and it was a great one, and it came when she least expected it, in the way relapses always come: under cover of a good reason, dressed as love, so that it is almost unrecognisable as a setback and looks rather like a duty.

Felismina fell ill, suddenly, of a thing that laid her in bed for a while, and her house was left with no one to tend her, and her husband worked, and her children were small. And Perpétua, at Felismina's first telephone call, mother, I mean, sister, I am ill, I cannot move, felt the old column wake within her with all its force, and before thinking, before asking herself anything, she was already taking everything up again. She went to Felismina's house, and tended her sister, and tended the nephews, and tended her sister's house, and tended, along the way, the things her sister had in her charge at the House of the Mill, and in three days she was again carrying everything, doing turns, deciding, seeing to things, being the column, as though the months of learning had not existed.

And the siblings, on seeing Perpétua take up the weight, did what those grown used to a column always do the moment she offers: they leaned again, relieved. Belmiro, who had been seeing to his papers, left them to the sister, for she does it better. Tó, who had grown, drew back a little, went back to ringing Perpétua about things he already solved alone. And in a short time the house went back to what it was, a single column bearing everything and the others leaning, as though the months had taught nothing.

And the terrible thing was that Perpétua, on relapsing, felt good, at first, and it was that which was most dangerous. She felt herself needed again, in her place again, knowing who she was again. The emptiness of the months of learning to be a person, which had so anguished her, filled all at once with the weight; and Perpétua, instead of taking fright at the relapse, rested in it, because carrying was what she knew, and being a person cost her, and the weight, however much it cracked her, was her zone of comfort, was the known, was the lap of the old identity. In the fright of her sister's illness, she ran to that lap, to the weight, as one runs to the known when one is afraid, even when the known does one harm.

There was, in the relapse, a perverse sweetness, and it was that sweetness that held her. At Felismina's house, seeing to everything, Perpétua felt herself, again, in her place. The nephews ran to her, aunt Perpétua this, aunt Perpétua that. Felismina, from the bed, said it is a good thing you are here, sister, with you I am at ease. Felismina's husband thanked her on his knees. And Perpétua, in the midst of that, felt what she had not felt for months: she felt herself needed, important, indispensable, the saint, the one who bears. The emptiness of the Sundays with nothing to do filled all at once, and it was an enormous relief, because the emptiness hurt her and the weight, that, she knew how to carry. It is easier to suffer a known weight than an unknown emptiness, and Perpétua, set to choose between the tiredness of carrying and the anguish of not knowing who she was without carrying, chose, without thinking, the tiredness, because the tiredness had at least a form, a name, a place. And it was that the trap of the relapse: it was not Felismina's illness, it was Perpétua preferring the weight to the emptiness, running to the known lap of the column out of not yet knowing how to live in the new freedom.

It took her weeks to see that she had relapsed, because the relapse came dressed as duty. And who would reproach a woman for caring for her sick sister? She herself said but she is my sister, she is ill, it is my duty, what was I to do, leave her? And behind the duty, which was true, hid the setback: that she had taken up everything alone again, had pushed the siblings away, had gone back to being the single column, and had done all that not only for Felismina, but for herself, out of not knowing how to be in the emptiness, out of the weight being more comfortable to her than the freedom. And it was the body, again, that warned her, because after weeks of carrying, the pains came back, the shortness of breath came back, the doubling over came back; and it was in that pain, in the exact repetition of the pain that had taken her to the doctor, that Perpétua woke, and understood, with a tightening, that she had relapsed, that she was again on the road to cracking, that the months of learning to set down had gone down the drain at the first emergency, and that the column, after all, did not undo itself like that, that it was within her, lying in wait, ready to wake at the first good reason.

And it was Tó, the youngest, the one who had grown the most, who told her the truth, and it was a fine turn of the book, because it was the brother she had carried her whole life who now held her up. He came to her at Felismina's house, and found her exhausted, seeing to everything, with the face of one who had cracked again, and said to her, sitting down at her side:

"Perpétua, you have relapsed. You took up everything again. I know it is Felismina, I know she is your sister, but look at yourself, you are cracking again, and we are leaning again, and this is not because of Felismina, this is because you do not know how not to carry, and we do not know how not to lean, and we have both gone back to what it was." And he said to her, lower, the thing that saved her: "Let me carry my part, sister. You taught me. Do not take from me now what you taught me. When you take up everything, it is not only that you crack yourself; it is that you make me small again, you make me again the boy who does not carry. Felismina is our sister, she is everyone's. Go home and rest, for Felismina I will see to, and Belmiro will come to help, and we will share it. Set down, sister, for I bear it. Trust me, for it was you who taught me to bear."

And Perpétua, on hearing from the youngest brother, the one she had most spoiled, the one who had grown the most, that set down, sister, for I bear it, wept, because it was the sign that the learning had not been in vain, that even with her relapse something had stayed, that the brother had truly grown and was now the one holding her up. And she understood, with humility, that the relapse was part of it; that no one sets down a weight of forty years without taking it up a few more times; that the learning to set down was not a straight line, but a coming and going, a setting down and drawing back and setting down again; and that what mattered was not never to relapse, for that was impossible, it was to recognise the relapse when it came, dressed as duty, and to set down again. And that to have at her side one who would tell her sister, you have relapsed, and one who would tell her set down, for I bear it, was the difference between cracking alone and rising up accompanied.

And it cost her to forgive herself the relapse, more than it cost her to relapse. Because Perpétua, on perceiving that she had relapsed, was hard with herself, called herself foolish, weak, without sense, thought she had thrown away months of learning, that she was worth nothing, that she would never manage it. And it was Benvinda, the friend, who undid that hardness, at the table of a café, when Perpétua told her, ashamed, that she had relapsed. Oh Perpétua, said Benvinda, and you thought you were going to cease being the column from one day to the next, after forty years? That is not unlearned in a single season. You will relapse many times yet. What matters is not not to relapse, it is to notice that you have relapsed, and to turn back, as you have noticed now. Whoever never relapses is whoever never tried to change. Your relapse is not the proof that you failed; it is the proof that you are changing, because only one who has moved from the spot relapses. And Perpétua, on hearing that, forgave herself a little, and understood that to learn to set down was not a straight line made once, it was a road of zigzags, of advances and setbacks, and that to be indulgent with the setbacks was part of learning to set down, because a person who punishes herself at each relapse gives up, and a person who forgives herself the relapse goes on.

And the siblings, that time, did a thing they had never done: they cared for her by force. Tó and Belmiro came to Felismina's house, and instead of letting Perpétua see to everything, they sent her away. Go home, sister, said Tó, with a new firmness, of a man. Go home and rest, and do not come back here this week, for Felismina we will see to. And Perpétua wished to protest, wished to say but you do not know how, but the uncle, but this and that; and Belmiro cut her off, with affection but without giving in: we have learned, Perpétua. You taught us. Trust, for it is your turn to trust. And they took her coat, and put it on her, and led her to the door, and sent her home as one sends a stubborn child, with love; and Perpétua, on the way back, alone, with difficulty, with the instinct crying out to her go back, they do not know how, went forcing herself not to go back, to let herself be sent away, to let herself be cared for by the siblings she had raised. And it was strange to her, and it was hard, to be put out of the house where she had always been the indispensable one, but beneath the strangeness there was a new and sweet thing, which was the relief of being, for the first time, the cared-for and not the caregiver, the one sent home to rest instead of the one who sends the others to rest.

And she set the weight down again, this time with the help of Tó and Belmiro, who came, and shared Felismina between them, and Perpétua went home to rest, with difficulty, against the habit, but she went. And it was less hard for her than the first time, because she already knew the way, and because she was no longer alone in doing it. And she learned, from that relapse, to watch herself. She came to know the signs that she was going to relapse, which were always the same: she began to feel herself useless, began to invent reasons to be needed, began to say yes to everything, began to stop singing and reading and going to the café, signs that the column was waking and wishing to return to the weight. And when she noticed those signs, instead of giving in to them, she stopped, and asked herself: am I doing this because it is needed, or because I need to be needed? Am I helping, or am I relapsing? And many times, on asking herself the question, she perceived that she was on the way to a relapse dressed as duty, and checked herself in time, before taking up the whole weight. Not that she managed it always; but she managed it ever earlier, caught the relapse ever more at the beginning, and a relapse caught at the beginning is halfway to not being had. And Benvinda, the friend, helped her watch herself, asked her at the café oh Perpétua, this week you have the air of one who has gone back to carrying the world, is all well?, and Perpétua, on hearing the question from outside, noticed, and recovered herself. To have at one's side someone who knows our vice and asks us in time is the best defence against it.

And she understood that that was, after all, the strong house the Keeper had spoken of: not the one that has no falls, but the one that, when a timber gives way, has the others to bear it up, and to set it on its feet again. The column had given way, at the first emergency; and the house, instead of falling, held her up, which was the deepest proof that the house had, at last, learned to bear itself up, and even to bear up the old beam.
