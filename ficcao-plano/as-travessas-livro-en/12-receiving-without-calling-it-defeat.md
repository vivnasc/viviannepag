## 12. Receiving without calling it defeat

And the day came on which Socorro received without calling the receiving a defeat, and it was the climax of the book, without festivity, as those of Véspera are, a small gesture that changed everything within: she accepted a dish, ate it to the end, and returned it empty, without distress, without alarm, without feeling it as a fall, but as what it was, a trust, an embrace, a bond.

It was Lucinda, of course, who made sense that it should be, because it had been Lucinda who first broke through her wall with the bean soup on an afternoon of rain. Lucinda appeared, on a late afternoon of spring, with a dish, for no reason at all but the pleasure, and Socorro opened the door to her, and this time there was no stubbornness at all. It was not needed for Lucinda to come in by force, nor to sit without asking, nor to threaten not to leave until she saw the dish empty. Socorro opened the door, saw the dish, and said only, with the naturalness of one who says a simple thing:

"I'm glad you came. Come in, for we'll eat together."

And it was in that sentence, said without cost, without struggle, without that old voice screaming return it within her, that Lucinda understood, before Socorro herself, that the cure was made. They ate together, at the table, and talked of what one talks of, of the cabbages coming good that year, of Lucinda's son who was going to marry, of Aurélia's grandchild who had been born in good health; and Socorro ate the whole dish, with pleasure, without noticing she ate it, the way one eats at the table of one one loves, without noticing she was doing the hardest thing of her life, because it was no longer the hardest thing of her life, it was only dinner with Lucinda.

And in the middle of the dinner, Lucinda, who noticed everything, said a thing that settled the cure into words:

"Do you know what I noticed in you, Socorro? The first time you ate from my soup, that afternoon of rain, you ate weeping, with your face down, like one doing a forbidden thing. Remember? And I was left thinking, it costs her so, this woman, to eat a soup that hurts her so to eat. And look at you now. You're here eating my dish talking of cabbages, without noticing it, as though it were the most natural thing in the world. And it is. It's the most natural thing in the world, Socorro. To eat with those who love us. It took you fifty-five years to find natural a thing that children know from the moment they're born, which is to let them set food in front of them. But you got there. And it's worth more to have got there late than not to have got there, for many people die without getting there, eating their dry bread on their feet, alone, thinking it's strength."

And Socorro laughed, and told Lucinda that she talked too much, like Idalina, and Lucinda laughed too, and said well I do, but I talk well, which is different; and the two laughed, at the table, and Socorro understood, in the middle of the laughter, that the laughter at the table with another person was also a dish, and that she was eating it, and that she did not return it, and that it was good.

Because that, after all, was the whole crossing, and it was now fulfilled: it was not only to learn to receive, for that she had been doing for months; it was to learn to receive without feeling defeated by receiving, to accept a dish without each dish costing her her pride, to trust without the enormous effort of one who trusts against the wound and has to bear it spoonful by spoonful. Socorro had got there. She had got to the point of receiving with naturalness, with pleasure even, of accepting a lift, a help, a dish, the way one accepts a caress on the face, without making of it a battle within, without feeling it as a defeat of her strength. And it was in that light receiving, without defeat, that Socorro knew the wall had fallen for good; because the wall had never been the not-receiving, the wall had always been the feeling of the receiving as a fall, and when the receiving ceased to be a fall and became, simply, dinner with Lucinda, the wall no longer had anywhere to rise, it lacked the ground.

And she understood, then, the deepest thing of the book, the one that answered the name they had set on her at the font. Her name was Socorro, and she had fulfilled it her whole life giving help to others and never receiving it, and so the name had been a wound, that of being everyone's help and no one's being hers; she was called the one who comes to the rescue and no one came to the rescue of the one who comes, because she did not let them. And now, in learning to receive, in letting others be her help, Socorro fulfilled the whole name, on both sides. She gave help and received help. And the name, which had been a wound, became whole, because help in earnest, Socorro understood at the table with Lucinda, is not only what is given, it is what circulates, what goes and comes, what is given in one direction and received in the other, like the dishes that went full and came back empty along the paths of Véspera; and she, who had known only half the name, that of giving, had learned at last the other half, that of receiving, and was now, for the first time in fifty-five years, the complete help, the one who shelters and lets herself be sheltered.

And she understood the last truth, the one the Keeper had seeded in her at the bedside: that to receive is a trust, and not a defeat; that to return the dish full is to say I do not trust you, and to return it empty is to say I trust; that she had spent twenty years saying to the whole village, with full dishes, I do not trust you, because of one person she had trusted once and who had left her in the cold; and that in beginning to return the dishes empty she was saying, at last, to the village, I trust. And that this trust, rebuilt dish by dish after an empty door, was the bravest thing she had done in her life, braver than all the help she had given, because to give help had never exposed her, had always kept her on the upper side, the safe one; and to trust anew, after having been left, exposed her entire, put her again on the stone bench waiting for a dish that might not come back; and she had done it all the same, had chosen the risk of trust over the safety of solitude, and it was that choice, and none of the helps she had given, that was the greatest victory of her life.

And it was the Keeper, one day, who gave her the news that the entry was made. She passed by Socorro's house, in the afternoon, and found her on the stone bench with Lucinda and Aurélia, doing lacework, sitting, at home, in the midst of her own; and she stayed a moment at the head of the path looking at the scene, and then drew near, and said only:

"Socorro, I came only to tell you one thing. This morning I opened my book and entered a new entry, with your name. And do you know on which side? The other. That of the one who is helped. I wrote: in this year, Socorro, who was everyone's help, learned to let herself be the help of someone. It is your first entry, in fifty years, on that side. And it is the loveliest I've written in a long time, because it is the one of whoever learned to receive, which is the rarest of all." And she smiled, with that deep smile. "You can enter it yourself now, within you, for I've already entered it in the book. It is set down. You are, at last, on both sides."

And Socorro thanked her, and this time did not say it wasn't needed, she said thank you, and the Keeper went off down the path, slowly, with her shawl trembling in the wind, content as one who has closed an entry that had been open for fifty years.

And she looked at the life that remained to her, the open house, the daughter near in truth, the stone bench with people sitting on it, the village circulating around her with dishes that went and came; and she looked at it without the wall and without the wound governing. And she felt, for the first time in twenty years, the thing the one who only gave had lacked: she felt herself a part. Not the upper part, that which shelters and stays alone; the part within, that which gives and receives, that which is bound below, in the intimacy, to the people who love her. And she understood that the whole life of giving and never receiving had made her strong and alone, a creditor full of credits and empty of bonds; and that it had been enough to learn to receive, to accept a dish and return it empty, to make her, at last, not weaker, as she had feared her whole life, but more bound, more accompanied, more human; and that the greatest help of her life had not been any of those she had given, to the whole village, in fifty years of births and deaths and wakes; it had been the one she had learned, at the end, ill, at fifty-five, to receive, because it was that, the help received, that saved her from the solitude to which her giving, without the receiving, had condemned her.

And there was a day, that spring, that joined the two halves of her name in a single afternoon, and it was the best proof that the cure was made. In the morning, Socorro went to come to the rescue, as always: Aurélia's grandchild, the newborn, had colic and would not sleep, and Aurélia, worn out, sent for her, and Socorro went, and took the child, and did what she knew how to do better than anyone in Véspera, gave calm where there was trouble. And in the afternoon, coming back home tired, she found on the stone bench a dish, Lucinda's, with the spoon inside, the spoon she had kept in the drawer on the day of the bean soup and never returned; and Lucinda, in a note, said only, give me back the spoon one day, woman, for I know you've kept it, and fill it first with what's here, which I made on purpose for you. And Socorro laughed alone, at the door, with the spoon recognised, and carried the dish inside, and ate, and went to the drawer to fetch Lucinda's spoon, and washed the two things, the dish and the spoon, and returned them the next day, the dish empty and the spoon at last given back, with a note, I return you the spoon, Lucinda, for I no longer need to keep it to remember that afternoon, because now I don't forget. And that afternoon Socorro was, in the same day's journey, the one who comes to the rescue and the one who is rescued, she gave help to Aurélia's grandchild and received the help of Lucinda's soup, and closed in herself the whole name, the two halves turning on the same wheel, the giving and the receiving circulating through her as the dishes circulated through the village.

And she thought, in those days, of the blue clay dish she had left on her sister's coffin, the grandmother's, the one of the only time she had asked; and she understood that, in leaving it there, in the big town, on top of the wood, she had freed herself not only of Doroteia but also of the dish itself, which for twenty years had been, at the bottom of the chest, the relic of her wound, the thing she kept the way one keeps a scar so as not to let it close. She no longer had it, and did not miss it, because the wound it stood for was now settled; and in its place, on top of the dresser, in plain sight, beside those in use, there was now the dish of white crockery with the blue line, Aurélia's, the first she had received and returned full and the first she had, at last, returned empty, and that Aurélia had given her for good, keep it, woman, for between us that dish is worth more full of story than full of broth. And when Glória noticed it and asked why her mother kept in plain sight a cracked, common dish, instead of the good one, Socorro said only, it's the most valuable dish I have, daughter, one day I'll tell you why; and she did not tell yet, but kept it in plain sight, in the light, in use, because a dish that comes back from the stone bench to the dresser, and stays, is a wound that comes back from the depths to the light and stops hurting from being, at last, on show and in service, like everything that heals.

And on one of the dishes she returned empty, in those days of spring, Socorro put a note, and the note did not say, as those of before, thank you, it wasn't needed. It said another thing, it said the thing it had taken her fifty-five years to learn to say, and she wrote it with a firm hand, without trembling, without shame: thank you, it was good, and it did me good not to be alone. And in writing it did me good not to be alone, Socorro knew, for good, that she was cured, because she had just admitted, in writing, to the village, on a little note pinned with a safety pin, the thing the wall had forbidden her her whole life: that she needed, that she received, that she was not alone, and that this, far from being the defeat she had feared in a winter of broken roof, was the warmest and truest thing she had written on a note in fifty-five years of writing them all to refuse.
