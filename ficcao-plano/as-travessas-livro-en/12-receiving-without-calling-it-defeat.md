## 12. Receiving without calling it defeat

And the day came on which Socorro received without calling the receiving a defeat, and it was the climax of the book, without a feast, as those of Véspera are, a small gesture that changed everything within: she accepted a dish, ate it, and returned it empty, without distress, without alarm, without feeling it as a fall, but as what it was, a trust, an embrace, a bond.

Because that was the whole crossing, and it was now fulfilled: it was not only to learn to receive, for that she already did; it was to learn to receive without feeling defeated by receiving, to accept a dish without each dish costing her the pride, to trust without the enormous effort of one who trusts against the wound. And Socorro reached there. She reached the point of receiving with naturalness, with pleasure even, of accepting a lift, a help, a dish, like one who accepts a tenderness, without making of it an inner struggle, without feeling it as a defeat of her strength; and it was in that light receiving, without defeat, that Socorro knew the wall had fallen for good, because the wall was not the not receiving, the wall was the feeling of the receiving as a fall, and when the receiving ceased to be a fall and became an embrace, the wall no longer had anywhere to rise.

And she understood, at that point, the deepest thing of the book, the one that answered the name they had given her. Her name was Socorro, help, and she had fulfilled it her whole life by giving help to others and never receiving it, and so the name had been a wound, that of being everyone's help and of no one being hers; and now, on learning to receive, on letting others be her help, Socorro fulfilled the name whole, on both sides, she gave help and received help, and the name, which had been a wound, became whole, because true help, she understood, is not only what is given, it is what circulates, what goes and comes, what is given and received, and she, who had known only half the name, that of giving, had learned at last the other half, that of receiving, and was now, for the first time, the complete help, the one that shelters and lets herself be sheltered.

And she understood the last truth, the one the Keeper had sown in her: that to receive is a trust, and not a defeat; that to return a dish full is to say I do not trust you, and to return it empty is to say I trust; that she had spent twenty years telling the whole village, with the full dishes, I do not trust you, because of one person she had trusted and who had left her; and that on beginning to return the dishes empty she was telling, at last, the village, I trust, and that that trust, rebuilt dish by dish after an empty door, was the most courageous thing she had done in her life, more than all the help she had given, because to give help did not expose her, and to trust anew, after having been left, exposed her whole, and she did it, she chose the risk of trust against the safety of solitude, and it was that choice her greatest victory.

And she looked at the life left to her, the open house, the daughter near in truth, the neighbours, the village circulating around her with dishes that went and came, and she looked at it without the wall and without the wound governing it; and she felt, for the first time in twenty years, the thing the one who only gave lacked: she felt herself part. Not the upper part, that which shelters and is alone; the inner part, that which gives and receives, that which is connected below, in intimacy, to the people who love her; and she understood that the whole life giving and never receiving had made her strong and alone, a creditor full of credits and empty of bonds, and that it had been enough to learn to receive, to accept a dish and return it empty, to make her, at last, not weaker, as she had feared, but more connected, more accompanied, more human; and that the greatest help of her life had been none of those she had given, it had been what she had learned, in the end, to receive, because it was that, the received help, that saved her from the solitude to which her giving, without the receiving, had condemned her.

And in one of the dishes she returned empty, in those days, Socorro put a note, and the note did not say, like those of before, thank you, it was not needed; it said another thing, it said the thing she had taken her whole life to learn to say: thank you, it was good, and it did me good not to be alone. And on writing it did me good not to be alone, Socorro knew she was healed, because she had just admitted, in writing, to the village, the thing the wall had forbidden her her whole life, that she needed, that she received, that she was not alone, and that this, far from being the defeat she had feared, was the warmest and truest thing she had written in a note in fifty-five years of writing them to refuse.
