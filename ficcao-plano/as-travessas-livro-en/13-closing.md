## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Socorro, daughter of the village, who was everyone's help and whom no one helped, who returned full the dishes the village put at her door because once, in the year she most needed, she had counted on someone and been left looking at the empty door, learned, at fifty-five, the hardest thing for one who only knows how to give: to accept a dish, and to eat it to the end, and to return it empty, which in Véspera is the greatest declaration of trust there is. Be it set down that she lowered the wall, and let in the daughter, and the neighbours, and the village, and that on receiving she bound herself to people below, in the intimacy, where the giving had never bound her.

Be it set down that the stubbornness was broken through by the neighbours who did not give up, Aurélia of the broth, Benilde of the marmalade, and above all Lucinda, who sat in the kitchen on an afternoon of rain and did not leave until she saw the dish empty; that she was opened by the daughter, Glória, who had struck against the wall for forty years and who was the first to whom Socorro returned an empty dish; and that she was cured at the root by Ermelinda, the widow in whom Socorro recognised herself of before, and for whom she did what had been done for her. Be it set down that the empty door had a name, and the name was Doroteia, the sister, the one who said I'm coming and did not come, the one who left her in the cold of a winter of broken roof and made her shut all the doors because of one; and that Socorro, in letting go of that door on the sister's coffin, with the blue clay dish of the grandmother, ceased to live waiting on the sill and went back to the village where there were people who arrived.

The register keeps who gives and who receives, and there are names that always appear on the side of those who come to the rescue and never on that of those who are rescued. This Keeper saw, in the book, the name of Socorro in hundreds of entries, always giving, at the births, at the deaths, in the illnesses, and never, in fifty years, receiving; and saw, at last, her entry appear, for the first time, on the other side, that of the one who is rescued, that of one who accepted a dish and returned it empty. And the Keeper understood that Socorro had fulfilled at last the whole name: she was the help of others, and learned to let others be her help, and made of help not a thing she only gave, but a thing that circulates, that is given and received.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Socorro who is everyone's help and lets no one come to her rescue, who returns the dishes full, who says it is not needed, I manage on my own, who refuses support by method because once she counted on someone and was left, let her know what she learned and what this house records: that to receive is not a defeat, it is a trust; that to return a dish full is to say I do not trust, and to return it empty is to say I trust; that only to give and never to receive is not generosity, it is a way of protecting oneself, that leaves us strong and alone; and that the greater courage, for one who was left wanting, is not to go on giving without ever receiving, it is to accept the first dish, and to eat it, and to return it empty, and to risk trusting again, after the empty door, because most dishes, unlike that one, stay.

Socorro traded the smooth wall for the open door, the returning full for the returning empty, the solitude of one who only gives for the connection of one who gives and receives. And in the houses of Véspera, since the one in which she accepted the first dish from the daughter and ate it and returned it empty, there is one more woman who no longer confuses receiving with losing, because she learned that the empty dish returned is an accepted embrace, and that to let in those who love us is the greatest courage of one who was once left, and the only one that heals the empty door.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
