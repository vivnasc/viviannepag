## 7. Receiving is not a debt

For Socorro to be able to receive, there still remained to dismantle an idea that held her as much as the fear, and that was harder to see because it had the face of virtue: the idea that to receive is to be left owing.

Socorro kept an account without noticing she kept it. She saw giving and receiving like the debt ledger of the shop, in which whoever gives becomes the creditor and whoever receives becomes the debtor; and she had wanted, her whole life, to be on the creditor's side, the side of one who owes nothing to anyone, the clean side of the ledger. That was why she refused the dishes: because every dish accepted was, in her account, an entry to her debit, a line left uncrossed, a debt she would later have to pay. And as long as she thought that way, every dish weighed on her, and she went on returning them, to keep her ledger with the village always in her favour, always a creditor, never in debt.

It was Benilde, the youngest of the stubborn ones, who laid the account bare for her, without knowing it, in a small gesture. Benilde had brought, one day, not a dish of food, but a jar of quince marmalade, of hers, of the kind she made in autumn and was proud of; and Socorro, who already ate from the soups but still got flustered with everything else, thanked her, and right after, in a reflex, went to the cupboard and took out a pot of her own honey to give Benilde in exchange. And Benilde, who was young but not foolish, was left with the pot of honey in her hand looking at it, and then set it down slowly on the table, and said a thing that stayed with Socorro:

"Socorro, I didn't bring you the marmalade to carry off honey. If I wanted honey, I'd buy it. I brought you the marmalade because I felt like thinking of you eating it at breakfast and finding it nice. Now you give me the honey, and we're even, and it's all settled, and we owe each other nothing, isn't that so? But then we gave each other nothing. We traded. I wanted to give to you, Socorro. Giving isn't trading. Keep your honey, and eat the marmalade, and give me nothing for it, for that's how you give a thing: for free. As you gave your whole life, for free, without ever wanting change. Why is it that when it's you who gives it's for free, and when it's you who receives you've got to pay me at once with honey?"

Socorro was left with the pot of honey in her hand, and Benilde's question went into her deep, because it was exact. Why was it that when it was she who gave, she gave for free, without ever thinking they were left owing her, gave freely, out of love, to the whole village, and never, in fifty-five years, opened a debt ledger to anyone; and when it was she who received, she could not receive without opening the ledger at once, without making up the account, without having to pay back on the spot, with honey, with a cold rice inside Aurélia's dish, with anything, whatever it might be, so as not to be left owing? And she understood, with Benilde there in front of her and the pot of honey weighing in her hand, a deep and ugly thing about herself: that this difference, giving without an account and receiving with an account, could only mean one thing. That she did not believe the others could love her for free as she loved them. That she thought, at bottom, that the love of others came with a bill. That if she accepted Benilde's marmalade without paying, she was left owing, and that one day Benilde, or another, would charge her for it.

And beneath that was, again, Doroteia. There was the year she had given her sister everything, the inheritance of the land, the three weeks in January, and in which, the one time she put the dish at her sister's door asking it back, the dish was charged to her with a you always manage. Doroteia had taught her, in that winter, the thing that afterward poisoned all the love that came to her: that the love of others is not free, it comes with conditions, and one day it is charged or withdrawn precisely at the hour one most needs it. And it was that distrust, and not a lack of generosity, that made Socorro incapable of receiving, because whoever was once left distrusts all the love she receives, and prefers to refuse it beforehand, to return it full, rather than risk that it too, one day, be charged to her or withdrawn in the cold.

"You're right, Benilde," said Socorro, slowly, and it was the first time she granted anyone she was right in that matter. "I don't know how to receive for free. I, who give for free, don't believe they give to me for free." And she set the pot of honey down beside the marmalade, and did not give it, and it cost her not to give it, it cost her to leave a thing untraded on the table, but she left it.

And she remembered, with Benilde's marmalade in her hand, a man of the village, Telmo, who years before had tried to settle with her an account she had never opened to him. Socorro had watched over his mother the last months, and when the mother died Telmo, who was a man of few words and much shame, appeared at her door with a live lamb on a rope, the best of his flock, and said only, without looking at her, this is for you, Dona Socorro, for I don't know how to say things but I know what I owe you. And Socorro refused the lamb. She refused it with manners, with firmness, take that home, Telmo, I did nothing to be paid for, I did what one does; and the man went off with the lamb on the rope, head down, and never spoke to her properly again, because a man who is not let pay a debt of honour is left with the debt rotting inside him, and the shame turns into distance, and Telmo, who had been grateful to her, was left, without her meaning it, resentful of her. And Socorro, years later, with the marmalade in her hand, understood at last what she had done to Telmo, and to Rosalina, and to half the village: by refusing to be paid, she had left everyone in her debt forever, and a debt that cannot be paid does not bind, it pushes away, because no one wants to be eternally in debt to a saint who does not receive. She had spent her whole life thinking she relieved people of owing her, when, on the contrary, she had condemned them to owe her with no way out, and to flee her for it.

And the account came apart, slowly, scene by scene, more than word by word. Socorro began to notice the neighbours giving to her without charging, Lucinda who left her the spoon and did not come to fetch it, Aurélia who brought the dish and stayed talking of the weather and went off with nothing, and she understood, on seeing them, that they gave as she gave, for free, out of love, without a ledger; and that if she herself could give for free her whole life, then others could too, and their love was as free as hers. And she understood that by refusing to receive out of fear of the bill she was, without meaning it, insulting the love without a bill of those who wished her well, treating Benilde as a debt collector when Benilde only wanted to think of her eating marmalade, distrusting the true love of the village because of the false love of a sister who had once left her.

And the way of looking changed. Each dish accepted ceased, slowly, to be a debt and became a tenderness. Each care received ceased to be a defeat and became a bond. And Socorro went on discovering that the ledger she had kept with the village, always a creditor, never a debtor, had not made her rich, it had made her alone, because a creditor who never receives anything back is left alone with her credits, sitting atop a heap of favours no one can repay, and so shut off that no one any longer dares draw near her. And that the true wealth was not being always owed to; it was the exchange, the giving and the receiving circulating between the houses as the dishes circulate, full in one direction and empty in the other, the love going and coming with no one stopping to count it. And that she, in beginning to receive, was not contracting debts; she was entering, at last, after a lifetime at the door, into the circulation of love from which she had excluded herself, out of fear of a bill that most love, unlike Doroteia, never presents.

And there was a conversation with Glória that finished off dismantling the account. The daughter brought her, one day, not food, but a medicine from the pharmacy in the big town, which the mother needed and had no way to go and fetch; and Socorro, in an old reflex, went at once to the drawer to take out the money to pay the daughter for the medicine, to the penny. And Glória was left looking at her with the money in her mother's hand held out to her, and instead of taking it, sat down, and said, slowly, with a sadness deeper than anger:

"Mother, you want to pay me for the medicine? Me, your daughter?" And Socorro, with the note held out, did not at once see what was wrong, because to her paying was just, it was not owing. "Mother, when I was small and you bought me my medicines, did I pay you for them? Well then. It's the same thing in reverse, only now it's I who buy and you who need. Don't pay me, Mother. Let me buy you a medicine without your paying me for it, which is a thing a daughter has the right to do for her mother, and that you've been taking from me for a lifetime. You paid for everything for me, when I was small, and never let me pay for anything for you, when I was grown. And do you know what that makes me feel? It makes me feel that I still owe you everything and that I'll never be able to pay you anything. Keep the note, Mother. Let me be left owing you a little less, for once."

And Socorro kept the note, slowly, and it cost her, and she understood, with the daughter before her, the last turn of her twisted account: that by wanting to pay for everything, by not wanting to owe anything, she left everyone, even her own daughter, owing her everything forever, and that this debt she did not let be settled was, itself, a wall, because no one truly draws near to one who never lets us give back.

And she remembered, in dismantling the account, a thing her mother had told her when she was small and that she had kept badly, in that kitchen where the laws of the houses are learned. The mother, who had also been a woman of giving and not receiving, used to tell her, in sending her to carry dishes to houses in trouble, take it, child, and expect nothing back, for whoever gives expecting does not give, she lends. And Socorro had grown up with that law, take it and expect nothing, and had made it her own, and it was a good law on one side, that of giving; but she understood now that the mother had taught her only half the law, the easier half, and that the other was missing, the hard one, the one the mother had never told her because the mother had never learned it either: receive, child, and don't be left owing, for whoever receives making up the account does not receive, she contracts. And the two halves together were what made the whole law, giving without expecting back and receiving without being left owing; and Socorro, who had one half so deep it seemed to her the world, had gone her whole life lame of the other, and limping she had called it walking straight.

And she ate Benilde's marmalade at breakfast, alone, at the kitchen table, and found it nice, as Benilde had wished; and it was the first thing she ate, in her life, without having paid for it, and without having been left owing, and without having made up the account. Only an autumn marmalade on a slice of bread, for free, out of love, for nothing. And it tasted better to her than everything she had bought with her honey in fifty-five years.
