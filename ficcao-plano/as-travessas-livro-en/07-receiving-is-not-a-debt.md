## 7. Receiving is not a debt

What Socorro still lacked, to be able to receive, was to dismantle an idea that bound her as much as the fear: that to receive is to be left owing, it is a debt, it is a defeat, and that while she thought so each accepted dish would be a weight and not an embrace, and she would go on returning them so as not to be in want.

Because Socorro, deep down, did a sum, without realising she did it: she saw giving and receiving as a ledger of debts, in which one who gives becomes a creditor and one who receives becomes a debtor; and she wanted always to be on the side of those who give, on the creditor side, on the side of those who owe nothing to anyone, and so she refused to receive, because to receive was to be left owing, to pass to the debtor side, to contract a debt that she would then have to pay; and that sum, that of giving as credit and receiving as debt, was what made her return the dishes, so as not to be left owing, to keep her ledger with the village always in her favour, always creditor, never debtor.

And the Keeper, and the stubborn neighbour, and the daughter, went on showing her, without saying it in words, that that sum was wrong, that giving and receiving among those who love one another is not a ledger of debts, it is an exchange of love, in which no one is left owing anyone, because the love that is given and that which is received are not counted, do not generate debt, they circulate; and that when the neighbour put a dish at her door she was not making her a loan that Socorro would have to repay, she was giving her an embrace, and an embrace is not paid, it is received; and that Socorro, by treating the dishes as debts to avoid, was turning embraces into loans, love into accounting, and refusing the love so as not to be left owing, when love, received, leaves no one owing, it leaves everyone richer.

And she understood, dismantling the sum, a deep thing about herself. She understood that she could give without doing the sum, she gave to the whole village without ever thinking they were left owing her, she gave freely, out of love; but that she could not receive without doing the sum, that on receiving she thought at once of the debt, of having to repay, of being in want; and that this difference, giving without a sum and receiving with a sum, showed that she did not believe the others could love her freely as she loved them, that she thought the others' love came with an invoice, that if she accepted she would be left owing; and beneath that was, again, the empty door, the old distrust that the others' love is not free, that it comes with conditions, that one day it is charged or withdrawn, as the help had been withdrawn in the year she had needed; and that it was that distrust, and not the lack of generosity, that kept her from receiving, because one who was left once distrusts all the love she receives, and prefers not to receive it than to risk that it too will be, one day, charged or withdrawn.

And it was hard for her, but Socorro began to dismantle the distrust, to understand that most of the people who put dishes at her door did it freely, out of love, without an invoice, as she herself did to others; that if she could give freely, then the others could too, and their love was as free as hers; and that by refusing to receive out of fear of the invoice she was insulting, without meaning to, the free love of those who wished her well, treating them as debt collectors when they were only people loving her, distrusting true love because of a false love that had once left her. And she decided to begin to trust, to believe that the love she received was free, to accept the dishes not as debts but as embraces, and to understand that to receive an embrace does not leave us owing, it leaves us closer, and that the only repayment an embrace asks is to let it in, and, one day, to return it, not out of debt, but out of love.

And on beginning to see it so, receiving ceased to weigh on her. Each accepted dish ceased to be a debt and became a tenderness; each received care ceased to be a defeat and became a bond; and Socorro went on discovering that the ledger she had kept with the village, always creditor, never debtor, had not made her rich, it had made her alone, because a creditor who never receives anything is alone with her credits, and that the true wealth was not always being owed to, it was the exchange, the giving and receiving circulating, the love going and coming without a sum, and that she, by beginning to receive, was not contracting debts, she was entering, at last, the circulation of love from which she had excluded herself her whole life out of fear of an invoice that most love never issues.
