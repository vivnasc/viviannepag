## 4. The daughter against the smooth wall

There was a daughter, who lived nearby, and who suffered most from the mother who did not receive, because she hit against her as against a smooth wall, with not a crack to enter by, and the daughter, over the years, had gone on wounding herself against that wall, and the mother's illness brought the wound to the surface.

The daughter loved the mother, and wanted to care for her, and it was natural, and it was what a daughter wants, to be near the ill mother, to shelter her, to repay the care the mother had given her as a child; but the mother did not let her. The daughter arrived, loving, came to care, brought things, offered to stay, to see to everything; and the mother refused, with that courteous and impenetrable firmness, it is not needed, daughter, go to your own life, I manage, do not trouble yourself; and the daughter hit against the it is not needed as against a wall, and went home with empty hands of the care she had come to give and that the mother had not let her, and was hurt, because a daughter who is not let care for the mother feels rejected, feels that the mother does not want her, does not trust her, does not need her, and Socorro's daughter had grown up and grown old feeling so, on the outside, against the smooth wall of a mother who gave to the whole village and to her, the daughter, did not let her give anything.

And there was, in this relationship, a particular sadness, that of an impossible closeness. Because the daughter lived nearby, could see the mother every day, and yet was far from her, further than if she lived in another land, because the distance was not of kilometres, it was of the wall, of the mother who did not let herself be cared for, who did not open herself, who did not receive; and the daughter, who had the mother right there beside her, had no access to her, could not shelter her, could not know her in the intimacy of vulnerability, because the mother never showed herself vulnerable, never needed, never received, and a mother who never needs the daughter is a mother who does not let the daughter in, who keeps her at the door her whole life, with the care in her hand and the door shut.

And there was, in the illness, a confrontation, that hurt them both and that opened a chink. The daughter, tired of being refused, tired of hitting against the wall, said to the mother, with a grief of many years: mother, why do you not let me? I am your daughter. You cared for me my whole life, and now that I want to care for you, you do not let me. You return my dishes as you return the neighbours'. To me, mother. To your daughter. You give to everyone and to me you do not let me give anything. Do you know how I feel? I feel that you do not need me, that I am of no use to you at all, that you would rather die alone than let me help you. What did I do to you, mother? Why do you shut the door on me?

And Socorro, on hearing the daughter, on seeing the grief of years the wall had made her, was shaken, because she understood, for the first time, the damage her refusal did, not only to her, but to the daughter; she understood that the daughter did not feel spared by the mother who did not trouble her, she felt rejected by the mother who did not let her in; that the smooth wall Socorro had raised to protect herself from the empty door had left the daughter on the outside her whole life, hitting against it, wounding herself, feeling dispensable; and that she, by not receiving from the daughter, was not sparing her, she was depriving her, depriving her of the care the daughter wanted to give, of the intimacy the daughter wanted to have, of the mother the daughter wanted to know and shelter, and that the daughter, on the other side of the wall, suffered from a mother who seemed strong and was only shut.

And she understood, with the daughter, a thing that began to open the wall for her: that to receive was not only a thing she refused to do to protect herself; to receive was also a gift one gives to those who love us, because those who love us want to care for us, want to be needed, want to enter, and the mother who never receives takes from the daughter the joy of caring for her, and shuts the door on her as it had been shut on her in that year; and that she, by returning the dishes to the daughter, by not letting her shelter her, was doing to the daughter what the empty door had done to her, leaving her in want, waiting, on the outside; and that perhaps the cure of her old wound, that of the empty door, was not to go on shutting herself, it was, on the contrary, to open herself to this daughter who knocked at the door, who wanted to enter, who offered a dish full of love, and who deserved, more than anyone, that the mother, at last, accept her, and eat her dish, and return it empty, which was to tell her I trust you, daughter, I receive you, I let you in.
