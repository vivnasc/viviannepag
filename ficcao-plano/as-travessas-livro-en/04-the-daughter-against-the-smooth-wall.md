## 4. The daughter against the smooth wall

Glória lived three houses from her mother, and was, in all the village, the one who suffered most from Socorro's wall, because it was on her that she ran up against it every day, and a wall one runs up against every day ends by wearing down the one who strikes more than the stone.

She was forty, Glória, and since she was a girl she had known the underside of her mother. She knew it by heart, the way one knows the wall of the room where one sleeps: the mother who went down the path at any hour to come to the rescue of the village and who, at home, would not let her own daughter heat her soup. She remembered a time, as a small girl, of her mother having a bad cold, the kind that keeps you in bed, and she, Glória, with seven or eight years, having made a bread-and-milk soup, the only thing she knew how to make, and having carried it to her in the room, proud, with her hands trembling on the plate; and the mother having sat up in bed, and having thanked her with a kiss, and having said you eat it, child, you're growing, and having sent the soup back without tasting a spoonful. Glória had eaten the soup on the kitchen step, alone, without knowing why it hurt to have eaten the soup she had made for her mother; and it took her forty years to know. It hurt because the mother had taught her, in that refused spoonful, the first lesson of the wall: you give me nothing. With me it's only in one direction.

And there was the day of her wedding, which Glória kept the way one keeps a stone in the shoe, small and always there. She married young, Glória, and wanted, as all daughters want, her mother to dress her, to tie the cords of her gown, to be, that morning, a mother to her; and the mother dressed her, and tied the cords with those hands that knew everything, and it was a lovely thing; but in the middle of the morning, when there was almost nothing left to do, word came that the old woman up on the mountain was passing badly, and Socorro, without blinking, set the comb down on the chest of drawers and said I'll just go there and come right back, child, it's only a moment, and went down the path of trouble in her feast clothes, and came back with the church already full, in time, always in time, but reeking of the rosemary of the sick and with her hands unwashed. And Glória married with her mother beside her, and the mother wept in the right place, and no one from outside saw anything; but Glória knew, that morning, with the gown tied by her mother's hands, the thing it would take her a lifetime to forgive: that for the mother the trouble of a strange old woman always, always came before the daughter; that Glória's place, even at her own wedding, was the place of the one who gives no trouble, of the one who waits, of the one who understands that the mother has more to do. The mother served the whole village on her knees. The daughter had to serve herself.

And so it always was. Glória had grown up trying to break through the wall and hurting herself on it. She brought things, and the mother said it wasn't needed. She offered to stay, and the mother said go to your own life. She came to shelter her on a bad day, and the mother, with that polite, impenetrable firmness that was the whole texture of the wall, said I manage on my own, child, don't trouble yourself; and Glória went back home, three doors down, with empty hands of the care she had brought to give and that the mother had not let her set down, and went on hurting herself on that it wasn't needed like one who spends a lifetime rubbing the same stone on the same spot of the arm, until the skin grows thin and any rub bleeds.

And the wall had not stopped at Glória; it had come down, the way things come down from house to house, to Glória's children, to Socorro's grandchildren. Because the grandmother gave them everything, presents, sweets, coins hidden in the hand on the way out of Mass, but did not let them give her anything back; and the little ones, who are sharp at these things, learned early that grandmother Socorro was for receiving and not for giving to, and when they wanted to make her a drawing, or pick her flowers from the path, or give her a kiss too many, she thanked them and slipped aside, with that knack of not wanting too much affection that Glória knew by heart; and Glória watched her children striking against the same wall she had struck against her whole life, and it hurt her doubly, for her and for them, to see the curse of the wall passing from generation to generation, the grandmother who gives and does not receive teaching, without meaning to, one more batch of people to think that with Socorro it's only in one direction. And once the youngest, four years old, asked her mother, aloud, at the table, why doesn't grandmother like us to give her things; and Glória did not know what to answer, and Socorro, who was at the table, heard, and pretended not to hear, and carried the grandchild's question sideways the rest of the day, without knowing why, the same way she had carried, years before, the neighbour's sentence about her eldest; and only in the illness would she come to know what the question of a four-year-old grandchild had truly asked her, which was grandmother, why don't you let us love you.

Because there was, in that, a particular sadness, which was that of an impossible nearness. Glória had her mother three houses away, could see her every day, and yet was further from her than if she lived on the other side of the mountain, in Doroteia's big town, because the distance was not measured in houses, it was measured in the wall. The mother was right there, within reach, and was unreachable. The mother gave to the whole village and to the daughter let her give nothing, and a mother who never needs the daughter is a mother who keeps the daughter at the door her whole life, with the care in her hand and the door ajar, and Glória had grown up, and married, and had her own, and grown old already with white threads in her hair, always on the outside of that door, the daughter of a woman whom everyone called a saint and who to her, to her own daughter, had never let her be a daughter entirely, only a daughter from the outside.

It was the illness that brought it all to the surface. Glória, who had found her mother abed that morning, threw herself into the care with the hunger of one who has waited forty years for a door to open: she cleaned the house, made the broths, wanted to stay and sleep there, wanted to see to everything. And the mother, ill, weak, with hardly the strength to lift her head from the pillow, still found strength, from the depth of the wall, to refuse her, with the same firmness as always, go home, child, go to your man and to your children, I manage on my own, and when Glória saw, on the stone bench at the door, on the third day, the dish she herself had made returned full, with the mother's cold rice inside and the little note, it wasn't needed, it was as though the whole wall fell on her at once.

She came into the house without knocking. The mother was in bed, looking at the ceiling, and Glória stood at the foot of the bed with the returned dish in her trembling hands, and said, with a grief of forty years coming out all at once, without sub-clauses, without care, from the depths:

"Mother, why won't you let me? I'm your daughter. You cared for half the village and you cared for me my whole life, and now that I want to care for you, you return me the dish. Full. To me, Mother. Look at this." And she set it down on the bed, hard, and the cold rice shook inside the clay. "You return it to me as you return it to Aurélia, as though I were any neighbour to whom you owe no trust at all. You give to everyone, and to me you let me give nothing. Do you know how I feel, Mother? I feel that you don't need me. That I'm no use to you at all. That you'd rather die here alone, in this room, than let me in. What did I do to you? Tell me, for I want to know, forty years I've wanted to know: what did I do to you that you'd shut the door on me?"

And Socorro, in the bed, was left without an answer, because the daughter had said, without knowing it, the exact thing. The daughter had set before her what she had never had the courage to look at: that the wall she had raised to protect herself from Doroteia's empty door had left her own daughter on the outside her whole life, running up against it, hurting herself, growing up convinced she was dispensable. She had thought she was sparing the daughter by not troubling her, by not weighing on her, by not letting her lose nights with an ill mother. And she understood, in that bed, with the returned dish standing as proof between them, that she had not spared her, she had deprived her; that the daughter had not felt spared, she had felt rejected; that she, in not receiving from Glória, had not taken a weight from her, she had taken a mother from her; that she was doing to her daughter, without meaning to, and for forty years, exactly what Doroteia had done to her in a single winter: leaving her in want, waiting, on the outside of a door that does not open.

"Daughter," she said, and the voice came out small, without the firmness of the wall, for the first time. "You did nothing to me." And she wanted to say more, and did not know how, because the truth she had to say was too great and too old and was at the bottom of the chest, beneath her grandmother's clothes, and she did not yet have the strength to go and fetch it.

But something opened in that room, a chink, thin as the blade of light that comes through a door left ajar. Socorro looked at her forty-year-old daughter weeping at the foot of the bed, with a clay dish in her hands, and understood, slowly, a thing that began to move the stone: that to receive was not only a thing she refused to do to protect herself. To receive was also a gift one gives to whoever loves us. Because Glória did not come to collect anything, did not come to weigh on her, did not come to make up her account; Glória came to ask her to let her care, which is a hunger that children have and that parents, when they shut themselves off, never come to still. And she, in returning the dishes to her daughter, in not letting her in, was not sparing Glória the work, she was robbing her of the joy of caring for one she loves, she was making for her, for the daughter, the empty door that Doroteia had made for her; and perhaps, thought Socorro for the first time, in that room, with the chink of light coming in, perhaps the cure of her old wound was not to go on shutting the door, it was, on the contrary, to open it to this daughter who had been striking against it for forty years, who offered a dish full of love without a bill, and who deserved, more than anyone in the village, to hear from her mother, at last, what an empty dish says without words: set it down there, daughter, and stay, for I'll eat with you, for I trust you, for I let you in.

That afternoon, after the daughter left with the dish returned full still in her hands, Socorro lay in bed looking at the ceiling, and there came to her memory, without her calling it, a thing from when Glória was small. The little girl had been about five, and had fallen ill with a children's thing, measles or the like, and had stayed in bed a week with fever; and Socorro remembered having cared for her with all the care in the world, having made her broths and porridges and having spent sleepless nights at her bedside. But she remembered also, now, with a pang, a thing she had not seen at the time: the little girl, in the middle of the fever, holding out her little arms asking to be held, and she, Socorro, having said lie down, child, the lap will heat your fever, stay lying down, mother's here close by. And she had been close by, yes, watching, caring, giving; but she had not given the lap. She had not lain down beside the daughter and let her lean against her. She had cared for the daughter on her feet, at the bedside, as she cared for the village, and the little girl had learned there, at five and with a fever, the first lesson of the wall: that the mother cares but does not lean in, that the mother gives but does not draw near, that with the mother there is a lap from afar but no lap up close. And Socorro understood, now, lying there, with her own fever coming down, that she had spent her life confusing caring with sheltering from outside, and that the daughter, who had always had her at the bedside, had never had her lying beside her; and that perhaps the thing the daughter had come to ask of her, at forty, with a dish in her hands, was the same she had asked of her at five with her arms held out, and that she had, finally, to learn to give: not the care from outside, which she had always given, but the leaning-in, the drawing-near, the letting-herself-be-leaned-against.

She did not say it yet. It was early. But she came to know it, and to know is the first stone one takes from a wall.
