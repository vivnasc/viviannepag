## 6. The Keeper of the register

The Keeper of the Register came to see Socorro on an afternoon of late winter, and she came on foot, slowly, with her dark shawl and her step of one who measures the path the way she measures her words, because an illness of the one who had always come to everyone's rescue was a thing the Keeper did not let pass without going, herself, to the living book, to the bedside, to read the entry from within.

She sat in the chair by the bed, the same where Lucinda had sat, and stayed a moment silent looking at Socorro, in that way she had of looking at people as one reads a page of old letters. She noticed, without making anything of it, the pile of washed dishes on the chest of drawers, all of them others', all ready to be returned full; she noticed the little notes already written, folded beside them, it wasn't needed, I manage on my own; and she understood, in a glance, the whole stubbornness, the village putting and Socorro returning, and what lay beneath it.

"Socorro," she said, without hurry. "I've known your name in my book for fifty years. It was my predecessor who entered it for you at the baptism, and it was I who went on entering it, and do you know a thing I noticed, turning the pages all these years? You appear in hundreds of entries. Hundreds, Socorro, I don't exaggerate. At Rosalina's birth, helping. At the death of Aurélia's Justino, shrouding. In old Custódia's illness, watching the whole night on the kitchen floor. In Benilde's winter, carrying broths on all fours. Hundreds of times, always you, always on the same side. And do you know, on the other side, that of the one who is helped, how many times you appear?" She made a pause, and the pause weighed in the room. "None. In fifty years of book, not a single time on the side of those who receive. You are the person who has given the most in this village. And you are the one who has received the least, because you never received anything, you never let it."

And the Keeper, as if to prove to her that she did not speak from rote, closed her eyes a moment and recited, from memory, like one reading from a page she has within:

"In the year of the great flood, when the river carried off Quitéria's lower house, who was it that slept three nights with her and the children in the granary by the churchyard? You. It is entered. In the winter the fever went from house to house and the blacksmith's two boys died, who was it that went door to door with the broths, without sleeping, until the fever passed? You. It is entered. When Bernardina lost her mind from grief and no one would have her near, who was it that went there every day to feed her by the mouth and wash her, for two years, until she died? You, Socorro. It is all entered, in my book, with your name, always on the same side." She opened her eyes. "And I went on turning the pages, year after year, waiting to see you, once, just once, on the other side. Waiting for an entry that said: on this day, Socorro let herself be helped. On this day, someone cared for Socorro and she accepted. Fifty years, Socorro. I never saw it. And a person who appears in hundreds of entries always giving and not once receiving is not a saint, my child. She is a woman with a door shut somewhere, which I did not know where it was, and which I came here today, to your bed, to find."

Socorro opened her mouth for the usual thing, I manage on my own, and the Keeper, who knew the usual thing better than she did, raised her hand slowly, like one asking for just a moment more.

"Let me tell you what that is, Socorro, for I've seen it many times in this book and learned to read it, and I'll tell you a thing that will be hard for you to hear. A person who only gives and never receives seems the most generous of all. And she is not. She is the most shut. Because giving is easy, Socorro, for one who fears receiving. Giving keeps us on the strong side of the exchange, the side of the one who can, the upper side; no one sees us in our want while we are giving. And receiving, that is what is hard, because receiving puts us on the weak side, that of the one who needs, the lower, in plain sight, vulnerable. You, who watched over the dead and brought births without blinking, have no fear at all of giving. But you have a fear of receiving that makes you tip the food out of the neighbours' dishes and fill them with your own so that not even the warmth of another's soup gets in through your mouth. Your giving, which the whole village calls saintliness, has beneath it a fear. And until you heal it, you'll go on alone, however much you give, because it is the receiving, and not the giving, that lets us into one another."

And Socorro, hearing, felt the wall tremble, because the Keeper was saying aloud what she had hidden from everyone and almost from herself; and she lowered her guard, and did what she had not done in twenty years, she told.

"There was a year," she began, and the voice came out hoarse, of a thing not used in a long time. "I never told this to anyone, not even to my daughter. There was a year when everything fell on me at once. My man died, slowly, eight months, and I spent on him all I had. And no sooner had I buried him, the flood came, and the river carried off half my house, and I was left with the roof open to the cold. And in that cold I fell ill, for the first time in my life, without strength, without anything, with the mourning fresh and the house in ruins." She stopped, and the Keeper waited. "And I, who had never asked anyone for anything, did the hardest thing of my life. I took from the bottom of the chest the blue clay dish, my grandmother's, the feast one, filled it, and crossed the mountain on foot, and went to set it at my sister's door, at Doroteia's, in the big town. And I asked her. I said to her, sister, for the first time it's I who need, will you come and stay with me this winter, help me cover the house? I did it for you when your little one was lost. And she said yes. She looked me in the eyes, with her hands in mine, and said of course I'll come, sister, wait for me, I'll come right behind you."

The Keeper said nothing. She waited for the rest, which she knew came worse.

"And I waited," said Socorro, and now the tears came. "I put the empty dish on the sill of the window that looks out on the path, to have somewhere to look while I waited. And I waited the whole winter, with the blanket nailed with tacks over the hole in the ceiling, and every night the blanket beat in the wind I woke thinking it's her, she's come. And it wasn't. It was the wind. I waited two months, and she didn't come, nor send word, nor anything. And when I, at the end of two months, crossed the mountain again only to know if she was alive, I found her well, with the house warm, and she lowered her eyes and said the sentence I've carried for twenty years. She said: ah, sister, I was going to, but so much kept coming up, and I thought that you, poor thing, you who are so strong, you'd manage on your own. You always manage."

She told it all, and the Keeper listened, without interrupting her, without judging her, with her hands still in her lap, and when Socorro finished, the Keeper stayed a moment in silence, letting the entry settle, and then said to her the thing that began to heal her:

"Socorro, I understand. You put your dish at one person's door, once in your life, at the door of the one from whom you most had the right to expect it, and she returned it to you full, or did not return it at all, and it hurt you so much that you decided, on that mountain, never again to put a dish at anyone's door. And I don't reproach you the decision. You were left in the cold by the one who had the duty to come. But look what you did with that decision, mark it well: because of one door that stayed empty, you shut yourself to all doors. Because of one person who failed you in one winter, you refuse, twenty years later, the whole village that does not fail you. The dishes you have at your door now are not Doroteia's, Socorro. They are Aurélia's, who watched over your man... no, it was you who watched over hers, see how you confuse it, from so much giving; they are Lucinda's, Benilde's, your own daughter's, people who love you and who will not fail you. And you return them full, and make them pay, them, for another's empty door, of twenty years ago, that has nothing to do with this. Don't let one empty door shut all your doors for you. Most doors, Socorro, when you put the dish to them, return it to you full of love, without a bill. Doroteia's was one. It was one, and it cost you your whole life. But don't make it the law of all of them."

"And I shut myself off," said Socorro, at the end, almost in a breath. "I came back to Véspera with the full dish I never managed to leave at her door, and I put it at the bottom of the chest, and never used it again, and never asked anyone for anything again, and never let anyone give me anything again. I made myself everyone's help so as never again to need anyone's help. And the village thought I was strong, and even I believed it, that it was easier to believe it was strength than to know it was fear."

The Keeper stayed a moment silent, and then said, slowly:

"It was not strength, Socorro. It was a wound that dressed itself as strength because strength in Véspera has applause and a wound has pity, and you always preferred the applause to the pity, like everyone who gives too much. But a wound dressed as strength is the worst of wounds, because no one treats it, unlike those that can be seen; and yours went twenty years without treatment, shutting you off within, while the village clapped for you without. I, who read the book, always saw the wound beneath the applause. That is why I came."

Socorro wept, low, and the Keeper let her weep, for weeping too is an entry, and one of the true ones. After a while, she got up, settled her shawl, and at the door of the room she turned once more, for the last thing, the one she left planted in Socorro like a seed:

"And do you know which is the loveliest entry I write in this book of mine, Socorro? You'll think it's the one of whoever gave much. It is not. Of those there are plenty, and you are one, the greatest I have known. The loveliest entry I write is the one of whoever learned to receive. Because giving, many give. Receiving, letting oneself be sheltered, opening the door and trusting again after having been left in the cold, that is rare, that is of the bravest of the brave. You have already been the bravest in this village at giving. You lack being brave at receiving, and it is harder, and it is the only courage you have not yet tried. Accept a dish, Socorro. One, just one. Eat it to the end. And return it empty. It is the bravest thing you will do in your life, more than everything you have given, because it is to risk trusting after the empty door, and to trust after having been left is the greatest courage there is, and it is the only one I cannot enter in the book for you. That one, you enter it yourself, with your own hand, in returning the first dish empty."

And she went off down the path, slowly, with her shawl trembling in the late-winter wind, and Socorro stayed in bed with the Keeper's truth settling within her like dust that comes to rest in a room where, at last, someone has opened a window. And she looked at the dishes on the chest of drawers in another way: no longer as dangers to refuse, but as hands held out that she, because of an old door and a sister who did not come, had been refusing for twenty years without telling one from another. And she understood that her crossing, from then on, was not to go on giving, for that she knew better than anyone in Véspera; it was to learn to receive, to accept a dish, eat it, return it empty with her own hand, and to enter, herself, in the living book of her life, the entry the Keeper had said was the loveliest of all and the only one no one could write for her.
