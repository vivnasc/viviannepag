## 6. The Keeper of the register

It was the Keeper who visited Socorro, that time, because an illness of the one who had always come to everyone's rescue is a thing the Keeper does not let pass; and it was at the bedside, with the register in her memory, that the Keeper told her the truth that was missing, that of giving and receiving.

The Keeper sat at the bedside, and they spoke, and the Keeper, who saw people as she saw the entries, understood at once the stubbornness of the dishes, Socorro returning full what the village put at her door; and said to her, with that manner of one who reads deep: Socorro, I have known your name in my book for fifty years. And do you know a thing I noticed? You appear in hundreds of entries, always on the same side. At the births, helping. At the deaths, shrouding. In the illnesses, watching. Hundreds of times, Socorro, always you coming to the rescue. And never, not once in fifty years, do you appear on the other side, that of one who is helped. You are the person who gave most in this village, and the one who received least, because you never received anything, you never let yourself.

And the Keeper said a thing that shook Socorro: and I am going to tell you what that is, because I have seen it many times and learned to see it. A person who only gives and never receives seems the most generous of all, and is not. She is the most shut. Because to give, Socorro, is easy for one who is afraid to receive, giving keeps us on the strong side, on the side of those who can, on the upper side; and to receive is what is hard, because to receive puts us on the weak side, on that of those who need, on the lower side, exposed, vulnerable. And one who only gives and never receives is, deep down, protecting herself: giving so as not to have to receive, staying on the upper side so as never to be on the lower, sheltering others so as never to have to be sheltered. Your giving, Socorro, that the whole village praises, has beneath it a fear, the fear of receiving, and while you do not heal it, you will go on alone, however much you give, because it is the receiving, and not the giving, that lets us into one another.

And Socorro, listening, lowered her guard, and told the Keeper what she had never told anyone: the year of misfortune, the dish she had put at someone's door, the empty door, the help that did not come, the decision of never again. And the Keeper listened, and did not judge her, and said to her the thing that began to heal her: Socorro, I understand. You put your dish at a person's door, once, and it came back empty, or did not come back, and it hurt you so much that you decided never again to put a dish at anyone's door. But look at what you did: because of one door that stayed empty, you shut yourself to all doors. Because of one person who failed you, you refuse the whole village that does not fail you. The dishes you have at your door now are not that person's, they are the stubborn neighbours', your daughter's, of people who love you and who will not fail you; and you return them full, and make them pay for the empty door of another, from twenty years ago, who have nothing to do with this. Do not let one empty door shut all doors for you, Socorro. Most doors, when you put the dish at them, return it full to you. That one was one. Do not make it the law of all.

And the Keeper, on leaving, said to her still a thing, that stayed with Socorro: and do you know what is the most beautiful entry I write in this book? It is not that of one who gave much, for of those there are many. It is that of one who learned to receive. Because to give, many give. To receive, to let oneself be sheltered, to trust again after having been left, that is rare, that is of the courageous. You have already been the most courageous at giving. It remains for you to be courageous at receiving, which is harder. Accept a dish, Socorro. Eat it. Return it empty. For it is the most courageous thing you will do in your life, more than all you gave, because it is to risk trusting, after the empty door, and to trust after having been left is the greatest courage there is.

And Socorro stayed with the Keeper's truth, and looked at the dishes at the door in another way: no longer as a danger to refuse, but as hands held out that she, out of fear of an old door, had been refusing; and she understood that her crossing, from there on, was not to go on giving, for that she knew, it was to learn to receive, it was to accept a dish, eat it, return it empty, and to risk, at last, trusting again, after a life of not trusting anyone so as never again to see, at her door, a dish that does not arrive.
