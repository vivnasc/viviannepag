## 8. The first dish accepted

One thing is to be made to eat by the stubborn neighbour who will not leave the chair; another is to accept, by choice, the dish one could still return. And it was the second, the dish accepted of one's own will, that began to heal Socorro in earnest, and it made sense that it was with the daughter, because it was on the daughter that the wall hurt most, and it was with the daughter that Socorro most needed to be reconciled.

Glória arrived one morning, with a dish in her hands, as she had so many times arrived, and already with her armour ready for the usual thing, already with her heart half shut against the it wasn't needed she expected to hear; she came out of habit, out of a daughter's stubbornness, without much hope, to set the dish on the stone bench more so as not to give up than out of belief that it would serve any purpose. It was a stew, the kind the mother had taught her when she was small, with the beans and the cabbage and the sausage, and Glória had made it on purpose exactly like her mother's, perhaps so the mother would see that the daughter had learned to make what the mother made, perhaps without quite knowing why.

And Socorro, that morning, with all the wound of the empty door screaming at her within return it, don't accept, don't trust, remember the sill, remember the mountain, did the thing she had never done in her life. Before Glória could set the dish on the bench and flee as she always did, Socorro said, from the door, with her voice trembling in her throat:

"Don't put it there, daughter." Glória stopped, halfway to the bench, not understanding. "Set it down inside. On the table. And stay. Stay, for I'll eat with you."

Glória stood still with the dish in her hands, and did not believe it. She looked at her mother in search of the catch, of the it wasn't needed that should come next, and it did not come. The mother was at the door, with the blanket on her shoulders, with wet eyes and a thing on her face Glória had never seen on it: fear. The mother, who had no fear of the dead nor of births nor of nights on kitchen floors, had a fear of letting her in with a stew. And it was that fear, more than the invitation, that told Glória this was in earnest, because a thing that costs so much to the one who does it can only be true.

"Mother?" she said, only that, low, still at the door.

"Come in, daughter. Please. Before I change my mind."

And there was, still, an instant in which Socorro almost changed her mind. She had her hand halfway to taking the dish from the daughter, to carrying it to the kitchen and doing the usual thing; and Glória saw her hand hesitate, and said nothing, stayed still, giving her mother the time the mother needed, because she had learned, in that month, that with the mother one could not push, that pushing raised the wall, that one could only wait on this side of the door for her, from within, to unbar it. And Socorro, with her hand halfway, heard within the old voice, return it, don't trust, and heard beneath it, thinner and deeper, the new voice that Lucinda and the Keeper had seeded in her, tell them apart, this is your daughter, this is not Doroteia, this one stays; and she lowered her hand, and it was like unbarring a door rusted shut for forty years, that creaks and resists and at last gives way.

And Glória came in, and set the dish on the table, and took two plates from the cupboard where they had always been, and served the stew, and the two sat down, the mother and the daughter, in the kitchen of always, at the table of always, to eat the food the daughter had made. And it was, for the two of them, an immense thing disguised as a small one, of those that in Véspera are done without anyone from outside noticing them.

For Glória, it was to be, at last, let to care for her mother. It was to see her mother lift to her mouth the stew she had made, and chew it, and swallow it, and not return it full with a cold rice on top; it was to come in, after forty years of striking against the wall, into the kitchen where the mother let her, at last, be a daughter entirely. And Glória, in the middle of the plate, had to set down her fork, because the joy would not fit in her chest and needed a moment to put itself in order; and she said only, watching her mother eat, is it good, Mother?, and the mother said it is, daughter, it's like mine, you learned it well, and Glória took up the fork again because if she didn't take up something she would fall apart.

And for Socorro, eating that stew was the hardest thing she had done in her life, harder than watching over her man, harder than crossing the mountain to ask Doroteia. Because with each spoonful she had to bear the whole fear of the empty door, the old voice that told her she's going to turn against you like Doroteia, she's going to leave you, you'll accept and one day she won't come, and she had to silence it, spoonful by spoonful, and trust, against the wound, that that stew, the daughter's, was not going to be an empty sill. And she bore it. She ate the whole stew, to the end, with Glória in front of her, and when the plate was clean, she felt, beneath the fear that little by little tired, the same sweetness Lucinda had shown her, but deeper, because it was the daughter, because it was the door that hurt most to open.

And there was, at the end, the gesture that sealed it all, and that in Véspera says more than a thousand words. Socorro got up, took the dish, carried it to the sink, washed it with her hands worn from sheltering, dried it with the cloth, and returned it to the daughter. Empty. And she put it into her hands, and held her hands over the dish a moment, and said, with the voice that finally no longer trembled:

"Take it, daughter. Empty. Do you know what this means?"

And Glória knew. She had known since she was a girl what a returned empty dish says, in Véspera, and never, in forty years, had she received one from her mother; she had always received them full, refused, and now she had it empty in her hands, and she understood what her mother was saying without saying it: I received you, daughter. I accepted your care. I trust you. I let you in. And Glória hugged the empty dish against her chest like one hugging a treasure, because she carried, in that empty clay, more than a clay: she carried the mother, at last, after a lifetime of wall, letting her pass the door.

"It means I trust you," said Socorro, because the daughter deserved to hear it aloud, once, after reading it so many times the other way round. "Twenty years telling you the opposite, daughter, with the full dishes. Forgive me those twenty years. But begin here."

And Glória went off weeping and laughing, three houses up, with her mother's empty dish pressed to her chest.

And Socorro, alone, went to the chest. She went without deciding to go; she opened it, took out her grandmother's clothes, and there at the bottom was the blue clay dish, the one she had put at Doroteia's door, the relic of the wound. She took it up with both hands, and stood looking at it by the window light, and for the first time in twenty years she looked at it without the usual tightening, with a kind of pity, the way one looks at a thing that hurt much and can no longer hurt. And she set it back in the chest, but this time on top of the clothes, in plain sight, not buried, because she did not yet have the strength to use it but she already had the strength to look at it, and to look at the wound without fleeing it is the first step toward letting it heal. And she went back to the kitchen, where the dish of the daughter's stew was drying beside the sink, empty and washed, the first she had returned empty in twenty years.

And she stayed in the kitchen, with the strange warmth of having received heating her within, understanding that nothing she had feared had happened. She had accepted the dish, and the daughter had not left her, had not turned against her, had not been an empty door. She had accepted the care, and the care had not been a debt nor a defeat, it had been an embrace. And Doroteia's empty door, of twenty years ago, that had made her shut herself to all receiving, revealed itself, in that washed dish, to be what the Keeper had told her: a door among many, an old misfortune, and not the law of all doors. Socorro understood, alone in the kitchen, that she had lost twenty years refusing dishes that would have stayed, shutting the door on people who would not have left her, because of a single one who had left her; and that each dish she had returned full had been a refused embrace, a severed bond, a solitude chosen out of fear of an old want. And that the daughter, whom she had kept twenty years at the door, had been the first to come in, and brought no bill at all, brought a stew just like hers and the old hunger to be, at last, let to care.

And from that first dish accepted by choice, she went on venturing more. She accepted Lucinda's soup, and ate it, and returned it empty; and Lucinda, on finding the empty dish on her bench, went running, with her old woman's steps, to show it to Aurélia, look, woman, look, she ate it all, here it is, empty, scraped to the bottom, I told you that if you took away her way out she'd eat. And the two stood looking at an empty dish the way one looks at a small miracle, which is what it was.

And she accepted Benilde's marmalade and did not pay with honey. And she accepted, at last, Aurélia's broth, the one of the saint's-day hen, and did it on purpose, because it was to that dish she owed most, to the first she had received and returned full, the one that had opened the whole stubbornness; and she ate it, and washed the dish of white crockery with the blue line, and returned it empty, with a note that said only, it was good the way only your broth is, Aurélia, and forgive the other one, the first, for that one I wasn't ready yet. And Aurélia, on finding on her stone bench the dish she had given full that first day and that had come back to her full, turned now empty and washed, with that note, sat right there on the bench, with the empty dish in her lap, and wept; and when Lucinda passed and asked her what it was, Aurélia said only, laughing and crying at the same time, she ate my broth, Lucinda, the saint's-day one, she ate it all, and asked my pardon for the first; and she carried the empty dish home, and never used it again for any broth, she kept it on the top of the cupboard, the way one keeps the feast crockery, because that dish, from then on, was worth more than all her good crockery together. And Socorro went on discovering, dish by dish, that receiving, which she had feared her whole life, did not lower her nor put her in debt; on the contrary, it bound her to people in a way the giving had never bound her, because the giving had kept her above, distant, untouchable, and the receiving put her alongside, near, vulnerable, human. And that she had, on the other side of the wall she had raised in a winter twenty years ago, not the empty door she feared, but a whole village of full dishes, waiting, for so long, to be, at last, accepted.
