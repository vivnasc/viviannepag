## 8. The first dish accepted

And Socorro accepted the first dish by her own decision, and it was a small gesture and it was enormous, because one thing is to be forced to eat by the stubborn neighbour who does not leave, another is to accept, by choice, the dish one could return, and it was that, the dish accepted by choice, the beginning of the cure.

It happened with the daughter, and it made sense that it should be with the daughter, because it was to the daughter that the wall most hurt, and it was with the daughter that Socorro most needed to be reconciled. The daughter arrived, one day, with a dish, as so many times, expecting, as always, to see it come back full; and Socorro, who had so many times returned it, that time, with her heart leaping, with the whole wound of the empty door crying out to her return it, do not accept, do not trust, did the thing she had never done: she accepted. She said to the daughter, in a trembling voice: set it down there, daughter. And stay. Stay, for I will eat with you. And the daughter, who expected the wall, on hearing the stay, for I will eat with you, stood still, not believing, because it was the first time in her life that the mother accepted a dish from her, the first time she let her in.

And they ate together, the mother and the daughter, the food the daughter had brought, and it was, for them both, an immense thing disguised as a small one. For the daughter, it was to be, at last, let care for the mother, to be accepted, to enter, after a life of hitting against the wall; and the daughter, watching the mother eat what she had made, felt a joy that did not fit, the joy of one who cares for one she loves and is, at last, let to. And for Socorro, it was to receive from the daughter, to lower the wall, to let in, to risk trusting; and while she ate the daughter's dish, Socorro felt all the fear of the empty door, the fear of accepting and being left, and she had to bear it, spoonful by spoonful, trusting, against the old wound, that that dish, the daughter's, was not going to turn against her, was not going to leave her, was not going to be an empty door.

And there was, at the end, the gesture that sealed it all, and that in Véspera says more than a thousand words: Socorro took the empty dish, washed it, and returned it to the daughter, empty. And to return the dish empty, in Véspera, is to say I received, I accepted your care, I trust you; and the daughter, on receiving from the mother the empty dish, the first empty dish instead of the full ones of always, understood what the mother was telling her without words, I receive you, daughter, I trust you, I let you in; and she went away with the empty dish pressed against her chest like one who carries a treasure, because she carried more than a dish, she carried the mother, at last, after a life of wall, letting her in.

And Socorro, alone, after the daughter left, with the strange warmth of having received, understood that nothing of what she feared had happened. She had accepted the dish, and the daughter had not left her, had not turned against her, had not been an empty door; she had accepted the care, and the care had not been a debt nor a defeat, it had been an embrace; and the empty door of twenty years ago, which had made her shut herself to all receiving, revealed itself, in that accepted dish, a door among many, an old misfortune, and not the law of all doors; and Socorro understood that she had lost years refusing dishes that would have stayed, shutting the door on people who would not have left her, because of a single one who had left her, and that each dish she had returned full had been a refused embrace, a cut bond, a chosen solitude out of fear of an old want.

And from that first accepted dish on, she ventured to more. She accepted the dish of the stubborn neighbour, and ate it, and returned it empty. She accepted that of another neighbour. She accepted the lifts, the helps, the cares; and she went on discovering, dish by dish, that to receive, which she had feared her whole life, did not lower her nor indebt her, on the contrary, it connected her to people in a way the giving had never connected her, because the giving kept her above, distant, and the receiving put her beside, near, vulnerable, human; and Socorro, who had connected to the whole village by giving and been alone below, began, on receiving, to connect also below, in intimacy, and to discover that the village, which she had sheltered her whole life, was full of people willing to shelter her, if she, at last, let them, and that she had, on the other side of the wall she had raised, not the empty door she feared, but a whole village of full dishes waiting to be accepted.
