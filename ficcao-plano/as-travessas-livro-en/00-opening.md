## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Socorro — help, succour — daughter of the village, and the name was a wound set in baptism: they called her Socorro, the one who comes to the rescue, and she fulfilled the name her whole life, being the help of others, the one who comes, the one who shelters, the one who is there when someone needs; but the name, which was for giving, turned against her, because the one who is the help of others learns to give and never to receive, and Socorro, who came to everyone's rescue, did not know, when misfortune struck her, how to let them come to hers.

The register keeps who gives and who receives in the houses of the village, and there are names that always appear on the side of those who come to the rescue: at births helping, at deaths shrouding, in illnesses watching, in troubles coming; and Socorro was one of those names, she was in the register of Véspera on the side of those who give, always, in fifty-five years, never on the side of those who receive, because Socorro gave herself to all and did not let anyone give themselves to her, and had her hands worn from sheltering and shut to being sheltered.

This is the sixth book of the shelf of the Mountain, the shelf of those who ask what they had to become in order to bear up, and within it, it is the book of the one who does not know how to receive help. It tells the story of a woman who, when she falls ill, and the village does what the village does, dishes of food at the door, offers of a lift, neighbours for the chores, returns the dishes full, with a note of thanks, because to count on someone, one day, in the year she most needed, ended in pain, and since then she refuses support by method, not out of pride in strength like others, but out of a wound of one who gave herself once and was left looking at the empty door. It is not a book against giving; giving is good, and Socorro gave her whole life; it is a book about one who only knows how to give and does not know how to receive, and about the wound that lies beneath, and about the courage, the greatest for one who was left wanting, of accepting the first dish, and eating it, and returning it empty, which in Véspera is the greatest declaration of trust there is.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Socorro who is everyone's help and lets no one help her, who returns full the dishes left at her door, who says thank you, it is not needed, I manage, and who refuses support by method because once she counted and was left, let her know, from the first line, what it took Socorro a whole life not to know: that to receive is not a defeat, it is a trust; that to return a dish full is to say I do not trust you, and to return it empty is to say I trust; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to go on giving without ever receiving, it is to accept the first dish, and to eat it to the end, and to return it empty, and to risk, after the empty door of old, trusting again.
