## 5. The stubborn neighbours

There were, in Véspera, three neighbours who did not give up on Socorro, and it was their stubbornness, more than anything that was said, that began to break through the wall from the outside, because there is love that only breaks through by sheer not-giving-up.

They were Aurélia, Lucinda and Benilde, and the three had, each one, a debt without a debt to Socorro, of those she had scattered through the village her whole life without letting anyone pay them. Aurélia was a widow, and it was Socorro who had watched over her man the last nights and who had stayed with her in the empty house the first week of mourning, not letting her wake alone; Lucinda had had a son who came too early, before his time, and it was Socorro who had brought the birth and said in her ear hold on, Lucinda, he's coming, and he had come, and was a man now; and Benilde, the youngest of the three, owed Socorro a whole winter of broths in the year the rheumatism had left her on all fours. The three had, therefore, a thing to give back, and when Socorro returned the dishes to them full, instead of being hurt and giving up like the other neighbours, they grew stubborn.

Because many had given up, and it was natural that they gave up, and that made the stubbornness of the three stand out the more. The miller's wife, who had put her chestnut soup and seen it come back full, was hurt and put no more, and said about the village, with some spite, that Socorro was proud, that she always had been, that whoever won't be helped must bear it. Quitéria, the one of the granary by the churchyard, put one once, received it back, and shrugged, each one knows their own business, I offered, she didn't want it, that's her affair. And so the siege fell away, neighbour by neighbour, as the dishes came back full and the pride of each was wounded; and at the end of two weeks there were only three stone benches left from which food still went out, stubbornly, to Socorro's door, Aurélia's, Lucinda's and Benilde's, three women who did not give up because they had understood, before the village and before Socorro herself, that this was not pride refusing, it was a wound defending itself, and that a wound is not healed by telling it to bear up, it is healed by not giving up on it.

Aurélia put a dish again the day after the refused broth. And Socorro returned it full. And Benilde put beans. And Socorro returned it full. And Lucinda put a turnip soup, and one more loaf each time, and one more preserve, and Socorro returned it all full, and the three, instead of giving up, gathered at the edge of the washing tank to plan, the way one plans a stubborn campaign of war, the way to break through the most stubborn woman Véspera had ever produced. They did it laughing, but they did it in earnest, because they had understood, before Socorro herself, that this was not pride, it was a wound, and that a wound is not healed with one dish, it is healed with many, and with patience, and with no one budging.

"Returning it full, she'll return it till she dies," said Aurélia. "I know her by now. By note, we'll send her dishes till judgment day and she'll send them back, and she'll die clean and alone, with her pantry full of the food she didn't eat from ours."

"Then we don't send it by note," said Lucinda, pressing her lips the way she had when she was about to decide a thing. "By note she always wins. We send it in person. One of us goes, comes in, sits down, and doesn't leave until she sees her eat. To her face. We take away her way out."

Aurélia tried first, in her own way, which was the milder. Instead of sending the dish, she wrote a note and pinned it to the linen, and the note asked for nothing, it only told a thing: Socorro, this broth is from the hen you made me kill, remember, when my Justino went and I hadn't eaten in three days and you came and killed it for me by force and made me broth and stayed and watched me drink it. I drank because you stayed. Now you drink, for I'll stay. And Socorro read the note, and the note touched her, and she stood looking at the dish a long while, with the memory of Justino and of Aurélia's three days pulling her toward the spoon; but the fear was stronger than the memory, and she returned the broth full again, with a note beneath Aurélia's, I remember, sister, and I'm glad you've got your hunger back, but I'm well, keep that. And Aurélia, on receiving her own note back with the answer beneath it, understood that with Socorro words were not enough, that Socorro was more stubborn than any word, and went to tell Lucinda that words did not break through that woman, that something else was needed, a body in a chair was needed.

And it fell to Lucinda, who was the one who pressed her lips, to go.

It was on an afternoon of fine drizzle, the kind on which Véspera goes still and the kitchens smell of smoke. Lucinda did not set the dish on the stone bench and go off, as was always done. She knocked at the door, with the hot dish between her hands, and when Socorro opened, weak, with the blanket on her shoulders, already getting the it wasn't needed ready, Lucinda came in. She came in without asking, with the dish out front like a shield, went to the kitchen table, set it down, uncovered it, and the kitchen filled with the smell of a bean soup with cabbage and a bone giving it body. Then she pulled out a chair, sat down, folded her hands in her lap, and looked at Socorro with the affectionate stubbornness of one who has already decided not to move.

"Socorro," she said. "I'll tell you straight off how it is, so we don't go round in circles, for you go round well and I tire. I brought you this soup, and you're going to eat it here, in front of me, today, now, with the spoon I brought, because I'm not getting up from this chair until I see that dish empty. You can pull a sour face, I won't leave. You can tell me it wasn't needed, I won't hear it. I'm old now, I've the evening free, and the rain out there doesn't tempt me at all. I'll stay as long as it takes."

Socorro opened her mouth for the usual thing, and Lucinda raised a finger.

"And before you say I've more to do, just hear one thing, and then I'll be quiet and you'll eat. When my Joaquim was born before his time, that night, purple, not crying, who was it that spent sixteen hours with her hand on my belly saying hold on, Lucinda, he's coming? It was you. And who cleaned me, and washed me, and made me food when I didn't even know my own name? It was you. I don't pay you for that with a soup, Socorro, I know. There's no soup that pays for that night. But you're not going to take from me the pleasure of setting one in front of you. That, that's mine, and it's my turn, and I'll have it even if I have to stay and sleep here."

And Socorro, with Lucinda planted there in the chair, decided, with no way of doing what she always did, return it full, send her off, felt all the old distress rise in her, the distress of being made to receive, to eat in front of another, to be seen in her want. And she almost refused all the same, almost said go away, Lucinda, I'm not up to this today. But Lucinda stayed. And Socorro, cornered in her own corner, with the soup steaming and the neighbour watching her, took up the spoon.

The first spoonful cost her what it costs to swallow a whole life the wrong way round. She lifted it to her mouth trembling, with her eyes lowered, like one doing a forbidden thing. And Lucinda said nothing, did not hurry her, made no fuss, only stayed there, with her hands in her lap, watching her eat, with a quiet tenderness that did not weigh. And Socorro ate the second spoonful, and the third, and the soup was good, it was of the beans and the cabbage and the bone and of one thing more that Lucinda had put into it and that was not in the recipe, and beneath the distress, slowly, Socorro felt a thing she had not expected and that disarmed her entirely: she felt cared for. She felt the warmth of having there, in front of her, on an afternoon of rain, a person who had spent her time making a soup just for her, and who stayed to watch her eat it, without hurry, without a bill, only for the pleasure of seeing her fed. She was not alone in the illness. There was an occupied chair in her kitchen, and the person in the chair was there for her, because of her, for her. And it was the first time in twenty years that Socorro tasted what it is to receive, and it was so warm, and so unfamiliar, that at the middle of the dish the tears came to her eyes, silent, and fell into the soup, and she went on eating, because a thing that is healing is not to be interrupted.

Lucinda saw the tears and said nothing of them. She waited for the dish to be empty, right to the end, to the last bean Socorro scraped up with the spoon, and only then got up, took the empty dish, and smiled.

"There," she said. "It's done. And you didn't die, see? You're here, alive, with your belly warm and your face wet, but alive." She wiped the dish on her apron, slowly, like one caressing a trophy. "That's how it's done, Socorro. You gave your whole life, to the whole village, to me, to my Joaquim. Fine. You were good. Now let them give to you, for it's no weakness, it's your turn. Whoever only gives and never receives, do you know what that is, Socorro? It's a miser. It's a miser in reverse. She keeps all the receiving to herself and gives it to no one. Don't be a miser of your receiving, woman. Share it with us, for we too are hungry to give to you."

And she went off into the rain, with the empty dish under her shawl like one carrying a child in her arms, content, humming low; and Socorro stayed in the kitchen, with the bowl washed and Lucinda's spoon forgotten on the table, understanding a thing that turned her life inside out. To receive, which she had feared for twenty years as a defeat, as an exposure, as the risk of the empty door, was after all, beneath the fear, a sweetness: the sweetness of being cared for, of not being alone in the want, of letting whoever loves us draw near, at last, to us. She had spent twenty years refusing that sweetness out of fear of a door that did not open; and Lucinda, by not giving up, by staying, by taking away her way out, had just shown her, with a bean soup, what Doroteia had taught her the other way round: that not all dishes come back empty from others' doors. There are dishes that come full, and that stay, and that sit in our kitchen on an afternoon of rain, and that do not leave until we let ourselves be loved. That night, Socorro did not sleep, but it was not the anxious insomnia of her sick nights. She lay awake looking at the ceiling, with her belly still warm from Lucinda's soup, going over the whole afternoon again, the spoon lifted to her mouth, the tears fallen into the clay, Lucinda saying don't be a miser of your receiving. And there was, in that wakeful night, a new thing, which was almost fear and was almost joy, and that she could not name and that was, though she did not know it, the first stone of the wall coming loose. She got up in the middle of the night, went to the drawer where she had put Lucinda's spoon, and took it out, and stood looking at it by the lamplight, an ordinary wooden spoon, worn on one side from so much stirring of pots, of no value at all; and she understood that that spoon, which she should have returned with the dish and had not returned, was the first thing she had accepted to keep in twenty years. The first thing of another person that came into her house and stayed. And she put it away again, with care, like one keeping a relic, and went back to bed, and slept, at last, a sleep without watch, the first in many years.

And the next morning, when Lucinda passed on the path and asked, from afar, well, did you sleep better with your belly warm?, Socorro, instead of the usual thing, instead of I'm well, don't trouble yourself, answered a small thing that for her was enormous: I slept, Lucinda. I slept well. Thank you for the soup. And Lucinda, who understood the size of that I slept well, thank you, made no fuss at all, only waved and went on her way, but she went smiling within, because she knew, from mother to daughter, that to break through a wall like that always begins this way, with a soup eaten by force and a thank you said by accident.

And Socorro kept Lucinda's spoon in the drawer, and did not return it, and it was the first thing she accepted to keep in twenty years.
