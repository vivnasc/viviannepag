## 5. The stubborn neighbours

There were, in the village, some stubborn neighbours who did not give up on Socorro, who went on putting dishes at her door however much she returned them full, and it was their stubbornness, more than anything, that began to pierce Socorro's wall from outside, because there is love that only pierces by dint of not giving up.

There were two or three of them, old neighbours, people Socorro had come to the rescue of many times and who wanted to repay her by coming to hers now; and when Socorro returned their dishes full, with the notes, instead of being hurt and giving up, like the others, these were stubborn. They put out a dish again the next day. And Socorro returned it full. And they put it out again. And Socorro returned it again. And so, day after day, the stubborn neighbours and Socorro held a stubbornness of dishes, they putting and she returning, without words, a stubbornness of love against a stubbornness of fear, and neither side gave in, and the thing seemed it would last forever, the village insisting and Socorro refusing.

And one of the neighbours, the most stubborn, one day, instead of putting the dish and going away, stayed. She knocked at the door, and when Socorro opened, the neighbour entered, with the dish in her hand, and said, with the affectionate stubbornness of one who does not give up: oh Socorro, I am going to put this dish here on the table, and you are going to eat it in front of me, today, now, because I am not leaving until I see it empty. You came to me when my man died, you stayed with me nights, you gave me to eat when I had no head to cook. Do you think I am going to let you return the dish full to me? I am not. Eat. For it is my turn to give to you.

And Socorro, with the neighbour there, seated, determined not to leave without seeing the dish empty, found herself cornered, unable to do what she always did, return it full, send her away; and she felt an enormous distress, that of being forced to receive, to eat in front of another, to accept; and she almost refused all the same, almost said go away, I will not eat; but the neighbour stayed, stubborn, and Socorro, with no way out, ate. She ate a spoonful, and another, and another, of the food the neighbour had made her with love, in front of the neighbour who watched her with tenderness; and it was strange to her, and hard, and almost painful, to eat another person's care, to let herself be cared for, to be on the side of those who receive; but she ate, and on eating, she felt, beneath the distress, a thing she did not expect, that disarmed her: she felt cared for. She felt the warmth of being sheltered, of not being alone in the illness, of having there a person who had made her food and who stayed to watch her eat, and it was the first time in many years that Socorro felt what it is to receive, and it was so warm, and so unknown, that tears came to her eyes, there, eating the dish of the stubborn neighbour.

And when she finished, and the dish was empty, the neighbour took it, empty, and smiled, and said: there. Done. Do you see how it was not so hard? And it cost you nothing, and it gave me an enormous pleasure. That is how it is done, Socorro. You gave your whole life. Let them now give to you. It is not weakness. It is your turn. And she went away with the empty dish, glad, like one who carries off a trophy; and Socorro stayed, with the empty dish carried off, with the strange warmth of the received care, understanding a thing that turned her life inside out: that to receive, which she had feared her whole life as a defeat, as an exposure, as a risk, was after all, beneath the fear, a sweetness, the sweetness of being cared for, of not being alone, of letting those who love us shelter us; and that she had spent years refusing that sweetness out of fear of the empty door, and that the stubborn neighbour, by not giving up, by staying, by forcing her to eat, had just shown her that not all dishes come back empty from others' doors, that there are dishes that come full and that stay, and that she could, at last, stop returning them, and eat them, and be, after a life of giving, sheltered too.
