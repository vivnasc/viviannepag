## 10. To eat and return empty

And Socorro entered a new time, that of receiving not only in the emergencies of illness, but as a way of living, and it was the sweetest crossing of the book, that of discovering that to eat another's dish and return it empty, far from being a defeat, is one of the most beautiful declarations of trust there is.

She went on healing, and began to let in. She let the daughter care for her, come, stay, see to things, and the relationship of the two, which had been a smooth wall, went on becoming an open door, with the mother receiving and the daughter giving, and the intimacy they had never had, that of shared vulnerability, was born, slowly, because the daughter could, at last, see the mother in want, care for her, know her weak and not only strong, and a mother who lets herself be seen weak by the daughter gives the daughter the greatest gift, that of letting her in where no one else enters. And Socorro, by letting the daughter in, recovered a daughter the wall had pushed away from her, and understood what she had lost in the years of wall, and what she gained now by lowering it, which was the daughter in truth, in intimacy, and not only the daughter at the door.

And she let in the neighbours, the village, and her house, which had been that of the help who gives and does not receive, became the house of one who gives and receives, with dishes arriving full and leaving empty, the Véspera conversation of care given and care accepted, and Socorro discovered the pleasure, which she had never had, of being on the side of those who receive, of being sheltered, of not being alone in the want; and she understood that the village, which she had sheltered her whole life, was full of people who wanted to shelter her, and that it had been enough to lower the wall to discover, on the other side, not the empty door she feared, but dozens of hands held out that had waited, for years, for her to let them, at last, reach her.

And she understood a deep thing about returning the dish empty, which was the central gesture of the book. In Véspera, to return a dish full is to say I do not trust, I do not accept, I do not let you in; and to return it empty is to say I trust, I received, I let you in. And Socorro, who had returned the dishes full her whole life, on beginning to return them empty, was telling the village, dish by dish, I trust you, I let you in; and each empty dish she returned was a rebuilt trust, a brick taken from the wall, a door of childhood, or of the year of misfortune, at last opened; and she understood that to return empty was not a weakness, it was the greatest courage, because it was to trust again after having been left, it was to risk the empty door again, it was to tell a person I know you can leave me as I was once left, and even so I let you in, because I prefer to risk the trust to the certainty of solitude.

And there was a day that sealed the cure, and it was when Socorro did the whole gesture for the first time by full choice, without being forced, without an illness to compel her. A neighbour put a dish out for her, on a day on which she was already well, on which she did not need it, and Socorro, instead of returning it full, as the woman of before would do, accepted it, ate it, not for needing it, but for trusting, for wanting to tell that neighbour I receive you; and she returned it empty, washed, with a note that said not thank you, it was not needed, as before, but it was delicious, thank you for thinking of me. And on writing that note, on returning the dish empty by choice and not by need, Socorro knew she was healed, because she no longer received only when she had no remedy, she received out of pleasure, out of trust, out of love, which is the only way of receiving that heals, that of one who receives not out of weakness, but out of having learned that to let in those who love us is one of the warmest things of life.

And she looked at her house, now open, with the daughter coming and going, the neighbours, the dishes circulating full and empty, and she looked at it without the wall that had shut her and without the wound governing her; and she felt, for the first time in twenty years, the thing the one who only gave lacked: she felt herself part. Not the upper part, that which shelters and is alone; the inner part, that which gives and receives, that which is connected below, in intimacy, to the people who love her; and she understood that the whole life giving and never receiving had made her strong and alone, a creditor full of credits and empty of bonds, and that it had been enough to learn to receive, to accept a dish and return it empty, to make her, at last, not weaker, as she had feared, but more connected, more accompanied, more human; and that the greatest help of her life had been none of those she had given, it had been what she had learned, in the end, to receive, because it was that, the received help, that saved her from the solitude to which her giving, without the receiving, had condemned her.
