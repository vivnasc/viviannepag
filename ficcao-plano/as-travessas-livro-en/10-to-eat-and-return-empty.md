## 10. To eat and return empty

The relapse past, Socorro entered a new time, that of receiving not only in the urgencies of illness, but as a way of living, and it was the sweetest crossing of the book, that of discovering, slowly, that to eat another's dish and return it empty, far from being a defeat, is one of the most beautiful declarations of trust there is.

She was cured of the fever, and there remained to her the other cure, the one beneath, that of the wall, which was slower. She let Glória in for good. The daughter now came without knocking, and stayed, and saw to things, and Socorro let her; and the relationship of the two, which had been a smooth wall on which the daughter hurt herself, went on becoming a door without a latch, with the mother receiving and the daughter giving, and there being born, slowly, between the two, the intimacy they had never had in forty years, that of shared vulnerability. There was an afternoon on which Socorro, still weak in the legs, had to ask the daughter to help her bathe, a thing that would have killed her with shame a month before; and Glória helped her, making nothing of it, talking of this and that as one who is not washing her mother, and Socorro let herself be washed, and understood, with the warm water and the daughter's hands, that a mother who lets herself be seen weak by the daughter gives the daughter the greatest gift there is, that of letting her in where no one else comes in. And in letting the daughter in, she recovered a daughter the wall had pushed away from her, and understood, with grief and with joy at the same time, all she had lost in the forty years of wall, and all she now gained in lowering it: the daughter in truth, in the intimacy, and not only the daughter at the door with the care in her hand.

And there was a night that paid, with twenty-five years' delay, the lap she had not given the five-year-old girl with the fever. Socorro had a small relapse, a fever that came back, and Glória stayed to sleep there, and in the middle of the night Socorro woke trembling, with chills, and called the daughter, low, with a voice that was not that of anyone's help, it was that of one who needs. And Glória came, and instead of staying at the bedside, as the mother had always done for her, lay down beside her, over the blankets, and put her arm over her, and drew her to herself, and said only, now just stay still, Mother, for I've got you. And Socorro, who her whole life had held everyone and never let anyone hold her, let herself be held. She leaned her head on the daughter's shoulder, in the dark, and felt the warmth of her arm over her, and it was strange to her, and it was enormous to her, because it was the first time in fifty-five years that she fell asleep leaning against someone, sheltered by another body, and it was not to watch over anyone's breathing, it was to let her own be watched over. And she fell asleep so, and in the morning understood that the fever had passed, and never knew whether it had passed from the illness or from the lap, and understood that perhaps it was the same thing, that perhaps half the fevers of life are cured with an arm over one in the dark, and that she had spent her life giving broths to those who only needed a lap, for never having learned, herself, that the lap is given by lying down beside, and not by watching on one's feet.

And she let the grandchildren give her, at last, what she had always turned aside from them. The youngest, the one of four years, appeared one day with a bunch of wildflowers from the path clutched in her hand, poppies and daisies already wilting from the heat of the child's hand, and held it out to her with that face of one who already expects the usual turning-aside; and Socorro, instead of thanking and slipping aside, knelt down, with difficulty, with her knees hurting, came to the grandchild's height, and received the flowers with both hands, and said they're lovely, my precious, they're the loveliest flowers anyone ever gave me, I'll put them in a glass of water on the table to look at them while I eat. And the little girl, who was expecting the turning-aside, on seeing the grandmother kneeling to receive her wilted flowers as though they were of a feast, opened a smile that filled her whole face, and ran to tell her mother that grandmother had liked the flowers, that she was going to put them in a glass; and Glória, from the kitchen, heard, and had to lean against the stone of the sink, because she understood, in that moment, that the wall had truly fallen, that it had come down as far as this, as far as the four-year-old grandchild, and that the curse that passed from generation to generation broke there, in a grandmother kneeling to receive a bunch of wilting poppies. And Socorro put the flowers in the glass, on the table, and left them there to wilt slowly, and looked at them many times that day, and they were the loveliest flowers she had in the house in fifty-five years, for being the first she let anyone give her.

And she let the neighbours in, and the village, and her house, which had been the house of the help that gives and does not receive, went on becoming the house of one who gives and receives. The stone bench at the door, which for weeks had dawned with full dishes to be returned full, became a bench where people sat. Lucinda came at the end of the afternoon and stayed to do lacework. Aurélia brought her sewing. Benilde appeared with the marmalade and with the village's latest. And Socorro, who her whole life had only received people in trouble, in haste, to come to the rescue, discovered the pleasure, which she had never had, of receiving people with no trouble at all, only to be, only to talk, only to let the afternoon fall with company; and she discovered, on the side of the one who receives, a warmth she had never known on the side of the one who only gives, because the giving had always kept her on her feet, at the door, on her way out, on the way to the next trouble, and the receiving sat her, at last, in her own kitchen, on her own bench, in the midst of her own people, still, present, at home.

And she learned to receive also what did not come in a dish, which was harder still, because a dish at least has clay to take hold of. There was a morning on which Benilde saw her come down the path with a bundle of firewood on her back, still weak, and instead of making a fuss, as before Socorro would have made her make, she simply went to meet her and took half the bundle from her shoulders without asking leave, and walked her home alongside, talking of something else, as though she were doing nothing at all; and Socorro, who felt the old temptation to say leave it, I'll carry it, I can bear it, kept quiet, and let Benilde carry her half, and it was the first time in her life that she shared a weight with someone instead of carrying it all or handing it all over. And she noticed, on reaching home with the wood, that the bundle had not weighed less for being half; it had weighed less for being shared, which is another thing, and that she did not know, because whoever carries everything alone never learns that a weight in halves weighs less than half.

And there were the afternoons of the stone bench, which were the gentle school of her cure. Lucinda came with the lacework, Aurélia with the sewing, Benilde with whatever there was, and the three sat on the bench at Socorro's door, and Socorro sat with them, and they stayed there watching the afternoon fall over the mountain, talking of what one talks of, of the dead and the living, of the rains coming, of the weddings being announced. And one late afternoon Aurélia, without lifting her eyes from the sewing, said a thing that stayed:

"Do you know, Socorro, what changed in you? It's not that you eat from our dishes. It's that you're here, sitting. Twenty years I never saw you sitting. I always saw you on your feet, on your way out, off to come to someone's rescue. You were a woman on her feet. And now you're sitting with us, and you stay, and you're in no hurry to go anywhere. That's it. You learned to stay."

And Socorro understood that Aurélia was right, that her whole life she had been a woman on her feet, at the door, on her way out, and that receiving had, at last, sat her down; and that to be sitting on her own bench, at the end of the afternoon, with three women who loved her, with no trouble at all pulling her outside, was a thing she had never given herself, and that it was, she discovered, one of the good things of life.

And she understood a deep thing about returning the dish empty, which was the central gesture of her new life. In Véspera, to return the dish full is to say I don't trust, I don't accept, I don't let you in; and to return it empty is to say I trust, I received, I let you in. And Socorro, who had returned the dishes full her whole life, in beginning to return them empty, was saying to the village, dish by dish, day by day, I trust you, I let you in; and each empty dish she returned was a trust rebuilt, a brick of the wall taken out by her own hand, a door of the mountain, or of the empty sill of the year of misfortune, at last opened. And she understood that returning it empty was no weakness at all, it was the greatest courage, because it was to trust again after having been left, it was to risk the empty door again, it was to say to each person, without words, with the washed clay, I know you can leave me as once someone left me, and even so I let you in, because I prefer to risk the trust to the certainty of solitude.

And the village noticed the change, and the change ran from mouth to mouth as the dishes returned full had run before. It was said now, at the washing tank, that Socorro was another, that she let herself be helped, that she sat on the bench in the afternoon with the neighbours, that she received people at home; and there were those who said, without malice, it was the illness that softened her, and there were those who said, nearer the truth, it wasn't the illness, it was Lucinda, who sat in her kitchen and didn't leave. And Custódia, the old woman Socorro had found on the kitchen floor at the start of it all, and who had recovered, sent her word, by a grandchild, that if one day she, Custódia, could give back the floor of that night, she should send for her; and Socorro, instead of answering the usual thing, it wasn't needed, answered, for once, I'll send, Custódia, I promise I'll send; and the old woman, on receiving the message, wept, because she knew the size of that I'll send, coming from one who had never sent for anyone for anything.

And there was a day that sealed the cure, and it was when Socorro made the whole gesture for the first time by full choice, with no illness to force her, no stubborn woman to corner her. She was well now, for weeks. She walked about the village, went down the path, came to the rescue again of whoever needed, had gone back to being the help of others; and on one of those afternoons Aurélia put a dish on her stone bench, for no reason at all, only because, only because she had made a duck rice too much and remembered her. And Socorro, who no longer needed it, who was on her feet and strong and able to cook her own, stood a moment with the dish in her hands, at the door, and understood that she was facing a choice the woman of before would not even have seen: she could return it full, with a thank you, it wasn't needed, I'm well now, and it would have been true, and no one would reproach her; or she could accept it, eat it, not out of need, but out of trust, out of wanting to say to Aurélia I receive you, I'm glad you thought of me.

And she chose the second. She ate Aurélia's duck rice, without needing it, only for the pleasure of it, at her kitchen table, slowly, savouring it; and when she finished, she washed the dish, dried it, and put inside it, instead of the rice, a little note. And the note did not say, as those of before, thank you, it wasn't needed. It said another thing, and Socorro wrote it with a firm hand, without trembling: it was a delight, Aurélia, thank you for thinking of me. And in writing that note, in returning the dish empty by choice and not by need, Socorro knew she was cured, because she no longer received only when she had no remedy, she received out of pleasure, out of trust, out of love, which is the only way of receiving that truly heals, that of one who receives not from weakness, but from having learned that to let in whoever loves us is one of the warmest things of life.

And there was an afternoon on which Socorro understood, with a new clarity, the size of what she had lost and of what she gained, and it was on account of a small thing. Lucinda, Aurélia and Benilde were on the stone bench, and Socorro went to the kitchen to fetch a dish of fritters she had made for them, because now she gave and received, she gave them fritters and received their company; and on setting the dish in the middle of the three, and seeing them take the fritters and praise them and eat them there, in front of her, in the warm afternoon, she understood that it was the first time in her life that she gave a thing to eat to someone and stayed to watch them eat it. Her whole life she had given food and fled. She had carried broths to the sick and left before seeing them drunk. She had made food for houses of mourning and eaten her dry bread alone, on her feet, at midnight. And now she was sitting, on her bench, watching three women eat her fritters and talk to her of their lives, and she understood that to give and stay to watch them eat was another thing, completely another, than to give and flee; that true giving has a receiving inside it, that of staying to watch the other receive, and that she, her whole life, had not even allowed herself that, not even the pleasure of seeing others enjoy what she gave them, because to see is also to receive, and she fled even from that. And she stayed to watch them eat the fritters to the last, and it was as sweet to her as receiving, because it was, at bottom, the same thing from the other side.

And she looked at her house, now open, with the daughter coming and going, the neighbours on the stone bench, the dishes circulating full in one direction and empty in the other, the way the blood circulates; and she looked at it without the wall that had shut it, with the lightness of one who no longer has to bear everything alone nor to return all the love that comes to her door. And she felt, for the first time in twenty years, the thing the one who only gave had lacked: she felt sheltered, received, cared for, on the lower side of the exchange, where she had never been; and she discovered that that side, which she had feared her whole life as the side of weakness, was after all the side of warmth, of intimacy, of belonging, and that the whole life of giving and never receiving had made her strong and alone, and that it had been enough to learn to receive, to accept a dish and return it empty, to make her, at last, not weaker, as she had feared, but more bound, more accompanied, more human, and never again alone.
