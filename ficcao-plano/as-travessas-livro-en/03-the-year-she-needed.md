## 3. The year she needed

There was a year, twenty years before, in which Socorro's life crumbled all at once, as sometimes it crumbles, without a warning that came in time, several misfortunes falling one on top of another like the tiles of an old roof; and it was in that year that she learned not to receive, and it was a single person, with a name and a face, who taught it to her.

The person was called Doroteia, and she was her sister. The elder. The one who, when they were small, tied her shoes and braided her hair on Sundays, the one who had married first and left their parents' house for the big town, on the other side of the mountain, and to whom Socorro, in twenty years of sisterhood, had never asked for anything, because Socorro did not ask, but to whom she had given much, in silence, in her own way. When Doroteia had lost her first child, still in the womb, it was Socorro who had crossed the mountain on foot, in January, to be at her side three weeks, washing her clothes and making her food and sleeping at the foot of her bed so she would not wake alone in the dark. When Doroteia's husband had got himself into bad ways and left her in debt, it was Socorro who had sold the inheritance of a piece of land she had from their parents and carried the money to her sister in a knotted handkerchief, without counting it, without telling anyone, and who, to Doroteia's question, but how am I to repay you for this, had answered the usual thing, there's nothing to repay between us, we're sisters.

So when the year of misfortune came to her, it was to Doroteia that Socorro turned. It was the only time in her life that she asked.

The misfortune came in three. First her man fell ill, Justino, with a thing in the chest that the doctors could not stop, and Socorro watched over him eight months, day and night, and spent on him all the savings, those in the bank and those in the tin, and buried him in an autumn in which she no longer had the strength to weep in front of anyone. Then, no sooner had the mourning begun, came the water: the river Fundo rose higher than the memory of Véspera held, and carried off half the lower house, the one with the yard, where she kept the animals and the whole winter's firewood, and Socorro was left with the roof open to the sky and the cold coming in and nothing with which to cover it. And finally, as though misfortunes call one another, came her own illness, a weakness like this one of now but worse, because it was the first, because she still had the mourning fresh and the house in ruins and no one, for the first time in her life, whom she could go to the rescue of to forget herself.

At Justino's wake, what was to come could already be seen. The man had died at the end of the afternoon, after eight months of wasting away, and Socorro shrouded him herself, alone, without letting other hands touch him, because he was her man and she would compose him; and she stood at the head of the bed the whole night of the wake, receiving the village that came to give condolences, stiff, dry, thanking each one with a firm handshake, without a tear in front of anyone. And the women of the village, who knew what is done for a widow, brought her food, brought her dishes, set before her broths and bread, eat, Socorro, you have to keep yourself up to bear this; and Socorro refused everything, on her feet, at the dead man's head, I'm not hungry, thank you, see to those who are hungry. Lucinda's mother, who was living then, even took her by the arm and said to her, woman, a widow who doesn't eat gives one corpse after another, sit down and eat; and Socorro sat, for manners' sake, and tasted a spoonful, and got up again. She could not sit and receive while there was a dead man to watch over and a village to serve. And the village saw in that the strength of a woman who not even in her own widowhood stopped caring for others, and admired her; and no one saw what it truly was, which was a woman so terrified of being on the side of those who receive that she would rather watch over her own husband on her feet, fasting, than let herself sit and be sheltered like any widow. Justino was buried in a coffin composed by her hands, and Socorro came back from the grave alone, refused company, refused the arm Aurélia offered her for the path, and went to the house that within a month the river would carry off, to shut herself in with the mourning and with the hunger she had not given anyone the pleasure of stilling.

And Socorro, who had never asked, did the unthinkable: she took the blue clay dish that had been her grandmother's, the loveliest she had, the one used only at feasts, filled it with the little that was left her, crossed the mountain, and set it at Doroteia's door, in the big town. It was not food she was carrying. The food was the gesture, it was Véspera's way of saying what the mouth does not know how to: I am in want, sister, for the first time in my life it is I who am in want, and I come to set my dish at your door, and I wait for you to return it to me empty, which is how we say I trust you, count on me, I'll come. She knocked, and Doroteia came to the door, and Socorro said to her, with the voice she had never trained for it:

"Doroteia, I need. For the first time it's I who need. The house is open to the cold and I'm without strength and without anything, and I have no one to ask but you. Will you come? Will you stay with me this winter, help me cover the house, help me get up again? I did it for you, sister, when your little one was lost. Now it was my turn."

And Doroteia said yes. She said yes with her eyes full of water and her hands in hers, she said of course I'll come, sister, how could I not, let me just put a few things in order here and I'll be right behind you, wait for me, I'll come. And Socorro went back to Véspera with her heart lighter than it had been in a year, because she had done the hardest thing of her life, which was to ask, and she had done it of the right person, of the sister, of the one of always, and the sister had said yes.

And Socorro waited.

She waited the first day, and Doroteia must be putting her things in order. She waited the second, and the mountain is far, she comes on foot, give her time. She waited the first week, and put the blue clay dish, empty, on the sill of the window that looked out on the path, to have somewhere to rest her eyes while she waited, and every morning she went down the path to the bend from which the mountain could be seen, and every morning she came back with the bend empty. She waited the whole cold of a winter of a broken roof, with a blanket nailed with tacks over the hole in the ceiling standing in for a wall, and every night the blanket beat in the wind she woke thinking it's her, she's come, and it was the wind. She waited a month. She waited two.

And the waiting had its seasons, and Socorro remembered them all. There was the season of certainty, the first, in which she put the house in order for the sister to arrive, swept what there was to sweep, put clean sheets on the bed where Doroteia would sleep, and left the fire burning late so the sister would not arrive to a cold house. There was the season of doubt, in the middle, in which she began to go down every morning to the bend from which the mountain could be seen, and to stand there a while looking at the empty path, and to invent reasons, the mountain has snow on it, she won't cross with snow, it's natural; and to go back home saying within tomorrow, tomorrow she comes. And there was the worst season, that of the end, in which she no longer went down to the bend because she could no longer bear the empty bend, and in which she stopped putting clean sheets on the sister's bed and took them off, and in which she put out the fire earlier, and in which the blue clay dish, which she had put on the sill of the empty window waiting to fill it when the sister arrived, stayed there gathering dust, day after day, empty, turned toward a path that brought no one. And Socorro looked at that empty dish on the sill, every night, and understood, slowly, that the empty dish was not only the dish: it was her. It was the woman who had set herself at the window waiting to be filled by someone and who went on growing empty, day after day, turned toward a deserted path.

Doroteia did not come.

She did not come, nor send word, nor write, nor apologise. And when Socorro, at the end of two months, crossed the mountain again, no longer asking anything, only to know whether the sister was alive, she found her well, with the house warm and her life in order, and Doroteia, on seeing her at the door, lowered her eyes and said the sentence Socorro would carry for twenty years, the poorest sentence a mouth can say to one who has waited:

"Ah, sister, I was going to, I swear I was going to, but then so much came up, and the time kept passing, and I thought that you, poor thing, you who are so strong, you'd manage on your own. You always manage."

You always manage. It was there, on that step, with the cold mountain at her back and the sister telling her to her face that she had left her for thinking her too strong to need, that Socorro took the blue clay dish, which she carried in her hand, still with the foolish hope of leaving it there, and put it back in her lap. And she crossed the mountain again, alone, with the full dish she had never managed to set at the door, and decided one thing along the way, without words, with the whole wound aching beneath her ribs like a broken bone: never again.

Never again would she put her dish at anyone's door. Never again would she ask. Never again would she count on a single soul, never again would she expose herself to that, to the door that does not open, to the empty sill, to the you always manage. And since counting led to counting on, she decided also the reverse, which was the part the village would come to praise as strength: never again would she let anyone count on her from the wrong side, that is, never again would she let them give to her, so that never again could they take from her. She turned herself entirely toward giving. She made herself the help of others with a devotion that had beneath it a calculation of fear: giving did not expose her, giving kept her on the upper side of the exchange, the safe side, the side of one who never waits on the sill; receiving, that was dangerous, receiving was to put the dish at the door and be left at the mercy of some Doroteia who found her too strong to come.

And there was, in that winter of the broken roof, a neighbour who tried. It was Lucinda's mother, who was still living then, an old woman with crooked hands who put at Socorro, on a frosty night, a dish of beans at the door of the ruined house, with a blanket folded beneath it, a good one, of wool, for the hole in the ceiling. And Socorro, who was by that time already turned wholly inward, with Doroteia still at the bend of the path not arriving, refused. She returned the beans full and the blanket folded, with a message, it wasn't needed, keep that for your own cold, you're old and I can bear it. And the old woman, who was of the old school and who understood these things better than anyone, shook her head and said to her daughter, who was the young Lucinda, still unmarried: that woman is waiting for a dish that doesn't come, and while she waits for it, she eats from none of those that do. There are people like that, child. They put their whole life on one door, and when the door won't open, they shut all the others so as not to have to see it shut too. Remember this, for one day it'll be you who has to break through her stubbornness. And Lucinda kept her mother's saying for twenty years, without knowing what for, until the rainy afternoon on which she sat in Socorro's kitchen and did not leave until she had seen the bean soup finished.

And so it was that the one who gave to all became the one who received from no one. She put the blue clay dish at the bottom of the chest, beneath her grandmother's clothes, and never again used it at feasts, because the dish that had been lovely had become, forever, the face of the only time she had asked; and the village forgot that Socorro had once had a feast dish, and Socorro herself almost forgot, and would have forgotten entirely had it not been for this illness of now, at fifty-five, with the stone bench full of others' dishes, compelling her to go down into the chest, within herself, to the one place where the thing still hurt, and to take from there, with her grandmother's clothes, the memory of the door that did not open.

And there was, in the year of misfortune, a person who did not leave her, and whom Socorro had refused too, at the time, and whom she only much later understood: the daughter. Glória was newly married that winter, but she came, she crossed the three houses every day, she wanted to stay with her mother in the ruined house, she wanted to help her cover the roof, she wanted to shelter her in the mourning and the illness. And Socorro refused her, as she refused everything, go to your man, child, this is no place for a newlywed, I manage on my own; and she sent the daughter away, and stayed waiting for the sister, for Doroteia, for the help that was to come from afar, from the mountain, instead of accepting what she had at the door, near, every day, in the daughter who wanted to stay. And that was the cruellest irony of that year, which Socorro would only see twenty years later, ill again: that while she waited on the sill for a dish that never came from the big town, she refused every day the dish the daughter brought her from the three houses above; that she had put all her hope on the far door and shut the near one; and that, had she let Glória in that winter, perhaps she would never have raised the wall, because the daughter would not have let her, the daughter was there, the daughter was the dish that stays, and she sent her away to wait for the one that did not come.

Because it is always to the wound that one must return to heal it, and Socorro, on looking now at her fear of receiving, understood, for the first time, that it was not strength nor pride, it was scar; it was Doroteia, still; it was a door of the big town, shut for twenty years, which she had been setting, without noticing it, in front of all the doors that opened to her; and that she was making Aurélia, and Lucinda, and Benilde, and her own daughter, who put her dishes full of love without a bill, pay for the lack of a single person who had left her waiting in the cold. And she understood that if she wanted, at last, to stop being alone beneath the giving, she would have to do the hardest thing she could imagine, harder than watching over the dead and bringing births her whole life: she would have to accept a dish, and eat it to the end, and risk putting, again, her own at someone's door, after a whole life of putting none so as never again to see, at the break of day, an empty sill.
