## 3. The year she needed

Socorro learned not to receive in one year, and it was a year of misfortune, the year she most needed in her life, and in which she counted on someone, and in which that someone left her wanting; and it was that want, and not another thing, the root of the returned dishes, because there are wants that teach a person not to count on anyone again.

She was young, or middle-aged, in a time when life collapsed all at once, as it sometimes collapses, several misfortunes together, an illness, a death, a lack of money, a great trouble, of the kind a person does not bear alone; and Socorro, who had always been the one who came to others' rescue, found herself, for the first and only time in her life, on the side of those who need, in want, not bearing up, needing someone to come to her rescue. And there was someone, in her life, from whom she expected that help, someone close, from whom she had the right to expect, someone she herself had come to a thousand times, and to whom, in that year of misfortune, she did what she never did, she asked: she counted on that person, she opened herself, she showed the want, she put her dish at that person's door, and waited for it to come back full, for the help to come, for the shelter she deserved and needed.

And it did not come. The person from whom Socorro expected the help, in the year she most needed, failed her. Did not come, or came badly, or turned her back, or disappeared at the hour it was needed, or gave her, instead of the shelter, a disappointment, a betrayal, an abandonment; and Socorro, who had exposed herself, who had asked, who had counted, was left looking at the empty door, was left waiting for a help that did not come, was left in want and alone precisely at the moment she had given herself, had lowered her guard, had trusted; and it was that, the want of one who should have come, the greatest pain of her life, greater than all the misfortunes of that year together, because the misfortunes were of life, and that one was of a person, of a betrayed trust, of a dish put at the door with the heart and returned empty, or not even returned.

And Socorro, in that year, alone, in want, abandoned by the one who should have come, decided a thing, without words, with her whole wound, as one decides the things that last a life: she decided never again. Never again would she count on anyone. Never again would she put her dish at someone's door waiting for it to come back full. Never again would she expose herself to that, to the want, to the empty door, to the disappointment of having counted and having been left; and from that year Socorro shut herself to receiving, turned herself whole to giving, made herself the help of others and never again let anyone be her help, because giving was safe, giving did not expose her, giving kept her on the strong side of the exchange; and receiving was dangerous, receiving was to expose oneself, to count, to risk the empty door, and Socorro, after that year, preferred never to receive than to risk receiving and being, again, left wanting.

And it was thus that the one who gave to all became the one who received from no one, and that her name, Socorro, was fulfilled the wrong way round: she was everyone's help, but no one was her help, because she did not let them, because once she had let them and been left waiting, and the waiting for a help that does not come is a pain that teaches a person not to wait for any again; and Socorro, by returning now the dishes full at the door, by refusing the village's care, was not only being proud, she was protecting herself, she was doing what she had learned in the year of the empty door, not to accept so as not to get used to it, not to count so as not to be left waiting, to return the love before getting attached to it, because to get attached to the love one receives is to risk the pain of seeing it, one day, stop coming, and Socorro had already felt that pain once, and had organised her whole life so as not to feel it again.

And when, at fifty-five, ill, with the dishes at the door, Socorro was forced to look at her refusal, it was to that year that she had to return, because it is always to the wound that one has to return to heal it; and on remembering the dish she had put at someone's door and that had come back empty, or had not come back, she understood, for the first time, that her incapacity to receive was not strength nor pride, it was a scar, it was the fear of an old want, and that she was making the whole village, which put full dishes at her door with love, pay for the empty door of a single person from many years ago; and that if she wanted, at last, to stop being alone beneath the giving, she would have to do the hardest thing she could imagine, harder than coming to everyone's rescue her whole life: she would have to accept a dish, and eat it, and risk counting again, after a life of not counting on anyone so as never again to see an empty door.
