## 2. The dishes at the door

The first dish stood on the stone bench a whole afternoon, cooling, before Socorro gathered the courage to get up and go and look at it.

She knew what it was without seeing it. She knew the custom of Véspera by heart, because she had kept it a thousand times from the other side: in Véspera, when someone falls ill, the village puts food at their door, and the food is not only food, it is a message. The dish left on the bench of a sick person is an embrace in the shape of clay, a hand held out without a face, a we know, you are not alone, count on us. And the sick person, on receiving the dish, eats what comes in it, and then returns the dish washed and empty, and the empty dish is the answer, it is the sick person saying I received, I accepted, thank you, I let you care for me. So, with dishes that go full and come back empty, the village and the one who falls hold a conversation without words that lasts the time of the illness, and it is the oldest and surest way Véspera has of sheltering its own.

Socorro dragged herself to the door, barefoot, with the blanket on her shoulders, and opened it, and there it was: a dish of white crockery with a blue line around it, which she knew, it was Aurélia's, and beneath the linen cloth a broth with its smell of old hen and mint, still warm from the heat that had been kept under the linen. It carried a little note pinned to the cloth with a safety pin. For Socorro, who so many times brought to me. Eat while it's hot. Aurélia.

And then the thing happened that said everything about her, and that she herself could not name that afternoon, she only felt it rise from her chest like a tide that will not be ordered: distress. Not gratitude, not relief, not the warmth she should have felt. Distress. The distress of having there, at the door, a proof that everyone knew. The distress of imagining Aurélia in her kitchen killing the hen and thinking poor Socorro. The distress of being on the side where one is an object of pity, of care, of warm broth, she who was subject, who was verb, who was the one who does and never the one who lets herself be done for.

And the distress was stronger than the fever. Socorro took the dish, carried it inside, and did, with the little strength she had, the gesture that was the reverse of her whole life and the exact sum of it: she tipped Aurélia's broth into a bowl of her own, washed the dish of white crockery with the blue line until not a smell remained, and filled it again. She filled it with a carrot rice she had from the day before, cold, of her own food, and covered it with Aurélia's cloth, and pinned to it a little note with the safety pin. It took her half an hour to write the three lines, with her hand trembling from the fever and from the other thing that was not the fever. Many thanks, Aurélia, but it wasn't needed, I'm already much better, keep that for yourself, you're the one who's run down. God repay you the care. Socorro.

And when she had the strength, the next day, it was she herself, pale, clinging to the walls, who went to set the full dish on Aurélia's stone bench, and returned home before Aurélia could see her, because returning the dish was one thing, and having to bear the face of the one who receives it full was another.

Because to return the dish full, in Véspera, says one thing only, and says it loud, and Socorro knew what she was saying, though she would never admit it aloud. To return it full is to refuse. It is to say I don't need you, I don't accept, take back your embrace, I don't want it. And Aurélia, on finding on her bench the dish she had given full come back full, with another woman's cold rice pretending to be an answer, was left with the linen in her hand and her eyes burning, because she had put love into that broth and the love had come back to her door without having been touched, refused with manners, wrapped in thank you.

"The woman returned it to me full," said Aurélia to Lucinda, at the washing place, the astonishment still in her voice. "I made her the broth with the fat hen, the one I was keeping for the saint's day, and the woman tipped it out and put in there a cold rice of her own, like someone paying. Like someone paying me, Lucinda. Me."

And Lucinda, who was the most stubborn of the three and who had a way of pressing her lips together when she was about to decide a thing, pressed her lips together.

"She'll eat from mine," she said, only that. "Even if I have to stay and watch her eat it."

But that was for later. For now, what there was was the dishes arriving, many, because Socorro had come to everyone's rescue and everyone wanted to come to hers, and the stone bench at her door dawned, every morning of that winter, with two, three, four dishes covered in linen, with smells that filled the path, Benilde's beans, the miller's wife's chestnut soup, a rice pudding with cinnamon set down without a note by someone ashamed to sign it. And Socorro, weak, with the fever coming and going, got up to every dish, emptied out its food, washed it, filled it again from what she had at home, pinned the little notes, it wasn't needed, I manage on my own, I'm well, God repay you, and returned them at the break of day, before the village woke, like someone hiding a vice.

One of those dawns, Benilde, who went early to the spring, caught her in the act. She saw her come out of the house weak, clinging to the wall, with a full dish in her hands, on her way to someone's stone bench to return it before the village woke; and she stopped on the path, with the water jar on her head, and stood looking at her, and Socorro, on seeing Benilde, blushed like a child caught doing what she shouldn't, and tried to hide the dish behind her back, which made no sense at all and which said everything.

"Socorro," said Benilde, very low, without reproach, only with pity. "You're doing this in secret, before dawn, so we won't see you? You, sick, getting up in the cold to return us our food before we wake, so we won't have to see that you refuse it?" She set the jar down on the wall. "Woman, that's worse still. You won't even let us see the refusal, to spare us the grief. You refuse the care and on top of it you hide the refusal, so that no one gets hurt. You spare us even your shut door. And there is no one more alone than the one who won't even let others see her solitude."

And Socorro did not know what to answer, and went off down the path with the dish behind her back, and Benilde stayed by the jar watching her go, understanding that that woman was sicker in soul than in body, and that her soul, unlike her body, had no doctor who came from the big town, only the stubbornness of the neighbours and time.

And so it began, at Socorro's door, a stubbornness of dishes that was, seen from afar, almost comical, the village putting and Socorro returning, a coming and going of clay across the stone bench of a sick house; and that was, seen up close, one of the saddest things Véspera had ever seen: a woman ill, alone, surrounded by love at the door, refusing it dish by dish, returning to the whole village the embrace the whole village held out to her, and calling that manners.

There was a morning on which Rosalina, the miller's daughter, the one from the birth Socorro had brought, appeared with the boy on her hip, grown now, and a dish of flour porridge in the other hand, balancing the two things with the knack of young mothers; and she knocked, and when Socorro opened, Rosalina almost put the boy into her arms before the other could refuse, and said, with the joy of one who had found at last the way to repay a debt that had weighed on her for years:

"Socorro, do you remember telling me, when this one was born, that there was nothing to repay, that you didn't give in order to receive? Well, I never forgave you that." And she laughed, but she was in earnest. "I went all these years with your night stuck in here, with no way to give it back to you, because you wouldn't let me. Now it's you in bed, and I bring you porridge, and you're going to eat it, because it's my turn, and this time it's me who says there's nothing to repay. We're even by not being even."

And Socorro held the boy, who smiled at her knowing nothing of any of it, and for a moment, with the warm weight of the child in her arms, she was almost about to give way; but she set the boy on his mother's lap, and took the dish of porridge, and did the usual thing, with a gentleness that was worse than harshness, carried it inside, and Rosalina was left on the stone bench with her son on her lap watching the door close, and understood, with a new sadness, that the debt she had carried for years was not a debt of hers, it was a door of Socorro's, and that that door did not open with flour porridge however much love one put inside it. She went off down the path, with the boy waving at the shut door, and the dish came back to her full the next day, with the usual little note, and Rosalina kept it without eating from it, because to eat from one's own dish returned full is a thing that tastes of gall.

And there was, beneath everything, more than pride, and that was the secret the book was circling toward saying. Socorro did not return the dishes only out of not wanting to owe, out of not wanting to be in want, out of not wanting to be seen weak. She returned them out of a deeper thing, out of a fear that came from far off and that she hid so well she had almost hidden it from herself: the fear of accepting and being left. The fear of growing used to the warmth of a hot dish at the door, of counting on it, of waiting for it, and then, one day, of its ceasing to come, and of being left waiting, barefoot, on the stone bench, for a dish that does not arrive. Because that had happened to her once. There was a year, in Socorro's life, when she put her dish, the blue clay one that had been her grandmother's, at the door of a person from whom she had the right to expect everything, and when she stood looking at that door day after day waiting to see it open, and when the dish came back full, or did not come back at all, and when Socorro learned, by force, on the stone bench of a door that did not open, that it was better not to put any dish at anyone's door than to put one and be left waiting. But that was the next door of the book. And that one, Socorro had kept shut for twenty years.
