## 2. The dishes at the door

The dishes began to arrive at Socorro's door on the day it was known that she was ill, and it was the village doing what the village does, coming to the rescue of the one who had always come, and it was beautiful, and it was love, and Socorro, instead of receiving it, returned the dishes full, with a note of thanks, and in that gesture, that of returning the dish full, was the whole book.

Because in Véspera, when someone falls ill, the village puts food at the door. It is an old custom, and it is more than food: it is the village's way of saying you are not alone, we are here, count on us; and the dish that is set at a sick person's door is an embrace in the form of food, a hand held out, a we are caring for you. And whoever receives the dish, in Véspera, eats what is in it, and then returns the dish empty, washed, and the empty dish returned is the answer, it is the sick person saying I received, I accepted your care, thank you; and so, with dishes that go full and come back empty, the village and the sick person hold a conversation without words, of care given and care accepted, which is Véspera's way of sheltering whoever falls.

And to Socorro the dishes came, many, because she had come to everyone's rescue and everyone wanted to come to hers; and Socorro, ill in bed, on seeing the dishes at the door, instead of the relief and the gratitude she ought to feel, felt a thing she did not expect and that took her over: she felt distress. Distress at receiving, at being on the side of those who need, at having the village at the door seeing her in want; and the distress was so great that Socorro, the moment she had strength, did the thing that said everything about her: she got up, went to the kitchen, took the food out of the dishes, put something else in them, food of her own, or left them as they were but did not eat of them, and returned the dishes full, untouched, with a note in each, thank you very much, it was not needed, I am well, God repay you.

And to return a dish full, in Véspera, says a thing, and Socorro knew what it said, and said it to the whole village without wanting to admit it: to return a dish full is to refuse the care, it is to say I do not need you, I do not accept, take back your embrace for I do not want it; and the village, on receiving back Socorro's dishes full, with the notes of thanks, understood that Socorro, even ill, even in want, did not accept being cared for, refused the shelter, returned the embrace; and some of the neighbours were hurt, because they had put love in those dishes, and the love had come back to them untouched, refused; and others, the more stubborn ones, did not give up, and put out dishes again, and Socorro returned them full again, and so began, at Socorro's door, a stubbornness of dishes, the village putting and Socorro returning, which was, seen from outside, almost comic, and which was, beneath, one of the saddest things of Véspera: a woman who could not let herself be loved.

And there was, in returning the dish full, more than pride, there was fear, and it was that fear the secret of the book. Socorro did not return the dishes only for not wanting to owe, for not wanting to be in want; she returned them for a deeper thing, for being afraid of accepting and being left, for an old wound that had taught her that to count on someone, to accept someone's care, to give herself to someone's shelter, ended in pain, in the pain of having counted and having been left looking at the empty door; and so Socorro refused the dishes, not for not wanting them, but for being afraid of wanting them, of getting used to them, of counting on them, and then, one day, having them stop coming, and she left waiting for a dish that does not arrive, as she had once been left waiting for a help that did not come.

And it was with the dishes going and coming back full, at her door, that Socorro began, ill, to be forced to look at the thing she had hidden for years: that she, everyone's help, did not know how to be helped; that she returned the love they put at her door; that she was alone, ill, refusing the whole village that wanted to come to her rescue; and that that refusal, which she dressed as not wanting to trouble, as I manage, as it is not needed, had beneath it a wound she had never told anyone, that of the year she had needed, and in which she had put her dish at someone's door, and the dish had come back empty, or had not come back at all, and in which she had learned, by force, never again to accept a dish from anyone, so as never again to be left waiting for one that does not come.
