## 11. To trust again

For Socorro to be cured for good, there still remained to make peace with Doroteia, or at least with the empty door Doroteia had left within her, because as long as that door of the mountain stayed open inside her, every dish accepted would always be a risk, and she would never receive in peace, only in alarm, waiting for the knife beneath the embrace.

And there happened, in those months, a thing that put the wound to the test: Doroteia died, on the other side of the mountain, in the big town, without warning, from one day to the next. They sent word, and Socorro received it standing in the kitchen, and stayed a while with the message in her hand, waiting to feel something great, and what she felt was not what she expected. She did not feel the ugly joy of revenge, for she had never been a woman of that; but neither did she feel the clean grief of one who weeps a sister. She felt, above all, that it was late. That Doroteia was going without ever having asked her pardon, without ever having mended the want, without ever having crossed the mountain the other way to tell her sister, I failed you, forgive me; and that if Socorro had been, all those twenty years, waiting, deep down, for Doroteia to come back and set straight what she had twisted, that waiting ended there, in a grave, without having been fulfilled.

And as she crossed the mountain for the wake, she remembered the only time, in twenty years, that the two had crossed paths. It was at a fair, in the big town, years after the winter of the broken roof; Socorro had gone to sell some cheeses, and Doroteia appeared in front of her, in the middle of the stalls, with a basket on her arm, and the two stopped, face to face, in the midst of the people, not knowing what to say. And Doroteia, who should have said forgive me, sister, I failed you, you opened the door to me and I did not go, said another thing, said how the weather had behaved badly that winter, and how the cheeses were selling, and whether Socorro wanted to come to the house for a coffee; and Socorro, who had waited twenty years for a word that would set the thing straight, on hearing coffee instead of forgiveness, said no, that she had to go back before the mountain darkened, and went off among the stalls with the cheeses unsold, and never saw her alive again. Doroteia, Socorro understood now on the way to the wake, had not asked pardon at the fair because perhaps she had never truly understood what she had done; perhaps she had really thought, all those years, that Socorro, being so strong, had managed on her own and had not needed her after all; and that, Socorro discovered, was the cruellest and the most freeing part at the same time: that the person who had destroyed her trust in life her whole life perhaps did not even know she had destroyed it, perhaps had died thinking herself a good sister, and that to expect a mending from her was to expect a blind woman to see a colour.

And she went to the wake, crossed the mountain she had crossed with the blue clay dish twenty years before, and went alone, and stood by the dead sister, and understood, beside the coffin, the thing that began to free her in earnest. She understood that it was not needed that Doroteia come back, nor that she apologise, nor that she mend anything, because that was never going to happen now, and had never depended on her; what was needed was another thing, it was for Socorro to stop living out of that empty door, to stop putting it in front of all the others, to stop letting a want of twenty years ago, now sealed in a grave, go on governing her receiving of today. And that, she understood, was not done by demanding of the sister a mending death had already made impossible; it was done within her, by deciding that that empty door had been one, one misfortune, and not the truth about love, and that she would not let a dead sister rob her, for the rest of her life, of all the living people who did not fail her.

Because that was what the empty door had robbed her of, and what she only there, beside the coffin, saw entire: not only Doroteia, but all the others. Because of one, she had shut herself off from all. And as long as she kept the wound open, Doroteia went on doing her harm, twenty years later, dead and all, not by the old want, for that had passed, but by the door the want had left within her and that went on, alone, shutting all the others. And Socorro understood, on looking at the dead sister, that if she wanted to free herself of her it was not by keeping the grief, for that was to keep her alive and governing from within a grave; it was by letting her go. It was to decide that the door of the mountain stayed there, in that village, in that grave, in the past, and that she would go back to Véspera and live out of the full doors of the present, of the daughter, of the neighbours, of Lucinda, of Aurélia, of Benilde, and not out of the empty sill of a winter that was now twenty years old and now had a gravestone.

And she put, before coming away, a thing on top of the sister's coffin. She had brought, in her lap, wrapped in a handkerchief, the blue clay dish, her grandmother's, the one she had carried to Doroteia's door and never managed to leave there, the one she had kept twenty years at the bottom of the chest beneath the old clothes. And she set it there, empty, washed, on top of the wood, and no one understood what it was, they thought it a family keepsake, a thing of sisters; but it was another thing, it was Socorro returning, at last, to Doroteia, the empty dish that twenty years before she had not been able to return to her; and to return it empty, in Véspera, is to say I trust, I received, it is settled; and Socorro had no trust at all left to give the dead sister, but she had the gesture, and the gesture was not for Doroteia, it was for her: it was to let go of the dish, and with the dish the waiting, and with the waiting the door, and to go back home, for the first time in twenty years, with empty hands of the weight she had carried there on the way in.

And, coming back from the wake, she stopped at the bend of the mountain from which, twenty years before, she had gone down every morning to look at the empty path waiting for the sister; and she stayed there a moment, and looked at the path, and understood that she no longer waited for anyone from that side, and that this, which for twenty years would have been the greatest of sorrows, was now a rest. To no longer wait for Doroteia was to stop setting her eyes on an empty bend; and Socorro understood, at that bend, that the cure of the waiting had not been the arrival of the awaited, for that had never come nor would come, it had been to stop waiting, it had been to turn her back on the empty bend and go down to the village where there were people who arrived. And she went down, and did not look back again, and it was the first time in twenty years she passed that bend without her heart tightening.

And she went on freeing herself, slowly, not by forgiving in words, for perhaps she would never come to feel the whole forgiveness, but by ceasing to live bound to it; and as she let go of the empty door, the dishes accepted at home ceased to give her alarm and came to give her peace, because she was no longer, in receiving them, waiting for them to turn against her like the old one. She received them trusting, and trust, discovered so late, was a rest she did not even know she had lacked: it was to be able to accept an embrace without expecting the knife, to be able to let in without watching the way out, to be able to love and be loved without the alarm that everything, at any moment, might turn into an empty sill. And she understood that to trust again, after having been left, was the greatest courage of her life, greater than all the help she had given, because it was to open the door knowing it can stay empty, and to do it all the same; that to live shut so as never again to have an empty door was to have, in exchange, all the doors shut forever, which is a permanent empty door, the worst of all, the one the person herself keeps, from within, against herself.

And there was a moment in which Socorro knew she was cured for good, and it was when the village set a mirror before her. There appeared, in the village, a woman, Ermelinda, who had been widowed young and stayed alone with her grief, and who, on falling ill that autumn, began to do what Socorro had done: to return the dishes full. The village put food at her door, and Ermelinda returned it, with notes, it wasn't needed, I manage on my own; and the village, which had seen that once, turned to Socorro as one turns to whoever has already walked the road, and Aurélia said to her, at the washing place:

"Socorro, you go there. You know better than anyone what it is to be on that side of the wall. You go there, for us she won't let in."

And Socorro went. She went to Ermelinda's house with a dish of broth in her hands, Aurélia's broth, which she had borrowed on purpose, to close the whole circle; and when Ermelinda opened the door to her, already getting the it wasn't needed ready, Socorro did what Lucinda had done to her on an afternoon of rain: she came in. She came in without asking, went to the table, set down the dish, uncovered it, pulled out a chair, sat down, and said, with the affectionate stubbornness of one who no longer gives up on anyone because she no longer gave up on herself:

"Ermelinda. I'll tell you straight off how it is, so we don't go round in circles. You're going to eat this broth here, in front of me, now, because I'm not leaving this chair until I see it empty. And I know what you're feeling, I know it better than you think, because I was you. I returned the dishes full to the whole village for twenty years, because of one door that once stayed empty. And do you know what that gave me? Solitude. Twenty years of solitude that I called strength. Don't do what I did, woman. Eat. For it's your turn. And let me in, for I know the road, for I've already been on that side, and the only thing one sees from there is a door one shuts on oneself and calls the world."

And Ermelinda, cornered, as Socorro had been, ate. She ate the first spoonful trembling, and the second, and at the middle of the dish she began to weep, as Socorro had wept in Lucinda's soup, and Socorro said nothing of the tears, only stayed there, with her hands in her lap, watching her eat, being, for another, the presence that had been lacking to herself.

And in the middle of it, Ermelinda set down the spoon and said, with her voice all broken, the thing Socorro had never said but recognised as though it were her own mouth:

"You don't know what it is, Socorro. I put my life in one person's hands, and that person went. And since then I can't. I can't let them put food at my door, because if I grow used to it, and then it stops coming, I die again. Better not to grow used to it. Better to eat my own, alone, for at least my own doesn't leave me."

And Socorro took her hand across the table, a thing she had never done to anyone her whole life, she who gave everything but her hand, and said to her:

"I know what it is, Ermelinda. I know better than you think. Because I said that sentence, word for word, for twenty years, without saying it aloud, only living it. I ate my own alone for twenty years so as not to grow used to others'. And do you know what I got from that? Twenty years of eating alone. That's all I got. I spared myself no pain at all, Ermelinda, I traded one big pain once for a small pain every day, that of eating alone, and at the end of twenty years the small pain of every day was greater than the big one once. Grow used to it, woman. Risk growing used to it. For most dishes, unlike that one, stay. And if one doesn't stay, you eat the pain of that one, which is one, and you keep all the others, which are many. But don't eat your own alone for twenty years out of fear of one. Don't do what I did. Eat my broth, and tomorrow eat Aurélia's, and let them stay."

And Ermelinda ate, to the end, weeping and eating, and Socorro stayed to watch her, being, for another, the presence that had been lacking to herself.

And she came back the next day, and the next, and the week after, because she knew, better than anyone, that one dish does not break through a wall of twenty years, that many are needed and time and not giving up; and she was to Ermelinda what Lucinda had been to her, the stubborn one who does not budge, and she brought others with her, brought Aurélia and Benilde, and taught them to do for Ermelinda what they had done for her, to insist without pushing, to wait on this side of the door for her, from within, to unbar it. And one afternoon, weeks later, Ermelinda returned, on Socorro's stone bench, an empty dish, washed, the first she returned empty to anyone; and Socorro, on finding it, sat on the bench with the empty dish in her lap, the way Aurélia had once sat, and wept, but wept from another thing, wept that the wheel had come full circle, that what had been done to her she had done to another, and the other had received, and the chain of care, which in her had been cut twenty years, came back to circulating through her hands. And she understood that the help she had given Ermelinda had cured, retroactively, the woman in want of twenty years ago, because in giving another the presence she herself had lacked, she gave it also, within, in time, to the Socorro of the broken roof who still waited, deep down, for someone to come; and that Socorro, at last, on seeing Ermelinda receive, stopped waiting, because what she waited for had arrived, twenty years late and by her own hand. And in doing for Ermelinda what the stubborn ones had done for her, in becoming herself the stubborn neighbour who breaks through the wall of one who does not receive, Socorro closed the circle of the book and cured the empty door at the root; because the deepest way to heal a want we suffer is to become, ourselves, the presence that failed us, and give it to another, and be, for someone, the dish that stays. And Socorro, who had waited in vain for help in the year of misfortune, became, at last, the help she herself had waited for in a winter of broken roof, not only for the village, which she had always been, but for herself, because in giving Ermelinda what Socorro had lacked, she gave it also, within, back in time, to the woman in want of twenty years ago who still dwelt in her, and who, in that kitchen, on seeing Ermelinda scrape the dish to the end, received at last, twenty years late, her own.
