## 11. To trust again

What Socorro still lacked, to heal for good, was to make peace with the person who had left her, or at least with the wound that person had opened in her, because while the empty door of the year of misfortune stayed open within her, each accepted dish would always be a risk, and she would never receive in peace, only in alarm.

And she understood, on beginning to heal, that it was not necessary that that person come back, nor that she apologise, nor that she repair the failure, that this might never happen and did not depend on her; what was necessary was another thing, it was for Socorro to stop living from that empty door, to stop putting it in all doors, to stop letting a want of twenty years ago govern her receiving of today. And that, she understood, was not done by demanding repair from the one who had left her, that was to keep her governing; it was done within her, deciding that that empty door had been one, a misfortune, and not the truth about love, and that she was not going to let one person who had failed her rob her, forever, of all the people who did not fail her.

Because that was what the empty door had robbed her of: not only that person, but all the others, because of her Socorro had shut herself to everyone; and while Socorro kept the wound open, the person who had left her went on doing her harm, twenty years later, not by the old failure, but by the door the failure had left within her and that went on shutting all the others. And Socorro understood that if she wanted to free herself from that person, it was not by keeping the grief, for that was to keep her governing; it was by letting her go, by deciding that the empty door was past, was one, and that she was going to live from the full doors of the present, the daughter, the neighbours, the village, and not from the empty door of the past.

And she went on freeing herself, slowly, not perhaps forgiving with her mouth, but ceasing to live bound to that; and as she let go of the empty door, the accepted dishes ceased to give her alarm and began to give her peace, because she was no longer, on receiving them, waiting for them to turn against her like the old one; she received them trusting, and the trust, discovered, was a rest, it was to be able to accept an embrace without expecting the stab, to be able to let in without watching the exit, to be able to love and be loved without the alarm that everything, at any moment, would turn into an empty door. And she understood that to trust again, after having been left, was the greatest courage of her life, because it was to risk again what had hurt her, it was to open the door knowing it can stay empty, and to do it all the same, because to live shut so as never again to have an empty door was to have, in exchange, all doors shut, which is a permanent empty door, the worst of all, the one the person herself keeps.

And there was a moment in which Socorro understood that she was healed, and it was when another person, in the village, in want, shut, returning the dishes as she had done, appeared before her; and Socorro recognised in her herself of before, the one who does not receive, the one who returns full, the one who has an empty door within; and instead of leaving her, she did for her what the stubborn neighbours had done for her: she insisted, did not give up, stayed, put the dish before her and forced her to eat it, and told her what they had told her, receive, it is not weakness, it is your turn, let me in. And on doing for the other what they had done for her, on becoming, she, the stubborn neighbour who pierces the wall of one who does not receive, Socorro closed the circle of the book, and healed her empty door at the root, because the deepest way to heal a want we suffer is to become, ourselves, the presence that failed, to give it to another, to be, for someone, the dish that stays; and Socorro, who had waited in vain for a help in the year of misfortune, became, at last, the help she herself had waited for, not only for the village, which she had always been, but for herself, because by giving another what she had lacked, she gave it also, retroactively, to the woman in want she still was, within her.
