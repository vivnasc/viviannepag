## 1. The help of others

Socorro was the help of the village, and had been her whole life, and had in this her identity and her pride: it was she they came to when someone needed, and she always came, with her hands, with her time, with what she had, without ever asking anything in return, because to give was what she knew, and to receive was what she did not know, and it was that missing half that came to be the book.

Wherever there was a trouble, there was Socorro. A child was born, and Socorro was the first to arrive, to help the mother, to see to the house while the other was in labour; someone died, and Socorro was the one who shrouded, who watched, who cooked for the mourners who had no head for food; a neighbour fell ill, and Socorro was the one who did her turns, who took her broths, who stayed the night; and the whole village knew that in any misfortune it could count on Socorro, that Socorro comes, that Socorro is there, and that was her place, that of coming to the rescue, and it was a good place, a place of good, and Socorro did it with a true love, without calculation, because she genuinely liked to come, to relieve, to be there for others.

But there was a thing no one saw, beneath the giving, and that was its reverse: that Socorro, who gave to all, received from no one, ever, and not for not needing, for she needed like everyone, but for not knowing how, for not letting, for a deep incapacity to be on the side of those who receive. When they offered her a thing, she refused; when they wanted to come to her rescue, she said it is not needed; when they wanted to give her what she gave to all, she shut herself, was flustered, returned it; and the village, used to a Socorro who only gives, thought that her nature, strength, generosity, and did not see that it was also a wound, that of one who can only be on one side of the exchange, that of giving, and to whom the other side, that of receiving, gave a fear no one guessed.

And there was, in this incapacity to receive, a hidden loneliness Socorro did not admit. Because one who only gives and never receives is, deep down, alone, even surrounded by people, because giving connects us to others above, on the surface, but it is receiving that connects us below, in intimacy, in shared vulnerability; and Socorro, who gave herself to all but did not let anyone give themselves to her, was connected to the whole village by the surface of giving and alone below, in the intimacy of receiving, because she did not let anyone enter that intimacy, did not let anyone see her in want, in need, in lack, which was the only way the village could truly shelter her, and which she did not give.

And the years went by, and Socorro grew old giving, and the body, at fifty-five, began to warn her that the giving had a limit, that one day she herself would need someone to come to her rescue, that the help of others would one day need help; and Socorro pushed that thought away, because it was unbearable, the idea of being on the side of those who receive, of being the sheltered and not the shelterer, of having to accept what her whole life she had only given; and she told herself no, that she bore up, that while she could it would be she who came and never she who was come to, because to be come to was a thing that hurt her even to think, for an old reason that lay at the bottom of her, in the year she had needed and the door had stayed empty.

And there was a day, at the start of the book, on which the body forced her to stop. She fell ill, in earnest, of an illness that laid her in bed, that left her without strength, dependent, unable to fend for herself, and she was for the first time in her life put in the place she most feared, that of one who needs, of one who does not bear up, of one who has to be helped; and the village, on learning that Socorro, everyone's help, was ill, did what the village does, what Socorro herself had done to all her whole life: it came. There began to arrive at Socorro's door the dishes of food, the offers, the neighbours wanting to help, the whole village wanting to come, at last, to the rescue of the one who had always come; and it was there, at the moment the village turned to shelter her, that Socorro found herself before the thing she had never known how to do, and that the whole book was going to ask her to learn: to receive.
