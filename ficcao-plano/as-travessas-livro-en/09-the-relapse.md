## 9. The relapse

One does not lower a wall of twenty years without its rising again at the first blow, and Socorro had her relapse, and it came in the form in which the relapses of those who learned not to receive always come: at the first sign that a dish could, in fact, turn against her, the wall went up all again, at a stroke, the way the water of the river Fundo rises in the big rains, without giving anyone time to cover anything.

It came by a small thing, which is how the big relapses come. There was, among those who helped her, a younger neighbour, Idalina, who was not of the deeply stubborn ones but who had come to Socorro's care with pleasure, and who had a fault the whole village knew of her and forgave: she talked too much. And Idalina, one evening, in the churchyard, telling whoever wished to hear how the thing was going, let slip, with no malice at all, only for the pleasure of telling, a sentence that flew from mouth to mouth and reached Socorro distorted and raw:

"Poor Socorro, now she eats from everyone's hand, she who was so much her own mistress. Who'd have thought it. Illness softens anybody."

She eats from everyone's hand. The sentence reached Socorro through a third mouth, in one of those messages brought with the face of one doing a favour, oh, you don't know what Idalina's been saying about you, and Socorro heard it standing in the kitchen, with Lucinda's spoon in her hand, and felt the floor flee from under her again, as in the vertigo of the beginning. Because this was exactly what she had feared her whole life. It was the bill. It was the love one accepts and that one day becomes churchyard talk, comment, pity, she eats from everyone's hand. She had accepted, lowered her guard, let them in, and there was the proof that to accept is to risk being talked about, being charged, being left exposed in front of the village; and the old voice of the empty sill rose from within her with all its ancient force, see, I told you so, see how the love of others comes with a bill, see how you were right in twenty years of shutting yourself off.

And the wall went all up in an instant. The next day, Lucinda's soup was returned full again. Aurélia's dish came back full. Socorro went back to it wasn't needed, I manage on my own, went back to tipping the food out of the dishes and filling them with her own, went back to the little notes, God repay you, I'm well now; and Lucinda, on receiving her soup back full after weeks of receiving it empty, pressed her lips, but this time from sadness, and said to Aurélia, at the washing place:

"She's fallen. The woman's fallen again. Someone pushed her and she fell all the way. And I don't know if this time we'll get her up."

And there was a night, in the middle of the relapse, that was the bottom of it. Socorro did not sleep, and walked through the house in the dark, from kitchen to room and from room to kitchen, as she had once walked through the house of the broken roof waiting for Doroteia; and on the table was Lucinda's dish, full, already filled again with her own food, ready to be returned full in the morning, and Socorro would stop in front of it and look at it and say within, I knew it well, I was right, twenty years I was right and I was a fool to let this go, and she would almost have returned it, and almost have shut the door for good, and almost have undone all she had learned, going back to being the woman on her feet, at the door, on her way out, alone. She sat at the table, with the full dish in front of her, and stayed looking at it the whole night, and it was the cold soup forming a skin, and she neither ate it nor returned it, she stayed there between the two things, caught, as she had been caught twenty years between giving and receiving, unable to do either one. And she remembered, that night, the cold soup she had eaten on her feet at Justino's wake, the hunger she had refused at others' tables, and understood that she was doing the same thing again, refusing the hot to eat the cold, refusing others' to eat her own, alone, in the dark; and she had, for an instant, at the bottom of that night, the temptation to give herself over for good to the solitude, for at least the solitude did not disappoint her, at least the door shut from within gave no frights. It was the morning that saved her, and Glória, who arrived early, and who found her mother at the table, in front of a cold soup with a skin, with the face of one who spent the night losing what she had gained.

And the worst was that Socorro convinced herself she was right. She told herself within that she had seen well, that to accept had been a mistake, that that sentence of Idalina's proved she should have stayed shut, that her wall of twenty years was wisdom and not sickness, and that she had been foolish, weak, naive, in letting some Lucinda take away her way out with a bean soup. And she generalised, again, from one mouth to all mouths, from one sentence in the churchyard to all the love that came to her, exactly as twenty years before she had generalised from one empty door to all doors; and she was a step from shutting herself off for good, from undoing with her own hands all she had learned in that winter, from confirming herself forever in the solitude, when it was Glória, once more, who showed it to her.

Glória, who no longer ran up against a wall but spoke with her mother as equal to equal, saw the dishes come back full to the stone bench and understood at once the retreat. She went to her mother's house, found her filling Lucinda's dish with her own rice, with the shut face of the old habit, and instead of hurting herself as before, pulled out a chair and sat down, and spoke to her slowly, with the tenderness of one who has already come in and knows she can stay:

"Mother. Idalina talked too much, I know. It's reached my ears too, and I made Idalina see that one doesn't say it that way, and she was ashamed, for she's not bad, she's just mouth. But look what you're doing with it." She pointed at the dish the mother was filling. "Because of one person who talked too much, you're going to return me the dish full again? You're going to shut the door on Lucinda, who said nothing, who only wishes you well? You're going to shut it on me, Mother, who haven't failed you?" And Glória's voice took on a new firmness. "You're doing the same as twenty years ago. Doroteia failed you once, and you punished the whole village, shut yourself off from everyone, even from those who never failed you. And now Idalina talks too much once, and you want to punish the whole village again, shut yourself off again from everyone. I haven't failed you, Mother. Lucinda hasn't failed you. Aurélia hasn't failed you. Don't shut the door on us because of Idalina. Tell them apart, Mother. There are those who fail and those who don't, and you can't treat everyone as though they were Doroteia, or else you stay alone forever, and this time by your own hand, for the other time at least it was Doroteia who shut you in, this time it would be you."

Tell them apart, Mother. Socorro was left with Lucinda's dish half full in her hands, and the word went into her like a key into a lock she did not know she had. To tell apart. Her whole life she had not told apart. Her whole life she had put Doroteia's face over all faces, the empty door of the mountain in front of all doors, and so she had shut them all at once, those that failed her and those that did not fail her, and stayed alone with her wall thinking it was strength. And the daughter was right: Idalina had talked too much, and that was Idalina, it was not Lucinda, it was not Aurélia, it was not Glória, it was not the village. A loose mouth was not the law of mouths, as an empty door had not been the law of doors.

And she understood, with the dish in her hands, a deep thing about learning to receive, and that the Keeper had not told her because it had to be discovered in the falling: that to receive is not a thing one learns once and it stays, like one who learns to make bread and never again forgets it. It is a thing one has to relearn always, because there will always be an Idalina, there will always be someone who fails, or who talks too much, or who charges lightly, or who does not come on a day one was already counting on them; and each such failure makes the wall rise again, in an instant, and what matters is not never again being disappointed, for that is impossible and whoever expects it stays shut forever, it is not letting one disappointment shut the door on whoever did not disappoint. It is to tell apart. It is to go on receiving from those who do not fail, even after one who failed.

And there was, days later, the meeting with Idalina, which Socorro had feared and which the daughter had told her she had to have, not to punish her, Mother, but to see that she is not Doroteia. Idalina appeared at her door, ashamed, twisting her apron, asking pardon before Socorro had even opened the door properly:

"Socorro, I spoke without thinking, in the churchyard, and the thing got away, and I know it hurt you, and I come to ask your pardon, for I didn't mean to speak ill of you, on the contrary, I was even speaking well, saying how good it was that you let yourself be cared for; but it came out crooked, and you heard the crooked, and the fault of the crooked is my mouth's, which was always bigger than my head."

And Socorro, who had her heart ready for the bill, for the confirmation that she was right to shut herself off, was disarmed, because Idalina did not come to charge anything, she came to give back. She came to put at her door, instead of a dish, a request for pardon, which is the dish of words; and Socorro understood, with Idalina there twisting her apron, that the failure of a loose mouth was not a bill, it was a stumble, and that Idalina, in coming to ask pardon, was doing the opposite of leaving her in the cold, she was coming, arriving, mending, a thing Doroteia had never done in twenty years. And instead of sending her away, Socorro did a thing that healed her more than a thousand dishes accepted: she invited her in.

"Come in, Idalina, and have a coffee with me, for it's cold at the door. And we'll say no more of it, for you're not bad, you're mouth, and a mouth we all have. Come in."

And Idalina came in, astonished, and they drank the coffee, and Socorro understood, in serving it to her, that to receive is sometimes also to receive a request for pardon, it is to let whoever hurt us mend, it is not to shut the door on whoever comes back to set it straight; and that if she had shut the door on Idalina, as she had shut it her whole life, she would have lost, because of a sentence in the churchyard, a neighbour who after all wished her well and who had had the courage, that Doroteia had never had, to cross her own churchyard head down to come and say I spoke ill, forgive me.

And Socorro set down the dish, and tipped out the rice she had put in it, and washed it again, and left it empty to return it to Lucinda empty; and she went to Glória, who was still in the chair, and put her hand on her face, a thing she never did, and said only, low:

"I'll tell them apart, daughter. I'll learn to tell them apart. Have patience with me, for this is not like bread, this is unlearned at the first rain." And she understood, with her hand on the daughter's face, that to have at her side one who would tell her tell them apart, Mother, don't shut the door on all because of one, was the difference between shutting herself off forever at the first disappointment and learning to receive in spite of some disappointments, which is the only way of receiving that exists; because to receive without ever being disappointed there is not, and what there is is to receive knowing that sometimes one is disappointed, and not to let that shut the door on whoever does not disappoint us, which is most, which is almost everyone, which is the whole village minus a Doroteia and a loose mouth in the churchyard.
