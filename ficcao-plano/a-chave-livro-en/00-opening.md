## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Preciosa — precious, the needed one — daughter of the village, and the name was a prophecy and a trap: they called her Preciosa, the one who is worth much, the one of price, and she fulfilled the name by making herself indispensable, the one who cannot be done without, the one without whom nothing moves; but the name, which seemed a blessing, was a trap, because one who makes herself indispensable becomes bound to the thing that makes her indispensable, and Preciosa made herself the key of a mill, and became bound to the mill, without knowing who she would be without it.

The register keeps who holds the houses and the trades of the village, and there are names that appear bound to a place as though they were of it: Preciosa, in the register of Véspera, appears bound to the Spinning Mill, for thirty years, always, the one who opens before all and closes after all, who knows where everything is and how everything is done, the key; and she is bound to the mill in such a way that no one thinks them separate any more, Preciosa and the Mill, as though the mill were hers and she were of the mill, and the book would not know how to write one without the other, which is the sign, in the register, of a person who confused herself with what she does.

This is the first book of the shelf of the Spinning Mill, the shelf of those who ask who they are without what they do, and within it, it is the book of the one who made herself indispensable. It tells the story of a woman who for thirty years has been the key of the mill, who never took a whole holiday, for whom to fall ill is to organise the illness so as not to be absent, and who sustains herself on a phrase, if I stop, everything falls, which is the same that fences her in; and of the year she hands over the key, for a week, and watches, with terror and then with astonishment, the mill working without her, and discovers what the phrase hid: it was not the mill that fell without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill. It is not a book against work nor against being good at what one does; it is a book about one who confused herself with what she does, and made herself a key, and forgot to be a person, and about the courage, the greatest for one who is indispensable, of handing over the key, and discovering, on the far side of the fear, who one is without it.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Preciosa who made herself indispensable, who opens before all and closes after all, who takes no holiday because if I stop everything falls, who confused what she is worth with what she does, let her know, from the first line, what it took Preciosa thirty years not to know: that no one is indispensable, and that one who thinks herself so is bound, not safe; that the phrase if I stop everything falls does not protect the mill, it binds the person; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to hold the key more, it is to hand it over, and to see the mill move without us, and to cross the mourning of not being indispensable, in order to discover, on the far side, the person left unlived in the shadow of the trade, and who is worth, at last, not for what she does, but for being.
