## 6. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to some papers of the mill at the House of the Register, in the owner's name, as always, because even that passed through her. Magalhães had not set foot in the House of the Register for years; he sent Preciosa, with a folder of papers and a power of attorney, and said see to this for me, my Preciosa, you'll sort it out. And it was so, for one more thing of which she was the key, that Preciosa sat down before the Keeper, on an autumn afternoon, with the light coming in sidelong through the high window and the dust dancing in it, and the Keeper, who saw people the way she saw trades, read her life in the papers and told her the truth that was missing.

The House of the Register was a deep, cool room, with shelves from floor to ceiling, full of dark-covered books, the registers of the village, where there was kept, it was said, not what people had, but what people were. The Keeper was a woman of indefinite age, with broad, still hands, who wrote slowly and listened more than she spoke. She received Preciosa, bade her sit, opened the folder of papers, and went on signing and stamping what there was to sign and stamp, without haste, and halfway through she raised her eyes over the papers and asked, with that manner of one who reads deep:

"It's Preciosa who sees to all this, the mill's business. Even the owner's."

"It is, yes," said Preciosa. "For thirty years. I open in the morning, lock up at night, I know where everything is." And she said it with pride, as she always said it, because it was the thing she was most proud of.

The Keeper set down her pen. She looked at her a moment, without judging, which was her manner, and said:

"Preciosa is the key of the Spinning Mill."

"I am," said Preciosa, and she almost smiled, because it was the exact word, the word she herself used.

"So you are," said the Keeper. And she was silent an instant, smoothing with her broad hand the page of the book open before her. "Let me tell you a thing, Preciosa, that I've learned to see in this book, over many years, in the people who make themselves the key of something. I don't tell it you to wound you. I tell it you because it's my trade, to see what people are, and what you are is here, in these papers, plainer than you think."

And she rose, and went to one of the shelves, and took from it not a book, but a key. An old key, of iron, which she kept in a box among the books, and that Preciosa had never known what it served. She set it on the table, between the two of them, in the sidelong light.

"Look at this key," said the Keeper. "It's a fine key, old, well made. It opened, in its time, the door of a house that no longer exists. The house fell, long ago, it came to the ground, and this key was left, which I keep. And now I ask you, Preciosa: how much is this key worth?"

Preciosa looked at it, not knowing.

"It's worth nothing," said the Keeper, slowly. "It's a piece of iron. A key is not worth anything in itself. It is worth something by its door. A key without its door opens nothing, closes nothing, serves for nothing; the worth of a key is all borrowed from the door it opens. While the door exists, the key is important, is sought, is kept with care, is the thing without which one cannot go in. The day the door falls, or they change the lock, the key becomes this: a piece of iron in a box, which I keep out of pity, and which no longer opens anything at all."

And she looked at Preciosa, and her voice dropped, grew deeper:

"You made yourself the key of a mill, Preciosa. And in that you have your pride, and I understand the pride. But did you notice what it did to you? You put all your worth in the door, and none in yourself. You came to be worth something, in your own eyes, not for the Preciosa you are, but for the Spinning Mill you open. And the day the door changes its lock, or the day you can no longer turn the key, you'll be left like this one: a piece of iron, of no worth, because you gave all your worth to a door, and kept none for yourself."

Preciosa looked at the old key on the table, and looked at her own, the one of the Spinning Mill, that she carried on the ring of its own, and felt, in her chest, the same tightening as that morning, but deeper, because this one was not of the body. And the Keeper, seeing that she understood, sat down again, and told her what she had seen in the book over the years:

"I've seen many a Preciosa pass through. Many a person who made herself the key of a thing, of a mill, of a house, of a family, and who believed herself indispensable. And I tell you a thing I've learned, and that is hard to hear: no one is indispensable. No one. The mills that seemed not to run without one person ran without her when she was missing. The houses that seemed to rest on a single pillar reorganised themselves when the pillar fell. And the person who believed herself irreplaceable, when she was missing, was replaced, with more or less cost, with more or less missing, but she was, because the world stops for no one, not for us, not for that apprentice you're to have, not for me, who've been in this book so many years I've come to think myself part of it, and who know that, the day I'm missing, another comes, and the book goes on. And do you know what's saddest in the entry of those people, Preciosa? It is not that they believed themselves indispensable. It is that they gave their lives to being so, and discovered at the end, or never even came to discover, that they were not, that the thing they gave themselves to went on without them, and that they had traded a life of their own for the illusion of being the key of a door that opened, after all, without them."

And she told her a name, because the Keeper, when she wanted a truth to take hold, gave it a face. Do you remember Engrácia, of the upper mill, Preciosa? Preciosa remembered vaguely, a dry woman, who had died some years before. Well, Engrácia, said the Keeper, was the upper mill for forty years. It was she who knew the millstone, who knew the water, who regulated the weir by ear, and she said, just like you, that if she stopped the mill stopped. And no one learned, because she did not let them, as you do not let them. And when Engrácia fell ill, in earnest, mortally, do you know what happened to the mill? It stood still three weeks, because no one knew how to set it going, and the village had to take the grain to be ground in the big town, in the rain, three weeks. And Engrácia, in her bed, instead of resting to die in peace, tormented herself over the stopped mill, and sent messages, and wanted to get up, and they would not let her, and she died like that, clinging to a millstone no one knew how to turn. And do you know the end of the story? A nephew came from the Porto side, who had never set foot in the mill, and in two months learned the thing from an old man who still remembered, and the mill ran again, and runs to this day. Forty years Engrácia believed herself the only one, and two months and a nephew were enough. That was all her indispensability lasted after her: two months. I wrote up her entry, Preciosa, and what I put there, deep down, was this: she gave her life to being irreplaceable, and was replaced in a season. It is the saddest entry I have written.

Preciosa heard the story of Engrácia, and saw herself in it as in a mirror, and shivered.

And the Keeper then told her the greater truth, the one that began to set her free:

"You're afraid of handing over the key because you're afraid of discovering that the mill runs without you. But think of it the other way, Preciosa. If the mill does not run without you, you've done a bad job, because a good head leaves things running even in her absence. And if the mill runs without you, you've done a good job, but you have to bear the truth that you are not indispensable. Either way, notice: your worth does not lie in the mill falling or not falling without you. Your worth lies in you. In the person you are, who is not the key, who is Preciosa, and who is worth something for being, not for opening a door." She took the old key, and put it away again in the box, among the books. "Hand over the key, Preciosa. For one week, even. And see. Either the mill falls, and then you're right, and you'll be at ease knowing it; or the mill runs, and then you discover you are free, because you were not bound, and that you are worth the same, not for the key, but for yourself."

Preciosa came out of the House of the Register with the truth of the key weighing on her more than the folder of papers. She crossed the square, slowly, with the light already low, and stopped, without noticing it, by the fountain of Véspera, the old stone fountain in the middle of the square, that has poured water for more years than the book keeps. She stood watching the water fall into the basin, and the women filling their pitchers and going off without looking at the fountain, and the children drinking from their hands and running away, and no one, she noticed, no one thought of the fountain that gave the water, no one thanked it, no one looked at it; and the fountain did not mind, it poured its water just the same, for all, without making itself indispensable to any.

And Preciosa thought: the fountain is not worth something for being thought of. It is not worth something for being needed, for being irreplaceable, for being the only one. It is worth something for giving the water, for being what it is, a fountain. If one day it dries up, other water comes, another pipe is opened, and the village does not fall, and not for that did the fountain cease to have been worth something while it ran. And I, she thought, I go about living to be thought of, to be needed, to be called, to be the key. And that binds me. And perhaps it is time to learn from the fountain, which gives without binding itself, which serves without making itself master of those who drink, which is worth something for being a fountain and not for being the only water in the world.

And she thought, looking at the fountain, of Aurora. Of the girl she had braked for years, who had a gift, who wanted to learn, and whom she did not let. And she understood, there, by the fountain, that if she wanted to hand over the key to anyone, it was to her she had to hand it; and that to hand the key to Aurora was not only to go off for a week, it was to mend, late though it was, a harm of years; it was to do with the girl what the fountain did with the water, give it, pass it on, let it run forward, instead of damming it for herself. And the idea hurt her and eased her, as everything to do with Aurora hurt her and eased her, because Aurora was, at one and the same time, the threat to the wall and the only way out of it.

She went home by the upper street, so as not to speak to anyone, with the key of the Spinning Mill weighing in her pocket as it had never weighed, and with a decision forming inside her, still without shape, but already with a name: to hand over the key. And to see. Either the mill fell, or the mill ran, and either way, said the Keeper, she had something to discover on the far side.
