## 6. The Keeper of the register

She went to see to some papers of the mill at the House of the Register, in the owner's name, as always, because even that passed through her, and it was thus, by one more thing she was the key of, that Preciosa found herself seated before the Keeper, and the Keeper, who saw people as she saw the trades, told her the truth that was missing.

The Keeper saw to the papers, and noticed that it was Preciosa seeing to everything of the mill, even what was the owner's; and asked her, with that manner of one who reads deep: Preciosa is the key of the Spinning Mill, is she not? The one without whom nothing moves? And Preciosa, with pride, I am, for thirty years, I open in the morning and close at night, I know where everything is. And the Keeper looked at her, and said: and that is your pride, I know. But let me tell you a thing I have learned to see, in this book, over the years, in the people who become the key of something.

And the Keeper said: a key is a useful thing, it opens and closes; but a key has this about it, that it is not worth anything by itself, it is worth by the door. A key alone, without its door, is nothing, serves nothing, is a piece of iron; the worth of the key is all borrowed from the door it opens. And you, Preciosa, made yourself the key of a mill, and have in this your pride, but did you notice what that did to you? It made you a key, and a key is worth by the door, not by itself; and you, by confusing yourself with the key, came to be worth, in your own eyes, not by yourself, but by the mill you open, and were left with no worth of your own, only the worth borrowed from the Mill. And the day the door changes the lock, or the day you can no longer open it, you are left a key without a door, a piece of iron, worthless, because you put all your worth in the door and none in yourself.

And Preciosa heard this, and it went deep. And the Keeper, seeing that she understood, told her what she had seen in the book: I have seen many Preciosas pass by, many people who made themselves the key of a thing, of a mill, of a house, of a family, and who thought themselves indispensable, and I will tell you a thing I have learned: no one is indispensable. No one. The mills that seemed not to move without a person moved without her when she was absent; the houses that seemed to rest on a single column reorganised themselves when the column fell; and the indispensable person, when she was absent, was replaced, with more or less effort, with more or less longing, but she was, because the world does not stop for anyone. And do you know what is saddest in the entry of those people? It is that they thought themselves indispensable, gave their lives to being so, and discovered, in the end, or did not come to discover, that they were not, that the thing they gave themselves to moved without them, and that they had traded a life of their own for the illusion of being irreplaceable in a thing that after all replaced them.

And the Keeper told her the greatest truth, the one that began to free her: you are afraid to hand over the key because you are afraid to discover that the mill moves without you. But think the other way round, Preciosa. If the mill does not move without you, you did a bad job, because a good chief leaves things moving even in her absence; and if the mill moves without you, you did a good job, but you have to bear the truth that you are not indispensable. Either way, your worth is not in the mill moving or not without you. Your worth is in you, in the person you are, who is not the key, it is Preciosa, and who is worth by being, and not by opening a door. Hand over the key, Preciosa, for a week even. And see. Either the mill falls, and then you are right and rest easy; or the mill moves, and then you discover that you are free, because you are not bound to it, and that you are worth all the same, not by the key, but by yourself.

And Preciosa left the House of the Register with the truth of the key weighing on her, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain of Véspera, and the fountain said a thing to her: that the water ran for everyone, and that no one thought of the fountain that gave it, and that the fountain did not mind, because its worth was not in being thought of, in being indispensable to those who drank, it was in giving the water, in being what it was; and that she, Preciosa, went about living to be thought of, to be indispensable, to be needed, and that this bound her, and that perhaps it was time to learn from the fountain, which gives without making itself indispensable, which serves without binding itself, which is worth by being a fountain and not by being irreplaceable, and that if one day it ran dry, another water would come, and the village would not fall, and not for that had the fountain ceased to have been worth something while it ran.
