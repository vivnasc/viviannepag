## 5. The settled owner

There was the owner of the mill, and the owner was settled in Preciosa's indispensability, comfortable, at ease, because to have her was not to have to worry about anything; and it was the owner, without malice, who most fed Preciosa's prison, because there are those who benefit from our carrying a key on our backs, and never tell us to set it down.

The owner had inherited the Mill, or bought it, and governed it from afar, without meddling in the day to day, because he had Preciosa, and Preciosa saw to everything; and the owner liked that, of course, because Preciosa spared him the work, the worry, the presence, and he could make his own life, at ease, knowing the mill moved because Preciosa made it move. And the owner praised her, and told her oh Preciosa, what would become of this mill without you, you are the soul of it, I without you would do nothing; and Preciosa heard, and liked to hear, because it was the recognition of her importance, and she did not see that the owner, by praising her so, by telling her she was indispensable, was tightening her chain, because to an indispensable one rest is not given, more work is given, and one who calls us irreplaceable rarely does it to free us, he does it, without malice, to keep us carrying.

Because an indispensable Preciosa suited the owner. It suited him that everything pass through her, that she take no holidays, that she not delegate, that she be the single key, because so he did not have to worry, did not have to learn the mill, did not have to pay anyone else to do what Preciosa did alone; and so the owner, without thinking it as malice, never encouraged Preciosa to rest, to delegate, to teach the apprentice, to set down the key; on the contrary, whenever she threatened to stop, the owner was the first to say oh no, Preciosa, the mill needs you, and to place the key back in her hand, because Preciosa's indispensability was convenient for the owner, it spared him work and money, and he, settled in it, had no interest at all in its ending.

And there was, in this relationship, an injustice Preciosa began to see. That she gave the mill much more than she received: she gave her life, her health, her holidays, her own life, everything, and received a wage and some praise; and the owner received much more than he gave: he received a mill that moved by itself, at Preciosa's cost, and gave a wage and some praise; and the sum, seen so, was unjust, it was a woman burning herself her whole life for an owner to be at ease, and the owner feeding that burning with praise that cost little and yielded much. And Preciosa understood that her if I stop everything falls served not only her illusion about herself; it served also the interest of the owner, who needed her to believe in it to go on sparing himself at her cost, and that it was, in part, the owner who gained most from Preciosa's indispensability, and who would least want to see it end.

And there was a conversation that showed it to her. Preciosa, exhausted, ill, spoke to the owner of resting, of delegating, of teaching the apprentice so she could, at last, take a holiday; and the owner, instead of supporting her, as he should, reacted at once with the usual, oh Preciosa, but the mill without you does not move, the apprentice is not ready, it cannot be now, leave things be; and Preciosa, that time, instead of accepting as she had always accepted, heard the owner's phrase another way, heard the interest beneath the praise, understood that the owner did not say that for her, he said it for himself, to keep her carrying, so as not to have to change anything, to go on at ease at her cost; and she understood that if she waited for the owner to give her leave to set down the key, she would never set it down, because it suited the owner that she hold it forever; and that if she wanted to free herself, she would have to do it against the owner's interest, by her own decision, handing over the key even though the owner did not want it, because the freedom of one who has made herself indispensable is never given by one who benefits from her indispensability, it has to be taken by the one who suffers it.
