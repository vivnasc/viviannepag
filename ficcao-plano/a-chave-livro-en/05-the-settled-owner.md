## 5. The settled owner

There was the owner of the mill, and the owner was settled into Preciosa's indispensability, comfortable, at ease, because to have her was to have to worry about nothing; and it was the owner, without malice, who most fed Preciosa's prison, because there are those who benefit from our carrying a key on our backs, and who never, never tell us to set it down.

His name was Magalhães, and he had inherited the Spinning Mill from his father, who had inherited it from his, and he governed it from afar, from the big town, where he had his house and his other businesses and the time to have them. He appeared at the mill once a month, or every month and a half, in a large car that he parked in the yard, and stayed an hour, two at most, always in the same way: he arrived in good humour, shook a few hands, walked through the shed with his hands behind his back looking at the machines the way one looks at a painting one likes but did not paint, and shut himself in the office with Preciosa to go over the accounts, which was his way of saying to see whether Preciosa has this in order, and Preciosa had it, always, all of it, by heart, and Magalhães would leave at ease, and go to lunch in the big town without thinking again about the mill until the following month.

Idalina, who saw everything, said one thing of him, under her breath, when he went off in the large car: that man treats the mill the way one treats a milk cow, Preciosa, he comes to see if she gives milk and goes off, and the cow is you. And Preciosa would scold her, don't talk like that about Sô Magalhães, Idalina, he's a good person; and he was, in his way, a good person, Magalhães, he was not a bad man, he ill-treated no one, he paid on time, he gave the good-mornings; and that was what made the thing harder to see, because the chain he tightened on her came of no malice, it came of comfort, and the comfort of some rests so often on the weariness of others without either the ones or the others ever coming to think of it as injustice. Magalhães slept at ease in the big town because Preciosa did not sleep; and it never crossed his mind that there was any trade in it, because to him Preciosa was the mill, and the mill seeing to itself was the natural order of things, as it is natural for water to run downhill.

And Magalhães was fond of Preciosa. He was fond of her the way one is fond of a good machine that never breaks down, of a clock that never runs slow, of a foundation that is never seen and never gives way. He treated her with a broad cordiality, called her my Preciosa, and had, for her, a phrase that he repeated at every visit, on leaving, his hand already on the handle of the car, and that was, without his knowing it, the phrase that most tightened her chain:

"Ah Preciosa, what would this mill be without you. You are the soul of this place. I, without you, would do nothing."

And he said it with a smile, and clapped her on the shoulder, and got into the large car and went off, and Preciosa stayed in the yard watching him leave, with that phrase warming her inside the way praise warms, and for years she did not see what lay beneath it. Because the phrase seemed a tribute, and was a sentence. Whoever tells us we are the soul of a thing, and that without us it does not run, rarely says it to set us free; he says it, with malice or without, to keep us at the post. To the indispensable one is given no rest; one is given more work, and one is given the word irreplaceable, which weighs less than gold and binds more.

Because an indispensable Preciosa suited Magalhães, and suited him in a way he did not even need to think about. It suited him that everything pass through her, that she take no holidays, that she not delegate, that she not teach Aurora, that she be the single key; because that way he had no need to worry, no need to learn the mill, no need to hire a second foreman, no need to pay anyone else to do what Preciosa did alone for a single wage. Preciosa was, to Magalhães, the best saving he had: a woman who was worth three and cost as one, and who even thanked him for the praise.

There was a sign of this, years before, that Preciosa only now joined up. There had been, at one time, a younger man, one Adriano, whom Magalhães had put into the mill to learn the thing, the talk being that he was to come to help Preciosa, perhaps to come to be able to stand in for her in some impediment. Adriano was a serious lad, from the big town, with studies in machines, and he arrived full of will, with a new notebook and a pencil behind his ear, wanting to learn everything. And Preciosa, on the first day, scarcely having shaken his hand, felt the cold at the back of her neck, the same she was to feel, years later, with Aurora. Because Adriano had come to learn what she knew, and what she knew was she.

And she did to him, without thinking of it as wrong, what she would come to do to Aurora, but more quickly, because she did not yet have the conscience the Keeper was to give her. She gave him the small tasks, sent him to check the thread, count the parts, and when Adriano asked her show me the boiler, Preciosa, let me see the accounts, she said there's no time now, it'll keep for later, and the later never came. Once, Adriano, who was sharp, noted in his new notebook the sequence in which Preciosa started the machines in the morning, to learn it, and Preciosa saw the notebook, and said, with a gentleness that hid the steel: lad, that's not learned on paper, that comes of years, put the notebook away. And Adriano put the notebook away, and understood, slowly, that there was no room there for him to grow, because the room was all taken, and after a few months he asked for his reckoning and went off to a spinning mill on the river side, where, it was learned later, he rose to foreman. And Magalhães, instead of being surprised at the departure, was pleased, and said to Preciosa, with that smile, at the office door: it's a good thing that lad went, my Preciosa, what do I want with another, when I have you? I saved his wage. And Preciosa, then, had received that as praise, and had even agreed, because Adriano would never learn as she did. Only now did she see what it was: the owner and the key, without arranging it, without a word said, shutting the same door, each for his own reason, hers fear, his the reckoning. And she thought of Adriano, capable, growing at another mill, and understood that she had braked him as she braked Aurora, and felt, for him, a remorse late by many years.

And there was a detail that said everything about Magalhães, and that Preciosa had carried thirty years without weighing it. When, many years before, she had asked Magalhães for a proper helper, a second foreman, saying that the mill had grown and that she could not see to everything alone, Magalhães had heard her with that smile, and then had done the sum out loud, to her face, without shame: oh Preciosa, a second foreman is twelve more wages a year, and you've done the work of two from the start without complaint, why should I pay two when one is enough for me? And he had laughed, like one telling a joke, and Preciosa had laughed too, then, flattered to do the work of two, without seeing that she had just been praised for the very reason that exploited her. Only now, on remembering that sum said out loud, twelve more wages, did she see what Magalhães had in fact told her: that she saved him a whole wage a year, for thirty years, and that it was that saving, and not the soul of the mill, that he truly praised when he called her irreplaceable.

And she was beginning to see the injustice, now that she had learned to look. For she gave the mill far more than she received: she gave her life, her health, her summers, the holidays she never took, the life of her own she never had, everything, and received a wage and a few praises on the way out. And Magalhães received far more than he gave: he received a mill that ran on its own, at her cost, and gave a wage and a few praises that cost him the air spent in saying them. The reckoning, seen like that, cold, was a woman burning herself up her whole life so that a man might rest at ease in the big town, and the man feeding that burning with the word soul, which yielded much and paid nothing.

And there was, at last, a conversation that showed it to her for good. It was after Aurora, after the ache in the chest, on one of the monthly visits. Preciosa, who had been turning it all over, gathered courage and spoke to Magalhães, in the office, in earnest, for the first time: she told him she was tired, that her body was warning her, that she wanted to begin teaching Aurora in earnest, to pass things on to her, so that she might, at last, take some whole holidays, even a single week, to rest. And Magalhães, instead of backing her, as he ought, instead of saying of course, Preciosa, it's more than time, reacted at once, without even thinking, with the usual:

"Ah Preciosa, but the mill doesn't run without you. The apprentice isn't ready, that takes years. Now it can't be done, you see how we are for orders. Stay where you are, woman, no one does it like you. Next year we'll see."

And Preciosa, that time, instead of accepting as she had always accepted, stood looking at Magalhães from the other side of the desk, and heard his phrase in a new way. She heard the interest beneath the praise. She understood that Magalhães did not say it for her, he said it for himself: to keep her bearing the load, so as not to have to change anything, so as not to have to learn the mill nor pay a second man, so as to go on coming down from the big town once a month and finding everything in order. She understood that the next year we'll see was the same next year of thirty years, and that it would never come, because it suited the owner that she hold the key forever.

"Sô Magalhães," she said, and the voice came out serene, which astonished her herself. "The apprentice isn't ready because I never let her be. That changes when I want it, not when the year comes."

Magalhães laughed, not understanding, and clapped her on the shoulder on the way out, and said my Preciosa, ever with his charm, and got into the large car and went off. And Preciosa stayed in the yard, as always, watching him leave, but with a new thing in her chest, hard and clear: the certainty that, if she waited for the owner to give her leave to set down the key, she would die with it in her hand. That the freedom of one who has made herself the key of something is never given by the one who benefits from the key; it has to be taken by the one who carries it. That if she wanted to hand over the key, she would have to hand it over by her own decision, even against Magalhães's smile, even if he said ah no, Preciosa, and wanted to put it back in her hand. Because the owner was settled, comfortable, at ease, upon her weariness, and one who is settled upon another's weariness never tells them to rest.
