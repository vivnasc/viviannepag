## 9. The terror of watching

Preciosa's week of holiday was not rest, at first, it was terror: the terror of watching, from afar, the mill move without her, which is the greatest trial of one who has made herself indispensable, to bear seeing the thing work in our absence without running to save it.

Because to hand over the key had been hard, but the hardest came after, in the week, far away, doing nothing. Preciosa, who had departed without a telephone to force herself to switch off, discovered that she could not switch off, that her thought was all on the mill, that she imagined every hour what would be going wrong, that the apprentice would be getting into a muddle, that a machine had broken down and no one knew how to mend it, that the owner would be furious, that everything would be falling; and the imagination, fed by thirty years of thinking herself indispensable, painted her the mill in disaster, and Preciosa, far away, on holiday, instead of resting, lived the torment of imagining the mill collapsing without her and of not being able to run to save it.

And the temptation to return was enormous. There were days on which Preciosa was almost about to pack her bags, to catch the first transport, to return to the mill to see what was happening, to take up the key, to save what she imagined was falling; and the phrase cried out to her, if I stop everything falls, you see, it is falling, return, save, you are the one who knows, without you they do not move; and Preciosa, in the holiday room, far away, struggled against that temptation as against an addiction, because it was an addiction, that of being needed, of coming to the rescue, of being the key, and to let it go, even for a week, gave her a withdrawal, a physical agony of not being indispensable, of being, for the first time, dispensable, done without, on the outside.

And it was in that agony that Preciosa touched the bottom of the book, and that it was necessary to touch in order to turn. On one of the nights, alone, far away, bearing the terror of imagining the mill falling, Preciosa understood, suddenly, a thing that shook her: that the terror she felt was not for the mill. It was for her. If she had the certainty that the mill was running well without her, she should not feel terror, she should feel relief, rest; and yet the idea of the mill running well without her filled her with an anguish even worse than that of imagining it falling; and Preciosa understood, with horror, that deep down she did not want the mill to run well without her, she wanted it to fail a little, to feel her absence, to prove that it needed her, because if the mill ran perfectly well without her, then the truth she most feared was confirmed: that she was dispensable, that she was not indispensable, that she had given her life to an illusion.

And it was terrible to admit it, but it was freeing. Preciosa understood that part of her hoped for the mill to fail in her absence, not out of malice, but out of despair, the despair of one who needs to be indispensable and who, if the thing moves without her, loses what makes her who she is; and she understood how sick that was, how sad to want a thing she loved to go wrong only to feel herself needed, and how the indispensability had deformed her, to the point of preferring the disaster of the mill to the proof of her dispensability; and she understood that she had to want the opposite, that she had to hope for the mill to run well without her, because that would be the proof that she had done a good job, that she had left things moving, that the apprentice had learned; and that if she wanted to heal herself, she had to learn to rejoice in the mill moving without her, instead of fearing it, she had to want to be dispensable, she had to wish for the door to open without her key.

And she bore it. She bore the week without returning, without telephoning, without running to save anything; she bore the terror, the withdrawal, the temptation; she bore the doing nothing, the being on the outside, the being dispensable; and each day she bore without returning was a day against thirty years of indispensability, it was the key staying, on purpose, in another's hand, it was Preciosa learning, by force, the thing she had never learned, to be outside, not to be needed, to let the mill move; and after a few days, in the midst of the terror, she began to feel, very deep, a new thing, a thing she had never felt in thirty years, a rest, a silence, a time that was hers and not the mill's, and she understood, with astonishment, that perhaps, on the far side of the terror of not being indispensable, there was a thing she had never known, and that was called, simply, to rest.
