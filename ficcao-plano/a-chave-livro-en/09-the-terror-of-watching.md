## 9. The terror of watching

Preciosa's week of holiday was not rest, at first. It was terror. The terror of watching, from afar, the mill running without her, which is the greatest test of one who has made herself the key of something, to bear knowing the thing working in our absence without running to save it.

She had chosen the guesthouse for being far, on purpose, far enough that she could not go back in a single leap, far enough to force her to bear it; and on buying the coach ticket, in the square, from the woman at the ticket window, this one asked her return as well?, and Preciosa hesitated, because to buy the return at once was to give herself a way out, and she said, with a tightening, one way only, I'll buy the return there, so as to have no way of fleeing before the time. And the woman at the ticket window looked at her the way one looks at a madwoman, but Preciosa knew what she was doing: she was taking from herself the door of escape, the way one burns the bridges behind one, because she knew herself, and knew that if she had the return in her pocket, she would have used it at the first alarm.

The guesthouse was at some baths, three hours by coach from the village, and it was a small room, clean, with a window onto a pinewood and an iron bed and a vase with paper flowers. Preciosa arrived, set down the badly packed case, sat on the edge of the bed, and stood looking at the room, and the room looked back at her, empty, silent, with nothing to do. And it was then that she understood the size of what she had done. She had no gate to open. No motor to start. No notebook in her pocket, no key, no one to call her. She had a room, and a window, and a pinewood, and a whole week of hours she did not know how to spend.

She had left without telephone, on purpose, to force herself to switch off. And she discovered, on the first afternoon, that she could not switch off her head even without a telephone, that her thought was all at the mill, three hours away, and that her imagination, fed by thirty years of believing herself the key, painted her disasters. It's eight o'clock, she thought, looking at the station clock: Aurora must be opening the gate, did she do the trick, is it raining and the key biting. It's ten: something has surely broken down, and no one knows, and Aurora is going round and round the loom without knowing the third pinion. It's noon: Magalhães must have called, must be furious, must be asking after me. And the imagination, which is the worst of companions for one who lets go of the key, painted her the Spinning Mill in flames, the machines stopped, the clients fleeing, and Aurora drowning in the middle of it all, and Magalhães saying I told you the mill doesn't run without her.

And the temptation to go back was enormous. There was a night, the second, when Preciosa was actually packing the case again. She took it down from on top of the wardrobe, opened it on the bed, began folding the clothes to go back on the first coach of the morning, to go and see, only see, with her own eyes, whether the mill was standing. And the phrase cried out to her, with the voice of the vice: you see, it's falling, go back, save it, you're the one who knows, without you they can't run, you're still in time. And Preciosa, sitting on the iron bed, with the case open and the clothes in her hands, struggled against it as against a vice, because it was a vice, she understood it that night with clarity: the vice of being needed, of rushing to help, of being the key; and to let go of it, even for a week, gave her a physical agony, a tightening, a sweat, a lack, just like that of one who lets go of a thing the body has grown used to depending on. She was suffering withdrawal. Withdrawal from being indispensable.

She closed the case again, that night, without packing it, and put it back on top of the wardrobe, and went to the window, and stood looking at the dark pinewood, breathing, bearing it. And to bear it, she did a thing she had never done: she spoke to herself, in a low voice, in the dark, like someone holding another by the hand. She said: you stay, Preciosa. Aurora's there. If the loom broke down, she'll think of what you would do, and that you taught her. If the gate bit, she'll find the knee. And if it falls, if it really falls, a mill falls, not you, and a mill gets back up, for that there are owners and there are insurances and there are people. You stay. And it was strange to hear herself say that, because it was the first time in her life that Preciosa held herself up, instead of holding others up, and she understood, there at the window, that never, in thirty years, had anyone held her up, because she had always been the one who held others, and that to be holding herself up now, in the dark of a guesthouse room, was a thing she should have learned young and was only learning now. And she bore it.

And it was on one of the following nights, alone, at the window, bearing the terror of imagining the mill falling, that Preciosa touched the bottom of the book, the bottom she needed to touch in order to turn. She understood, suddenly, a thing that shook her so much that she had to sit down: that the terror she felt was not for the mill. It was for herself.

Because, she thought, if I were certain the mill was running well without me, I ought not to feel terror. I ought to feel relief. Rest. I ought to think how good, it's all going well, Aurora is capable, I can breathe at last. And yet, she understood with horror, the idea of the mill running well without her did not ease her: it filled her with an anguish even worse than that of imagining it falling. And why? She made herself answer, there at the window, by force, because the truth was right underneath and she no longer wanted to put it off: because, deep down, she did not want the mill to run well without her. She wanted it to fail a little. She wanted it to feel her absence. She wanted Aurora to flounder, Magalhães to call anxious asking when she was coming back, the mill to prove, with even a single stumble, that it needed her. Because if the mill ran perfectly well without her, then the truth she most feared was confirmed: that she was dispensable, that she was not the key, that she had given her life to an illusion.

And it was terrible to admit it. Preciosa, at the window of the guesthouse room, three hours from the mill, understood that part of her was rooting for the mill to fail in her absence, and felt disgust at herself, because what is rooting for a thing we love to go wrong only so as to feel ourselves needed? It was sick. It was how far the key had deformed her, after thirty years: to the point of preferring the disaster of the Spinning Mill to the proof of her own dispensability. To the point of wishing ill on the mill she had given her life to serving, only so that it would not run without her. And she stood a long time at the window, with that shame, which was the deepest shame of her life, deeper than that of the credit in childhood.

There was, on the third day, a small encounter that helped her to turn, and that she never forgot. She was taking her breakfast in the guesthouse parlour, alone in a corner, when at the next table there sat down a woman of her own age, with worn hands, who had come to the baths because of her legs. They exchanged the words one exchanges, the weather, the water, the food of the house, and at a certain point the woman asked her and you, what work do you do? And Preciosa, without thinking, answered the usual, the answer of thirty years: I'm the key of a spinning mill, I'm the one who opens and closes, I'm the one who sees to everything. And the woman looked at her, and said, with the simplicity of one who did not know what she was touching: ah, poor thing, and here you are at your ease, with the mill in others' hands? And Preciosa opened her mouth to say I am, but the truth came out ahead of it: no, I'm not at ease at all, I'm here dying of worry over it. And the woman laughed, without malice, and said a thing that stayed: then it's the mill that has you, not you that have the mill. And she went back to her coffee, not knowing what she had said, and Preciosa stood looking at her cup, with that settling in, it's the mill that has you, and understood that the stranger of the guesthouse had told her, in three sips of coffee, what she had taken thirty years not to see.

But shame, however much it hurts, is the door one passes through. Because in seeing, at last, what she was rooting for, Preciosa understood that she had to want the contrary. She had to learn to root for the mill to run well without her. Because that, and not the disaster, was what would prove she had done a good job; that was what would be the mark of a true mistress: to leave things running, to leave Aurora capable, to leave the door opening without her key. And that if she wanted to cure herself of that thirty-year sickness, she had to learn to rejoice in the mill running without her, instead of fearing it; she had to want, with true will, to be dispensable; she had to wish, against everything she was, that the door would open without her hand.

And in the last days, as the terror eased, Preciosa began to do things she had not done since childhood, and that astonished her. She went walking by the sea, alone, without aim, only to walk, and discovered that she could spend an hour doing nothing but watching the water, and that time did not punish her for it, nothing fell because she stood still watching the sea. She sat at a terrace café and drank a coffee slowly, watching the people go by, with no haste to finish it and run nowhere, and it was strange to her, and it was good. She bought, in a shop on the street, a coloured scarf, for herself, without needing it, only because she liked it, and it was the first thing she had bought for herself in she did not know how many years, because she always bought for the mill, nails, oil, belts, and never a coloured scarf that served for nothing but to be pretty at her neck. And at night, in the room, she put on the scarf, and looked at herself in the guesthouse's spotted mirror, and saw, for an instant, not the key of the Spinning Mill, but a woman of fifty-four with a coloured scarf, who could, if she wished, come to have a life; and she was afraid of her, of that woman, and curious about her, both things at once.

And she bore it. She bore the week without going back, without telephoning, without rushing to save anything. She bore the terror, the withdrawal, the temptation, the case packed and unpacked. She bore doing nothing, being on the outside, being, for the first time in her life, done without. And each day she bore without going back was a day against thirty years, was the key staying, on purpose, in Aurora's hand, was Preciosa learning by force the thing she had never learned: to be outside, not to be needed, to let the mill run. And by the fourth, the fifth day, in the midst of the terror that was easing, she began to feel, very deep down, beneath everything, a new thing, a thing she had never felt in thirty years: a silence within. A time that was hers and not the mill's. A morning when she woke and lay in bed listening to the birds in the pinewood, without having to get up for anyone, and in which the not having to get up, which had terrified her on the first afternoon, tasted, suddenly, of a strange and warm thing she barely recognised, and that had, she understood with astonishment, a very simple name, a name everyone knows and that she did not: to rest.
