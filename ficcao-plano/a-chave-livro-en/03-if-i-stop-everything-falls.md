## 3. If I stop, everything falls

If I stop, everything falls was Preciosa's phrase, and she said it with pride, like one who states a truth about her importance, and did not see that the phrase, which seemed to describe the mill, described after all her prison, because it is the phrase of all who have made themselves indispensable, and it is always, beneath, less about what would fall and more about what binds the one who says it.

Preciosa said it many times. When they suggested she take a holiday, if I stop, everything falls. When they told her she should rest, that she was exhausted, if I stop, everything falls. When someone offered to help her, to learn to do what she did, it is not worth it, I do it faster, and if I stop, everything falls. And the phrase always closed the conversation, because it seemed unanswerable, it seemed a truth: the mill needed her, she knew everything, if she stopped, who would do it? And so Preciosa, with the phrase, justified never stopping, never delegating, never resting, and no one contradicted the phrase, because it seemed true, and she herself believed it with all her strength.

But the phrase had, beneath it, two lies that Preciosa did not see. The first was that the mill would not fall if she stopped; it would fall perhaps at first, it would creak, it would get into a muddle, like any house when the column sets down, but it would reorganise itself, and would move, because mills, like houses, do not depend on a single person, they depend on it through bad organisation, not through necessity, and a mill in which everything depends on one person is not a strong mill, it is a fragile mill, on the verge of falling on the day that person is absent; and Preciosa, by making herself the single key, had not made the mill safer, she had made it more fragile, dependent on a single person, who was she, and who was not eternal.

And the second lie, the deepest, was that the phrase was not about the mill, it was about Preciosa. When she said if I stop, everything falls, the everything she feared would fall was not only the mill; it was she. It was her importance, her place, her meaning, her identity, which all rested on the indispensability; and if she stopped, and the mill did not fall, then another thing would fall, the illusion that she was indispensable would fall, the pedestal she held herself on would fall, what she was would fall; and so Preciosa could not stop, not because the mill was going to fall, but because she was afraid of discovering that the mill did not fall, that she was not so indispensable as she thought, that she could be dismissed, like the father, and that without the mill she did not know who she was. The phrase if I stop everything falls protected, deep down, not the mill, but Preciosa's illusion about herself.

And there was, in the phrase, a cruel trap: that the more Preciosa made herself indispensable, the more bound she became, and the truer the phrase seemed, in a circle that tightened by itself. Because by not delegating, by keeping everything for herself, by teaching no one, Preciosa became in fact more and more the only one who knew, and therefore the phrase became more and more true, and she more and more bound; and the indispensability fed on itself, because each thing she did not delegate was one more thing only she knew, and one more chain binding her; and Preciosa, after thirty years like that, was so bound that she could no longer even imagine stopping, because the phrase, by dint of her fulfilling it, had become almost true, and the mill did depend on her for almost everything, not for her being irreplaceable, but for her having contrived that it be so.

And there was a moment, in those days, in which Preciosa glimpsed the trap. It was when she heard herself say the phrase, once more, refusing a holiday, and for an instant, instead of feeling it as a proud truth, she felt it as a chain; and she asked herself, for the first time: and if the mill does not fall? And if I stop, and it moves? What does that say about me? And she understood, with a tightening, that the phrase that sustained her was also the one that bound her, and that she was afraid, not of the mill falling if she stopped, but of the opposite, of the mill not falling, because if the mill moved without her, then the phrase was a lie, and her indispensability was an illusion, and she would have to face the question she had avoided for thirty years: if I am not indispensable, who am I? And it was that question, and not the tiredness, that began to push her towards the hardest thing she could do, to hand over the key, and to discover, once and for all, whether the mill fell without her, or whether it was she who did not know how to fall, that is, to stop, without coming undone.
