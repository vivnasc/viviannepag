## 3. If I stop, everything falls

If I stop, everything falls was Preciosa's phrase, and she said it with pride, like one stating a truth about her own importance, and she did not see that the phrase, which seemed to describe the mill, in fact described her prison, because it is the phrase of all those who have made themselves the key of something, and it is always, underneath, less about what would fall and more about what binds the one who says it.

Preciosa said it often, and the mill knew it by heart. She said it to Conceição, the one from the canteen, a stout and kindly woman who set her plate in front of her at noon and who was the only one who dared to scold her, and who said to her often, with the ladle from the pot in her hand: Preciosa, you're eating standing up again, one of these days you'll drop down on me here. And Preciosa, already leaving the canteen with the half-eaten plate in her hand to go and settle who knows what in the shed: if I stop, everything falls, Conceição, I'll eat later. And Conceição would shake her head at the pot and say it's you who'll drop, and she was right, and neither of them yet knew how right.

She said it to Belmiro, when the man, kind of soul, would suggest she leave earlier, that he would lock up: go home, Preciosa, rest, I'll see to this. And Preciosa, without raising her eyes from the machine: and tomorrow I arrive and it's all switched round, Belmiro, it's not worth it, I'll lock up, if I stop everything falls. And Belmiro, who had heard that a thousand times, would shrug and go off, and Preciosa would stay, and in staying she proved herself right, because no one stayed to contradict her, and a phrase that has no one to contradict it becomes, by dint of being said into the void, a kind of truth.

And she said it, above all, when holidays were mentioned to her. There was an August when Otília, from the office, brought her a leaflet for a trip, one of those coach trips, to some baths, with other women her age, and set it on the desk in front of Preciosa, shyly, and said: there was a place going, Preciosa, I thought of you, it was only a week, it would do you so much good. And Preciosa looked at the leaflet, the photographs of the sea and the women laughing, and for an instant, one instant only, she felt something like a longing; and straight after she said the phrase, gently, because she was fond of Otília: ah child, and the mill? If I stop a week, everything falls. What a dear you are, but keep that for yourself, you go. And Otília kept the leaflet, and never brought her another, and Preciosa did not go, that August as in thirty Augusts, and the phrase closed the conversation, as always, because it seemed beyond answer.

And the phrase had not been born with her; it had been built up, year by year, like everything. Preciosa remembered the first time she had said it, still new at the Spinning Mill, in the days when there was still a foreman over her, one Lucas, a rough and drunken man, who missed Mondays and left the mill to its luck. It was on one of those Mondays, with the foreman curing his drink at home, that Preciosa, then little more than twenty, saw the central motor choke and no one know how to take hold of it, and the women workers standing looking at one another, and the day being lost. And Preciosa, who had been learning the motor on the sly, at the end of the day, alone, because Lucas taught her nothing, went there, touched where she had seen it touched, and the motor caught. And the women clapped, and the oldest of them then said this girl is worth more than the foreman, and Preciosa, who had never in her life heard anyone say she was worth anything, felt something enter her chest and stay there forever. That night, on locking up, she said to the oldest, half laughing, half in earnest: now if I don't come, this stops. And it was the first time she said the phrase, and she said it with pride, and did not see that she was making a vow.

Lucas ended up dismissed, years later, for one drunkenness too many, and Preciosa was left with the mill, and the phrase was left with her, and the two grew up together. And the more Preciosa made everything pass through her, the more the phrase came true, in a circle she fed without seeing.

But the phrase had, underneath, two lies that Preciosa did not see.

The first was that the mill would not fall if she stopped. It would fall perhaps at first, would creak, would flounder, like any house when the pillar is removed; but it would reorganise itself, and would run, because mills, like houses, do not depend on a single person out of necessity, they depend on it out of bad arrangement, and a mill in which everything passes through one head is not a strong mill, it is a fragile mill, on the verge of falling the day that head is missing. And Preciosa, in making herself the single key, in keeping the notebook in her pocket and the trick of the gate to herself and the knowledge of the machines only in her own hands, had not made the Spinning Mill safer, she had made it more fragile, hung upon a single nail, which was she, and which was not eternal. The proof of it she had had on the morning of the ache in the chest, when, for an instant, she had been the weak point, and the whole mill had been left depending on the fist letting go of her heart.

And the second lie, the deeper, was that the phrase was not about the mill, it was about Preciosa. When she said if I stop, everything falls, the everything she feared would fall was not the Spinning Mill. It was she. It was her importance, her place, her name bound to that of the mill in the book of the village, her way of being someone, which rested wholly on the key. And if she stopped, and the mill did not fall, then something else would fall, deeper than any mill: there would fall the illusion that she was indispensable, there would fall the pedestal she stood upon without knowing it, there would fall the only answer she had to the question of who she was. That was why Preciosa could not stop, not because the mill would fall, but because she was afraid of discovering that the mill did not fall, that she was not as necessary as she believed, that she could be done without, like her father, and that without the mill she did not know how to be anyone.

And there was, in the phrase, a cruel trap: that the more Preciosa made herself the key, the more bound she became, and the truer the phrase seemed, in a circle that tightened of itself. Because in not delegating, in keeping everything to herself, in teaching no one, Preciosa did in fact become more and more the only one who knew, and therefore the phrase became less and less a lie, and she more and more bound. Each breakdown that Belmiro never got to learn because she mended it in front of him was one more chain; each account that stayed only in her head and not in Otília's books was one more knot; each morning she opened alone was one more day on which no one else learned the trick of the gate. The indispensability fed upon itself, the way the fire feeds on the wood it consumes, and Preciosa, after thirty years like that, was so bound that she could no longer even imagine stopping, because the phrase, by dint of her fulfilling it, had become almost the truth.

And there was a day when the trap showed itself in full, though Preciosa still did not want to see it. She had set out, in a burst of good will, to teach Otília to make the orders, to ease the load; and she sat down with her one afternoon, and began to explain, but halfway through, seeing that Otília did not catch it at once, she grew impatient, and took the pencil from her hand and said leave it, I'll do it and then show you clean; and she did it herself, quickly, and Otília was left watching, and did not learn, and the order was made and the knowledge stayed with Preciosa, as always. And at the end of the afternoon Preciosa said to herself, satisfied, well I did try to teach Otília but she hasn't the head for it, better it be me, and did not see that it had been she who would not let Otília learn, that the impatience had been the brake, and that each time she said better it be me she tightened one more knot in the chain and made the phrase if I stop everything falls a little more true. That was how the circle closed: each attempt to teach interrupted halfway was one more proof, in her eyes, that no one learned, when the truth was that no one learned because she did not let them learn to the end. And the more proofs she gathered that she was the only one, the more the only one she became, and the phrase went on fulfilling itself, like a prophecy that strives to come true.

And there was a moment, in the days that followed the ache in the chest, when Preciosa at last glimpsed the trap. It was on a Friday, at the end of the day, with the shed already emptying. Idalina, who was on her way out, leaned against her desk to tighten her headscarf and said, in her tired voice of an old woman: oh Preciosa, you're staying again. I've been here thirty-two years now, two more than you, and look what I tell you, what no one tells you: the day you're missing, this mill will weep one morning and it'll run by the afternoon, because the world is like that, hard, it goes on without us. Don't kill yourself, woman, the mill weeps no one more than a morning. And she went off, slowly, limping at the hip, and Preciosa stayed sitting at the desk with that driven into the middle of her chest, in the very place the fist had gripped her.

The mill weeps no one more than a morning.

And Preciosa, alone in the shed as it went dark, remembered, with that hurting, Engrácia of the upper mill, a dry woman who had died some years before, and who had said her whole life the same phrase as she, if I stop the mill stops, and who had died clinging to a millstone no one knew how to turn, and whose mill, it was said, had run again two months later at the hand of a nephew from the Porto side who had never set foot in it. She remembered Engrácia, and she shivered, because for an instant she saw herself in her as in a mirror, and did not like what she saw.

And for the first time, instead of hearing her phrase as a proud truth, she heard it as a chain. And she asked herself, there alone, in the shed going dark: and what if the mill does not fall? What if I stop, and it runs? What does that say of me? And she understood, with a tightening she already knew, that the phrase that held her up was also the one that bound her, and that she was afraid, not of the mill falling if she stopped, but of the contrary, of the mill not falling, because if the mill ran without her, then the phrase was a lie, and the key was illusion, and she would have to face the question she had avoided for thirty years, ever since the tin standing still on the shelf: if I am not indispensable, who am I? And it was that question, and not the weariness, and not even the ache in the chest, that began to push her, slowly, towards the most difficult thing she could do, to hand over the key, and discover, once and for all, whether the mill fell without her, or whether it was she who did not know how to stop without coming undone.
