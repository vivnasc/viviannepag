## 8. Handing over the key

And Preciosa handed over the key. It was the greatest gesture of the book, and it was harder for her than any work of thirty years, because to hand over the key was not only to give a piece of iron to someone. It was to let go of the thing that made her who she was, it was to risk discovering herself dispensable, it was to put into another's hands the door that had been her life.

She decided to hand it to Aurora, and it made sense that it should be to her, for two reasons that crossed: because it was to her she owed the most, for having braked her so many years, and because it was in her, and in no one else, that the future of the trade lay. She called her to a corner of the shed, on a Thursday at the end of the day, and Aurora came, wiping her hands on her smock, with that caution of one who has often been reproved and no longer knows whether what is coming is a scolding or an errand. And Preciosa said to her the thing she had never said to anyone in thirty years:

"I'm going away a week. On holiday, in earnest. Without telephone, without notebook, without coming back for any breakdown. And the mill stays with you."

Aurora stood looking at her, not understanding, waiting for the rest, for the catch, for the but in fact.

"With you, Aurora," Preciosa repeated. "You open in the morning, lock up at night, see to whatever there is, decide whatever's needed. I'll teach you, in these days, everything you need. And then it's yours."

And the girl, who had waited years for a phrase like that, and to whom the phrase had always been denied, was left without words, and her eyes filled, and she was ashamed of her eyes, and Preciosa pretended not to see, and the two of them were, in that very instant, closer than they had been in all the years together.

And the days before leaving were, for Preciosa, an agony and a revelation. She set herself to teach Aurora what she had never taught anyone, to open, one by one, the drawers she had kept shut for thirty years. And it was by that that she discovered the two things that most shook her.

The first was in the trick of the gate. She took Aurora to the gate, in the morning, before the others, and showed her: you see, half a turn, draw the leaf towards you with the knee, and only then turn it the rest of the way, otherwise it bites the key. And Aurora took the key, and at the first try did exactly what she should, half turn, knee, and the gate gave way with its usual groan and opened. At the first try. For thirty years Preciosa had kept that as a secret, as a thing that only she knew, and Aurora learned it in one gesture, and Preciosa stood looking at the open gate, and felt a strange thing, between pride and loss, because the secret was, after all, no secret, it was only a thing she had never let anyone learn.

The second was the notebook. She took the black notebook from the pocket of her smock, the notebook that had slept in her house for thirty years, and set it on the bench in front of Aurora, and said, with a tightening in her throat: this is the notebook. It's all here, the orders, the deadlines, the ways of the clients, which one pays at thirty days and which is late but pays. It stays with you this week. And Aurora looked at the notebook the way one looks at a sacred thing, and did not dare take it up at once, and Preciosa had to push the notebook half a hand's width towards her, and it was, that push of half a hand's width, one of the most difficult gestures she made in her life, because it was the pocket emptying, it was the house ceasing to take the mill home to sleep, it was the secret going out of her hands.

There was one lesson, in particular, that stayed. Preciosa took Aurora to the central motor, the heart of the mill, the machine she had always mended in front of everyone without letting anyone put a hand to it, and said, the voice costing her: come here. She set her hand on the gear, and this, Aurora, this is what I never let anyone learn. When it chokes, it's almost always here, at this point, do you feel? And Aurora put her hand where Preciosa showed her, and felt, and Preciosa explained everything to her, slowly, without haste, without interrupting her, without taking her hand away, and Aurora listened the way one receives a sacred thing, because she knew, without being told, that that motor was Preciosa's most guarded secret, and that Preciosa, in showing it to her, was handing over not a machine, but the trust she had denied for thirty years. And when Aurora, at the end, did alone what Preciosa had shown her, and the motor answered to her hand, the two of them stood watching the gear turn, in silence, and it was one of the most serious moments of the lives of the two of them, because there, in that motor, the key passed from one hand to the other long before it passed in fact.

And in teaching, she discovered what hurt her most: that Aurora learned quickly, too quickly, that she took up everything in an instant, that she was capable, far more capable than Preciosa had wanted to admit; and that if she learned so quickly it was because she had always been capable, and that Preciosa had braked her not because she was not ready, but out of fear of letting her be ready. Each thing she passed to her, Aurora caught it out of the air, and Preciosa saw, in those days, the living proof of everything the Keeper had told her: that it had not been the apprentice who fell short, it had been the mistress who would not let her. And she discovered, at the same time, with a tightening, that in teaching Aurora she was making herself dispensable, step by step, undoing with her own hands the wall she had built for thirty years; and that this hurt her and eased her at the same time, the pride hurt and the chain eased, and the two things, the hurt and the ease, fitted into the same gesture, as they always do when one lets go of a thing that has bound and defined us.

And there was, on the eve of leaving, a thing Preciosa did not expect, and that cost her in a different way. At the end of the day, with the news already spread through the mill that Preciosa was going on holiday for a week, a thing never seen, the oldest gathered at the office door, half-awkward, and Idalina, who was their voice, said: so it's true, Preciosa, you're really going? And Preciosa said I'm going, Idalina, a week, and Idalina shook her head slowly and said, with those failing eyes that yet saw everything: it was more than time, woman. Thirty-two years I've been here with you, and it's the first time I've seen you give the key to anyone. Go and rest, the mill won't run off, nor will we. And Belmiro held out his hand to her and said go without worrying, Preciosa, and Conceição appeared with a parcel, a cake she had made on the sly, for the journey, and Preciosa, who had never known how to receive, stood there, with the cake in her hands, at the office door, with all those people wishing her good rest, and understood, with a knot in her throat, a thing that disarmed her: that they wanted her to go. That she should rest. That she should stop. That it was not they who bound her to the key; it was she. The whole mill was there, pushing her gently out, and the one who held her back was she herself, and no one else, and never had been anyone else.

And the afternoon came to hand over the key in person, and what Preciosa felt was almost physical. She took the key off the ring, the great key of dark iron, the key of the Spinning Mill, the one she had carried for thirty years on the cord she changed herself, the key with the head shaped like a rough heart, the one that was she. She slipped it off the cord, slowly, and stood with it in her hand, closed, warm from her hand, and Aurora held out hers, open, waiting, and Preciosa would not let it go.

She stood there a moment that Aurora respected, saying nothing, with her hand out. And Preciosa, with the key shut in her fist, felt the instinct of thirty years cry out inside her, with the voice of the fear, with the voice of the tin standing still on the shelf: take the key, it's yours, without it you're nothing, stay, say after all no, say you've changed your mind, I'll stay. And she almost said it. She opened her mouth to say in the end leave it, I'm not going, and it was the most difficult thing of her life not to say it.

But she did not say it. She opened her fist, slowly, and laid the key in Aurora's open hand, and on seeing the key pass from her hand to the girl's, on seeing Aurora's fingers close over the dark iron, she felt a thing she had not expected: she felt naked, stripped, diminished, as though they had taken a part of her body from her, a finger, a rib, because the key had been a part of her for so long that her hand, without it, was strange, light, wrong. And she had to close her own empty hand to make it stop searching for the key that was no longer there.

"Take good care of her," she said, her voice trembling. "Of the key. And of the mill. I trust you."

And in saying I trust you, she understood that she was saying more than that. She was saying it's not only you who receives the key; it's I who learn to let it go. The I trust you was also, and above all, an I trust myself: an I trust that I'm able not to be the key, to be only Preciosa, to go away and have the mill run and not come undone. Aurora closed her hand over the key, and said only, under her breath, with her eyes filling again: I'll take care, Preciosa. Go and rest. And Preciosa nodded, because she did not trust her voice.

She went off that afternoon with the ring of keys lighter, without the one of the Spinning Mill, and it was the first time in thirty years that she left the mill without her key in her pocket. On the way, her hand went of itself to the pocket, searching, and did not find it, and Preciosa shut it in a fist to teach it to be empty. She felt, going down the street, an enormous emptiness, in her hand and in her chest, the emptiness of the key she had let go; and beneath the emptiness, very deep down, a thing she did not yet know how to name, and that was the beginning, timid, frightened, barely born, of a freedom.

The night before leaving was the longest. Preciosa packed her case, and unpacked it, and packed it again, because she had never packed a case for herself, only for no one, and did not know what a woman takes who is going to rest, and she put in too much and things that were no use, and halfway through she caught herself wanting to put in the black notebook, out of force of habit, and had to take it out of the case and leave it on the table, and to look at it on the table, outside the case, cost her the way it costs to leave behind a part of the body. She lay down and did not sleep, and the room filled with that fine current that runs through houses where someone is about to leave, and Preciosa, lying with her eyes open, did all the reckonings of what could go wrong at the mill, one by one, and then, by force, made herself stop doing them, and was left only listening to the silence of the house, rehearsing a thing she had never done: being nowhere, seeing to nothing.

And she left. She went off the next day, early in the morning, with the case badly packed, on the coach to the big town, without telephone, without notebook, without key. The coach left the square at seven, and Preciosa, at the hour she usually stood opening the gate of the Spinning Mill, was, for the first time in thirty years, sitting on a coach seat with a case at her feet, watching the village file past the window, the square, the fountain, the corner of the mill street, and she had to make an effort not to look towards the mill, not to search for the chimney, not to see whether Aurora had opened; and she looked ahead, at the road, and the coach pulled away, and the village was left behind. And while the coach drew her away from the village, and from the mill, and from the door that had been her life, Preciosa went with her heart leaping, with the terror of having handed over the key, with the phrase hammering inside her to the rhythm of the wheels, if I stop everything falls, if I stop everything falls. And she went too, beneath the terror, with a new question, that of the whole book, the one that was to have its answer that week, and that was, at one and the same time, the thing she most feared and the thing she most needed to know: will the mill fall without me? And if it does not fall, who am I, who gave thirty years to being the key of a door that opens, after all, without me?
