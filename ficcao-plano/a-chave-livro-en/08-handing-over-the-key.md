## 8. Handing over the key

And Preciosa handed over the key, and it was the greatest gesture of the book, and it was harder for her than any work of thirty years, because to hand over the key was not only to give a piece of iron to someone, it was to let go of the thing that made her who she was, it was to risk discovering herself dispensable, it was to put into another's hands the door that had been her life.

She decided to hand over the key to the apprentice, the young woman with talent whom she had held back so long, and it made sense that it should be to her, because it was to her that she most owed, for having held her back, and it was in her that the future of the trade lay. She called her, and said to her the thing she had never said to anyone: I am going away for a week. On holiday, in earnest, without a telephone, without coming back for any breakdown. And the mill stays with you. Here is the key. You open in the morning, you close at night, you see to what there is, you decide what is needed. I will teach you what you need before I go, and then it is up to you. And the apprentice, who had waited years for an opportunity that never came, was left without words, because it was the first time Preciosa entrusted her with anything in earnest, let alone the whole mill, let alone the key.

And the days before departing were, for Preciosa, an agony and a revelation. She set about teaching the apprentice what she had never taught, passing her the knowledge she had kept for thirty years, showing her where everything was, how everything was done, the tricks, the ways; and in doing so, she discovered two things. She discovered, first, that the apprentice learned quickly, too quickly, that she grasped everything in an instant, that she was capable, much more capable than Preciosa had wanted to admit, and that if she learned so quickly it was because she had always been capable, and that Preciosa had held her back not for her not being ready, but for fear of leaving her ready; and she discovered, second, with a tightening, that by teaching the apprentice she was making herself dispensable, step by step, undoing with her own hands the indispensability she had built for thirty years, and that this hurt her and relieved her at the same time, it hurt her pride and relieved her chain.

And there was the moment of handing over the key, in hand, to the apprentice, and what Preciosa felt was almost physical. She took the key from the ring, the key of the Mill, the one she had carried for thirty years, the one that was she, and put it in the young woman's hand; and on letting it go, on seeing the key pass from her hand to another's, she felt a thing she did not expect, she felt herself naked, stripped, diminished, as though they had taken a part of her body, because the key was part of her, it was what made her the key, and to let it go was to cease to be the key, was to be left only Preciosa, and Preciosa, without the key, did not quite know what she was; and she had to hold herself not to ask for it back, not to say after all no, leave it, I will stay, because the instinct of thirty years cried out to her take the key, it is you, without it you are nothing.

But she did not ask for it back. She left the key in the apprentice's hand, and said to her, in a trembling voice: take good care of it. And of the mill. I trust you. And on saying I trust you, Preciosa understood that she was saying more than that, she was saying it is not only you who receives the key, it is I who learns to let it go, and that the I trust you was also an I trust myself, an I trust that I am able not to be the key, to be only myself, to depart and the mill to move and I not to come undone; and she went away, that afternoon, with the ring of keys lighter, without that of the Mill, and it was the first time in thirty years that she left the mill without her key, and she felt, on leaving, an enormous emptiness in her hand and in her chest, the emptiness of the key she had let go, and beneath the emptiness, very deep, a thing she did not yet know how to name and that was the beginning, timid, frightened, of a freedom.

And she departed. She went away, on holiday, in earnest, for the first time in her life, without a telephone, without coming back, without the key; and while she moved away from the village, from the mill, from the door that had been her life, Preciosa went with her heart leaping, with the terror of having handed over the key, with the phrase hammering at her, if I stop everything falls, if I stop everything falls; and she went also, beneath the terror, with a new question, that of the whole book, the one that was going to have an answer that week, and that was at once the thing she most feared and the thing she most needed to know: will the mill fall without me? And if it does not fall, who am I, who gave my life to being the key of a door that after all opens without me?
