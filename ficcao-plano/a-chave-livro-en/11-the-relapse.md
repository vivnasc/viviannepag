## 11. The relapse

But one does not undo a key of thirty years all at once, nor with a week of holiday, however well it went; the hand used to the key wants to take hold of it again at the first occasion, the way the drunkard's body wants the glass at the first sorrow, and Preciosa had her relapse. And it came in the form in which the relapses of those who have made themselves the key of something always come: at the first problem Aurora did not know how to solve, Preciosa ran to take hold of the key again, relieved, almost happy, to be needed once more.

It came not long after the holiday, some weeks after. The mill had run without her, yes, but not perfectly, and there were things Aurora did not yet know, and it was natural there should be, because seven days do not undo what thirty years of braking had built. And there was, one morning, a serious problem, of the kind that asks for the experience of thirty years: the boiler, which was the most treacherous thing in the mill, began to lose pressure in a new way, that was none of the usual breakdowns, and Aurora laid her ear to it, and put her hand to it, and did not know, because that was not in the notebook nor had she seen it done; and the shed began to grow cold, and the looms to lose strength, and Aurora, in distress, went to Preciosa at the office, to ask for help.

And Preciosa, on seeing Aurora distressed at the office door, on hearing Preciosa, the boiler, I don't know what's wrong with it, come and see, felt a thing that shamed her and that she could not check in time. She felt joy. A deep, warm, immediate joy, the joy of being needed again, of being the key once more, of proving, with that boiler, that after all she was not so dispensable. And she rose with a leap, faster than she had risen for years, and went to the boiler, and diagnosed the trouble in an instant, which was a valve, and mended it with her own hands, quickly, teaching nothing, showing nothing, only get out of there, Aurora, leave it to me; and the boiler recovered its pressure, and the shed warmed, and the looms came back to strength, and Preciosa felt, wiping her hands on the rag, the enormous relief of one who returns to the familiar prison. Because the prison of the key, however much it bound, was what she knew, was her home, and the new freedom, of being dispensable, frightened her even more than the chain.

And it was Idalina, even before Aurora, who noticed. The old weaver, who saw everything with her failing eyes, leaned against Preciosa's desk one afternoon and said, without going round about, which was her manner: oh Preciosa, you here again at six in the morning? I arrive at eight and the light's already on, like before. The girl saw to the mill a whole week, and you, at the first chance, took it all out of her hands. Did you notice? And Preciosa, defending herself, said it was the boiler, Idalina, the boiler is dangerous; and Idalina shook her head and said the boiler was the excuse, woman, what you wanted was to come back, I saw it in your face, you came running to the boiler like one going to a feast. And she went off, limping at the hip, and left Preciosa with that, you came running like one going to a feast, and Preciosa could not deny it, because it was true: she had risen from her chair with a joy she had not felt in months, and the joy had not been for the boiler, it had been for being needed.

And in a short time everything was as before. Preciosa, without noticing it, had taken up the key again, had gone back to seeing to everything, had gone back to arriving at a quarter to six instead of eight, had gone back to keeping the notebook in her pocket at night. And Aurora, on seeing Preciosa take everything up again, drew back too, stopped deciding, went back to being a helper, sweeping waste, waiting to be called, because that is what always happens when the key takes hold of everything again: the others lean back, because why should they do it, if there is someone who will. And the mill went back to depending only on Preciosa, and the phrase came back to her mouth, and this time with the air of one who had proved herself right: you see, at the first serious thing I was absent for, it nearly fell, just as well I came back. And Preciosa convinced herself that the week of holiday had after all proved her right, that she really was the key, that the mill needed her; forgetting, with the skill of fear, that the mill had run a whole week, seven days, and remembering only the boiler Aurora had not known, to justify the return to the chain.

And it was Aurora, again, who showed it to her, and it was with the courage of the young, who say the simple things the old can no longer manage to say. She came to Preciosa, a few days later, with the key of the boiler in her hand, and said:

"You've relapsed, Preciosa."

Preciosa raised her eyes from the notebook.

"You took everything up again. The boiler, yes, I didn't know it. It's true. But you, instead of teaching me to solve it for next time, you solved it and kept everything to yourself again." Aurora had no anger in her voice, she had clarity, which is worse. "You didn't give me a problem. You took one from me. I came here with a thing I didn't know, which was the chance to learn, and you solved it in front of me without letting me put a hand to it, as you always did, and now I still don't know the boiler either, and we're back at the beginning: you doing everything, and me sweeping waste."

Preciosa opened her mouth to defend herself, to say but the boiler is dangerous, it's not learned in a hurry, and Aurora would not let her:

"The week proved the mill runs without you, Preciosa. Seven days. A boiler I didn't know doesn't prove the contrary. It only proves I still have things to learn, and that you have to teach them to me, not do them for me. If you take everything up at the first thing I don't know, I'll never know it, and you'll never be free, and we'll both stay bound, me small and you tired, forever." And she said, at last, the phrase that Preciosa was to repeat to herself many times in the months that followed: "Teach me the boiler, Preciosa. Don't solve it for me. Don't take the problem from me. Give it to me."

And Preciosa, on hearing don't take the problem from me, give it to me, was silent, with the notebook in her hand, and understood that Aurora was right. That she had relapsed. That she had seized the girl's first stumble, not to teach her, but to take hold of the key again; not because the mill needed it, but because she needed to be needed, and the boiler had been the excuse the vice was waiting for. And she understood, there, a deep thing about handing over the key, that no one had told her and that cost her to learn: that it was not a single gesture, to hand over the key once and be free forever. It was a thing that had to be done every day, against the habit of thirty years, which always comes back, at the first occasion, at the first distress of others, wanting to take hold of everything. That to delegate was not a day, it was a discipline; and that what mattered was not never to relapse, because that was impossible, no one undoes thirty years without going backwards; what mattered was to recognise the relapse, and to hand over the key again, and to go back to teaching instead of doing, and to bear that Aurora should stumble and learn from the stumble, instead of taking the stumbles from her to make herself, again, necessary.

And she understood, on looking back, that the relapse had not been only hers; it had been the whole mill's, because the mill too has its habit, and the habit of the Spinning Mill was to lean on Preciosa. It was enough for Preciosa to take hold of the key again for Belmiro to go back to calling her instead of trying, for Otília to go back to asking her instead of searching the books, for Idalina herself to go back to saying fetch Preciosa; and Preciosa understood that if she relapsed, the mill relapsed with her, because thirty years had taught everyone to depend on her, and to unteach a whole mill was even slower than to unteach herself. And that for this reason the handing over of the key could not be only a decision of hers; it had to be a thing she taught the whole mill to accept, day after day, saying to Belmiro you try first, saying to Otília search the books, saying to Idalina fetch Aurora; because a key is only truly shared when those who leaned on it learn not to lean, and that takes its time, and has its backslidings, like everything that is undone from thirty years.

And she went to the boiler, with Aurora, that same afternoon. And this time she did not say get out of there. She said come here, put your hand here, do you feel? It's the valve, when it tightens like this it's because the steam isn't escaping, and you have to loosen it half a turn, no more, because more than half a turn loses all the pressure; see, you do it. And Aurora did it, with her own hand, slowly, and Preciosa stood behind, watching, and it cost her to stand behind, it cost her not to take the key from the girl's hand, but she stood; and Aurora loosened the valve half a turn, no more, and the boiler answered, and the girl looked at Preciosa with a joy Preciosa had seen in her few times, the joy of one who has learned, and Preciosa understood that that joy, that of seeing Aurora learn, was better, far better, than the ashamed joy of being needed that she had felt with the boiler the first time.

And they agreed a thing, there, at the mouth of the boiler, that was to change everything: that Aurora had leave, from then on, to tell her you've relapsed whenever she saw her relapse. Preciosa, who had never in her life given anyone leave to correct her, gave it to that girl, and said: when you see me take everything up again, when you see me arrive at six instead of eight, when you see me take a problem out of your hands, tell me. Tell me you've relapsed, Preciosa, without fear. For I promise to hear you, even if it costs me, even if I scold you at first. And Aurora promised, and kept her promise, and in the months that followed there were many times she came to Preciosa and said only you've relapsed, and Preciosa, at first, still defended herself, but then she breathed, and let go, and handed over again; and it was that word, you've relapsed, said by a girl to whom she had given leave to say it, that became the rope that pulled her back whenever the vice of thirty years pushed her towards the prison.

Because Preciosa understood, with the relapse, a thing she had not known: that no one frees herself alone from a key of thirty years. That one needs someone beside one, someone who sees, someone who says you've relapsed without fear of us, someone to whom we delegate not only the mill but also the right to call us to account; and that having always been the one who held others up had kept her, her whole life, from letting anyone hold her up, and that to learn to be held up, at fifty-four, by an apprentice, was perhaps the most difficult and the most necessary thing of all.

And she handed over the key again, at the end of that afternoon, this time knowing she would have to hand it over many times, day after day, against herself, and that to have Aurora beside her saying you've relapsed, teach me, don't take the problem from me, was the difference between going back to the prison forever and learning, slowly, with backslidings, with stumbles, to be free.
