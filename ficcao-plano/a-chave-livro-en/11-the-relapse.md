## 11. The relapse

But one does not undo a key of thirty years without its wanting to be taken up again at the first thing, and Preciosa had her relapse, and it came in the form the relapses of those who have made themselves indispensable always come in: at the first problem the apprentice did not know how to solve, Preciosa ran to take up the key again, relieved, almost happy, to be needed once more.

It came soon after returning from the holiday. The mill had moved without her, yes, but not perfectly, and there were things the apprentice did not yet know, and there was a problem, a serious one, of the kind that asks for thirty years of experience, and the apprentice, before it, got into a muddle, did not know, went to Preciosa to ask for help; and Preciosa, on seeing the apprentice in a muddle, on being once more necessary, on feeling that after all they still needed her, felt a thing that shamed her and that she could not check: she felt joy. The joy of being needed again, of going back to being the key, of proving that after all she was not so dispensable; and she ran to solve the problem, to take up the key, to take everything back, with an enormous relief, that of returning to the known prison, because the prison of indispensability, however much it bound her, was what she knew, was her safe zone, and the new freedom, of being dispensable, frightened her more than the chain.

And in a short time everything was as before. Preciosa had taken back the key, gone back to seeing to everything, gone back to making herself the only one, and the apprentice, on seeing Preciosa take everything back, retreated too, stopped deciding, went back to being a helper, because that is what happens when the key takes everything up again, the others go back to leaning; and the mill went back to depending only on Preciosa, and the phrase went back to her mouth, if I stop everything falls, you see, at the first thing I was absent it almost fell, it is a good thing I came back; and Preciosa convinced herself that the week of holiday had proved her right, that she was indeed indispensable, that the mill needed her, forgetting that the mill had moved a whole week, and remembering only the problem the apprentice had not known, to justify the return to the prison.

And it was the apprentice, again, who showed it to her, and it was with the courage of the young. She said to Preciosa: you have relapsed. You took everything up again. There was a problem I did not know, it is true, but you, instead of teaching me to solve it for next time, solved it and took everything back. You did not give me a problem, you took one from me. And now we are as before, you doing everything and I learning nothing. The week proved that the mill moves without you, Preciosa. A problem I did not know does not prove the contrary, it proves only that I still have to learn, and that you have to teach me, not do for me. If you take everything up at the first thing I do not know, I will never know, and you will never be free. Teach me the problem. Do not take it from me.

And Preciosa, on hearing do not give me the problem solved, teach me to solve it, understood that the apprentice was right, that she had relapsed, that she had used the apprentice's first stumble to go back to taking up the key, not because the mill needed it, but because she needed to be needed; and she understood a deep thing about delegating: that it was not a single gesture, to hand over the key once and that is that, it was a thing one has to do every day, against the habit of thirty years of taking everything up, which always returns, at the first stumble of the others, wanting to take back the key; and that what mattered was not never to relapse, for that was impossible, it was to recognise the relapse, and to hand over the key again, and to teach instead of doing, and to bear the apprentice stumbling and learning, instead of taking the stumbles from her to make herself, herself, necessary. And she went back to handing over the key, this time knowing she was going to have to hand it over many times, and she understood that to have at her side the apprentice telling her you have relapsed, teach me, do not take the problem from me, was the difference between returning to the prison forever and learning, slowly, with setbacks, to be free.
