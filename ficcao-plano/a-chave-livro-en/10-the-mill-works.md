## 10. The mill works

And Preciosa came back, at the end of the week, and found the thing she most feared and most needed to find: the mill working. It had not fallen. It had run. Aurora had opened in the morning and locked up at night, seven days running, had settled whatever there was to settle, and the Spinning Mill, after a week without the key of thirty years, was standing, spinning, alive.

She came back at the end of seven days, and the seven days had changed her within in a way she did not yet measure, but that could be seen, Conceição said afterwards, in her face, which came more rested and at the same time more frightened, like one who comes back from a foreign land and does not know whether she liked it.

She arrived on the afternoon coach, and instead of going home she went straight to the mill, not letting go of the case, because she could not bear one more minute without knowing. She came down the street slowly, with her heart leaping, and as she turned the corner she saw the chimney of the Spinning Mill against the sky, and the chimney gave off smoke, and the smoke, which had always been the most ordinary thing in the world, was, that afternoon, an almost unbearable thing to look at, because the smoke meant the machines were running, and the machines were running without her. She stopped at the corner, with the case in her hand, and stood looking at the smoke, and did not know whether to laugh or to weep.

She went in through the gate, which was open, thrown wide, at the right hour, and the shed received her with its usual noise, the beating of the looms, the hum of the central motor, the smell of oil and thread and stone. Everything was running. Idalina, at her loom, raised her eyes and saw her, and opened a broad smile, and shouted over the noise: Preciosa, you're back! And it ran through the shed, from one machine to the next, the news that Preciosa had come back, and the women waved to her, and Belmiro came to wipe his hands on the rag and shake hers, and Conceição came out of the canteen with the ladle from the pot in her hand saying glad to see you with a better face, woman; and Preciosa, in the midst of it, with the case still in her hand, understood a thing that ran through her from top to bottom, and that was astonishment and was mourning at the same time, the two together, which were the whole book in a single blow: the mill was glad she had come back, but the mill had not needed her to run.

Astonishment, because she did not believe it. Because thirty years had told her, with the voice of the phrase, that without her the mill fell, and there was the mill, standing, run, without her, seven days, the living proof that the phrase was a lie. And mourning, because if the mill ran without her, then she was not the single key, and the indispensability that had been her life, her pride, her way of being someone, revealed itself, in that mill that had not fallen, less true than she believed; and to discover herself dispensable, after thirty years of making herself the key, was a mourning, the mourning of the illusion about herself that she had fed her whole life. The two things came together, and could not be separated, and Preciosa stood there, in the middle of the shed, feeling them both, and did not know which hurt more, the astonishment or the mourning, because they were the same wound seen from two sides.

And she learned, that same afternoon, that Magalhães had appeared, in the middle of the week, on his usual visit, and had found the mill running and Preciosa absent, and that Aurora had received him, and shown him the accounts, and served him as Preciosa served him. And Magalhães, Idalina told her, laughing, was left open-mouthed, asked after Preciosa, and when they told him she was on holiday, in earnest, without telephone, he made a face never seen on him, between astonishment and discomfort, because one thing was Preciosa indispensable and at ease was he; and he went off earlier than usual, and on the way out said, with a smile that was no longer the same: well, so the mill does run without Preciosa. And Preciosa, on hearing that, understood a fine thing about Magalhães: that he had not been glad the mill had run. He had been uncomfortable. Because a Preciosa who discovered the mill ran without her was a Preciosa who might one day ask for what she deserved, or go off, or demand the second foreman, or stop doing the work of two for a single wage; and it suited Magalhães better to have the key bound than the mill running, because the bound key was his saving. And Preciosa kept that, the face of Magalhães on learning she had truly gone away, and it was, in the midst of the mourning, a small secret victory.

Aurora came to her, from the far end of the shed, wiping her hands on her smock, and she still had the key of the Spinning Mill on its cord, and Preciosa looked at the key in the girl's hand and had to breathe deep. But Aurora, before giving it back, told her the week, and there was, in Aurora's telling, a delicacy and a truth that touched Preciosa more than anything.

"It went well, Preciosa," said Aurora. "I won't lie to you, it didn't go as you would do it. On the Monday the gate bit the key and I was a quarter of an hour in the rain until I found the knack, and when I found it it was the knee, it was as you said, but I'd forgotten. On the Wednesday Idalina's loom broke down, in earnest, and I was frightened, and I thought what would Preciosa do, and I laid my ear to it as I'd seen you do, and it was the third pinion asking for oil, and I oiled it, and the loom ran again, and Idalina said oh, and I almost wept there in the middle of the noise." She laughed, with her eyes shining. "There was a client from the big town who called me complaining about the sheet cloth, and I went to the notebook, and it was there, in your writing, pays at thirty days, tends to be late but pays, and I spoke to him as you would speak, calmly, and he calmed down, and the cloth went out on the Thursday as the notebook said. I missed your experience in a great deal, Preciosa, and there were days when I'd have given anything to be able to call you. But the mill ran."

And she made a pause, and said the thing Preciosa took home that night and kept for the rest of her life:

"And you know something? You are not less important because the mill ran without you. You're more. Because the mill ran this week because of everything you taught me in these last days, and of what I went on learning, even braked, all these years. When the loom broke down, it was I who oiled it, but the ear for the pinion I learned from watching you. You have not ceased to be needed, Preciosa. You've changed the way of being so." And she held out the key to her, in her open hand. "Do you want me to give it back, or shall I keep it until Monday?"

And Preciosa, on hearing Aurora, and on looking at the key in the girl's open hand, understood a thing that began, there and then, in the middle of the shed, to turn the mourning into something else, that did not yet have a name but was no longer mourning. She understood that to be dispensable was not to cease to be worth something. That the mill having run without her did not wipe out the thirty years in which she had made it run, nor the ear Aurora had learned from watching her, nor the notebook where her writing went on speaking to the clients even in her absence. That her worth did not vanish because she was not irreplaceable. And that there was, after all, a way of being needed that was not the prison of the single key: it was that of the mistress, the one who teaches, who leaves behind her someone to carry on, who makes the mill able to run without her, and who is needed not for being the only one, but for having shaped the others; and that this, the mistress's way, was a far greater way of being worth something than the key's, because the key dies with the door, and the mistress lives on in all those she taught, in every ear she tuned, in every trick she passed on. Aurora had oiled the third pinion. Preciosa was in that gesture, and would be in it forever, even on the day she was no longer there.

And then, over that afternoon and the following days, the week came to her by the mouths of the others, in pieces, and each piece was to her at once a joy and a small death. Idalina told her, at the end of the day, leaning against the loom: the girl Aurora on the Monday was in a fix, poor thing, with the gate biting her, but on the Wednesday, when my loom broke down, she went there so serene, laid her ear to it like you, Preciosa, just the same, and I shut my eyes and for an instant it was you standing there, I swear; the girl has your hand. And Preciosa heard your hand and smiled on the outside and within it hurt her a little, because her hand was now in another, and it was good, and it was strange, both things. Belmiro said to her, wiping his hands: it went, Preciosa, it went. There were a couple of things I sorted out myself, that with you here I never got to touch, and after all I did know how, I'd just never been let. And that, I'd just never been let, said without reproach, by a mild man, entered Preciosa like a fine needle, because it was true, and the truth was hers. Even Conceição, from the canteen, said to her, ladle in hand: and you know the girl ate sitting down all week, at the table, like people, unlike you who've eaten standing for thirty years? The mill ran, and there was even someone who sat down to lunch. And Preciosa laughed, and it was a wet laugh, because Conceição, without knowing it, had just measured, in a sitting lunch, everything she herself had never allowed herself.

"Keep it until Monday," said Preciosa to Aurora, and her voice trembled, but not from loss. "And on Monday you open. I'll arrive at eight, like everyone."

Aurora looked at her, and understood the size of that, and said nothing, because there were things that were not said, and closed her hand over the key, and went back to the loom.

And Preciosa stood in the middle of the shed, with the case still in her hand, watching the mill working before her, and understood, at last, the truth the phrase had hidden, the one the Keeper had sown in her with the old key: that it was not the mill that fell without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill. That the if I stop everything falls had never been about the Spinning Mill; it had always been about Preciosa, about the fear that, on stopping, there would fall not the mill, but the illusion that she was the key, and with it the only identity she had known ever since the tin standing still on the shelf. And that the mill, in running without her, had not destroyed her. It had set her free. It had taken the chain from her, and given her back the question she had avoided for thirty years, the question that was now, at last, her crossing: if I am not the key, if the mill runs without me, if I am dispensable like everyone, then who am I?

And for the first time, there, in the midst of the noise of the looms, with Idalina spinning and Aurora with the key and the smoke rising from the chimney, that question, which had always terrified her, did not terrify her at all. Because beneath it there was now, timid, barely born, but breathing, the possibility of a new answer, that she did not yet know how to say whole, but that began like this: I am a person. Not a key. A person, who is worth something for being, and not for what she opens.

She left the mill, that afternoon, still with the case in her hand, and instead of going straight home she stopped in the yard and stood looking at the Spinning Mill from outside, the chimney smoking, the windows lit, the noise of the looms coming out through the cracks, all of it alive, all of it running, without her key in her pocket. And it was a strange moment, because for thirty years Preciosa had looked at that mill the way one looks at an extension of one's own body, and now she looked at it the way one looks at a thing one loves and that is separate from us, that has a life of its own, that goes on without us. And instead of the pain she had expected, she felt, beneath the mourning, a thing almost like the pride of a mother who sees her child walk alone for the first time and who knows that that walking is, at one and the same time, the loss of him and the proof that she raised him well. The Spinning Mill walked without her. And she had taught it to walk, even without wanting to, even fighting against it, and now, at last, she let it go. She took the case and went down home, slowly, and her hand, for the first time in thirty years, did not go to her pocket searching for the key, because the hand was beginning, too, to learn to be empty without feeling lost.
