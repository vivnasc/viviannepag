## 10. The mill works

And Preciosa returned, at the end of the week, and found the thing she most feared and that she most needed to find: the mill working. It had not fallen. It had moved. The apprentice had opened in the morning and closed at night, had solved the problems, had managed everything, and the Mill, after a week without the key of thirty years, was standing, spinning, alive.

And it was astonishment, and it was mourning, at the same time, and the two together were the book. Astonishment, because Preciosa did not believe it, because thirty years had told her that without her the mill fell, and there was the mill, having moved, without her, the living proof that the phrase was a lie; and mourning, because if the mill moved without her, then she was not indispensable, and the indispensability that had been her life, her pride, her identity, revealed itself, in that mill that had not fallen, less true than she believed, and to discover herself dispensable, after thirty years of making herself indispensable, was a mourning, the mourning of the illusion about herself that she had fed her whole life.

And the apprentice came to her, glad, to tell how it had gone, and there was, in the apprentice's telling, a delicacy and a truth that touched Preciosa. The apprentice said: it went well, Preciosa. There was a breakdown, and I thought of what you would do, and I managed to mend it. There was a problem with a client, and I solved it. It did not go as well as you would do it, I lacked your experience in some things, and there were days on which I missed being able to call you; but the mill moved, Preciosa, and I learned more in this week than in all the years in which you let me do nothing. And do you know a thing? You are not less important for the mill having moved without you. You are more. Because it was you who taught me, who let me, who trusted. The mill moved this week because of all you passed on to me. You did not cease to be needed. You changed the way of being so.

And Preciosa, on hearing the apprentice, understood a thing that began to turn the mourning into liberation: that to be dispensable was not to cease to be worth anything; that the mill having moved without her did not erase the thirty years in which she had made it move; that her worth did not disappear by her not being irreplaceable; and that there was, after all, a way of being needed that was not the prison of indispensability, it was that of the mistress who teaches, who leaves behind her those who continue, who makes the mill able to move without her, and who is needed not for being the only one, but for having trained the others, and that that, that of the mistress, was a far greater way of being worth than that of the single key, because the single key dies with the door, and the mistress continues in those she taught.

And she understood, with the mill working before her, the truth the phrase hid, the one the Keeper had sown in her: that it was not the mill that fell without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill; that the if I stop everything falls had never been about the Mill, it had been about Preciosa, about the fear that, on stopping, there would fall not the mill, but the illusion that she was indispensable, and with it the only identity Preciosa had known; and that the mill, by moving without her, had not destroyed her, it had freed her, because it had taken from her the chain of indispensability and given her back the question she had avoided for thirty years, the question that was now, at last, her crossing: if I am not the key, if the mill moves without me, if I am dispensable like everyone, then who am I? And for the first time that question, which had always terrified her, did not terrify her altogether, because beneath it there was now, timid, the possibility of a new answer: I am a person. Not a key. A person, who is worth by being, and not by what she opens.
