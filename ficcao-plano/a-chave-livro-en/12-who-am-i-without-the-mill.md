## 12. Who am I without the mill

And the day came when Preciosa understood, with her whole body and not only with her head, what the phrase had hidden, and it was the climax of the book, without festivity, as those of Véspera are: she understood that it had never been the mill that would fall without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill, and that her true crossing was not to save the Spinning Mill, it was to find herself, on the far side of the key.

It was seen over the months, slowly, with backslidings. As Preciosa came to delegate in earnest, day after day, against the habit, teaching Aurora the boiler and the motor and the ways of the clients, sharing out what she knew, letting the mill become able to run without her; as she passed the black notebook to a drawer of the office, in plain sight of all, instead of taking it home in her pocket; as she arrived at eight, like everyone, and let Aurora open the gate with the trick that was now hers; as all this happened, the mill ceased to depend only on her. And Preciosa, instead of the relief she had expected, felt the emptiness the Keeper had foretold her.

It was an emptiness that came in the afternoon, above all. She left the mill at a normal hour, for the first time in her life, and arrived home with the afternoon still standing, with light, with hours before her, and stood at the door of the house not knowing what to do with them. For thirty years the mill had filled her days to the last drop, and now the days opened before her, empty, and the emptiness, which ought to have been freedom, was anguish. She sat at the kitchen table, with the tea going cold, and the silence of the house weighed on her, because it was a house she had never inhabited by day, a house she knew only by night, for sleeping, and now she had to inhabit it awake, and did not know how. There were afternoons when she rose to her feet and went to the mill, with nothing to do there, only because there she knew how to be, and caught herself leaning against a loom watching the spinning, with her feet itching to take hold of the key, and had to force herself to go back home, to bear the emptiness, as she had borne the terror at the guesthouse of the baths.

And that, Preciosa understood, was the true crossing, the one the phrase had hidden from all the others. It was not learning to delegate, which she was already doing, with stumbles. It was learning to be someone without the mill. It was discovering, after thirty years of being the key, who Preciosa was, the person, beneath the key. And it was the most difficult thing of her life, more difficult than handing over the key, more difficult than the week at the guesthouse, because the Preciosa-person was buried under thirty years of Preciosa-key, and to dig her out was to begin from the beginning, was to ask herself, at fifty-four, things that everyone asks themselves young and that she had never had the time nor the courage to ask. What do I like, outside of work? What do I want, for myself, that is not for the mill? Who am I, when I am not opening and closing a door? And she did not know, at first. She had no answers. Because she had never asked herself, because the mill had always answered for her, had always given her something to serve, had always spared her the meeting with herself.

It was around that time that the body took her to the new doctor at the centre, on account of the ache in the chest that had come back once or twice, and the doctor, a woman from away, young, who asked things in a way that was not used in the village, listened to her heart and took her blood pressure and then stood looking at her and asked, just like that, out of nothing: and you, what do you do to rest? And Preciosa, who was not expecting the question, was silent, and the doctor waited, and Preciosa ended by telling the truth, which astonished her herself: I don't know. I don't know how to rest, doctor. I never learned. And the doctor did not laugh, nor say it was an old woman's answer, she wrote a thing on the paper and said, gently: then that is what you're going to learn, which is more important for your heart than any pill I can give you. And Preciosa came out of the centre with the prescription in her hand, and the prescription did not name any remedy, it said, in her head, one thing only: learn to rest. And it was the most difficult prescription to fill of her life, more difficult than opening a mill for thirty years, because the work she knew by heart, and rest was a language no one had ever taught her.

And she began, slowly, awkwardly, as everything begun late is begun, to dig out the person. She accepted, one Sunday, the invitation Conceição had been making her for years and to which she had always said no, and went with her and other women on an outing to the serra, and sat on a stone in the sun eating bread and cheese with people who laughed, and discovered, with astonishment, that she liked it, that she knew how to laugh, that she had spent thirty years believing she had no time for what she did not even know whether she wanted. There was one of the women on the outing who asked her, in conversation, and you, Preciosa, what do you like to do, of your own? And Preciosa opened her mouth to answer and did not know, and had to invent, and said I like to sew, which was a lie, and then blushed, alone, because she understood that she did not know how to answer the simplest question in the world, what do I like, and that to be fifty-four and not know how to answer that was the exact measure of what the key had taken from her. She put some seeds in a box of earth on the kitchen sill, remembering her mother counting coins and her father talking of times of waiting, and discovered the slow span of watching a thing be born that she did not have to hurry, that grew alone, without her hovering over it, as the mill had learned to grow. At first, with the habit of thirty years, she went to look at the seeds hour by hour, stirring the earth, wanting to hasten what cannot be hastened, until one afternoon she heard, in her head, the voice of one who had told her, somewhere in her life, that what is sown is worked well and then left in peace, covered, unstirred, for it grows alone, and one who uncovers it hour by hour only manages to keep it from growing. And she stopped stirring the earth, and left the seeds in peace, and they came, in their own time, two green threads breaking the dark earth, and Preciosa stood looking at them with a disproportionate joy, the joy of one who understands, with two threads of grass, an enormous thing: that there are things that grow better when one gets off them, and that perhaps that was the secret she had denied Aurora for thirty years, and herself her whole life.

And she came to know the house by day, which she had never known. She discovered that the afternoon light came in a certain way through the kitchen window, golden, around five, and that it was beautiful, and that she had lived there forty years without ever having seen that light, because at five in the afternoon she was always at the mill. She discovered the silence, which at first grazed her like a threat and which she came to learn to hear the way one hears company. She discovered that she knew how to cook for herself, slowly, things that gave her pleasure and not only haste, and that to eat sitting down, at the table, with time, as Conceição had told her Aurora had learned to do in the week of the holiday, was a good thing she had denied her own body for thirty years of eating standing up. And each of these small things, the light, the silence, the food sitting down, was a piece of the person being dug out, slowly, with care, the way one digs out an old and fragile thing that has been long underground and that can break if we pull it quickly. She went, one August, at last, to the baths, alone, with time, without a badly packed case and without a telephone, and this time she did not come back halfway. And she went to the chest of drawers, before leaving, and took from the dark depths the bathing costume she had bought twenty years before for a sea she never went to, with the tag still on it, and Preciosa looked at that tag, twenty years on, and unfastened it with her fingers, slowly, and it was like unfastening twenty years. She put the bathing costume in the case, and took it, and put it on, and put her feet in the water, and stood looking at the sea, which needed her for nothing, and wept a little, not quite knowing why, or knowing: for the Branca who never invited her again, for all the Augusts she had not had, for the bathing costume that had waited twenty years in the dark, and for that day, at last, being hers. And it was strange and it was good to feel the cold water rise up her legs, she who for thirty years had only put her hands in machine oil, and she understood, with her feet in the foam, that the sea had been there all the while, waiting, like life, and that it was enough to have stopped once to find it whole.

And she went, at last, to the big town, one Sunday, to her sister's house, Júlia's, after so many years of this Sunday I can't. She caught the morning coach, with a cake wrapped in a cloth, and Júlia opened the door and stood looking at her the way one sees an apparition, and said only you, and embraced her, and Preciosa, who was not one for embraces, let herself be embraced. The nephews and nieces, grown now, whom she knew only from the photographs with the date on the back, called her aunt with the ceremony of strangers, and by the end of the afternoon they were calling her aunt in another way, and Preciosa spent the whole Sunday in a house that was not the mill, doing things that served for nothing, peeling potatoes with her sister in conversation, listening to the nephews and nieces argue, laughing, and went back on the night coach with the cake cloth empty and a thing full in her chest, and understood, looking out of the window at the dark road, that that, a Sunday in a house, with people who were hers, for nothing, without her being needed for anything, had been the richest thing that had happened to her in years, and that she had had it there, a coach ride away, for thirty years, waiting, while she said this Sunday I can't.

And there was an afternoon when she went back to the House of the Register, with no mill papers this time, only to go, and the Keeper received her, and Preciosa told her that she had handed over the key, that the mill had run, that she was learning to be a person. And the Keeper heard her, without astonishment, because she already knew, for it was her trade to know, and when Preciosa finished, she went to the shelf and took the box with the old key, the one of the fallen house, and put it on the table between the two of them, as the first time. And she said: do you remember what this key was not worth? And Preciosa said I remember, it was worth nothing, it was a piece of iron, because the door had fallen. And the Keeper smiled, and said: well, now notice. You handed over your key, and the door did not fall, it ran; but you did not become a piece of iron either, did you? You discovered you were worth something without the key. That is the difference, Preciosa, between a key and a person: the key, without the door, is nothing; the person, without the trade, is all that she ever was, only now she knows it. And she put the old key away in the box, and Preciosa came out with that, light, because this time the truth of the key no longer weighed on her, it served her now.

It cost her. There were days when the emptiness pushed her back to the mill, to the key, to being needed, and she relapsed, and went, and had to go out again. But she went on learning, slowly, with backslidings, to be a person, and not a key; to be worth something for being, and not for what she did; and to discover that the Preciosa who had been left unlived in the shadow of the Spinning Mill existed, after all, buried but alive, and that it was worth coming to know her, and that she had, before her, years to live her.

And there was a day when Preciosa went up to the attic to look for the blue tin. She did not quite know why, but she needed to hold it in her hand. She went through the old boxes, among the dust and the cobwebs, and found it at last, at the bottom of one, wrapped in a cloth, the tin of enamelled iron, blue, with the chipped lid, the same one her mother filled in the morning and washed at night, the same one that had stood still on the shelf the whole year of her father without work. She took it out, wiped the dust from it with her sleeve, and stood holding it, sitting on the attic floor, and the tin weighed in her hand the way the key of the Spinning Mill had weighed for thirty years. And Preciosa understood, with that tin in her hands, that she had spent her life between two objects: the tin standing still, which was the fear, and the key held fast, which was the defence against the fear; and that she had never had, between the two, a thing that was only hers, that was neither fear nor defence. She took the tin to the kitchen, and washed it, and did not put it away again; she left it on the shelf, in plain sight, and from that day it was in it, in her father's tin, that she put the seeds left over, so that the tin that had been the clock of distress should come to keep what was to grow, which was her way, without words, of telling her father and the child she had been that there was no fear there any more.

And it was on one of those afternoons, sitting at the kitchen table, with the box of seeds greening on the sill and her father's tin full of earth beside it, that Preciosa understood the last truth, the one that answered, at last, the year of her father. She remembered the blue tin standing still on the shelf, and her father covering his face with his large hands, saying I'm no use for anything any more. She had learned, as a child, seeing that, that without work a man loses his worth, his dignity, his being; and she had made herself the key so as never to lose the work, so that they would never say to her what they had said to her father, so as never to be dispensable as he had been. But she understood now, at fifty-four, at the kitchen table, what the child of eleven had not been able to understand: that her father, on losing the work, had not lost his worth. That he had gone on being her father, being worth something, being loved, even without the tin going out in the morning. That her mother had knelt at his feet and said you are of use to me, you are of use to these daughters, and that this was true, and that it had been the fear, and not the truth, that made her father weep and say I'm no use any more. Her father had always been worth something, with work or without it. It was the child who had not been able to know it.

And she understood that she had spent her life fleeing a false lesson, that worth lies in what one does, when the truth was the contrary: that worth lies in who one is. That her father had always been worth something, with the tin full or standing still on the shelf; and that she was always worth something, with the key in her hand or in Aurora's drawer; and that the key she had built to ensure her worth had been, after all, what had kept her from discovering that her worth had never depended on being the key, that it was in her, in the person, and not in the dark iron of the gate of the Spinning Mill.

And there came the Sunday when she understood she was cured, or on the way to being, and it was a Sunday like any other, without festivity, in the manner of Véspera. She woke, and it was Sunday, and the mill was shut, and Preciosa, by the habit of thirty years, felt her foot wanting to go down to the deserted mill, to check what needed no checking, to be where she knew how to be. And she rose, and dressed, and took her coat, and went to the door. And at the door she stopped. And instead of going down to the mill, she turned the other way, and went off to the serra, alone, with bread and cheese in a bag, and spent the Sunday on a stone in the sun, looking at the village down below, the little Spinning Mill with its chimney without Sunday smoke, and she did not feel any wish to go there. For the first time in thirty years, on a Sunday, Preciosa was far from the mill by choice, and not by punishment, and the Sunday did not fall on her empty, it filled itself with sun and serra and a peace she did not know; and on going down, at the end of the afternoon, she understood that she had not gone down to the mill even once, and that she had not missed it, and that this, that small nothing, was the greatest thing that had happened to her, because it was the proof that she no longer needed the key to know how to be in the world.

And she looked at the mill, in thought, now able to run without her, Aurora grown, the notebook in the drawer in plain sight, the key shared out; and she looked at it without the sick pride of indispensability and without the fear of being dispensable, which were the same thing seen from two sides. And she felt, for the first time in thirty years, that she was not the key, she was Preciosa. A woman who had given thirty good years to a mill, who had taught, who had left someone to carry on, and who had now, at last, the right to be also a person, with a life, with afternoons, with likings, with seeds on the sill and sea on her feet; and who was worth something for that, for being, and not for being irreplaceable.

And there was a morning, near the end of all this, when Preciosa arrived at the mill at eight, as she had come to arrive, and found the gate already open, and Aurora finishing starting the machines, and she stopped an instant at the gate, and looked at the jamb. And she saw that the groove, the mark of the hand, the one into which she had sworn no other hand would fit, now had, beside hers, a second groove, newer, lighter, still beginning: the mark of Aurora's hand, which had been opening for months, and which was engraving in the iron, slowly, its own shape, beside the shape of Preciosa's hand. And Preciosa ran her thumb over the two grooves, hers and Aurora's, side by side on the same jamb, and instead of the pain she would have expected to feel, she felt a deep and quiet thing, because she understood that the gate had ceased to keep a single hand, and that this, which for thirty years had seemed to her the greatest of losses, to have another hand fit where only hers had fitted, was after all the greatest of proofs that she had done, in the end, the right thing: to leave in the iron, beside her own, room for another hand.

And she understood that to hand over the key, which had seemed to her the loss of everything, had been after all the beginning of everything, because it had given her back to herself, the person who had been left unlived. And that the true phrase, the one that came to replace the old, the one she would say from then on, was not if I stop everything falls. It was another. It was if I stop, I discover, at last, who I am. And that she, in stopping, in handing over the key, in letting the mill run, in bearing the terror and the emptiness and the relapse, had not lost everything. She had gained herself, which was the one thing that, in thirty years of being the key of a door, she had never had in her hands.
