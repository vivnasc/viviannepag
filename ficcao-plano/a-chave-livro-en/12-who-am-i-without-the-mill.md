## 12. Who am I without the mill

And the day came on which Preciosa understood, with her whole body, what the phrase hid, and it was the climax of the book, without a feast, as those of Véspera are: she understood that it had never been the mill that would fall without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill, and that her crossing was not to save the mill, it was to find herself, on the far side of the key.

It went on showing itself, over the months, as Preciosa, slowly, with setbacks, went on delegating in earnest, teaching the apprentice, sharing the knowledge, letting the mill become able to move without her. And as the mill ceased to depend only on her, Preciosa, instead of the relief she expected, felt the emptiness the Keeper had foretold: the emptiness of one who let go of the thing that made her who she was, and who finds herself, suddenly, with time, with holidays, with life, and does not know what to do with it, because she never had any, because the mill had taken up everything, and without the mill filling her days, the days opened empty, and the emptiness, which should be freedom, was anguish, the anguish of one who does not know how to be anyone without what she did.

And it was that, Preciosa understood, the true crossing, the one the phrase had hidden: it was not to learn to delegate, for that she already did; it was to learn to be someone without the mill, it was to discover, after thirty years of being the key, who Preciosa was, the person, beneath the key. And it was the hardest thing of her life, because the Preciosa-person was buried under thirty years of Preciosa-key, and to dig her up was to begin from the beginning, was to ask herself, at fifty-four, things everyone asks young and that she had never had time to ask: what do I like, outside of work? What do I want? Who am I, when I am not opening and closing the mill? And she did not know, at first, she had no answers, because she had never asked herself, because the mill had always answered for her, had always given her a what-to-be-for, had always spared her the meeting with herself.

And she began, slowly, to dig up the person. With the mill moving without her, with the time she had never had, Preciosa began to try things she had never done: to have free afternoons, and to learn to be in them; to discover likings, small ones, that the mill had eaten; to have, at last, a life outside the key; and she was clumsy, as is everything begun late, and it cost her, and there were days on which the emptiness pushed her back to the mill, to the key, to being needed; but she went on learning, slowly, to be a person, and not a key, to be worth by being, and not by what she did, and to discover that the Preciosa left unlived in the shadow of the Mill existed, after all, and was worth knowing, and that she had, ahead, years to live her.

And she understood the last truth, the one that answered the year of the father. The father had lost his work, and she had learned, as a child, that without work a man loses his worth, his dignity, his being; and she had made herself indispensable so as never to lose her work, never to be dispensable like the father. But she understood now, at fifty-four, what the child could not understand: that the father, on losing his work, had not lost his worth; that he had gone on being her father, being worth, being loved, even without work; that it had been the fear, and not the truth, that had told her that without work a man is worth nothing; and that she had spent her life fleeing a false lesson, that the worth is in what one does, when the truth was the contrary, that the worth is in who one is, and that the father had always been worth, with work or without it, and that she was always worth, with the key or without it, and that the indispensability she had built to guarantee her worth had been after all what had kept her from discovering that her worth had never depended on being indispensable, that it was in her, in the person, and not in the key.

And she looked at the mill, now able to move without her, the apprentice grown, the key shared, and she looked at it without the sick pride of indispensability and without the fear of being dispensable; and she felt, for the first time in thirty years, that she was not the key, she was Preciosa, a woman who had given thirty good years to a mill, who had taught, who had left those who would continue, and who had now, at last, the right to be also a person, with a life, with time, with likings, and who was worth by that, by being, and not by being irreplaceable. And she understood that to hand over the key, which had seemed to her the loss of everything, had been after all the beginning of everything, because it had given her back to herself, the person left unlived, and that the true phrase, the one that replaced the old, was not if I stop everything falls, it was another, it was if I stop, I discover, at last, who I am; and that she, by stopping, by handing over the key, by letting the mill move, had not lost everything, she had gained herself, which was the one thing that, in thirty years of being indispensable, she had never had.
