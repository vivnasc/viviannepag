## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Preciosa, daughter of the village, who was the key of the Spinning Mill for thirty years, who opened before all and closed after all, who never took whole holidays because if I stop everything falls, learned, at fifty-four, the hardest thing for one who has made herself indispensable: to hand over the key, and to let the mill run without her, and to bear the terror of watching, and the astonishment of seeing that it ran. Be it set down that she discovered, on the far side of the fear, what the phrase was hiding: it was not the mill that fell without her, it was she who did not know who she was without the mill; and that, in handing over the key, she did not lose everything, she gained herself.

The register keeps who holds the trades of the village, and there are names that appear bound to a place as though they were of it, without one knowing where the person ends and the trade begins. This Keeper saw, in the book, the name of Preciosa bound to the Spinning Mill for thirty years, as though they were one; and saw, at last, them part, the mill running without the key, the apprentice growing, and Preciosa appearing, for the first time, in the book, not as the key of a door, but as a person, with a life that was hers. And the Keeper understood that Preciosa had fulfilled the name at last, but not as she had built it: she was not precious for being indispensable to a mill, she was precious for being who she was, and that was worth something with the key or without it.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Preciosa or other who has made herself indispensable, who opens before all and closes after all, who takes no holiday because if I stop everything falls, who confused what she is worth with what she does, let her know what she learned and what this house records: that no one is indispensable, and that one who thinks herself so is bound, not safe; that the phrase if I stop everything falls does not protect the mill, it binds the person, and hides the fear of discovering that the mill runs without us; that the greatest worth is not to be irreplaceable, it is to leave those who carry on; and that the greater courage, for one who is the key of something, is not to hold it any longer, it is to hand it over, and to see the door open without us, and to cross the mourning of not being indispensable, in order to discover, on the far side, the person who was left unlived in the shadow of the trade, and who is worth, at last, not for what she does, but for being.

Preciosa traded the single key for the shared key, indispensability for freedom, the mill that defined her for the person she discovered herself to be. And in the houses of Véspera, since the one in which she handed over the key for a week and the mill ran, there is one more woman who no longer confuses her worth with her usefulness, because she learned that no one is indispensable, and that this, which seems a loss, is the greatest of liberations, that of being able, at last, to stop, and to discover that one is worth something by being, and not by never being able to be done without.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
