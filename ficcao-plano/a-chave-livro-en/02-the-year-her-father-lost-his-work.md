## 2. The year her father lost his work

Preciosa made herself indispensable for a reason that came from far away, from a year of childhood, the year her father lost his work, and it was in that year that she learned the fear that governed her life, and that made her cling to the mill like one who clings to a plank so as not to sink again.

She was young, and the house, in that year, lived what houses live when the breadwinner loses his work: the trouble, the want, the fear, the humiliation of having nothing. The father, who was a good man, hardworking, lost his job, by a factory that closed, or a master who dismissed him, or some misfortune of the kind that takes a man's work; and the house, all at once, was left without ground, without the money that came in, with the bills piling up, with the larder emptying, with the father going out in the morning in search of work and coming back at night with nothing, more and more bent, more and more silent, more and more diminished by the lack of work, which to a man of that time took more than money, it took his dignity, his place, his being.

And the child Preciosa saw all this, and it marked her forever. She saw the mother's trouble doing sums; she saw the larder emptying; she saw the father diminishing, day after day, without work; she saw the house living the fear of having nothing, the deepest fear of the poor houses, that of being left without; and the child, in the midst of that fear, decided a thing, without words, with her whole fear, as one decides the things that last a life: she decided never again. Never again, in her life, would anyone at home go through that, the lack of work, the empty larder, the diminished father; and the way the child found to ensure that, without knowing how to formulate it, was to make herself indispensable, to make herself so necessary in a work that never, never could she be done without, so that what had happened to the father would never happen to her, to be dismissed, to be left with nothing.

And it was with that fear, and with that decision, that Preciosa entered the Mill, young, and that she made herself, over the years, the key. Because what for others was ambition, or zeal, or love of work, for Preciosa was, deep down, fear: the fear of being dismissed, as the father had been; the fear of being left with nothing, as the house had been; and to be indispensable was the wall against that fear, it was to ensure that they could never send her away, that they would always need her, that the humiliation and the want she had seen in the father would never happen to her. And Preciosa made herself, for that reason, more than good, she made herself indispensable, not only by talent, but by terror, the terror of one day being dispensable and reliving, in herself, the year of the father without work.

And there lay the error, which was no one's error, it was the error of fear: that Preciosa, by making herself indispensable so as never to be dismissed, bound herself to the mill in a way that kept her from having a life outside it; by ensuring that they always needed her, she ensured that she could never stop, never rest, never have anything else, because indispensability demands constant presence, demands that everything pass through her, demands that she always be there; and so Preciosa traded the fear of being dispensable for a prison, that of being indispensable, and did not understand, for thirty years, that the two were daughters of the same fear, and that she, by fleeing from being like the father, dispensable, had made herself the slave of the opposite, indispensable, and that neither of the two was freedom, that freedom was another thing, it was to be worth without depending on being needed, it was to be someone even without the mill, and that Preciosa did not know, because since the year of the father she had learned to be worth only by being indispensable.

And when, at fifty-four, exhausted, with the body warning her, Preciosa was forced to look at her prison, it was to that year that she had to return, because it is always to the fear that one has to return to understand it; and on remembering the father without work, the empty larder, the terror of being left with nothing, she understood, for the first time, that her indispensability was not merit, it was fear, it was the child of then still trying, thirty years later, to ensure that she would never be dismissed; and she understood that if she wanted, at last, to free herself, she would have to do the most terrifying thing she could imagine, the thing against which she had built her whole life: she would have to make herself dispensable, on purpose, to hand over the key, to let the mill move without her, to risk discovering that she was, after all, dispensable, in order to discover, on the far side of that terror, that to be worth is not to be indispensable, and that she could be dispensable and be worth all the same, like the father, who had lost his work and not for that ceased to be her father, and to be worth.
