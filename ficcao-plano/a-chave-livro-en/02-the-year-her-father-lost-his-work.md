## 2. The year her father lost his work

That night, after the ache in the chest, Preciosa did not sleep, and the question born of her in the dark of the mill did what true questions do: it went opening, one by one, all the doors of memory, as far as the first. And the first door was a year of childhood, the year her father lost his work, and it was in that year that she learned the fear that was to govern her life, and that made her cling to the key of the Spinning Mill the way one clings to a plank so as not to sink again.

She was eleven. Her father was called Joaquim, and he was a locksmith at the foundry workshop of the Alto, a man of large hands and few words who left the house before the sun with his lunch tin under his arm and came back at dusk with the tin empty and his nails black with filings, and who had, in his chest, a silent pride: that of putting bread on the table, every day, without fail, owing no one. The tin was of enamelled iron, blue with a chipped lid, and her mother, Ermelinda, filled it every morning and washed it every night, and that tin going in and out of the house was, in Preciosa's childhood, the surest clock in the world: while the tin went out full and came back empty, the house went on.

The workshop closed one October. Preciosa remembered the word that was said in the house that autumn, closed, spoken as though they were saying died, and she remembered her father coming home that afternoon without the tin under his arm, because there was no longer a workshop in which to open it, and her mother looking at his empty hands at the door and understanding, before he spoke, and saying only, under her breath, God help us, Joaquim. The tin stayed on the kitchen shelf, washed, with the chipped lid turned upwards, and there it stayed that whole year, without going out, without coming back, and Preciosa, a child, would look at the tin standing on the shelf and knew, without anyone telling her, that while that tin did not go out again in the morning the house was not well.

Her father went out just the same, at first. He went out in the morning, without a tin, looking, and went knocking at the doors of the foundries of the big town, of the warehouses, of the building works, and came back at night, and her mother would ask him then, with hope trembling in her voice, and he would say tomorrow they'll let me know, or that man said he'd see, or there's still work out there, and would go and sit by the hearth without taking off his coat, silent, looking at the fire. And Preciosa, from the corner where she did her schoolwork, saw her father stoop from week to week, fall silent, grow smaller, and did not understand, a child, that a man of that time, on losing his work, lost more than the wage: he lost his place at the table, his voice in the house, the right to give an order, the way of walking down the street with his head up. Her father began to pull his hat lower when he met people he knew, like one begging pardon for existing without earning, and Preciosa saw, and kept it.

Her mother did the sums at night, at the end of the table, by the weak light, with a pencil she wet on her tongue and a sheet of wrapping paper where she added and crossed out and added again. Preciosa pretended to sleep on the pallet beside her and saw, between half-shut eyes, her mother counting coins from a tea tin, separating them into little heaps, this for the bread, this for the light, this for the rent of Sô Abílio who would not wait, and passing her hand over her brow, and beginning again. There was a night when her mother had no way of making the little heaps reach all the corners, and she went very still, with her head in her hands, and Preciosa heard her say under her breath, to the empty tea tin, another month like this and I don't know, and that phrase, another month like this and I don't know, entered the child and never left again, because it was the first time she heard, in her mother's voice, that the floor of a house can give out.

And the pantry emptied. Preciosa, who was sent to Romana's grocery with the errand and the tea tin, learned that year the shame of asking for credit. Her mother sent her with the phrase rehearsed, tell senhora Romana your father will pay as soon as things mend, and Preciosa would go into the grocery with her eyes on the floor and say the phrase, and Romana, who was a good woman, would mark it in her notebook and give the bread and the oil, but there was always, in the shop, someone who heard, and Preciosa felt the eyes of the village on her back the way one feels cold rain, and she would go home with the bread under her arm and her face burning, and she made that year, without words, with her face burning up the street, the promise that was to govern her life: never again. Never again will anyone in my house ask for credit with a lowered face. Never again.

There was, in the middle of that winter, the night of Sô Abílio, which Preciosa kept her whole life without ever telling it. Sô Abílio was the landlord, owner of the house and of half the street, a man with a gold chain across his waistcoat who came to collect the rents in person, on Sundays, so that the whole neighbourhood might see him collect. The rent had fallen two months behind, because the tin stood still on the shelf, and on a night of rain there was a knock at the door, and it was he, with his umbrella dripping and the gold chain shining in the lamplight. Her mother sent Preciosa and her sister to the bedroom, but Preciosa stayed to spy through the crack of the door, and saw and heard everything. She saw her father, standing in the middle of the kitchen, without his coat, smaller than Sô Abílio though he was taller, saying one more month, Sô Abílio, give me only one more month, and I'll find work and pay it all, I swear. And she saw Sô Abílio look her father over, slowly, up and down, and say, without raising his voice, which was worse than if he had raised it: oh Joaquim, I don't live on oaths. This house brings me rent, and a man who doesn't work is no use to me as a tenant. You have until the end of the month. After that it's the street, and it wasn't I who put you out of work.

And he went off, with his umbrella and his chain, leaving the door open to the rain, and her father stayed standing in the middle of the kitchen, without moving, looking at the floor, and her mother went to shut the door and leaned against it, and neither of them said anything, and Preciosa, behind the crack, at eleven, heard, in the silence, the heart of the house beating like that of a cornered animal. They did not go out into the street, at the end of the month, because her mother sold her grandmother's earrings and her father found a few days' work breaking stone, and the rent was paid by a hair. But Preciosa never forgot the phrase a man who doesn't work is no use to me, spoken with the gold chain shining, nor the way her father went small in the middle of his own kitchen; and it was that night, more than any other, that taught her that to be without work was to be at the mercy of whoever had the gold chain, was to be liable to be put out into the rain, was to depend on the kindness of men who had none. And the child decided, behind the crack, that never, never would some Sô Abílio or other knock at her door to say you're no use to me; and the way to ensure it was to be so necessary that it was she who had the key, and not the others.

There was another day, in that year, that Preciosa also never told anyone, and that was the cornerstone of everything. Her father had gone, in the morning, to a foundry in the big town, where he had been told there was a vacancy, and came back in the middle of the afternoon, earlier than usual, and Preciosa was at the door and saw his face before he said anything. Her father came in, set his hat on the table, and her mother stopped sewing and looked at him, and her father said, in a voice Preciosa had never heard from him, a voice washed clean of everything: they gave it to a younger man, Ermelinda. They told me I'm getting on in years. And he sat down, and covered his face with his large locksmith's hands, the hands that had worked iron a whole life, and wept. Preciosa, from the corner, saw her father weep for the first and only time in his life, and what marked her was not the weeping, it was what he said between his hands, under his breath, more to himself than to his wife: I'm no use any more, Ermelinda. I'm no use for anything any more. And her mother knelt at his feet and took his hands from his face and said to him you are of use, Joaquim, you are of use to me, you are of use to these daughters, and her father shook his head, not believing, because he had learned, as Preciosa was to learn from him, the false lesson that seems the truest of all: that a man is worth what he does, and that without work he is of no use.

Preciosa, in the corner, at eleven, heard her father say I'm no use for anything any more and there, in that instant, made the reckoning she was to make her whole life without noticing it. If her father, who was good, who was hardworking, who was the most upright man she knew, could be put out and left to think he was of no use, then being good was not enough. One had to be more. One had to be so necessary that they could put no one in our place, not a younger man, not anyone, because the place was ours in such a way that to remove us was to dismantle the whole house. One had to be the key. And the child Preciosa, with no words for this, with all the fear standing in for words, decided that she would be.

The year ended by passing, as bad years pass. Her father found work the following spring, at a smaller locksmith's, earning less, and the blue tin went out again in the morning and came back empty at night, and the house righted itself, but it was never the same again, and her father never again walked down the street as upright as before. And Preciosa kept the tin in memory the way one keeps a relic and a warning, and when, years later, she entered the Spinning Mill, young, with the fear already turned to bone, it was with the blue tin behind her eyes that she began to make herself the key, to learn everything, to keep everything, to make herself so necessary that never, never would they say to her what they had said to her father, that he was getting on in years, that they'd given it to a younger man, that he was no use any more.

Because what for the others, in the mill, was zeal, or ambition, or love of the trade, for Preciosa was, deep down, this: the fear of the day her father came back without the vacancy and said I'm no use any more. To be the key was the wall against that day. And Preciosa made herself, for that reason, more than good, she made herself indispensable, not only by talent, but by terror, the terror of one day being expendable and reliving, in herself, the afternoon her father covered his face with his hands.

And there lay the error, which was not her error, it was the error of the fear: that Preciosa, in making herself the key so as never to be put out, bound herself to the mill in a way that kept her from having a life outside it; in ensuring that they would always need her, she ensured that she could never stop, never rest, never have anything else. She traded the fear of being expendable for a prison, that of being indispensable, and did not see, for thirty years, that the two were daughters of the same fear, and that she, in fleeing from being like her father, expendable, had become a slave to the contrary, and that neither of the two was freedom. Freedom was another thing, it was to be worth something without depending on being needed, it was to be someone even without the key, and that Preciosa did not know, because ever since the year of the tin standing still on the shelf she had learned to be worth something only by being necessary.

There was a detail of that year that Preciosa only now, in bed, joined to all the rest. When her father at last found work at the small locksmith's, in spring, he came home that afternoon with a thing in his hand, and it was a key. The key of the locksmith's, which the master had entrusted to him, and her father came into the house and set the key on the table, before his wife and daughters, and said, in a voice that had become his own again: they've given me the key, Ermelinda. They trust me with the key. And her mother looked at the key on the table the way one looks at a miracle, and the house breathed. And Preciosa, at eleven, learned there, without words, one more piece of the fear that was to govern her: that to have the key was to be trusted, was to be needed, was to have one's place assured; that the key was the contrary of the street in the rain and of Sô Abílio and of I'm no use any more; that whoever held the key of a thing was not put out. And no wonder, she thought now, lying in the dark, no wonder at all that her whole life she had run after a key, and that she had clung to that of the Spinning Mill as to the plank of salvation, because the first key she had seen in her life was the one that had given her father back to himself, the one that had pulled the house from the edge of the street, the one that had said, on a poor kitchen table, they need you again.

She rose before dawn, without having slept, and went to the kitchen to drink water, and stood at the window looking at the dark serra, and thought of her father, dead now many years, and of her mother, and of the blue tin, which must still be about in some box up above, forgotten. And for the first time she put to her father, in her head, the question she had never put to him in life: and you, father, were you worth less for being without work? And she knew the answer at once, knew it in her chest before her head: no. Her father was worth the same, without work as with work, was worth it for being her father, for those large hands, for that voice that said few things and all of them true; and it had been the fear, and not the truth, that told him that afternoon he was no use any more. But the child had not been able to know this. The child had only seen the tin standing still and her father covering his face. And it had taken her fifty-four years, and an ache in the chest, and a key weighing in her hand in the dark of a mill, to begin, at last, to suspect that she had spent her life fleeing a lesson that was false.
