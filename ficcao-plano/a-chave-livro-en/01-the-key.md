## 1. The key

Preciosa had the key of the Spinning Mill, and the key was more than a key: it was she. For thirty years it had been Preciosa who was the first to arrive, in the morning, to open the gate, to start the machines, and the last to leave, at night, to switch everything off, to lock up; and in those thirty years the key of the mill had stuck to her hand in such a way that one no longer knew where Preciosa ended and where the Mill began.

It was Preciosa who knew where everything was, in the mill. She knew on which shelf each part lay, how each machine was mended when it broke down, which supplier delivered what, on which day, what was owed to which client; she knew the tricks of each loom, the ways of each gear, the stories of each worker; and when there was a problem, whatever it was, it was Preciosa who was called, because Preciosa knows, Preciosa solves, Preciosa sees to it; and so, year after year, the mill had built itself around Preciosa, dependent on her for everything, and Preciosa had become the key without which the Mill did not open, did not move, did not close.

And Preciosa had in this her pride, the greatest of her life. To be indispensable was what she was, was what gave her her place, her importance, her meaning; to arrive in the morning and be the first, to have the key, to open the mill, to set everything going, to be sought all day long to solve this and that, to be the one without whom nothing was done, was the thing that filled her, that made her feel herself someone, that justified her life; and each time she solved a problem that only she knew how to solve, each time the mill needed her and she came, Preciosa felt a deep satisfaction, that of being indispensable, of being worth, of not being able to be done without.

But there was, beneath that pride, a thing Preciosa did not see and that was to be the book: that she had not made herself indispensable only because she was good; she had made herself indispensable on purpose, without knowing it, contriving that everything pass through her, not teaching anyone to do what she did, keeping the knowledge, the keys, the tricks for herself, so that no one in the mill could do what she did, and that she was, for that reason, irreplaceable; and that irreplaceability, which she lived as merit, was in part a construction, a wall she had raised around herself, so that no one could do without her, so that they would always need her, because to be needed was, for Preciosa, the only way of feeling herself safe.

And the price of that wall was enormous, and Preciosa paid it without complaint. She never took whole holidays, because if she were absent a week the mill did not move, or she thought it did not move; when she fell ill, she organised the illness so as not to be absent, went to work ill, took work home; she had no life outside the mill, no time, no hobbies, almost no relationships, because the mill took up everything, and she let it take it up, because without the mill she would not know what to do with herself; and the years went by, and Preciosa, at fifty-four, had given her whole life to the Mill, and had nothing else, not a husband, not children, not a life of her own, only the mill, only the key, only being indispensable, which was all she was.

And the body, at fifty-four, was beginning to warn her. The tiredness of thirty years without whole holidays weighed on her; the pains, the tension, the exhaustion of always being the first and the last, of having the whole mill on her back, were beginning to exact their due; and Preciosa, who put off everything that was hers, put off the rest too, the caring for herself, because to stop was impossible, if I stop everything falls. And there was a day, at the start of the book, on which the body forced her to look at the phrase that sustained her: she felt unwell, in earnest, at the mill, and for an instant she had to stop, to sit, to be unable; and in that instant of being unable, Preciosa had a thought that frightened her more than the indisposition: if I fall, for good, what becomes of the mill? And then the other, the one the book had been keeping for her, and that was worse: if the mill moves without me, what becomes of me? And it was the first time that Preciosa suspected that the phrase if I stop everything falls was not only about the mill, it was also, and above all, about her, about the fear of discovering that the mill moved without her, and that she, without the mill, did not know who she was.
