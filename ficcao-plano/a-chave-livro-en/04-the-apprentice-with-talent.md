## 4. The apprentice with talent

There was, at the Spinning Mill, an apprentice with talent. Her name was Aurora — dawn — and she had entered the mill at nineteen, come from the serra side, with quick hands and watchful eyes, and within the first month it was plain that she had a gift, of the good kind, the kind that is not taught. And Preciosa held her back, without knowing that she held her back, and it was that girl, more than anyone, more than Idalina or Belmiro or the Keeper, who showed her, at last, the ruin the key had wrought.

Aurora came from the serra side, from a poor house, the eldest of many brothers and sisters, and she brought into the mill a hunger that Preciosa recognised at once, because it was her own, the same as when she had entered young with the blue tin behind her eyes. The girl always arrived before the hour, always stayed after, and in the early days Preciosa saw her, at the end of the day, prowling about the stopped machines, touching them slowly, trying to understand alone what they would not let her learn by day; and instead of being softened by that hunger, which was her own, Preciosa, without understanding why, grew irritated, and said girl, don't go meddling with the machines after hours, you'll spoil them, and Aurora would let go, and go off, and Preciosa would be left with something stuck crosswise in her that only years later she could name: fear. Fear of that hunger, because that hunger was capable of anything, was capable of learning the whole mill, was capable of making her, Preciosa, dispensable.

Aurora learned quickly, too quickly. She had only to see a thing once to know how to do it, and sometimes she saw Preciosa do it and understood at once the why of it, which was what no one else understood. One day, still at the start, with Aurora there only a few months, a shuttle broke on Idalina's loom, and Idalina was calling Preciosa, as always, but Preciosa was on the other side of the shed, and Aurora, who had seen Preciosa do that just once, weeks before, went there, took out the broken shuttle, fitted another, tuned the tension by the sound, and the loom ran again. Idalina was left open-mouthed and said oh, and when Preciosa arrived, now no longer needed, she found the loom spinning and Aurora wiping her hands on her smock, content, waiting for praise.

And the praise did not come. Something else came, that Preciosa did not understand she was doing. There came a who told you to meddle with Idalina's loom, said without apparent anger but with something tight inside; and a next time you call me, that has tricks you don't yet know; and Aurora, who had expected a well done, received a reproof, and lowered her eyes, and did not understand, and no one understood, and Preciosa herself did not understand, that what had just happened there was not the apprentice putting herself where she ought not, it was the mistress feeling, for the first time, the cold at the back of her neck: because if Aurora mended a loom alone after seeing it once, then Aurora was capable, and if Aurora was capable, then Aurora was, one day, a possible replacement, and Preciosa, without thinking it in these words, shut the door she ought to have opened.

There was a day, still in that first year, that stayed hurting in Aurora and that Preciosa only much later understood. The girl, at night, at home, bent over a notebook by the weak light, had done a thing: she had copied, from memory, what she had managed to glimpse of the thread accounts, and added them up, and redone the calculations of the order of sheets, for herself, only to learn. And she brought the notebook to the mill, proud, and showed it to Preciosa, saying: look, Preciosa, I did the accounts of the order, and they came out the same as yours, see. And Preciosa took the notebook, looked at the numbers, exact, exact to the last figure, in Aurora's still-round writing, and instead of saying very well, girl, you have the head for this, she felt the tightening, and said: and who told you to go copying my accounts? These are mill matters, they're not for going about in your notebooks at home. And she shut the notebook, and gave it back, and Aurora carried it away with her face burning, and never again showed Preciosa anything she did on her own account, and Preciosa, years later, on remembering that notebook with the exact accounts, that round writing, felt a deep shame, because she understood that the girl had brought her, that day, the proof that she was capable, and that she, instead of embracing it, had punished her for being capable.

And so it was, from then on, and for years. Aurora asked, with the hunger to learn of the young: let me come to the boiler with you, Preciosa, let me see how you tune the motor, teach me to do the thread accounts. And Preciosa always gave her the lesser tasks, and kept the important ones, and said, with the phrase that was sister to the other: I do it quicker. She set her to tidy the thread, sweep the waste, take the messages to the office, and when the thing was serious, the big breakdown, the difficult account, the awkward client, it was get out of there, Aurora, leave it to me, and Aurora would get out, and stand behind watching, and watched badly, like Belmiro, because one who only watches never learns, and Preciosa knew it, and that was why she only let her watch.

And Preciosa said to herself, when her conscience pricked her, that the girl was not yet ready, that it took years to learn, that she herself had taken years. And they were excuses, and she almost believed them, because fear is skilled at disguising itself as prudence. The truth, which she would not see, was another: that to teach Aurora to do what she did, to pass on her knowledge, to let her become capable, was to create with her own hands the person who would make her, Preciosa, dispensable. It was to open, from within, the very door against which she had raised her whole life. And Preciosa held Aurora back not because she had no gift, but because she had too much gift, because she was the threat to the wall, the one person in the mill who, if she learned everything, would turn the phrase if I stop everything falls into a lie.

And there was in this a cruelty that Preciosa did not see and that would have shamed her had she seen it: that she was doing to Aurora the contrary of what a mistress does. A mistress teaches, passes on what she knows, makes the apprentice grow, rejoices when the apprentice surpasses her, because the trade lives on in her. And Preciosa, instead, kept the knowledge, braked the growth, kept Aurora small, so as to keep herself irreplaceable, and in doing so robbed the girl of her future, and robbed the trade of its continuity, because a knowledge that is not passed on dies with the one who has it. There was even a day when she saw it in passing, and put it away at once: it was when Aurora, after some years, still sweeping waste, said to her with a sad smile I've been here five years now, Preciosa, and I still sweep the floor, and Preciosa felt a pang, and said patience, child, each thing in its time, and the time never came, because the time was hers to hold.

The turning point came on a winter afternoon, with the mill already dark outside and the shed emptying. Aurora had stayed to help Preciosa lock up, a rare thing, because Preciosa did not like company for the locking up, and at a certain point, with the machines already hushed and only the central motor cooling, the girl, who had been gathering courage for months, let fall the cloth she had in her hand and said, all at once, with the forthrightness of the young, which is the cleanest thing there is:

"You won't let me learn."

Preciosa stopped, with the key already in her hand to switch off the motor.

"I have a gift, you know I have. Everyone knows it, Idalina knows it, Belmiro knows it. And you don't teach me what matters. You always keep the important things, you send me to sweep, you send me to tidy, and when there's a serious breakdown you tell me get out of there. Why, Preciosa? What are you afraid of?"

"I'm afraid of nothing," said Preciosa, and the voice came out harder than she wanted. "What I have is a mill to lock up."

"You're afraid I'll learn," said Aurora, under her breath, and it was not an accusation, it was astonishment, it was a girl understanding a thing out loud as she said it. "You're afraid I'll come to do what you do. I don't want your place, Preciosa. I've told you, and I'll tell you again: I don't want your place. I want to learn. You ought to want to teach me. You ought to take pride in leaving someone to carry on what you know, instead of taking it all with you. And instead you brake me, as though I were an enemy." She picked the cloth up from the floor, folded it, set it down slowly. "And that, forgive me for saying it, is not zeal. It's fear of ceasing to be needed."

The central motor finished cooling. There fell, in the dark shed, a silence of the kind that has weight. And Preciosa stood there, on her feet, with the key in her hand, and on hearing it's fear of ceasing to be needed she felt, at the back of her neck, the same cold as the afternoon of the shuttle, but this time she could not push it away, because the girl had just said, out loud, to her face, the thing she had never said to herself in thirty years.

She did not answer at once. She switched off the motor, and the two of them stood in the dark, and Preciosa heard herself say, in a voice she barely recognised as her own:

"And if I taught you everything, Aurora. Everything. And one day they no longer needed me. What would I be, then?"

Aurora was silent a moment. And then she said, with the simple wisdom the young sometimes have and the old have lost:

"You'd be the woman who taught me. That's not little, Preciosa. It's the opposite of little."

And there was, days after that conversation, a small scene that entered her through the crack Aurora had opened. Preciosa, saying nothing to the girl, set herself to watch her, from afar, for a whole morning, to see her work as she had never seen her, without reproving her, only watching. And she saw Aurora help a new worker, Belinha, who was floundering with the warper, and saw how Aurora took her hand and showed her, slowly, without haste, come, it's like this, do you feel the tension? you do it; and she saw Belinha learn, there, in front of her, in five minutes, because Aurora let her put her hand to it. And Preciosa, on the other side of the shed, was left with that hurting her in a new way, because she had just seen, in Aurora, the mistress she herself had never been: Aurora taught as she breathed, hand in hand, letting the other do, and Preciosa, in thirty years, had never taken anyone's hand to show them anything, had only said get out of there, leave it to me. And she understood, with shame and with admiration, that the girl she had held back had, for teaching, a gift she had never had, and that to hold Aurora back had not been only to hold back a good worker, it had been to hold back a good mistress, someone who would do for the trade what she had never done, pass it on, from hand to hand.

Preciosa locked the gate that night with the usual trick, alone, and went down the street with Aurora's phrase behind her eyes. And she began to understand, slowly, a thing that opened the wall stone by stone: that her indispensability, which she lived as merit, did harm, and not only to herself. It harmed Aurora, whom she had braked for years; it harmed the trade, which she condemned to die with her; it harmed the very mill, which she hung upon a single nail. That she, in not passing on the key, was not protecting anything, she was depriving. And that perhaps the greatest worth of a mistress was not to be irreplaceable, it was the contrary, it was to make herself replaceable, it was to leave behind her someone to carry on, because a knowledge is only worth something if it is passed on, and a person is only truly precious, she understood with a tightening that had her own name inside it, not when she makes herself the single key, but when she leaves, by her own hand, someone who can do without her, because she taught, because she passed it on, because she made others grow, and what she knew does not die with her.
