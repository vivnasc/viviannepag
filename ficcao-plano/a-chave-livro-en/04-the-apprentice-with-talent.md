## 4. The apprentice with talent

There was, in the Mill, an apprentice with talent, a young woman who had the knack, who learned quickly, who could, if she were let, come to do what Preciosa did; and Preciosa held her back, without knowing she held her back, and it was that apprentice, more than anyone, who showed her the damage of the indispensability.

The young woman had entered the mill young, and one saw at once that she had quality, that she was clever, that she wanted to learn, that she had the same knack Preciosa had had at her age; and the young woman looked at Preciosa with admiration, wanted to learn from her, asked her to teach her, to let her do, to pass her the knowledge; and Preciosa, who should have been glad of an apprentice like that, of someone to pass on what she knew, reacted, without realising it, the opposite way: she held her back. She did not teach her the essential, always gave her the lesser tasks, kept the important ones for herself, said I do it faster, did not pass her the keys, the tricks, the knowledge that made her the indispensable one; and the apprentice, who wanted to grow, hit against a brake she did not understand, against a Preciosa who admired her and at the same time did not let her learn, and went on getting frustrated, year after year, without understanding why that woman, who seemed to like her, did not let her be more than a helper.

And Preciosa did not understand, at first, that she held her back, nor why. She told herself that the young woman was not ready yet, that she needed more time, that it took years to learn; and they were excuses, because the truth, which she did not want to see, was another: that to teach the apprentice to do what she did, to pass her the knowledge, to let her become capable, was to make her, the apprentice, a possible replacement, was to create someone who could do what only Preciosa did, and therefore was to threaten Preciosa's indispensability, was to risk ceasing to be the only one, was to open the door to being, one day, dispensable; and Preciosa, without admitting it, held the apprentice back not for her having no knack, but for her having too much knack, for her being the threat to the wall, the person who, if she learned, would make Preciosa replaceable.

And there was in this a cruelty Preciosa did not see and that would have shamed her if she saw it: that she was doing to the apprentice the opposite of what a mistress should do. A mistress teaches, passes on what she knows, makes the apprentice grow, rejoices when the apprentice surpasses her, because the trade goes on in him; and Preciosa, instead, kept the knowledge, held back the growth, kept the apprentice small, to keep herself irreplaceable, and in doing so robbed the young woman of her future, of growing, of becoming a mistress in her turn, and robbed the trade of its continuity, because a knowledge that is not passed on dies with the one who has it; and Preciosa, by not passing the key, by holding the apprentice back, was condemning her knowledge to die with her, and the apprentice never to grow, all to keep herself the only one, the indispensable one, in a selfishness she dressed as zeal and that was, deep down, fear.

And there was a day on which the apprentice told her the truth, and it was with the frankness of the young. Tired of being held back, she said to Preciosa: you do not let me learn. I have the knack, you know I have, everyone knows; and you do not teach me what matters, you always keep the important things, you pass me nothing. Why? What are you afraid of? That I learn? That I come to do what you do? You should want to teach me, you should be proud to leave someone to continue what you know. And instead you hold me back, as though I were a threat. I do not want your place, Preciosa. I want to learn. But you prefer to be the only one to letting what you know continue in me. And that, forgive me for saying it, is not zeal. It is fear of ceasing to be needed.

And Preciosa, on hearing it is fear of ceasing to be needed, was shaken, because the young woman had just said aloud what she had never admitted to herself; and she understood, with the apprentice, a thing that began to open the wall for her: that her indispensability, which she lived as merit, did harm, and not only to her, it did harm to the apprentice, whom she held back, and to the trade, which she condemned to die with her; that she, by not passing the key, was not protecting anything, she was depriving, depriving the apprentice of the future and the mill of continuity; and that perhaps the greatest worth of a mistress was not to be irreplaceable, it was the opposite, it was to make herself replaceable, it was to pass on what she knew to someone who would continue it, because a knowledge is only worth something if it is passed on, and a person is only truly precious, she understood with a tightening, not when she makes herself indispensable, but when she leaves behind her those who can do without her, because she taught, because she passed on, because she made them grow, and what she knew does not die with her.
