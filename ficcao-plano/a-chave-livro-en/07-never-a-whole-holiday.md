## 7. Never a whole holiday

To understand the depth of Preciosa's prison, one need only look at one thing: that she had never, in thirty years, taken a whole holiday, and that to fall ill, for her, was to organise the illness so as not to be absent; and it was on looking at this, after the Keeper, that Preciosa saw the size of what the indispensability had robbed her of.

Thirty years without whole holidays. She took a few days, sometimes, but even on those days she was bound to the mill, she carried the telephone, answered the calls, solved the problems at a distance, came back early if there was a breakdown; never, in thirty years, had she truly switched off, departed without leaving instructions, entrusted the mill to another and forgotten it for a whole week; and the reason was always the phrase, if I stop everything falls, and it was also the fear, that of, if she absented herself and the mill moved, discovering herself dispensable. And so Preciosa had spent thirty summers without truly going anywhere, thirty years without the rest everyone has, without the right to depart and not think of work, because the key would not let her go, or she would not let go of the key, which was the same.

And to fall ill, then, was the saddest chapter. When Preciosa fell ill, instead of resting, as illness commands, she organised the illness so as not to be absent: she went to work ill, took work home, scheduled the appointments outside hours, managed the recovery so that the mill would not notice her absence; and there was in this a kind of heroism, but it was a sick heroism, in the service of the prison, because a person who organises her own illness so as not to be absent from work is a person who no longer belongs to herself, who belongs to the work, who has put the work ahead of her own body, of her own health, of her own life, and who confuses so completely her worth with her usefulness that she would rather burn herself than fail.

And Preciosa, on looking at the thirty years without holidays, at the organised illnesses, saw, for the first time, what the indispensability had cost her, and not in pride, in life. She saw the summers she had not had, the journeys she had not made, the rests she had not enjoyed; she saw the life of her own she had never had, because the mill had taken up everything; she saw that she was fifty-four and that, outside the mill, she had almost nothing, not deep relationships, not hobbies, not memories that were not of work, because she had given everything to the Mill and nothing to herself; and she understood that the indispensability, which she lived as her worth, had eaten her life, and that to be the key of a mill for thirty years was, seen another way, to have spent thirty years without living, bound to a door, opening and closing the lives of others and never opening her own.

And she understood a thing that frightened her: that if she handed over the key, and the mill moved, and she was left with time, with holidays, with a life of her own, she would not know what to do with it. Because a person who lived thirty years bound to a door, when she is released, does not know how to walk, like the prisoner who comes out and finds the street strange; and Preciosa, on imagining a week without the mill, felt, beneath the relief, a terror, that of not knowing how to fill that week, that of not knowing who she was without the key, that of finding herself before a stranger, herself, a woman of fifty-four who had never lived for herself, and that that stranger frightened her more than the work; and it was there that she understood the deepest truth, the one the phrase hid: that she did not refuse the holidays for the mill needing her, she refused them for being afraid of not needing the mill, of discovering, in a free time, that she did not know how to be anyone without it.

And she decided, then, after the Keeper, that she was going to do the hardest thing of her life, harder than thirty years of work: she was going to hand over the key, for a week, she was going to depart, she was going to let the mill move without her, she was going to force herself to have, for the first time, a whole holiday; not for being tired, for that she had always been, but for a greater reason, for wanting to discover, at last, whether the mill fell without her, or whether it was she who did not know how to stop; and for wanting to face, once and for all, the stranger she was, the Preciosa without the key, the person left unlived, and to know, at last, who she was, if she was anyone, without the mill that had been, for thirty years, all she had known how to be.
