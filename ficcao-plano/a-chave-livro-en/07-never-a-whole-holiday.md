## 7. Never a whole holiday

To understand the depth of Preciosa's prison, it is enough to look at one thing, and to look at it in earnest: that she had never, in thirty years, taken a whole holiday, and that to fall ill, for her, was to arrange the illness so as not to be absent. And it was on looking at this, in the nights that followed the Keeper, that Preciosa saw, at last, the size of what the key had robbed her of.

Thirty years without whole holidays. She took a few days, sometimes, forced by circumstance, but even on those days she was bound to the mill. She remembered one, many years before, when her sister married in the big town, and she took two days, two, and went to the wedding, but took with her the telephone and the black notebook, and halfway through the reception, in her good dress and the shoes that hurt, she received the call from the mill, a breakdown in the central motor, and she excused herself to her sister, and left the reception still with the carnation in her lapel, and caught the coach back, and mended the motor in her ceremony dress, with her good shoes dirtied with oil, and her sister never quite forgave her, and was right. She remembered another, when she had resolved to go to the baths, and packed her case, and at the hour of departure Belmiro telephoned about a late order, and Preciosa unpacked the case and stayed, and the baths were left for next year, and the year never came.

There had been, many years before, one time when she was almost. A schoolfriend, Branca, who had emigrated and was coming back on a visit, invited her for a few days by the sea, just the two of them, like girls; and Preciosa, by a miracle, said yes, and bought a bathing costume, a thing never seen, and dreamed of it for some weeks, and was happy only in the waiting for it. And on the eve of departure a large order came in suddenly, with a short deadline, and Preciosa, without thinking twice, telephoned Branca and said I can't, child, it'll keep for next year. And Branca, on the other end, was silent, and then said, with a mild sadness: you can never, Preciosa. For twenty years you've never been able to. And she never invited her again, and the friendship, which was one of the few Preciosa had outside the mill, went out the way things go out when they are not watered, and Preciosa was left with the order done and the bathing costume in the drawer, with the tag still on it, year after year, in the dark depths of the chest of drawers, the silent witness of a sea she never went to.

She had never, in thirty years, truly switched off. She had never gone away without leaving instructions, without taking the telephone, without the notebook, without saying if it's needed, call me. She had never entrusted the mill to another and forgotten it for a whole week. And the reason was always the phrase, if I stop everything falls, and it was also the fear, that of, if she truly absented herself and the mill ran, discovering herself dispensable. And so Preciosa had spent thirty summers without truly going anywhere, thirty years without the rest everyone has, without the right to leave and not think about work, because the key did not let go of her, or she did not let go of the key, which was the same thing.

And falling ill, then, was the saddest chapter, and Preciosa, looking at it now, felt shame. When she fell ill, instead of resting, as the illness orders, she arranged the illness so as not to be absent. There was a flu, some years before, of the kind that lays a man in bed a week, and Preciosa went to work with a fever of thirty-eight, shivering, wrapped in two jerseys, and she went to the office, and came back to the shed, and opened in the morning and locked up at night with the fever rising, and Conceição, from the canteen, laid her hand on her brow at noon and said, frightened, woman of God, you're burning, go home; and Preciosa said I can't, Conceição, and what if I don't come, and drank a tea and went back to the shed, and only really took to her bed at the end of the week, when she was already better, irony of ironies, and even then she telephoned in the morning to ask if everything was all right.

And there was the time of the tooth. A tooth that had pained her for months, and that she kept putting off, because to go to the dentist was half a morning away from the mill, and Preciosa did not have half a morning for herself. She put it off until the tooth rotted and made an abscess, and her face swelled on one side, and even so she made the appointment for the lunch hour, so as not to be absent, and came back from the dentist with her mouth numb and the anaesthetic still working, and went straight to the shed, and Idalina said to her, shaking her head, you treat the machines better than you treat yourself, Preciosa, the machines you stop to oil them, and yourself you never stop. And Preciosa laughed, with half her mouth, because the other half was numb, and did not understand, then, that Idalina had told her one of the truest things of her life.

And there was her sister, Júlia, who had married in the big town and to whom Preciosa had never given her due. Júlia, younger, telephoned now and then, and it was always the same thing: come and spend a Sunday, woman, it's years since you came, your nephews and nieces don't even know your face. And Preciosa said this Sunday I can't, there's the order, there's the inventory, and the following Sunday there was always something else, and the years passed, and the nephews and nieces grew, and Preciosa knew them from the photographs Júlia sent by post, with a date on the back, and never from holding them. There was a Christmas when Júlia, hurt, said to her on the telephone a phrase that Preciosa put away then and that now, on looking at the thirty years, unfolded itself whole again: you don't have a family, Preciosa, you have a mill. Us you only see when the mill gives you leave, and the mill never gives it. And Preciosa, then, thought the phrase unjust, and took offence, because it was easier to take offence than to notice that the phrase was right; and only now, telling it to herself, did she understand that Júlia had not been unjust, she had only been the first to say the price to her out loud, years before Aurora and the Keeper.

Because that was it, she understood now: that a person who arranges her own illness so as not to be absent from work is a person who no longer belongs to herself. Who belongs to the work. Who put the work before her own body, her own health, her own mouth rotting, and who confuses her worth with her usefulness so completely that she would rather burn than fail. There was a heroism in this, yes, but it was a sick heroism, in the service of the prison, the heroism of a woman who oiled the machines and never oiled herself.

And on looking at the thirty years without holidays, at the arranged illnesses, at the put-off tooth and her sister's wedding and the baths she never went to, Preciosa saw, for the first time, what the key had cost her, and not in pride, in life. She saw the summers she had not had. She saw the journeys she had not made, the places she knew only from Otília's leaflets. She saw that she was fifty-four and that, outside the mill, she had almost nothing: no deep relations, because she had never had time to cultivate them; no pastimes, because she had never known what to do with a free afternoon; not even memories that were not of work, because she had given everything to the Spinning Mill and nothing to herself. And she understood that to have been the key of a mill for thirty years was, seen another way, to have spent thirty years without living, bound to a door, opening and closing the lives of others and never opening her own.

And she understood a thing that frightened her more than everything. That if she handed over the key, and the mill ran, and she were left with time, with holidays, with a life of her own, she would not know what to do with it. She imagined herself one morning at home, without having to go down to the mill, without the gate to open, without the motor to start, and instead of relief she felt a cold emptiness, the emptiness of not knowing what to do with a morning that was hers. Because a person who has lived thirty years bound to a door, when they let her go, does not know how to walk, like the prisoner who comes out of jail and finds the width of the street strange and catches herself wanting to go back to the cell, which at least she knew. And Preciosa, on imagining a week without the mill, felt, beneath the relief she ought to feel, a terror: that of not knowing how to fill that week, of not knowing who she was without the key, of finding herself face to face with a stranger, herself, a woman of fifty-four who had never lived for herself. And that stranger frightened her more than any breakdown, than any client, than any owner.

And it was there that she understood the deepest truth, the one the phrase had hidden for thirty years: that she did not refuse the holidays because the mill needed her. She refused them out of fear of not needing the mill. Out of fear of discovering, in free time, with empty hands and open days, that she did not know how to be anyone without the key.

And she remembered, on thinking of this, a thing Aurora had said to her years before and that she had buried. The girl, still young, had offered once to come earlier, to learn to open the mill, so that Preciosa might, now and then, sleep an hour more; teach me to open, Preciosa, the knee against the gate, start the machines, and that way you don't have to come first always. And Preciosa had said no, with the usual gentleness, this business of opening is mine, child, it has its tricks. And she understood now that that no had been one of the highest walls of the prison: because while only she knew how to open, it was always she who was first, every day, thirty years, without a single morning off, and not because the mill needed it, but because she would let no one else learn the trick. Aurora had offered to set her free, and Preciosa, without seeing it, had refused her own freedom, because freedom came disguised as loss of control, and she, in those days, preferred the prison she commanded to the freedom she had to share.

And she decided, then, after the Keeper, after the fountain, after thirty years, that she was going to do the most difficult thing of her life, more difficult than any breakdown, than any fever, than thirty years of dawns: she was going to hand over the key, for a week. She was going to go away in earnest, without telephone, without notebook, without coming back for any breakdown. She was going to force herself to have, for the first time in her life, a whole holiday. And she was not going to do it because she was tired, which she always had been, but for a greater reason: because she wanted to discover, at last, whether the mill fell without her, or whether it was she who did not know how to stop; and because she wanted to face, once and for all, the stranger she was, the Preciosa without the key, the person who had been left unlived in the shadow of the Spinning Mill, and to know, cost what it cost, who she was, if she was anyone, without the mill that had been, for thirty years, all she had known how to be.
