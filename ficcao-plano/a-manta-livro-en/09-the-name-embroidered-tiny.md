## 9. The name embroidered tiny

And Velada embroidered the name in the corner of a blanket, for the first time, and embroidered it tiny, so tiny it could barely be seen, and it was a minuscule gesture and it was enormous, because it was the first time in her whole life that she put her face on what she did, that she said, in a corner, in a thread, it was I.

It cost her as nothing had cost her. She took the needle, and the thread, and chose the corner of the blanket, the most discreet, and began to embroider the name, and her hand trembled, and her heart raced, as when they praised her, but worse, because this time she was not fleeing the look, she was going to meet it, she was going, herself, to put herself in sight; and each stitch she made was a struggle against the wound of the friend, against the voice of the mother, against the equation of a life, and there was, halfway, the wish to undo it, to take out the name, to leave it anonymous as always, not to risk; but Velada did not undo it, she continued, stitch by stitch, and finished embroidering the name, tiny, in the corner of the blanket, and stayed looking at it, her name on her work, for the first time, and felt a thing she did not expect, beneath the fear: she felt pride. A timid pride, frightened, but pride, the pride of one who owns, at last, what she made, of one who says it was I, of one who shows herself.

And she embroidered it tiny on purpose, because she could not do more, and it was well that it was tiny, because to show oneself, for one who has veiled herself her whole life, is not done suddenly, it is done little by little, and a tiny name in the corner of a blanket was the exact size of the courage Velada had that day, and it was already enormous for her, it was already a world, because it was the first time. And she let the blanket go out so, with the tiny name, to the trader from away, who was going to sell it with the label, with the mark, with Velada's name bound to it; and on letting the blanket depart with her name, Velada felt the terror of seeing it go into the world saying it was I, and she felt, beneath, the wish fulfilled, that of existing, at last, as the maker, that of sending into the world, for the first time, a work that said who had made it.

And there came what she most feared, the return, the world's reaction to seeing her; and it was the opposite of what the fear had promised her. The blanket with Velada's name sold, and the trader came back, glad, to say it had sold well, and better than the ones without a name, because whoever bought it wanted to know who Velada was, wanted the story, wanted the hand, and paid more, and was happier, to carry off a blanket with a maker than an orphan blanket; and the trader brought Velada a thing she had never had, a message from the one who had bought the blanket, a thank you, a word from one who would stay warm by her work and who now knew the name of the one who had made it; and Velada, on reading the message, on receiving, for the first time, at first hand, the thanks of one who received her work with her name, felt a joy she had never known, the joy of being seen in what she did well, the joy the hiding place had denied her her whole life, and she understood, in that joy, that the trader and the Keeper were right, that to be seen was not to lose, it was to gain this, this joy, this thank you, this connection with the one who received her beauty.

And the envy she feared did not come, or came so little, so without importance beside the joy, that Velada barely minded it. There was, perhaps, one or another who turned up her nose, who commented, who envied; but it was nothing, it was insignificant, beside the admiration of many, the recognition, the joy of existing as a maker; and Velada understood that she had spent her life fleeing an envy that, when it came, was much smaller than the fear had painted it, and that she had sacrificed years of visible existence to avoid a thing that after all barely touched her; and she understood that the fear had lied to her, that it had magnified the envy and hidden the joy, that it had shown her only the danger and never the gain, and that if she had believed the fear she would have stayed hidden forever, losing the much for fearing the little.

And it was with the tiny name embroidered on the first blanket, and with the joy of the first thank you, that Velada began to come out of the hiding place; and she understood that she had taken the hardest step, the first, and that on the far side of it there was, not the danger she had feared, but the joy she had desired, and that she was going, from there on, slowly, with setbacks, to go on coming out, to embroider the name less tiny, to show herself more, to exist, at last, as the maker of the beauty she wove, with her face more and more uncovered.
