## 9. The name embroidered tiny

The blanket was finished, spread on the loom, one of the most beautiful Velada had made, a field of indigo with a single stripe of madder down the middle, like a path of fire in the midst of the night. It lacked one thing. Velada went to fetch the box of threads, chose a colour that would not shout, a thread of a quiet brown, of the tone of the weave itself, threaded the needle, and sat at the corner of the blanket, and stayed there, with the threaded needle in her hand, a long time, without taking a single stitch.

It cost her as nothing had cost her in her life.

Her hand trembled, and her heart was leaping, as when they praised her, but worse, because this time she was not fleeing the look, she was herself going to put herself in sight; she was going, with her own hands, to make in the corner of the blanket the thing she had learned, beside a tank, never to make. She took the first stitch, and the first letter, the V, and halfway through the V she stopped, with the needle stuck in the cloth, and it all came to her at once: the Aurélia who stopped laughing, the Idália saying don't go about saying it's yours, the mouths of the playground, the blood rising to her face at a christening. There came to her the hand of the girl of fourteen pulling her arm back, saying don't take that stitch, remember what happened the last time, remember what it cost. And Velada felt the want to take out the needle, to pull the thread, to undo the V and leave the blanket bare, anonymous, safe, as always.

But she did not undo it. She gritted her teeth, as the Idália gritted her teeth at her sewing, and went after the V the E, and after the E the L, stitch by stitch, slowly, with her hand trembling and the cloth growing wet with a water she only noticed once she had already wept it. And she finished. She embroidered the whole name, Velada, tiny, so tiny that one had to know it was there to see it, in the most discreet corner of the blanket, hidden almost in the weave itself. And she stayed looking at it. Her name on her work, for the first time in forty-four years. And she felt, beneath the fear that still beat at her breast, a thing she did not expect, a thing timid and new and warm: she felt pride. A frightened pride, shrunk, ashamed of itself, but pride, the simple pride of one who owns what she made, of one who says, in a corner, in a thread, it was I.

And there was, before she embroidered it, a choice no one would see and that for Velada had weight: that of the thread. She hesitated between two threads. One was of the tone of the weave, quiet, of the brown of the wool itself, which would make the name almost invisible, which is read only if one knows it is there; the other was a madder red, hers, which would make the name leap to the eye. And Velada, that day, did not have the courage for the red, she chose the quiet one, and was ashamed to choose it, because she knew she was hiding the name even within the gesture of putting it, making it in such a way that it could scarcely be seen. But then she forgave herself the choice, because she understood that courage is made by steps, and that one does not climb the whole stair at a leap, and that to put the name with the quiet thread was the first step, and that from the first step one sees the second, and that one day, when she had climbed a few, she would reach the red. And she reached it, later; but that day it was the quiet one, and it was well that it was, because it was the exact size and colour of the courage she had.

And she embroidered it tiny on purpose, because she could do no more, and it was well that it was tiny. To show oneself, for one who has veiled herself her whole life, is not done suddenly; it is done little by little, the size of the courage of each day, and a tiny name in the corner of a blanket was the exact size of Velada's courage that afternoon, and it was already enormous for her, it was already a world, because it was the first time. She folded the blanket with the name outward, in sight, and when the Matilde came up to the serra, the following Thursday, she handed it to her without saying anything, only with her heart leaping, and let the Matilde find the corner herself. The Matilde found it, and passed her finger over it, and did not say a big word, she understood it was not the time for big words; she said only, low, here it is, and smiled, and folded the blanket with even more care, and took it.

And on the Thursday morning, before the Matilde came up, Velada was on the point of drawing back. She sat beside the folded blanket, with the tiny name outward, and there came to her a fear that was no longer of the world, it was of letting it go. It was like letting a child depart with its face uncovered into a land of people one does not know. While the blanket was there, folded, with the name only for her eyes, it was safe; but, the path descended, that name was going to go about the world without her beside it, was going to be read by mouths she did not choose, judged by eyes she did not see, and Velada could not go after it to defend it. And she almost undid the corner, once more. But she remembered the fountain, the water given to the square without going after it, and she remembered the Esperança of the Lameiras, the voice that stayed under the earth for never having been loosed, and understood that a blanket with the name kept at home was a voice to be loosed, and that it was no use embroidering the name if then she did not let the blanket go. And she let it go. When the Matilde arrived, Velada handed it to her without undoing it, with her hands trembling only a little, and when the Matilde went down the path with the blanket in her bag, Velada stayed at the door watching it go, as she had watched so many blankets go by the Souto's hand, but this time it was different, this time there went a blanket of hers with the name of hers, and it was the first time in her life that she let a thing of hers depart with its face uncovered, and it was like breathing deep after a life held in.

And there came what Velada most feared, the return, the world's reaction to seeing her at last. And it was the opposite of what the fear had promised her.

The Matilde came back, three weeks later, and climbed the path faster than usual, and carried in her hand a folded paper. The blanket had sold, she said, and had sold better than the ones without a name, and dearer, because the woman who bought it, on seeing the name in the corner, wanted at once to know who Velada was, wanted the story, wanted to know if she was living, if she was of the serra, if she made more; and she paid what the Matilde asked without haggling, and said that so the blanket was worth double, because it had a face. And the Matilde held out the paper to Velada, at the door, and said, she wrote you this, dona Velada, she asked me to give it to you by hand.

Velada opened the paper at the door, with her hands trembling, and read, in a city hand, round and steady:

To Velada, of the blankets. I bought yours, the blue one with the red stripe, and I slept the first night under it and I never slept so well. I saw your name in the corner, tiny, and I stood looking at it a long time, because it made a difference to me to know that there was a woman, with hands, with a name, who made this comfort I sleep in. Thank you. Make more. And embroider the name bigger, for it deserves more room.

And Velada read the paper at the door, and read it again, and had to lean against the doorpost, because it was the first time in her life, in forty-four years, that she received at first hand, in her own hand, the thank-you of one who received her work with her name. All her life the thank-you had gone to the Souto. Now it was hers, said to her name, written to Velada, of the blankets. And she felt a joy she did not know, a joy the hiding-place had denied her for forty years, the joy of being seen in what she did well, and she understood, with the paper pressed against her breast, that the Matilde and the Keeper were right, that to be seen was not to lose, it was to gain this, this joy, this thank-you, this thread that now bound a woman of the city, whom she would never see, to a night of sleep under a blanket she had woven in the dark.

And there was more, and it went on growing. The woman of the city who had written the paper ordered again, a month later, and sent word that she wanted another, of the same hand, and that she had spoken of Velada to her friends, and that two of those friends wanted some too, and that one of them had asked whether the weaver made to order, with the colours one asked for. And Velada, on receiving this message by the Matilde, felt a new thing, the thing of being sought by name, and not by chance; for the first time in her life there were people, far off, who wanted a blanket not because it was cheap nor because it was at hand, but because it was hers, because it was a Velada. And one of the ladies asked for a blanket with a red that would call to mind the fire, and Velada drew her from the madder her best red, the ember one, the one that has ash inside it, and embroidered her the name in the corner, and on sending it she felt, beneath the rest of the fear, a deep pride, that of making a thing to measure for a real person, who wanted her and awaited her, and who was going to receive the blanket knowing whose it was. It was the encounter the hiding-place had denied her her whole life, reaching her at last, by a label and a name, from far off, but reaching her.

And the envy she had so feared, that, either did not come, or came so little and so without importance that Velada barely minded it. There was, perhaps, one or another in the serra who turned up her nose on learning that the Velada now went about signing; a vague echo of a comment reached her. But it was nothing, beside the paper pressed against her breast, beside the joy of existing. And Velada understood, with astonishment, that she had spent her life fleeing an envy that, when it finally came, was much smaller than the fear had painted it, and that she had sacrificed forty years of visible existence to avoid a thing that after all barely brushed her. The fear had lied to her. It had magnified the envy until it covered the sky, and hidden all the joy behind it, and shown her only the danger and never the gain. And if she had gone on believing the fear, she would have stayed hidden forever, losing the much for fearing the little.

And that night, alone, Velada did a thing no one saw and that the book must tell, because it was there that the crossing held. She went to the chest, and took out the prize-winning blanket, the Aurélia's, with the ribbon already the colour of nothing, and spread it on the bed beside a new cloth on which she had embroidered, apart, only to practise, the tiny name, time and again, like one learning a letter. And she set the two things side by side: the blanket of forty years, without a name, the proof of the girl who hid herself, and the new cloth with the name embroidered ten times, the proof of the woman who was trying to come out. And she stayed looking at the two, a long time, in bed, with the lamp. And she understood a thing that made her weep softly, so as not to wake a house that was empty: that if she, at fourteen, had embroidered the name on that prize-winning blanket, instead of hiding it at the bottom of the chest, perhaps the Aurélia would have been lost all the same, for that was not in her hands; but she, Velada, would not have been lost too. She would have kept the blanket and the name, even without the friend. And instead she had been left without the friend and without the name, she had lost the two things at once, and had spent forty years thinking the fault was the name's, when the name was precisely the only thing she could have saved. She folded the prize-winning blanket and put it away again, and put the cloth of the names beside it, together in the chest, the old and the new, to remember, every time she opened it, what the hiding-place had cost and what it cost to come out of it.

And it was with the tiny name embroidered on the first blanket, and with the paper of the woman of the city kept, not at the bottom of the chest as she had kept the prize-winning blanket, but on top, to hand, that Velada began to come out of the hiding-place; and she understood that she had taken the hardest step, the first, and that on the far side of it there was not the danger she had feared, there was the joy she had always desired; and that she was going, from there on, slowly, with setbacks, to go on coming out, and to embroider the name less tiny, and to show herself more, and to exist, at last, as the maker of the beauty she wove, with her face ever more uncovered.
