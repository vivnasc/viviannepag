## From the register of Véspera

And of everything a record was made.

Be it set down that Velada, of the Highlanders, weaver, who made the most beautiful blankets of the region and hid them her whole life, who sold through an intermediary, refused the fairs, did not sign, and who, when praised, fled the praise with her heart racing, learned, at forty-four, the hardest thing for one who is afraid of being seen: to embroider the name in the corner of the blanket, first tiny, then less tiny, then whole, and to show herself, without losing herself. Be it set down that she discovered that what she created deserved to reach the world with her attached to the name, and that to be seen was not to lose, it was to exist.

The register keeps who makes the beautiful things of the village, and there is work that is known without knowing who made it. This Keeper had, for years, a blanket of Velada's without knowing whose it was, and saw, in the book, the most beautiful blankets of the mountain without a name; and saw, at last, a name appear bound to them, tiny first, then larger, and Velada come out of the anonymity and become, with her face uncovered, the maker of the beauty she wove. And the Keeper understood that Velada had fulfilled the name the other way round: she was called the covered one, the veiled one, and she took off the veil, and made of the name not a fate of hiding, but a crossing, that of one who was veiled and uncovered herself.

Whoever reads this entry and recognises herself in some Velada who is afraid of being seen, who makes beautiful things and hides them, who does not sign what she creates, who changes the subject when praised with her heart racing, who learned, by a wound, that to show oneself is to lose, let her know what she learned and what this house records: that to hide does not protect, it impoverishes, because it takes from the world what we created and from us the right to have created it; that to be seen is not to lose, it is to exist; that the envy of some is no reason for the great to put themselves out, because envy is the problem of the one who has it; and that the greater courage, for one who has veiled herself her whole life, is not to stay covered, it is to embroider the name in the corner of what we make, and to show oneself, without losing oneself, and to discover that what we create, and we with it, deserves to reach the world with the face uncovered.

Velada traded the anonymity for the name, the ownerless blanket for the signed blanket, the hiding place for the visible existence. And in the houses of Véspera, since the one in which she embroidered the name in the corner of the first blanket, there is one more woman who no longer confuses hiding with being humble, because she learned that what is created deserves the name of the one who made it, and that to take off the veil, and to show oneself, and to sign one's own beauty, is not to boast, it is to exist, and is the only way not to waste the gift one has.

And of everything a record was made, by the hand of the Keeper, in the register of Véspera.
