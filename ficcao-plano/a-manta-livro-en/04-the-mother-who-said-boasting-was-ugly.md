## 4. The mother who said boasting was ugly

There was, beneath the fear of envy, another voice, older, that of the mother, who said that to boast was ugly; and it was that voice, as much as the wound of the friend, that taught Velada to hide, because there are mothers who, wishing to teach humility, teach invisibility.

Velada's mother was a woman of the mountain, hard, of the time when to show oneself was a sin and to boast was one of the worst faults; and she raised the daughter in that law, that of a person of worth not boasting, not showing herself, not putting herself forward, not saying she was good at anything, because that was ugly, was pride, was a lack of modesty; and each time the small Velada showed pride in a thing she had made, the mother checked her, do not boast, that looks ugly, whoever is good does not need to say it; and the child learned, from the mother, that pride in what one does was shameful, that to show oneself was ugly, that the virtue lay in hiding what one was worth, in not appearing, in staying behind.

And the mother, in teaching this, thought she taught humility, and taught another thing, taught invisibility. Because there is a difference between humility and hiding, and the mother confused them, and passed the confusion to the daughter. Humility is not to think oneself superior to others, it is not to scorn anyone, it is to recognise what one owes to others; and that is a virtue. But to hide what one does well, not to sign what one creates, to flee from recognition, to make oneself invisible, that is not humility, it is fear, or it is a false virtue that impoverishes the person and deprives the world of what she has to give; and Velada's mother, in teaching that to boast was ugly, taught the daughter to hide not the pride, which is good to hide, but her own worth, her own gift, her own light, and made of her a woman who confused showing what she did well with boasting, and who therefore hid it, thinking herself humble when she was only invisible.

And the two things joined in Velada, the wound of the friend and the voice of the mother, and reinforced each other: the wound told her that to be seen brought the envy and the loss, and the voice of the mother told her that to show oneself was ugly, was pride; and between the two, Velada was doubly bound, afraid of being seen and guilty of wanting to be, thinking that her desire for recognition, which is natural and good in one who creates, was a fault, a pride, an ugly thing she should hide; and so she hid not only the work, but the wish to sign it, the wish to be seen, the wish to hear a praise, because even that wish seemed to her ugly, prideful, against the mother's law, and Velada veiled it too, and felt herself bad for having it.

And it was necessary, in those days, for Velada to begin to separate the two things the mother had confused. She began to understand that to want to be recognised for what one does well is not pride, it is a human need, just, sound; that to sign what one creates is not to boast, it is to own, it is to say it was I, with the naturalness of one who made it and has the right to say so; that to show what one does well is not ugly, it is to give the world what we have, with the face uncovered; and that the mother, in teaching that all that was ugly, had not made her humble, had made her hidden, and had deprived her, and deprived the world, of what she had to give with her face shown. And she understood that true humility is not to hide what one is worth, it is to be worth and not think oneself for that superior to others; and that she could sign the blankets, show herself, be seen, and remain humble, because humility is not in hiding the gift, it is in not scorning anyone for having it.

And it was hard for her, because the mother's voice was old and strong, and each time Velada imagined signing a blanket, showing herself, the voice came at once, that looks ugly, do not boast, whoever is good does not need to say it; but Velada began to answer it, with the new truth: I am not boasting, mother, I am owning what I do; it is not ugly to say it was I, it is just; and to hide what I do well does not make me humble, it makes me invisible, and takes from the world my blankets with my name, and takes from me the joy of having made them with my face shown. And it was so, separating humility from hiding, that Velada began to undo one of the bonds that held her, understanding that she could be seen without being prideful, sign without boasting, show herself without ceasing to be humble, and that the mother's law, which had veiled her her whole life, was not the law of the world, it was the fear of a hard woman passing itself from generation to generation, and that she could, at last, cut it.
