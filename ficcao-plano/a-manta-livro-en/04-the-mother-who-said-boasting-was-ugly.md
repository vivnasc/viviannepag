## 4. The mother who said boasting was ugly

Beneath the wound of the Aurélia there was a voice older still, and Velada, in her bed, opening doors, came at last to that door, and on opening it the voice was there whole, waiting, as if it had never gone out: the voice of her mother, of the Idália — sorrowful one — saying that to boast was ugly.

The Idália of the Highlanders was a dry woman of the serra, of the time when the virtue of a woman was measured by how little was said of her. She had raised Velada in that law, and the law had a single article, repeated so many times that it entered the daughter as the cold enters the bones, unnoticed until it is already inside: one who is good has no need to say it. Does not boast. Does not show oneself. Does not put oneself forward. Stays in one's place, low, silent, and lets it be others who speak, if they wish, and if they do not wish so much the better, for what is spoken of is spent.

Velada remembered whole scenes, lying there, with the sharpness that the scenes of childhood keep.

She remembered being seven or eight and coming home running, one Sunday, with a holy picture she had won at catechism for knowing all the doctrine by heart, the only one in the class to know it, and holding it out to her mother still panting, ah mother, I was the only one, I was the only one who knew it all. And the mother, without letting go of her sewing, without lifting her eyes from the needle, saying only: and do you have to come home shouting that across the whole serra, for the whole neighbourhood to know? Put that away. Whoever knows the doctrine knows it for God, not to show off. And the picture, which had come warm in the child's hand, cooled there, on the table, and Velada put it away without another word, and put away with it, without knowing, a lesson: pride in what one does is shown at the table and the table cools it.

She remembered another, from her twelfth year. She had made, alone for the first time, all the bread of a Saturday, risen right, baked right, and a neighbour, aunt Custódia — keeping — who had come in to ask for yeast, tasted it and said to the Idália, in front of the child, this bread is better than yours, Idália, this daughter of yours has a hand. And the child, on hearing the praise, felt the blood rise to her face with gladness, and looked at her mother expecting to see her glad too. And the Idália said to the neighbour, with a half-smile that did not reach her eyes: oh, don't say that in front of her, she'll get full of herself yet. And then, when the neighbour went out, she turned to the daughter and said to her the sentence, the sentence of always, the one that stayed: to boast is ugly, Velada. A girl who boasts finds no one to want her. The best thing a piece of work has is not needing to be spoken of. And the child, who was boiling with pride in her bread, felt the pride turn against her, turn into shame, as if having felt it were already a fault.

And so she grew, beneath that voice, and learned what her mother taught her, but learned too much, learned beyond what the mother meant to teach, which is what always happens with fears handed from mother to daughter: they are passed with interest.

For the Idália herself believed she was teaching humility, and Velada, lying with her eyes open at forty-four, now understood, for the first time, the difference the mother had never known how to make, the difference between two things she had confused into one and passed confused to the daughter. One thing is humility, which is not thinking oneself above others, not despising anyone, knowing what one owes to those who raised and helped us; and that is virtue, and of that the Idália had plenty, and taught it well. Another thing, very different, is to hide what one does well, to silence one's own worth, to flee recognition, to make oneself invisible; and that is no humility at all, it is fear dressed as virtue, and it impoverishes the one who does it and robs the world of what that person had to give. And the Idália, without malice, with the best of intentions, in saying to boast is ugly each time the daughter showed pride in a thing well made, did not teach the daughter to hide her arrogance, which is good to hide; she taught her to hide her own gift, her own light, and made of her a woman who confused showing what she did well with boasting, and who for that reason hid it her whole life, believing herself virtuous when she was only invisible.

And there was a scene, the last and the worst, that Velada had kept at the bottom, and that unfolded whole that night.

The Idália was dying, already old, in the little room facing the sunrise, and Velada, a grown woman, was the only one tending her, because the brother had emigrated and did not come back. In those last days the mother spoke little, and Velada, to entertain her, had gone to fetch a blanket she had just woven, one of the most beautiful she had made, to lay it over her, and said, low, almost pleading, this one was mine, mother, do you like it? The Idália passed her bony hand over the warp, slowly, and for a moment, only one moment, Velada saw in her eyes a thing she had waited for her whole life, she saw pride. But the Idália was the Idália to the end, and the pride did not pass her lips. What passed her lips was the voice of always, weaker, but the same:

"It's pretty, daughter. But don't go about saying it's yours. Let them say it, if they will. To boast is ugly. Even at the edge of the grave it's ugly."

And she died soon after, with the blanket over her, without having told her daughter, in forty-odd years, not once, without reservation, that she was proud of her; because the Idália thought that to say it was to spoil her, that praise swells people, that a mother's love is shown by correcting and not by praising, and she carried to the grave, under the most beautiful blanket her daughter had woven, the pride she felt and never allowed herself to give, for fear that it would do the daughter the harm she believed praise did.

And there was a thing about the Idália that Velada only that night joined to the rest, and that explained the whole mother to her. The Idália too had a gift, and had hidden it too her whole life. She embroidered. She embroidered as few could, kerchiefs, towels, trousseaux, with whites upon whites that could only be seen against the light, a work of a patience and a fineness that the women of the serra knew and sought. But the Idália never embroidered for sale, never sold, never put a single initial. She embroidered the trousseaux of other people's daughters for nothing, at night, and when they came to fetch them she handed them over without a word, wrapped up, and if they praised her she shrugged her shoulders and said it was nothing, that it had been her duty. And once, a neighbour took a kerchief embroidered by the Idália to an exhibition in the big village, without her knowing, and the kerchief won a mention, and when the news reached the Idália, instead of glad, the Idália was furious, ashamed, as if she had been wronged, and asked for the kerchief back and never embroidered for that neighbour again. Velada, as a child, had not understood the mother's fury. Now she understood it whole: the Idália had as much fear of being seen as the daughter would come to have, and the to boast is ugly she repeated to the daughter was, first of all, the rope with which she bound herself, the law she had invented to justify her own hiding-place. The Idália did not teach the daughter to hide herself out of malice nor even only out of custom; she taught her because it was the only way of living she knew, and she passed her the fear as one passes a colour of the family, without knowing she was passing a sickness and not a virtue. And Velada understood, with an immense pity for the dead mother, that the Idália had embroidered her whole life in secret and had died without the world knowing what those hands knew how to make, exactly as the Esperança — hope — of the Lameiras had sung alone on the hill, and that she, Velada, was going the same way, heir to a hiding-place of two generations, and that if she did not cut it she would be the third woman of that line to carry her whole gift down under the earth.

And the two things joined in Velada, and for that she was doubly bound. The wound of the Aurélia told her that to be seen brought envy and loss. The voice of the Idália told her that to want to be seen was already ugly, was already pride, was already a fault. And between the two, Velada was left not only afraid to appear, but guilty of desiring it, thinking that her wish for recognition, which is so natural and so healthy in one who creates as hunger in one who works, was a vice to hide; and so she veiled not only the blankets, but the wish to sign them, the wish to hear a thank-you, the wish to exist, because even that wish seemed to her, in her mother's voice, an ugly thing of which she should be ashamed.

And there was a scene, already of Velada a woman, in which the two voices, that of the Idália and that of the Aurélia, spoke at the same time, and Velada obeyed them both and regretted it her whole life. There was, in the big village, an exhibition of the region's handcraft, with a prize, years after the mother died, and the Souto himself, who went about the fairs, said to her, dona Velada, you ought to send a blanket to that, you would win for certain, that's your place. And Velada even chose the blanket, the best she had, and wrapped it, and set it at the door for the Souto to take. And at night, alone, there came to her the voice of the Idália, to boast is ugly, to send a thing to a contest is to want to appear, it is pride; and there came, beneath, the voice of the Aurélia, beside you everybody feels small, you win the prize and lose the rest, remember what it cost the last time. And the two voices together were stronger than the want, and Velada unwrapped the blanket, and put it away, and told the Souto, the next day, that she had changed her mind, that that was not for her. And the Souto shrugged his shoulders and did not insist. And the blanket stayed. And months later it was learned that the prize had gone to a blanket of the Engrácia of the Valley, a weaker one, the whole serra knew it, but one that was there, in sight, with the initials well embroidered, while Velada's, the better one, slept folded in a chest. And Velada, on learning it, felt an anger she did not know whose it was, whether the Engrácia's, whether the mother's, whether her own, and that was all three, and that was, deep down, anger at having let two dead women and a sentence of fourteen years old decide, for her, that she did not deserve the place that was hers.

But in those days, separating the scenes one from another, Velada began to separate too the two things the mother had confused, and it was like untying a knot that seemed a single thread and was two.

She began to understand, slowly, that to want to be recognised for what one does well is not to boast, it is a human hunger, just and healthy. That to sign a blanket is not to say I am better than you, it is to say it was I, with the same naturalness with which a farmer says this field is mine. That to show what one does well is not ugly, it is to give the world what we have with our face in sight, and that to hide is what takes, what impoverishes. And she began, there in bed, to answer her mother's voice, a thing she had never done while she lived, to answer it with the new truth and with all tenderness, because the mother had not been at fault, she had inherited the voice as she passed it. I am not boasting, mother. I am owning what I do. It is not ugly to say it was I; it is just. And to hide what I do well did not make me humble; it made me invisible, and took from the world my blankets with my name, and took from me, mother, your daughter, the joy of having made them with my face in sight, which is a joy you never let yourself have, and so you taught me not to have it either.

And she understood, at last, the thing that freed her from this knot: that true humility does not lie in hiding what one is worth, it lies in being worth and not thinking oneself above anyone for it; and that she could, without betraying her mother, without ceasing to be humble, sign the blankets, show herself, be seen, because humility does not live in the hiding-place, it lives in the heart, and a humble heart can embroider the name in the corner of what it makes without ceasing to be humble by a single thread. The voice of the Idália, Velada understood, was not the law of the world. It was the fear of a hard woman passing itself from generation to generation, mother to daughter, and she, Velada, could be, in that line, the first to cut it.

And she thought of the daughter she had not had, and of the one she might still have near her, some niece, some apprentice, and understood that the way to cut the rope was not only to silence the mother's voice within herself; it was not to repeat it to anyone else. That if one day she had before her a girl with a hand and with pride in a thing well made, she would not say to her to boast is ugly, put that away; she would say to her show it, that's pretty, it was you, and good for you. For the Idália had not invented the saying; she had received it, for certain, from another mother, and that one from another, and the rope came from far off, from a time when perhaps it was needed to teach women not to draw attention so they could survive; but the time had changed, and the rope went on strangling, and someone had to cut it before passing it on. And Velada decided, that night, that it would be her, and that the cut was not against the mother, it was in favour of whoever came after, so that some girl or other of the serra would not have to reach forty-four, as she had, to untie alone, in the dark, a knot that had been tied for her without malice and had robbed her of half a life.
