## 10. The relapse

One does not undo a hiding-place of a lifetime without its calling again at the first thing, and Velada had her relapse, and it came in the form the relapses of those who are afraid of being seen always come in: at the first thorn of envy, the fear came all the way back, with thirty years of bottom, and Velada wanted to pull the name off the blankets and go back to veiling herself.

It came by the mouth of the Engrácia of the Valley.

The Engrácia was the other weaver of the serra, the one who signed with her initials well in sight, the one who went to the fairs, the one who had a stall. She wove well, the Engrácia, she had woven for more years than Velada, and her whole life she had been the weaver of the serra, the one who was named, because the best of all was hidden and did not count. And now the best of all had appeared, with a name in the corner and a trader of the city climbing up to her door, and the Engrácia, who was no bad woman, was only a woman from whom the first place had been taken without warning, could not bear it.

She said it in the Benvinda's grocer's, one morning, in front of people, loud enough, in the way one says things to reach the one who is not there. She said, folding her shawl with offended hands:

"That's right, now we have the Velada signing the blankets. The Velada, who for forty years wouldn't say her name to anyone, who was so modest, so without vanity, who wouldn't even go to the fairs. Modesty, they called it. Modesty. It took one woman from the city giving her a leg up and look where the modesty went. Now she even embroiders the name and all. There's those born to show themselves and there's those who spend forty years pretending not to be, waiting for the moment."

And the Benvinda, who was at the counter, opened her mouth to defend her, and the Engrácia had already gone out, and the saying stayed in the shop, and from the shop it climbed to the serra as sayings always climb, from mouth to mouth, bettered in malice at each mouth, and reached Velada at the end of the afternoon, brought by a neighbour with the face of one who brings a grief and likes to bring it.

And the Engrácia's saying touched Velada exactly where it hurt, on the exact wound, that of the tank, that of the Aurélia, that of the old sum. She's been pretending. She thinks herself something. It took someone giving her a leg up. It was the same accusation of always, that of the mouths of the playground, thirty years later, in the same serra, by another mouth; and Velada felt the ground flee from under her, and felt, with all the old force, the sum remake itself whole: to be seen is to be envied, to be envied is to be talked of, I was right to hide, you see, no sooner did you put the name than they envy you, they point at you, it is only the beginning, next they will leave you as the Aurélia left you. And the fear came all the way back, and with it the wish to draw back, to take off the name, to send word to the Matilde to go back to how it was before, without a signature, to the Souto, to the anonymity, to the loom in the dark, to the safety of not drawing attention.

And she almost did it.

She even took a finished blanket off the loom and put the scissors to its corner, at the edge of the embroidered name, ready to unstitch it. She even felt the anticipated relief of going back to the hiding-place, of leaving the sight, of giving no more envy to any Engrácia, of no one being able to say of her any more that she thought herself something. Because to hide was what she knew, was her home, and the new sight, for all the joys it brought, at the first thorn made her want to run back home, to the dark, to the wall of the crag. The scissors leaned against the first stitch of the V.

And it was a long night, that of the scissors, and the book must tell it because it was there the relapse was won or lost. Velada did not sleep. She went about the house, from the kitchen to the loom, from the loom to the chest, with the Engrácia's saying hammering in her ears, she's been pretending, she thinks herself something, it took someone giving her a leg up. Twice she took up the scissors, and twice she leaned them against the first stitch of the name, and twice her hand stayed stopped in the air, without cutting and without letting go. The first time, what made her stop was the ribbon on the beam, the faded ribbon she had pinned beside the loom, which she saw, by the light of the lamp, and which reminded her that once, at fourteen, she had cut, not with scissors but with the hiding-place, the most beautiful thing she had, and what that had cost her. The second time, already at daybreak, the ribbon was not enough, and what saved her was the open chest, and inside it the paper of the woman of the city, to Velada, of the blankets, thank you, make more, which she picked up with a trembling hand and read by the weak light, and on reading it she understood that she had in her right hand the Engrácia's scissors and in her left the paper of the woman of the city, the envy of one on one side and the thank-you of the other on the other, and that she was going, that daybreak, to choose which of the two would rule her life. And she set down the scissors. She did not let them go in relief, she let them go with effort, finger by finger, as one lets go of a thing the hand has gripped without leave, and stood in the kitchen with the paper in her hand and the scissors on the table, far off, and it was so the morning found her, on her feet, with the name still on the blanket, by a hair's breadth, by a paper, by a ribbon on the beam.

But she had now, against the fear, all she had learned, and the scissors stayed set down.

Velada set them down, and sat beside the blanket, and asked herself the new question, the one she had not known how to ask at fourteen: does this envy of the Engrácia prove that I should hide, or is it only envy, that of one who cannot bear to see another shine, and no reason at all for me to put myself out? And she remembered the Keeper, the voice of the Keeper in the House of the Register, the envy is the problem of the one who has it, not of the one who is envied; to put oneself out so as not to disturb the envy of others is not virtue, it is waste. And she remembered the paper of the woman of the city, to Velada, of the blankets, thank you, make more. And she set the two things on the scales: on one side, the Engrácia, an offended mouth in the grocer's; on the other, the woman of the city who had slept the best night of her life and had asked her to embroider the name bigger. And she saw that she was doing the of-always, letting one mouth of envy weigh more than all the thank-yous together, letting the Engrácia speak louder than the new life, letting the wound of thirty years rule the woman of forty-four once more.

And there was, in those days of the scissors, a thing Velada did not know at once and that reached her later, and that helped too, in the quiet manner of the serra. The Benvinda, on hearing the Engrácia say what she said in the grocer's, had not stayed silent as Velada had thought. She had stayed silent while the Engrácia was there, so as not to make a scene in front of people, but the next day, when the Engrácia came in again to buy her things, the Benvinda said to her, from the counter, without raising her voice, in a way that stayed. She said: oh Engrácia, I've known you for fifty years, and I've known Velada's blankets for nearly as long, and I'll tell you a thing as a friend, for your own good. Velada took nothing from you. Her place was always hers, only she had it hidden, and you knew it, we all knew it. Now that she shows it, keep yours, which is good, and let her keep hers, which is greater, for that is no shame; the shame is your wanting to put her out to seem the first yourself. Envy, Engrácia, only looks well hidden, and you brought it to the shop today in sight of everyone, and it was you who came out small, not her. And the Engrácia went out without answering, and what the Benvinda said to her ran the serra alongside what the Engrácia had said, and half the serra gave the Benvinda the right of it; and when this reached Velada, days later, with the scissors already put away, it was to her like a blanket laid over her in the cold, because she understood that she was not alone on the side of those who show themselves, that there was someone to defend her in the face of the envy, and that the Aurélia, after all, had had a thing that she, Velada, now had and had not had at fourteen: a Benvinda. At fourteen there had been no one at the counter to defend her from the mouths. Now there was. And that, she understood, made all the difference in the world, because what makes a hiding-place is not only the envy of the envious, it is the lack of someone to stay on our side when it comes.

And it was the Matilde, that time, who helped her not to draw back for good, with the frankness of one who sees from outside and does not carry the fears of the serra in her baggage. She came up to the serra on the Thursday, as she came, and Velada, hesitant, with the blanket of the scissors still at the edge, spoke to her of taking off the name, of going back to how it was before, made the whole roundabout before getting there, spoke of the Engrácia, of the saying, of the serra being small, of the people who talked. And the Matilde heard her to the end, and then said to her, straight:

"Velada. I'm going to repeat what you said to me, for you to hear yourself from outside. A woman envied you. And you, because of a woman who envied you, want to pull the name off the blankets, go back to the shadow, throw away everything you gained. Think well what that is. You are going to put out your name, give up the joy of all the people who love what you make, because of one single person who can't bear that you make it. Envy, dona Velada, is the price of shining, and it is a small price; whoever shines is always envied by someone, always, everywhere, and whoever puts herself out so as not to be envied puts herself out only to please the envious, who are the last people one should wish to please. Don't take off the name. Embroider it bigger, if you will, for the Engrácia to see it well. For the envy of one is not worth your light."

And Velada, hearing the Matilde, remembered a thing her mother had said to her once, against everything, without knowing what she said. The Idália, who preached to boast is ugly, had also a saying she used against the envy of others, and that one was wise: whoever envies you gives you the measure of what you are worth, for no one envies what is little. And Velada, who had kept the saying without understanding it, understood it there, with the blanket of the scissors on her lap: that the Engrácia's envy, instead of proof that she should hide, was proof of the opposite, proof that she had light, because the darkness is not envied, only the light; and that the thing she had spent her life fearing as the sign that she had stood out too much was, read aright, the sign that she was worth something. The mother, who had given her the rope of the hiding-place, had given her also, without noticing, the scissors to cut it, and Velada only now joined the two things.

And she understood that the Matilde and the Keeper, one of the city and the other of the book, without knowing each other, told her exactly the same thing the mother, without meaning to, had told her once, and that it was true. And she understood, there, with the blanket on her lap and the scissors set down far off, a deep thing about showing oneself, that she had not known before: that to show oneself was not a single gesture, embroider the name once and be done; it was a thing one has to re-choose each time the envy comes, and the envy comes always, because whoever shines is always envied by someone. What mattered was not never to be envied, which was impossible for one who shows herself; it was not to let the envy of one make her put herself out again.

And she did not take off the name. She took up the scissors and put them away in the box, far from the blanket. And on the next blanket, in a small gesture of stubbornness that was also of cure, she embroidered the name bigger, on purpose, a little bigger than the tiny one of the first; and she understood that to have at her side someone to tell her the envy of one is not worth your light was the difference between going back to hiding forever at the first offended mouth and learning to shine in spite of the envy, which is, in the end, the only way to shine.
