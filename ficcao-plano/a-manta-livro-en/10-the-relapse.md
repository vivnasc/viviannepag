## 10. The relapse

But one does not undo a hiding place of a lifetime without its calling again at the first thing, and Velada had her relapse, and it came in the form the relapses of those who are afraid of being seen always come in: at the first sign of envy, of a sidelong look, of a comment, the fear came all the way back, and Velada wanted to take the name off the blankets and go back to veiling herself.

It came when the envy, which at first had been nothing, appeared in a way that touched the old wound. There was a person, in the village, perhaps another weaver, perhaps someone who could not bear to see Velada stand out, who did or said a thing of envy, a malicious comment about the blankets with a name, about the Velada who now boasted, who thought herself something, who before was so modest and now went about signing; and the comment reached Velada, and touched her exact wound, that of the lost friend, that of the envy that had cost dear, and Velada felt, again, with all the old force, to be seen is to be envied, to be envied is to be left, I was right to hide; and the fear came all the way back, and Velada wanted to retreat, wanted to take the name off the blankets, wanted to go back to the anonymity, to the intermediary, to veiling herself, to flee that envy that confirmed what she had learned when young.

And she almost did it. She even thought of telling the trader that she did not want the name on the labels any more, that they should go back to how it was, without a signature; she even felt the anticipated relief of going back to hiding, of leaving the sight, of not drawing attention any more, of not provoking more envy; because to hide was what she knew, was her safe zone, and the new exposure, however many joys it brought, at the first thorn of envy made her want to run back to the hiding place, to the safety of invisibility.

But she had now, against the fear, all she had learned, and instead of retreating, Velada stopped, and asked herself the new question: does this envy prove that I should hide, or is it only envy, that of one who cannot bear to see another shine, and which is no reason for me to put myself out? And she remembered what the Keeper had told her, that the envy is the problem of the one who has it, not of the one who is envied, and that to put oneself out so as not to disturb the envy of others is not virtue, it is waste; and she understood that she was doing the of-always, letting the envy of one person put out the joy of many, letting a malicious comment weigh more than all the thank-yous she had received, letting the old wound speak louder than the new life; and she understood that if she retreated now, at the first envy, she was going to go back to hiding forever, and was going to lose all she had gained, the joy, the recognition, the existence, because of an envy that was, exactly, what the Keeper had said, the problem of the one who had it.

And it was the trader, that time, who helped her not to retreat, with the frankness of one who sees from outside. When Velada spoke to her, hesitant, of taking off the name, the trader said to her: Velada, someone envied you, and you want to hide again. But think: are you going to erase your name, go back to the shadow, give up all you gained, because of one envious person? Are you going to let the envy of one take from you the joy of all the others who love your work? Envy, Velada, is the price of shining, and it is a small price; whoever shines is always envied by some, and whoever puts herself out so as not to be envied puts herself out to please the envious, who are the last people one should please. Do not take off the name. Embroider it bigger, if you will. For the envy of one is not worth your light.

And Velada, on hearing the envy of one is not worth your light, understood that the trader and the Keeper said the same, and that they were right, and that she was relapsing, letting the envy govern her again; and she understood a deep thing about showing herself: that it was not a single gesture, to embroider the name once and that is that, it was a thing one has to re-choose each time the envy comes, which comes always, because whoever shines is always envied by some, and what matters is not never to be envied, for that is impossible for one who shows herself, it is not to let the envy of some make her put herself out again. And she did not take off the name. She left it, and even decided to embroider it bigger on the next blanket, and she understood that to have at her side one who would tell her the envy of one is not worth your light was the difference between going back to hiding forever at the first envy and learning to shine in spite of the envy, which is the only way to shine.
