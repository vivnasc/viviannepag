## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Velada — the veiled one — of the Highlanders, weaver, and the name was a fate: they called her Velada, the covered one, the one who is under the veil, the one who does not show herself, and she fulfilled the name her whole life, hiding herself, veiling herself, making the most beautiful work of the region and not letting anyone know it was hers; and the name, which was only a name, became her way of being in the world, covered, behind what she did, without ever appearing.

The register keeps who makes the beautiful things of the village, and there is work that is known without knowing who made it: Velada's blankets, in the register of Véspera, are among the most beautiful things the region produces, half the village sleeps warm by them, and almost no one knows they are hers, because Velada sells through an intermediary, refuses the fairs, does not sign; and the book, which records the most beautiful blankets of the mountain, barely knows how to bind them to the hand that weaves them, because that hand hid itself her whole life, and made of anonymity not a modesty, but a hiding place.

This is the fourth book of the shelf of the Spinning Mill, the shelf of those who ask who they are without what they do, and within it, it is the book of the one who is afraid of being seen. It tells the story of a woman who weaves the most beautiful blankets of the region and does not sign them, who sells through an intermediary, who refuses the fairs, and who, when someone praises a blanket before her, changes the subject with her heart racing; because the only time she showed herself, when young, with a prize-winning work, the envy that followed cost her her only friend, and she learned that to be seen was to lose. It is not a book against modesty; it is a book about one who hid behind what she did out of fear of being seen, and about the courage, the greatest for one who learned that to appear is dangerous, of embroidering her name in the corner of the blanket, first tiny, then less tiny, and of discovering that what one creates deserves to reach the world with the person attached to the name.

There is, in this book, a loom at the back of a stone house, turned towards the crag and not towards the road, so that the hand may stay in the shadow; there is a root of madder from which is drawn a red that has ash inside it and is slow to go out; there is a faded ribbon, won at fourteen and hidden at the bottom of a chest for forty; and there is, above all, a name yet to be embroidered in the corner of a blanket, which is what a work lacks to cease being an orphan. It is around these small things, a loom, a colour, a ribbon, a name of six letters, that the crossing of Velada is made, because it is always in the small things that lives turn, and hers turned on the day on which a needle, with the hand trembling, wrote in the wool what the mouth had not managed to say for forty years.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Velada who is afraid of being seen, who makes beautiful things and hides them, who does not sign what she creates, who changes the subject when praised with her heart racing, who learned, by a wound, that to show oneself is to lose, let her know, from the first line, what it took Velada a whole life not to know: that to hide does not protect, it impoverishes, because it takes from the world what we created and from us the right to have created it; that to be seen is not to lose, it is to exist; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which the greater courage is not to stay covered, it is to embroider the name in the corner of what we make, and to show oneself, without losing oneself, and to discover that what we create, and we with it, deserves to reach the world with its face uncovered.
