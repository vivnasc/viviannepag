## 2. The prize-winning work

Velada learned that to be seen was to lose in a single time, when young, with a prize-winning work, and the lesson was so hard that it lasted her life; because there are wounds of youth that teach a person to hide, and she hides forever, behind what she does, out of fear of reliving the day she showed herself and lost.

It was at school, or in some thing of school, when young, in some work, of the hands, of art, of the kind that were made and shown; and Velada, who even young had the knack, made a work that stood out, that was better than the others, that was prized, shown, praised in front of everyone; and it was, in that moment, the greatest joy of her life, to see her work prized, to be pointed out as the one who had made it, to be seen, at last, in what she had of best. And it lasted little, the joy, because right after came what she did not expect and that taught her the lesson: the envy.

Because the prize-winning work was followed by the envy of the others, and the envy, at an age when one is fragile, did damage. The schoolmates, who before were friends, changed with her; there was jealousy, there was malice, there was that drawing away of those who do not forgive another for standing out; and the worst, what most hurt, was that Velada's best friend, the only true friend she had, drew away too, out of envy, or out of not bearing the envy of the others, or out of not wanting to be on the side of the one who had stood out; and Velada, who had won a prize, lost a friend, and the sum, in the head of a girl, was done in a way that marked her life: I showed myself, I stood out, I was seen, and I lost my friend. Therefore, to be seen is to lose.

And it was that, the sum of the envy, the source of Velada's hiding place. The girl learned, in that episode, that to show oneself brought the envy, and that the envy cost dear, it cost the friendships, the belonging, the place among the others; and she decided, without words, with her whole wound, that never again. Never again would she stand out. Never again would she show herself. Never again would she let them see that she was good at something, because to be seen good brought the envy, and the envy took the others from her; and from that day Velada began to hide what she did well, not to stand out, to veil herself, to stay behind, to make herself invisible, so as never again to provoke the envy that had cost her the friend, so as never again to lose by being seen.

And she carried with her, into life, a tragic equation: to be seen is to be envied, to be envied is to be left, therefore to be seen is to be left, is to lose. And that equation governed everything. When she discovered her gift for weaving, and made beautiful blankets, instead of signing them, of showing them, of being seen as the artist she was, she hid them, sold them through an intermediary, refused the fairs, did not put her name; because to sign was to stand out, and to stand out was to provoke the envy, and to provoke the envy was to lose, as she had lost the friend; and Velada preferred to make beauty in the dark to risking the light, preferred the safe anonymity to the dangerous exposure, preferred not to be seen to being seen and envied and left, and made of hiding her way of being safe, without understanding that she was also, in the hiding place, losing, only in another way, slower, more silent.

And when, at forty-four, Velada began to feel the weight of the hiding place, it was to that day that she had to return, to the prize-winning work, to the envy, to the lost friend, because it is always to the wound that one has to return to understand it; and on remembering, she understood, for the first time, that her hiding was not modesty nor even a free choice, it was a scar, it was the girl of then still protecting herself from the envy that had cost her the friend; and she understood that she was letting a wound of youth govern her whole life, depriving her, at forty-four, of the recognition, the pride, the joy of being seen in what she did well, all out of fear of an old envy; and she understood that if she wanted, at last, to come out of the hiding place, she would have to do the most terrifying thing she could imagine, the thing against which she had built her whole life: she would have to show herself, to put her name on what she did, to risk the envy again, in order to discover, on the far side of the fear, whether to show oneself was really to lose, or whether that time, that of the friend, had been one, and whether she could be seen, at last, without losing.
