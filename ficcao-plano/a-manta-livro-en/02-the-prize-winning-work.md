## 2. The prize-winning work

The question she had put to the empty house did not fall silent, and that night, lying with her eyes open, Velada let it do what true questions do: go on opening doors backwards, one by one, until the first. And the first door was a schoolyard, thirty years back, and Velada knew it by heart, because it was there she had learned, at one stroke and for the whole of life, that to be seen was to lose.

She was fourteen years old, and she had the Aurélia.

The Aurélia was the farrier's daughter, a girl of frizzy hair and loud laughter who laughed at everything and was afraid of nothing, and was Velada's friend since the days of the primer, with the whole and unreserved manner with which one is only a friend at fourteen. They went hand in hand about the serra, they told each other things they told no one else, they slept sometimes in the same bed on winter nights, cold feet against cold feet, laughing low so the mother would not hear. The Aurélia called her Vê, only Vê, which was a pet-name she had made from her name, and no one else ever called Velada that again, and for many years, hearing someone say her whole name, it hurt her not to hear the Vê.

There was a thing between the two that Velada, in her bed at forty-four, still felt on her skin, and it was the grandmother's loom. It was the Aurélia who was the first person to whom Velada showed that she knew how to weave, before she really knew, when it was still only a playing with the threads of the old loom that no one used in the attic. The two of them would climb to the attic on rainy afternoons, and Velada would set up the clumsy warp, and the Aurélia would sit at her side, on the floor, passing her the skeins and inventing stories for the colours, this one is night, this one is fire, this one is the blood of a king. And when there came off the loom a thing crooked but with colour, the Aurélia clapped her hands as if it were the eighth wonder, and said, Vê, you are going to be the best weaver this serra ever saw, I know it, I have a nose for these things. It was the Aurélia, and no one else, who first told her she had a gift, and she said it with joy, without a shadow, from the bottom, because at twelve the Aurélia could still wish well to the brightness of her friend. That is why the loss hurt as it hurt, and hurt still: because the first person to celebrate Velada's gift was the same one the gift took from her, and the girl never managed to separate the two Aurélias, the one in the attic clapping her hands and the one at the tank who did not laugh, and she came to think, against all justice, that it was the gift that turned one into the other, that she had only to be good for whoever loved her to love her no more.

There was, that year, at the parish school, a show of handwork.

It was a thing done every year about springtime: the girls brought a piece of handwork, an embroidery, a lace, a piece of sewing, and the teacher, Dona Aurora, set everything out on a long table against the window, and the ladies of the Council came and the parish priest, and they gave a ribbon to the best. The Aurélia brought a kerchief embroidered in cross-stitch, with flowers in the corners, well made, of what was expected of an accomplished girl. And Velada brought a small blanket.

She had woven it alone, on an old loom that had been her grandmother's, all through the winter, by the light of the lamp, after the chores. No one had told her to. It came out of her from inside, in the same way all the others would come out of her her whole life: she began with a colour and the colour asked for another, and she went after the colours as one goes after a voice, and at the end of the winter she had in her hands a thing she herself did not know she knew how to make. It was small, the size for wrapping a child, with a madder red in the middle and indigo stripes at the ends, and the Aurélia, when she saw it, stopped laughing, which in the Aurélia was a great deal, and said, Vê, this is no work of people, this is the work of a fairy, take it to the show, take it. And Velada did not want to. Even then she did not want to. But the Aurélia took up the blanket and her hand and brought the two of them, the blanket and the friend, to the school.

And the blanket won. It won by far.

Dona Aurora raised it in the air in front of everyone, opened it against the light of the window so the red would catch fire, and said that in twenty years of the show she had never seen come out of the hands of a child a thing like that, and that this little girl had a gift, and she said the word gift in front of everyone, in front of the mothers, the ladies of the Council, the parish priest, who even came up and felt the warp and nodded his head. And they put the ribbon on Velada's blanket, and said her name aloud, Velada of the Highlanders, and for a moment, only one moment, in the middle of the yard, with the sun on the blanket and her name in the air, Velada felt the most beautiful thing she had felt in her life: she felt herself seen, and seen in what she had of best, and it was good as nothing had been good until then.

And she sought the Aurélia with her eyes, to share that with her, because it was with the Aurélia that she shared everything. And the Aurélia was looking at the ribbon. And she was not laughing.

It was only that, that day: the Aurélia was not laughing. Velada did not know how to read the sign, she was young, she was happy, she thought her friend had the sun in her eyes. She did not have the sun in her eyes. She had the first seed of the thing that was going to grow all that spring, watered by other mouths, because envy, in girls of fourteen, rarely comes alone; it comes helped.

The first to change were the others, those who were not Velada's friends and never had been. They began with sayings about the corners of the playground, sayings half-voiced, calculated for her to hear only half. That the blanket was not hers, that it was her dead grandmother's and she had only put her name to it. That Dona Aurora favoured her because Velada's mother did the washing at the teacher's house. That she had been full of herself since the ribbon, that she gave herself airs, that she thought a lot of herself. Velada gave no airs at all; she went about with her head down, lower than before, shrunk under the weight of a thing she had not asked for. But mouths do not care for the truth; they care for being right, and their rightness was simple and old as the serra: that one had stood out, and whoever stands out pays.

And the Aurélia drew away.

Not all at once, which would have been cleaner. It was little by little, in the way that hurts most, the way that leaves a person wondering whether she is imagining it. First she stopped calling her Vê. Then she stopped waiting for her at the school gate. Then she began to go about with the others, with the ones of the sayings, at first by force, like one defending herself, and then from within, because envy is catching and the Aurélia caught it from the others as one catches a fever. Velada saw her across the playground, in the midst of those who mocked, and saw in the friend's face a thing she had never seen there: shame at having been her friend. And she did not understand. What harm had she done. She had woven a blanket. Only that. She had woven a beautiful blanket, and because of a beautiful blanket she was losing the only person in the world who called her Vê.

There was a day, by the tank, on which Velada caught her alone and went to her, with the blanket of friendship still warm in her hands, to ask what was the matter, to beg her to come back, to say that she did not want the ribbon, that she would give the ribbon, that she would give the blanket, that she would give the whole gift, that she only wanted the Aurélia. And the Aurélia, leaning against the tank, without looking at her, said to her the sentence that Velada would carry her whole life, said not with malice, which was worse, said with weariness, like one who can no longer defend a thing:

"Look, Vê. Things were good before you were better than everyone. It's not your fault. But I can't. Beside you now everybody feels small, and so do I. It was easier when you were like us."

And she went away from the tank, and never again called her Vê, and at the end of the summer the Aurélia married early, as one married, and went to live in another parish, and Velada did not go to the wedding because she was not invited, and the friendship that had lasted since the primer ended beside a tank over a blanket with a ribbon.

And the sum was made in the head of a girl, and it was made wrong, and it stayed.

I showed myself, said the sum. I stood out. I was seen, I was prized, I was pointed out as the best. And I lost the Aurélia. Therefore, to be seen is to lose. The sum did not have the right weight, half its numbers were missing, it did not count the Aurélia's weakness nor the baseness of the others nor that a friendship undone by a ribbon was frail from the start; but the pain of a girl does not do just sums, it does the sum that eases her. And there is a thing children do with pain, and it is the most dangerous of all: they draw a rule from it. A hurt girl does not think I was unhappy this time; she thinks the world is so, and she puts the rule away forever, and spends her life obeying it without ever checking it again. Velada drew from the tank a rule, to be seen is to lose, and never again put it to the test, because to put it to the test was to risk being hurt again, and she built her whole life precisely so as not to be hurt, that is, she built her whole life so as never to have to discover whether the rule was false. That is why the rule lasted thirty years intact: not because it was true, but because Velada never again let anything contradict it. And that, she understood now in bed, was the most perfect prison there is, the one that keeps itself, the one that needs no jailer, because the prisoner, out of fear, refuses to try the door so as never to have to know whether it was open. And that sum eased her, because it gave Velada a thing to do so as not to hurt again: to hide. And she hid, and from that summer she made of hiding a law. Never again would she stand out. Never again would she let it be seen that she was good at anything. She put the grandmother's loom in a corner of the house and for years did not touch it, and when she wove again, already a woman, already with her hand better than ever, she wove inwards, in the dark, and did not put the name, and sold through an intermediary, and refused the fairs, and made of all that the wall behind which the blanket of the ribbon would not happen again.

And the grandmother's loom, that one Velada put away.

It was the saddest thing of that summer, and she did it alone, on a Saturday, with no one knowing. She undid the warp she had begun, rolled up the threads, and pushed the loom into the darkest corner of the attic, and covered it with an old cloth, and came down, and did not climb to the attic again for years. It was like burying a living thing. The loom had been her grandmother's, and the grandmother wove, and it had been the grandmother, before she died, who put Velada's hands on the threads for the first time, and said, this child has a spinner's fingers, this child will go far. And Velada, in covering the loom with the cloth, covered also the grandmother, and the grandmother's will go far, and the whole gift, because the gift seemed to her now the source of all that hurt her, and the surest way not to lose another Aurélia was not to be better than anyone in anything again. She set about making herself mediocre on purpose, hiding her hand, giving in her tasks just enough and never more, never lifting her head in the things in which she could shine; and for years the serra had a dimmed girl where it had had, for one spring day, a prize-winning child, and no one was surprised, because the world is always less surprised by a person dimming herself than by a person shining.

Only much later, already a woman, already with the house to herself, did Velada climb to the attic, take the cloth off the loom, bring it down and set it up in the back room, leaning against the crag, turned so that the light came in at her back and she stayed in the shadow, and weave again; but she wove now with the whole law learned, in the dark, without a name, through an intermediary, with her back to the door, like one doing a forbidden thing of which she is ashamed, though the thing was the most beautiful those hands knew how to make.

The prize-winning blanket, that one Velada never threw away. She kept it, folded in four, at the bottom of the chest, under the sheets that were not used, with the ribbon, already faded, still pinned to a corner. And she almost never took it out of there. But it was there. The proof that once she had shown herself, and of what that had cost her, folded at the bottom of a chest as one keeps a wound that does not heal because one does not let it be seen.

And it was to that blanket, and to that tank, and to the Aurélia who did not come back, that Velada had to go down that sleepless night, at forty-four, with the new question opening all the doors for her; because it is always to the first wound that one has to return, and on returning, for the first time, Velada understood a thing that made her sit up in bed in the dark: that her hiding was not modesty, nor even a choice of her own, it was a girl of fourteen protecting herself, still, beside a tank, from a pain that was thirty years old and had never finished hurting. And that that girl, with no one giving her leave, was governing the whole life of a woman of forty-four.
