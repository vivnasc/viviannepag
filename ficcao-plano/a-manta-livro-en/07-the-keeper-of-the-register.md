## 7. The Keeper of the register

She went to the House of the Register to see to some paper, and it was thus that Velada found herself seated before the Keeper, who knew the blankets and had never known how to bind them to the hand; and the Keeper, on understanding, at last, who she had before her, told her the truth that was missing, that of the name and the work.

The Keeper, on seeing to the paper, understood that that woman was Velada, the weaver of the mountain, and looked at her with a new astonishment, and said: so it is you? It is you who makes those blankets? I have one, at home, for years, one of the most beautiful things I have, and I never knew whose it was. And after all it was yours. And you went all this time hidden, without a name, making that beauty, and no one knew? And Velada, with her heart racing, said yes, that it was she, but that she did not like to appear, that she preferred it so; and the Keeper, who saw deep, looked at her, and told her the thing that made her stay.

She said: Velada, I keep in this book the things the village makes, and I learned a thing about what is created. A work has two parts: it has the work, and it has the name of the one who made it. And there are those who think that the name is vanity, that what matters is the work, and that to put the name is to boast. But it is not so. The name of the one who made it is part of the work, completes it, binds it to a life, to a hand, to a story, and without the name the work is left an orphan, it goes about the world without one knowing where it came from, and that does not magnify it, it impoverishes it. When you do not sign your blankets, you are not being humble, Velada. You are leaving them orphans. You are sending them into the world without a mother. And a blanket of yours, without your name, is half a work; the other half, the name, stays hidden with you, on the mountain, and the world receives the blanket without knowing there was a Velada, and you are left without what is owed to you, which is to be known for what you do so well.

And the Keeper said more, and it was what most shook her: and I know why you hide. It is not out of modesty. It is out of fear. Somewhere, in your life, to be seen cost you dear, and you learned to hide. But let me tell you a thing I have learned to see in this book: the fear of being seen is one of the things that most robs the village. Because there are people with enormous gifts, who hide, who do not show, who die without their gift reaching the world, out of fear of the look of others; and when there comes to me the entry of those people, it is one of the saddest I write, because it is the entry of a wasted gift, of a light that stayed under the bushel, of a beauty the world did not have because the one who had it was afraid to show it. Do not die so, Velada. Do not let your gift die hidden with you. Embroider your name on your blankets. Not out of vanity. Out of justice, towards them and towards yourself. Let the world know there was a Velada, and that these hands wove this beauty.

And the Keeper, on finishing, told her the greatest truth: and as for the envy, Velada, because I know it is of that you are afraid, let me tell you: the envy comes, sometimes, yes, when we show ourselves. But the envy of the small is no reason for the great to hide. If you hide your gift so as not to provoke the envy of those who do not have it, you are letting the envy of others govern your life, you are putting out your light so as not to disturb the darkness of others, and that is not virtue, it is waste. Show yourself, Velada. Let them envy, if they will; the envy is the problem of those who have it, not of those who are envied; and you were not born to put yourself out, you were born to weave beauty and to sign it, with your face shown.

And Velada left the House of the Register with the truth of the name weighing on her, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain of Véspera, and the fountain said a thing to her: that it gave its water to those who passed, in sight of all, in the middle of the square, without hiding itself, and that it was not ashamed to be seen giving, because to give in sight is not to boast, it is to serve with the face uncovered; and that the fountain did not hide its water out of fear that they would envy its source, it gave it, and whoever wished let them drink, and whoever envied let them envy; and that she, Velada, could be like the fountain, give her beauty with her face shown, in the middle of the square, with the name in sight, without shame and without fear, because what is created, like the water, is to be given with the face uncovered, and not hidden out of fear of those who do not know how to make it.
