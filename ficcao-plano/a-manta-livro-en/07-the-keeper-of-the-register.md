## 7. The Keeper of the register

It was not over the blankets that Velada went down to the House of the Register; it was over a paper, a matter of the house she had inherited, some bounds of land that had to be entered in the book. But it was so, seated on the far side of the oak table, with the light of the high window falling on the inkwell, that Velada found herself, without meaning to, before the Keeper, who had known her blankets for years and had never known how to bind the hand to them; and it was the Keeper, on understanding at last whom she had before her, who told her the truth she still lacked.

The Keeper saw first to the paper, with the patience of one who enters what lasts. She wrote in an upright hand, asked the names, dried the ink with the blotter; and at a certain point, on writing Velada of the Highlanders, weaver, she stopped the pen in the air, and lifted her eyes over her spectacles, and looked at her in another way.

"Weaver," she said, slowly. "You are the Velada of the Highlanders. The one of the blankets."

Velada felt her heart leap, and wanted to say no, that it was a mistake, but the Keeper was not asking.

"I have one of yours," said the Keeper, and set down the pen, and the talk of the paper was left behind. "For years. I bought it at the fair, from the man who sells them, and I spread it on my bed in winter, and it is one of the most beautiful things I have at home. And I never knew whose it was. The man said it was by a weaver of the serra and said no more. And after all it was yours. You went all this time up there, hidden, without a name, making that beauty, and no one knew how to bind the blanket to the hand?"

"I don't like to appear," said Velada, in a small voice, her eyes on the inkwell. "I prefer it so."

The Keeper stayed a moment silent, looking at her, with those eyes of one who spends her life reading what people do and writing it in a book, and who for that reason sees deep. Then she pushed the inkwell a handsbreadth aside, like one moving the small matter away to make room for the great, and said to her the thing that made her stay in her chair when she was already getting up.

"Velada. I keep in this book the things this village makes, for many years now, and there is a thing I have learned about what is created, and I am going to tell it to you because no one has told it to you and you have need of it. A work has two parts. It has the work, and it has the name of the one who made it. And there are those who think the name is vanity, that what matters is the work and that to put the name is to boast. But it is not so. The name of the one who made it is part of the work. It completes it. It binds it to a life, to a hand, to a story. Without the name, the work is left an orphan, it goes about the world without one knowing where it came from, and that does not magnify it, it impoverishes it. When you do not sign your blankets, you are not being humble. You are leaving them orphans. You are sending them into the world without a mother. And a blanket of yours, without your name, is half a work; the other half stays hidden with you, in the serra, and the world receives the blanket without knowing there was a Velada, and you are left without what is owed to you, which is to be known for what you do so well."

Velada listened, with her hands clasped in her lap, and the Keeper, seeing that she listened, said more, and it was what most shook her, because the Keeper, knowing nothing of the tank nor of the Aurélia, went straight to the wound.

"And I know why it is you hide. It is not out of modesty. It is out of fear. Somewhere, in your life, to be seen cost you dear, it cost you a thing you loved, and you learned, that time, to hide, and never again unlearned it. But let me tell you a thing I have learned to see in this book, at the end of so many entries. The fear of being seen is one of the ills that most robs this village. Because there are people with enormous gifts who hide, who do not show, who die without their gift reaching the world, out of fear of the look of others; and when the entry of those people comes to me, it is among the saddest I write, because it is the entry of a wasted gift, of a light that stayed under the bushel, of a beauty the world did not have because the one who had it was afraid to show it. Do not die so, Velada. Do not oblige me to write one day, on your line, that there was a woman in the serra who made the most beautiful thing of the region and carried it whole to the grave because she was afraid to put her name to it. Embroider your name on your blankets. Not out of vanity. Out of justice, towards them and towards yourself. Let the world know there was a Velada, and that these hands wove this beauty."

And so that Velada would not think it was only words, the Keeper did a thing she did not do for just anyone: she turned the book, leafed it back with care, with that care of one who handles lives, and stopped at a page, and laid her finger on a line, and said, read. Velada leaned forward, and read, in the hand of an old Keeper, already faded, an entry of a woman she had never heard of.

"This was the Esperança of the Lameiras," said the Keeper, low, as if she spoke of a dead one of the family. "She sang. She sang in a way that, say those who heard her, stopped the work in the fields when she let loose her voice, the men leaving the hoe to listen. But she was ashamed. She sang only alone, on the hill, with the goats, and when anyone came near she fell silent at once, with a red face, and said it was nothing, that it was only to pass the time. They invited her to sing in the church, at the feasts, and she never went. She was afraid of the look. She died old, and when her entry came to me, I, who was young, asked the Keeper of that time what I should write, because the Esperança left no work, left nothing that could be seen. And the old woman said to me: write that she sang. And I wrote that she sang, but there is no one who knows it, because no one heard her sing in earnest, and her voice, which was one of the most beautiful things this serra had, went with her down under the earth, and the world never had it, and it is as if it had never existed. This, Velada, is the saddest entry there is. That of the light that stayed under the bushel. And I do not want to write yours."

And Velada stayed looking at the faded line of the Esperança of the Lameiras, the woman who sang alone on the hill and fell silent when anyone came, and she went on recognising herself in her point by point, the shame, the falling silent with a red face, the it was nothing, and she felt a cold, because she saw, in that line, her own entry yet to be written, she saw herself carrying the blankets down under the earth as the other had carried the voice, and the world left without them, and it as if she had never woven anything.

And on finishing, the Keeper told her the greater truth, the one that answered the fear underneath, the one Velada had never told anyone and that the Keeper had divined beneath everything.

"And as for the envy, Velada, because it is of that you are afraid, I know it, let me tell you. The envy comes, sometimes, yes, when we show ourselves. I won't lie to you, for it is no day for lies. But the envy of the small is no reason for the great to hide. If you hide your gift so as not to give envy to those who do not have it, you are letting the envy of others govern your life, you are putting out your light so as not to disturb the darkness of others, and that is not virtue, Velada, it is waste. Show yourself. Let them envy, if they will; the envy is the problem of the one who has it, not of the one who is envied; and you were not born to put yourself out, you were born to weave beauty and to sign it, with your face shown."

Velada left the House of the Register with the truth of the name weighing on her breast as weighs a parcel that cannot be set down, and crossed the square slowly, without seeing anyone, and went and sat on the rim of the fountain of Véspera, the old fountain in the middle of the square, and stayed looking at the running water.

And while she sat on the rim of the fountain, there passed through the square a woman with a pitcher, and she stopped to fill it, and she was just any woman of the village, and she drank of the water from her hand, and went on, saying nothing to the fountain, without thanks, without even noticing that the fountain was there; and Velada stayed looking at that, the woman drinking and the fountain giving, and understood that the fountain did not wait for the woman's thanks, and was no less for not having them, and did not cover the water because the woman did not praise it; it gave all the same, to the next, and to the next, in sight of all, in the middle of the square, and was one of the most useful and most beautiful things of the village precisely for giving so, uncovered, without conditions. And Velada understood, watching the woman draw off with her full pitcher, that she, Velada, had done the opposite of the fountain her whole life: she had hidden the spring. She had been afraid they would envy her water and had covered it with a cloth, and given it through hidden pipes, through a go-between, so no one would know where it came from; and in doing so she had protected herself from nothing, she had only given her water with her back turned, without ever seeing anyone's thirst slaked in it.

And the fountain, in that way things have of telling us what we already know, said a thing to her, without words, only by its running. It told her that it gave its water to whoever passed, in sight of everyone, in the middle of the square, without hiding; that it was not ashamed to be seen giving, because to give in sight is not to boast, it is to serve with the face uncovered; that it did not cover the spring with a cloth out of fear they would envy its water, it gave it, and whoever wished let them drink, and whoever envied let them envy, for the water does not mind the thirst of others, it only runs. And Velada, with her hand on the wet stone, understood that she could be like the fountain: give her beauty with her face shown, in the middle of the square, with the name in sight, without shame and without fear; because what is created, like the water, is to be given with the face uncovered, and not to be hidden out of fear of those who do not know how to make it. And she rose from the fountain, and climbed the path slowly, with the afternoon going down on the serra, and along the way there came to her, one after another, all the voices that in those days had joined against the fear: that of the Matilde, your blankets deserve your name; that of the Keeper, you are leaving them orphans; that of the Esperança of the Lameiras, silent, under the earth, which she did not want to be; that of the fountain itself, which gave the water to the square without shame. And they all said, in different words, the same thing, and the thing was simple and Velada no longer had any way not to hear it: that to hide what one does well is not virtue, it is waste; that the name of the one who makes is part of what is made; that the envy is the problem of the one who has it; and that the life left to her, from there on, could be with the face uncovered, if she had the courage to live it so. And on reaching home, already with the first star, Velada did not light the fire at once; she went to the loom, in the dark, and put her hand on the blanket she had begun, and understood that that one, the one on the loom, was going to be the first to go out with the name in the corner, and she felt her heart leap, but that time she let it leap, and stayed with her hand on the weave until it calmed, training, there, alone, the thing she was going to have to learn to do: to stay, and not to flee. And it was so, with her hand on the weave in the dark, that there was settled a thing that trembled but was firm: that the next blanket was going to go out of that loom with the name in the corner.
