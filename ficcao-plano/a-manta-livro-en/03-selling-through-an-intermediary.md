## 3. Selling through an intermediary

The Souto came up to the serra on Tuesdays, and for twenty years Velada had measured time by those Tuesdays.

He was a man from the lower village, short and broad, with a donkey called Lampião and a way of talking a great deal that filled the silence of the house for an hour and then went away and left the silence greater than it had found it. He always came at the same hour, mid-morning, calling from the path ho, the house before he could even be seen, so that Velada would have time to make herself ready, which was a respect he had for her without quite knowing why, the respect one has for a person one does not understand. Velada already had the blankets folded on top of the chest, ready, counted, and he came in, took off his hat, sat down at the edge of the table, and began.

"Well then, dona Velada, how was the week. Mine was a dog's. Things down below are worse and worse, everyone haggles, everyone wants it for free. But these, oh, these sell." And he took up the first blanket, opened it, passed his hand over the make of it with the eye of one who understands the trade but not the thing. "This is one of the good ones. This will get me good money at the Penacova fair. There are people from the city there who pay, who pay well, when they see this."

And he counted out the money from the last time on the table, note by note, slowly, and took his share first, in front of her, without disguise, because that was the agreement and the Souto was honest within the agreement. His share was a good one. Velada watched it leave the top of the table every Tuesday and never said anything, because the Souto's share was not only for the road and the donkey; it was for the face. It was the price of Velada not having to put hers. The Souto did not only sell the blankets; he sold the boldness of standing beside them, and that, to Velada, was worth all he took.

But that Tuesday, with the sleepless night still upon her and the Aurélia still fresh in her memory, Velada looked at the arrangement in another way, and saw in it what she had never seen.

"The man who bought that big one off me at the last fair," said the Souto, folding the blanket back, satisfied, "he even shook my hand. Told me it was the most beautiful blanket he had at home, and that his wife wept when she spread it on the bed. Imagine, dona Velada, a woman weeping over a blanket." And he laughed, pleased. "I told him you're welcome, what else was I to say."

Velada was left with her cup halfway to her mouth.

The man had shaken the Souto's hand. The wife had wept, and the wife's thank-you had gone to the Souto. The most beautiful praise a blanket of hers had ever received had ended up in the hand of a man who could not tell madder from bale-dye, and the Souto had kept it, and brought it to her now like one bringing a message of little consequence, with no idea that he was handing back to her a thing that belonged to her and that had been taken from her without her noticing. The thank-you was hers. The woman had wept over a thing she had woven in a winter, and would never know it, and Velada would never see her face, and between the hand that made the blanket and the eyes that wept over it there had always been, and would always be, the Souto, with his hat and his Lampião, catching along the way not only his share of the money, but her share of the joy.

"He shook your hand," said Velada, slowly.

"He did." The Souto did not understand. "Good people."

"And said it was the most beautiful one he had."

"He did. I was all puffed up, look." And he laughed again, and his vanity, innocent, with no guilt at all, went into Velada like a needle. The vanity was hers. He had received it by mistake, as he received everything, and did not even know it.

Velada said nothing more that Tuesday. She paid what there was to pay, handed over the blankets, watched the Souto go down the path talking to the donkey, and stayed at the door understanding, slowly, with a clarity that hurt, that the arrangement that had seemed to her, for twenty years, a protection, was in truth a robbery, and the worst of robberies, the one done with our leave. The Souto took his share of the money, which was just. But he took also, and that was not in the agreement, Velada's share of the world. He took the faces of those who received her work. He took the thank-yous. He took the women who wept over the blankets spread on the bed. He took everything that fills one who creates, everything that pays for a whole winter of lamp and cold hands, and went down with it to the bottom of the serra, and Velada stayed up there with the share that no one wanted to take from her, the beauty yet to be made, the next winter, the loom in the dark.

And that night, after the Souto went down, Velada did a thing she had never done: she took a paper and a pencil and made the sum. Not the sum of one Tuesday, which she knew by heart, but the sum of the twenty years. How much the Souto had taken, Tuesday by Tuesday, year by year, of her good share. She added it up slowly, with her small handwriting, and the number that came out at the end frightened her, because it made a house, it made a life, it made far more than she had ever gathered in the tin of skeins where she kept her reserve money. Twenty years of blankets, and a good part of it had gone to the hat of an honest man, not because he robbed her, for he did not rob, but because she paid him, every week, the price of not appearing. And she looked at the number on the paper, and understood that that number was the price, in money, of one single thing: of the fear. She had paid twenty years of work to a man so that he would spare her the leaping heart, and the heart leapt all the same, and the money was gone. She folded the paper and put it away in the tin of skeins, beside the little she had there, so as not to forget how much it cost, in counted coin, to be afraid of being seen; and it was the first time she saw the hiding-place written as a number, and numbers, unlike feelings, do not let themselves be veiled.

And there was more, she understood, and it was worse than the money and worse than the thank-yous.

It was the relationship. It was the thing the Velada of fourteen had felt for an instant in the yard, before the Aurélia did not laugh: the joy of a real person receiving from our hands a thing we made with them. Whoever makes a blanket and hands it over in person, and sees the face of the one who receives it, and hears the thank-you, and knows that it is going to warm a certain bed in a certain house, is bound by a thread to that person, and that thread is the greatest payment of the craft, greater than the ribbon, greater than the name. And Velada, in selling through an intermediary, cut that thread at its birth, every Tuesday, with her own hands; she stayed a step from the joy of creating for someone, always a step, always hidden behind the Souto's hat, weaving for a world with its back turned.

And there had been, years before, a time when Velada had tried to be the one to hand over a blanket, one single time, and the time had gone in such a way that it left her grateful to the Souto for the rest of her life. A woman of the village, the Aninhas of the miller, had ordered a betrothal blanket for her daughter, and had asked, it being a thing of esteem, that it be Velada herself who brought it, by hand, to the house, on the day. And Velada, who hardly knew the Aninhas, thought that this time perhaps she could manage, that it was only a house, a woman, a blanket. She went down to the village with the blanket folded against her breast, and halfway, at the door of the Aninhas's house, with people at the window and children in the yard and the Aninhas calling to her from far off, ah, the weaver, come and see the weaver, her heart began to leap in such a way that her sight darkened. She was not able. She set the blanket down on the wall, by the door, told a boy to give it to the mistress, that she was in a hurry, and went back by the upper path, almost running, with the Aninhas calling at her back, ho, woman, ho, weaver, won't you come in then. And never again. From that day, she swore to herself that she would not hand over anything by hand again, that for that there was the Souto, and the Souto became, more than a seller, a windbreak, the wall she put between herself and the door of any Aninhas who might want to see her arrive with the thing she had made. The Souto handed over without his heart leaping. That was the part of him worth more than the money he took: he handed over without fear, and Velada paid him, without knowing it, so that he would not have the fear she had.

And she refused the fairs, which was the same flight by another door.

The Souto, now and then, tried to persuade her. Dona Velada, you ought to go just once, if only once, to see your own people buying your work, it would do your heart good. And there was a great fair, in August, the one of Our Lady, where the weavers of the serra all set out their stall, the Engrácia of the Valley with her initials well in sight, the others with their names on hand-written signs, and where, said the Souto, she would sell in one afternoon what he sold her in a month. And Velada said no, year after year, and gave outward reasons, that she had no one to mind her house, that the serra was far, that the sun did her harm; and the reasons were lies, and the Souto knew they were lies, and out of respect made as if he believed. The truth is that the fair was the place where the weavers showed themselves, standing beside what they had made, in sight, pointed out, and that was exactly what Velada had not done since a certain day beside a tank, and she preferred to sell less and hidden than to sell more and exposed, because what she spared herself at the fair was not the weariness, it was the risk of some Aurélia or other in the crowd ceasing to laugh at the sight of her full stall.

And once, only once, Velada had gone to a fair, not to sell, to look, hidden, with her kerchief drawn tight, in the midst of the crowd, to see what it was she fled from. She had gone to the great one, the one in August, years before, very early in the morning, before the heat, and had walked among the stalls of the weavers like one walking through a forbidden land. And she had seen the Engrácia of the Valley at her stall, selling, talking with people, saying it was I to a lady who praised her, and she had seen the joy of it, the full stall, the blankets going out with the initials in sight, and she had felt, in the midst of the crowd, a thing that made her leave there almost running: it was not envy of the Engrácia, it was longing for a thing she had never had. Longing for what her own stall could have been, if she were able. And she had understood, that day, that fleeing the fair did not spare her suffering; it only exchanged the suffering of appearing for the suffering, quieter, of seeing another appear in the place that could have been hers. And she had gone back to the serra with that new suffering, the worst of all, that of one who exiles herself from her own place and then sees it taken by whoever has less right to it and more courage.

And that Tuesday, at the door, watching the dust the Souto raised on the path, Velada weighed for the first time what the hiding-place cost her, and made the sum in earnest, and the sum came out the opposite of what she had always supposed. She had always thought she was protecting herself. She was depriving herself. The Souto and the refusal of the fairs were not a shelter, they were a wall, and on the inside of the wall she made beauty and on the outside the world received it without ever knowing where it came from, and she stayed alone, with the loom and the crag, giving the world the best she had and keeping for herself the fear, all over an old envy that perhaps would not even come back, or would come back and no longer cost what it had cost at fourteen.

And that Tuesday, for the first time, beneath the fear, another thing stirred. It was small, and it came with shame, but it came: the want. The want, just once, only once, to see with her own eyes the face of whoever wept over a blanket of hers. The want to hear the thank-you at first hand, and to be able to say it was I. The want, timid, barely born, to be the hand that shakes the hand, and not the shadow behind the hat of an honest man who took, without knowing it, the most beautiful part of what was hers.
