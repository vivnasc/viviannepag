## 3. Selling through an intermediary

Velada sold the blankets through an intermediary, and the intermediary was her mask, the face she put between herself and the world so as not to have to appear; and it was on looking at that arrangement, which had lasted years, that Velada began to see what the hiding place cost her, not in money, in life.

The intermediary was a man of the village who went about the fairs and the markets, and who fetched Velada's blankets from the mountain, and sold them, and gave her the money, keeping his share; and the arrangement suited Velada, because it spared her from appearing, from going to the fairs, from being seen, from selling herself, from hearing the praises and the haggling and from having to put her face on what she made. But the arrangement, which spared her from appearing, cost her two things she was only now beginning to see. It cost her money, because the intermediary kept a good share, and the blankets, sold by him, yielded her less than they would if she sold them directly. And it cost her, above all, the recognition, because the intermediary, in selling the blankets, received also, without meaning to, their credit; the praises went to him, or to no one, never to Velada, and the beauty she created reached the world with another's face, or with no face, but never with hers.

And there was, in this selling through an intermediary, a renunciation Velada had not weighed. She renounced not only the money and the credit, she renounced the relationship with those who received her work, which is one of the greatest joys of one who creates. Because one who makes a beautiful thing and hands it over in person, and sees the face of the one who receives it, and hears the thank you, and feels that what she made is going to warm, gladden, serve a real person, has in that a joy that fills; and Velada, in selling through an intermediary, never had that joy, never saw the face of those who slept warm by her blankets, never heard the thank you at first hand, was always a step away from the joy of creating for someone, always hidden behind the intermediary, always deprived of the meeting between what she did and those who received it.

And she refused the fairs, which was the other face of the same fear. The fairs were where the weavers showed themselves, put out their blankets, stood beside them, talked with the clients, were seen as the makers; and it was exactly that Velada did not want, to be seen as the maker, to be there, exposed, beside her work, receiving the looks, the praises, and also, she feared, the envy, the comparisons, the standing out that had brought her, when young, the loss of the friend. And so she refused the fairs, year after year, even when they told her she should go, that she would sell more, that she would make her work known; she refused, because to go to the fair was to show herself, and to show herself was what she did not do, and she preferred to sell less, hidden, than to sell more, exposed.

And she understood, on looking at the selling through an intermediary and at the refusal of the fairs, a thing that began to shake her: that she was not protecting herself, she was depriving herself; that the intermediary and the refusal of the fairs were not modesty, they were a wall, and that on the inside of the wall she made beauty and on the outside the world received it without knowing her, and that she stayed alone, on the mountain, with the loom, without the recognition, without the pride, without the relationship with those who received her work, all out of fear of an envy that perhaps would not even come, or that would come and not cost what it had cost when young; and that she was living a life of a hidden creator, giving the world her beauty and keeping the fear for herself, and losing, in the dark, all that the light could give her.

And there was a moment in which Velada imagined herself coming out of the hiding place, and the fear came at once, all of it, the image of the envy, of the lost friend, of the prize-winning day that ended in loss; but beneath the fear, for the first time, came also a wish, timid, the wish to be seen, to sign a blanket, to go to a fair, to hear, at first hand, a praise of her work and be able to say thank you, it was I; and it was that wish, born of the weight of the hiding place, the beginning of Velada's crossing, because a person only comes out of the hiding place when, at last, the wish to be seen in what one does well becomes greater than the fear of the envy that made one hide.
