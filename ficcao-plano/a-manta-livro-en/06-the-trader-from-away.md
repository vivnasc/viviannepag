## 6. The trader from away

There came to the mountain a trader from away, who discovered who wove those blankets, and who wanted to put her name on the labels; and it was she, with her insistence of one who sees worth where the maker sees only danger, who most pushed Velada out of the hiding place, because sometimes it takes someone from away, who does not have our fears, to see us as we do not dare to see ourselves.

The trader was a woman of the city, who sold beautiful things, craftwork, fabrics, and who had an eye for quality; and one day, in the larger town, she saw a blanket of Velada's, and stopped, because it was different, it was better, it was of a rare hand; and she asked whose it was, and the intermediary, or someone, told her that it was of a weaver of the mountain, one Velada, who lived hidden and did not care to appear; and the trader, instead of contenting herself with the intermediary, wanted to go to the source, to climb the mountain, to know the hand that wove that, because, she said, a work like that has a maker, and a maker has a name, and that name is worth something.

And she climbed the mountain, and went to Velada, and it was hard for the trader, because Velada, on seeing a stranger arrive who sought her, who wanted to know her, who wanted to put her name on the work, took fright, shut herself, wanted to flee; but the trader, who was persistent and who saw worth, did not give up, and said to Velada a thing she had never heard: your blankets are among the most beautiful things I have ever seen in this region. And you sell them through an intermediary, without a name, hidden, and that is a waste, it is almost an injustice, because a work like that deserves to have the name of the one who made it, deserves to be known, deserves to reach the world with its maker attached to it. I want to sell your blankets, but with your name on the label, Velada, with your mark, because whoever buys a thing like that wants to know who made it, wants the story, wants the hand; and you, by hiding, are not being humble, you are robbing yourself and your work of the name it deserves.

And Velada heard this, and it was a terror and a temptation at the same time. Terror, because to put the name on the label was exactly what she most feared, it was to show herself, it was to be seen, it was to risk the envy, the look, the loss; and temptation, because beneath the terror was the old wish, that of being seen in what she did well, that of hearing her name bound to her beauty, that of existing, at last, as the maker of that work, and not as a shadow behind an intermediary. And the trader, seeing Velada's struggle, did not force her, but left her the seed: think about it, Velada. I will come back. Your blankets deserve your name. And you deserve your name on them. It is not vanity. It is justice. What you create is so good that it deserves to reach the world with its face uncovered, and you deserve to be the face the world sees when it admires what you made.

And she went away, the trader, leaving Velada with that, with the your blankets deserve your name, and you too; and Velada, alone, with the loom, was left turning over the proposal, divided between the terror and the wish, and she understood a thing that the trader, without knowing it, had opened for her: that there were people, in the world, who saw worth in what she did, and that that worth asked for a name, asked for a face, asked that the maker appear; and that she, by hiding, was not protecting the work, she was diminishing it, depriving it of the name that completed it, because a work without the maker's name is an orphan work, and the maker's name is not vanity, it is part of the work, it is what binds it to a life, to a hand, to a story; and that perhaps to be seen was not, as she had learned, to lose, but the opposite, was to give her work, and herself, what they lacked, the name, the face, the existence with the face shown.

And for the first time Velada let in, in earnest, the possibility of showing herself, not because the fear had passed, for it had not, but because the trader had put before her a truth the fear hid: that her work deserved her name, that she deserved her name on her work, and that to hide, far from being modesty or protection, was an injustice, against the work and against the maker; and she began to desire, with her heart racing, to do the hardest thing of her life, to embroider the name in the corner of a blanket, and to let it go out into the world, for the first time, with the uncovered face of the woman who had woven it.
