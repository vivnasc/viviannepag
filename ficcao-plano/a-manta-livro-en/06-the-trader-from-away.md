## 6. The trader from away

There came to the serra, on an October evening, a trader from away, and it was she, with the stubbornness of one who sees worth where the maker sees only danger, who most pushed Velada out of the hiding-place; because sometimes it takes someone from away, who does not carry our fears in her baggage, to see us as we do not dare to see ourselves.

Her name was Matilde — mighty in battle. She was a woman of the city, of about fifty, of well-kept hands and a quick eye, who had a shop of beautiful things, fabrics, cloths, handwork of the serra, and who lived by knowing how to tell what was made for love from what was made by weight. She had seen a blanket of Velada's in a shop window in the big village, on sale on the Souto's account, and had stopped in the street, by her own account, like one stopped by an arm. She had gone in, asked to see it close to, opened it against the light, and understood, at the first touch, that this was no common fair-work; it was a rare hand, of the kind that appear once in a region and sometimes not even that. She had asked whose it was. And the shopkeeper had shrugged his shoulders: it's by a certain Velada, up there in the serra, but there's no use looking for her, that one doesn't show herself, she sells through a go-between and won't hear of appearing.

The Matilde, instead of contenting herself with the go-between, as everyone did, did what no one did: she went to the source. She found someone to take her up to the serra, climbed the path of the Highlanders at evening with her city shoes stumbling on the stones, guided herself by the sound of the loom, and knocked at Velada's door when the sun was already setting behind the crag.

Velada opened, and on seeing at her door a woman from away, of well-kept hands, who asked for her by name, who said she came over the blankets, she shut all up inside like a flower at night. She wanted to say there had been a mistake, that she was not the weaver, that she knew nothing. Her heart was already leaping. But the Matilde was not of the Souto nor of the Benvinda, she did not know the hiding-places of the serra nor respect them, and before Velada could shut the door in her face she had already put her foot on the threshold and the blanket from the window open in her hands, spread to the dying light.

"It was you who made this," said the Matilde, and it was not a question. "I have sold beautiful things for thirty years. I know a hand when I see it. This is yours."

Velada looked at her own blanket in the hands of the stranger, and could not lie to the blanket. She lowered her eyes and nodded, once, badly, with her head.

And the Matilde came in, because Velada, by not pushing her out, had let her in, and sat down at the table without asking leave, with the blanket on her lap, and said to Velada a thing she had never heard in forty-four years, said like that, straight, without the roundabout ways of the serra:

"Your blankets are among the most beautiful things I have ever seen in all my life of shops, and I have seen many a thing, in many a place. And you sell them through a go-between, without a name, hidden in the serra. That, dona Velada, is a waste. It is almost an injustice. A work like this has a maker, and a maker has a name, and that name is worth something, it is worth money and it is worth more than money. I want to buy your blankets, all you make. But I want them with your name on the label. With your mark. Because whoever buys a thing like this does not buy only the blanket; they buy the hand, they buy the story, they want to know that there was a woman in a serra who drew this red from a root. And you, in hiding yourself, are not being humble. You are robbing. You are robbing yourself, and robbing your work of the name it deserves, and robbing whoever buys it of the right to know whose it is they have."

And Velada, on hearing this, felt two things she already knew, the terror and the want, but with a force they had never had.

Terror, because to put the name on the label was exactly, precisely, what she most feared since the tank of the Aurélia: it was to show herself, it was to be seen, it was to risk the envy, the look, the loss. And want, because beneath the terror, in the same place where her heart leapt when the Benvinda praised her, was the old hunger, that of being seen in what she did well, that of hearing her name bound to her beauty, that of existing, at last, as the hand that made that, and not as a shadow behind the Souto's hat. And the Matilde, who saw from outside and for that saw better, perceived the struggle in Velada's face, and did not force her. She stood up, folded the blanket with care, and at the door, the night already falling, left the seed, said slowly so it would stay:

"I won't hurry you, dona Velada. I'll come back another day. But think on one thing while I'm gone. Your blankets deserve your name. And you deserve your name on them too. It is not vanity. It is justice. What you make is so good that it deserves to reach the world with its face uncovered, and you deserve to be the face the world sees when it admires what you have made. To hide a thing so beautiful does not make it more beautiful. It only makes fewer people have it."

And she went off down the dark path, in her city shoes, guided by the lantern of whoever had brought her, and Velada stayed at the door watching her go down, with the sentence weighing on her whole breast, your blankets deserve your name, and you too.

The Matilde came back, as she had promised, two weeks later, and that time she brought a thing in her bag, and it was that thing, more than the words, that entered Velada. She set on the table a folded piece of cloth, a fine shawl, of a silk Velada had never seen, and said, look at the corner. And in the corner of the shawl was embroidered a name, in small and steady letters, the name of whoever had made it, a woman of another land, and beneath the name the year. This is by a weaver I have sold for twenty years, said the Matilde. She began like you, hidden, selling through others. Today the ladies of the city seek her out by name, order from her with a year's wait, and this corner, this name in the corner, is worth half the price, because whoever buys it wants to have a thing made by her and not by any hand. See how the name does not spoil the cloth. It is not vanity, it is the cloth saying where it came from. And she let Velada take the shawl, and Velada passed her finger over the embroidered name of another woman, and felt a strange thing, an envy without malice, a good envy, the first of her life: the want to have, in the corner of what she made, a thing like that, a name that said it was I without shame. And she understood, with her finger on the other's name, that what she had always called modesty was only lack of courage to do what that woman of another land had done, which was to put herself in the corner of her own work and let it go.

And it was that night, after the Matilde went down, that Velada did not sleep. She sat at the loom in the dark, without weaving, with the shuttle in her hand, turning it over. And she understood, in the turning-over, a thing that the Matilde, without knowing it, had thrown wide open for her. She understood that there were people in the world, people she did not even know, who saw worth in what she made, and that that worth asked for a name, asked for a face, asked that the maker appear to complete it. And she understood that she, in hiding, was not protecting her work, she was diminishing it, sending it into the world lame of half, without the name that bound it to a life and to a hand; because a work without the name of the one who made it is an orphan work, and the name is not vanity hung on the outside, it is part of the work, it is the last stitch of the weave, the one that binds the blanket to the woman and the woman to the world. And she began to mistrust, that night, with her heart leaping merely to think it, that perhaps to be seen was not, as she had learned at the tank, to lose; that perhaps it was the opposite, was to give at last to her work, and to herself, what both had lacked for forty years: the name, the face, the existence with the face shown.

And in that turning-over, at the loom, in the dark, Velada did a thing she had never allowed herself to do: she imagined herself. She imagined herself doing what the Matilde proposed, in earnest, without putting it away at once. She imagined a blanket of hers, in a shop of the city, with a label, and on the label her name, and a lady picking it up and reading the name and asking who Velada was. She imagined herself standing at a fair, behind her blankets, saying it was I without fleeing. And her heart leapt merely to imagine it, there alone in the dark, as if she were already exposed, and she had to set down the shuttle; but, the leap past, beneath it, there stayed a warm thing, a thing that was not fear, and Velada recognised it, with astonishment, with shame, with a guilty joy: it was want. It was the desire, beneath everything, that that should be true. And she understood, that night, that the fear and the want dwelt in the same place of her breast, and leapt the heart in the same way, and that she had spent her life calling danger what was, after all, desire, and fleeing, in the name of fear, from the thing she most wanted.

And for the first time in her life she let in, in earnest, without pushing it out at once, the possibility of showing herself. Not because the fear had passed, for it had not, and was not going to pass so soon. But because a woman from away had put before her, in a single evening, a truth that the fear had hidden from her for thirty years, and the truth, once seen, cannot be unseen. And when the Matilde was about to go down, that second time, she stopped at the door and said to Velada a last thing, that stayed. She said: dona Velada, I have sold beautiful things for thirty years, and do you know what I learned? That the world does not have too many beautiful things. It has too few. And most of the ones that are missing are missing not because they were never made; they are missing because they were made and hidden, because someone was afraid to show them. Every beautiful thing that is hidden is one fewer in the world, and the world, which is hard enough already, becomes a little poorer for it. You think you are keeping yourself for yourself. You are not. You are keeping from the world your blankets, which it needs, because a warm night under a beautiful blanket is one of the few good and cheap things still left. Don't do that to us, dona Velada. Don't hide from the world what it needs. And she went, and Velada stayed with that, and understood, for the first time, that her hiding was not only against herself, it was against others, against the women who wept over her nameless blankets and against the ones who would never come to have one because she made few, hidden; and that there was, in the hiding, a kind of avarice she had never seen, that of one who keeps for herself, in the dark, a thing that was to be given.

And Velada, at the loom, in the dark, with the shuttle stopped in her hand, began to want, with terror and with want at the same time, the hardest thing she could imagine: to embroider the name in the corner of a blanket, and to let it go out into the world, once in her life, with the uncovered face of the woman who had woven it.
