## 12. Showing oneself without losing oneself

And the day came on which Velada understood, with her whole body, that she could show herself without losing herself, and it was the climax of the book, without a feast, as those of Véspera are: it was the day she signed a blanket with her whole name, in sight, without her heart racing, and understood that to show herself had not made her lose anything, it had given her all the hiding place had denied her.

Because that was the whole crossing, and it was now fulfilled: it was not only to show herself, for that she already did; it was to show herself without losing herself, it was to appear without ceasing to be who she was, it was to be seen and to stay whole, instead of the exposure destroying her, as the fear had promised her. And Velada reached there. She reached the point of signing the blankets with naturalness, of going to the fairs without distress, of receiving the praises without fleeing, of existing as a maker without that making her lose herself; and it was in that calm showing, without the racing heart, that Velada knew the veil had fallen for good, because the veil was not the hiding, the veil was the fear that showing herself would destroy her, and when showing herself ceased to destroy her and began to build her, the veil no longer had anywhere to hold.

And she understood a deep thing about showing herself without losing herself, that answered the wound of the friend. She had learned, when young, that to show oneself was to lose, because on showing herself once she had lost the friend; and she understood now that that had been a loss, yes, but a small loss, of a fragile bond, and that by fleeing it her whole life she had lost much more, she had lost herself; and she understood that to show oneself could make one lose, yes, sometimes, a fragile bond here, a sympathy there, the esteem of an envious one; but that this was little, and that in exchange it gave much more, it gave the existence, the recognition, the connection, the being whole in the eyes of the world; and that therefore to show oneself was not to lose, it was to gain much and lose little, and that the fear had lied to her in calling it losing, because it had shown her only the little that is lost and never the much that is gained.

And she understood the last truth, the one the Keeper and the trader had sown in her: that what she created deserved to reach the world with her attached to the name; that a work without an author is half a work, an orphan; that to hide what one does well is not modesty, it is waste, it is to deprive the world of what we have and ourselves of the right to have made it; and that to be seen, which she had feared her whole life, was not the danger she had learned, it was the most natural and most just thing in the world for one who creates, it was to put one's face on what one makes, it was to exist beside one's own work, it was to let the world know there was a Velada, and that those hands wove that beauty. And she understood that to show herself had been the hardest and most important thing of her life, because it had been to undo the veil that had covered her since she was young, and to become, at last, visible, existent, a maker with her face uncovered.

And she looked at the life left to her, the loom, the blankets with the name in sight, the fair she went to, the trader, the people who knew her now as the Velada who weaves the beauty; and she looked at it without the veil and without the equation that had veiled her; and she felt, for the first time in her life, the thing the covered one lacked, she felt herself seen, recognised, existent, beside what she made, and she discovered that this, which she had feared as the danger, was after all the joy, the joy of creating and of being seen creating, the joy of giving the world her beauty with her face uncovered and of receiving, in exchange, the recognition, the connection, the existence. And she understood that to take off the veil, which had seemed to her the greatest of dangers, had been after all the beginning of the visible life, and that the true phrase, the one that replaced the old equation, was not to be seen is to lose, it was another, it was to show myself is to exist; and that she, by embroidering the name, by coming out, by going to the fair, by showing herself, had not lost anything worth anything, she had gained herself, visible, at last, in the eyes of the world, which is the only way for a maker not to waste the gift she has.

And on one of the blankets she wove, in those days, Velada embroidered the name in the corner, and not tiny, and not less tiny, but whole, in sight, like one who signs without shame and without fear; and on embroidering the whole name, in sight, Velada knew she was healed, because she had just done, with calm, the thing that for forty-four years she had not dared to do, to put her face on what she made, to tell the world, in the corner of a blanket, with her face uncovered, without her heart racing: it was I, Velada, who wove this beauty, and I have, at last, the right and the joy to say so.
