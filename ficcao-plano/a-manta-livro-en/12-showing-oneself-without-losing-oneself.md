## 12. Showing oneself without losing oneself

And the day came on which Velada understood, with her whole body and not only with her head, that she could show herself without losing herself; and it was the climax of the book, without a feast, as those of Véspera are: it was a day of autumn, at the loom, with the November light coming in at the window, and it was a name embroidered, and it was a letter.

The letter came by the hand of the Matilde, who brought it from the city with the air of one who brings a thing whose weight she does not know. It was a hand Velada did not know, of a woman, already weary. It came from far off, from a parish away towards the border, and it said so:

Velada. If you are the Velada of the Highlanders, who weaves blankets, then you are my Vê. I saw a blanket of yours in the house of a lady, with your name embroidered in the corner, tiny, and I stood looking at your name a long time, because it is forty years since I called you anything and suddenly I had you there, in a corner of wool. I am the Aurélia, of the farrier, who left you beside the tank. I do not write to ask anything of you, nor to excuse myself, for there are things that are not excused by letter nor perhaps any other way. I write only to tell you a thing I should have said that day and said the opposite of. You were better than us, yes. And it was no fault of yours. The fault of not bearing it was mine, and the others', and it weighs on me still today, because I knew, when I left you, that I was doing wrong, and I did it all the same, because I was young and weak and afraid to stay on the side of the one who stands out. I saw your name on the blanket and I thought: good. Good that she came at last to put it. It took the time it took, but she put it. And I, who took it from you once, am glad to see it there, even from afar, even without the right. Yours, always, Aurélia.

Velada read the letter at the loom, with the November light on her face, and she wept, but it was not the old weeping, that of the tank, that of one who loses. It was that of one who receives back, at the end of forty years, a thing she had given up for lost forever, not the friendship, for that did not come back, but its truth: that the Aurélia had not left her for being too much, she had left her for being weak; that the fault had never been the blanket's nor the ribbon's nor the gift's; that the tank had lied to her her whole life, and the letter, at the end of forty years, came to give the lie to the tank with the hand of the one who had dug it. And Velada understood, with the letter on her lap, that that was the last proof, the one that was missing, of all the Matilde and the Keeper and the fountain had told her in other words: that showing herself had not made her lose the Aurélia; the Aurélia had lost herself alone, by hiding from the brightness of others, which is the hiding-place the other way round, and as sad as her own. The two of them had hidden, after all, each in her way, one from her own light, the other from another's light; and the letter was the Aurélia coming out, at last, of hers, to tell her good that you came out of yours.

And Velada answered the letter, a thing that cost her almost as much as embroidering the first name, because to write to the Aurélia at the end of forty years was to open a door she had thought walled up forever. She sat at the table at night, with the lamp, and wrote slowly, crossing out much, until there came a thing that was true. She wrote: Aurélia. I received your letter and read it many times. I do not write to say all is well, because it is not, I lost you at fourteen and there is no letter that gives you back to me. But I write to tell you that I forgive you, and that I needed to say it more than you to hear it, because I spent forty years hiding because of you, and it was not just for you to carry that, for the choice to hide was mine, and your weakness beside the tank, however great, did not oblige me to cover myself my whole life. It was I who did that. Now I am coming out, slowly, and when I saw that you, from afar, were glad I had put the name on the blanket, I understood a thing: that if you, who left me, are glad that I show myself, then to show myself does not drive away from me anyone worth anything, not even one who left me. I am going to send you a blanket, Aurélia, with my name in the corner, well in sight, for you to sleep warm and remember me as I was before the tank, your Vê, that you called me, and that no one else called me, and that is still here under everything. And she signed it, and sent the letter, and sent with it, days later, by the Matilde, who saw to its reaching, a blanket of indigo with the name embroidered in red in a corner, large, and it was the first blanket Velada gave in her life with the name well in sight to someone she had loved, and it was to the one who had left her, and it was that, and not another thing, that sealed the cure, because the exact place where the wound had opened was the exact place where she put, at last, the name.

And it was then, with the letter answered and the blanket on its way, that Velada understood the whole crossing, and understood it fulfilled.

Because it was not only to show herself, for that she already did, she went to the fair, she signed, she received the thank-yous. It was to show herself without losing herself. It was to appear without ceasing to be who she was. It was to be seen and to stay whole, instead of the exposure destroying her, as the fear had promised her beside the tank. And Velada had reached there. She signed the blankets with naturalness, went to the fair without distress, received the praises without fleeing, and none of that had destroyed her; it had made her more, not less. And she understood that the veil, after all, had never been the hiding; the veil was the fear that showing herself would undo her. And when showing herself ceased to undo her and began to build her, blanket by blanket, thank-you by thank-you, the veil was left with nothing to hold to, and fell, without her noticing the fall, as falls a cloth that no one needs any more.

And she looked at her own hands, once, in this new light, and saw them as she had never seen them. They were the hands of a weaver of forty-four, with the madder set into the folds of the fingers that even soap did not wholly take out, with the callouses of the shuttle, with the skin rough from a life of thread and of vat. Her whole life she had hidden these hands, had contrived that no one should see them at work, as if there were a shame in them. And now, in the new light, she saw that there was no shame in them at all, there was a whole life of beautiful work, and that those hands had warmed half the serra and people of the city and three children in a poor house and the altar of Our Lady, and that to hide hands like that had been the true pride inverted, that of one who thinks herself unworthy of what she does so well. And she put the two hands open on the weave, turned to the light, and did not hide them, and it was the first time in her life that she looked at her own hands without wanting to cover them.

And she thought of the ribbon, on the beam, beside the loom, the faded ribbon of forty years. She looked at it, in that new light, and understood that the ribbon and the embroidered name were the same thing, separated by a whole life: the two were the proof that she had made a beautiful thing and let it be seen. The ribbon she had hidden forty years at the bottom of the chest, out of fear; the name, now, she embroidered in the corner, in sight, out of courage. Between the two, deep down, there was no difference at all, it was the same girl, it was the same gift, it was the same beauty asking to be seen; what had changed was not the gift, it was she, who had learned, at the end of forty-four years, to do with the name what she had not known how to do with the ribbon: to let it stay in sight, and not to die of it, and even to live better for it. And there they were, on the beam and in the corner of the blankets, side by side, the ribbon of fourteen and the names of forty-four, with neither of them hidden, for the first time in her life.

And she looked at the life she had, from there on. The loom at the back of the house, turned now not only towards the crag but, when she wished, towards the door, towards the path, towards whoever came up. The blankets with the name in sight. The August fair, where she now had a place and a name. The Matilde, who came up on Thursdays and was now more than a trader. The Benvinda, who held her dear. The country woman who was going to sleep warm and would remember her. And even the Engrácia, who with time had stopped turning up her nose, because the envy, without the fear of the other to feed it, also dwindles, and one day, at the fair, the Engrácia came up to Velada's stall and said, dry but true, that red, how is it you draw it like that, and Velada taught it to her, and it was the peace possible between two women who had woven the same serra their whole lives. And she looked at all this without the veil and without the equation that had veiled her, and felt, for the first time in her life, the thing the covered one lacked: she felt herself seen, recognised, existent, beside what she made. And she discovered that this, which she had feared as the greatest of dangers, was after all the joy.

And there was a Sunday, in the village, on which there happened at last the thing Velada had fled her whole life, and that time she stayed. She went to mass, a rare thing, over a feast, and the church was full, and at a certain point the parish priest, at the end, thanked from the pulpit the weaver of the serra, the Velada of the Highlanders, who had given a new blanket for the altar of Our Lady, and asked, before the whole church, that she rise so that the village might thank her. And there was that murmur, and all the eyes began to seek, from bench to bench, and Velada felt her heart leap as it had leapt at the goddaughter's wedding, years before, when she had fled to the threshing-floor. But this time there was no threshing-floor to flee to, and this time, more than there being none, Velada no longer wished to flee. She rose. On her feet, in the middle of the church, with all the eyes upon her, with her heart beating, but on her feet, she let herself be seen, she, the Velada, the weaver, the one who made the most beautiful blankets of the serra, in sight of all the people who had received her work their whole lives without knowing her face. And the village saw her, at last, and bound the woman to the blankets, and there were those who said low so it's her, so it was her all along, and Velada heard it, and instead of the danger she expected she felt a warm thing rise from her breast, and understood that that, to be seen so, recognised in the midst of her own people, was not destroying her; it was putting her, at last, into the world, giving her a place of flesh among the others, making her exist in the eyes of those who had always slept by the warmth of what she made and had never known her name. She sat down when the priest finished, with her face warm and her heart calming, and it was the first time in her life that she let herself be seen whole by the whole village without fleeing, and the sky did not fall, and the church went on, and she went on, more whole than she had entered.

And she understood the last truth, the one the Keeper and the Matilde had sown in her and life had proved to her: that what she created deserved to reach the world with her attached to the name; that a work without an author is half a work, an orphan; that to hide what one does well is not modesty, it is waste, it is to deprive the world of what we have and ourselves of the right to have made it. And that the sentence that had governed her life, to be seen is to lose, was false, and the true one, the one that replaced it, was neither hard nor long, it was three words: to show myself is to exist.

And there was, in those days, a small thing that for Velada was worth a life. The Matilde came up to the serra with a message from the city: they wanted to put a blanket of Velada's in a shelf, in one of those houses where they keep the things a land makes and that deserve to remain, beside the names of those who made them. The shelf of the Spinning Mill, they called it, the shelf of those who spin and weave and leave the name in what they spin and weave. And they asked for a blanket with the name well visible, and a line about the one who made it, to set beside it. And Velada, who her whole life had fled any shelf, any show, any place where her name would stand beside what she made, that time did not flee. She chose the best blanket she had, embroidered the whole name on it, and wrote, with her hand trembling only a little, the line they asked for: Velada, of the Highlanders, weaver, who weaves the blankets of the serra and, at the end of a covered life, learned to put her name to them. And she sent it, and the blanket went to the shelf, with the name in sight, beside the line, and stayed there, in sight of whoever passed, she who for forty-four years had not let anyone bind a blanket of hers to the hand that made it. And on learning that her blanket was in the shelf with her name, Velada did not feel the leaping heart. She felt a deep peace, the peace of one who reaches, at last, the place she fled her whole life and discovers it was a good place.

And on one of the blankets she wove in those days, the most beautiful she had made until then, a field of deep indigo with a path of madder down the middle, like that first one of the school but made by hands forty years older, Velada embroidered the name in the corner. And not tiny, and not less tiny, but whole, in sight, in red on the blue, the size of one who signs without shame and without fear. Velada, of the Highlanders. And on embroidering it, stitch by stitch, with a steady hand, without her heart leaping, seated at the loom with the November light on her face and the letter of the Aurélia in the chest beside the prize-winning blanket, Velada knew she was healed; because she had just done, with calm, in sight, the thing that for forty-four years she had not dared to do: to put her face on what she made, and to tell the world, in the corner of a blanket, with her face uncovered, it was I, Velada, who wove this beauty, and I have, at last, the right and the joy to say so.
