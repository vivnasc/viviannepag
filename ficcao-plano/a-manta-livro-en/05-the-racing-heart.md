## 5. The racing heart

There was a small sign that said everything, and Velada knew it by heart, because it was her body doing it without her leave: when someone praised a blanket before her, her heart leapt in her breast, the blood rose to her face, and she changed the subject in a hurry, or left, like one fleeing a danger. And the cruellest of it is that she fled the thing she most wanted.

It happened many times, and Velada remembered the times one by one, because each had left her the taste of the same shame.

She remembered a christening, years before, of a great-niece, at which the child's mother, not knowing she had the weaver at the table, spread over the cradle, in the midst of the feast, a blanket she had bought at the fair from the Souto without knowing whose it was, and said aloud, to the whole room, look at this beauty I got for my boy, this is what a blanket is. And half a dozen mouths praised it, how lovely, what colours, and the blanket was Velada's, and Velada was three steps off with her plate in her hand, and felt her heart begin to leap as if they had caught her doing harm to someone. The heat rose to her neck, to her face, to her ears. Everyone was looking at the blanket, but to Velada it seemed everyone was looking at her, that from one moment to the next someone was going to tie the blanket to the woman and point at her, and she could not bear it. She set the plate down, said she was going to see to the water on the fire, and went out into the yard, and stayed out there, leaning against the wall, with her heart beating like a bird shut in a hand, until the talk inside changed subject. She went back when they were no longer speaking of the blanket. That was how she measured the safety of a place: by how long was left before they stopped speaking of what she did.

She remembered a woman, in the grocer's, who, picking up a sample of wool, said to her, knowing nothing, you who understand these things, who is the good weaver hereabouts in the serra, they say there's one who makes blankets that are a marvel but that nobody knows who she is. And Velada, who was the weaver they were asking about, felt her heart bolt, lowered her eyes to the wool, and answered, with her voice failing her, I don't know, my lady, I'm not from here, and she lied in her own land, and left the shop without buying what she had come to buy, trembling, because they had put before her the recognition she most desired and she had fled from it as from a dog.

And she remembered, above all, a wedding, that of a goddaughter, at which she herself had given the blanket, and at which she was almost, almost, caught in front of everyone. She had woven the blanket on purpose for the goddaughter, the best she knew how to make, and had given it without saying it was hers, wrapped in paper, without a card, because even to give cost her in the open. And in the middle of the banquet the bride opened the gifts before all the tables, and on reaching the blanket she opened it in the air, and a murmur ran through the room, that murmur that beautiful things make, and the bride's mother-in-law, a woman of many words, stood up and said aloud, but this is from a master's hand, this you can't buy anywhere, who was the soul who gave this, let her stand up for I want to shake her hand. And all the eyes of the room began to seek, from table to table, who would stand up. And Velada was at the end of a back table, with her heart beating in her throat as never before, with the certainty that the goddaughter, innocent, was going to point at her at any moment, it was my godmother, it was my godmother. And she felt the blood rise to her face, and the air fail her, and she did the only thing she knew how to do: she stood up, yes, but to leave, saying she was going for air, that she was stifling, and went out of the room with the blanket being passed from hand to hand behind her, praised by mouths she heard through the door, and went out there, leaning against the wall of the threshing-floor, with her heart hammering at her breast, she who had received the greatest praise of her life, with her back turned, alone, hidden from her own glory. The goddaughter came looking for her later, distressed, godmother, you fled, and Velada said it had been a faintness, and the goddaughter believed her, because no one imagines a person fleeing being praised as one flees a danger; and no one in the room ever knew that the soul of a master's hand whose hand the mother-in-law wished to shake had been the whole time three tables away, trembling, praying not to be seen.

And there was a scene that hurt her in another way, and it was to see another receive what she could not receive. Once, at the small winter fair, the one she only crossed in passing, with her kerchief drawn tight, she saw the Engrácia of the Valley receive a praise at her stall. A lady from the city had picked up a blanket of the Engrácia's, and praised it, and the Engrácia, instead of fleeing, instead of changing the subject, had smiled, and said thank you, it was I who made it, and even added, with a calm pride, this one took me three weeks, look at the stripe, and the lady had stayed to listen, charmed, and had bought the blanket, and had gone off happy, and the Engrácia had stayed happy too, the two happy, one in giving and the other in receiving, without the sky falling, without any envy coming, without anything bad happening. And Velada, leaning against a corner, watching that, felt a thing that was not envy of the Engrácia, it was envy of a capacity of the Engrácia's, that of staying in the praise without the heart leaping, that of saying it was I with naturalness, that of receiving what one has given. The Engrácia wove less well than Velada, the whole serra knew it, but she had a thing Velada did not have, and that was worth more than the hand: she had no fear. And Velada, at that corner, understood for the first time that perhaps her suffering was not to make worse, for she made better, it was not being able to stand where the Engrácia stood, on this side of the praise, receiving it, and that the better hand was no good to her if the better hand lived hidden and the worse lived in the light.

And there was, in this flight, a deep sadness, that of one who cannot receive what she most desires.

For Velada desired the recognition, of course she desired it, everyone who creates desires it, it is as natural as being thirsty. But she had such fear of it, because of the Aurélia and the voice of the Idália, that when the recognition came she could not receive it; she fled from it, pushed it away, oh it's nothing much, and lost it. It was to be thirsty and flee the water. It was to have the jug before her, full, and cover her mouth. And Velada lived that contradiction every day, that of desiring to be seen and fleeing being so, that of wanting the praise and repelling it, that of having a gift that asked to be shown and locking it in the dark; and that contradiction had a name in her body, and the name was the racing heart, was the flesh wanting and fearing at the same time, running to the water and fleeing it in the same gesture.

And her body knew this better than her head, and gave signs she had learned to read. The leaping heart was the greatest, but it was not the only one. There was the heat that rose from her neck to her face, and that she covered with the kerchief; there was the mouth that went dry of a sudden, so that the sentence it was I, even if she wished to say it, came out caught; there were the hands, that set about doing something, smoothing the apron, tidying what was not untidy, feigning an occupation to excuse themselves leaving there. Velada's body set up, at each praise, a whole small flight, rehearsed of so many years, and did it so fast that she no longer even decided to flee, she found herself already outside, on the threshing-floor, in the yard, in the street, with her heart beating and without quite remembering having got up. It was a body trained to disappear, like that of an animal that at the first shadow is already in its burrow without having thought of running. And Velada understood, at forty-four, that she was going to have to untrain the body as slowly as she had trained it, point by point, praise by praise, teaching it, by force, to do the opposite of what it had learned: to stay.

And there was a day on which the racing heart hurt her more than usual, and it was that day that stayed.

It was on a winter afternoon, at the house of the Benvinda, of the grocer's, who had called her to mend an old blanket that was falling apart, one she held dear, her mother's. Velada went, and was looking at the blanket to see what could be saved, when the Benvinda, watching her work, said to her, low, not to change the subject, but in earnest, looking her in the eyes:

"Velada. I know you don't like it said. But I'm going to say it, just once, and then I'll hold my tongue. You make the most beautiful thing that comes out of this serra. I see many a blanket pass through people's hands, year after year, and when one of yours passes I know it at once, without seeing any corner, I know it by the colour. And it grieves me, woman. It grieves me to see the most beautiful thing of the serra go out of here without a name, as if it were nobody's, as if you were nobody. You are somebody. Your blankets say it, and you won't let it be said."

And Velada felt her heart leap, as always, and her hand trembled on the old blanket, and her mouth was already halfway through the of-always, ah Benvinda, don't say that, this is nothing, leave off. She was already going to say it. And she said it. She changed the subject, spoke of the old blanket, of what could be saved and what could not, and the Benvinda, who was of the serra and respected the hiding-places of her own, let her change the subject and held her tongue, as she had promised.

But that time, climbing home in the dark, with the cold cutting, Velada did not feel the of-always relief, the relief of one who has escaped the look. She felt the opposite. She felt empty, and sad, and cheated by herself, because she understood, climbing the path, that she had just refused a good thing, a sincere praise from the Benvinda, one of the few people in the world who truly held her dear, and that she had refused it not out of modesty, out of fear, and that she was going to go on refusing it, time after time, in every Benvinda that life put before her, as long as she lived hidden. And she understood, stopping halfway up the path with her breath steaming in the cold, a new thing about the racing heart: that the leap was not only fear. It was also desire. It was the heart wanting that which she fled from. The heart leapt because the water was near, not because it was poison. And perhaps, she thought, and it was the first time she thought it, perhaps, if she bore the leap, if she stayed in the praise instead of fleeing, if she let the heart beat until it calmed of itself as the bird calms when the hand is opened, perhaps she would discover, on the far side of the leap, not the danger she had learned to fear, but the joy she had always, underneath, desired.

And she began, from that night, to mistrust her own fear, to mistrust that it lied to her. She began to suspect that the racing heart was not a warning of danger, but the betrayal of a desire; that to be seen perhaps was not to lose; that the water she had fled her whole life was, after all, water. And she began to want, still timidly, still with her heart leaping merely to think it, to do one day the thing the fear had forbidden her since she was fourteen: to stay in the praise instead of fleeing it; to bear the look instead of avoiding it; and, more than anything, to put her name in the corner of a blanket, and to let it go out into the world saying who had made it, and to see with her own eyes whether the heart, in beating so, was warning her of a fall or calling her to a life.
