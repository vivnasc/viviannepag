## 5. The racing heart

When someone praised a blanket before Velada, she changed the subject with her heart racing, and it was in that small gesture, that of fleeing the praise, that one saw, whole, the fear of being seen; because there are those who so desire recognition and so fear it that, when it comes, they flee from it with their heart beating like one who flees a danger.

It happened many times. Velada, in the village, or in someone's house, or wherever, and someone, without knowing she was the weaver, or knowing it, praised a blanket, what beauty, what work, and Velada, instead of receiving the praise, of savouring it, of saying thank you, felt her heart race, the blood rise to her face, a physical distress, and changed the subject in a hurry, or deflected the praise, oh it is nothing much, or left; because the praise, which she so desired deep down, when it came, frightened her, put her at the centre of attention, made her be seen, and to be seen was the danger, and Velada fled the praise as one flees a danger, with her heart racing.

And there was, in this racing heart, a deep sadness, that of one who cannot receive what she most desires. Because Velada desired the recognition, of course she desired it, every creator desires it, it is natural; but she was so afraid of it, because of the old envy and the voice of the mother, that when it came she could not receive it, she fled from it, refused it; and it was like being thirsty and fleeing the water, being hungry and pushing away the plate, desiring a thing and fleeing it when it comes; and Velada lived that cruel contradiction, that of desiring to be seen and of fleeing being so, that of wanting the praise and of repelling it, that of having a gift that asked to be shown and of hiding it, and that contradiction was the racing heart, was the body desiring and fearing at the same time, wanting to appear and fleeing the appearance.

And she understood, on noticing the racing heart, a thing about the fear of being seen, that answered everything. The fear was not of being seen badly; it was of being seen, full stop. Because to be seen, for Velada, whether for good or for ill, meant to expose herself, to be at the mercy of the look of others, and the look of others, in her head, was dangerous, brought the envy, the judgement, the comparison, the loss; and so even the praise, which was a good look, frightened her, because it was a look, and any look put her in danger. And Velada understood that she lived hidden from the look of others, from all looks, bad and good, and that that flight from the look left her alone, because the look of others, which she feared, is also the one that sees us, that recognises us, that makes us exist in the eyes of the world, and one who flees all looks flees also from being seen with love, from being recognised, from existing; and Velada, in fleeing the look out of fear of the bad, lost also the good, and stayed invisible, seen by no one, neither badly nor well, which is a form of not existing.

And there was a day on which the racing heart hurt her more than usual. Someone praised a blanket of hers in front of her, and Velada, as always, fled the praise, changed the subject, with her heart beating; but that time, afterwards, alone, instead of relieved at having escaped the look, she felt sad, empty, because she understood that she had just refused a good thing, a sincere praise of her work, a thing she deserved and desired, and that she had refused it out of fear, and that she was going to go on refusing it, time after time, as long as she lived hidden; and she understood that the racing heart was not only fear, it was also desire, it was the heart wanting that which it fled from, and that perhaps, if she bore the racing heart, if she stayed in the praise instead of fleeing it, if she received the look instead of avoiding it, she would discover, on the far side of the heart's racing, not the danger she feared, but the joy she desired, that of being seen, at last, in what she did well.

And it was from that day on that Velada began to suspect that her fear lied to her, that to be seen perhaps was not to lose, that the racing heart perhaps was not warning her of a danger, but betraying a desire; and she began to want, timidly, to do the thing the fear forbade her, to stay in the praise instead of fleeing, to bear the look instead of avoiding it, and, more than anything, to put her name on what she did, to sign a blanket, to come out of the hiding place, and to risk discovering whether to be seen was the danger she had learned, or the joy that, beneath the fear, she had always desired.
