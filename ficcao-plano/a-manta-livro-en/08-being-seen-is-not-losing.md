## 8. Being seen is not losing

What Velada still lacked, to be able to show herself, was to dismantle the equation that had bound her since she was young: to be seen is to be envied, to be envied is to be left, therefore to be seen is to lose; and while that equation governed, each praise would be a danger and each signature a threat, and she would stay covered.

And she began to dismantle it, piece by piece, with the help of what the trader and the Keeper had told her. The first piece was to be seen is to be envied, and Velada understood that it was false thus, in the absolute: to be seen brings sometimes the envy, yes, but it brings also, and much more, the admiration, the recognition, the affection, the connection; the envy is a reaction of some, of the envious, not of all, and most people, on seeing a beautiful thing, do not envy, they admire, and they want to know who made it, and they are grateful; and Velada, in fearing the envy of some, forgot the admiration of many, and measured being seen by the worst possible reaction, the envy, when most of the reactions would be good.

The second piece was to be envied is to be left, and Velada understood that this had been true once, with the friend, when young, but that it was not the law. The friend had left her out of envy, or out of weakness, and that had hurt; but that said more about the friend, or about the fragility of a friendship of youth, than about being seen; and true friends, Velada understood, do not leave us for standing out, on the contrary, they rejoice, they are proud, and whoever leaves us for shining was no true friend, was a fragile bond the shine undid, and that is not worth keeping at the cost of putting ourselves out. And Velada understood that, by hiding so as not to lose others, she was keeping bonds that only held up with her put out, and that those bonds were not worth the putting-out, and that the good bonds, the ones that were worth it, those bore her shining, and even liked her more for shining.

And the third piece, the conclusion, to be seen is to lose, fell with the other two. Velada understood that to be seen was not to lose, it was the opposite, it was to gain: to gain the recognition, the pride, the connection with those who receive what we make, the existence in the eyes of the world; and that what she had called losing, when young, had been the loss of a fragile friend, a price, yes, but a small one beside all that the hiding had cost her since then, a whole life of invisibility, of denied recognition, of beauty given to the world without a name; and that if she did the sum in earnest, the hiding had cost her much more than the showing had ever cost her, and that she had been, her whole life, fleeing a small danger and falling, by fleeing it, into a much greater loss, that of never having been seen in what she did well.

And she understood a deep thing about the fear of being seen, that freed her: that to hide had not protected her from losing, it had made her lose in another way. She had hidden so as not to lose others to the envy, and by hiding she had lost herself, had lost the recognition, the pride, the joy of creating with the face shown, had lost years of visible existence; and that therefore there was, in hiding, no protection at all, there was only one loss traded for another, the visible and noisy loss of the envy traded for the silent and slow loss of invisibility, and that the second, by being silent, seemed smaller, but was greater, because it lasted the whole life. And Velada chose, at last, the first loss, if it came, over the second: she chose to risk the envy of some to gain the visible existence, she chose to show herself and lose perhaps one or another fragile bond, over hiding and losing herself whole; because she understood that between being seen and envied by some and never being seen by anyone, the first, however much it hurt, was life, and the second was a slow death pretending to be safety.

And she decided to show herself. Not suddenly, not all at once, for that would be too much for one who had veiled herself her whole life; but to begin, to take the first step, to do the first thing, which she already knew what it was, which the trader had put before her and the Keeper had confirmed: to embroider the name in the corner of a blanket, tiny, to begin, and to let it go out into the world, for the first time, with the uncovered face of the woman who had woven it, and to see, on the far side of the fear, whether to be seen was really to lose, or whether it was, as she was beginning to suspect, the thing she had her whole life, beneath the fear, desired: to exist.
