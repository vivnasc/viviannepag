## 8. Being seen is not losing

Between telling the fountain she would embroider the name and embroidering it, Velada still had to undo a thing, and it was the deepest of all: the sum she had made beside the tank at fourteen and that had governed everything since. To be seen is to be envied; to be envied is to be left; therefore to be seen is to lose. While that sum stood, each praise would be a danger and each embroidered name a threat, and Velada would stay covered by however many fountains and Keepers pushed her. The sum had to fall. And to make it fall, Velada did a thing she had not done for years: she went to the chest.

She took out the sheets that were not used, and there at the bottom, folded in four, with the ribbon already the colour of nothing pinned to a corner, was the prize-winning blanket. The Aurélia's blanket. Velada spread it on the bed, in the light of the window, for the first time in a long while, and sat to look at it, and it was looking at that red of forty years, the first beauty her hands had made, that she dismantled, piece by piece, the sum that that same blanket had taught her.

The first piece was to be seen is to be envied.

And Velada, looking at the blanket, saw that it was false thus, in the absolute, said point-blank. She remembered the yard, the day of the ribbon, and she remembered, now that she had the courage to remember it whole, not only the Aurélia who stopped laughing. She remembered Dona Aurora, who raised the blanket to the sun with tears in her eyes and had no envy at all, only the joy of having a pupil like that. She remembered the parish priest, who felt the warp and nodded, without envy. She remembered the mothers who clapped their hands, the other girls who were not those of the sayings, that one, the Felismina, who came up to her and said, with seven years of no envy at all, will you teach me to make it like that? There had been envy in that yard, yes, that of the three or four of the sayings. But there had been, much more, admiration, joy, curiosity, tenderness. And the Velada of fourteen, wounded by the one reaction that hurt, had forgotten all the others, and had carried into life the sum made only with the envy, as if the whole yard were the envy of three mouths, when the whole yard had been almost all good. The envy was of some, of the envious, who are few; most people, on seeing a beautiful thing, do not envy, they admire, and they want to know who made it, and they are grateful. Velada had measured being-seen her whole life by the worst possible reaction, and the worst possible reaction is the rarest.

The second piece was to be envied is to be left.

And that had been true once, and Velada was not going to lie to her own blanket. The Aurélia had left her. It had hurt, and the hurt was real, and it was there, in the faded ribbon, to prove it. But, looking at the blanket as a grown woman, Velada saw what the girl could not have seen: that the Aurélia had left her out of weakness, not out of truth; that a friendship that came undone over a ribbon was frail from the start, and that the fault of undoing it had not been the ribbon's, it had been the fragility's; and that the Aurélia's sentence beside the tank, it was easier when you were like us, said everything about the Aurélia and nothing about being-seen. True friends, Velada understood, do not leave us for standing out; on the contrary, they rejoice, they are proud, they tell others. The Benvinda had not left her for making beautiful blankets; she held her dearer for it. Whoever leaves us for shining was no friend in earnest; it was a bond that only bore up with us put out. And Velada understood, with a shiver, that she had spent her life hiding so as not to lose others, and that, in doing so, she had kept only the bonds that needed her put out to last; and that those bonds, the ones that break at the first light, were not worth the putting-out, and the ones that were worth it, the good ones, those bore her whole and alight, and liked her more for shining.

And there was a living proof of that second piece, and Velada went to fetch it from her memory on purpose, because she needed to set it beside the Aurélia to make the weight. It was the Benvinda. The Benvinda knew Velada's blankets better than anyone, knew them by the colour without seeing a corner, she had told her so herself; she knew, therefore, for years, that Velada was the best weaver of the serra, better than the Engrácia, better than all of them. And the Benvinda had never left her for it. On the contrary. She kept her praises to give to her in private, with care, as one gives a fragile thing to one who is afraid of it; she defended her when she was spoken of; she served her first in the shop, at the hour she knew she came, so that she would not run into people. The Benvinda knew Velada was better and liked her more for it, not less. And Velada, looking at the prize-winning blanket, understood that she had had her whole life, there, at the counter of the grocer's, the proof that to be better does not drive away true friends, and that she had never wanted to see it, because to see the Benvinda was to have to admit that the Aurélia had been the exception and not the rule, and that therefore the whole hiding-place, the whole life of hiding, had been raised on a sum made with the exception. One friend had left her for shining. Another loved her for it. And she had chosen to govern her life by the one who left and to make as if the one who stayed did not count.

And the third piece, the conclusion, to be seen is to lose, fell with the two above, because a conclusion does not stand up when its reasons are taken from it.

Velada understood, folding the prize-winning blanket back, slowly, with another care than she had folded it at fourteen, that to be seen was not to lose, it was to gain. It was to gain the recognition, the pride, the connection with whoever receives what we make, the existence in the eyes of the world. And that what she had called losing, in the yard, had been the loss of a fragile friend, a price, yes, but a small one beside everything the hiding-place had charged her since then: a whole life of invisibility, of thank-yous that went to the Souto, of women who wept over her blankets without knowing her name, of beauty given to the world without a mother. If she did the sum in earnest, with all the numbers, and not only with what hurt, the hiding had cost her much more than the showing had ever cost her. She had spent her whole life fleeing a small danger and, by fleeing it, falling into an enormous loss: that of never having been seen in what she did well.

And she understood the deep thing that freed her, and understood it with the old blanket in her hands: that the hiding-place had not protected her from losing. It had made her lose in another way. She had hidden so as not to lose others to the envy, and in hiding she had lost herself. She had traded a loud loss, that of the envy, which comes and passes, for a silent loss, that of invisibility, which does not pass, which lasts a whole life, and which precisely for being silent seems smaller and is greater. There was, in the hiding, no protection at all. There was only one loss traded for another, worse.

And to convince herself for good, Velada did one more thing, which was to look ahead, to the entry the Keeper did not want to write. She imagined herself old, older than she was, dying in the house against the crag, and imagined what would remain of her in the book of Véspera if she stayed covered to the end. There would remain a line like that of the Esperança of the Lameiras, the woman who sang alone on the hill: she wove, but no one knew; she made the most beautiful thing of the serra and carried it whole down under the earth, and the world, which slept warm by her blankets, never knew there was a Velada. And on imagining that line, Velada felt a cold that was not the night's, and understood that the invisibility was not only a loss of hers, it was a waste of the world's, which would be left not knowing where its warmth had come from; and that to hide was not a private act, without victims, it was to take from whoever received what she made the right to know whose it was they received, and from whoever came after, some girl of good hands who needed to know that one could show oneself, the example that it was possible. And she understood that she had, still, time to change the line of her own entry, to make it be not that of the light that stayed under the bushel, but that of the woman who took off the veil; and that that was the only thing, that night, that depended entirely on her.

And Velada chose, at last, there in bed, with the Aurélia's blanket on her lap, the first loss over the second. She chose to risk the envy of some, if it came, to gain the visible existence. She chose to show herself and to lose perhaps one or another fragile bond, over hiding and losing herself whole; because she understood that between being seen and envied by a few and never being seen by anyone, the first, however much it cost, was life, and the second was a slow death making itself pass for safety. And she put the prize-winning blanket back in the chest, but not at the bottom, under the sheets that were not used; she put it on top, to hand, in sight of whoever opened the chest, because it was no longer the proof of a loss, it was the proof that once she had shown herself and the sky had not fallen, and that she wanted to hand.

And there was, at the end of that afternoon of sums, a small gesture that was worth the whole decision. Velada rose from the bed, went to the chest, took out the faded ribbon that had been forty years pinned to the corner of the prize-winning blanket, the ribbon she had won at fourteen and had hidden with the blanket as one hides a shame, and stood looking at it at the window. It was the colour of nothing, eaten by time, but you could still see it had been red, of the red she would come to learn to draw from the madder years later, as if life had given her the sign of the colour right there, at the first prize, and she had not known how to read it. And Velada, instead of putting the ribbon back at the bottom, did a thing she had not done for forty years: she pinned it with a pin to the beam of the house, beside the loom, in sight, where she would see it every day on sitting down to work. Not to punish herself with it, but on the contrary, to remember that once she had made a thing so beautiful that it deserved a ribbon, and that the ribbon had not been the beginning of the loss, it had been the beginning of the gift, and that the mistake had not been in winning the ribbon, it had been in hiding it. And the ribbon stayed on the beam, and it was the first thing Velada put in sight in her life, before even the name on the blanket, and it was only for her own eyes, but it was already a putting-in-sight, it was already a beginning.

And she decided to show herself. Not suddenly, not all at once, for that would be too much for one who had veiled herself her whole life. But to begin. To take the first stitch. To do the first thing, which she already knew what it was, which the Matilde had put before her and the Keeper had confirmed and the fountain had sealed: to embroider the name in the corner of a blanket, tiny, to begin, the size of the courage she had that day, and to let it go out into the world, for the first time in her life, with the uncovered face of the woman who had woven it, and to see, on the far side of the fear, with her own eyes, whether to be seen was really to lose, or whether it was, as she was beginning at last to suspect, the thing she had her whole life, beneath the fear, always desired: to exist.
