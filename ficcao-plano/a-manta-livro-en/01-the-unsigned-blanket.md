## 1. The unsigned blanket

Velada wove the most beautiful blankets of the region, and no one knew it, or almost no one, because she contrived that no one should know, selling through an intermediary, refusing the fairs, not signing; and the unsigned blanket, the beautiful blanket that does not say who made it, was she herself, was the way Velada was in the world, present in what she did and absent from herself.

Velada's blankets were a rare thing. She had a knack, an eye for colours, a hand for the loom, that made of her blankets works of a beauty that stopped whoever saw them; and half the village, and people from away too, slept warm by Velada's blankets, without knowing they were hers, because they bought them from an intermediary, a man who fetched them from the mountain and sold them in the village and the larger town, and who was the visible face of a work whose maker stayed hidden, up there, at the loom, without a name, without a face, without anyone binding that beauty to that woman.

And Velada wanted it so. It was not distraction, nor chance; it was choice, it was method. She refused to go to the fairs, where she would have to stand beside the blankets, to sell them herself, to be seen as the weaver; she preferred the intermediary, who spared her from appearing. She did not embroider her name on the blankets, as other weavers did, who put their mark, their signature, in the corner of the work; Velada's blankets went out anonymous, without a name, and she wanted it so, because to put the name was to say it was I, was to appear, was to show herself, and to show herself was what Velada did not do, for an old reason that lay at the bottom of her.

And there was, in this hiding, a thing that seemed modesty and was not. Others, who knew her from afar, thought her modest, humble, without vanity, and praised her for it, oh Velada, what a woman so without vanity, she makes those blankets and does not even boast; and Velada let them think so, because it was better to pass for modest than to show the truth, which was not modesty, it was fear; because modesty is not to give importance to being seen, and Velada gave it all the importance, she had a dread of it, and her hiding was not the calm of one who does not mind, it was the flight of one who fears, and that she hid too, beneath the appearance of humility, because even her fear Velada veiled.

And the price of hiding was enormous, and Velada paid it without complaint. She lived alone, on the mountain, with the loom, and her life was to weave, and it was beautiful, the life, in part, because the work was beautiful and she loved it; but it was also poor in one thing, in recognition, in pride, in the joy of being seen in what one does well; because Velada never heard, at first hand, a praise of her blankets, never saw the face of those who received them, was never pointed out as the maker of that beauty, and all that, which fills one who creates, passed her by, went to the intermediary, went to the anonymity, and Velada stayed on the mountain, alone, weaving beauty that the world received without knowing from whom, and living, for that, a life in which she gave much and received little, in which she created much and appeared not at all.

And there was a day, at the start of the book, on which the hiding weighed on her in a new way. Velada was in the village, by chance, and heard two women talking of a blanket, a blanket of hers, without knowing the maker was there; and the women praised the blanket, said what beauty, what work, who would make this, it must be an artist; and Velada, on hearing her work praised before her without their knowing it was hers, felt two things at once, and the struggle between them was the book. She felt, first, a secret joy, that of hearing what she had made praised; and she felt, right after, a deep sadness, that of not being able to say it was I, of having to stay silent, anonymous, hidden, hearing her work praised without being able to claim it, without being able to say thank you, it was I who made it; and it was in that sadness, in that of hearing what is ours praised without being able to own it, that Velada felt, for the first time, the weight of the hiding place, and made, without meaning to, the question of the book: why can I not say that it was I? What am I afraid of, after so many years, in putting my name on what I do well?
