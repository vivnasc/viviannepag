## 11. The name less tiny

And Velada embroidered, on the next blanket, the name less tiny, and then, on the one after, less tiny still, and it was so, stitch by stitch, blanket by blanket, name by name ever larger, that she went on coming out of the hiding place, because to show oneself, for one who has veiled herself her whole life, is done by growing the name, slowly, until it fits in the corner in sight of all.

She went on venturing. After the tiny name, and the relapse overcome, Velada embroidered the name a little bigger, and felt her heart race, but she bore it; and then bigger still, and the heart raced less; and she went on discovering that each time she embroidered the name bigger, and the blanket went out more signed, and she showed herself more, the fear, instead of growing, diminished, because the fear of being seen feeds on not being seen, and dwindles when one shows oneself and discovers one does not die of it; and Velada, in showing herself more, went on having less and less fear of showing herself, in a circle the reverse of the old one, because the old one was to hide so as to have less fear and to have more and more, and the new one was to show oneself in spite of the fear and to have less and less of it.

And she went to a fair. It was the thing she had most feared her whole life, to stand beside her blankets, to be seen as the maker, to receive the looks, the praises, and also the comparisons, the envy; and Velada went, with her heart racing, and set up her stall, with her blankets, with her name, and stayed there, exposed, in sight, she, Velada, the weaver, the maker, for the first time with her face uncovered beside what she made. And it was hard for her, at first, to bear the looks, to be pointed out, to hear the praises without fleeing; but she bore it, and went on staying, and went on receiving the praises at first hand, and saying thank you, it was I, and went on discovering, at the fair, the joy the hiding place had denied her her whole life, that of standing beside what one makes, and of being seen making it, and of receiving, in person, the recognition, and of existing, at last, beside her work, as its maker.

And there was, at the fair, a moment that sealed the cure, and it was small and it was enormous. A woman bought a blanket from her, and on learning that it was Velada who had made it, there, before her, said to her a simple thing: what luck to know who made a thing so beautiful; I am going to sleep warm by this, and now I know your name, and each time I use it I will remember you; and Velada, on hearing this, on being known, named, remembered by one who received her work, felt undone within her, for good, the old equation; because there was the proof that to be seen was not to lose, it was to gain this, this connection, this I will remember you, this existence in the eyes of one who received her beauty; and she understood that she had spent her life fleeing this, the most beautiful thing there is for one who creates, the meeting with those who receive what we created, and that the hiding place had deprived her of it her whole life, and that it had been enough to show herself, to come out, to sign, to go to the fair, to gain it.

And she understood a deep thing about the ever-larger name. Each time she embroidered the name bigger, Velada was not only signing a blanket; she was undoing a little more the veil that had covered her since she was young, she was coming out a little more of the hiding place, she was existing a little more; and the name growing on the blanket was Velada growing in the world, ceasing to be the covered one, the veiled one, the nameless one, and becoming, slowly, the Velada with her face uncovered, the maker, the woman who weaves beauty and signs it; and she understood that her name, Velada, the covered one, which she had fulfilled her whole life by hiding herself, could now be fulfilled the other way round, could be the name of one who uncovered herself, of one who took off the veil, of one who was veiled and ceased to be, and that this, the taking off of the veil, was the crossing of her life, and was, blanket by blanket, name by name, being made.

And it was not all at once, and the fear still came to her, and there were still days on which the heart raced and on which the wish to cover herself returned; but the name, on the blankets, went on getting ever larger, and Velada went on being ever more in sight, and more at ease in the sight, and she understood that she was going, from there on, to go on growing the name, and showing herself, and existing, and that the veil that had covered her her whole life was undoing itself, thread by thread, as she embroidered, ever larger, in the corner of what she made, the thing that for forty-four years she had not dared to say: it was I.
