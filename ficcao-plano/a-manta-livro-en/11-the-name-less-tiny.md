## 11. The name less tiny

And it was so, stitch by stitch, blanket by blanket, name by name ever larger, that Velada went on coming out of the hiding-place; because to show oneself, for one who has veiled herself her whole life, is done by growing the name, slowly, until it fits in the corner in sight of all without the hand trembling.

She went on venturing. After the tiny name, and the relapse overcome with the scissors put away in the box, Velada embroidered the name a little bigger, and felt her heart leap, but she bore it, she stayed in the leap until it calmed. And on the next blanket bigger still, and the heart leapt less. And there was a small sign that the fear was dwindling, and Velada noticed it one day, at the loom. She noticed that she no longer turned the loom with its back to the door. For forty years she had set it turned towards the crag, in the shadow, so that whoever peered from the path would see the blanket and not the hand; and now, without having decided it, she had been bringing it to the light, turning it a little more towards the window, towards the door, so that, if someone climbed the path and looked, they would see her, the weaver, doing what she did. It was not yet an open door, but it was the loom turning towards the world instead of turning its back on it, and Velada understood, on noticing it, that the body, which had learned to hide, was learning, slowly, to let itself be seen; and that the fear, which she had always thought a wall of stone, was after all more like a mist, which seems solid from afar and undoes itself as one walks into it.

And she went on discovering a thing that surprised her, because it was the opposite of all she had learned: that each time she embroidered the name bigger, and the blanket went out more hers, and she showed herself more, the fear, instead of growing, dwindled. Because the fear of being seen feeds on not being seen, it fattens in the dark; and when one comes out into the light and discovers one does not die of it, the fear is left with nothing to eat. Velada's old circle was to hide so as to have less fear and to have more and more; the new circle was to show herself in spite of the fear and to have less and less of it.

And there was a day on which she chose to change the tone of the thread itself. The first names she had embroidered with a quiet thread, of the tone of the weave, so they would scarcely be seen; and on a winter blanket, a field of grey of raw wool, she chose, for the first time, a madder red for the name, her colour, the one she drew from the root, and embroidered Velada in the corner in red, in sight, without hiding it in the colour of the cloth. And on finishing she stayed looking at it, her name in red on the grey, and understood that it was no longer a name asking forgiveness; it was a name saying itself.

And she went on changing the tone of the name too in another thing, not only in the colour. The first ones, Velada embroidered with her hand trembling, and it showed in the letters, which came out hesitant, uneven, like those of one who writes a forbidden word. But the hand, by dint of embroidering the name blanket after blanket, went on steadying, and the letters came out ever more upright, more sure, more hers, until one day Velada noticed that she embroidered the name without thinking, without her hand trembling, with the ease of one who signs a thing that is hers and has the right to sign. And she understood that the steadiness of the letters was the map of the cure: one could measure how far she had come out of the hiding-place by the steadiness with which her hand wrote the name, as one measures the health of a person by the pulse. At first, a trembling pulse; now, a steady pulse. And she kept one of the first blankets, of the trembling name, to remember where she had come from, and to set it one day beside one of the last and see, in the difference of the two hands, the distance a woman can travel, in a few months, when at last she decides to travel.

And she went to the fair.

It was the thing she had most feared her whole life, the fair of Our Lady, in August, the great one, the one she had never gone to, where the weavers of the serra all set out their stall and where the Engrácia had had hers for thirty years. Velada went, with her heart leaping since the eve, with the Matilde helping her carry the blankets and the Benvinda keeping her a good place, by the fountain, in the shade. And she set up her stall, and spread her blankets on a cord, with the names in sight, and stood beside them, she, the Velada, the weaver of the serra, the maker, for the first time in her life with her face uncovered beside what she made.

And it was hard for her, at first. People came, and stopped before the blankets, and praised, and asked who made them, and Velada had to say it was I with her mouth trembling, and the first time she almost fled, almost sent the Benvinda to answer for her. The Engrácia, three stalls along, watched her over her own initials with eyes that Velada felt at her back. But Velada stayed. She bore the looks, bore the praises without fleeing, and went on staying, and as the morning ran it grew easier for her, because she discovered, at the fair, the joy the hiding-place had denied her for forty years: that of being beside what one makes, and of being seen making it, and of receiving, in person, to the face, the recognition, and of saying thank you, it was I, and of seeing the joy of whoever carries off a blanket of hers rise to their face there, before her.

And there was, in the afternoon, at the fair, a moment that sealed the cure, and it was small and it was enormous.

There came to the stall a woman already of years, of the country, with hands cracked from a whole life's work, with little money in her purse and much time to choose, and she lingered before a blanket, the blue one with the red stripe, passing her hand over it many times. And she asked the price, and Velada said it, and the woman did her sums by her face, and she would just reach it and would not, and Velada, on an impulse she did not make by calculation, lowered it for her a little, because the woman looked at the blanket as one looks at a thing one needs to have and knows perhaps one will not have. And the woman bought it, and on paying, counting the coins slowly, asked Velada, and the blanket, who made it, was it you, miss? And Velada said, it was I. And the woman looked at her, and said, simple, not knowing she was closing a wound of thirty years:

"What luck is mine, to know who made a thing so lovely. I'm going to sleep warm by this the whole winter, in my bed up there at the top, and now I know your name. Every time I pull it up to my chin, with the cold, I'll remember you, the woman who made it. It's not like the other blankets, that you don't know whose they are. This one has an owner, and I knew her."

And Velada, on hearing this, on being known, named, promised in the memory of a woman who was going to sleep warm by a blanket of hers, felt undo itself within her, for good, with a quiet snap, the old equation, that of the tank. Because there it was, standing before her, with the coins counted in her hand, the proof in the flesh that to be seen was not to lose; it was to gain this, this connection, this I'll remember you, this existence in the eyes of one who received her beauty. And she understood that she had spent her life fleeing this, the most beautiful thing there is for one who creates, the encounter with whoever receives what we created, and that the hiding-place had denied it her for forty years, and that it had been enough to come out, to sign, to go to the fair, to stand beside the blankets and bear the leaping heart, to gain it.

And there were, at the same fair, two more encounters that Velada would keep. One was with the Souto. The Souto passed by her stall midway through the morning, and stopped, and stood looking at her behind the blankets with the name in sight, and took off his hat, and said, with a thing in his voice that Velada had never heard from him, it was humility, it was almost emotion, so dona Velada came to the fair. I never thought to see this. And he looked at the blankets spread on the cord, with the name in the corner of each, and said, low, the name suits them, dona Velada. They always lacked it and I didn't know. And Velada understood, in the Souto, that the man bore no grudge at all that she had come out of the hiding-place, though it took work from him; on the contrary, he seemed relieved, like one who sets down a weight he had carried without knowing it was heavy, the weight of being the face of a woman who had no face. The mistress, from here on, won't need me so much, he said, and it was no complaint, it was almost joy, and it's well so, for each should carry his own thing, and your thing, dona Velada, was too beautiful for a man like me to carry. And he went off, with his hat and his Lampião, and Velada stayed watching him go with a new tenderness for that man who had served her twenty years carrying off her thank-yous and bringing her the money, and who now saw her keep her own thank-yous without a shadow of envy, he who had more reason than anyone to have it.

And the other encounter was with a girl. At the end of the afternoon, when she was already packing up, there came to the stall a girl of about twelve, barefoot, of the country, who had stayed the whole afternoon at a distance looking at the blankets without daring to come near, and who at last came near, and asked, with her eyes on the ground, do you teach how to make it like that. And Velada, on hearing that, felt time fold over, and saw herself at twelve in the attic, and saw the Aurélia passing her the skeins, and saw the Felismina of the yard asking will you teach me, and understood that she had before her, barefoot and with downcast eyes, the same hunger she had had and that no one had fed in her, the hunger of one who feels she has a hand and has no one to tell her yes. And instead of fleeing, instead of changing the subject, Velada crouched to the height of the girl, and said, I teach, yes. What's your name. And the girl said her name, and Velada said, then look, and took her hand and put it on the weave of a blanket, and showed her how the stripes are felt with the palm, and the girl laughed with gladness, and Velada understood, in that laugh, that to come out of the hiding-place was not only to receive, it was also to be able to give, to be able to be the Aurélia of the attic for some other girl, to be able to tell a girl you have a hand, a thing she had waited her whole life for someone to confirm to her without fear. And she told the girl to come up to the serra one Sunday, that she would show her the loom, and the girl went off running to tell her mother, and Velada stayed watching her run, and thought that perhaps that was the true end of a gift, not the name in the corner, but the hand of another hand learning to feel it, and that this too, for forty years, the hiding-place had denied her.

And she understood, on packing up the stall at the end of the day, with the Engrácia packing up hers without looking at her, a deep thing about the ever-larger name. Each time she embroidered it bigger, Velada was not only signing a blanket; she was undoing a little more the veil that had covered her since she was young, coming out a little more of the hiding-place, existing a little more. The name growing on the blanket was Velada growing in the world, ceasing to be the covered one, the veiled one, the one without a name, and becoming, slowly, the Velada with her face uncovered, the maker, the woman who weaves beauty and signs it. And she understood that her name, Velada, the covered one, which she had fulfilled her whole life by hiding herself, could now be fulfilled the other way round: it could be the name of one who uncovered herself, of one who took off the veil, of one who was veiled and ceased to be. And that this, the taking off of the veil, was the crossing of her life, and was, blanket by blanket, name by name, being made.

And the barefoot girl of the fair did come up to the serra, the following Sunday, with her mother staying to wait on the path, without the courage to come in, and Velada, who her whole life had contrived that no one should see her at the loom, did that Sunday the opposite of everything: she sat the girl at her side, turned the loom so that the light fell on the weave and not on her back, for the first time in years, and showed her. She showed her the warp and the weft, showed her the shuttle, let her pass the thread once, and again, and the girl passed it crooked, and laughed, and Velada did not correct her with the voice of the Idália, did not say that looks ugly, she said, with the voice the Aurélia had had in the attic, it's good, it's good for a first time, look, like this, and took her hand with her own. And the girl wove, that afternoon, a handsbreadth of a thing crooked and full of colour, and wanted to take it, and Velada let her take it, and even gave her a skein of her madder red to go on at home. And when the girl went down the path running, with the handsbreadth of cloth in one hand and the skein in the other, showing them to her mother from afar, Velada stayed at the door watching her, and understood that she had just done, without thinking it, the thing she had most lacked in her life: to give another hand the confidence no one had given her without reserve, to tell a girl you have a hand, show it, without the poison of to boast is ugly underneath. And she understood that the hiding-place had not deprived her only of receiving; it had deprived her, all those years, of being able to give this, which was perhaps the most beautiful thing an old weaver can give a young one, more beautiful than a blanket: the leave to show oneself.

And it was not all at once, for there is no cure that is, and the fear still came to her, and there were still days on which the heart leapt and on which the want to cover herself came back to call her to the dark. But the name, on the blankets, went on getting ever larger, and Velada went on being ever more in sight, and more at ease in the sight; and she understood that she was going, from there on, to go on growing the name, and showing herself, and existing, and that the veil that had covered her her whole life was undoing itself, thread by thread, as she embroidered, ever larger, in the corner of what she made, the thing that for forty-four years she had not dared to say: it was I.
