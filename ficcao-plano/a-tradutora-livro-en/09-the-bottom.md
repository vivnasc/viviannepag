## 9. The bottom

But there was a price Eulália had not foreseen, and it was that price that took her to the bottom, and her bottom was not her son needing more: it was her son needing less. Because as Tomé gained confidence, as he learned to make himself understood by others, he came, naturally, to need his mother less, and Eulália, who had spent her life being necessary, found herself, all at once, becoming dispensable, and discovered that she did not know who she was when she was no longer necessary.

No one had warned her that this was coming. They had warned her, all her life, of the dangers of a son like that, of the difficulties, of the care, of the tiredness, but no one had told her that the worst might not be the son needing more, but needing less. It was a mourning with no name, a mourning that had no right to exist, because how does a mother weep for the son who gets better. And it was perhaps for being a mourning with no name and no right that it hurt so much, because Eulália could share it with no one, could not even admit it to herself without shame, and had to carry it alone, in silence.

And then came the morning of the bottom.

It was an April morning, with the sun already coming into the kitchen. Tomé went out alone, a thing that before would have been unthinkable and that now, slowly, became possible: he went to the grocer's and to the mill to fetch a thing, to a place where they already knew him and knew how to hear him, and Olímpia was to watch him from afar, without his knowing, and Eulália stayed at home. And it was the first morning in many years when she had nothing to do for her son, no translation, no consultation, no bridge, nothing. She stayed at the door watching him go down the path, with the empty bag in his hand, and he did not even turn back, he went confident, and Eulália stayed at the door long after he had disappeared at the bend, with her hand still half raised in a goodbye he had not seen.

And she came in. And the house was silent with that silence that is worse than noise, the silence of a house from which the bustle has gone and where only the emptiness is left. Eulália, who had spent her life wishing for a moment of quiet, discovered that morning that quiet, when at last it came, was no quiet at all, it was abandonment. She put the water on the fire out of habit, and stayed watching it heat, and understood that she had no one to make the coffee for, that she had made it for a son who was not there, and she turned off the gas, and stayed there, on her feet, in the kitchen, not knowing what to do with her hands.

She sat at the table, that morning, and tried to think of what she wanted, of Madalena's question, of the voice the Keeper swore she had. She went to the dresser drawer, took from beneath the receipts the sheet folded in four, the one of the crossed-out I, and unfolded it, and smoothed it on the table, and took up the pencil of the errands. I, said the sheet, with the I crossed out over it. And Eulália stayed looking at the blank of the sheet, with the pencil in her hand, and looked for a wish, and had none; looked for a liking, and did not know one; looked for an opinion, a single one, that was hers and not about her son, and her head was empty, clean, swept, like a house from which all the furniture has been taken. She tried to remember when had been the last time she had done a thing only out of liking, only for herself, and she had to go back so far, so far, years and years, to before Tomé, to the girl of the photograph, the one of the clogs, and the size of that going-back was perhaps the cruellest measure of all she had lost, because it showed that it had not been a year or two of having set herself aside, it had been a whole life. And the sheet stayed blank. And Eulália understood, at that table, the hardest thing of her life: that in giving her voice to her son she had not lent hers, she had spent it; that in effacing herself for him she had not kept herself anywhere for afterward; that there was no Eulália waiting to return, because Eulália had really gone, had gone whole into her son. She had thought the hard thing would be to let her son go. The hard thing, she discovered that morning, was what was left of her after she let him go: nothing.

She got up from the table, left the sheet open, and walked through the house, that morning of the bottom, from room to room, like a stranger, like one who visits the house of someone unknown. And she went on noticing, room by room, a thing she had never noticed: that there was not in that house a single sign of herself. In the sitting room, Tomé's armchair with the blanket he liked; the gramophone and the old fado beside it; the objects of his routine, his mug, his place at the table. In his room, his things. And of her, of Eulália, there was nothing. Not a corner that was hers, not an object that said here lives a woman called Eulália who likes this and that, because she liked nothing, she had no corners of her own. Thirty years living in a house that was all her son's and nothing hers. She stopped in the middle of the corridor, and understood that if she disappeared for good, when they came to tidy away her things, no one would find anything at all that was hers, because a function leaves no traces, a function has no objects, and she had lived thirty years as a function and not as a person.

In the middle of that empty morning, Eulália had an idea that seemed to her, for an instant, a way out: to go and see Conceição. Conceição, the friend of her girlhood, the one of the clogs, the one who had knocked at her door for years until she gave up. She was alive, lived at the end of the village, and Eulália thought, I will go there, I will go to her, I will tell her she was right, that I should have gone with her every time she called me. And she went to the door, and took up her shawl, and even opened the door. And she stayed there, at the threshold, with the shawl on her shoulders, and understood that she did not know what she would say to her, that she had no conversation at all to take, that if Conceição asked her well then, Eulália, of your life, she would have only Tomé to tell, and that she would arrive at her friend's house and be, once again, Tomé's mother, because that was all she had to be, and that therefore there was nowhere to go, because she carried with her, wherever she went, the same emptiness. And she took off the shawl, and hung it back on the nail, and closed the door, and it was that the saddest gesture of that morning: the door closing from within, not because the world refused her, but because she had nothing to take to the world.

She ended up in the bedroom, and there, on top of the chest of drawers, leaning against the mirror, was the only thing of hers in the whole house: the photograph of the girl of the lively eyes, with the clogs and Conceição. Eulália took it up, and sat on the edge of the bed, and looked at the girl, and the girl laughed at her from the day of the fair, with all the future ahead. And it was there, with the photograph in her hand, that Eulália wept, as she had not wept for years. She wept for the son who was growing and who needed her less, which was what she wanted and was also her mourning. She wept for her own self, for the Eulália who had lost herself along the way, for the girl of the line of birth who had had a voice and a life ahead and had spent them all being voice and life of another. And she wept from fear, from the fear of being too late, of being fifty-and-some with no life of her own and no certainty that she was still in time to have one.

It is fitting to say that no one saw it, this bottom of Eulália's, because she went on, on the outside, functioning. When Tomé came back, in the middle of the morning, pleased, with the bag full and a story to tell of how the miller had understood him, Eulália received him with her face of always, celebrated his feat, made the lunch, and no one in Véspera would have imagined that Dona Eulália, the exemplary mother, the saint, had spent the morning touching the bottom of a life wasted on her own self. And that was perhaps the cruellest of her bottom: to be invisible, to have no right even to seem low, because a mother like her cannot allow herself the luxury of collapsing in plain sight. Eulália understood, that day, that many mothers like her lived so, with a hidden bottom beneath a functional face, despairing in silence while they cared, and that no one saw them, because everyone praised them for being always well.

But the bottom, as Eulália would come to understand, is not where one stays; it is where one sets one's feet. She thought, that afternoon, of Benvinda, of the mother who had clung until she wasted away, and understood, with a shiver, that she was a step away from being Benvinda; and the only difference between the two was that Benvinda had clung to a daughter who went on needing her, and she, Eulália, clung to a son who was beginning to need her less, and that therefore her well was, in a certain way, even more bottomless. And she thought of Reparada, of the mother whose son had died, of the cold cup in her hands saying make the most of the tiredness, and was afraid of arriving there, at an old age of empty hands, without even the tiredness to fill the days. And she thought, at last, of Madalena, of the whole mother, and understood that Madalena was the only one who could reach a hand to her out of the well, because Madalena had been there and got out.

And she did, at the end of that afternoon of the bottom, a thing that for her was enormous: she decided to ask for help. She, who had never asked anything of anyone, who was always the one who gave, who supported, who was there for others. She waited for Tomé to fall asleep, and went to the telephone in the corridor, and stayed a good while with the receiver in her hand without dialling, because to ask cost her as breathing under water cost her. And she dialled. And Madalena answered, and Eulália opened her mouth to say some thing, a pretext, an excuse for the call, and what came out of her, at the third ring of the night, was the naked truth: Madalena. It is me, Eulália. I am lost. I do not know who I am. Help me. And on the other side there was a silence, and then Madalena's voice came, and it came without astonishment, came almost with relief, like one who had been waiting for that call for years: I am glad you called, Eulália. I know the place where you are. I have been there. Tomorrow I will come to you. And that decision, to ask, was already the first thread of the ladder out of the well, because one who asks for help is no longer completely sunk, one who asks for help has already reached a hand upward.

And still that night, after hanging up, Eulália did, without knowing she did it, the first thing for herself in decades. She went to the bedroom, to the mirror of the chest of drawers, beside the photograph of the girl, and looked at herself, and said, aloud, to the woman in the mirror, a thing that was still a question but that was already, for the first time, about her: and you, Eulália? Who are you? What do you want? And the voice that asked the question was small, and hoarse from disuse, and it trembled in saying her name aloud, Eulália, which it had been so long since anyone said. But it was a voice. It was her voice. It had come out, at last, after decades, not to say her son, but to look for herself, and however small and trembling it was, it was the beginning of a thing, it was the first word of a language Eulália would have to relearn from the beginning: the language of saying herself, the language of which she had been, one day, at birth, promised the mistress.
