## 9. The bottom

But there was a price Eulália had not foreseen, and it was that price that took her to the bottom, and her bottom was not her son needing more: it was her son needing less. Because as Tomé gained confidence, as he learned to make himself understood by others, as he grew toward the world, he came, naturally, to need his mother less, and Eulália, who had spent her life being necessary, found herself, all at once, becoming dispensable, and discovered that she did not know who she was when she was no longer necessary.

No one had warned her that this was coming. They had warned her, her whole life, of the dangers of a son like that — of the difficulties, of the care, of the tiredness —, but no one had told her that the worst might not be the son needing more, but needing less; no one had prepared her for the day when, after a life of wishing her son would gain autonomy, that wish came true and brought her, instead of relief, a mourning. And it was a mourning without a name, a mourning that had no right to exist, because how does a mother mourn the son who gets better, how does one admit such a sadness without seeming a monster. And it was perhaps for being a mourning without a name and without a right that it hurt so much, because Eulália could not share it with anyone, could not even admit it to herself without shame, and had to carry it alone, silent, this mourning for the loss of her own indispensability, this weeping not for the son but for the place the son had given her and that now slipped through her fingers as he grew away.

It was a strange thing, and Eulália was ashamed to feel it. She wanted her son to grow, she had worked for it, it was the right thing, it was the greater love. And yet, each time Tomé understood himself with someone without her, each time he resolved alone a thing it had always been hers to resolve, Eulália felt, beneath the joy, a sting of a terrible thing, a thing that resembled loss, mourning, almost jealousy. Because her whole life, all her worth, all her reason to exist, had been to be Tomé's voice, to be the bridge, to be indispensable, and now that her son was learning to do without her, what was left of Eulália? Who was she, if she was no longer the only person in the world who understood her son? What was she for, if her son was beginning to be able to live without her? And the answer, when Eulália looked for it, was an emptiness, a silence, an empty house, because beneath Tomé's mother there was not, or Eulália could not find, anyone else.

There were those who told her, in that time, that she ought to be happy, that it was what every mother wants, to see her son grow, gain autonomy, need her less. And Eulália nodded yes, that she was happy, and on the outside she was, and within she died a little at each step of her son away from her, and she was ashamed to feel it, and said it to no one, because what mother is this who suffers when her son gets better, what kind of love is this that grieves at the son's happiness. But what Eulália felt was not a not-wanting of her son's good; it was discovering, in seeing her son need her less, that she had nothing else in life beyond being needed, and that as her son did without her there opened, before her, not the freedom that would open before another mother, but a desert, the desert of a woman with nothing of her own, looking at the years that remained to her and seeing in them nothing but the growing absence of the only role she had learned to play.

And then came the morning of the bottom. It was a morning when Tomé went out alone, a thing that before would have been unthinkable and that now, slowly, was becoming possible, he went to see to a thing of his own at a place where they already knew him and knew how to hear him, and Eulália stayed at home, alone, and it was the first morning in many years in which she had nothing to do for her son, no translation, no consultation, no bridge, nothing. And instead of the rest it ought to have been, that free morning was a horror, because Eulália, alone at home, with her son not needing her, understood that she did not know what to do with herself, that she had no life at all beyond her son, that there was no Eulália waiting to live while Tomé was not there, that the house was empty and so was she, that she had given everything she was to her son and that now, that the son was beginning to be able to walk, she was left behind with nothing, a woman of fifty-and-some who did not know who she was when she was not being a mother.

The house was silent with that silence that is worse than noise, the silence of a house from which the bustle has gone and where only the emptiness remains, and Eulália, who had spent her life wishing for a moment of quiet, discovered that the quiet, when it came at last, was no quiet at all, it was abandonment, it was the confirmation that she had nothing of her own to fill that time she had so wished for. How many times, across the years, had she not wished for a free morning, a morning only for herself, with her son not needing her. And now that she had it, that free morning was the cruellest thing that had happened to her, because it showed her that free time, which she had imagined as a rest, was in truth a mirror, and that the mirror gave her back the emptiness, gave her back a woman who did not know what to do with herself when she was not doing for her son, a woman who had wished for time her whole life without ever having cultivated anything to fill it with.

She sat at the kitchen table, that morning, and tried to think of what she wanted, of Madalena's question, of the voice the Keeper swore she had, and found nothing, and the nothing frightened her more than anything. She looked for a wish, and had none; she looked for a liking, and did not know; she looked for an opinion, a single one, that was hers and not about her son, and her head was empty, clean, swept, like a house from which all the furniture has been taken. She tried to remember when had been the last time she had done a thing only for pleasure, only for herself, and she had to go back so far, so far, years and years, to before Tomé, almost to the girl of the photograph, and the size of that going-back, the having to return decades to find the last time she had lived for herself, was perhaps the cruellest measure of all she had lost, because it showed that it had not been a year or two of having set herself aside, it had been a whole lifetime, it had been everything there had been since she became a mother, and that therefore what she had ahead was not to take up an interrupted life, it was to build, from almost nothing, with the years that remained to her, a life she had never truly had. And Eulália understood, at that table, the hardest thing of her life: that in giving her voice to her son she had not lent hers, she had spent it; that in effacing herself for him she had not kept herself anywhere for afterward; that there was no Eulália waiting to return, because Eulália had truly gone, had gone wholly into her son, and what remained was a husk, a woman who was only the memory of having been a mother and who no longer had an I to return to now that the being-a-mother was diminishing. She had thought the hard thing would be to let her son go. The hard thing, she discovered that morning, was what remained of her after letting him go: nothing. Or a nothing waiting to be someone again, and she no longer even knew if she had the strength, at the age she was, to remake from scratch a person who had spent herself a whole lifetime.

She walked through the house, that morning of the bottom, and the house, which had always been full of Tomé's presence, of the bustle of caring for him, was suddenly empty and silent, and Eulália walked from room to room like a stranger, like one who visits the house of an unknown woman, and understood that there was not in that house a single sign of herself. There were Tomé's things everywhere, the things he liked, the objects of his routine; but of her, of Eulália, there was nothing, not a corner that was hers, not an object that said here lives a woman called Eulália who likes this and that, because she liked nothing, had no corners of her own, had no objects of her own, had lived for thirty years in a house that was wholly her son's and nothing hers. And it was perhaps that, the house without a trace of her, the cruellest image of the bottom: the proof, in things, that she had effaced herself so completely that not even in her own house had she left a mark, that if she disappeared for good no one would find, in tidying her things, a single thing that was hers, because she had no things, had no likings, had no life, was only the function of caring for another, and a function leaves no traces, a function has no objects, a function effaces itself without leaving a trace, and Eulália understood that she had lived thirty years as a function and not as a person, and the person, Eulália, was that blank sheet, that house without a corner of her own, that silence where someone ought to dwell.

And she wept, that morning, as she had not wept in years, she wept for the son who grew and needed her less, which was what she wanted and was also her mourning; and she wept for herself, for the Eulália who had lost herself along the way, for the girl of the line of birth who had had a voice and a life ahead and had spent them all being another's voice and life; and she wept from fear, from the fear of being too late, of being fifty-and-some with no life of her own and no certainty that she was still in time to have one. It was the bottom, that morning. It was the moment when Eulália touched the worst, not the loss of her son, whom she had not lost, but the loss of herself, the discovery that she had effaced herself so well that perhaps there was no longer an Eulália to rekindle.

It should be said that no one saw this bottom of Eulália's, because she went on, on the outside, functioning. She made the lunch, that day, saw to things, and when Tomé came back she received him with her usual face, and no one in Véspera would have imagined that Dona Eulália, the exemplary mother, the saint, had spent the morning touching the bottom of a life wasted of itself. And that was perhaps the cruellest of her bottom: to be invisible, to have not even the right to seem low, because a mother like her cannot allow herself to collapse in plain sight, she has to go on functioning for her son, and therefore her despair had to be silent, hidden, lived within with the face composed without. Eulália understood, that day, that many mothers like her lived so, with a hidden bottom beneath a functional face, despairing in silence while they cared, and that no one saw them, because everyone praised them for being always well, always available, always holding on, without imagining what that holding-on cost, without imagining that beneath the mother who holds on there is often a woman drowning in silence so as not to worry anyone.

But the bottom, as Eulália would come to understand, is not where one stays; it is where one sets one's feet. She thought of Benvinda, the mother who had clung until she wasted away, and understood, with a shiver, that she was a step from being Benvinda, that the only difference between the two was that Benvinda had clung to a daughter who went on needing her, and she, Eulália, clung to a son who was beginning to need her less, and that therefore her well was, in a way, even more bottomless, because Benvinda still had the excuse of being needed and she was losing even that. And she thought of Madalena, of the whole mother, and understood that Madalena was the only one who could stretch out a hand to her out of the well, because Madalena had already been there and got out, and she did, that morning of the bottom, a thing that for her was enormous: she decided to ask for help. She, who had never asked anything of anyone, who was always the one who gave, who supported, who was there for others, decided, from the bottom of that kitchen table, that she was going to ask Madalena for help, that she was going to say to another person I am lost, I do not know who I am, help me, and that decision, to ask, was already the first thread of the stairway out of the well, because one who asks for help is no longer completely sunk, one who asks for help has already stretched a hand upward.

And it was at that bottom, at that kitchen table, in the midst of that weeping, that Eulália did, without knowing she was doing it, the first thing for herself in decades. She got up, went to the mirror, looked at herself, and said, aloud, to the woman in the mirror, a thing that was still a question but that was already, for the first time, about her: and you, Eulália? Who are you? What do you want? And the voice that asked the question was small, and hoarse from disuse, and trembled in saying her name aloud, Eulália, which it was so long since anyone had said; but it was a voice. It was her voice. It had come out, at last, after decades, not to say her son, but to look for herself, and however small and trembling it was, it was the beginning of a thing, it was the first word of a language Eulália was going to have to relearn from the beginning: the language of saying herself, the language of which she had been, one day, at birth, promised mistress.
