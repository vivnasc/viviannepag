## 11. Tomé speaks for himself

The net Eulália had begun to weave around her son went on thickening, and it was a beautiful thing to see, for one who knew what was behind it. Where before there had been a single person who understood Tomé, there were now several, each one knowing a little of his language, each one a thread of the net. Olímpia, the neighbour. The woman who helped in the house on Wednesdays. Idalina, from the market, who had learned the sound of the apples. Madalena. And Quintela, the new doctor, who had had the patience to learn.

And it was with Quintela that the circle closed, and the irony did not escape Eulália, because everything had begun in a consultation, decades before, in a green-walled office, and it was now in a consultation that everything was decided again. Tomé had to go to the doctor, because of a pain in his knee, and Eulália, who her whole life had gone and translated, that time did a thing that cost her everything: she went with him, but, at the door of the surgery, she stopped. She said to Quintela, in front of Tomé, doctor, today I let him go in alone. Hear him out with patience, for he makes himself understood, and what you do not understand, call me. And she sat in the waiting room, and let her son go in alone, with the doctor, without the bridge, without the translation, alone before a world that had always needed her to hear him.

And it was the longest time of Eulália's life, those minutes in the waiting room. She sat in a chair against the wall, next to a woman with a child on her lap, as she herself had been, fifty years before, with Tomé on her lap, in a room just the same. And she stayed with her hands gripped one in the other in her lap, looking at the closed door of the surgery, with her heart leaping. Each sound that came from within made her rise halfway from the chair, ready to go in, to come to his aid, to translate; and each time she forced herself to sit down again, to breathe, to trust. It was like holding herself to the chair with both hands so as not to run, and her whole body hurt from the effort of not doing what she had done her whole life.

And there was a moment when she heard, through the door, her son make the sound of distress, that high one she knew better than her own voice, and she nearly did not bear it, she rose all the way, took a step toward the door, with her hand already in the air to open it. And she stopped. She stopped with her hand in the air, and stayed there, on her feet, trembling, and said to herself, low, looking at the door, he can do it, leave him, he can do it. And she sat down again, and it was perhaps the most difficult instant of her life, to stay sitting hearing her son distressed on the other side of a door and not come to his aid, trusting against all her mother's instincts. And she bore it. She stayed. And the sound of distress passed, and there came a silence, and then there came calmer sounds, and Eulália understood, from the waiting room, that within the son and the doctor were understanding each other, slowly, at the cost of patience, without her.

And Tomé came out, at the end of those minutes, and came out well. It had not been perfect, Eulália knew it afterward; there had been difficulties, Quintela had had to make an effort, to guess, to learn there, on the spot, a little more of Tomé's language. But they had understood each other, the two of them, without her, and Tomé came out of the surgery with that joy in his face, the joy of one who has discovered himself capable, the joy of a man who, at the end of fifty years, had spoken for himself to the world and been heard. He came to his mother in the waiting room, and beat his hand on his knee, on the knee that hurt, and made her the sound of I told him, and Eulália understood, and had to bite her lip so as not to weep before everyone.

And Quintela, in taking his leave, at the door, said to Eulália a thing that filled her breast. You have here a son who makes himself understood very well, Dona Eulália, when one gives him time. It costs a little at first, but one understands. You spoiled us, you know; it was always you speaking for him, and we waited comfortably. Good thing you let him try. He can do it. And Eulália heard that, the he can do it, and it was like hearing the absolution of an error of a whole lifetime, because the doctor was telling her, without knowing it, that Tomé had always been capable of more, that it was she, in always translating him, who had kept him dependent. It was not Tomé who had changed. It was she. And in her changing, a world had opened for her son.

It must be said that not everything always went well. There were consultations and situations, after that one, in which the son was not understood, in which things went badly, in which Eulália had to step in at the end, defeated, to translate what the world had not managed to hear. To let go was not a straight line upward; it was a zigzag. And the temptation, at each setback, was enormous: it was enough for the son not to be understood once for the old voice to tell her you see, he needs you, this letting-go is a fantasy, go back to being the bridge. And Eulália had to learn to answer that voice that the son succeeding nine times and failing once did not mean that he could not, it meant that he was learning, and that if she gave up at the first fall she would never let him come to walk.

And before that consultation there had been a hundred small victories, tiny, that no one besides Eulália noticed and that for her were milestones. One, in particular, stayed with her. It was the day Tomé wanted, alone, to buy a thing for his mother. It was for her birthday, and Tomé, who noticed everything, knew, and made Olímpia, on a day when they went out together, the sound and the gesture of wanting to go to the shop, and pointed, and Olímpia, who already knew a little of his language, understood that he wanted to buy something, and took him, and stood aside, and let him understand himself with the woman of the shop. And Tomé, in his way, with gestures, with sounds, with patience, made the woman of the shop understand that he wanted a scarf, of the kind to put round the neck, and of what colour, and the woman showed him several, and he chose one, blue, and paid with the coins Olímpia had put in his hand, counting them himself, slowly, on the counter. And at home, at night, he gave it to his mother, wrapped in a paper the woman of the shop had folded for him, and made her the sound of birthday, congratulations, and Eulália opened the wrapping and it was the blue scarf, and she understood, by her son's face, that that scarf had not been bought for her, it had been chosen by him, alone, said by him to a stranger who had understood him. And Eulália put the scarf round her neck there and then, and wept, and Tomé beat his hand on his leg, out of contentment, because the mother had liked it, and Eulália understood that that scarf was worth more than any present of her life, because it was the proof, in cloth, that her son could reach the world for himself, and that from the world there came back to him a good thing, chosen and paid for by him, said in his language to one who had had the patience to hear it. The day of the bread asked of the baker alone. The day of the nest shown to the neighbour. The day the woman of the Wednesdays understood, on her own, that Tomé wanted to change the music. Eulália went on keeping these victories as one keeps coins in a jar, and the jar went on filling, and each coin was a proof against the fear, a proof that her son would not be left helpless if she was gone.

And there was a thing Eulália did, in that time, that was the opposite of everything she had always done: she set to teaching Tomé's language to whoever wished to learn it. Instead of keeping it for herself, as she had done her whole life, she sat of an evening with Olímpia, with the woman of the Wednesdays, with Madalena when she came, and even with Quintela one afternoon when he appeared at the house on purpose, with a little notebook, saying he wanted to note down some signs so as not to forget. And Eulália taught the little she could manage to teach of that language of particulars: this sound means this, this gesture that, when he does this with the open hand it is distress, give him time, look to where he points. It cost her, because it was to give up what made her unique, it was to share out her treasure; but she had understood, at last, that to keep her son's language only for herself had been selfishness disguised as love, and that the true love was to spread it, so that Tomé would have many bridges and not one alone.

And even some in the village, on seeing Tomé make himself understood, began themselves to try. There was a scene, in the square, that Eulália kept as a proof that the village was changing. Tomé was alone at the fountain, filling a jug, and there came Norberto, a rough man, one of those who had always treated Tomé over the shoulder, one of those who said, in passing, there goes Eulália's simpleton. And Eulália, from afar, at the window, saw, and her heart tightened, ready to go running down. But she stayed. And Norberto, who had come to fill his jug, stopped beside Tomé, and Tomé made him a sound and pointed to the spout, which was blocked with leaves, and Norberto, at first, made that face of one who does not understand and does not want to understand. And he was going to go on. But Tomé insisted, pointed again, and made the gesture of taking out the leaves, and Norberto, perhaps from shame that everyone in the village was now understanding Tomé but him, looked to where the man pointed, and saw the leaves, and took them out, and the water ran, and Tomé beat his hand on his leg, out of contentment, and made him the sound of thank you. And Norberto, who had never in his life given Tomé the time of day, stayed there an instant, flustered, and then said, half afraid, you are welcome, man, and filled his jug, and went off. And Eulália, at the window, saw Norberto go down the square, and understood that she had just seen a thing worth more than all of Quintela's papers: the village learning, one rough man at a time, to look at Tomé as at a person.

And Eulália watched this happen and understood that she was changing not only her son, but the village, that she was teaching Véspera to hear a person Véspera had always treated as though he had nothing to say, and that this was perhaps the most important thing she did, because a village that learns to hear Tomé is a village that would hold him on the day the mother was gone.

And there was a thing that filled Eulália with a joy hard to explain: to see that Tomé, in gaining other roads to the world, did not draw away from her, on the contrary, drew near in another way, a better one. Because while she had been her son's only bridge, Eulália was for Tomé a necessity, almost a vital organ, and there is little love possible in a dependence so total, there is above all fear, the fear of losing that which one cannot do without. Now that Tomé had other bridges, to be with the mother became a choice and not a necessity, and there is far more love in a choice than in a necessity. Tomé sought his mother because he liked to be with her, not because he had no other remedy, and Eulália felt the difference, felt that her son loved her now more freely, and understood that in letting him go she had not lost his love, she had gained it in a better form.

And there was a night, after that consultation of the knee, when mother and son sat together, with the old fado playing, as they always did, and Eulália looked at Tomé, at the man he was, capable, confident, with a bit of world that was his, and said to him, in the language of the two of them, with gestures and with the sounds that only they understood, a thing that was in her soul: I am proud of you. And Tomé understood, of course, he understood everything, and looked at her a good while, and then answered, in his way, and what he answered, and Eulália translated it to herself with tears in her eyes, was also I am proud of you, mother. Because Tomé saw, he who saw everything, that the mother had changed, that the mother who before was only his shadow went about now more alive, more herself, and Tomé rejoiced in it, because children, even those who speak another language, want happy mothers. And the two of them, that night, in their language of two, with the fado playing, said to each other their pride in each other, no longer as a bridge and the bank it joins, but as two banks that had learned, each one, to have its own ground.
