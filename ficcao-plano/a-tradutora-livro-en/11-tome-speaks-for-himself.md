## 11. Tomé speaks for himself

The net Eulália had begun to weave around her son went on thickening, and it was a beautiful thing to see, for one who knew what was behind it. Where before there had been a single person who understood Tomé, there were now several, each one knowing a little of his language, each one a thread of the net. The trusted neighbour. The woman who helped in the house. That doctor who had learned to have patience. Madalena. And even some in the village who, on seeing Tomé make himself understood, began themselves to try, to have patience, to look at him in another way, not as Dona Eulália's mute son, but as Tomé, a man with whom one could communicate if one wished. And Eulália watched this happen and understood that she was changing not only her son, but the village, that she was teaching Véspera to hear a person Véspera had always treated as though he had nothing to say, and that this, this change in the village, was perhaps the most important thing she did, because a village that learns to hear Tomé is a village that would hold him on the day the mother was gone, a village that would not let him fall into silence, and Eulália, in weaving that net, was doing for her son's future more than any paper of any doctor, she was building him a world that would hear him, instead of being she, alone and mortal, his only ear.

There was a day, in that spring, when Eulália saw a thing that paid for all the pains of letting go, and it was to see her son make himself understood by another without her, in a thing that mattered, and to understand that Tomé, at the end of a lifetime mute to the world, was at last learning to speak for himself, in his way, and that it was she, in falling silent, who had opened that door to him.

Before that consultation there were a hundred small victories, tiny ones, that no one but Eulália noticed and that for her were milestones. The day Tomé asked the baker for the bread alone and was understood. The day he made himself understood by a neighbour to whom he wanted to show a thing. The day the woman who helped in the house understood, alone, that Tomé wanted to change the music, and changed it, and he was pleased. Each one of these things, which in any other family would be nothing, was for Eulália an accumulated proof that the world was capable of learning to hear her son, that Tomé was capable of making himself heard, and that all of it had only waited, for fifty years, for a space she had never given. And Eulália went on keeping these victories like one who keeps coins in a jar, and the jar went on filling, and each coin was a proof against the fear, a proof that her son would not be left helpless if she were gone, that there were already, around him, people and paths, that the net she had begun to weave was holding weight. And it was with the jar already full of those small coins that Eulália had the courage for the great test, that of the consultation, because it was no longer a leap in the dark, it was a step confirmed by a hundred small steps that had gone well.

It was at a consultation, and the irony did not escape her, because everything had begun at a consultation, decades before, and it was now at a consultation that the circle closed. Tomé had to go to the doctor, and Eulália, who her whole life had gone and translated, that time did a thing that cost her everything: she went with him, but let him go in alone. She sat in the waiting room, with her hands trembling, and let her son go in alone into the consulting room, with the doctor, without the bridge, without the translation, alone before a world that had always needed her to hear him. And it was the longest time of Eulália's life, those minutes in the waiting room, with her heart leaping, with the mad desire to go in, to attend to him, to translate, and holding herself, holding herself with all her strength, because she knew that if she went in she undid everything, went back to being the bridge, went back to robbing her son of the chance to learn to cross alone.

It must be said that not everything always went well, and that there were consultations and situations, after that one, in which her son was not understood, in which things went badly, in which Eulália had to intervene at the end, defeated, to translate what the world had not managed to hear. To let go was not a straight line upward; it was a zigzag, with advances and retreats, with good days and bad days, and Eulália had to learn not to give up on the bad days, not to conclude, at the first difficulty, that after all her son could not do it and that it was better to go back to doing everything for him. Because the temptation, at each retreat, was enormous: it was enough for her son not to be understood once for the old voice to tell her you see, he needs you, this letting-go is a fantasy, go back to being the bridge. And Eulália had to learn to answer that voice that one retreat did not erase the advances, that her son managing nine times and failing once did not mean he could not do it, it meant he was learning, and that to learn is just like that, by stumbling, and that if she gave up at the first fall she would never let him come to walk. It was that, perhaps, the hardest discipline: that of bearing the retreats without giving up, of holding the course of letting-go even on the days when the letting-go seemed to be going badly, trusting in the whole and not in each episode.

And Tomé came out, at the end of those minutes, and came out well. It had not been perfect, Eulália knew afterward, there had been difficulties, the doctor had had to make an effort, to have patience, to learn there, on the spot, a little of Tomé's language; but they had understood each other, the two of them, without her, and Tomé came out of the consulting room with that joy on his face, the joy of one who has discovered himself capable, the joy of a man who, at the end of fifty years, had spoken for himself to the world and been heard. And Eulália, on seeing that joy, understood that it had been worth all the pain of letting go, because she had just seen her son do a thing that she, with all her love, had never let him do: to exist in another's eyes without the mother in between, to be a person in his own right and not by translation, to have a little of the world that was his and his alone.

Eulália, in the waiting room, during those eternal minutes, lived a struggle that was the struggle of her whole life concentrated in a single moment. Each sound that came from within made her rise half off the chair, ready to go in, to attend to him, to translate; and each time she forced herself to sit down again, to breathe, to trust. It was like holding herself to the chair with both hands so as not to run, and her whole body hurt from the effort of not doing what her whole life she had done. And there was a moment when she heard her son make the sound of distress, that sound she knew better than her own voice, and she almost could not bear it, almost went in, and it was perhaps the hardest instant of her life, to stay seated hearing her son in distress on the other side of a door and not attend to him, trusting that he and the doctor would manage, trusting against all her instincts as a mother. And she bore it. She stayed. And the sound of distress passed, and there came silence, and then there came calmer sounds, and Eulália understood, from the waiting room, that within her son and the doctor were understanding each other, slowly, at the cost of patience, without her, and that therefore it was possible, that the world was capable, that she could, at last, cease to be the only door through which her son passed to the world.

And the doctor, in taking his leave, said to Eulália a thing that filled her breast. He said: you have a son here who makes himself understood very well, Dona Eulália, when one gives him time. It costs a little at first, but one understands him. You spoiled us, you know, it was always you speaking for him, and we waited comfortably. It is a good thing you let him try. He manages. And Eulália heard that, the he manages, and it was like hearing the absolution of a mistake of a whole lifetime, because the doctor was telling her, without knowing it, that Tomé had always been capable of more, that it had been she, in always translating him, who had kept him dependent, and that now, in falling silent, she freed in her son a capacity that had always been there waiting for room. It was not Tomé who had changed. It was she. And in her changing, a world had opened for her son.

And there was, in that time, a thing Eulália did that was the opposite of everything she had always done, and that was perhaps the most courageous gesture of her life: she began to teach Tomé's language to other people. Instead of keeping it for herself, as she had done her whole life, instead of being the only one who understood him, Eulália sat down with those who dealt with her son — a trusted neighbour, a woman who helped in the house, Madalena, that patient doctor — and went on teaching them the little that could be taught of that language of small particulars: this sound means this, this gesture means that, when he does this it is because he is distressed, give him time, look at where he points. It cost her, because it was to give up what had made her the only one, it was to share out her treasure, it was to cease being the only bridge; but Eulália had understood, at last, that to keep her son's language only for herself had been selfishness disguised as love, and that the true love was to spread it, to teach it, to make many know it, so that Tomé would have many bridges and not only one. And it was so that, around Tomé, there went on forming slowly a small net of people who learned to hear him, and each person who learned was a thread of that net that would hold him on the day the mother was gone, and Eulália, in weaving it, did at last for her son the only thing that truly protected him: not to be everything herself, but to guarantee that, she lacking, he would not be left with nothing.

And there was a moment, in that time, that was for Eulália a kind of prize for everything, and that she kept as one keeps the dearest memory. It was when she understood that Tomé, in gaining confidence, in discovering himself capable of reaching the world for himself, became happier. It was not only that he understood himself better with others; it was that he seemed happier, more whole, more master of himself. Throughout his whole life, Tomé had been a man who depended on his mother for everything, and there is in total dependence, even in the most loving, a silent humiliation, that of being unable to do anything alone, that of needing another for the smallest thing; and Tomé, in beginning to do things for himself, in being heard by others, gained a dignity he had never had, the dignity of one who is a little self-sufficient, of one who is not only a beloved burden but a capable person. And Eulália saw that dignity born in her son and understood that she had taken it from him her whole life without wishing it, that in doing everything for him she had spared him difficulties but deprived him of dignity, and that now, in letting him do, she gave him back a precious thing that she, with the best of loves, had been denying him: the pride of being capable.

Tomé, for his part, changed too, but changed in a way that filled the soul: he gained an ease he had never had, a confidence, a joy of one who discovers that he belongs to the world and not only to his mother. He went on needing her, of course, and there were things that were only with her; but it was no longer the total dependence of before, it was no longer a whole life passing by a single road. Tomé had now other roads, other people who knew how to hear him a little, other paths to the world, and each one of those paths was an insurance against the day the mother was gone, because a son with many roads to the world is not left mute when one of them closes. Eulália understood, seeing her son so, that she was at last doing the only thing that truly protected him for the future: she was weaving him a net of people and of paths so that, on the day she was no longer there, Tomé would not fall into the abyss, because there would be others to hold him, others who had learned to hear him, others whom she, in falling silent, had forced the world to form.

And there was a thing that filled Eulália with a joy hard to explain: to see that Tomé, in gaining other roads to the world, did not draw away from her, but on the contrary, drew near in another way, a better one. Because while she had been her son's only bridge, Eulália was for Tomé a necessity, a dependence, almost a vital organ, and there is little love possible in a dependence so total, there is above all fear, the fear of losing that which one cannot do without. Now that Tomé had other bridges, that he no longer depended on his mother for everything, the relationship of the two changed in nature: Tomé no longer needed his mother to live, and therefore to be with her became a choice and not a necessity, and there is much more love in a choice than in a necessity. Tomé sought his mother because he liked to be with her, not because he had no other remedy, and Eulália felt the difference, felt that her son loved her now more freely, more cleanly, and understood that in letting him go she had not lost his love, she had gained it in a better form, because the love that remains when one takes away the dependence is the true love, the one that stays not because it is needed but because it is wanted.

And there was a night, after that consultation, when mother and son sat together, as they always did, and Eulália looked at Tomé, at the man he was, capable, confident, with a little of the world that was his, and said to him, in the language of the two of them, with gestures and with the sounds that only they understood, a thing that went deep in her soul: I am proud of you. And Tomé understood, of course, he understood everything, and answered, in his way, and what he answered, and Eulália translated it to herself with tears in her eyes, what Tomé answered was also I am proud of you, mother, because Tomé saw, he who saw everything, that the mother had changed, that the mother who before had been only his shadow was now more alive, more herself, more whole, and Tomé rejoiced at that, because children, even those who speak another language, want happy mothers, and Tomé saw his mother learning to be happy, learning to exist, and rejoiced for her as she rejoiced for him, and the two of them, that night, in their language between two, said to each other their pride in one another, no longer as a bridge and the bank it joins, but as two banks that had learned, each one, to have their own ground.
