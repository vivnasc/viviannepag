## 4. The chorus of mothers

Eulália was not the only mother in Véspera carrying a son the world did not know how to read, and in Tomé's years she came to know others, in a group that gathered now and then, and it was in seeing those other mothers that Eulália began, without knowing it, to see herself, like one who sees themselves in a mirror they did not ask for.

It cost her, at first, to join the group, because Eulália was not one for unburdenings, did not like to expose her life, and besides, at bottom, she thought herself different from the others, thought no one understood her case, her Tomé, her giving. But the solitude of carrying everything alone went on pushing her toward the group, the need to be with those who would understand without her having to explain, and she went on staying, and went on seeing. And what she saw was that she was not so different after all, that each one of those mothers also thought her case unique, and that at bottom they all carried variations of the same thing: a son the world did not know how to read, and a life of their own sacrificed to being the bridge between that son and the world. They were a mirror of one another, those mothers, though none of them liked to see herself in the mirror of the others, and Eulália, who had gone to the group looking for company, found there, without wishing it, the portrait of what she herself was and of what she might come to be, in the various paths those women took before the same weight.

There was Madalena, who had let go too soon. Madalena had a son like Tomé, a boy who communicated in another way, and Madalena, tired, or frightened, or out of strength, had left her son in the care of an institution, had sent him to one of those places to which the doctor had wanted to send Tomé, and visited him on Sundays, and the rest of the week lived her life, had her work, her friends, a life of her own. And Eulália, at first, judged Madalena harshly, thought her a cold mother, a mother who had abandoned her son, and there were those in Véspera who judged her so, Madalena who put her son in a place and went off to live her life. But with the years Eulália went on seeing another thing in Madalena: she saw a whole woman, a woman who was her own self, who had a voice, who had wishes, who had not disappeared behind her son, and Eulália, who thought herself a far better mother than Madalena, began to feel, without confessing it, a prick of envy, because Madalena, be she a good or a bad mother, was still a person, and she, Eulália, the perfect mother, no longer knew if she was one.

Madalena, with time, became the most important person of that group for Eulália, though Eulália was slow to admit it, because to admit that the mother who had let go had something to teach her was to admit that she herself, the perfect mother, was perhaps wrong. But Eulália noticed Madalena, observed her sidelong, and saw a thing that intrigued her and troubled her: Madalena spoke of her son with love, visited him, worried for him, but spoke also of herself, of her own things, of her life, with a naturalness that to Eulália seemed almost indecent, because Eulália could not conceive that a mother could have things of her own without taking them from her son. And yet Madalena's son was well, as well or better than Tomé, surrounded by people who knew him, and Madalena was whole, and Eulália could not reconcile that with all she believed, with the idea that a mother's love is measured by the sacrifice, by the renouncing, by the effacing. Madalena was the living proof that one could love a son like that without effacing oneself, and that proof troubled Eulália as every truth troubles us that contradicts that on which we have founded our life.

There was Benvinda, who had clung even more than Eulália, if that was possible. Benvinda had a daughter who needed care, and Benvinda had made her daughter her whole life, even more than Eulália, to the point of not sleeping, of not eating, of not leaving the house, of having become a shadow clinging to her daughter, a woman who no longer had a body of her own, only a body in the service of her daughter. And Eulália looked at Benvinda and saw, with horror, an exaggeration of what she herself was, saw where the path she walked led, and Benvinda frightened her, because Benvinda was ill from so much giving herself, was wasting away from so much caring, and Eulália recognised herself in her, recognised her own path taken to the extreme, and did not like what she saw, but also did not know how to get out of it, because Benvinda's path looked, from within, the path of love, and how does a mother get off the path of love without feeling herself a traitor.

And between these two extremes, the Madalena who had let go and the Benvinda who had clung until she disappeared, Eulália placed herself in the middle, and thought herself wise for it, thought she had found the balance the others lacked: neither to let go like Madalena, nor to sink like Benvinda, but to give herself with measure, to be present without losing herself. That was what she told herself. But the truth, which she did not see, is that she was much closer to Benvinda than she liked to admit, that the only difference between her and Benvinda was that Benvinda could no longer hide what was happening to her and she still could, that Benvinda wasted away in plain sight of everyone and she wasted away within, looking well, functional, efficient, but equally effaced. Eulália used Benvinda as proof that it was not so grave after all, there are those worse off, she said to herself, at least I still manage myself; and to use those worse off as proof that we are well is one of the oldest ways of not looking at one's own well, and Eulália used it, as so many use it, to put off the day of looking at hers.

And there were still others, each with her way of carrying what had fallen to her, and Eulália, in the midst of them, was held to be the best, the most devoted, the one who best understood her son, the one who never failed, and the other mothers looked at Eulália with admiration, oh Eulália, you are a saint, you understand your son, I don't even know how you manage. And Eulália received that admiration as one receives a prize, because it was the only thing she had, it was her worth, to be the best mother, the one who gave herself most, the one who translated best, and the more they admired her the more she gave herself, because the admiration was the food that made her go on, it was the confirmation that it was worth effacing herself, that her effacement was a virtue and not a loss.

There was still, in the group, an older mother, the oldest of all, one Reparada, whose son had already died, and who went on going to the meetings not for having a son to carry but because, she said, she did not know how to be without going. And Reparada was, for Eulália, the most disturbing vision of all, because she was what remains of a mother when the son who was her whole life disappears: there remained a woman without a course, not knowing what to do with her days, with no life of her own to retreat into now that her son was gone, a woman who had given everything to a son and who, the son lost, was left with nothing, not even her own self, who had spent herself wholly. Reparada said, sometimes, with her eyes lost, that since her son had departed she no longer knew what she was for, that she got up in the morning and had nothing to do, that life seemed to her over though her body went on. And Eulália looked at Reparada and saw, with a shiver, her own possible future taken to the extreme: a woman who had given her life to a son and who, the son lacking, discovered that she had kept no life at all for herself, and was left, at the end, with empty hands and empty days and no Eulália to fill them. Reparada was the last warning, the one that showed where Eulália's path led if she followed it to the end: not to an old age of peace, but to an old age of emptiness, that of one who has spent herself wholly in another and kept nothing for the day when that other was no longer there.

But there was a conversation, at one of those meetings, that stayed with Eulália, and that began to set her thinking. It was Madalena, the one who had let go too soon, who said it to her, without malice, almost with pity. The mothers were talking, and someone praised Eulália, as always, oh you are devoted, and Madalena, who listened in silence, said, slowly: devoted, yes, but, Eulália, let me ask you a thing, without your taking offence. You, when you are not caring for Tomé, what do you do? What do you like? What do you want, for yourself, only for yourself? The question was asked without malice, almost with tenderness, but it fell upon Eulália like a stone, because it was a question so simple that any person ought to know how to answer, and she did not, and the not knowing was in itself the most terrible answer. Another woman would say, without thinking: ah, I like to read, or to garden, or to be with my friends, or I don't know. They are things everyone has, likings, wishes, small things that make a person be themselves. And Eulália, set before the question, understood that she had none, that they had all been effaced, that if someone asked her who Eulália was beyond mother she would not know how to answer, because the answer had emptied itself over the years, drop by drop, without her noticing, until nothing remained. And it was that, the discovery of the emptiness where a simple answer ought to be, that opened in Eulália the wound that would lead her, later, first to the bottom and then to the cure, because there are questions that, once asked, can no longer be forgotten, and what do you want, for yourself, was one of those, a question that, planted in Eulália, was going to germinate whether she wished it or not.

And Eulália opened her mouth to answer, and did not know. She stayed there, mouth open, searching for an answer, and did not find it, because there was none. There was no Eulália likes, there was no Eulália wants, there was not, beneath Tomé's mother, an Eulália with likings and wishes, or if there was she was so buried that she no longer knew her name. And Madalena saw that, saw Eulália without an answer, and did not insist, changed the subject with delicacy, but the damage was done, the question had stayed, and Eulália took it home, and the question did not let her go: what do you want, for yourself, only for yourself? And for the first time in many years Eulália found herself thinking not of what Tomé wanted, but of what she wanted, and the silence she found within herself, in asking that question, was a silence that frightened her, because it was the silence of a house where no one lives any more, the silence of a voice that has fallen silent so long ago that it no longer knows if it still exists.

The group of mothers gathered in a borrowed room, at the end of the afternoon, now and then, and they were afternoons that mixed the unburdening and the complaining, the mutual help and a certain silent competition over who sacrificed more, because there is among mothers who carry difficult children a rivalry that no one confesses, that of who suffers more, of who gives more, as though suffering were a medal and the one who suffered most were the best mother. And Eulália, it must be said, liked to win that competition. She liked to be the one who gave herself most, the one who best understood her son, the one who never failed, and she left those afternoons with her invisible medal, confirmed in her sainthood by the others. Only much later did she understand how sick that competition was, how much harm it did to all of them, how it pushed them all deeper into the well of effacement, because in a competition of who sacrifices more the only way to win is to sacrifice more, and therefore to disappear more, and those afternoons, which ought to have been of relief, were after all an engine that made them all sink faster, each one pulling the other down with the example of her own sacrifice. Madalena was the only one who did not enter the competition, and so the others looked at her sidelong, dismissed her, oh Madalena can well talk, she who put her son in a place and washes her hands of it; and Eulália understood, with time, that Madalena did not enter the competition because she was the only one who had already understood that it was a competition that had only losers, and that the prize, the medal of best mother, was paid for with the whole life of the one who won it.

And it was so, by the mirror of the other mothers, by Madalena's question, that Eulália began, slowly, not yet knowing where she was going, her descent and her ascent, to go down into the well of having lost herself and to begin, there at the bottom, to look for a stairway to come back to herself. But that was later. On that day, Madalena's question only opened in her a wound she did not know she had, and Eulália did what she always did when a thing hurt her: she cared for Tomé, filled his day, was his voice, and tried to forget that there was a question about her own self waiting for an answer. But questions that go unanswered do not go away; they stay, silent, growing, and Eulália's was growing, and would come to burst, one day, in the worst way, on the day when her body, tired of a life carrying two, decided, itself, to ask the question again, and that time with a voice Eulália could not ignore.
