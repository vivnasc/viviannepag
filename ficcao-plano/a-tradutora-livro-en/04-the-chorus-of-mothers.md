## 4. The chorus of mothers

Eulália was not the only mother in Véspera to carry a son the world did not know how to read, and in Tomé's years she came to know others, in a group that gathered now and then, in a room lent behind the church, at the end of the afternoon, and it was in seeing those other mothers that Eulália began, without knowing it, to see herself, like one who sees herself in a mirror she did not ask for.

The room had a long table, chairs that did not match, and a gas ring on which the water for the coffee was heated, and it smelled of wax and of damp, and the mothers arrived one by one, some still in their aprons, and sat down, and there was always a cake one brought in turn. It cost Eulália to join them, at first, because she was not one for unburdenings, and besides, deep down, she thought herself different, thought no one understood her case, her Tomé, her giving. But the loneliness of carrying everything alone went on pushing her, and she went on staying, and went on seeing.

There was Madalena. Madalena had a son like Tomé, a boy who communicated in another way, Nelo, and Madalena, years before, had put Nelo in one of those places to which Seixas had wanted to send Tomé, and visited him on Sundays, and the rest of the week lived her own life, had her work as a seamstress, her friends. Eulália, at first, judged her harshly. She remembered the first meeting she went to, when Madalena, asked by one of the women, said with all naturalness that on Sunday she had not been able to go and see Nelo because she had gone to a wedding, and Eulália felt a shiver of disapproval, a mother who trades her son for a wedding. And there were those in Véspera who judged her so, the Madalena who put her son in a place and went off to live her life.

But with the years Eulália went on seeing something else in Madalena. Madalena laughed. She had talk that was not of her son. She spoke of a film she had seen in the large town, of a quarrel she had had with her landlady, of a man who was courting her and whom she did not know whether she wanted. She was a whole woman, a woman who was her own self, and Eulália, who thought herself a far better mother than Madalena, began to feel, without confessing it, a tip of envy, because Madalena, good mother or bad, was still a person, and she, Eulália, the perfect mother, no longer knew whether she was. And there was a thing that intrigued and troubled her: Nelo, in the place, was well, surrounded by people who knew him, and Madalena was whole, and the two seemed happy each in their own way, and that fit nothing of what Eulália believed, of the idea that a mother's love is measured by sacrifice, by renunciation, by effacement.

There was Benvinda. Benvinda had a daughter who needed care, Lúcia, and Benvinda had made of her daughter her whole life, more even than Eulália, to the point of not sleeping, of not eating, of not leaving the house except for Lúcia's errands. She came to the meetings with deep shadows under her eyes and hands that trembled a little, and when they asked her how she herself was, she always said, Lúcia is thus and thus, and never said anything of herself, because there was, in Benvinda, no self to report. Once, a mother, with kindness, said to her, Benvinda, you have to rest, you have to look after yourself, and Benvinda looked at her without understanding, and said, rest for what, I rest when Lúcia rests, Lúcia and I are the same person. And Eulália, hearing that, felt a chill, because she saw, with horror, an exaggeration of what she herself was, saw where the road she was walking led.

There was still, in the group, an older mother, the oldest of all, Reparada, whose son had already died, and who went on going to the gatherings not for having a son to carry but because, she said, she did not know how to be without going. Reparada always sat in the same place, at the back, and spoke little, and had the still eyes of one who looks inward. One afternoon, when the others were complaining of the tiredness of caring, Reparada said, without anyone asking her, with the cold cup in her hands: make the most of the tiredness. That was all she said, and the others fell silent, and Reparada went on, looking at the cup. My Joaquim died nine years ago. You complain that you do not have a minute. I have all the minutes, now. I have the whole day. I get up in the morning and the day is there, all of it, empty, waiting for me, and I have nothing to do with it, because my life was him, and he is gone, and I kept nothing for myself, not a little bit, I thought I always had Joaquim to fill the days. Make the most of the tiredness, for you still have someone to be needed by. And she set down the cup, and no one said anything, and Eulália looked at Reparada and saw, with a shiver, her own possible future: a woman who had given her life to a son and who, the son being gone, discovered she had kept no life at all for herself.

And between these extremes, the Madalena who had let go and the Benvinda who had clung until she disappeared, Eulália placed herself in the middle, and thought herself wise for it: neither to let go like Madalena, nor to sink like Benvinda, but to give of herself with measure. That was what she told herself. But the truth, which she did not see, is that she was far closer to Benvinda than she liked to admit, and that the only difference was that Benvinda could no longer hide what was happening to her and she still could, that Benvinda wasted away in plain sight and she wasted away within, with a good look, functional, efficient, but equally effaced. She used Benvinda as proof that it was not so grave after all, there are those who are worse, she said to herself, I at least still govern myself; and to use those who are worse as proof that we are well is one of the oldest ways of not looking at one's own well.

There was an afternoon, in that room, when the whole thing came to the light of day, in a small dispute over nothing. One of the mothers was telling, with a tip of ill-hidden pride, that she had not slept for three nights, that her daughter was restless and that she stayed on her feet at the bedside the whole night, and she said it waiting for the usual, waiting for the oh poor thing, oh how you sacrifice yourself. And it came, of course. Several nodded, oh three nights, God help us, you are a saint. And Benvinda, stung, said that she had once gone a whole week without lying down, that Lúcia had had a crisis and that she had not closed an eye for seven days, and she said it as one lays down a higher card on the table. And the first mother answered that a week was still little, and was going to go on, and it was then that Madalena, who was listening in silence stirring her coffee, set down the spoon and said, without raising her voice, but in a way that silenced the room: do you hear yourselves? You are arguing about which of you sleeps least. As though it were a prize. Who wins, in this game? The one who wears out first? The one who falls first? And your children, what do they win, with you competing to see who finishes herself off the soonest?

And there was a bad silence, of that kind, and Benvinda flushed, and said, with her voice trembling with hurt, you cannot talk, Madalena, you put yours in a place and washed your hands of it, you do not know what it is not to sleep for a child. And Madalena did not grow angry. She looked at Benvinda with a deep pity, and said, slowly: I know what it is not to sleep for a child, Benvinda. I did not sleep for years. And that is why I understood a thing you have not yet understood: that my son did not need me to die for him. He needed me to live. You do not sleep for Lúcia, and Lúcia needs a whole mother, not a mother coming apart. I do not judge you. I only feel pity, because I have been you, and I know where that ends. And Benvinda did not answer, lowered her eyes to her trembling hands, and Eulália, beside her, saw it all, and within she granted Madalena the right of it, and was ashamed to grant it, because to grant Madalena the right of it was to admit that she herself, the saint of the group, was on the wrong side.

Eulália was held, in the group, to be the best, the most devoted, the one who best understood her son, the one who never failed. And the other mothers looked at her with admiration, oh Eulália, you are a saint, you are the one who understands your son, I do not even know how you manage it. And Eulália received that admiration as one receives a prize, because it was the only thing she had, it was her worth. There was among those mothers, it must be said, a rivalry no one confessed, that of who suffered most, of who gave most, as though suffering were a medal. And Eulália liked to win that competition, and left those afternoons with her invisible medal, confirmed in her sanctity by the others, without seeing that in a competition of who sacrifices herself most the only way to win is to sacrifice oneself more, and therefore to disappear more, and that those afternoons, which should have been of relief, were in fact an engine that made them all sink faster, each one pulling the other down with the example of her own sacrifice.

And there was an afternoon, in that room, that stayed with Eulália for the rest of her life.

They were talking, and someone praised Eulália, as always, oh you are the devoted one, Eulália, you are the one who never fails that boy. And the others nodded. And Madalena, who was listening in silence, with the cup in her hand, set it down, and said, slowly, with a tenderness that took from the question all its malice: devoted, yes. But, Eulália, let me ask you a thing, without your taking offence. You, when you are not seeing to Tomé, what do you do? What do you like? What do you want, for yourself, only for yourself?

And there was a silence at the table. Another woman would say, without thinking, ah, I like to embroider, or to go to Sunday Mass, or a walk by the river, or I don't know. They are things everyone has. And Eulália opened her mouth, and stayed there, with her mouth open, searching for an answer, and did not find it, because there was none. There was no Eulália likes, there was no Eulália wants. She searched, in distress, for some thing to say, and the only thing that came to her head was Tomé, and she understood that if she said Tomé she was proving the question, and she fell silent, and the falling silent was the most terrible answer of all. She felt the blood rise to her face, and the silence lasted what it lasted, and it was a silence that hurt, and Reparada, at the back, raised her still eyes and looked at Eulália, and Eulália understood that Reparada understood, that Reparada was seeing herself of thirty years ago, and she was afraid.

Madalena saw that, saw Eulália without an answer, and did not insist. She changed the subject delicately, set to talking of the cake, asked who had brought it. But the damage was done. The question had stayed, and Eulália carried it home, and the question did not let go of her: what do you want, for yourself, only for yourself? And that night, after putting Tomé to bed, Eulália sat at the table and caught herself asking it again, of herself, in the dark of the kitchen, and the silence she found within herself was a silence that frightened her, because it was the silence of a house where no one lives any longer, the silence of a voice that has been quiet so long it no longer even knows whether it still exists.

Madalena was the only one who did not enter the competition of the mothers, and that was why the others looked at her askance, oh Madalena can well talk, she put her son in a place and washes her hands of it; and Eulália understood, with time, that Madalena did not enter the competition because she was the only one who had already understood that it was a competition with only losers, and that the prize, the medal of best mother, was paid for with the whole life of whoever won it. And so it was, by the mirror of the other mothers, by Madalena's question, that Eulália began, slowly, without yet knowing where she was going, her descent and her ascent.

But that was later. That day, Madalena's question only opened in her a wound she did not know she had, and Eulália did what she always did when a thing hurt her: she saw to Tomé, filled his day, was his voice, and tried to forget that there was a question about her own self waiting for an answer. But the questions that go unanswered do not go away; they stay, silent, growing, and Eulália's was growing, and it would burst, one day, in the worst way, on the day when her body, tired of a life carrying two, decided, itself, to ask the question again, and that time with a voice Eulália could not ignore.
