## 2. The consultation

To understand Eulália one must go back to a consultation, far back, when Tomé was still a small boy, because it was in that consultation that everything was born: the love that saved her and the effacing that consumed her, the two in the same instant, of the same gesture, like twins that come into the world clasped together.

Tomé would have been five, six years old. The parents, Eulália and Aníbal, went from doctor to doctor in search of someone who would tell them what their son had, and they all went to a large town, on the seven o'clock coach, with the boy between the two of them, and Eulália remembered those waiting rooms as a nightmare that repeated itself: the smell of disinfectant, the hard wooden benches, the other mothers with their boys, each with her own silent distress. She remembered how her heart tightened each time they called the name, because she knew what was coming: another doctor looking at her son as at a riddle, more big words spoken over the boy's head, another diagnosis that said nothing of the Tomé she knew, of the Tomé who laughed when she sang, who was afraid of thunder, who loved the feel of wool.

And there was one consultation, one in particular, in an office with green walls and a clock that ran slow, that changed Eulália's life. A doctor from away, a specialist, Doctor Seixas, to whom they had been sent as one sends a last hope. He was a man of a very white coat and gold-rimmed spectacles, and he had before him a folder with Tomé's papers, and it was to that folder that he spoke almost the whole time, without looking at the boy. He examined Tomé without saying a word to him, took his head, turned it, shone a light into his eye, and Tomé let him, rigid, with his mother's hand gripped in his, and at a certain point Seixas set down the light and began to speak, turned toward the parents.

The child has a considerable delay, he said. I see no conditions here, at home, to stimulate him as he would need. There are institutions, now, good ones, with people who are prepared, where a child like this is looked after by those who know. I know it is hard to hear, but the lady will come to understand, in time, that it is better for everyone. For him and for you. And he spoke slowly, with the patience of one who had given that news many times, and he had the pencil in his hand pointing to an address on a paper, the address of the place to which he wanted to send the boy, a place far off, of boys like him.

And Tomé, who understood everything, understood that. Not all the words, but enough: he understood the tone, he understood the paper, he understood the mother turning pale, and he grew distressed, and began to make his sound, that high sound of great distress, and to beat with his open hands on his legs, and to turn his head in search of the mother. And Seixas raised his eyes from the folder, and pointed at the boy with the pencil, and said, you see. You see how agitated he is. It is what I say. One cannot keep a boy like this at home, madam, this comes undone over anything and nothing, and the lady will not have hands enough to manage him.

And he was going to go on, he was going to write the address, he was going to decide, on the strength of a sound he took for agitation and that was, Eulália knew it, was terror, was a boy understanding that they wanted to take him from his mother and crying out, in his language, no.

And Eulália, who until then had kept silent, who until then had been the humble and frightened mother who listens to the doctors with her head bowed, rose to her feet. She rose from the chair, which was not done, and Aníbal stretched out his hand to make her sit, Eulália, and she did not sit. Doctor, she said, and the voice came out of her from a place she did not know she had. He is not agitated. He is afraid. He understood what you said and he is saying that he does not want to go, that he wants to stay with me. Seixas frowned, and was going to speak, and Eulália did not let him. Look, doctor. Look at his hand. When he is afraid, he opens his hand like this, and beats with it open. When it is temper, it is the closed fist. Look. It is open. This is not coming undone, doctor, it is a terrified boy hearing that they are going to send him far from his mother, and he understands everything that is said in this room, everything, and you are calling want of sense what is the only thing he can manage to tell you.

And Seixas stopped. He set down the pencil. He looked at the boy, and then looked at the boy's hand, the open hand beating on the leg, and then looked at Eulália, and for the first time in that consultation he looked at Tomé as at a person.

There was, in that room, after Eulália spoke, a silence she never forgot. The clock that ran slow struck an hour that was not the hour. Seixas, who had spoken the whole time with the assurance of one who knows, fell silent, and took off his spectacles, and cleaned them with a handkerchief, slowly, which was what he did, it was understood later, when he needed time. And he asked, in another voice: you understand him as well as that? And Eulália said yes, that she was the mother, that she had learned to read him. And Seixas, who was after all a decent man, only hampered by his own ignorance, put his spectacles back on, looked again at the boy, and said, perhaps I was hasty. With a mother like that, the situation is another. Perhaps there is no need to send him anywhere. And he took the paper with the address, and folded it, and set it aside, and it was that gesture Eulália never forgot: the paper being folded and set aside, her son's life changing course because of thirty seconds in which she had opened her mouth.

Thirty seconds. And the rest of Tomé's life had depended on them. That was the lesson that drove itself into Eulália deeper than any other: that she, with her voice, could change her son's fate; and that therefore she had to be always, always, ready to use it, because an absence of hers of thirty seconds in the wrong room could cost Tomé a whole lifetime.

That night, in the boarding house where they stayed because it was too late for the coach back, Eulália did not sleep. Lying in the dark, with the boy asleep between her and Aníbal, she went over and over the instant in which she had spoken and the doctor had changed his mind, and she felt a thing she had never felt: power. She, Eulália, a simple woman of Véspera, who had never been anyone in particular, had discovered that she had an immense power, the power to make the world see her son, the power to save him from those who did not understand him, and that power filled her with a meaning she had never had. For the first time in her life, she knew exactly what she was for: she was for being Tomé's voice. It was her mission, her place in the world, and there was a fierce joy in that discovery, the joy of one who finds a purpose that makes them indispensable. What Eulália did not see, on that night of power, was the reverse of the medal: that a purpose that makes us indispensable is also a chain, and that she had just chained herself, with joy, to a life of never being able to be absent.

There was, years after that one, another room that repeated the lesson, and it was a classroom. Tomé went, for some years, to the village school, because the new teacher, Dona Idília, thought it worth trying, and Eulália went with him in the morning and stayed at the door, and often inside, on a bench at the back, because the teacher, alone with thirty children, had no way of understanding Tomé. There was a day when Dona Idília did a sum on the board and asked the class how much it was, and the children said numbers every which way, and the teacher noticed that Tomé, at the back, was beating his hand on the desk, quickly, three times, three times, and had his eyes on the board. And Dona Idília, who did not know the language, was going to go on, was going to call another child, and Eulália, from the bench at the back, rose and said, teacher, he knows. He is saying that it is three. He beats three times because it is three. Ask him. And Dona Idília, with some mistrust, asked, Tomé, how much is it, and Tomé beat three times again, slowly, looking at the teacher, and the teacher looked at the sum, and it was three, and she was silent a moment, and then said, before the whole class, he is right. It is correct. And the children turned round, to look at Tomé, and Tomé lowered his eyes, but it could be seen that he was pleased, because he had his open hand beating lightly on his leg, which was the opposite of the closed fist of distress, it was the hand of joy.

And Eulália left the school, that day, with the same thing in her breast that she had left the green-walled office with: the certainty that without her no one would know that Tomé knew, that she was the difference between her son being taken for a simpleton and her son being taken for intelligent, and that she had, therefore, to be always there, always, in every room, in every lesson, in every place where the world might judge her son ill for not knowing how to hear him. The teacher, the next time, looked again at Eulália when Tomé beat on the desk, waiting for the translation, and Eulália translated, and the teacher never learned to count Tomé's taps, because she had no need to, because there was always the mother to count them for her. And so the school, like the consultation, was one more place where Eulália saved her son from being judged wrong and, in saving him, spared the world from learning to hear him.

It is just to say, before speaking of the prison, that without that love Tomé would have had no life at all. There were a thousand consultations and a thousand lessons and a thousand counters after those, a thousand situations in which, without the mother speaking, the son would have been ill-treated, ill-understood, decided for by those who did not know him, and Eulália was always there. One cannot tell Eulália's story without saying this out loud: that what she did was heroic, that few mothers would have had the patience and the attention to learn a son's language and to be his voice for decades, and that Tomé had, because of her, a far better life than he would have had without her. The prison does not cancel the love; the love was true, and it was great. It is only that it had, as so many loves have, a reverse that no one sees while they are loving.

Aníbal, he had sat through the whole consultation. He was the father, he was there, but when the thing grew tight it was the mother who rose, it was the mother who spoke, it was the mother who folded the paper with the address with a sentence. And on the way back, the next day, on the coach, with the boy asleep on Eulália's lap, Aníbal said only one thing, looking at the road. Good thing you understood, Eulália. I would not have known what to say. He said it without bitterness, that day, almost with gratitude. But Eulália would come to remember that sentence many years later, and to hear it in another way, because it was there, on that coach, that there was said aloud for the first time the division that would come to separate them: she understood, he did not. She knew what to say, he stayed at her side, mute. And from that day on, consultation after consultation, situation after situation, it was always so, always she with the power and he looking on, until the gulf was so deep that Aníbal could no longer cross it. The consultation that saved Tomé and that gave Eulália her mission was also, without anyone seeing it that day, the consultation that began to lose the marriage.

And there was still a thing, in the consultation, that Eulália would only understand much later. It is that in that office Seixas had learned to look at Tomé because Eulália had shown him how; but, the next time, and every next time, no doctor ever again had to learn Tomé's language, because there was always the mother to translate, and no one learns what they do not need. In being so good a bridge, Eulália became the only bridge, and a road by which only one person passes closes itself to all the others. Without wishing it, in being her son's voice, she was also, throughout his whole life, the reason the world never learned to hear him: because she heard him so well that no one else had to try.

But that was a truth for the end of the story. At the beginning, in that green-walled office, there was only the love and the fear, and Eulália left there Tomé's mother forever, with the paper of the address folded in her mind like a trophy and like a sentence, and she went, from that day on, ceasing to be, slowly, waiting room by waiting room, year by year of translation, the Eulália who was her own self.
