## 2. The consultation

To understand Eulália one must go back to a consultation, far back, when Tomé was still a small boy, because it was in that consultation that everything was born: the love that saved her and the effacing that consumed her, the two in the same instant, of the same gesture, like twins that come into the world clasped together.

Eulália reconstructed that scene in her head many times, across her life, because it was the instant in which everything was decided, and the more she reconstructed it the more she understood what had happened there, not only with her son, but with her. Because until that day Eulália had been a frightened mother, lost, not knowing what to do with a son she did not understand, feeling herself a bad mother for not managing to reach him, doubting herself at every consultation in which the doctors looked at her as though the fault were hers. And in that consultation everything changed, not only for Tomé, but for the very image Eulália had of herself: from lost and guilty mother she became, in an instant, the mother who knew, the mother who understood, the indispensable mother, and that change was for her a salvation, because it took her out of doubt and guilt and gave her a certainty, a place, a worth. Eulália clung to that new self — the translator, the one who knows — with the strength of one who clings to a plank after years of drowning in doubt, and never again let it go, because to let it go would be to return to the lost and guilty mother she had been before, and that was what she feared most.

Tomé would have been five, six years old, and he was still a mystery to everyone, a boy who did not speak like the others and whom the village did not know where to place. The parents, Eulália and her husband, went from doctor to doctor in search of someone who would tell them what their son had and what could be done, and Eulália remembered those waiting rooms as a nightmare that repeated itself, she with the boy on her lap, the boy distressed by the strange place, and the doctors who looked at Tomé the way one looks at a broken thing that needs mending, and spoke of him in front of him, as though he were not there, as though he did not understand, when he understood everything, Eulália saw him understand, saw the distress on her son's face when they spoke of him as of a case.

Eulália remembered those waiting rooms with a sharpness the years had not worn away: the smell of disinfectant, the hard benches, the other mothers with their boys, each with her own silent distress, and she with Tomé on her lap trying to calm him in a place that frightened him. She remembered how her heart tightened each time they called the name, because she knew what was coming: there came another doctor looking at her son as at a riddle, more big words spoken over the boy's head, another diagnosis that said nothing of the Tomé she knew, of the Tomé who laughed when she sang, who was afraid of thunder, who loved the feel of wool, who was a whole person with likings and fears and a way of his own of loving. The doctors saw a case; she saw a son; and between the case they saw and the son she knew there was an abyss that only she seemed able to cross, and she crossed it always, in every consultation, translating her son from person to those who knew only how to see him as a problem.

And there was one consultation, one in particular, that changed Eulália's life. A doctor from away, a specialist, to whom they had been sent as one sends a last hope. The man examined Tomé without speaking to him, speaking only to the parents, and at a certain point he began to say things, big words, and to decide, there, in front of the boy, what was going to be done with him, and what was going to be done with him was a thing that frightened Eulália, a thing of sending the boy away, to a place for boys like him, because, said the doctor, at home there were no means, and a boy like that needed an institution, and it was better for everyone, and the mother would come to understand, in time, that it was a relief.

And Tomé, who understood everything, understood that. He understood that they were talking of sending him away, and he grew distressed, and began to make his sound, that high sound of great distress, and to beat with his hands, and the doctor, who did not speak his language, saw in that the confirmation of what he was saying, you see, he said, he is agitated, it is what I am telling you, one cannot keep a boy like this at home. And he was going to go on, going to decide, going to send the boy away on the strength of a sound he took for agitation and that was, Eulália knew it, was terror, was a boy understanding that they wanted to take him from his mother and crying out, in his language, no, do not send him away, that he wanted to stay with his mother.

And Eulália, who until then had kept silent, who until then had been the humble and frightened mother who listens to the doctors with her head bowed, rose up within, and translated. She said, in a voice she did not know she had, doctor, he is not agitated, he is afraid. He understood what you said, and he is saying that he does not want to go, that he wants to stay with me. He understands everything that is said in this room, doctor, everything, and what you are taking for want of sense is a terrified boy hearing that they are going to send him away from his mother. And the doctor stopped, and looked at her, and looked at the boy, and for the first time in that consultation he looked at Tomé as at a person, and it was Eulália's translation that opened his eyes, it was she, and she alone, who in that instant saved her son from a fate decided by one who did not understand him.

There was, in that room, after Eulália translated, a silence she never forgot. The doctor, who had spoken the whole time with the assurance of one who knows, fell silent, and looked at the boy with other eyes, and then looked at Eulália, and said, with a new humility: you understand him as well as that? And Eulália said yes, that she was the mother, that she knew him, that she had learned to read him. And the doctor, who was after all a decent man, only hampered by his own ignorance, did a rare thing: he almost apologised, said that perhaps he had been hasty, that if the lady understood him then the situation was another, that with a mother like that perhaps there was no need to send him anywhere. And Eulália understood, in that moment, that the difference between her son going away and her son staying with her had been a sentence of hers, a translation, thirty seconds in which she had opened her mouth and said what the boy wanted to say. Thirty seconds. And the rest of Tomé's life had depended on them. That was the lesson that drove itself into Eulália deeper than any other: that she, with her voice, could change her son's fate; and that therefore she had to be always, always, ready to use it, because an absence of hers of thirty seconds in the wrong room could cost Tomé a whole lifetime.

And the fate changed. The boy did not go away. He stayed with his mother, at home, in Véspera, because the mother had proved, in that consultation, that she understood him, that there was a person there behind the mystery, and that that person had someone who knew how to read her. And Eulália left that consultation trembling, but trembling with something new, with a new power, because she had just discovered a truth that would govern her whole life: that she was the bridge. That without her her son was at the mercy of those who did not understand him. That she, and she alone, made the difference between Tomé being a person and Tomé being a case. And that therefore she had to be always there. Always. In every consultation, in every room, in every moment in which the world drew near her son, there had to be Eulália, translating, defending, making the bridge, because if she were absent, even once, what could happen to Tomé was what had nearly happened in that consultation: to be decided for by one who did not know how to hear him.

That night, after the consultation, Eulália did not sleep. Lying in the dark, with the boy asleep beside her, she went over and over the instant in which she had translated her son and the doctor had changed his mind, and she felt a thing she had never felt: power. She, Eulália, a simple woman of Véspera, who had never been anyone in particular, had discovered that she had an immense power, the power to make the world see her son, the power to save him from those who did not understand him, and that power filled her with a meaning she had never had. For the first time in her life, Eulália knew exactly what she was for: she was for being Tomé's voice. It was her mission, her reason, her place in the world, and there was a fierce joy in that discovery, the joy of one who finds a purpose that makes them indispensable. What Eulália did not see, on that night of power, was the reverse of the medal: that a purpose that makes us indispensable is also a chain, and that she had just chained herself, with joy, to a life of never being able to be absent.

And it is just to say, before speaking of the prison, that without that love Tomé would have had no life at all. There were a thousand consultations after that one, a thousand situations in which, without the mother translating, the son would have been ill-treated, ill-understood, decided for by those who did not know him, and Eulália was always there, always, making the bridge, defending, explaining, and each one of those times was a son saved from a wrong fate. One cannot tell Eulália's story without saying this out loud: that what she did was heroic, that few mothers would have had the patience and the attention and the giving to learn the language of a son and to be his voice for decades, that Tomé had, because of her, a far better life than he would have had without her, a life in which he was treated as a person because there was someone there insisting, against a hurried world, that he was a person. The prison does not cancel the love; the love was true, and it was great. It is only that it had, as so many loves have, a reverse that no one sees while they are loving, and that reveals itself only much later, when the price, kept silent for years, comes at last to be collected.

It was a love, that. It was the greatest love there is, that of a mother who places herself between her son and a world that does not understand him. But it was also, without Eulália seeing it, the beginning of a prison, because from that consultation on Eulália could no longer be absent. A mother who discovers that she is her son's only bridge to the world is a mother who condemns herself to being always present, to never falling ill, to never having a life of her own, to never being able to say today I cannot, because the today I cannot of a bridge is the son left mute, isolated, at the mercy of all. And Eulália, in discovering her power to save him, also discovered, without knowing it, her captivity: that of one who is so necessary to another that they cease to be able to belong to themselves.

Aníbal was at that consultation, beside her, and it was also a decisive moment for the marriage, though neither of them knew it then. Because it was there, in that room, that the division was drawn that would come to separate them: it was Eulália who translated, it was Eulália who saved the boy, it was Eulália the heroine of that instant, and Aníbal stayed at her side, mute, useless, a father who had looked on without being able to do anything while his wife saved their son with a sentence. And Aníbal left that consultation diminished, without saying so, with the sense that where his son was concerned he was good for nothing, that it was the mother who was needed, that he was a companion; and Eulália left it enlarged, with her new power, with her mission. And that difference, the wife enlarged and the man diminished before the same son, went on deepening from that day, consultation after consultation, situation after situation, always she with the power and he looking on, until the gulf was so deep that Aníbal could no longer cross it, and he went. The consultation that saved Tomé and that gave Eulália her mission was also, without anyone seeing it, the consultation that began to lose the marriage, because a couple does not survive one of the two always being the hero and the other always the witness, above all where what matters most to the two is concerned, which was the son.

And there was still a thing, in the consultation, that Eulália would only understand much later, and that was the reverse of all of it. It is that in translating her son so perfectly, in being so good a bridge, Eulália also became the only bridge, and a road by which only one person passes closes itself to all the others. In that consultation, the doctor learned to look at Tomé because Eulália translated him; but, the next time, and every next time, the doctor never again had to learn Tomé's language, because there was always the mother to translate, and no one learns what they do not need. And so, without wishing it, in being her son's voice, Eulália was also, throughout his whole life, the reason the world never learned to hear him: because she heard him so well that no one else had to try. But that was a truth for the end of the story, and at the beginning, in that consultation, there was only the love and the fear, and Eulália left there Tomé's mother forever, and from that day on she went, slowly, ceasing to be, queue by consultation queue, waiting room by waiting room, year by year of translation, the Eulália who was her own self.
