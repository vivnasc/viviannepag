## From the register of Véspera

In the record of the village, in the hand of the Keepers who have kept the book, the following is found:

Eulália, daughter of Véspera folk, mother of Tomé. She was, for a whole lifetime, her son's voice to the world: the one who understood him when no one else understood him, who translated him to the doctors, to the teachers, to the village, who spoke for him everywhere, because he had come into the world saying things in another way, and it was she, and she alone, who learned his language.

Her name was a promise, of the Véspera kind: her mother wanted her to bear the name of a thing that meant well-spoken, easy of voice, clear of word, in the hope of a daughter who would know how to say herself to the world. And the world gave a woman who spent her life saying others and never saying herself, who was the voice of everything but her own self, and who only very late, when she could barely remember the sound of her own voice, discovered that she had one too, and that the hour had come to use it.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs a mother to be the only bridge between a son and the world, nor what is lost of oneself when one spends a lifetime speaking for another. There are those who efface themselves so far in serving as the voice of one they love that they forget they have a voice of their own. For those, books such as this one are needed.

This is a novel of mother and son, this one, like the others on this shelf, and it is about the weight that is carried out of love. But the weight, here, is a weight of a particular kind: it is the voice of another, carried in one's own mouth for a lifetime, until one's own voice falls silent beneath it. Whoever has ever lost themselves in caring for someone they love, whoever has given so much that they forgot to exist, whoever no longer knows how to answer, if asked, what they want for themselves, that one will recognise themselves in Eulália, and will learn, with her, that to recover one's own voice is possible at any age, and that to love does not have to mean to disappear.
