## 3. The husband who was left outside

There was a man in Eulália's life, the husband, Tomé's father, and his story is the story of one who was left outside a language that only two people spoke, and went away, slowly, for not fitting inside it.

They married young, Eulália and Aníbal, before Tomé, and there was a time, brief, when it was only the two of them. Eulália, when much later she went looking for her own self, would fetch memories from that time, because it was the only one in which she had been someone before being Tomé's mother. She remembered a fair, in the first year of marriage, when the two of them argued, in front of everyone, over a pair of clogs she wanted and he thought dear, and she scolded him and bought the clogs all the same, and he laughed, and said, there you go being contrary, and they went home arm in arm, she with her new clogs. She remembered having been that woman, the one of the clogs, the one who said what she wanted and bought it. That time lasted little, because Tomé came early, and with Tomé came all the rest, and the woman of the clogs went on fading behind the mother it was necessary to be.

Aníbal was a good man, and he loved his son in his own way, which was an awkward way, of one who does not know what to do with a boy who does not answer to things the way other boys do. He tried, at first, to learn Tomé's language. There was a Saturday, Eulália remembered it well, when Aníbal sat on the kitchen floor with the boy and a little wooden cart he had made him, set on playing with him and on understanding what he wanted. And Tomé made a sound, and Aníbal did not understand, and gave him the cart, and it was not the cart, and Tomé made the sound again, more distressed, and Aníbal took him up in his arms, and it was not the arms, and the boy began to cry, and Aníbal, flustered, turned to the mother, who was stirring the broth, and said, Eulália, translate me what he wants, for I understand nothing. And Eulália, without letting go of the spoon, said, he wants the cart but moving, make it run along the floor, that is it, and Aníbal set the cart running along the floor, and Tomé fell quiet and laughed, and Aníbal stayed there, sitting on the floor, making the cart run, with a face that Eulália only much later knew how to read: the face of a man who understood, that Saturday, that to reach his own son he would have to go through his wife. And so, slowly, without anyone deciding it, a division of labour came to be created inside that house: the mother understood the son, the father did not, and what the son wanted to say to the father he said through the mother, and the mother stayed in the middle of the two.

A bridge, in the middle of a marriage, is a dangerous thing, because it joins two banks but also keeps them apart, and Aníbal came to feel himself, year after year, more and more a bank, more and more outside.

There were signs, which Eulália only knew how to read when it was already late. There was a supper, one, that she never forgot after she learned to see it. Aníbal had come home from work pleased, which was rare, and began to tell a thing, that the master had promised him a few more weeks of work, that perhaps there would be money to mend the roof, and he was in the middle of the sentence, with the eyes shining of one who brings good news, when Tomé, at the head of the table, made a sound. Eulália rose in the middle of her husband's sentence, went to the boy, understood that he wanted water, gave him water, sat down again, and said, well then, Aníbal, you were saying. And Aníbal looked at her, and the news had gone out of his face, and he said, nothing, it was nothing, leave it. And he finished eating in silence. And the next time there was good news, he no longer told it at supper; he kept it to himself, or said it at the café, to the men, who heard him out to the end.

There were the nights when Aníbal stretched out his hand to her in bed and Eulália, exhausted from a whole day of vigilance, with her ear still half in her son's room, turned to the other side, and he drew back his hand, and did not stretch it out again for weeks. There were the Sundays when he proposed the three of them go out, go to the river, do a family thing, and Eulália said it was complicated because of Tomé, that the boy came undone away from home, and they stayed, and Aníbal went off alone to take a walk, and came back with wild flowers in his hand that he put in a glass and that no one noticed. The signs were all there, but Eulália did not read them, because she had her eyes all on Tomé, and a person who has their eyes all on one thing does not see the rest coming apart around them.

Aníbal complained, at first. There was a night, after the boy fell asleep, when he called her to the kitchen and said to her, with a seriousness she did not know in him: Eulália, I feel myself outside in this house. You have eyes only for Tomé. I am here too. I am his father too, and your husband, and sometimes it seems I am a stranger who lives here on sufferance. And Eulália, who had no way of giving him what he asked without taking from her son, heard that as a selfish complaint, as a man who was jealous of his own sick son, and answered badly. She answered, Tomé needs me more than you, Aníbal. You are a grown man. Are you not ashamed of being jealous of a boy who cannot even govern himself? I do what I have to do, and you had better pull yourself together. And Aníbal stayed looking at her, and did not answer, and went off to bed, and Eulália would come to think much, later, about that answer, and to understand that it had been unjust. Because what Aníbal asked was not that she love her son less; it was that there be, in that house, room for more than the son, room for the couple, room for her as a woman, for him as father and as man. And Eulália did not know how to hear that, because for her there was only one way of loving, which was whole, exclusive, total, and one who loves like that thinks that loving one is betraying the others, and it was that incapacity to share out the love that cost her her husband.

There was, in the last winter before Aníbal went, a night that Eulália only managed to read much later, and that was, she understood then, the last time her husband stretched out his hand to her. Tomé had fallen asleep early, and the two of them were in the kitchen, and Aníbal, who had been silent for months, said, suddenly, without raising his eyes from his cup: Eulália, what if the two of us went somewhere or other, on Sunday. Just us. We would leave the boy with your sister an afternoon, and the two of us would go to the river, like before, or to the fair in the large town, or nowhere at all, just walk, the two of us. I still remember the woman of the clogs, Eulália. I should like to see her again. And Eulália, who at that time did not know how to hear a request except as a threat to her son, answered, without thinking, that Tomé would not stay with her sister, that no one understood him, that he came undone, that it was complicated, and that he, Aníbal, knew that well. And Aníbal nodded yes, slowly, and did not insist, and finished his coffee, and went off to bed. And Eulália, years later, would come to understand that he had not asked her for the river nor the fair; he had asked her for the woman of the clogs, had asked her to exist again for one afternoon, and that she had answered him with Tomé, as she answered everything, and that that had been, perhaps, the last door her husband opened to her before packing his case, and that she had closed it without even seeing it was a door.

And Aníbal fell silent, and his silence went on growing larger, and one night, at the end of a long winter, Aníbal put his clothes in a cardboard case, without hurry, folded with care, in front of her, in the bedroom. There was no great scene. Eulália, leaning against the door jamb, asked only, you are going away. And he said yes. And she asked, is there another woman. And he stopped folding the shirt, and looked at her, and said a thing that stayed with her: no, Eulália. There is no other. I am not going away because of anyone. I am going away because for years I have not been here. I left this house long before packing the case. The two of you speak a language I do not speak, and I have tired of asking for a translation to get into my own home. It is less painful to be outside on purpose than to be outside inside one's own house. And he finished packing the case, and went, without slamming the door, slowly, and Eulália stayed in the kitchen listening to his steps going down the path, and the broth on the fire, and the clock.

Tomé was then in his years of adolescence, and the father's leaving was, for him, a wound that Eulália only managed to measure later. Tomé, in his own way, felt the abandonment, and perhaps more than another son, because he had no way of speaking of it, of putting it into words, of unburdening it to anyone but his mother. Eulália remembered the night when the boy, lying down, made a sound she had never heard, a low sound, repeated, and she was slow to understand, because it was a new sound, and when she understood her heart tightened, because the sound meant where, and she knew that he was asking after his father, and she had to tell him, in the language of the two of them, with gestures and with her voice, that the father had gone, that he was not coming back to live there, and Tomé wept, in his language, and Eulália consoled him, and the two were left closer still, more alone in the world speaking their language between two. Tomé depended on his mother for everything, and depended on her even to suffer, even to weep for his father, because it was she who translated the world to him and himself to him, and a son who depends on his mother even to understand what he feels is a son doubly imprisoned, and Eulália, without wishing it, was the key to those two prisons, the outer and the inner.

And the truth, the truth she only admitted to herself many years later, is that her husband's leaving, in the midst of all the sadness, also brought her a relief that she was ashamed to feel: that of no longer having to divide her attention, of being able to be wholly her son's, of no longer having anyone complaining that she had eyes only for Tomé. Aníbal's leaving freed her to give herself whole to her son, and she gave herself, and in that total giving, with no husband to call her back to herself, she effaced herself faster still, because now there was no one at home to say to her Eulália, and you, what do you want. There was no longer anyone who saw her as a woman, there was only Tomé, who saw her as mother, as voice, as bridge, and a woman who is seen only as mother ends up seeing herself only as mother.

The years that followed Aníbal's leaving were, for Eulália, the years of total giving, and they were years she would recall with a strange mixture of longing and of horror. Longing, because they were years of a full intimacy with her son. Horror, because they were also the years in which she let fall the last threads that tied her to a life of her own. The friends went on tiring of an Eulália who never could, who never went out, who spoke only of her son; Conceição, who had been her best friend as a girl, still insisted for some years, still came to the door time without number, come along, Eulália, it is only an afternoon, I will stay with Tomé, and Eulália always said no, that Tomé would stay with no one, that no one understood him, and Conceição, after some years of no, stopped knocking at the door, and Eulália noticed she had stopped knocking when it was already late to call her back. The small likings she went on abandoning one by one. Her very vanity, the caring for herself, she left, because what for, for whom, if her life was her son and her son did not notice such things. Year after year, Eulália went on stripping herself of everything that was hers, and each stripping seemed to her, in the moment, an act of love, a proof of a good mother, and was in truth a step down into the well where she would come to find herself, years later, with nothing, with no one beyond her son, with no life to call her own.

Years later, looking at it all with other eyes, Eulália would acknowledge that she had been unjust to Aníbal, and that the injustice had been not letting him in. Because what Aníbal asked, at bottom, when he complained of being outside, was not that she take attention from Tomé; it was that she let him too learn his son's language, that she share the bridge with him instead of keeping it all for herself. And Eulália had not let him, without noticing it, because being the only one who understood Tomé was her place, her worth, and to share that place with her husband was to give up a part of what made her indispensable. It was a guilt Eulália would come to carry, later, when she learned to see: that perhaps her son could have had a father, if she had known how to share his language instead of hoarding it; that the Saturday of the cart could have been the first of many, instead of the last.

And there was an immense sweetness in that union, mother and son against a world that did not understand them, but there was also, beneath the sweetness, a thing that would come to collect its price: that two who are enough for each other close themselves to the rest, and Eulália and Tomé, in being enough for each other, went on closing, she in his voice, he in hers, until they were a single thing with two heads, and a single thing with two heads is a thing that cannot survive the loss of one of them, and it was that, that fragility at the bottom of all that strength, that life would come to show Eulália on the day when one of the two heads, hers, threatened to fail.
