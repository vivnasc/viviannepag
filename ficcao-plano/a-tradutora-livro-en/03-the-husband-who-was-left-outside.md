## 3. The husband who was left outside

There was a man in Eulália's life, the husband, Tomé's father, and his story is the story of one who was left outside a language that only two people spoke, and went away, slowly, for not fitting inside it.

They married young, Eulália and Aníbal, before Tomé, and there was a time, brief, when it was only the two of them, a man and a woman beginning a life, and Eulália, when much later she went looking for her own self, would fetch memories from that time, because it was the only one in which she had been someone before being Tomé's mother. She remembered being, then, a girl with likings, with wishes, with a voice; she remembered arguing with Aníbal about things, having opinions, wanting things for the life of the two of them. That time lasted little, because Tomé came early, and with Tomé came all the rest, and the girl of likings and wishes went on fading behind the mother it was necessary to be. But she had existed, and it was important that she had existed, because it proved that Eulália had not always been only someone's voice, that there had been an Eulália with a voice of her own, even if now it seemed of another life, even if she could barely remember what it was to have desires that were not for her son.

His name was Aníbal, and he was a good man, and he loved his son in his own way, which was an awkward way, of one who does not know what to do with a boy who does not answer to things the way other boys do. Aníbal tried, at first, to learn Tomé's language, as Eulália had learned it. He would sit with the boy, would strive to make out the sounds, the gestures, but he did not have Eulália's knack, or did not have the patience, or did not have that fierce attention that only the mother seemed to have, and he made mistakes, and the boy grew distressed at his father's mistakes, and the father felt himself a useless thing, and would go to the mother, translate for me what he wants, Eulália, for I don't understand him, and Eulália would translate, and so, slowly, without anyone deciding it, a division of labour came to be created inside that house: the mother understood the son, the father did not, and what the father did not understand he asked of the mother, and what the son wanted to say to the father he said through the mother, and the mother stayed in the middle of the two, the bridge between the father and the son as she was the bridge between the son and the world.

And a bridge, in the middle of a marriage, is a dangerous thing, because the bridge joins two banks but also keeps them apart, and Aníbal came to feel himself, year after year, more and more a bank, more and more outside, more and more a stranger in his own house, where his wife and his son spoke a language he did not speak and where he needed translation to reach his own son. Aníbal watched Eulália and Tomé understand each other with a look, with a sound, with a gesture, and watched himself having to ask what is it, what did he say, and each time he had to ask he felt himself more outside, more useless, more an intruder in that intimacy of two that had no place for a third.

There were signs, which Eulália only knew how to read when it was already late, the way one reads a letter one should have opened in time. There were the times when Aníbal, at supper, tried to tell a thing of his day, and Tomé made a sound, and Eulália left her husband in the middle of his sentence to attend to her son, and Aníbal was left with the sentence hanging, and fell silent, and the next time no longer told it. There were the nights when Aníbal reached his hand toward her in bed and Eulália was so exhausted from a whole day of vigilance that she turned to the other side, and he drew back his hand, and did not reach it out again for weeks. There were the Sundays when he proposed the three of them go out, do a family thing, and Eulália said it was complicated because of Tomé, that the boy came undone away from home, and they stayed, and Aníbal understood, slowly, that his family was two and he a companion, that there was a pair there, mother and son, and that he was what was left over. The signs were all there, but Eulália did not read them, because she had her eyes all on Tomé, and a person who has their eyes all on one thing does not see the rest coming apart around them.

And Eulália, busy being the bridge, did not see her husband draw away. Or she saw it, but had neither the time nor the head to hold him, because all her attention, all of it, went to her son, to the translation, to the vigilance, and what was left over for her husband was little, was the leavings, was the tiredness of the end of the day, and a marriage that lives off the leavings of the attention of one of the two is a marriage that goes on dying out, the way a hearth dies out that no one feeds with wood. Aníbal complained, at first. He said, once or twice, that he felt himself outside, that it seemed she had eyes only for Tomé, that he existed too, that he needed her too. And Eulália, who had no way of giving him what he asked without taking from her son, heard that as a selfish complaint, as a man who was jealous of his own sick son, and answered badly, answered that Tomé needed her more than he did, that a grown man could not be jealous of a boy, that she did what she had to do and he had better pull himself together. Eulália would think much, later, about those answers she had given Aníbal, and understand that they had been unjust, and that the injustice came from a place she only much later knew how to name. When Aníbal said he felt himself outside, that she had eyes only for Tomé, Eulália heard a threat: she heard someone asking her to take attention from her son, and to take attention from her son was, in her head, to put her son in danger, to fail as a mother, and so she defended herself tooth and nail, attacked her husband, called him selfish. But what Aníbal asked was not that she love her son less; it was that there be, in that house, room for more than the son, room for the couple, room for her as a woman, room for him as a father and as a man. And Eulália did not know how to hear that, because for her there was only one way of loving, which was whole, exclusive, total, and one who loves like that does not understand that one can love several things at the same time, thinks that loving one is betraying the others, and it was that incapacity to share out the love, to let it fit in more than one place, that cost her her husband, and that would cost her, afterward, her own self, because a person who knows only how to love in a total and exclusive way ends up giving everything to one and being left with nothing for the rest, including for herself.

And Aníbal fell silent, and his silence went on growing larger, and one day, without great drama, without great scene, Aníbal went away. Not to another woman, not out of malice: he went away because he had ceased to fit, because a man who feels himself outside for years ends up leaving for good, because it is less painful to be outside on purpose than to be outside inside one's own home.

Tomé was then in his years of adolescence, and the father's leaving was, for him, a wound that Eulália only managed to measure later, because Tomé, in his own way, felt his father's abandonment as any son feels it, and perhaps more, because he had no way of speaking of it, of putting it into words, of unburdening it to anyone but his mother. He was left with the wound shut in there, with no way out, and Eulália, the only one who could help him work it through, was herself hurt and busy holding the house together, and did not know, perhaps, how to give her son the space to mourn the father who had gone. And there was here another layer of the same thing: Tomé, dependent on his mother for everything, depended on her even to suffer, even to work through his pains, because it was she who translated the world to him and himself to him, and a son who depends on his mother even to understand what he feels is a son doubly imprisoned, imprisoned without and within, and Eulália, without wishing it, was the key to those two prisons, the outer and the inner, and it would take her time to understand that to free her son was not only to let him speak with the world, it was also to let him have feelings that did not pass through her, an inner life that was his alone, pains and joys that she did not need to translate or to manage.

And Eulália was left alone with Tomé, and the truth, the truth she only admitted to herself many years later, is that her husband's leaving, in the midst of all the sadness, also brought her a relief that she was ashamed to feel: the relief of no longer having to divide her attention, of being able to be wholly her son's, of no longer having a husband complaining that she had eyes only for Tomé, because now she could indeed have eyes only for Tomé, without guilt, with no one claiming their share. Aníbal's leaving freed Eulália to give herself whole to her son, and she gave herself, and in that total giving, with no husband to call her back to herself, she effaced herself faster still, because now there was no one at home to say to her Eulália, and you, what do you want, no longer anyone who saw her as a woman, as a person, as Eulália, there was only Tomé, who saw her as mother, as voice, as bridge, and a woman who is seen only as mother ends up seeing herself only as mother, and that is what happened to Eulália in the years that followed her husband's leaving: she ceased to exist as a woman to exist only as Tomé's mother, and for a long time she did not even notice the lack, because she was too busy being needed to notice that she had ceased to be herself.

Years later, looking at it all with other eyes, Eulália would acknowledge that she had been unjust to Aníbal, and that the injustice had been not letting him in. Because what Aníbal asked, at bottom, when he complained of being outside, was not that she take attention from Tomé; it was that she let him too learn his son's language, that she share the bridge with him instead of keeping it all for herself, that the two of them be translators and not only she. And Eulália had not let him, without noticing it, because being the only one who understood Tomé was her place, her worth, and to share that place with her husband was to give up a part of what made her indispensable. Without thinking it so, without any malice, Eulália had kept her son for herself, had made herself the only one, and in making herself the only one she had pushed her husband out not only of the couple, but of the relationship with his own son, and Aníbal had left home, at bottom, not for not loving his son, but for having given up on reaching him through a mother who, wishing the good of all, let no one cross the bridge alone. It was a guilt Eulália would come to carry, later, when she learned to see: that perhaps her son could have had a father, if she had known how to share his language instead of hoarding it.

Tomé, in his way, felt the lack of his father, and Eulália translated it to him, translated to her son the father's absence as she translated everything, explained it to him in her way, and Tomé wept, in his language, and Eulália consoled him, and the two were left closer still, more each other's, more alone in the world speaking their language between two. The years that followed Aníbal's leaving were, for Eulália, the years of total giving, and they were years she would recall with a strange mixture of longing and of horror. Longing, because they were years of a full intimacy with her son, years in which the two were everything to each other, and there is a sweetness in that which Eulália did not deny. Horror, because they were also the years in which she disappeared for good, in which she let fall the last threads that tied her to a life of her own: the friends she had had, who went on tiring of an Eulália who never could, who never went out, who spoke only of her son; the small likings that still remained to her, and that she went on abandoning one by one for want of time and out of a kind of guilt at having them; her very vanity, the caring for herself, the making herself up, which she went on leaving because what for, for whom, if her life was her son and her son did not notice such things. Year after year, Eulália went on stripping herself of everything that was hers, and each stripping seemed to her, in the moment, an act of love, a proof of a good mother, and was in truth a step down into the well where she would come to find herself, years later, with nothing, with no one beyond her son, with no life to call her own.

And there was an immense sweetness in that union, mother and son against a world that did not understand them, but there was also, beneath the sweetness, a thing that would come to collect its price: that two who are enough for each other close themselves to the rest, and Eulália and Tomé, in being enough for each other, went on closing, she in his voice, he in hers, until they were a single thing with two heads, and a single thing with two heads is a thing that cannot survive the loss of one of them, and it was that, that fragility at the bottom of all that strength, that life would come to show Eulália on the day when one of the two heads, hers, threatened to fail.
