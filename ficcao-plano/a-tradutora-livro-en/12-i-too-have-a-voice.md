## 12. I too have a voice

Time passed, and when Eulália looked back she understood that she had become, without noticing it, a woman she had not known in decades: a woman with a voice of her own, with a life of her own, with a name that had become hers again, Eulália, and not only Tomé's mother.

She had now a life that was hers, alongside the life with her son, and not in place of it. She went out. She had friends, conversations, likings. She sang, sometimes, and now without hoarseness. She cared for herself, she had gone back to making herself up, to looking at herself in the mirror without turning her eyes away, to recognising in that woman of grey hair a person and not only a function. And the most astonishing thing was that none of this took anything from Tomé; on the contrary, Tomé had now a lighter mother, more joyful, less anxious, a mother who did not pour upon him the whole weight of a life unlived, and Tomé breathed better with that new mother, as though he had always felt, without being able to say it, the weight of being the whole life of someone, and as though now, relieved of that weight, he could at last be only a son and not also the sole meaning of his mother's existence. The two of them, mother and son, lived now more broadly, each with their own space, each with their own voice, and the house, which had been cramped from the two of them fitting into one, had now air, had open windows, had more than two people coming in and going out, and it was a house where one lived better, because no one there carried any longer the impossible weight of being everything to the other. And the way she knew she had changed for good was by a sentence, a small sentence she said one day, without thinking, and that was the sentence that summed up everything she had learned.

It was at a meeting of the mothers, one of those gatherings where Eulália had always been the exemplary mother, the one who annulled herself, the one all admired for disappearing behind her son. And the mothers were talking, and there was a younger one, a new mother of a son like Tomé, at the beginning of the path, and that new mother said, with her eyes full of a devotion Eulália recognised at once because it had been her own, said that she was going to dedicate her whole life to her son, that she was going to be everything to him, that she was going to disappear for him, that a good mother is that. And the other mothers nodded, moved, as one nods before a saint. And Eulália, who in other times would have nodded too, who would have been the first to nod, did a thing she had never done: she spoke. She said, with a voice that no longer trembled, with a voice that was hers: no.

And all turned to her, surprised, because Eulália never disagreed, Eulália was the exemplary mother, and Eulália said, slowly, choosing the words, because they were words that had cost her a lifetime to learn: do not efface yourself for him, my girl. I did that. I effaced myself thirty years for my son, I thought it was love, I thought it was being a good mother, and I almost lost myself for good, and I almost imprisoned him forever. Love him, yes, give him your voice when he needs it, but do not give him your whole life, because an effaced mother is not a gift to a son, it is a weight. Go on existing, alongside him. Have your voice, alongside his. Because a day comes when he is going to have to learn to speak for himself, and if you were always his voice he never learns, and if you were only his voice you never find yourself again. The best thing you can do for your son is to be a whole person, with a life of your own, so that he learns, seeing you, that one can exist, and so that the guilt of being your whole life does not weigh upon him. This I know at my own cost. Do not efface yourself. I effaced myself, and it took me a lifetime to come back.

The new mother did not swallow it at once, of course, because these things are not swallowed suddenly, and she answered what Eulália would have answered twenty years before: that it was different, that her son needed much, that she did not mind sacrificing herself, that that was what a mother was for. And Eulália recognised each word, because they had been hers, and did not argue, only said, with gentleness: I used to say that too. And look where I ended up. I am not telling you to love your son less; I am telling you to love yourself too, at the same time, that there is no need to choose, that the idea that you have to choose between yourself and him is a lie they put into our heads and that only does harm to both. Your son does not become better for your effacing yourself; he becomes better for your having a life that fills you, because a full mother has more to give than an empty mother. Think on it. You do not have to change today. But keep what I tell you, for the day when you feel yourself disappearing, for that day comes, and when it comes remember that there was a woman who warned you, and that it was late for her but it may not be for you.

And there was silence in the room, and the mothers looked at Eulália as they had never looked at her, not with the admiration of before, the admiration for the saint who annulled herself, but with another thing, with respect, with a kind of gratitude, because Eulália had just told them a truth no one told them, a truth that went against everything that is expected of a good mother and that was, nonetheless, the deepest of truths about love: that to love is not to disappear, that to love is to exist alongside, that the mother who best loves her son is the one who goes on being a person, with a voice, with a life, with an I. And the new mother, the one at the beginning of the path, stayed looking at Eulália with open eyes, and Eulália saw that she had touched her, that perhaps she had spared her, with those words, thirty years of effacement, and it was that, Eulália understood, the most useful thing she had done with her pain: to transform it into a warning, to pass it on like a seed, so that another mother would not lose herself as she had lost herself.

Eulália noticed, in that spring of her life, that the house had changed its sound. For decades, the house had been silent in a strange way, full of the language between two but empty of any other voice, and now people came into it, conversations came into it, the friends Madalena had brought her came into it, opinions, laughter, discussions about things that were not Tomé came into it. The house, which had been a closed place where mother and son spoke their secret language against the world, had opened to the world, and in that opening the two breathed better, because a house of two who are enough for each other is a house without air, and Eulália had understood, late, that the two of them needed more people, more world, more voices, not only because of the day she would be gone, but to live better already, now, while they were both still here. Tomé flourished in that fuller house, and Eulália flourished, and the language between two, which had been their prison, became one among many ways of communicating, ceased to be the only road and became a dear road among others, and that, far from impoverishing it, freed it: what joined mother and son ceased to be the dependence and became the love, which is a different thing, because dependence binds and love, that, lets one walk.

That night, at home, with Tomé, Eulália thought of the sentence she had said, of the no, of the do not efface yourself, and understood that that sentence, said by her, was the proof of everything that had changed, because it was a whole sentence that began with her, that came from her, that was her voice saying what she thought, not about her son, not for her son, a voice of her own using itself for the first time in decades. She remembered, that night, Reparada, the mother whose son had died and who had been left with nothing, and understood that she had escaped, narrowly, Reparada's fate. Because if she had not changed, if she had gone on being only her son's voice to the end, she would have come, on the day Tomé was gone or on the day Tomé ceased to need her, to the same emptiness as Reparada, to the same not knowing what to be for, to the same old age of empty hands. And she had escaped, not because she had ceased to love her son, but because she had learned to love herself too, to keep for herself a life that did not depend on her son, and that therefore would not crumble when the son changed or was gone. She had, now, a life of her own, and that life of her own was the insurance against the emptiness, was what guaranteed her that, whatever became of Tomé, she would go on having a reason to get up in the morning, because her reason to live was no longer only her son, it was also her own self, her likings, her friends, her recovered voice. And there was a deep justice in this, Eulália understood: that in learning to live for herself beyond her son, she had also become a better mother, because a mother who has a life of her own does not collapse when her son grows, and does not weigh upon the son the threat of collapsing, and stays available to love him with lightness instead of suffocating him with a dependence in reverse, that of the mother who needs the son to have a reason to exist.

And she remembered the Keeper, the line of birth, the Eulália who had existed before being anyone's voice, and understood that she had come back to her, that the girl of the line, the one born promised of easy word, was at last back, old but alive, with her voice hoarse from disuse but working, and that the promise of her name, after all, had been fulfilled: she had become, in the end, a woman of word, a woman who knew how to say herself to the world, only it had taken her a whole lifetime to learn that the first person to whom she had to know how to say herself was her own self.

She thought, that night, of Aníbal, of the husband who had gone for not fitting, and felt pity, a serene pity, without bitterness. She understood, now that she had learned to see, that Aníbal had not been a man jealous of his son, as she had always said to defend herself; he had been a man who had asked for a place in a house where she left no place for anyone but Tomé, and who had gone from despair of not fitting. And she thought that, if she had known then what she knew now, perhaps she could have held him, perhaps she could have had a marriage and a son at the same time, perhaps it would not have been necessary to choose, because the choice she had made, that of giving herself whole to her son and letting the rest fall, had been a false choice, a choice no one had asked of her but her own wrong idea of what it was to be a good mother. It was late for Aníbal, and Eulália knew it, and it was not of that that it was a question; it was of not repeating the mistake with what was left her of life, of not letting fall again everything that was not her son, of learning, at last, that more than one thing fit in the life of a person, that one could be a mother and be a woman and be a friend and be Eulália, all at the same time, and that it had been she, and she alone, who had decided that only one fit, and who had effaced all the others in the name of a love that after all did not ask so much.

It was not a complete victory, and Eulália knew it, because these things are never complete. Tomé still needed her for much, and there were things that were only with her, and there always would be; and Eulália still had relapses, days when the guilt came back, when the old habit of annulling herself called her back, when her own voice seemed to her again a luxury she did not deserve. But the difference, now, and it was a difference of substance, is that Eulália already knew the way back to herself; she already had a voice to use when she lost herself; she already had friends who called her back, likings that anchored her, a name that had become hers again. She was not cured for good, because of these things one is not cured for good; but she had the tools to care for herself, and to have the tools was what made her different from Reparada and from Benvinda, was what guaranteed her that, even if she relapsed, she would not stay fallen, that she would rise again, with her voice, her life, her name. And it was that, more than any perfect state, the true arrival: not a point where everything was resolved forever, but the possession of a way back to herself that she could travel whenever she lost herself, and that she would travel, from then on, as many times as were needed, because to learn to come back to oneself is, after all, a thing one does not do once, one does it a whole lifetime.

And she said, aloud, alone in the kitchen while Tomé slept, rehearsing it like one who rehearses a new language that is after all her mother tongue rediscovered, the sentence that was her arrival, the sentence that summed up the whole journey: I too have a voice. I spent my life being his voice, and it was good, and it made him reach the world. But I too have a voice, I always had, and this, now, this is me using it. The promise of the name, after all, had been fulfilled contrary to how the mother had dreamed it, and had been fulfilled better. Eulália's mother had dreamed a daughter of easy word, who would know how to say herself to the world without effort; and life had given a woman who took fifty-some years to learn to say herself, and who therefore, when she learned at last, knew what few know: the worth of a voice, what it costs to lose it and what it costs to win it back, and so she used it now with a care and a gratitude that no easy word would have. Her voice was not easy; it had been the hardest thing of her life; and it was perhaps for that the most precious, because what is reconquered at a cost is worth more than what one has for free, and Eulália, who had had to reconquer her own voice after losing it entire, knew its worth as no woman of easy word would ever know it.

And there was no one listening, and there was no need for there to be, because the sentence was not for others, it was for her, it was Eulália saying to herself at last that she existed, that she was worth something, that she had a voice that was not her son's nor anyone's, a voice all her own, silent thirty years and at last in use, and that she was going, in the time that remained to her, to use it to say herself to the world and to herself, alongside her son's voice and not in place of it, two voices in the same house, Tomé's and hers, each its own, at last, after a whole lifetime of the two of them fitting into one, which was hers, and which after all had never truly been either of theirs.
