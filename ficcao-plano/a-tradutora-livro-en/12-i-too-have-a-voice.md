## 12. I too have a voice

Time passed, and when Eulália looked back she understood that she had become, without noticing it, a woman she had not known in decades: a woman with a voice of her own, with a life of her own, with a name that had become hers again, Eulália, and not only Tomé's mother.

She had now a life that was hers, alongside the life with her son, and not in place of it. She went out. She had friends, conversations, likings. She sang, sometimes, and now without hoarseness. She looked after herself, had gone back to making herself up, to looking at herself in the mirror of the chest of drawers without turning her eyes away, and the photograph of the girl of the lively eyes was still there, leaning against the mirror, and now, when Eulália looked at herself beside it, they were no longer two different people; it was the same woman, older, with a voice hoarse from disuse but working, returned from a long absence. And the most astonishing thing was that none of this took from Tomé; on the contrary, Tomé had now a lighter mother, less anxious, a mother who did not pour over him the whole weight of a life unlived, and Tomé breathed better with that new mother.

And the way she knew that she had changed for good was by a sentence, a small sentence she said one day, without thinking.

It was at a meeting of the mothers, in that room behind the church where Eulália had always been the exemplary mother, the one who annulled herself, the one whom all admired for disappearing behind her son. And there was, that time, a new mother, a young woman, Felismina, mother of a boy like Tomé had been when little, at the beginning of the road. And Felismina spoke, with eyes full of a devotion that Eulália recognised at once because it had been hers, said that she was going to dedicate her whole life to her son, that she was going to be everything to him, that she had already left her job, that she had left her friends, that she had left everything, because a good mother is that, it is to disappear for the child. And the other mothers nodded, moved, as one nods before a saint. And Benvinda, at the back, with the shadows of always under her eyes, nodded more than all of them.

And Eulália, who in other times would have nodded too, who would have been the first to nod, did a thing she had never done: she spoke. She said, with a voice that no longer trembled, with a voice that was hers: no.

And they all turned to her, surprised, because Eulália never disagreed. And Eulália said, slowly, choosing the words, because they were words that had cost her a lifetime to learn: do not efface yourself for him, my girl. I did that. I effaced myself for thirty years for my son, I thought it was love, I thought it was being a good mother, and I nearly lost myself for good, and I nearly imprisoned him forever. I left my husband, I left my friends, I left singing, I left everything, even my name, that everyone in this village forgot that I am called Eulália. Love him, yes, give him your voice when he needs it, but do not give him your whole life, because an effaced mother is not a gift to a child, it is a weight. Go on existing, alongside him. Have your voice, alongside his. Because a day comes when he is going to have to learn to speak for himself, and if you were always his voice he never learns, and if you were only his voice you never find yourself again. This I know to my cost. I effaced myself, and it took me a lifetime to come back.

Felismina did not swallow it at once, of course, because these things are not swallowed suddenly, and she answered what Eulália would have answered twenty years before: that it was different, that her son needed a great deal, that she did not mind sacrificing herself, that that was what a mother was for. And Eulália recognised each word, because they had been hers, and did not argue, only said, with gentleness: I used to say that too, Felismina. And look where I ended up. I am not telling you to love your son less; I am telling you to love yourself too, at the same time, that it is not necessary to choose. The idea that you have to choose between yourself and him is a lie they put in our heads and that only does harm to both. Your son does not get better for your effacing yourself; he gets better for your having a life that fills you, because a full mother has more to give than an empty one. You do not have to change today. But keep what I tell you, for the day you feel yourself disappearing, for that day comes, and when it comes remember that there was a woman who warned you, and that it was late for her but it may not be for you.

And there was a silence in the room, and the mothers looked at Eulália as they had never looked at her, not with the admiration of before, the admiration for the saint who annulled herself, but with something else, with respect, with a kind of gratitude. And Reparada, at the back, in her place of always, with the cold cup in her hands, raised her still eyes, and looked at Eulália, and said only, low, but that the whole room heard: would that someone had said that to me. And she set down the cup. And Felismina, the one at the beginning of the road, stayed looking at Eulália with her eyes open, and Eulália saw that she had touched her, that perhaps she had spared her, with those words, thirty years of effacement, and it was that, Eulália understood, the most useful thing she had done with her pain: to turn it into a warning, to pass it on like a seed.

There was a Sunday, in that time, that Eulália kept as a portrait of all that had changed. Madalena came, and Olímpia came, and one of the women from the husking came, and even Conceição, on whose door Eulália had at last gone to knock, with something to take that time, and Eulália said to her at the door, I have come late, Conceição, but I have come, and Conceição, who was good, only said, better late, woman, come on in. And they were all in the kitchen, and there was coffee and the sponge cake Olímpia had brought, and there was talk of everything, of the new fair in the upper square, that after all had been held and was as lively as Eulália had said it would be, of a wedding, of a recipe, and there was laughter. And Tomé was in the sitting room, with the fado playing, and at a certain point he got up and came to the kitchen, drawn by the noise, and stayed at the door looking at that table full of women laughing, and made a sound. And Eulália, who in another time would have dropped everything to attend to the sound, that time only looked at him and understood, and the sound was not of distress, it was of another thing, it was of contentment, and Tomé came in, and Olímpia pulled out a chair for him, come, Tomé, sit here, and Conceição cut him a slice of sponge cake, and Tomé sat at the table, in the midst of those women, and ate the sponge cake, and beat his hand on his leg, out of contentment, and the house, that Sunday, was full of people and of voices, and Tomé was no longer the mute son hidden in the sitting room, he was a man sitting at the table among people who knew him, and Eulália, from the other side of the table, looked at her son there, at ease, in the midst of the world she had for thirty years closed to him, and thought that no paper of Quintela's, no guardianship, no institution would give her son what that full table gave him: people who would stay, people who would hold him, people for whom he was Tomé.

The house of the two of them, in that time, had changed its sound. For decades it had been silent in a strange way, full of the language of two but empty of any other voice, and now people came into it, conversations came into it, Olímpia and Madalena and the friends came into it, laughter came into it, arguments about things that were not Tomé. The house, which had been a closed place where mother and son spoke their secret language against the world, had opened to the world, and in that opening the two of them breathed better, because a house of two who are enough for each other is a house without air. And the language of two, which had been their prison, became one among many ways of communicating, ceased to be the only road and became a beloved road among others, and that, far from impoverishing it, freed it: what united mother and son ceased to be dependence and became love, which is a different thing, because dependence binds and love, that, lets one walk.

She thought, one night, of Aníbal, of the husband who had gone for not fitting, and felt pity, a serene pity, without bitterness. She understood, now that she had learned to see, that Aníbal had not been a man jealous of his son, as she had always said to defend herself; he had been a man who asked for a place in a house where she left no place for anyone besides Tomé, and who had gone out of despair at not fitting. She remembered the Saturday of the cart, of Aníbal sitting on the floor making the cart run, and thought that, if she had known then what she knew now, perhaps she would have let the Saturday of the cart be the first of many, instead of the last; perhaps she could have had a marriage and a son at the same time, perhaps it had not been necessary to choose. It was late for Aníbal, and Eulália knew it, and it was not of that it was a matter; it was of not repeating the error with what was left of her life, of learning, at last, that more than one thing fits in the life of a person, and that it had been she, and she alone, who had decided that only one fit.

And she remembered the Keeper, of the line of birth written more than half a century ago, of the Eulália who had existed before being anyone's voice, and understood that she had come back to her, that the girl of the line, the one who had been born promised of easy word, was at last back, old but alive. And she went to the dresser drawer, and took out the sheet, the one of the crossed-out I and Madalena's question and the small list she had begun to fill, and read what was there, to sing, to sit in the sun, to say what I think, and took up the pencil, and added, at the end, in a firmer hand than before, a line that was the sum of everything: to be Eulália alongside being a mother. And she folded the sheet, but that time she did not put it beneath the receipts; she stood it upright, leaning against the photograph of the girl, on top of the chest of drawers, beside the mirror, where she could see it every morning.

It was not a complete victory, and Eulália knew it, because these things are never complete. Tomé still needed her for much, and there were things that were only with her, and there would always be; and Eulália still had relapses, days when the guilt came back, when the old habit of annulling herself called her back. But the difference, now, and it was a difference at bottom, is that Eulália already knew the road back to herself; she already had a voice to use when she lost herself; she already had friends who called her back, likings that anchored her, a name that had become hers again. She was not cured for good, because of these things one is not cured for good; but she had the tools to look after herself, and it was that which made her different from Reparada and from Benvinda, it was what assured her that, even if she relapsed, she would not stay fallen.

And she said, one night, aloud, alone in the kitchen while Tomé slept, rehearsing it as one rehearses a new language that is after all one's mother tongue found again, the sentence that was her arrival, the sentence that summed up the whole journey: I too have a voice. I spent my life being his voice, and it was good, and it made him reach the world. But I too have a voice, I always had, and this, now, this is I using it. The promise of the name, after all, had been fulfilled contrary to how the mother had dreamed it, and had been fulfilled better. Eulália's mother had dreamed of a daughter of easy word, who would know how to say herself to the world without effort; and life had given a woman who took fifty-and-some years to learn to say herself, and who therefore, when at last she learned, knew what few know: the worth of a voice, what it costs to lose it and what it costs to win it back. Her voice was not easy; it had been the hardest thing of her life; and it was perhaps for that the most precious, because what is reconquered at cost is worth more than what one has for free.

And there was no one listening, and there was no need that there be, because the sentence was not for others, it was for her, it was Eulália saying to herself at last that she existed, that she was worth something, that she had a voice that was not her son's nor anyone's, a voice all her own, silent for thirty years and at last in use, and that she was going, in the time left to her, to use it to say herself to the world and to herself, alongside her son's voice and not in place of it, two voices in the same house, Tomé's and hers, each its own, at last, after a whole life of the two of them fitting into one, which was hers, and which after all had never been, truly, either of theirs.
