## 8. The silence of Eulália

It took her time to decide, because the idea went against everything she was. For weeks, after the House of the Register, Eulália went about with the Keeper's word working in her, arguing with herself, thinking yes, that she had to let go, and right after thinking no, that it was madness, that her son would suffer, that no one would understand him, that she would be a bad mother to abandon him to the world. She weighed the pros and the cons, and the cons always seemed to her the greater, because the cons were concrete and immediate — the son distressed, the world not understanding — and the pros were abstract and future — the son prepared for when she was gone. And it is always easier to see the suffering of now than the good of later, and Eulália was nearly giving up many times, concluding that it was better to go on as always, to be the bridge, to translate, and that she would worry about the future when it came. But the image of the dawn always came back to her, the son trapped at home with the dead mother unable to cry out, and that image was stronger than all the cons, and it was that, in the end, that decided Eulália to do the impossible, to go against her deepest instinct, to attempt the thing that seemed to her a betrayal and that was, after all, the greater love.

The first thing Eulália attempted, after the House of the Register, was the hardest of all, and it was to fall silent. To fall silent on purpose, to cease translating her son, to leave a silence where her voice had always been, so that the world, before that silence, would at last have to learn to hear Tomé. And it was one of the most painful things she did in her life, because the silence of a mother who has always translated is no rest, it is a torture, it is to be watching her son in distress and not attend to him, it is to bite her tongue with all her strength when her whole body cries out to speak.

She began at the market, in a small test. They went to buy fruit, and the seller asked Tomé what he wanted, and Tomé made his sound, his gesture for the fruit he wanted, and Eulália, who in a second would have said he wants some apples, those red ones, fell silent. She fell silent and looked away, pretending to be distracted, leaving Tomé and the seller alone with the problem of understanding each other. Those seconds, in which Eulália fell silent and looked away, were among the longest of her life, because every fibre of her wanted to translate, wanted to end the small discomfort, wanted to be the bridge as she had always been, and to fall silent was to go against an instinct of decades, was like holding her breath until it hurt.

And it was a torment. The seller did not understand, stood waiting, looked at Eulália in search of the usual translation, and Eulália held herself silent, with her heart leaping, with the mad desire to attend to him, and Tomé, seeing that his mother did not translate, made the gesture again, more slowly, pointing, and the seller, forced to try because the mother did not speak, looked at where he pointed, and ventured, these? the red ones?, and Tomé made the sound of yes, his sound of contentment, and the seller understood, understood alone, for the first time, and gave him the apples, and smiled, pleased with herself for having understood. And Eulália, at her side, silent, with tears in her eyes that she hid, saw a thing that broke and filled her heart at the same time: she saw that the world was capable of understanding Tomé, if she let it, if she fell silent enough for the world to have to try.

She came home from that first market still trembling, and sat down, and tried to understand what she felt, and it was a mixture of things she had never felt together. She felt pride, in her son and in herself; she felt fear, that the next time it would not go well; and she felt, beneath everything, a thing that shamed her: she felt a loss. Because while the seller understood Tomé alone, Eulália had been there at her side, silent, dispensable, and to be dispensable, for a woman who had made of her indispensability her whole life, was a small death. Eulália understood, that afternoon, that to let her son go was going to cost her not only the distress of seeing him suffer difficulties, but also the mourning of ceasing to be necessary, and that that mourning was perhaps the hardest of all, because it went straight to the bone of her identity: if I am not the only one who understands him, who am I? And the question stayed in the air, unanswered, because the answer Eulália did not yet have, the answer was an Eulália she had not yet found again, and there was a frightening gap between ceasing to be her son's voice and having her own again, a gap in which she was, for the time being, no one.

But it was not always so, and Eulália learned quickly that to let go is not a clean victory, it is a stumbling path, full of retreats. There were times when she fell silent and the world did not understand, and Tomé was distressed, frustrated, and Eulália had to bear her son's distress without attending to him, and it was worse than the torment of the market, it was almost unbearable, to see her son suffer for not being understood and not run to understand him, and more than once Eulália could not bear it and translated all the same, retreated, went back to being the bridge, because a mother's love is stronger than any plan and to see a son suffer undoes the best of intentions. And then she felt guilty for having retreated, and guilty for when she did not retreat, caught between two guilts, that of leaving him helpless and that of imprisoning him, without knowing which was the good one, because both hurt and both seemed, according to the moment, the greater betrayal.

The hardest thing of all, in those first times of silence, was to learn to bear Tomé's distress without resolving it. Because there were moments when her son, in not being understood, grew frustrated, and the frustration grew, and made his sounds more and more distressed, and Eulália, who in an instant would have translated and ended the distress, had to stay still, watching him struggle, with every fibre of her body crying out to her to attend to him. It was against nature, that. A mother is made to attend to her distressed son, it is the deepest of instincts, and Eulália had to go against her deepest instinct, had to bear her son's distress trusting that from it a new capacity would be born, and it was a faith hard to have in the midst of Tomé's distressed sounds. There were times when she drew away, when she left the room so as not to attend to him, because she knew that if she stayed she would attend to him, and she stayed on the other side of the door listening to her son struggle, with tears running down her face, saying to herself it is for his good, it is for his good, without any certainty that it was, with only the Keeper's word and a trembling hope. And when Tomé, at the end of the struggle, managed it, when he understood himself at last with whoever it was, and the distress passed and there came the joy of having managed it, Eulália understood that it had been worth it, that the distress had been the price of growing; but in the moment, in the midst of her son's distressed sounds, none of this consoled her, and each one of those moments cost her years of life, because there is nothing that costs a mother more than to see her son suffer and to choose, out of love, not to save him.

And there was the fear that her son would not understand what she was doing, that Tomé, seeing his mother fall silent where she had always spoken, would think she was abandoning him, that she had ceased to love him, that she had grown tired of him. That fear tortured Eulália, because how was she to explain to her son, in his language, a thing as hard as I am falling silent because I love you, I am drawing away so that you grow, I am letting you suffer difficulties so that you learn to live without me, how does one translate this to one who speaks another language, how does one say to a son that the mother's silence, this time, is not unlove, it is the hardest love there is. And Eulália tried, in her way, tried to show Tomé with gestures, with her presence, that she was still there, that she still loved him, that the silence was not against him, and she did not know if he understood, at first, and that hurt her more than anything, the idea that her son might interpret her greatest gesture of love as an abandonment.

There was a scene, in the midst of these first times, that nearly made her give up everything. It was in a public place, with people around, and Tomé tried to ask a thing of a person who did not know him, and the person did not understand, and grew impatient, and treated Tomé as though he were simple, with that voice one uses with children or with people one thinks do not understand, and Tomé felt the contempt, because he felt everything, and was distressed, and humiliated, and Eulália, at his side, silent on principle, saw her son being humiliated and had to hold herself with all her strength not to leap to his defence. And she asked herself, in that moment, whether it was worth it, whether that letting-go was not after all a cruelty, whether she was not exposing her son to humiliations she had always spared him, and she nearly decided that it was not, that she was going to go back to being the bridge, that the protected son was better than the humiliated son. But then, at home, thinking it through coldly, she understood a thing: that she was not always going to be there to protect him from humiliations, that one day she would be gone, and that if her son never learned to deal with a world that would sometimes treat him badly, he would be left, when she was gone, with no defences at all. To protect him from every humiliation was to prepare him for a world that does not exist, a world where the mother is always present; and the real world, the one that awaited him after her, had humiliations, and Tomé had to learn to cross them, with his head high, in his way, and that he would only learn if she let him, hurting her as much as it hurt him, or more.

But Tomé, who understood everything, understood this too, faster than Eulália expected, and understood it in a way that moved the mother. Because Tomé, seeing that his mother fell silent and let him understand himself with others, began, slowly, to gain a thing he had never had: confidence. He began to understand that he was capable, that he managed to make himself understood by others besides his mother, that he had more ways of reaching the world than those that his mother, in always translating him, had never let him discover. And there was a day when Tomé, alone, without his mother, managed to ask a thing of a neighbour and be understood, and he came home with a joy on his face that Eulália had never seen, the joy of one who has discovered himself capable, and Eulália understood, seeing that joy, that in falling silent she was not taking anything from her son, she was giving him a thing that she, with all her love, had never given him: the chance to discover that he could. That he was more than his mother had let him be. That he had, beneath the dependence in which she had raised him, a capacity to live that only waited for room to grow.

There was a day, in the midst of this learning, that stayed with Eulália as a joy that confirmed her in the path. Tomé had gone with a neighbour, the trusted one to whom Eulália had begun to teach a little of his language, to run an errand, and he was late, and Eulália stayed at home in a small fretting, looking out the window, imagining a thousand disasters, the son distressed, no one understanding him, the neighbour despairing. And when the two came back, they came back laughing. They came laughing, the two of them, at a thing that had happened on the way, a small thing, and the neighbour told Eulália, still laughing, that they had understood each other beautifully, that Tomé had explained a thing to her by gestures and that she had understood, and that they had laughed together, the two of them, at the way she at first did not understand and he insisted with patience. And Eulália saw her son laughing with another person, sharing with another person a moment of complicity that until then he had had only with her, and instead of the jealousy she feared to feel, she felt a deep joy, the joy of seeing that her son could have complicity with others besides her, that Tomé's world, until then closed to two, was opening to more people, and that each person who entered that world made it less solitary, less dependent, safer for the future. And she understood, in that laughter shared between her son and the neighbour, that to let go did not empty Tomé's life, it filled it, gave it more people, more bonds, more world, and that she, in opening her hand, was not losing her son, she was giving him the whole world instead of only her.

And it was in seeing her son grow in that new space that Eulália began to make her peace with the silence, and to understand that the Keeper was right, that to let go was not to abandon, that her silence, far from leaving her son helpless, was freeing him from a dependence that imprisoned him, and that love, sometimes, is not to do for the other, it is to let the other do, it is to bear the distress of seeing them try and fail and try again, because it is in that trying that they grow, and a mother who does everything for her son robs him, without wishing it, of the growing. Eulália had spent her life doing for Tomé, with the best of loves, and only now, old, did she learn that there was a love greater than doing for her son, and that it was to let him do, and that that greater love was, precisely, the hardest to give, because it went against all the instincts of a mother, against everything she had always taken for caring. But it was the love Tomé needed, now, for the rest of his life, and it was the love that prepared him for the day she was gone, and Eulália, with her heart in pieces and full of hope at the same time, went on learning to give it, in silence, gesture by gesture, silence by silence, watching her son grow toward a world that at last, slowly, learned to hear him.
