## 8. The silence of Eulália

It took her time to decide, because the idea went against everything she was. For weeks, after the House of the Register, Eulália went about with the Keeper's word working in her, thinking yes, that she had to let go, and right after thinking no, that it was madness, that her son would suffer. The cons always seemed to her the greater, because they were concrete and immediate, the son distressed, the world not understanding, and the pros were abstract and future, the son prepared for when she was gone. And it is always easier to see the suffering of now than the good of later. But the image of the dawn always came back to her, the son making her the sound of good morning to a body that did not answer, and that image was stronger than all the cons, and it was that, in the end, that decided Eulália to do the impossible.

The first thing she tried was the hardest of all, and it was to fall silent. To fall silent on purpose, to stop translating her son, to leave a silence where her voice had always been, so that the world, faced with that silence, would have at last to learn to hear Tomé. And it was one of the most painful things she did in her life, because the silence of a mother who has always translated is no rest at all, it is a torture, it is to see her son distressed and not come to his aid, it is to bite her tongue with all her strength when her whole body cries out to speak.

She began at the market, in a small test. They went to buy fruit, at Idalina's stall, and Idalina asked Tomé what he wanted, and Tomé made his sound and his gesture, pointed to the apples, the red ones, and Eulália, who in a second would have said he wants those red ones, the ones on top, fell silent. She fell silent and looked away, toward some stall or other, pretending to be distracted, leaving Tomé and Idalina alone with the problem of understanding each other. Those seconds were among the longest of her life, because every fibre of her wanted to translate, wanted to put an end to the small discomfort, and to fall silent was to go against an instinct of decades, was like holding her breath until it hurt.

Idalina did not understand, she waited, looked at Eulália in search of the usual translation, but Eulália had turned away, and was pretending interest in a bunch of parsley she did not want. And Idalina, obliged to try because the mother did not speak, looked to where Tomé was pointing, and ventured. These, Tomé? The red ones? And Tomé made the sound of yes, his sound of contentment. And Idalina, still in doubt, pointed to the ones on top, or these, the ones below, and Tomé beat once on his leg, with his open hand, and pointed again upward, and Idalina understood, understood on her own, for the first time in thirty years, and put three of the apples from on top in his bag, and said, pleased with herself, so that is how you want them, Tomé, it is only a matter of patience. And Eulália, beside her, with tears in her eyes that she hid by turning more toward the parsley, saw a thing that broke and filled her heart at the same time: that the world was able to understand Tomé, if she let it, if she fell silent enough for the world to have to try.

She went home from that first market still trembling, and sat down, with the bag of apples on the table, and tried to understand what she felt, and it was a mixture of things she had never felt together. Pride, in her son and in herself. Fear, that the next time it would not go well. And, beneath everything, a thing that shamed her: a loss. Because while Idalina understood Tomé on her own, Eulália had been there beside her, silent, dispensable, and to be dispensable, for a woman who had made her indispensability her whole life, was a small death. She understood, that afternoon, with the apples before her, that to let her son go would cost her not only the distress of seeing him pass through difficulties, but also the mourning of ceasing to be necessary, and that that mourning went straight to the bone of her identity: if I am not the only one who understands him, who am I? And the question stayed in the air, without an answer, because the answer was an Eulália she had not yet found again.

But it was not always so, and Eulália learned quickly that to let go is not a clean victory, it is a road by stumbles. There was a scene, in the midst of those first times, that nearly made her give up everything. It was at the door of the pharmacy, with people around, and Tomé wanted to ask a thing of a man who did not know him, a stranger who was waiting for a lift, and he made his gesture, and the sound, and the man did not understand, and grew impatient, and said, loudly, with that voice one uses with children or with people one thinks do not understand: oh friend, I do not know what you want, speak properly. And he looked around, in search of someone to explain him, and gave a small laugh, of mockery, to an acquaintance beside him. And Tomé felt the contempt, because he felt everything, and grew distressed, and humiliated, and lowered his eyes, and Eulália, beside him, silent on principle, saw her son humiliated and had to drive her nails into her palm so as not to leap to his defence, so as not to say to the man, do you know who you are speaking to, this man understands him better than you, and shame on you, laughing at one who speaks in another way. The words were in her mouth, ready, sharpened. And she did not say them. She took Tomé's arm and led him from there, and within she was undone.

And she asked herself, that night, at home, whether it was worth it, whether that letting-go was not after all a cruelty, whether she was not exposing her son to humiliations she had always spared him. She almost decided that it was not, that she would go back to being the bridge, that the son protected was better than the son humiliated. But then, thinking it through coldly, she understood a thing: that she was not going to be always there to protect him from humiliations, that one day she would be gone, and that if her son never learned to deal with a world that would sometimes treat him ill, he would be left, when she was gone, with no defences at all. To protect him from all humiliation was to prepare him for a world that does not exist, a world where the mother is always present; and the real world, the one that awaited him after her, had men at the door of the pharmacy, and Tomé had to learn to cross them, with his head high, in his way.

And there was a night, after a day like that, when Eulália nearly let everything go to ruin for not bearing the silence. They had gone to the apothecary, and Tomé had wanted to explain to the apothecary a pain he had, and the apothecary, in a hurry, with a queue behind, had not the patience, and dispatched them with something that was not what Tomé asked for, and Eulália fell silent, and they left with the wrong bottle, and at home Tomé, frustrated, made his distressed sounds the whole night, pointing to the place of the pain, looking at the mother with eyes that said you knew, why did you not say. And Eulália, sitting at the table, with the wrong bottle before her, held herself with both hands to the edge of the table so as not to get up and run back to the apothecary to tell the man what Tomé wanted. And she did not run. She held herself. And it was the longest night of that time, to see her son distressed and with the pain untended and to know that with one sentence of hers everything was resolved, and not to say the sentence. She got up in the middle of the night, went to Tomé's room, sat on the edge of his bed, and made him, in the language of the two of them, with gestures and with her voice, a thing that was the hardest thing in the world to translate: that she knew, that she understood his pain, but that the next time it had to be he who made the apothecary understand, slowly, with patience, and that she would be there, beside him, but silent, not because she did not love him, but because she did. And she did not know whether he understood. Tomé looked at her a long time, in the dark, and then closed his eyes, and Eulália stayed there, on the edge of the bed, until he fell asleep, asking herself whether she had just made the greatest gesture of love of her life or the greatest cruelty, and not knowing, that night, which of the two.

And there was the fear that her son would not understand what she was doing, that Tomé, on seeing the mother fall silent where she had always spoken, would think she was abandoning him, that she had tired of him. How was she to explain to him, in his language, a thing as difficult as I am falling silent because I love you, I am drawing away so that you may grow. She tried, in her way, to show him with gestures, with her presence, that she was still there, that the silence was not against him. And she did not know, at first, whether he understood, and that hurt her more than anything.

And it was to a neighbour that Eulália went to ask for the first hand. Her name was Olímpia, she lived two doors down, was a widow, with no children, and had time and had patience, and had always liked Tomé, with that liking of one who is not afraid of what is different. And Eulália, one day, knocked at her door and said to her a thing that cost her to say: Olímpia, I would like to ask you a thing I have never asked anyone. I would like to teach you a little of my son's language. For the case of one day my being gone. And Olímpia, who was a straight woman, asked no questions, only said, then teach me, Eulália, and I will learn what I can. And they began, of an evening, the three of them, Eulália explaining, this sound means this, this gesture that, when he does this with the open hand it is distress, give him time, look to where he points, and Tomé, at first mistrustful that the mother was telling their secrets to another, went on understanding that it was not betrayal, it was the opening of a door, and went on loosening, and laughed when Olímpia got it wrong, and corrected her, in his way, with patience.

And there was a day, in the midst of this learning, that stayed with Eulália as a joy that confirmed her in the road. Tomé went with Olímpia to run an errand, to take some eggs to the house of an acquaintance, and was long, and Eulália stayed at home in a fine fret, looking out the window, imagining a thousand disasters, the son distressed, no one understanding him, Olímpia despairing. And when the two of them came back, they came back laughing. They came laughing the two of them at a thing that had happened on the way, a small thing, and Olímpia told Eulália, still laughing, that they had understood each other beautifully, that Tomé had wanted to show her a nest in a wall and that at first she did not understand what he was pointing at, and he insisted, and she looking at the wrong place, until she saw the nest, and that the two of them had laughed at her foolishness. And Eulália saw her son laughing with another person, sharing with another person a moment of complicity that until then he had had only with her, and instead of the jealousy she feared to feel, she felt a deep joy, that of seeing that her son could have complicity with others besides her, that Tomé's world, until then closed to two, was opening to more people.

But Tomé, who understood everything, understood too what the mother was doing, sooner than Eulália expected, and understood it in a way that moved her. Because Tomé, on seeing that the mother fell silent and let him understand himself with others, began, slowly, to gain a thing he had never had: confidence. There was a day when he came back from an errand at the grocer's alone, without even Olímpia, and came into the house with a joy in his face that Eulália had never seen in him, the joy of one who has discovered himself capable, and put on the table what he had gone to buy, and beat his hand on his knee, out of contentment, and made the mother the sound of tell me, and told her, in his way, with gestures, that the man at the grocer's had understood him. And Eulália understood, seeing that joy, that in falling silent she was not taking anything from her son, she was giving him a thing she, with all her love, had never given him: the chance to discover that he could.

And it was in seeing her son grow in that new space that Eulália began to make her peace with the silence, and to understand that the Keeper was right, that to let go was not to abandon. Her silence, far from leaving her son helpless, freed him from a dependence that imprisoned him, and love, she understood, sometimes is not to do for the other, it is to let the other do, it is to bear the distress of seeing him try and fail and try again, because it is in that trying that he grows. Eulália had spent her life doing for Tomé, with the best of loves, and only now, old, did she learn that there was a love greater than doing for her son, and that it was to let him do, and that that greater love was, precisely, the hardest to give. But it was the love Tomé needed, now, for the rest of his life, and Eulália, with her heart in pieces and full of hope at the same time, went on learning to give it, silent, gesture by gesture, silence by silence, seeing her son grow toward a world that at last, slowly, learned to hear him.

What she did not foresee was that the son, in needing less, would open within her an emptiness for which there was no name, and that that emptiness would lead her to a bottom of which not even the faint had been warning.
