## 5. The voice that was not hers

Madalena's question worked in Eulália for weeks, slowly, and led her to a discovery that changed the way she saw herself, and that was, at the same time, freeing and cruel: the discovery that the voice she used the whole day was not her own.

This discovery did not come all at once, it came little by little, the way the discoveries come that hurt, that the head keeps putting off because it knows they will disturb everything. It came in small moments, across weeks, in which Eulália caught herself noticing things she had never noticed. She noticed, for example, that when someone asked her how she was, she answered for Tomé: oh, Tomé has been so and so. The question was about her, and the answer was about her son, automatically, without her thinking, as though Eulália had no state of her own to report, as though the only thing that happened in her life were what happened with her son. She noticed that she did not know how to answer how are you without speaking of Tomé, that the question left her stranded, because she herself, Eulália, had no news, had no state, had no life that was not her son's. And each one of these small notes went on adding up, and it was that sum, more than any great revelation, that went on leading her to the deep discovery: that she had ceased to have an I to report, that, asked about herself, she only knew how to answer he.

She noticed this one day, without meaning to, in hearing herself. They were at the market, she and Tomé, and a neighbour came up and asked Tomé how he was, and Tomé made his sound, and Eulália, out of habit, translated, he is well, thank you, he was poorly but he is better now. And the neighbour went off, and Eulália stayed thinking of a thing she had never thought: that everything she said in the world was Tomé. That her voice, Eulália's voice, the sound that came out of her mouth the whole day, never said Eulália, always said Tomé. He is well. He wants this. He doesn't like that. He understood. He is saying that. The mouth was hers, the voice was hers, but the words were all his, her whole life spent lending her voice to say another, and never, or almost never, using her voice to say herself.

And she thought then of a thing deeper still, that Madalena, without knowing it, had opened to her. She thought: this voice I use for Tomé, is it his or is it mine? And she understood that it was a question with two answers, and that both hurt. Because the voice was hers, was her body, was Eulália speaking; but what she carried in that voice, what she said, was Tomé's, was what Tomé wanted to say, and Eulália went about carrying, in her own mouth, a speech that was not hers, went about carrying her son's voice the way one carries a weight that is not ours, and the question of the shelf, the question Eulália had never heard but that governed her life, was exactly that: what am I carrying that is not mine? And the answer, for Eulália, was clear as water: she carried her son's voice. She carried it with love, carried it willingly, but she carried a thing that was not hers, that was Tomé's, and in carrying it she had ceased to carry her own, had let fall along the way the only voice that was truly hers, Eulália's voice, and now, when she looked for it, she found only her son's, installed where hers ought to be.

And there was an extra weight to this borrowed voice, which was the weight of responsibility. Because when one speaks for another, each word counts double: if Eulália translated wrong, if she put into Tomé's mouth a thing he did not want to say, she could decide his life wrong, as had almost happened in the consultation of his childhood. And Eulália lived with that weight, that of knowing that his voice passed through her and that an error of hers was an error in her son's life, and that enormous responsibility made her weigh each word, doubt each translation, is this really what he wants, am I saying right what he feels, am I not putting into his mouth what I think and not what he wants. It was a terrible weight, that of being another person's voice, that of carrying the responsibility of representing them without betraying them, and Eulália had carried it for decades, and it was also for that that she was so worn, because to speak for oneself is light, a person says what they please and bears it, but to speak for another is heavy beyond words, it is to have always to guess, always to get it right, never to betray, and no one bears that weight a whole lifetime without wearing down to the bone.

But there was a second part to this discovery, and it was the hardest to swallow, because it called into question not what Eulália had lost, but what she, without wishing it, had taken from her son. Because in being Tomé's voice, in translating him so well, in being always there making the bridge, Eulália had prevented, throughout her son's whole life, the world from learning to hear him. The neighbour at the market had never learned Tomé's language because there was always Eulália to translate; the neighbour, the baker, the man at the parish office, no one in Véspera had ever learned a word of Tomé's language, because they did not need to, because Eulália knew it for everyone, and Eulália was always there. And so, without wishing it, with the greatest love in the world, Eulália had become the reason her son, at the end of a lifetime, went on being mute to everyone but her. It was not Tomé's language that had been left unlearned; it was Eulália who had learned it so well that she excused the world from learning it, and in excusing it she had bound her son to her, bound him to a single bridge, made of him a man who could only reach the world by one road, and that road was the mother, and what happens to a man whose only road to the world is his mother, on the day the mother fails, was a thing Eulália had never let herself think, because it was a thing too terrible to think.

There was an afternoon when Eulália made an experiment that frightened her. She sat down with a paper and a pen, and decided to write a list of things that she wanted, only she, for herself. Not for Tomé, not for the house, not out of duty: things that Eulália, the woman, wanted for her own life. And she sat looking at the blank paper, with the pen in her hand, and wrote nothing, because she had nothing to write, because she did not know what she wanted, because it was so long since she had asked herself what she wanted that the question sounded to her in a foreign language. She tried. She thought: I want... and stayed there, suspended in the I want, waiting for an end to the sentence that did not come. Want what? She did not know. She scratched out the paper, in anger at herself, and the blank sheet went on hurting her like a mirror, because it was exactly the portrait of what she was within: a blank sheet in the place where a person ought to be, an empty space where others had desires, plans, likings, a life. And Eulália understood, with the blank sheet in her hand, that the problem was not only having lost her voice; it was that, in losing her voice, she had also lost that of which the voice speaks, had lost the content and not only the form, had lost not only the way of saying what she wanted but the very wanting, and that to rebuild herself would be, therefore, much more than to speak again: it would be to have again something to say, to want again, to be again a person with a within, and not only a mouth lent to another.

And she began to think it, now, against her will. She began to look at her son, a grown man, and to ask herself: and if I die? And if I fall ill? And if one day I cannot be here to translate? What happens to Tomé, who has fifty years of world and whom no one in the world knows how to hear but me? And the answer was an abyss, was her son left mute from one day to the next, isolated, at the mercy of those who did not understand him, exactly as in that consultation of his childhood, but that time with no mother to save him, because the mother would be, precisely, the one who had failed. And Eulália understood, with a shiver that ran down her spine, that her devotion, her total giving, her love without limit, had built for her son the most dangerous of prisons: a life that depended entirely on her, and therefore a life that would crumble on the day she crumbled. She had loved her son in a way that had made him incapable of living without her, and that, which she had always taken for love, began now to seem to her, with horror, a form of having imprisoned him.

She tried, once, to speak of this with Benvinda, the mother who had clung until she disappeared, perhaps looking for someone who would understand her, and the conversation only showed her the size of the well. She said to Benvinda, fearfully, that she had been thinking she had lost herself, that she no longer knew what she wanted, that she was only her son's voice. And Benvinda, instead of understanding her, looked at her without understanding, and said: but Eulália, that is exactly what it is to be a mother, a mother is that, it is to have nothing else, it is to be her son's, and a good thing too, and would that all children had a mother like that. And Eulália understood, hearing Benvinda, that she was speaking with someone who was deeper still in the well than she, that Benvinda had not only lost herself but thought that losing oneself was the greatest virtue, and that therefore there was no help there, only the mirror of a deeper abyss. And she left that conversation more alone than she had entered it, because she understood that most of the mothers around her would never see what she was beginning to see, that for them to efface oneself for one's son was not a problem, it was the ideal, and that if she wanted to find a way out she would have to look for it against the current, against what everyone told her it was to be a good mother, alone, without a map, rowing against everything she had learned.

There was a moment, in the midst of these discoveries, when Eulália almost wished she had not made them, when she envied the other mothers who never asked themselves anything, who lived their effacement without seeing it, at peace with it, holding it a virtue. Because to see the well, Eulália understood, is in a certain sense worse than to be in the well without seeing it: while she did not see, Eulália was unhappy but did not know it, lived her effaced life convinced that it was how it ought to be, and there was a kind of peace in that blindness. Now that she saw, there was no peace at all, there was the sharp awareness of a life ill lived, of a self wasted, and that awareness hurt in a way the blindness had never hurt. But Eulália also understood, even in the midst of that pain, that there was no going back, that once the well was seen one could not return to not seeing it, and that therefore the only way out was not backward, to the comfortable blindness, but forward, to the hard work of getting out of the well, cost what it might. To see had been painful; but to see was the beginning, because no one gets out of a place whose existence they do not acknowledge, and Eulália, in seeing hers at last, was taking, in the pain, the first step out of it.

She did not know what to do with this discovery, at first. It was too large, it hurt too much, it called too much into question. Eulália had spent her life convinced that being her son's voice was the most beautiful thing she did, and to discover that that beautiful thing had a reverse so dark — that she had imprisoned her son, that she had effaced herself, that she had built a bridge that led nowhere but back to her — was too much for a woman to digest at once. There was a detail, in those days, that marked her more than any reasoning. She found an old photograph, from when she was a girl, before everything, and stayed looking at it. It was a girl laughing, with lively eyes, with a face full of future, a girl of whom one could see, even in the faded photograph, that she had things to say, opinions, a will to live. And Eulália looked at that girl and did not recognise herself, and it was a shock, because that girl was she, was Eulália, and she was so far from the woman she was now, from the woman who was only her son's voice, that they seemed two different people. What had happened to that girl, Eulália asked herself. Where had she gone, with her lively eyes and her things to say? And she understood, with sadness, that that girl had not died suddenly, she had gone on fading slowly, year after year of giving herself to her son, until she disappeared so completely that the woman who remained no longer even remembered having been her. And she kept the photograph in a place where she could see it, not to torment herself, but as a promise, as a north: the girl with the lively eyes had existed, and perhaps, who knows, she was still somewhere beneath it all, waiting for the Eulália of now to come and fetch her.

And then she did what she always did: she put it off. She pushed the discovery into a corner of her head, and went back to translating her son, went back to being the bridge, went back to the life of always, because to change that life was too frightening, was to have to dismantle everything she had built, and Eulália did not yet have the strength for so much. But the discovery stayed, in a corner, gnawing, and there are discoveries like that, that a person pushes aside but that do not disappear, that stay waiting for the day when life, with its hard way of forcing us to see what we put off, brings them back to the middle of the room and says to us: now. Now there is no putting it off. And that day, for Eulália, was coming, and it came in her own body, tired of a life being the voice of two.
