## 5. The voice that was not hers

Madalena's question worked in Eulália for weeks, slowly, and led her to a discovery that changed the way she saw herself, and that was, at the same time, freeing and cruel: the discovery that the voice she used the whole day was not her own.

This discovery did not come all at once, it came little by little, in small moments in which Eulália caught herself noticing things she had never noticed. She noticed them, more than anything, one day, on hearing herself. They were at the market, she and Tomé, and Idalina, the cabbage woman, came near and asked Tomé how he was, and Tomé made his sound, and Eulália, out of habit, translated, he is well, Idalina, thank you, he was poorly but he is better now. And Idalina went off, satisfied, and Eulália stayed standing in the middle of the market with the bag in her hand, thinking of a thing she had never thought: that everything she said in the world was Tomé. That her voice, Eulália's voice, the sound that came out of her mouth the whole day, never said Eulália, it always said Tomé. He is well. He wants this. He does not like that. He understood. The mouth was hers, the voice was hers, but the words were all his, the whole of her life spent lending her voice to say another, and never, or almost never, using her voice to say herself.

And she noticed another thing, in that same market. Idalina had asked Tomé first, but had asked looking at her; and Eulália understood that the neighbour at the market had never learned Tomé's language, and the man from the council office had never learned it, and the baker had never learned it, and no one in Véspera had ever learned a word of Tomé's language, because they had no need to, because Eulália knew it for everyone, and Eulália was always there. And so, without wishing it, with the greatest love in the world, Eulália had become the reason her son, at the end of a lifetime, went on being mute for everyone but her. It was not Tomé's language that had been left unlearned; it was Eulália who had learned it so well that she had spared the world from learning it, and in sparing it she had bound her son to a single bridge, had made of him a man who could reach the world only by one road, and that road was the mother, and what happens to a man whose only road to the world is the mother, on the day the mother is gone, was a thing Eulália had never let herself think.

There was an afternoon, in the midst of these discoveries, when Eulália made an experiment that frightened her. She sat at the kitchen table with a sheet torn from one of Tomé's account books and the pencil with which she noted the errands, and decided to write a list of things she wanted, only she, for herself. Not for Tomé, not for the house, not out of duty: things that Eulália, the woman, might want for her own life. And she put at the top of the sheet, in her hand of one who learned to write as a girl and hardly wrote again, one word: I. And she stayed looking at the paper, with the pencil in her hand, and wrote nothing more. She tried. She thought: I want. And she stayed there, suspended in the I want, waiting for an end to the sentence that did not come. I want what? She did not know. The sheet had an I at the top and the rest blank, and the blank hurt her like a mirror, because it was exactly the portrait of what she was within: a blank sheet in the place where there should be a person. She crossed out the I in anger, and folded the sheet in four, and put it in the dresser drawer, beneath the receipts, and did not throw it away, and she herself did not know why. There it stayed, folded, the sheet of the crossed-out I, and Eulália would come to take it from the drawer many times in the years that followed, without ever managing to fill it, until the day, much later, when at last she filled it.

There was, around those days, an encounter in the street that showed her this whole. She came upon a distant cousin, Domingas, who had come from away and who had not seen her for many years, and Domingas stopped, pleased, and embraced her, and asked her, with that interest of one who meets a relative again, well then, Eulália, tell me about yourself, how have you been keeping, all these years, tell me of your life. And Eulália opened her mouth, and began: Tomé has been well, he has learned to do a few things on his own, and the new doctor is a decent person, and... And Domingas, with kindness, interrupted her, laughing: no, woman, I am not asking you about Tomé, I am asking about you. You. Eulália. How are you, what have you been doing with your life, what is in your soul. And Eulália stayed standing in the middle of the street, and tried, tried truly, and found nothing to say that did not begin with her son. She had not been doing anything with her life that was not Tomé. There was nothing in her soul that was not Tomé. And she said, at last, with a yellow smile, oh, Domingas, my life is my son, I have nothing more to tell. And Domingas, without malice, said, oh woman, that cannot be, a person has to have something of their own, and took her leave, and went on, and Eulália stayed there, in the middle of the street, with that that cannot be ringing in her ears, because Domingas, from away, had seen in a minute what she had spent a lifetime without seeing: that a person has to have something of their own, and that she had not.

And there was an added weight to this borrowed voice, which was the weight of responsibility. Because when one speaks for another, each word counts double: if Eulália translated wrong, if she put in Tomé's mouth a thing he did not want to say, she could decide his life wrong, as had nearly happened in the consultation of his childhood. And Eulália lived with that weight, and it was also for that reason that she was so worn, because to speak for oneself is light, a person says what they please and bears it, but to speak for another is heaviest of all, it is having always to guess, always to get it right, never to betray, and no one bears that weight a whole lifetime without wearing down to the bone.

She tried, once, to speak of this with Benvinda, perhaps in search of someone who would understand her. She went to her house, took her a sponge cake, and found Benvinda feeding Lúcia her supper by the spoon, with an infinite patience and a face of bottomless tiredness, and the two of them stayed in the kitchen while Lúcia ate, and Eulália, timidly, said: Benvinda, I have been thinking of a thing. I have come to feel that I have lost myself. That I no longer know what I want. That I am only Tomé's voice, and that if I am asked who I am, beyond that, I do not know how to answer. And Benvinda stopped the spoon in the air, and looked at Eulália without understanding, with a genuine astonishment, and said: but Eulália, that is exactly what it is to be a mother. A mother is that, it is to have nothing else, it is to be the child's. And a good thing too. Would that every child had a mother like that. Look at me. I am nothing besides Lúcia, and it is as it should be, it is my grace. And she went back to the spoon, and to Lúcia, and Eulália stayed looking at Benvinda feeding her daughter, and understood that she was speaking with someone who was even deeper in the well than she, someone who not only had lost herself but thought that to lose oneself was the greatest virtue. And she left that house more alone than she had entered, with the sponge cake already cut on Benvinda's table, and understanding that if she wished to find a way out she would have to look for it against the current, against what everyone told her it was to be a good mother, alone, with no map.

There was a moment, in the midst of these discoveries, when Eulália almost wished she had not made them, when she envied the other mothers who never asked themselves anything, who lived their effacement without seeing it. Because to see the well, she understood, is in a certain sense worse than to be in the well without seeing it. While she did not see, Eulália was unhappy but did not know it, and there was a kind of peace in that blindness. Now that she saw, there was no peace at all, there was the sharp consciousness of a life ill-lived. But she understood too that there was no going back, that once the well was seen one could not return to not seeing it, and that the only way out was not backward, toward the comfortable blindness, but forward, toward the hard work of getting out, cost what it might.

And there was a detail, in those days, that marked her more than any reasoning. She was tidying the bottom of a chest, in search of some sheets, and found, beneath everything, an old photograph, in a yellowed envelope. It was she, as a girl, before everything, at the gate of the fair, with the new clogs on her feet and Conceição beside her, the two of them laughing, arm in arm. And Eulália sat on the edge of the chest, with the photograph in her hand, and stayed looking. The girl of the photograph laughed with lively eyes, had a face full of future, and it could be seen, even in the faded photograph, that she had things to say, opinions, a will to live. And Eulália looked at that girl and did not recognise herself, and it was a shock, because that girl was she, was Eulália, and was so far from the woman she now was that they seemed two different people. What had happened to that girl, she asked herself. Where had she gone, with her lively eyes and her clogs? And she understood, with sadness, that that girl had not died suddenly, she had gone on being effaced slowly, year after year of giving herself to her son, until she disappeared so completely that the woman who remained no longer even remembered having been her. And she did not put the photograph back in the bottom of the chest. She put it on top of the bedroom chest of drawers, leaning against the mirror, turned toward the bed, not to torture herself, but as a promise, as a north: the girl of the lively eyes had existed, and perhaps, who knows, she was still somewhere beneath everything, waiting for the Eulália of now to come and fetch her.

And she began to think a thing, now, against her will. She began to look at her son, a grown man, and to ask herself: and if I die? And if I fall ill? And if one day I cannot be here to speak for him? What happens to Tomé, who has fifty years of world and whom no one in the world knows how to hear but me? And the answer was an abyss, it was the son left mute from one day to the next, isolated, at the mercy of those who did not understand him, exactly as in that green-walled office of his childhood, but this time with no mother to save him, because the mother would be, precisely, the one who was missing. And Eulália understood, with a shiver that ran down her spine, that her devotion, her total giving, her love without limits, had built for her son the most dangerous of prisons: a life that depended entirely on her, and therefore a life that would crumble on the day she crumbled. She had loved her son in a way that had made him incapable of living without her, and that, which she had always taken for love, began now to seem to her, with horror, a form of having imprisoned him.

She did not know what to do with this discovery, at first. It was too great, it hurt too much, it called too much into question. And so she did what she always did: she put it off. She pushed the discovery into a corner of her head, folded it as she had folded the sheet of the I, and went back to translating her son, went back to being the bridge, went back to the life of always, because to change that life was too frightening, it was having to take apart everything she had built, and Eulália had not the strength, yet, for so much. But the discovery stayed, in a corner, gnawing, as the photograph stayed on top of the chest of drawers, looking at her every night, and the sheet in the drawer, beneath the receipts. There are discoveries like that, which a person pushes to the side but which do not disappear, which stay waiting for the day when life, with its hard way of forcing us to see what we put off, brings them back to the middle of the room and says to us: now. Now there is no putting off. And that day, for Eulália, was drawing near, and it came in her own body, tired of a life being the voice of two.
