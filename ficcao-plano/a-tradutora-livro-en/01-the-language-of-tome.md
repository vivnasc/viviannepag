## 1. Tomé's language

There was in Véspera a language that only two people in the world spoke, and one of them was Eulália, and the other was her son, and it was that language, spoken between two, that made Eulália's life and very nearly unmade it.

Tomé had come into the world saying things in another way. He was not deaf, he heard everything; he was not simple, he understood everything, and faster than many people who spoke at their ease; but what he had inside him did not come out of his mouth in the language of others, it came out in something else, in sounds that were not words, in movements of the hands, in ways of looking, in silences that said more than other people's silences. And the village, which knew only how to hear its own language, looked at Tomé and did not understand him, and because it did not understand him it thought he had nothing to say, which was the most wrong thing in the world, because Tomé had as much to say as anyone, only in a language no one had taken the trouble to learn.

It was a language that had neither grammar nor dictionary, that could not be taught to anyone all at once, because it was made of small things that were learned only across years of attention: the difference between two almost identical sounds, one that meant hunger and another that meant distress; the way Tomé turned his head when something pleased him and the different way he turned it when something frightened him; the manner in which his hands drew in the air things he had no words to name, and which Eulália had learned to read as one reads a letter. It was a language of small particulars, and a language of small particulars is learned only with time and with love, because one has to want very much to understand a person in order to notice things so small, and it was that wanting, that mother's love that does not rest until it understands its child, that made of Eulália, across the years, the only fluent speaker of Tomé's language there was in the world.

No one, save the mother. Eulália learned her son's language the way one learns to breathe, without realising she was learning, only because she loved him and could not bear to see him in distress without knowing why. She learned that a high, repeated sound meant one thing, and a low sound another; she learned that when he struck his hand against his leg in a certain way he was thirsty, and in another way he was afraid; she learned to read in his face, in his shoulders, in his hands, a whole vocabulary that no one else saw, and after some years Eulália understood Tomé as though he spoke, understood him better than many mothers understand the children who speak plainly, because she had studied him with an attention that only love and distress together can give.

It must be said too, if Tomé is to be understood, that he was not a sad boy nor a bitter man. On the contrary: he had a clean joy, a capacity to delight in simple things that many people who speak plainly do not have. He liked the sun on his face, he liked music, he liked the feel of certain things, he liked to laugh, and his laughter, when it came, was one of the most beautiful things in Véspera, a laughter that had nothing to feign, that came from the depths, that filled the house. Tomé had a rich inner life, he felt everything intensely, he loved intensely, and his tragedy was never that he had nothing within; it was that he had no way of showing the world the great deal he had within, except through his mother. He was a man full within and mute without, and that was the tragedy, not an inner poverty but an inner richness with no way out, a richness held captive, and his mother was the only chink through which that richness reached the world, and that was why his mother was so important, because without her the whole inner world of Tomé stayed shut in there, invisible, and the world outside saw only a man who made sounds and gestures and never imagined the universe behind them.

And so she became the translator. Wherever they went, it was Eulália who told the world what Tomé meant to say. At the doctor's: the doctor would ask where it hurt, and Tomé would make his sound, his gesture, and the doctor would look at the mother, and the mother would translate, it hurts him here, doctor, on the right side, these two days. At school, later: the teacher could not understand what he answered, and Eulália was there, always there, saying he knows the answer, miss, he is saying it is the three, he understood, give him time. In the village: the neighbours spoke to Tomé while looking at the mother, they asked him things but waited for her to answer, and she answered, always, because she was the bridge, she was the translation, she was the only road by which what Tomé had inside reached the world outside.

And there was in this, it must be said, a greatness. Eulália saved her son a thousand times, in a thousand consultations, in a thousand meetings, in a thousand situations in which, without her, Tomé would have been treated as a case and not as a person, in which without her they would have decided wrong things for him out of not understanding him, in which without her he would have been struck mute before a world that did not know how to hear him. Eulália was, for decades, the difference between Tomé being a person in the eyes of the village and Tomé being a problem, and that is no small thing, that is a whole lifetime of love translated into vigilance, into being always present, into never being able to be absent, because if she were absent her son was left without a voice.

The people of the village thought they knew what Eulália's life was, and praised her for it, oh the devotion of that woman, oh how well she cares for her son, but no one truly knew what it was to be always on duty, with no holidays, no days off, not a minute in which one could switch off. Because a mother who is her son's only bridge to the world never has a moment of rest: even asleep, she sleeps with one ear open; even ill, she has to get up; even in mourning, even tired, even at the end of her strength, she has to be there, because there is no one to take her place, because she is the only one who speaks the language, and a language that only one person speaks has no understudies. Eulália had been on duty for decades, twenty-four hours a day, never able to leave the post, and she had done it without complaint, with love, but the body and the soul of a person were not made to be on duty a whole lifetime without rest, and what does not rest wears out, and Eulália was wearing out, in silence, with no one seeing, behind the façade of the devoted mother that everyone admired without imagining what it cost.

But every bridge has a weight, and the weight of this one fell entirely on Eulália, and it fell in a way she did not see because she was too busy being the bridge to notice what the bridge was costing her. What it cost her was herself. Because a person who spends their life saying what another wants to say slowly stops saying what they themselves want; a person who is always attentive to another's voice stops hearing their own; and Eulália, after so many years of being Tomé's mouth, had almost no mouth left for herself, no longer knew, if she were asked, and no one asked her, what she wanted, what she thought, what she felt, because for decades everything that came out of Eulália's mouth began with "he," and nothing, or almost nothing, began with "I."

There was an example that summed it all up, and that repeated itself in a thousand forms every day. Eulália and Tomé would walk into some place, the registry office, the shop, somebody's house, and the person who received them, even the one who had known Tomé for years, always turned to Eulália. They spoke to Tomé while looking at the mother. They asked him questions while expecting her to answer. They said, in front of him, as though he could not hear, well then, Dona Eulália, how is he getting on, as though Tomé were an eternal child or a piece of the furniture, and not a man who was standing right there, listening, understanding everything. And Eulália, at first, had felt at this a dull anger, the anger of seeing her son treated as though he were not present; but with the years she had grown used to it, and worse than growing used to it, she had begun to collaborate, because it was quicker for her to answer than to wait for the world to take the trouble to speak to Tomé and to wait for his answer. And so, without noticing, Eulália became complicit in the effacing of her son in the eyes of the world, at the same time as she was the only one who saw him whole: a contradiction she would only understand much later, that of being at once the one who knew him best and the one who most helped the world not to have to know him.

And there was a thing that pained Eulália and that she said to no one: that sometimes, at the end of a whole day of speaking for her son, she herself no longer knew what she thought about anything. She had spent the day saying what Tomé wanted, defending what Tomé needed, being her son's opinion and her son's will before the world, and when at night she sat down, alone, and tried to know what she, Eulália, thought about some thing or other, what she wanted for herself, she came upon an emptiness, a silence, as though the part of her that had opinions and wishes had withered from so much not being used, the way an arm withers that is carried against the chest for years on end. She spoke all day, more than anyone in the village, and at the same time she was, without anyone knowing, the most silent woman in Véspera, because nothing of what she said was her own.

She was known, in the village, as Tomé's mother. Not by her name. Few people, perhaps, remembered her name, Eulália, the beautiful name her mother had given her dreaming of a daughter of easy word. She was Tomé's mother, and she was the translator, and those two things had eaten her name, had eaten Eulália, and she had accepted that it be so, had even thought it was how it ought to be, that a good mother is exactly that, someone who disappears behind her child, who makes herself wholly his, who has no life beyond him. Eulália thought she was being a good mother. And she was. But she was also, without knowing it, disappearing, and no one disappears for free, and the account of that disappearance would come to her one day, as all accounts come, when least expected and there is no longer any way to put it off.

And yet the reverse of this must also be said, so that the story be just: that language between two, which would come to prove a prison, was for many years the most beautiful thing in Eulália's life, and it was a true love, of the greatest there are. Because there was an intimacy between Eulália and Tomé that few mothers and few sons ever come to know, an intimacy of those who share a secret language, who understand each other without words, to whom a single look is enough. Eulália knew what her son felt before he showed it; Tomé knew what his mother felt before she said it; and there was in that complicity a sweetness that filled the days, a being-at-home in one another that was, in many moments, pure happiness. When Tomé laughed, in his language, with that clean joy he had, Eulália felt herself the richest woman in the world; when the two of them, of an evening, communicated without anyone understanding them, there was a world there that was theirs alone, warm, closed, safe, and Eulália would not have traded it for anything. The problem, she would understand much later, was never the love; the love was good. The problem was that the love had become the only thing there was, had filled Eulália's life so completely that it left room for nothing else, not even for herself; it was that the language between two had become the only language Eulália spoke, to the point that she forgot she had, somewhere, a language all her own, never yet used.

There was a routine, in those years, that was the shape of Eulália's life, and that showed well how wholly she had made herself her son's. She woke thinking of him, of what he would need that day, of the appointments, of the things to see to; she spent the day seeing to him, anticipating his needs, attending to everything he wanted to say; she lay down thinking of him, of the next day, of what was left to settle. There was not, in that routine, a single space that was hers, a moment in which she thought of herself, an interval in which Eulália existed beyond the mother. And the strangest thing is that Eulália did not miss that space, because it was so long since she had had it that she had forgotten it could exist, forgotten that a person can have thoughts that are not about another, wishes that are not for another, a life that is not in the service of another. She lived turned entirely outward, toward her son, like a plant turned wholly toward a single window, and like that plant she had gone on bending all to that side, until she lost the shape she would have had if she had grown straight, until she no longer knew how to grow except crooked, turned toward her son's light, without ever having learned to grow also toward her own.

She was fifty-and-some when this story begins, and Tomé was a man now, a grown man, with his beard, with his adult body, but who still needed his mother to reach the world, because the world, in all those years, had never learned his language, had never had to learn it, because there was always Eulália to translate, and no one learns a language they have no need to know, and the village had no need to know Tomé's language because Eulália knew it for everyone. And it was this, this arrangement of a life, this bridge of two people, that seemed so solid, so eternal, so proof against everything, that one fright, a single fright, would set trembling, showing Eulália, all at once, what she had built: a road by which her son went out to the world and by which she herself had never again come home to herself.
