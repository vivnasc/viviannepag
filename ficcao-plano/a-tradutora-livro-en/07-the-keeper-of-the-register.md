## 7. The Keeper of the Register

It was at the House of the Register that Eulália ended up, in that time of fear, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that began to loosen the knot, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, had over people a wisdom that came from centuries of seeing them born with a name of their own, before they were mother, or son, or anyone's voice.

She had gone to see to some papers, the papers the doctor had told her to see to, the papers for guarding against her son's future for the case of her being gone, and it was in seeing to that that she broke down before the Keeper, because the Keeper asked her, on seeing her with those papers in her hand and that face, what was the matter, and Eulália, who unburdened herself to no one because she had neither time nor voice for herself, unburdened herself, told everything, the faint, the fear, the son who would be left mute, the abyss. And the Keeper listened, without hurry, and at a certain point asked a question no one had ever asked Eulália, a question so simple that it hurt: and you, Eulália? You have not spoken to me of yourself a single time. You spoke to me of Tomé from beginning to end. And you, in the midst of all this, where are you?

And Eulália was left without an answer, as she had been left with Madalena's question, but that time the Keeper did not change the subject, that time she insisted, with gentleness, and went to fetch a thing, went to fetch the book of births of the year Eulália had been born, and opened it, and searched, and found, and showed her the line. Look, she said. Here you are. Eulália, daughter of whom you were daughter, born on such a day of such a year. This line was written when you were born, more than fifty years ago. Long before Tomé. Long before you were mother of anyone. Long before you were the voice of whoever it was. When this line was written, Eulália, you were not Tomé's mother, you were not the translator, you were nobody's bridge. You were only Eulália. A girl just born, with a name of her own, with a life of her own ahead, with a voice of her own waiting to grow. That Eulália, the one of the line, existed before everything you are today for others. And I will tell you a thing, from my office, which is the office of keeping those who existed: that Eulália has not disappeared. She is still there, beneath Tomé's mother, as this line is still here, beneath everything you came to be afterward. What you lost was not Eulália. It was the habit of hearing her.

The House of the Register had, for Eulália, a quiet she found nowhere else, and she went on returning there, in that time, not for papers, only to be a while with the Keeper, only to breathe that quiet of a place where the memory of those who were is kept. And she noticed a thing, in leafing, with the Keeper, through some of the old books: that the register did not distinguish people by what they had done. They were there, side by side, in the same hand, in the same ink, the names of everyone who had passed through Véspera, and the book did not say this one was a good mother, that one a bad, this one worked much, that one little; it said only the names, and the dates, and the bonds, who was born of whom, who married whom. For the book, Eulália understood, all were worth the same, all had their line by the simple fact of having existed, and no one had to earn their line by being a perfect mother or anything else. And that idea, which for the Keeper was her daily bread, was for Eulália a revelation, because her whole life had been founded on the contrary, on the idea that she had to earn her place by being the best mother, by giving herself whole, and the book told her no, that her place was guaranteed from birth, that she had to earn nothing, that she was worth something for existing and not for serving, and that idea, so simple, was at the same time the hardest thing for Eulália to accept, because it went against everything that had made her live.

And Eulália looked at the line of her birth, written more than half a century ago, before she was anyone's voice, and it was like seeing proof of a thing she had forgotten: that there had been an Eulália who had existed for herself, before existing for another, an Eulália with a name that was not Tomé's mother, a girl who had been born to have a voice of her own and not to lend hers to one who had none. And the Keeper, seeing her look at the line, said more, and it was what stayed with her most: you know, Eulália, you did a great thing for your son, you gave him your voice when he had no way of reaching the world, and that was good love, of the best there is. But there is a thing you confused, and it is a confusion many a mother makes: you thought that for your son to have a voice you had to lose yours. And it is not so. Your son needs a voice, yes, but he does not need it to be your voice for a whole lifetime; he needs to learn to have his own, in whatever way, and he needs the world to learn to hear him, and that, Eulália, that you cannot give him by being his voice forever, that you can only give him by drawing away enough for him to have to look for it. You silenced your voice to give him yours. Now, if you truly love him, you have to do the contrary: you have to use yours again, for him to understand that he has to use his.

The Keeper, seeing the fear on Eulália's face, left her too an image that Eulália took home and that helped her to understand what she had ahead. She said: think of a mother who gives her hand to a child learning to walk. At first, she holds him firmly, or he falls. But a day comes when, if she goes on holding him with the same firmness, he never learns to walk alone, he stays bound to her hand forever. Love, on that day, is not to hold more; it is to open the hand slowly, it is to let him totter, it is to bear seeing him almost fall without seizing him, because it is in that almost-falling that he learns to balance. You held Tomé firmly your whole life, and you did well, because he needed it. But Tomé is no longer learning to walk, Eulália; he has long known how to walk, and it is you who do not open your hand. Open your hand. Do not take it away, for that is to let him fall; open it, for that is to let him walk. And you will see that he walks more than you think. The mothers who hold too much never come to know how much their children can do, because they never gave them room to show.

And the Keeper, who saw deep, understood the knot of the problem and said it plainly, so that Eulália would not flee from it: what you call love, Eulália, that of being his voice forever, has within it too a little of fear, and it is good that you know it, so that you can let go. You are afraid that, if the world learns to hear Tomé, he will cease to need you so much. And you are right: he will cease to. But that is not a loss, woman, it is the aim. A good mother works her whole life to make herself dispensable, because what she wants is for her son to be able to live without her, since she, sooner or later, is going to have to leave him. The mother who makes her son need her forever is not loving him more; she is loving him in a way that leaves him helpless for the day she is gone. The true love of a mother, Eulália, the hardest, is the one that works to cease being needed. And Eulália heard that and it hurt her, because it was exactly the contrary of everything she had done, she who had worked her whole life to be more and more needed, so that her son could not live without her, thinking that that was the height of love, when it was, she understood now, the height of a thing that had love but had also, deep down, her own need to be needed, to have a place that no one could take from her.

To use hers again. The Keeper said it as though it were simple, and Eulália understood that it was the hardest thing they had asked of her in her life, because she no longer even knew if she had a voice to use, no longer even remembered its sound, what it was to have an opinion that was not about Tomé, a wish that was not for Tomé, a sentence that began with I and not with he. Her voice had been mute for so many years that Eulália was afraid to look for it and find nothing, to open her mouth to say herself and have only silence come out, or worse, have Tomé still come out, because it was all that was left within her. But the Keeper assured her that the voice was there, beneath everything, like the line beneath the years, and that it was only a question of relearning to hear it, and Eulália left the House of the Register with that impossible task in her breast: that of, at fifty-and-some, learning again to speak for herself, after a lifetime of speaking for another.

Before letting her go, the Keeper said to her one more thing that Eulália would come to repeat to herself many times in the months that followed, in the hours when courage failed her. She said: you are afraid that, if you cease to be his voice, your son will cease to need you, and that, if he ceases to need you, you will cease to have a reason to exist. That is the fear, is it not? It is not only for Tomé. It is for you. You are afraid that without being needed you are no one. And Eulália, hearing that, lowered her eyes, because it was true, it was the truth she had said to no one, not even to herself: that she clung to being her son's voice not only out of love for him, but because it was the only thing that made her feel herself someone, and that she was so afraid to let her son go because to let her son go was to be left without the only role that made her worth something. And the Keeper, seeing her lower her eyes, said, with sweetness: well, that is where the mistake lies, Eulália. You are not worth something for being Tomé's voice. You are worth something for being you, and that is prior to Tomé, it is written in this line from before he was born. When you cease to be needed, you are not going to cease to be anyone; you are finally going to be able to be you, which is a thing you have been putting off for thirty years with the excuse of being needed. To be needed was your refuge, Eulália. It was where you hid from having to be a person in your own right. To leave that refuge is frightening, but it is where your life is.

And she left too with a new word, which the Keeper had left her and which was the reverse of everything she had done: to let go. Not to let drop, for to let drop is abandonment, and Eulália would never abandon her son; but to let go, which is another thing, which is to hold with an open hand instead of a closed one, which is to love leaving room, which is to be present without substituting oneself, which is to trust that the other can do more than we have let them show. To let Tomé go enough for him to grow toward the world; and to let herself go from the role of his voice, enough to have hers again. They were two lettings-go, and they were linked, Eulália understood, because while she was his voice he would not have his, and while she carried his voice she would have no room for hers, and the only way for the two to gain a voice was for her to let go of the bridge that bound them to each other, and to let each one, mother and son, find their own way to the world and to themselves.

The Keeper, in taking her leave at the door, left her one more sentence that Eulália repeated to herself many times, and that was, perhaps, the one that most helped her to let go without feeling herself a traitor. She said: remember one thing, Eulália. You are not going to cease being Tomé's mother by becoming Eulália again. You are going to be both. Tomé's mother and Eulália, at the same time, which is what you should always have been. The mistake was not your being Tomé's mother; that is one of the most beautiful things you are. The mistake was your having ceased to be all the rest. I do not ask you to cease being a mother; I ask you to be Eulália again too, alongside that. There is room for it. There always was. It was you who thought there was not. And Eulália clung to that, because it took from her the weight of having to choose: she did not have to choose between being a mother and being herself, she could be both, and that changed everything, because what paralysed her was the fear that to come back to herself was to betray her son, and the Keeper showed her that it was not, that to come back to herself was only to add Eulália to the mother, and not to take away the mother to put Eulália, and that in a life there was room for more than one thing, there always had been, and it was she who had convinced herself, out of a wrong idea of love, that she had to be only one.
