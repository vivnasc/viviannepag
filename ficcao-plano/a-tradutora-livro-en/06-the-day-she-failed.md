## 6. The day she failed

It had been wearing out, Eulália's body, for a long time, and she had paid it no heed, as she had never paid heed to anything that was hers. She had slept badly for years, with the light sleep of a mother who listens for her son in the dark; she ate in a hurry, standing, what was left over; she did not go to the doctor for herself, only for her son; she carried in her body a permanent tension, the tension of one who is always on watch, always ready to attend, always with one ear on Tomé even when asleep. A body like that, treated like that for decades, goes on wearing out in silence, and Eulália's had worn out, and she had not noticed because she did not look at herself, because to look at herself was no part of her life, because her body, like everything else that was hers, had been set aside in the service of her son. And bodies set aside end up complaining, sooner or later, in the only way they know, which is by failing, and Eulália's failed on a Tuesday, without warning, or rather, with all the warnings she had never wished to read.

It happened on a Tuesday, without warning, the way these things happen: Eulália fell. It was nothing spectacular, it was only a dizziness in the kitchen, a floor that came up suddenly, and then the waking with her son leaning over her making his sound of distress, and Eulália understanding, still dazed, that she had fainted, and that it was the first time in her life her body had failed her, and that her son, alone with her fallen on the floor, had no way to ask anyone for help, because no one understood him but her, who was lying there.

That was the terror, more than the fall. Eulália got up, slowly, with the help of her distressed son, and sat down, and while she recovered her breath she understood, with a cold clarity, what had just happened: that if she had stayed unconscious, or worse, Tomé would have been left alone, in a world that did not understand him, with no way to call anyone to his aid, because the only person who knew his language was lying on the kitchen floor. Tomé could knock at the neighbours' door, make his sounds, and the neighbours would not understand, would think he was agitated, as the doctor had thought when he was a child, and would not know that he was saying come, my mother has fallen, help. The bridge had fallen, and with the bridge had fallen her son's only road to the world, and Eulália saw, on that kitchen floor, the abyss she had been pushing into the corner of her head, saw it open before her, real, a faint away.

That afternoon, after the fall, Eulália did a thing she had never done: she sat watching her son care for her. Because Tomé, on seeing her fallen and then seated and weak, did a thing that moved her to the depths: he cared for his mother. He fetched her water, in his way, awkward but right; he sat beside her and put his hand on her hand, making his sound of comfort, that sound she herself made to him when he was distressed; he stayed there, watching over her, caring for her, the son caring for the mother for the first time in his life. And Eulália, receiving that care, felt two things at the same time: an immense tenderness, at seeing that her son loved her and knew how to care for her, that it was not only she who gave, that he too knew how; and a new fright, because she understood that Tomé, on seeing her in danger, had been terrified in a way that said everything, had been terrified like one who sees the floor of the world crumble, because for Tomé the mother was not only the mother, she was his only bridge, and to see the bridge fall was to see himself left stranded on one side of the river forever. And Eulália understood, on seeing her son's terror, that the weight she had been carrying was not only hers: it was also Tomé's, who lived, without saying it, with the permanent fear of losing her, because he knew, in his way, that without her he was left without a world.

She went to the doctor, that time for herself, a rare thing. The doctor, after examining her, told her it was nothing grave, that it was tiredness, tension, a body worn from years without rest, but he also told her, looking at her over his glasses, a thing Eulália took home: that she had to care for herself, that she was not young, that she came from a life of stress and badly slept nights, and that if she went on not resting the body would come to fall again, and the next time perhaps it would not be only a dizziness. And he added, not knowing what he was saying, not knowing that he touched the bottom of her wound: you spend yourself wholly caring for your son, Dona Eulália, and who cares for you? Because if you fall for good, who is left to care for him? You have to think of that. You have to prepare things for the case of one day not being there.

Eulália left that consultation with a sheet in her hand, some papers the doctor had given her, information about what to do, about how to prepare the future of a dependent son, and it was in looking at those papers on the bus back home that the whole thing fell upon her. Because the papers spoke of who would be responsible for Tomé when she was no longer there, of institutions, of guardianships, of arrangements for a man who could not govern himself alone, and Eulália, in reading them, understood that her son's whole life, his whole future, was built upon the assumption that he would never learn to live without someone doing everything for him, and that that assumption was her work, it was she who had made it true by always doing everything, and that those papers were, at bottom, the price of a whole life of being indispensable: a son who, she lacking, would have to be handed over to strangers, because she had never prepared him, nor the world, for them to understand each other without her. The papers spoke of replacing the mother with an institution; and Eulália understood, on the bus, that there was another possibility that no paper mentioned, that of preparing her son and the world so that, she lacking, there would be no need to replace her with anything, because Tomé would already know how to walk and the world would already know how to hear him. But that possibility depended on a thing she would have to begin to do at once, and that seemed to her a betrayal: to stop doing everything. And the papers, in her hand, trembled, because the hand trembled, because Eulália knew she was going to have to choose, soon, between the love she knew how to give and the love her son needed, and that the two, for the first time in her life, pointed to opposite sides.

For the case of one day not being there. The words stayed with Eulália like a sentence, because they said out loud what she had never dared to say: that she was going to be gone, one day, sooner or later, because everyone is gone, because no mother is eternal, and that her son would be left, and would be left mute, would be left without a voice, would be left isolated in a world that had never learned to hear him because she had heard him for everyone. And for the first time Eulália could not push the discovery into the corner, because now it was not an idea, it was a dizziness on the kitchen floor, it was a body that had warned her, it was the doctor telling her prepare things for when you are gone, and there is no pushing into the corner of the head a thing the body itself has come to tell us.

There was a night, shortly after the fall, when the fear took a concrete and horrible form. Eulália woke at dawn with her heart leaping, with a pain in her chest that was perhaps only fright but that in that moment seemed to her the end, and her first thought, even before the fear of dying, was Tomé. If I die now, she thought, lying in the dark with her hand on her chest, Tomé wakes in the morning and finds me dead, and has no way to call anyone, no way to say my mother has died, and stays here, alone, with my body, with no way to ask a world that does not understand him for help, perhaps for days, until someone wonders and comes knocking at the door. The image was so horrible, the son trapped at home with the dead mother unable to ask for help, that Eulália sprang up, lit the light, and sat on the bed trembling, and understood, on that dawn, that her fear of dying was no longer even for herself, it was all for what her death would do to her son, and that she had lived, for a long time, hostage to that fear, unable even to fall ill in peace, because her illness or her death were not only hers, they were a catastrophe for the son who depended on her for everything. And it was that dawn, more than the consultation, that decided her: she could not go on like this, could not leave her son hostage to her survival, had to do something, had to prepare him to live without her, cost what it might, because the alternative was that, was the son trapped at home with his mother's body unable to cry for help, and that alternative was unacceptable, was the proof that her love, taken to the extreme, had become a mortal danger to the person she most loved.

And Eulália entered, from that day, a fear that did not let her go. She came to dread falling ill, not for herself, but for Tomé; she came to lie down thinking and if I die during the night, and what does he do in the morning, alone, without me; she came to look at her son, a man of fifty, and to see not a man, but a child who would be left helpless the instant she closed her eyes, and that fear, instead of making her act, paralysed her, at first, because it was a fear with no way out: she could not not die, could not guarantee that she would never be gone, and therefore the abyss was there, inevitable, and her son walked toward it without remedy, unless she did a thing she did not even dare to think, a thing that seemed to her a betrayal: to teach the world to live without her. To teach Tomé to be heard by others. To let go of the bridge. To cease being the only one.

It cost her even more to accept this for a reason she was ashamed to acknowledge and that she only admitted to herself much later: that being her son's only bridge was also what gave meaning to her life, and to let her son go was not only to risk him, it was to empty herself of what made her be someone. There was, at the bottom of her resistance to letting go, not only the fear for her son, but the fear for herself, the fear that, ceasing to be indispensable, she would cease to have a reason to exist, and that fear, selfish and human, mixed itself with the love in such a way that Eulália could no longer distinguish what, in her total giving, was love for her son and what was her own need to be needed. Perhaps they were inseparable, she thought; perhaps all love that is given in excess has, at bottom, this double root, that of the good one wishes the other and that of the good it does us to be needed, and perhaps the task was not to separate one root from the other, an impossible thing, but to acknowledge that both were there, and to love her son enough to let go of the part that was more her need than his good.

Because that was, Eulália now understood, the only way out of the abyss, and it was a way out that seemed to her a death. For Tomé not to be left helpless when she was gone, she had to leave him a little helpless now, already, in life, had to cease being his voice so that others would learn to hear him, had to draw far enough away that her son would discover other roads to the world besides her. And that, for a mother who had made of total presence her love and her worth, was the hardest thing that could be asked, because it was to ask her to love her son contrary to everything she had always done, to love him by drawing away instead of drawing near, by falling silent instead of speaking, by letting him suffer difficulties instead of sparing him them, and everything in her cried out that this was abandonment, that this was to betray her son, that a good mother does not do that. And yet the doctor was right, the body was right, the abyss was real: if she did not teach her son and the world to live without her while she was alive, she condemned him to be left mute when she died. The love she had always known how to give, that of being always present, had suddenly become the danger; and the love she did not know how to give, that of drawing away in time, had suddenly become the only salvation. She argued with herself for days, and the argument was always the same, always turned round the same knot. On one side, the instinct of a lifetime: to be present, to do everything, to protect her son from every difficulty, to be the bridge. On the other, the cold reason the faint and the doctor had imposed on her: that if she did everything until she died, her son died with her, was left without a world on the day she was gone. And what made the argument so harrowing was that the instinct and the reason asked for opposite things, and both in the name of love: the instinct said love him by being present, and the reason said love him by preparing him for your absence, and Eulália understood that she had loved her whole life by instinct and that now, if she truly wanted her son's good, she had to love by reason, against instinct, and that this, to go against a mother's instinct in the name of a greater good of her son's, was perhaps the hardest thing a human being can be called to do. Because the instinct does not fall silent, the instinct cries out, and Eulália was going to have to do, every day, from then on, a thing her whole instinct cried out to her was wrong, and to have, at the same time, the faith that it was right, and to live in that tension, between the instinct that cries go to him and the reason that whispers let him learn, without ever having the absolute certainty of which of the two was right in each moment.

And Eulália, at the bottom of that fear, knew that she was going to have to learn, old, with her heart in pieces, the hardest form of love there is: that of one who loves enough to make themselves dispensable, that of one who prepares the person they love for the day when they will no longer be there.
