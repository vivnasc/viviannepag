## 6. The day she failed

It had been wearing out, Eulália's body, for a long time, and she had paid it no heed, as she had never paid heed to anything that was hers. She had slept badly for years, with the light sleep of a mother who listens for her son in the dark; she ate in a hurry, standing, what was left over; she did not go to the doctor for herself, only for her son; she carried in her body a permanent tension, the tension of one who is always on watch. A body like that, treated like that for decades, goes on wearing out in silence, and Eulália's had worn out, and she had not noticed because she did not look at herself. And bodies set aside end up complaining, in the only way they know, which is by failing, and Eulália's failed on a Tuesday.

She was taking the pot off the fire, at midday, with Tomé sitting at the table waiting for his lunch, and suddenly the kitchen tilted, and the floor came up to meet her, and the pot fell from her hand and the broth spread steaming across the floor, and Eulália went after it. It was nothing spectacular, it was only a dizziness, a going out of the light, and then the waking with the smell of burning and her son bending over her making his sound of distress, that high one, the one of great distress, with his open hands beating lightly on her face, calling her in his language. And Eulália, still dazed, on the kitchen floor, with the spilled broth around her, understood two things at the same time: that it was the first time in her life that her body had failed her, and that her son, alone with her fallen, had no way of calling anyone for help, because no one understood him but her, who was fallen.

That was the terror, more than the fall. She got up slowly, with the help of her distressed son, sat in the chair, and while she got her breath back she saw, with a cold clarity, the abyss she had been pushing into the corner of her head. If she had stayed unconscious, Tomé would have been left alone, in a world that did not understand him, with no way of summoning anyone to help her. He could go knock at the neighbours' door, make his sounds, and the neighbours would not understand, would think he was agitated, as Seixas had thought when he was a child, and would not know that he was saying come, my mother has fallen, help. The bridge had fallen, there, in the kitchen, and with the bridge had fallen the son's only road to the world, and Eulália saw the abyss open before her, real, a faint away.

And then Tomé did a thing that moved her to the depths. He got up, went to the sink, filled a mug with water, spilling half of it along the way, and brought it to her, and put it in her hand, and helped her raise it to her mouth. Then he took a cloth and knelt on the floor to clean the spilled broth, in his way, awkward but sure. And then he sat beside her, and put his hand on her hand, and made her a sound, low, repeated, the sound of comfort, the one she herself made him when he was little and was afraid of thunder. The son caring for the mother, for the first time in his life. And Eulália, receiving that care, with her son's hand in hers, felt two things at the same time: an immense tenderness, at seeing that her son loved her and knew how to care for her; and a new fright, because she saw, in Tomé's face, a terror that said everything. Tomé looked at her as one who sees the floor of the world give way, because for him the mother was not only the mother, she was his only bridge, and to see the bridge fall was to see himself left stranded on one side of the river forever. And Eulália understood, on seeing that terror, that the weight she had been carrying was not only hers: it was also Tomé's, who lived, without saying it, with the permanent fear of losing her.

She went to the doctor, that time for herself, a rare thing. It was no longer Vilar, it was a new one, Doctor Quintela, younger, with more patience, who examined her slowly and took her blood pressure twice. It is nothing grave, Dona Eulália, he said, putting away the instrument. It is tiredness. It is a body worn out from years without rest. The pressure is high, you sleep badly, you eat badly, you live on a war footing. But you have to look after yourself, do you hear? You are not young. If you go on not resting, this will happen again, and next time it may not be only a dizziness. And he stayed looking at her over the papers, and said a thing Eulália took home pinned to her breast, and that he said without knowing where it touched: the lady wears herself out entirely caring for her son, Dona Eulália. And who cares for you? I ask you in earnest. Because if you fall for good, who is left to care for him? You have to think of that. You have to prepare things for the case of one day being gone. And he gave her some papers, some folded sheets, information about what to do, about how to safeguard the future of a dependent son.

Eulália left that consultation with the sheets in her hand, and it was reading them on the coach home that the whole thing fell on top of her. The papers spoke of who would be responsible for Tomé when she was gone, of institutions, of guardianships, of arrangements for a man who could not govern himself alone. They spoke, at bottom, of replacing the mother with one of those places Seixas had wanted, back there, when Tomé was a boy and she had risen to save him. And Eulália, with the sheets trembling in her hand because the hand trembled, understood that the whole of her son's life was set up on the assumption that he would never learn to live without someone doing everything for him, and that that assumption was her doing, it was she who had made it true by always doing everything. Those papers were, at bottom, the price of a whole life of being indispensable: a son who, she being gone, would have to be handed over to strangers, because she had never prepared him, nor the world, to understand each other without her.

That night, Eulália put the papers on the kitchen table, in the lamplight, to read them with attention, and Tomé, who went about her as he always did, came near, and took up one of the sheets, curious, because it was new paper in the house. And he made the mother the sound of what is it, pointing to the sheet. And Eulália, who translated everything, was left not knowing what to translate, because how does one say to a son, in his language, this is the papers of who is responsible for you when I die. She took the sheet from his hand, slowly, and said, it is nothing, son, it is accounts, and folded the papers and put them away. And Tomé looked at her, and Eulália understood, by his face, that he had not believed the it is nothing, that he had felt, in his way, that those papers had a weight, that the mother had hidden them from him, and he stayed looking at her with a shadow of distress in his eyes. And Eulália understood, in that instant, with the papers in her hand, that her son lived, without knowing how to say it, with the fear of losing her, and that she was seeing to his future in secret as one sees to an anticipated mourning, and that that sheet she had just hidden was, at bottom, the confession that she was going to be gone, and that, being gone, she left him handed over to strangers. And she put the papers in the dresser drawer, beside the sheet of the crossed-out I, two folded papers that said, each in its way, the same thing: that there was a life to be lived and another to be safeguarded, and neither of the two did she yet know how.

For the case of one day being gone. The words stayed with Eulália like a sentence, because they said aloud what she had never dared to say: that she was going to be gone, one day, because everyone is gone, and that her son would be left mute, isolated in a world that had never learned to hear him because she had heard him for everyone. And for the first time she could not push the discovery into the corner, because now it was not an idea, it was a dizziness on the kitchen floor, it was a body that had given warning, it was Quintela telling her prepare things for when you are gone, and there is no pushing into the corner of the head a thing that one's own body has come to tell us.

There was a night, a little after the fall, when the fear took a concrete and horrible form. Eulália woke before dawn with her heart leaping, with a pain in her chest that was perhaps only fright but that in that moment seemed to her the end. And her first thought, even before the fear of dying, was Tomé. She lay in the dark, with her hand on her chest, and the image assembled itself alone: if I die now, Tomé wakes in the morning and finds me dead in the bed, and goes to my bed to make me his sound of good morning, and I do not answer, and he makes the sound again, and I do not answer, and he has no way of calling anyone, no way of saying my mother is dead, and he stays here, alone, with my body, with no way of asking for help from a world that does not understand him, perhaps for days, until someone wonders at the closed house and comes to knock at the door. The image was so horrible, the son shut in the house with the dead mother making her the sound of good morning to a body that does not answer, that Eulália sprang up, lit the light, and stayed sitting on the bed trembling, with the photograph of the girl of the lively eyes looking at her from the chest of drawers, there beside her, in the dark beyond the lamp.

And she understood, that dawn, that her fear of dying was no longer even for herself, it was all for what her death would do to her son, and that she lived, for a long time now, hostage to that fear, incapable even of falling ill in peace. And it was that dawn, more than the consultation, that decided her: she could not go on like this, she could not leave her son hostage to her survival. She had to prepare him to live without her, cost what it might, because the alternative was that, was the son shut in the house with the mother's body unable to cry for help, and that alternative was unacceptable, it was the proof that her love, carried to the extreme, had become a mortal danger to the person she most loved.

But it still cost her to accept it, and it cost her for a reason she was ashamed to acknowledge and only admitted to herself much later: that being her son's only bridge was also what gave meaning to her life, and to let her son go was not only to risk him, it was to empty herself of what made her be someone. There was, at the bottom of her resistance to letting go, not only the fear for her son, but the fear for herself, the fear that, ceasing to be indispensable, she would cease to have a reason to exist, and that fear, selfish and human, mingled with the love in such a way that Eulália could no longer tell what, in her total giving, was love for her son and what was her own need to be needed.

Because that, she understood now, was the only way out of the abyss, and it was a way out that seemed to her a death. For Tomé not to be left helpless when she was gone, she had to leave him a little helpless already, now, in life; she had to cease being his voice so that others would learn to hear him; she had to draw away enough for her son to discover other roads to the world besides her. And that, for a mother who had made total presence her love and her worth, was the hardest thing one could ask, because it was asking her to love her son contrary to everything she had always done: to fall silent instead of speaking, to let him pass through difficulties instead of sparing him them. Everything in her cried out that that was abandonment, that that was betraying her son, that a good mother does not do that. And yet, the doctor was right, the body was right, the abyss was real: if she did not teach her son and the world to live without her while she was alive, she condemned him to be left mute when she died.

And Eulália, at the bottom of that fear, knew that she was going to have to learn, old, with her heart in pieces, the most difficult form of love there is: that of one who loves enough to make herself dispensable, that of one who prepares the person they love for the day they will no longer be there. But she did not know where to begin, nor whether she had the strength, and it was in seeing to those papers Quintela had given her, the ones for preparing her son's future, that her feet took her, without her quite deciding it, to the House of the Register.
