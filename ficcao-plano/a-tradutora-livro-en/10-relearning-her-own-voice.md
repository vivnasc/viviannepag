## 10. Relearning her own voice

To relearn one's own voice, Eulália discovered, was like relearning to walk after a life bedridden: what in others is natural, in her was an effort, gesture by gesture, and it hurt, and there were relapses, and she went on doing it slowly, in that spring of her late life, with the help of the few people who were willing to help her and with the stubbornness of one who had decided, at the bottom, that she was not going to stay there.

She had asked Madalena for help, at last, and Madalena did not let her down. She came, sat with her, listened to her, and did not judge her, and was not frightened, and said to her a thing that Eulália kept forever: I know the place where you are, Eulália, I have been there. And I will tell you what I learned: that to come back to oneself is not done all at once, it is done in little bits, and that one does not need to know already who one is, it is enough to begin to look. You do not have to discover today what you want from life. It is enough to discover, today, what you want for supper, and tomorrow what you want to wear, and then what you want to do of an afternoon, and so, little bit by little bit, you go on finding yourself again. The voice does not come back because we order it to come back; it comes back because we begin to use it in small things, and it gains strength with use, like a muscle. And Eulália clung to that, to the idea that she did not have to rebuild herself whole all at once, that a little bit at a time was enough, and it was that that made the thing possible, because the task of becoming a whole person again, seen all at once, was crushing, but seen little bit by little bit it was only a series of small steps, and the small steps Eulália knew how to take, because she had taken, her whole life, millions of them, only always toward her son, and now she learned, slowly, to take some toward herself.

The strangest thing, at first, was the guilt. Each time Eulália did a thing for herself — ate what she liked, went out an afternoon, sang — the guilt came right behind, the old voice telling her that she was stealing time from her son, that a mother does not go about pampering herself, that her place was watching over Tomé. And the guilt was so strong, so old, so ingrained, that often it spoiled the whole pleasure, and Eulália found herself doing a thing for herself with the guilt gnawing within her, unable to enjoy it. It took time, and it took Madalena repeating it to her many times, for Eulália to begin to understand that the guilt was not the voice of the truth, it was the voice of an old habit, the voice of a wrong idea of a mother that they had put into her head and that she had taken for conscience. And she went on learning, slowly, to hear the guilt without obeying it, to say to it yes, I know you think I am doing wrong, but I am going to do it all the same, because a small thing for me takes nothing from my son, and the guilt, seeing that Eulália did not obey it, went on making itself weaker, lower, the way a voice grows weak that one ceases to heed, and after a time Eulália could already do a thing for herself and enjoy it whole, without the guilt gnawing, and that, that enjoying without guilt of a thing only hers, was one of the greatest victories of her whole reconstruction.

She began with the small things, because the voice of one's own, she understood, does not come back all at once, with a great declaration; it comes back in crumbs, in small choices, in small I wants that a person who has effaced themselves has to relearn to say. Madalena, who knew the path, gave her a simple rule that Eulália followed as one follows a recipe, because she had no map of her own for finding herself again. The rule was: every day, do a thing that is only yours, however small. It does not matter what. It matters that it be for you and that it have nothing to do with Tomé. And Eulália, who needed concrete instructions because the terrain of her own self was strange to her, followed the rule to the letter, with the discipline with which she had always done everything: every day, one thing only hers. Some days it was to sing; others, to sit in the sun five minutes; others, to eat a thing she liked; others, to say an opinion of her own to someone. They seemed ridiculous things, too small to count, and they were, nonetheless, revolutionary, because each one was an act of existing for herself, and Eulália, in doing them one by one, day after day, went on accumulating proofs that she existed, that she was a person and not only a function, and those proofs, small but daily, went on adding up until they formed, slowly, an Eulália anew, rebuilt brick by brick, small gesture by small gesture.

She began by allowing herself likings. She asked herself a question she had never asked: what do I like, to eat, for myself, not for Tomé? And she discovered, with an almost ridiculous astonishment, that she did not know, that she had cooked her whole life what her son liked and never what she liked, and she had to experiment, like one who comes to know themselves for the first time, and she discovered that she liked a thing her son did not eat, and she made it, only for herself, and it was a tiny act and it was enormous, because it was the first time in decades that Eulália did a thing only for her own pleasure, and not for her son.

She did more: she went back to a thing she had liked as a girl, before Tomé, before Aníbal, a small thing she remembered with effort, like one who unearths a memory from another life. When young, Eulália had liked to sing. She sang, as a girl, she had a beautiful voice, and she had sung until Tomé's time, and then had left off, because the time was all the son's and to sing was a luxury of those who have time for themselves. And one afternoon, alone at home, Eulália set to singing, softly, an old song she remembered, and her voice came out hoarse, out of tune from years without use, and it was strange to hear herself singing, to hear come out of her mouth a thing that was not Tomé, that was no translation, that was only her, only Eulália, making a sound for the pleasure of making it. And she sang, and the voice went on loosening, and after a few minutes she was already singing better, the voice already remembered what it was, and Eulália understood, in singing, that the voice does not die, it only sleeps, and that hers, silent thirty years, was there still, waiting, and that it was enough to use it for it to wake. And it was in singing, more than in speaking, that Eulália began to find her voice again, because to sing was the voice in its purest state, the voice made only for pleasure, serving nothing and no one, and it was exactly that that Eulália needed to relearn: to use the voice not for her son, not with an end, but only for being hers, only for the pleasure of having it.

She continued with the opinions. She noticed that, in conversation, she never said what she thought, she always said what her son needed, or agreed with the others so as not to give trouble, and she began to force herself, with difficulty, to say an opinion of her own, I think that, I believe that, and it was strange to hear herself beginning a sentence with I, so strange that at first she blushed, stammered, like a child reading aloud. But she went on insisting, and with each I think that the voice gained a little body, and Eulália went on discovering, with surprise, that she had opinions, that she had always had them, silent, beneath her son's voice, and that to say them did no harm to anyone, and that there were even those who liked to hear them, who took an interest in what Eulália thought, a thing she had thought interested no one because to her own self it had ceased to interest.

There were retreats, of course, because these things never go straight. There were days when the old voice, that of duty, that of guilt, told her she was being selfish, that a mother does not go about thinking of herself, that her place was beside her son and not learning to like foods and to give opinions. There were days when she did a thing for herself and right after the guilt fell upon her so heavily that it undid the whole pleasure, and Eulália had to learn, slowly, to argue with that guilt, to answer it that to care for herself was not to take anything from her son, that a whole mother was worth more to her son than an effaced mother. And she did not always win the argument; often the guilt won, and Eulália retreated, went back to annulling herself for a few days, went back to being only Tomé's mother, and then, with effort, went back to trying. But, as in the vegetable garden, what is sown ends up taking if one does not give up, and each small I want that Eulália managed to say left a root, and the roots, slowly, went on taking hold, and Eulália went on coming back, retreat by retreat, advance by advance, from the long exile of her own self.

And it was Madalena, the mother who had let go too soon and who after all was not the cold mother Eulália had thought, who most helped her in this relearning, because Madalena knew the path, had made it in her own way, and stretched a hand to Eulália. She took her out, a thing Eulália had not done in years; took her to places where Tomé was not spoken of, where Eulália was only Eulália, a woman among women, and at first Eulália felt herself naked without her son for a subject, did not know what to talk about if it was not him, but she went on learning, slowly, to be in a place as a person and not as a mother, to have conversations that were hers, to laugh at things that had nothing to do with her son, and each one of those outings was a brick rebuilding the Eulália who had lost herself, a woman returning to herself after a long absence.

"Do you know what is strangest, Madalena?" said Eulália, one afternoon, the two of them sitting in the sun, without Tomé, with no urgency at all. "It is that I, my whole life, was afraid of losing myself if I ceased to give myself whole to my son. I thought that being a mother was that, was to disappear. And now that I have begun to appear again, to have my likings, my opinions, my life, do you know what I have discovered? That I am a better mother this way. That Tomé likes me more now, that I have a life, than he did when I was only his shadow. How can it be, that I am a better mother the less I annul myself?"

And Madalena laughed, with that laugh of one who has already understood, and said: because children do not want annulled mothers, Eulália. They want living mothers. A mother who annuls herself gives her son the weight of being the whole life of a person, and that is a terrible weight for a son to carry, to know that his mother has nothing else, that she has effaced herself wholly for him. You, in having a life of your own, took that weight off Tomé. You freed him from being your only reason to live. And there is no better gift a mother can give a son than that: that of her not being a burden of gratitude and of guilt, that of the son being able to live his life without feeling that he is abandoning a mother who has nothing else. You are not being less of a mother for having a voice of your own, Eulália. You are being a mother in earnest, for the first time: a mother who loves her son and who also loves herself, and who therefore does not weigh upon the son the weight of a whole life left unlived.

She went on coming to know, in that time, an Eulália she had no memory of, and some of the things she discovered about herself surprised her. She discovered that she had a sense of humour, that she knew how to make people laugh, a thing she had forgotten completely, because for decades she had not made a joke, had always been too tense, too vigilant to laugh. She discovered that she liked certain conversations, certain people, that she had likings and dislikings, things she enjoyed and things she did not, a way of her own of seeing things, and that all of it had been there, buried, waiting. She discovered, above all, that she liked to be heard, she who had spent her life speaking for another and who had never, or almost never, been heard for herself; and that when she said a thing of her own and someone heard it with interest, she felt a deep pleasure, the pleasure of existing in another's eyes as a person and not as a function. It was as if Eulália, in relearning her voice, were finding again, piece by piece, a whole woman who had been kept there, and each piece she found again was a small celebration, and also a small pain, the pain of thinking of all the years in which that woman had been locked away, alive but silent, present but unused.

And Eulália stayed with that, and understood that it was true, and that she had gone her whole life mistaken about what it was to be a good mother. She had thought that to be a good mother was to disappear; and after all to be a good mother was to go on existing, was to have a voice of one's own alongside the son's and not in place of it, was to love the son without annulling oneself, was to give him the example of a whole person instead of the weight of an effaced person. And in understanding this, Eulália freed herself from an old guilt, the guilt of each time she did a thing for herself seeming to her that she was robbing her son, because she understood at last that to have a life of her own was not to rob Tomé of anything, it was to give him the best possible mother: a living mother, a mother with a voice, a mother who did not depend on him to exist and who therefore let him, too, exist free.
