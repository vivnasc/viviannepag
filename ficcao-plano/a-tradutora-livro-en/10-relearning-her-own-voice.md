## 10. Relearning her own voice

To relearn one's own voice, Eulália discovered, was like relearning to walk after a life bedridden: what in others is natural, in her was an effort, gesture by gesture, and it hurt, and there were relapses, and she went on doing it slowly, in that spring of her late life, with the help of the few people who were willing to help her.

Madalena came, the day after the call, as she had promised. She sat with her at the kitchen table, and Eulália made her coffee, and Madalena saw, on the table, still open, the sheet of the crossed-out I, and said nothing, but laid her hand over it, slowly. And she heard Eulália, and did not judge her, and was not frightened, and at the end said to her a thing Eulália took with her forever: I know the place where you are, Eulália, I have been there. And I will tell you what I learned. To come back to oneself is not done all at once, it is done in little bits, and you do not have to know already who you are, it is enough to begin to look. You do not have to discover today what you want from life. Look at that sheet. You wanted to write there what you want from your whole life, and of course it did not come out, no one knows how to answer that all at once. Cross out the whole sheet. And ask a smaller question: what do you want for supper today? Only that. And tomorrow, what do you want to wear. And then, what you want to do of an afternoon. And so, from little bit to little bit, you go on finding yourself again. The voice does not come back because we order it to come back; it comes back because we begin to use it in small things, and it gains strength with use, like a muscle.

And Eulália clung to that. And Madalena, before going, took up the pencil of the errands and the sheet, and crossed out the crossed-out I and the whole blank, and wrote, at the top, in her seamstress's hand, a single question: what do you want for supper today? And she said, begin with this one. And she went. And Eulália stayed looking at the sheet, and that night, for the first time in decades, she asked herself a question she had never asked: what do I like, to eat, for me, not for Tomé? And she discovered, with an almost ridiculous astonishment, that she did not know, that she had cooked her whole life what her son liked and never what she liked. And she had to try, like one who comes to know herself for the first time, and she remembered, with effort, a thing her mother used to make her as a girl, a maize pap with honey, that Tomé would never eat because he did not like sweet things, and she made it, only for herself, that night, and ate it alone at the table, and it was a tiniest act and it was enormous, because it was the first time in decades that Eulália did a thing only for her own liking.

The strangest thing, at first, was the guilt. Each time she did a thing for herself, right behind came the guilt, the old voice telling her that she was stealing time from her son, that a mother does not go about pampering herself. And the guilt was so strong, so ingrained, that it often spoiled the whole pleasure. The maize pap, that first night, tasted to her halfway through with the guilt gnawing at her, telling her that she was there eating sweets like a little girl while there was Tomé's washing to be ironed. It took time, and it took Madalena repeating it to her many times, for Eulália to begin to understand that the guilt was not the voice of truth, it was the voice of an old habit, a wrong idea of a mother they had put in her head and that she had taken for conscience. And she went on learning, slowly, to hear the guilt without obeying it, to say to it yes, I know you think I am doing wrong, but I am going to do it all the same. And the guilt, seeing that Eulália did not obey it, went on making itself weaker, smaller of voice.

Madalena gave her a simple rule, which Eulália followed as one follows a recipe, because she had no map of her own. The rule was: every day, do a thing that is only yours, however small. It does not matter what. It matters that it be for you and that it have nothing to do with Tomé. And Eulália, who needed concrete instructions because the ground of her own self was strange to her, followed it to the letter, with the discipline with which she had always done everything. Some days it was to sit in the sun five minutes on the doorstep, with her eyes closed, doing nothing. Others, to eat a thing she liked. Others, to put on the old fado not for Tomé, but for herself, and stay listening to it. They seemed ridiculous things, too small to count, and they were, nevertheless, revolutionary, because each one was an act of existing for herself.

And there was the afternoon of the singing. As a young woman, Eulália had liked to sing; she sang at the husking gatherings, she had a beautiful voice, and Conceição always said, you sing, Eulália, for you have the best voice. She had sung until the time of Tomé, and then had stopped, because the time was all her son's and to sing was a luxury of those who have time for themselves. And one afternoon, alone at home, with Tomé out with Olímpia, Eulália set to singing, low, an old song from the husking gatherings that she remembered, and the voice came out of her hoarse, caught, off-key from years of disuse, and it was strange to hear herself, to hear come out of her mouth a thing that was not Tomé, that was no translation at all, that was only she. She stopped, at first, ashamed of herself, looking around as though someone might hear her. And then, stubborn, she began again. And she sang, and the voice went on loosening, and after a few minutes the voice already remembered how it was, already rose, and halfway through the second song Eulália caught herself with her eyes wet, singing on her feet in the middle of the kitchen, and understood, singing, that the voice does not die, it only sleeps, and that hers, silent for thirty years, was still there, waiting, and that it was enough to use it for it to wake.

She went on through the opinions. She noticed that, in conversation, she never said what she thought, she always said what her son needed, or agreed with the others so as not to give trouble. And she began to force herself, with effort, to say an opinion of her own. She went to the grocer's, and Custódia, the one at the counter, said that there was going to be a new fair in the upper square and that it was a folly, and Eulália, who in other times would have nodded, said, slowly, I think it is a good idea, Custódia, that the upper square is dead and a fair brings it to life. And she blushed in saying it, and stammered, like a child reading aloud, and Custódia looked at her with some astonishment, because Dona Eulália never said anything, and answered, look, perhaps you are right. And Eulália left the grocer's with her heart beating as though she had done a very great thing, and she had, because she had said I think, two small words that began with her and not with her son.

There were setbacks, of course, because these things never go straight. There were days when the old voice, the one of duty, told her she was being selfish, that a mother does not go about thinking of herself, and Eulália drew back, went back to annulling herself for some days, went back to being only Tomé's mother, and then, with effort, went back to trying. But, as in the garden, what one sows ends up taking if one does not give up, and each small I want that Eulália managed to say left a root.

And it was Madalena who most helped her in this relearning, because she knew the road. She took her out, a thing Eulália had not done for years; she took her to an afternoon of women at the house of a friend, where there was no talk of Tomé, and at first Eulália felt herself naked without her son for a subject, sat in a corner and did not know what to talk of. But she went on learning, slowly, and on one of those afternoons she told, without meaning to, an old story, from the time of the husking gatherings, of a time when she and Conceição had swapped the clogs on purpose of two older girls who gave themselves airs, and the women laughed, laughed truly, and one said, oh Eulália, the things you come out with. And Eulália understood, with astonishment, that she was funny, that she knew how to make people laugh, a thing she had forgotten completely, because for decades she had been too tense, too watchful to laugh.

There was an afternoon, that spring, when Madalena took her to a place where Eulália had not set foot for forty years: to a husking at a farm, on the other side of the river, of a family Madalena knew. Eulália did not want to go, said no, that she had Tomé, that she did not know how to be at festivities, and Madalena said, Tomé stays with Olímpia an afternoon, and you come, for this is an order. And they went. And there was the mound of maize in the yard, and women in a ring husking, and there was singing, as there had always been singing at the huskings, off the cuff, one pulled a song and the others answered. And Eulália sat in the ring, with her hands in the maize, and at first only husked, silent, listening, with that shame of one who is in a place she no longer knows. And at a certain point a woman pulled an old song, one Eulália knew, one of those from her girlhood, and Eulália, without thinking, low, joined in, only with her mouth, almost without sound. And Madalena, beside her, heard her, and gave her a nudge, and said, loudly, this one sings, this one sings for I have heard her, and the women turned round, then you sing, woman, sing the next one. And Eulália felt the blood rise to her face, and wanted to say no, that she had not sung for thirty years, and opened her mouth to refuse, and what came out, instead of the refusal, was the first note of the next song, hoarse, hesitant, and then the second, firmer, and the women answered her at the refrain, and Eulália sang a whole verse off the cuff, before people who did not know her, people for whom she was not Tomé's mother, was only a woman who sang, and had a good voice, and the whole ring answered her, and at the end one said, you there, the seamstress's friend, who is this who sings so well, and Madalena said, with pride, it is Eulália, she is a friend of mine. She is a friend of mine. Eulália heard that, it is Eulália, said by another, in a place where no one knew she was mother of whoever it was, and she had to lower her head to the maize to hide that her eyes had filled, because it was the first time in decades that she was introduced by a name that was only her own.

Do you know what is strangest, Madalena, said Eulália, one afternoon, the two of them sitting in the sun on the doorstep, without Tomé, with no urgency at all. It is that I, my whole life, was afraid of losing myself if I stopped giving myself whole to my son. I thought that was what it was to be a mother, it was to disappear. And now that I have begun to appear again, to have my likings, do you know what I have discovered? That I am a better mother this way. That Tomé likes me more now, that I have a life, than he liked me when I was only his shadow. How can it be, that I am a better mother the less I annul myself?

And Madalena laughed, that laugh of one who has already understood, and said: because children do not want annulled mothers, Eulália. They want living mothers. A mother who annuls herself gives her child the weight of being the whole life of a person, and that is a terrible weight for a child to carry, to know that the mother has nothing else, that she effaced herself entirely for him. You, in having a life of your own, took that weight off Tomé. You freed him from being your only reason to live. And there is no better gift a mother can give a child than that: not to be for him a burden of gratitude and of guilt. You are not being less of a mother for having a voice of your own, Eulália. You are being a mother in earnest, for the first time: a mother who loves her son and who also loves herself.

She went on coming to know, in that time, an Eulália she had no memory of. She discovered that she had a sense of humour. She discovered that she liked certain conversations, certain people, that she had likes and dislikes, tastes and distastes, a way of her own of seeing things, and that all of it had been there, buried, waiting. She discovered, above all, that she liked to be heard, she who had spent her life speaking for another and who had never, or almost never, been heard for herself; and that when she said a thing of her own and someone heard it with interest, she felt a deep pleasure, that of existing in the eyes of another as a person and not as a function. It was as though Eulália, in relearning her voice, were finding again, piece by piece, a whole woman who had been kept there, and each piece she found again was a small festivity, and also a small pain, the pain of thinking of all the years in which that woman had been locked away, alive but silent.

And one night, alone at the table, Eulália took the sheet out of the drawer again, the one of the crossed-out I and Madalena's question, and beneath the what do you want for supper today, which already had an answer, she wrote, in her hand, slowly: to sing. And then: to sit in the sun. And then: to say what I think. It was not the whole life, it was only a small list, but it was the first thing Eulália wrote on that sheet since the crossed-out I, and the sheet, which had been a mirror of her emptiness, began at last to fill, line by line, with the hand of a woman coming back to herself.
