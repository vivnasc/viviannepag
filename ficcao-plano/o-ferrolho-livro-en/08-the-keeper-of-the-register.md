## 8. The Keeper of the Register

It had not been on purpose that Dores had sought the Keeper; it had been because she could no longer bear it alone, and the House of the Register was the only place where she could weep without being pointed at. She went about, since the night of fear, in the state of one who has lost the ground, not knowing whom to turn to, because her problem was not one to complain of to the village, nor to take to the police, nor to bring to the doctor, it was a problem of the heart, that of a mother lost between the love and the fear of a son, and for problems of the heart there is no office nor remedy, there is, at most, someone who listens. And the Keeper was that someone, in Véspera; she gave no remedies nor solutions, she gave what was most lacking, a listening without judgement, a shoulder, an old wisdom about what people carry and about what can and cannot be saved. And Dores, who had never asked anything of anyone, who prided herself on managing alone, went there, to the House of the Register, and let herself, for the first time in a long while, be heard, be sheltered, be treated as someone who also needs, and that alone, only to be heard without judgement by someone who did not judge her, was already, before any word, a relief, the relief of one who lays down, even if for an hour, a weight they had carried alone for years.

It was to the House of the Register that Dores ended up, in the days after the night of fear, and it was there, with the Keeper, that she heard the words that gave her the courage to do what she had to do, because the Keeper, who kept the memory of the village, had seen many a Dores and many a Quim pass, and knew what remained of each one, and what could, and what could not, be saved.

The House of the Register was, in Véspera, the place where the memory of those who had passed through was kept, and the Keeper the woman who tended the books, and there was in the village the idea that people went there to see to papers and came out with another thing, with questions they did not know they carried. Dores was not one to believe much in such things, but she was so shaken from the night of fear, so without ground, that she went to the House of the Register half because of a paper and half because she needed to be with someone who would not judge her, and the Keeper, who received everyone without judgement, was perhaps the only person in Véspera to whom Dores could say the unsayable, the fear of her son, without feeling it as a betrayal, because the Keeper was not the village, she was the keeper of all, and what one said to her was kept as one keeps a secret in a church.

She had gone to see to a paper, but she went shaken, and the Keeper, who saw deep, perceived it, and asked her how she was, and Dores, who unburdened herself to no one because she had learned not to complain, that time unburdened herself, because the night of fear had broken something within her and she no longer had the strength to keep it. She told everything: the son, the drink, the requests, the always-giving, the debt she had taken on for herself, the fear, the night. And the Keeper listened, without being scandalised, without judging, with the serenity of one who has already heard everything, and when Dores finished, she asked a question no one had asked her: and you, Dores? How long is it since you lived? You have not spoken to me of yourself a single time. You have spoken to me of your son, as before you will have spoken of your husband. And you, in the midst of all this, where are you?

And Dores was left without an answer, because there was not, for years, a Dores outside the saving, and the Keeper, seeing the emptiness where an answer ought to be, said a thing Dores carried for the rest of her life. She said: hear me well, Dores, for this costs but it is the truth. You are not responsible for your son's illness. It was not you who gave it to him, it is not you who cures it, and there is no money nor sacrifice of yours that saves him, because there are things a person can only do for themselves, and to stop drinking is one of them, and that your son has to do alone, or he does not do it. You can love him, you can leave him the door open for when he wants to change, but you cannot save him, Dores, however much you kill yourself trying, because to save a man from the drink is not in your hands, it is in his. And while you kill yourself trying to save what you cannot save, you do two bad things: you kill yourself, and you take from him the bottom that perhaps would be the only thing to make him want to save himself. Your love, Dores, is serving his illness and killing you. You have to turn it around. You have to learn to love him without saving him.

And do you understand the difference, Dores?, the Keeper went on. To love and to save are not the same thing, though you, and so many people, confuse them. You can love your son your whole life, and you should; but to save him, that you cannot, because his salvation is not in your hands. To love is to want his good, is to leave him the door open, is not to strike him from your heart; to save, in the sense in which you do it, is to take from him the consequences, is to carry him, is to do for him what only he can do. The love you can give him forever; the salvation you cannot give him at all. And your mistake, Dores, and the mistake of many mothers, was to think that to love you had to save, and that if you stopped saving you stopped loving. No. You can stop saving and go on loving; indeed, only by stopping saving does your love become clean, without the poison of the saving that sinks. Learn to separate the two things, woman, and you will see that your love, freed of the saving, becomes greater and not less.

The Keeper spoke with the serene authority of one who sees the whole village pass through the books and knows, for that, all the fates of the mothers who save. I have seen many like you, Dores, she said. I have seen mothers save sons from the drink and from what is worse, and I have seen how it ends. It ends almost always in one of two ways: either the mother wears herself out saving and dies before her time, and the son sinks all the same, and the two die; or the mother, in time, sets a limit, and saves herself, and gives the son the only chance he has. I have never seen, in all these books, a mother save a son from the drink by force of giving to him and covering for him; that does not save, woman, that puts off and sinks. The ones who saved anything were those who stopped, those who set the door, those who had the courage to let the son fall so that he might perhaps rise. It is against the instinct, I know; the instinct orders one to grasp, to hold, not to let fall; but with the drink the instinct deceives, because to grasp sinks and to let go perhaps saves. You have to go against your instinct, Dores, and that is the hardest thing one asks of a mother, but it is what this situation asks of you. And Dores heard, and engraved it, because they were the words of one who had seen many like her pass and knew how the stories ended, and the Keeper was telling her, without circling, that the path she walked had a known end, that of the two sunken, and that there was only one way out, the one that seemed impossible to her, that of letting go to perhaps save.

And the Keeper went to fetch a book, that of the births, and showed Dores her line, written long ago, before the husband, before the son, before everything. Look, she said. Here you are, Dores, newborn, before you were the saviour of anyone. Look well at this line, Dores, said the Keeper, with her finger on the line of birth. This girl, when she was born, owed nothing to anyone. She did not have to save her father, nor her husband, nor her son; she did not have to carry anyone; she was born, as everyone is born, with a right to a life of her own, to be cared for, to be happy. And somewhere, far back, someone put on her back a weight that was not hers, that of saving others, and she carried it her whole life thinking it was her duty, thinking it was what she was for. But it was not, Dores. Look at the line. The line does not say born to save. It says only born. You were born to live, as everyone is, and the saving was a thing they put on your back and that you confused with your nature. Lay it down, woman. You were not born anyone's beast of burden. You were born a person, with a right to a life of your own, and you are still in time to live it, if you lay down what they put on your back and that was never yours to carry.

This girl was not born to let herself be consumed saving men who sink; she was born to live, as everyone, with a right to her own line, to her own life, to her own house without fear. Somewhere along the way they taught you that you were only worth something if you carried others, and you believed it, and you carried, and you are carrying until you die. But it is a lie, Dores. You are worth something carrying no one. You have a right to lay down the weight. You have a right to a house without fear. And to set it, that limit, does not make you a bad mother; it makes you a mother who at last understood that she cannot drown to save one who will not let themselves be saved, and that to a son who sinks sometimes what most helps him is not a mother drowned at his side, it is a mother firm on the bank, with the door open for when he wants to swim to her.

There was a moment, in that conversation, when the Keeper touched what was perhaps the deepest, and touched it with care, because she knew it was an old wound. She said: you learned, Dores, somewhere, far back, that you only have worth if you save someone, that your place in the world is to carry others, and that is why you do not know how to stop, because to stop saving seems to you to lose your worth, to cease being good for something. But that is a lie, woman, it is a lie they put into your head as a child and that you have carried for sixty years. You are worth something without saving anyone. You are worth something for being, as everyone is, not for carrying. And while you do not believe this, you are going to look always for someone to save, because without someone to save you feel yourself without worth, and that hunger to feel yourself useful is going to throw you always into the arms of one who sinks, and you are going to drown one time and another proving a worth you already have. Your son does not need you to drown for him; he needs you to stay standing. And you do not need to save him to be worth something; you are already worth something. Learn this, Dores, and you are free.

And Dores, hearing that, felt a strange thing, a mixture of pain and of relief, because the Keeper had put her finger on a truth she had never formulated but that she recognised the instant she heard it: that she saved to be worth something, that her giving had beneath it a hunger for worth, and that that hunger made her the slave of whoever sank. And to understand this did not cure her at once, but it gave her a key, the key that her problem was not only the son, it was the idea she had of herself, and that to change that idea — to come to believe that she was worth something without saving — was the deep work she had ahead, deeper even than to shut the door, because to shut the door was the gesture, and to believe that she had a right to shut it, that she was worth something even without saving anyone, was what made the gesture possible.

And Dores heard that, and it was as though they took off her back a weight of a lifetime, because the Keeper had given her two things she had never had: the absolution — you are not responsible, you cannot cure him, it is not your fault — and the permission — you have a right to a house without fear, you have a right to set a limit, and to set a limit is love and not abandonment. The Keeper, before letting her go, said to her one more thing about doors, that stayed with Dores and that gave her, perhaps, the image she needed. She said: a door, Dores, serves for two things: to open and to shut. A door that only opens is not a door, it is a hole in the wall, and through a hole everything enters, the good and the bad, and the house defends itself from nothing. Your door, yours, has gone about open like a hole your whole life, letting in everything they asked of you, and so your house, and you, were left without defence. To learn to shut the door is not to cease being hospitable; it is to make the door a real door, that opens to what is good and shuts to what is bad, that lets in the healthy son and bars the drink. A mother without a door, who only opens, protects no one, neither the son nor herself; a mother with a real door, who knows when to open and when to shut, that one, yes, has a house that is a refuge and not a hole. And Dores kept this image, that of the door that has to know how to shut to be a door, and it was with it in her head that, days later, she at last drew the bolt, understanding that she was not ceasing to be a hospitable mother, she was making her door, for the first time, a real door.

And it was with that absolution and that permission, still trembling but already firm in her breast, that Dores left the House of the Register determined to do, at last, what Rosalina had done and what she had always thought impossible: to shut the door — not to her son, but to the drink, to the endless asking, to the fear — and to love Quim, from then on, from a bank where she herself would not drown.
