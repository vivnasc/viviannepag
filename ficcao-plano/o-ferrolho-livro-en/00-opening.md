## From the register of Véspera

In the record of the village, in the hand of the Keepers who have kept the book, the following is found:

Dores, daughter of Véspera folk, widow of Aniceto, mother of Joaquim. She spent her life saving those who were sinking: first the husband, who drank, then the son, who inherited from his father the thirst and the shadow. She covered, excused, paid, endured, always placed herself between her own and the harm, and held the house alone at the cost of wearing herself away, because she had learned, as a girl, that to love was that, was to save.

Her name was a promise, of the Véspera kind, of the old and hard ones: her mother wanted her the name of the sorrows, in the hope of a daughter with a back to bear them. And life gave a woman who bore the sorrows of everyone and never her own, until the night when she had to choose between going on saving a son who was destroying himself and saving herself, and when she learned the hardest thing a mother can learn: that there is a love that destroys the one who gives it, and that to shut the door to the harm is not to shut the heart to the one who brings it.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs a mother to set a limit to a son she loves, nor what is carried from a life of saving one who will not let themselves be saved. There are those who let themselves be consumed entire in sheltering one who sinks, without seeing that to sink with him saves neither of the two. For those, books such as this one are needed.

This is a novel of mother and son, this one, like the others on this shelf, and it is about the weight that is carried out of love — and about the limit that, set in time, is more love than all the sacrifice. It is also, and so it asks for care, a story about the illness of one who drinks, told not to judge the one who has it, who deserves compassion and not condemnation, but to accompany the one who loves that person and loses themselves trying to save them. Whoever has ever worn themselves away sheltering someone who was sinking, whoever has given until they had nothing, whoever has confused loving with saving, whoever has been afraid of the one they loved, that one will recognise themselves in Dores, and will learn with her what it costs a lifetime to learn: that we are not responsible for the illness of those we love, that we do not cure it by destroying ourselves, and that to shut the door to the harm is not to shut the heart to the person.
