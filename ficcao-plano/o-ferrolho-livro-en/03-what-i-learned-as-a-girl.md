## 3. What I learned as a girl

There are those who call these people the ones who forget themselves, and Dores was one of them, of those who live so turned toward others that they lose sight of themselves, of those who, asked what they want, do not know how to answer, because it is so long since they have asked themselves what they want that the question sounds to them in a foreign language. And it is not for being saints, though they seem it; it is for an old wound, that of one who learned that they had no right to want, to need, to weigh, and who made themselves, for that, wholly at the service of others, because at the service of others they had a place and in wanting for themselves they had none. Dores had learned this so early, and so deep, that she no longer knew she had learned it; she took for her nature what was her wound, and lived at the service of others convinced that it was how it ought to be, that that was her way of being good. And it took life nearly killing her, it took the bottom of her son and the night of fear, for Dores to begin to suspect that that way of hers of being good, that living oneself at the service of others, was not only goodness, it was also a wound, and that perhaps to be truly good included, also, not forgetting oneself.

The role of saviour was not born in Dores with her husband; it was born much earlier, as a girl, in the house where she grew up, and it was there that she learned, without anyone teaching it to her in words, the thing that governed her life: that love is to keep the peace, to absorb the chaos, to hold the family so that it does not crumble, and never, never, to be herself one more weight.

It must be understood that Dores never looked at her childhood as at a thing that had marked her, because the people of her generation and her milieu did not look at childhood that way; childhood was childhood, whatever it had been, and one did not go about turning it over nor blaming it for what one was as an adult. And so Dores had never linked her saviour's temper to the house where she had grown up; she thought it was so, that it was her temperament, that she had been born helpful and devoted. It took the Keeper, later, to help her see the thread that linked the appeasing girl to the saving woman, to understand that what she took for temperament was, in truth, a learning, a thing that had been taught her early and that she had taken for her nature. And there was, in seeing this, a liberation, because what is nature is not changed, but what is learning can be unlearned, and to understand that her saviour's temper was not nature but learning gave Dores the hope of being able to change it, of being able to unlearn, already old, what she had learned as a girl, and of being able, at last, to be in another way than that of one who wears themselves out saving others.

Dores grew up in a house where there was a shadow, and the shadow made of the house a place of tension, of dull fear, of fragile peace that could break at any moment. And the small Dores, as happens often to the most sensitive child of a house like that, made herself, early, the one who appeased, the one who kept the peace, the one who placed herself between her parents and softened things, the one who foresaw the storms before they burst and tried to prevent them, the one who carried, on her child's shoulders, an adult's weight: that of holding a house that threatened to fall. She learned to read the atmospheres, to anticipate the moods, to make herself small and useful, not to give trouble, not to have needs of her own so as not to add weight to a house that already had too much, and she learned, above all, that her worth lay in serving as a buffer, in absorbing what came, in keeping others safe at the cost of herself.

She remembered small things of that childhood, things she had kept without knowing why and that were, she understood now, the first lessons of her life. She remembered staying awake listening, when little, to gauge the state of the house before going down, to know whether she could be a girl or whether she had to be the appeaser. She remembered swallowing her own needs, not asking, not crying, not giving trouble, because the house already had trouble enough and she had learned that her worth lay in relieving and not in weighing. She remembered being praised precisely for that, for being so sensible, so helpful, so little bother, and how that praise, which ought to comfort a child, had taught her the wrong thing: that she was loved when she served, when she did not weigh, when she carried, and that therefore she had to serve and carry to be loved. A child praised for effacing themselves learns to efface themselves, and Dores learned it, and carried that effacement into her whole life, always serving to be loved, always carrying to be worth something, always last so that others could fit.

It was a stolen childhood, though Dores never called it that, because she never allowed herself to see herself as a child robbed of childhood; she saw herself as a good daughter, a helpful girl, one who helped. But what she called helping was, in truth, a child playing the adult, carrying responsibilities that were not hers, caring for those who ought to care for her, and that early inversion, that of the child who cares for the parents instead of being cared for, marked Dores so deep that it became her way of loving for the rest of her life: to love, for Dores, was to care for the other at the cost of herself, was to put herself last, was to save, and any relationship in which she was not the saviour seemed to her, at bottom, a relationship in which she was good for nothing, had no place, was not needed.

There is a name for what Dores became, though she never knew it nor needed to know it: the one who cares for everyone and for whom no one cares, the one who makes herself responsible for the happiness and the salvation of others as though it were her own, the one who does not know how to exist except at the service of someone. It is a temper that forms early, in the houses where a child has to grow up too fast to hold what the adults do not hold, and that seems, from outside, a virtue — how devoted, how helpful, how good a soul — and that is, within, a wound, the wound of one who never learned that they had a right to be cared for, to have needs, to weigh, to simply be without having to serve. Dores carried that wound from girlhood, and life, instead of curing it, went on giving her, one after another, men who needed to be saved, and each one of them confirmed the wound instead of curing it, because each one gave her one more person to carry, one more proof that she was only worth something carrying, one more excuse never to look at herself.

And the saddest is that Dores, in carrying others, felt good, felt that she was doing what she ought, that she was a good daughter, a good wife, a good mother, and that sensation of doing good masked the wound from her, prevented her from seeing it, because it is hard to see as a wound a thing that everyone praises us for and that we ourselves take for a virtue. Who would tell Dores that her devotion had a sick root? No one, because everyone profited from her devotion, everyone praised her for it, and she herself needed it to feel herself worth something. And so the wound passed unnoticed a whole lifetime, disguised as a virtue, fed by the praise and by Dores's own need to be needed, until the story, and the bottom, and the Keeper forced her, at last, to look beneath the virtue and to see the wounded girl who, beneath the saviour, went on carrying others in the never-said hope that someone, one day, would carry her.

And it was for that, she would understand much later, that she chose Aniceto, or that Aniceto chose her, because these things find each other: a woman trained to save finds a man who needs to be saved, and they recognise each other, and join, and the wheel begins. Dores did not consciously choose a man who drank; but she was drawn, without knowing it, to someone who let her go on being what she had learned to be, the saviour, because it was in that role that she knew how to exist, it was the only love she knew. And when Aniceto died and Quim began to sink, Dores went back to the role not only out of love for her son, but because the role was she, was her identity, was the place where she knew how to be in the world, and a woman without her role is a lost woman, and Dores, without someone to save, would not know who she was.

This was the deep root of the thing, the one Dores did not see and that the story was going to show her: that she did not save her husband and then her son only out of love for them, she saved them also out of a need of her own, that of being the saviour, that of having a sinking one to shelter, because without that her life was left without the only meaning she had learned to give it as a girl. And there was in this a truth hard to swallow, that her sacrifice was not only generosity, it was also the repetition of a childhood wound, that of a child who learned that they are only worth something if they carry others, and who went on, an old woman, carrying others to feel herself worth something, even if carrying them was killing her.

There is a pattern that repeats itself in the families where someone drinks, and that passes from generation to generation without anyone deciding it, and Dores's family was an example of it, though she only saw it very late. The pattern is this: there is one who drinks and there is one who saves, and the two roles need each other and feed each other, and the children who grow up seeing their parents in those roles tend to repeat them, some in the role of the one who drinks, others in that of the one who saves. Dores had grown up in the role of the one who saves, and had carried it into the marriage; and Quim had grown up seeing his mother save his father, and had learned the two roles, and the one that fell to him was the father's, that of the one who drinks and is saved. It was not chance, Dores would understand: Quim did not become like his father by bad luck, he became so because he had grown up in a house where he had learned, without words, that that was how a man was and that there was always a woman to save him. And there was in this a responsibility that was not guilt, but was responsibility: that the arrangement Dores had kept with her husband, that of saving instead of setting limits, had taught the son, without wishing it, the path he had come to follow.

And it was for that that to shut the door to Quim was, for Dores, also a way of breaking the pattern, of doing with the son the contrary of what she had done with the husband, of showing him, at last, that there was a woman in the family who knew how to set a limit, that the cycle of drinking and saving could stop, that it did not have to pass forever from generation to generation. Too late to change the past, but perhaps not too late for the son, and certainly not too late for herself, who in setting the limit broke, at least in herself, the chain that had bound her a whole lifetime to the role of saviour, and that perhaps, in breaking it in herself, broke it also for whoever came after, for the grandchildren, for the future, because the patterns one generation breaks are patterns the following ones no longer inherit.

There were, it is just to say, moments in Dores's life when someone offered her, without naming it, the chance to be cared for instead of caring. A neighbour, in times past, on seeing her worn, had offered to help her, and Dores had refused, as she always refused, there is no need, I manage; a sister, long ago, had wanted her to rest, to let herself be pampered a few days, and Dores had not known how, had felt uncomfortable being cared for, had fled to her tasks. Because to receive, for Dores, was harder than to give; to give was her place, her safe zone, and to receive put her in a strange place, vulnerable, where she did not know how to be. And she went on refusing, throughout her life, the rare hands that were stretched to her, always preferring to be the one who gives the hand to the one who receives it, and so she went on being more and more alone in her role, because a person who only knows how to give and never to receive drives away, without wishing it, those who would want to give to them, and is left only with those to whom they give, who are, often, those who consume them. And it was that, at bottom, the knot beneath the knot: not only did Dores make herself the saviour of those who sank, she also refused to be sheltered by those who would want to shelter her, and those two things together — to always give and never receive — were the exact formula of a life draining away without ever being refilled.

But Dores did not come to this truth alone, nor quickly, because it is one of the hardest truths there is, that of discovering that the thing we take for our greatest virtue — the giving, the sacrifice, the saving — has, deep down, a root that is no virtue at all, it is a wound, it is a fear, it is the hunger of a girl who was never cared for and who learned to make herself indispensable to guarantee a place in a world that taught her that she, by herself, was not enough. That truth Dores would only reach at the bottom, with the help of a Keeper, after a night of fear that would force her, at last, to look at what she was doing and at the wounded child who, beneath the mother who saved, went on trying, by saving others, to save herself.
