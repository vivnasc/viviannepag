## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a mother, in Véspera, who wore herself out a lifetime saving those who sank and who only at the end learned to shut the door to the harm without shutting the heart to the person.

Dores, widow of Aniceto, mother of Joaquim. She saved her husband, who drank, and then her son, who inherited his father's thirst, and she covered, paid, endured, always gave, until she was afraid of her own son in her own house, and until she discovered that her love without limit sank the two of them. It took a night of fear, and a Keeper, and a neighbour who had already made the crossing, for her to understand what no one had taught her: that she was not responsible for her son's illness, that she did not cure it by sacrificing herself, and that to drown saving him saved neither of the two.

And it was recorded, so that it be set down and console whoever reads it, that in shutting the door to the son who sank Dores recovered the daughter who had drawn away, Glória, who could not bear to see her mother consume herself and who came back when she saw her save herself; and that she became, in the village, a voice for other mothers in the same strait, telling them what they had told her; and that she found again the company of others who had made the same crossing, and that in that company she ceased to carry alone what she had carried her whole life.

It was recorded that she drew the bolt — not on her son, but on the drink, on the endless asking, on the fear; and that she left the door open to the son who would come sober, for when he wanted it. That she recovered the house, the sleep, the life, and herself. And that she learned to love from a bank, firm on the ground, with her heart open and her feet steady, instead of drowning again. The son's outcome stayed in the son's hands, as it had to stay; what changed, and what was in her hands, was that Dores chose, at last, to stay alive.

And it is set down, for whoever reads and recognises themselves, that this is not a story against a mother's love, it is a story in favour of it: in favour of a love that does not destroy itself, that knows how to set a limit, that distinguishes saving from sinking, that loves without drowning. It does not condemn the mother who gives; it understands her, because to give comes from love; but it shows her that there is a love greater than always giving, and that that love, the one that knows how to shut the door to the harm and keep it open to the person, is harder and truer. And it does not condemn the son who sinks, who deserves compassion and not contempt, because his is an illness and not a wickedness; it shows only that there are things that not even the greatest mother's love can do for a son, and that one of them is to save him from himself, because that salvation, if it comes, will have to come from him.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say what it costs a mother to set a limit to a son she loves, nor that to shut the door to the harm can be more love than all the open doors. There are those who drown saving those who sink, without seeing that to stay on the bank saves more than to dive in. For those, books such as this one are needed. And of all of it a record was made.
