## 5. The chorus of the village

Dores had carried hers in secret her whole life, first the husband and then the son, without sharing, without asking for help, with the shame of one who thinks they are the only one. And one of the things that most relieved her, when at last she opened up, was to discover that she was not, that there were others, many, carrying the same behind the closed doors of Véspera, and that to share with them, to cease to carry in hiding, was already half a relief. But it was not a discovering all at once, it was through the women, one by one, and through two in particular, Rosalina and Encarnação, who put before her eyes, without knowing it, the two paths that opened before her.

Encarnação came first, because Encarnação died first.

They had been friends as girls, Dores and Encarnação, of the kind to go to the river together and to court at the same corner, and Encarnação had been, she too, of those who save. She had had a son, Aurélio, who sank in the drink like Quim, and Encarnação had given him everything, deprived herself of everything, and had never set a door. Dores remembered going to see her, in her friend's last years, and finding her each time smaller inside her clothes, with her hands trembling from a tiredness that was no longer tiredness, it was another thing. On one of those visits, of the last ones, Encarnação was sitting at the kitchen table counting coins from a saucer, slowly, separating them into little piles, and Dores asked her what she was doing, and Encarnação said, without lifting her eyes from the coins, I'm seeing if there's enough for Aurélio until Thursday, Dores, he's got something to pay. And Dores looked at the little piles of coins, which were the last money of a woman who no longer had what to eat, and looked at her friend, who was a shadow, and wanted to say something to her, and Encarnação, who read her face, said only, before she could speak, don't say anything to me, Dores, I know what you're going to tell me and I don't want to hear it. He's my son. While I have a coin, he won't go hungry. And she went back to the coins. And Dores said nothing, because she herself, at home, did the same, and one who does the same thing has no mouth to correct it.

Encarnação died soon after, worn, of a tiredness of a life saving a son who sank all the same, and Aurélio, he, had sunk all the same, because the mother's sacrifice had not saved him, it had only killed her first. And the village wept for her as one weeps for a saint. Dores was on the church terrace, on the way out, and heard the women, oh Encarnação, what a mother, she gave her life for her son, she was a saint, and Custódia from the pharmacy, who always had a word for everything, said, now that was a mother's love, she gave everything, kept nothing for herself. And Dores, on the terrace, with the sun beating on the back of her neck, wept for her friend so too, with admiration for the sacrifice, and carried home Custódia's sentence, now that was love, she gave everything, kept nothing for herself, and for a long time she thought it was a praise. Only much later, with Rosalina working on her, did that sentence come back to her turned inside out, and Dores understood that the village had been mistaken, that there had been no sainthood there at all, there had been a tragedy: a woman who had let herself die saving one who did not save himself, two shipwrecks where perhaps one could have been saved. And she understood, with a shiver, that she was looking at her own funeral, at her own terrace, at her own Custódia saying of her, a few years hence, oh Dores, what a mother, she gave everything, kept nothing for herself, she was a saint, over a coffin where she was to be the next Encarnação, wept for as a saint and dead in vain.

Rosalina was the other path, and Rosalina was alive.

Rosalina had had a son like Quim, Tó, sunk in the drink, always asking, and Rosalina, at a certain point, after years of saving him, had done a thing that scandalised the village: she cut off. She told her son that she gave nothing more, that the door of the house was shut to the drink, and she kept to it, however much it cost her, however much she wept, however much the village pointed at her as an unnatural mother. Dores, at first, had thought that of an unbelievable harshness, and when she crossed paths with Rosalina in the street she barely spoke to her, with a silent reproach, as one who does not want to catch the illness of one who lets a son fall.

It was on an October afternoon, at the door of the shop, that the thing changed. Dores was coming out with the bag of rice, and Rosalina was going in, and they crossed on the threshold, and Rosalina, who saw deep because she had already been through all of it, stopped, and looked at Dores, at her thinness, at the colour of her face, and said, without circling, with the gentle harshness of one who has learned at their own cost, Dores, you have the face I had. And Dores, who was going to answer the usual, I'm well, Rosalina, it's the heat, could not, because Rosalina put her hand on her arm, there at the door of the shop, and said, come with me to the bench in the square, I have a thing to tell you and you need to hear it, and Benvinda, from the counter, saw the two of them go out together and said nothing, but kept it.

They sat on the bench in the square, under the plane tree, and Rosalina said to her the thing that stayed, and said it looking at the fountain and not at Dores, because there are truths that are better said from the side: Dores, you are doing what I did so many years, and I tell you what no one told me. You are not saving him. You are paying so that he does not touch the bottom, and while he does not touch the bottom he will never want to climb. The money you give him does not save him, it cushions his fall enough for him to go on falling without pain. If you truly love him, let him touch the bottom, which is the only thing that perhaps will make him look up. And while you do not do it, he sinks and you sink with him, and the two of you die, and that, Dores, that is not love, it is only fear of letting him fall. And Dores, on the bench, opened her mouth to defend herself, to say but you don't know how he is, and Rosalina cut her off, I know, woman, I know better than you, mine did the same, and Dores fell silent, because it was true, Rosalina knew.

And Rosalina told her, there on the bench and on other afternoons that followed, how her crossing had been, and Dores heard her as one hears news of a country into which they are going to have to enter. She told her of the first night when she shut the door to Tó, and how he knocked, and shouted, and how she stayed on the inside with the dishcloth biting between her teeth so as not to open, which was the only way she had of not crying out for her son. She told her of the weeks when the village cut her, when her own sister stopped greeting her at Mass, when she went to the fountain and the conversations stopped when she arrived. She told her of the terror, of the nights when she telephoned those she knew in the big town to ask whether they had seen Tó, only to know if he was alive, without ever giving him a penny. But she told her also the other side: how, with time, the house became hers again, how the sleep came back, how she ceased to hide things and to lock the drawers; and how Tó, without his mother's net, went on descending to a bottom, and how from that bottom, at the end of a very long time and without any guarantee, he had begun to sketch a climb. I do not promise you that yours will be saved, Dores, said Rosalina, no one promises that, and there are those who are not saved however much the mother does or does not do. What I promise you is that you save yourself, if you shut the door, and that you give your son the only chance he has, which is to touch the bottom. To save yourself is certain; to save him is only a chance; but it is more of a chance than he has now, which, with your net beneath him, is none.

There was an afternoon, of those, when Rosalina took Dores home, and Dores noticed Rosalina's house, which was the house of a woman who had gone back to living: it had flowers at the window, and a smell of cake in the oven, and an unfinished crochet blanket resting on the chair, of the kind only made by women who have their heads free to make things without haste. And Dores, who had at home the fear and the ear listening for the doorbell, looked at Rosalina's unfinished blanket as one looks at a thing of another world, and thought, without saying it, this is what she gained in shutting the door, a quiet afternoon to do crochet, and I, who did not shut it, have not sat down in years without my heart leaping. And she understood, in that blanket, more than in all the words, the difference between the two paths: that Encarnação had died with the coins separated into little piles, and Rosalina lived with an unfinished blanket, and that between Encarnação's coins and Rosalina's blanket lay all the difference between drowning and staying on the bank.

There were still, in the village, those who saw the thing in another way, and Dores heard those voices too, which pulled her back. Custódia from the pharmacy, who had wept for Encarnação as a saint, said to Dores, when she saw her giving to Quim, you do right, woman, a mother is a mother, while there is bread in the house there is bread for the son; and a cousin of Aniceto's, Idalina, who appeared now and then, said to her one never saw a son left helpless, Dores, that thing of Rosalina's is the doing of one with no heart. And those voices comforted Dores in her yielding, gave her reason, made of her always-giving a virtue and of Rosalina's limit a cruelty, and it was comfortable to hear them, because they told her what she wanted to hear. But Dores began to distrust them, because she saw what the always-giving did to her, and she saw the unfinished blanket on Rosalina's chair, and the evidence contradicted the comfort. The voices that comforted her were pushing her toward Encarnação's path, that of the coins in little piles and the coffin wept for as a saint's; Rosalina's voice, which troubled her, pointed her to the harder path but perhaps the only one with a way out. And Dores had to learn to distrust the comfort and to hear the discomfort, because the truth, sometimes, is on the side of what it costs us to hear, and not on the side of what gives us reason.

Rosalina, with time, became for Dores the only person with whom she could speak of that without shame, because she had been through the same and did not judge her. The others, even the well-intentioned, either pitied Dores in a way that humiliated her, or gave her easy advice of those who never went through it, bring him into line, give him nothing more, it is easy to say; only Rosalina knew the strait of a mother in that situation, knew that it was not lack of sense that made Dores give, it was a deep web of love and of fear and of guilt from which one does not get out with a piece of advice. And so Rosalina's words weighed, when she told Dores to stop, because they were not the easy advice of one who is outside, they were the hard experience of one who had been inside and got out.

They were two mothers, then, Rosalina and Encarnação, and two paths, and two outcomes, and Dores saw herself between them, at the crossroads. And she began, slowly, to ask herself the unthinkable: and if Rosalina, the mother the village had called unnatural, had after all loved her son better than she loved Quim? And if to cut off, far from being abandonment, were the only real chance of salvation, for the mother and for the son? And if the love she gave, that of always saving, were, after all, Encarnação's path, the one that kills the mother and does not save the son?

And Dores stayed with that, and it hurt her, because it was exactly what she did not want to hear and what, at bottom, she knew to be true. That night, at home, she went to the chest of drawers, to the sewing box, and took out from there not the wedding portrait but the empty case where her mother's earrings had been, the ones she had sold a few years before to plug a debt of Quim's, and she was left with the empty case in her hand, lined with a velvet already worn into the shape of the earrings that were not there, and she thought of Encarnação separating the coins into little piles, and of Rosalina with the unfinished blanket, and of the empty case that was what remained to her of her mother after a debt of her son's, and she understood that she had been, her whole life, emptying cases to fill a well, and that the well did not fill, and that the cases did not fill again.
