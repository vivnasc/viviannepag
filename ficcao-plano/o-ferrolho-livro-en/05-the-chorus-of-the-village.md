## 5. The chorus of the village

It was a silent ill, this, in Véspera as everywhere, that of the families who carry someone who drinks, because it is one of the ills least spoken of, one of those most hidden, out of shame. The families of the one who drinks hide it, cover it, pretend it is not so, because there is a shame attached to drink that is not attached to other illnesses, and that shame makes each family carry its own alone, in secret, without knowing that there are so many others carrying the same behind the closed doors. And Dores had carried hers in secret, first the husband and then the son, without sharing, without asking for help, with the shame of one who thinks they are the only one, that it is a misfortune of theirs alone; and it was one of the things that most relieved her, when at last she opened up, to discover that she was not the only one, that there were others, many, carrying the same, and that to share with them, to cease to carry in secret, was already half a relief. Because the secret weighs more than the thing itself, and the shame isolates, and Dores, on discovering the chorus of the others who carried the same, discovered that part of her weight had been the solitude and the secret, and that to share, even if it did not resolve the son, eased the load, because a shared load is always lighter than a load carried in hiding.

Dores was not the only one in Véspera carrying someone who was sinking, and there were in the village, as there are everywhere, other people living versions of her crossing, and it was in seeing them, each with their outcome, that Dores began, without knowing it, to see the possible paths before her.

To know these other women was, for Dores, a mirror and a relief at the same time: a mirror, because she saw in them her own situation, reflected and therefore clearer; a relief, because she ceased to feel herself the only one, the odd one, the lonely wretch, and discovered that her drama was a shared drama, common, human. And there was, among these women, when they gathered and talked, an understanding without words, that of one who knows what the other carries without needing to explain, and that understanding was a balm for Dores, who had carried hers in secret her whole life. But the mirror also showed her the paths, and the paths were different, and it was there that the chorus of the mothers became, for Dores, more than a relief: it became a lesson, because in seeing the different paths of the different mothers, and the different outcomes, Dores could, for the first time, choose hers with her eyes open, knowing where each one led, instead of following blindly the only one she knew.

There was Rosalina, who had cut off. Rosalina had had a son like Quim, sunk in the drink, always asking, and Rosalina, at a certain point, after years of saving him, had done a thing that scandalised the village and that Dores, at first, thought of an unbelievable harshness: she cut off. She told her son that she gave nothing more, that the door of the house was shut to the drink, and she kept to it, however much it cost her, however much she wept, however much the village pointed at her as an unnatural mother. And Rosalina's son, without his mother's money, had a bad time, bad in earnest, and Rosalina suffered like a tormented soul to see him have that bad time without attending to him; but she endured, did not attend, and at the end of a long time, a long one, Rosalina's son had, it was said, begun to straighten out, slowly, because he had reached at last a bottom that the mother had ceased to put off for him. And Rosalina, whatever the outcome of her son, was alive, whole, she had not let herself be consumed, and Dores looked at her with a mixture of old censure and of new envy, because Rosalina had done the unthinkable and survived, and her son, it seemed, was perhaps even better for it.

The story of Rosalina's son gave Dores a hope she did not have, and at the same time a fear, because it showed her that the path of the limit, however much it made sense, had no easy guarantees. Rosalina's son, after his mother cut off, went through a very bad time, and there was a point when the worst was feared, and Rosalina lived that time in a hell of guilt and of fear, asking herself whether she had done right, whether she had not pushed her son into disaster. And only at the end of a long time, a long one, did the son begin to straighten out, and even so slowly, with relapses, without ever being a clean victory. Dores understood, with Rosalina's story, that to shut the door was not a magic remedy that saved the son suddenly; it was a hard wager, painful, without guarantees, in which the mother traded the certainty of sinking both for the uncertain chance of saving one or both. And even so the wager was worth it, she understood, because the certainty of Encarnação's path was the ruin of both, and the uncertain chance of Rosalina's path was, at least, a chance, and an uncertain chance is worth more than a certain ruin, however much the uncertain chance gives more fear, because the fear of the uncertain is greater than the fear of the certain, even when the certain is worse.

There was Encarnação, who had let herself be consumed to the end. Encarnação had been like Dores, had saved hers without limit, given everything, deprived herself of everything, and had never set a door, never said no, and Encarnação had ended as those end who give themselves without limit to one who sinks: consumed, ruined, dead before her time, worn to the bone saving one who did not let himself be saved, and hers, that one, had sunk all the same, because the mother's sacrifice had not saved him, it had only killed her first. Encarnação had been a friend of Dores's, and her death had marked her, though Dores had never drawn from it the lesson she should have, until now. Encarnação had died worn, consumed, of a tiredness of a life saving a son who sank all the same, and the village had wept for her as one weeps for a saint, oh Encarnação, what a mother, she gave her life for her son, and Dores had wept for her so too, with admiration for the sacrifice. Only now, with Rosalina's sentence working in her, did Dores look at the memory of Encarnação with other eyes, and see, instead of a saint, a tragedy: a woman who had let herself die saving one who did not save himself, a useless sacrifice, two lives lost where perhaps one could have been saved. And she understood that the village was mistaken in calling that sainthood, that there was no sainthood at all in dying to save one who will not let themselves be saved, that there was only waste, the waste of a life that could have been saved and that was thrown away in a sacrifice that saved no one. And to have been Encarnação's friend, to have seen up close that end, gave Dores a warning that Rosalina's words only confirmed: that that was her path if she did not change, that she was going to be the next Encarnação, wept for as a saint and dead in vain, and that she did not want to, that she deserved more than an end like that, that she had a right not to die saving one who did not save himself.

And Dores looked at the memory of Encarnação and saw, with a shiver, her own future if she went on in the path she was on: the mother consumed, dead from saving, and the son sunk all the same, two shipwrecks instead of one, because she had thrown herself into the water after him instead of throwing him a rope from the bank.

Rosalina, with time, became for Dores more than a neighbour; she became the only person with whom she could speak of that without shame, because Rosalina had been through the same and did not judge her. The other people of the village, even the well-intentioned, either pitied Dores in a way that humiliated her, or gave her easy advice of those who never went through it, bring him into line, give him nothing more, it is easy to say; only Rosalina knew what it was, knew the strait of a mother in this situation, knew that it was not lack of sense that made Dores give, it was a deep web of love and of fear and of guilt from which one does not get out with a piece of advice. And so Rosalina's words weighed, when she told Dores to stop, because they were not the easy advice of one who is outside, they were the hard experience of one who had been inside and got out, and Dores heard them as she heard no one else, because they came from one who had the authority to say them, the authority of having made the crossing.

They were two mothers, Rosalina and Encarnação, and two paths, and two outcomes, and Dores saw herself between them, at the crossroads. Encarnação's path, that of always giving, the one she walked, led to two sunken ones; Rosalina's path, that of cutting off, the one that seemed to her inhuman, led to a living mother and to a son who perhaps, without the mother's salvation putting off his bottom, would find at last his own. And Dores began, slowly, to ask herself the unthinkable: and if Rosalina, the mother the village had called unnatural, had after all loved her son better than she, Dores, loved Quim? And if to cut off, far from being abandonment, were the only real chance of salvation, for the mother and for the son? And if the love she gave, that of always saving, were, after all, Encarnação's path, the path that kills the mother and does not save the son?

There were still, in the village, those who saw the thing in another way, and Dores heard those voices too, which pulled her back. There were those who said that a mother is a mother, that one had never seen a son left helpless, that while there is bread in the house there is bread for the son, and those voices comforted Dores in her yielding, gave her reason, made of her always-giving a virtue and of Rosalina's limit a cruelty. And it was comfortable to hear them, because they told her what she wanted to hear, that she was doing right, that to always give was the true love; but Dores, at bottom, began to distrust those voices, because she saw what the always-giving did to her and to her son, and she saw what the limit, however hard, had done to Rosalina's son, and the evidence contradicted the comfort. The voices that comforted her were pushing her toward Encarnação's path, that of the two drowned; Rosalina's voice, which troubled her, pointed her to the harder path but perhaps the only one with a way out. And Dores had to learn to distrust the comfort and to hear the discomfort, because sometimes the truth is on the side of what it costs us to hear, and not on the side of what gives us reason, and she went on learning, slowly, that the voices that told her go on giving were sinking her with good intentions, and that the voice that hurt her, Rosalina's, was the one that perhaps would save her.

It was Rosalina, in fact, who said to her the thing that stayed. They met one day, and Rosalina, who saw deep because she had been through all of it, looked at Dores, at her worn air, and said, without circling, with the gentle harshness of one who has learned at their own cost: Dores, you are doing what I did so many years, and I tell you what no one told me: you are not saving him. You are paying so that he does not touch the bottom, and while he does not touch the bottom he will never want to climb. The money you give him does not save him, Dores, it cushions his fall enough for him to go on falling without pain. If you truly love him, let him touch the bottom, which is the only thing that perhaps will make him look up. And while you do not do it, he sinks and you sink with him, and the two of you die, and that, Dores, that is not love, it is only fear of letting him fall. Rosalina told her, in that conversation and others, how her crossing had been, and Dores heard her as one hears news of a country into which they are going to have to enter. She told her how much it had cost, at the beginning, to keep the door shut; the nights when she wept, when she nearly yielded, when the village pointed at her; the terror that her son would do himself a disaster and the guilt would be hers. But she told her also the other side: how, with time, her life remade itself, how she recovered the house and the sleep and the peace, and how the son, without her net, went on descending to a bottom, and how from that bottom, at the end of a long, long time and without any guarantee, he had begun to sketch a climb. I do not promise you that yours will be saved, Dores, said Rosalina, that no one promises, and there are those who are not saved however much the mother does or does not do. What I promise you is that you save yourself, if you shut the door, and that you give your son the only chance he has, which is to touch the bottom. To save yourself is certain; to save him is only a chance; but it is more of a chance than he has now, which, with your net beneath him, is none. And Dores kept these words, and it was they, more than any others, that prepared her for what she had to do, because they came from one who had made the crossing and survived, and showed her that on the other side of the shut bolt there was not only the loss she feared, there was also, perhaps, a salvation, hers for certain and her son's who knows.

And Dores stayed with that, and it hurt her, because it was exactly what she did not want to hear and what, at bottom, she knew to be true.
