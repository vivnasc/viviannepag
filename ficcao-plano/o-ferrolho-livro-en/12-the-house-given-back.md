## 12. The house given back

It did not all go straight, and it would be a lie to tell it so. There were relapses of Dores, days when she opened the bolt she should have kept shut, when she yielded to a request she should have refused, when the mother's heart defeated the decision. And at each relapse Dores punished herself, thought she had spoiled everything, that she was good for nothing, that she was weak; and it was Rosalina, once more, who taught her the right thing about this too: that a relapse does not spoil everything, that no one does this without relapsing, that what matters is not never to relapse, it is to go back to the limit after relapsing, it is not to make of the relapse a giving-up. You fall, and you get up, and you go back to the bolt, said Rosalina, and the next time you endure a little longer, and so one learns, by stumbling, like everything that is hard. And Dores learned to treat herself with that gentleness in the relapses, not to make of each yielding a catastrophe, to go back to the limit after breaking it, and it was that gentleness toward herself, that patience with her own relapses, that allowed her, on the whole and despite the stumbles, to keep the course, because one who punishes themselves at each relapse gives up, and one who forgives themselves and tries again, that one, slowly, gets there.

Time passed over the night of the bolt, and when Dores looked back she understood that she had recovered a thing she had thought lost forever: her house, and with the house her life, and with the life herself, the Dores who had lost herself beneath a whole life of saving men who sank.

It took time, the house becoming a house again, because fears do not go from one day to the next, and Dores, even with the bolt drawn, still started many times at a ring, at a step, at the memory of the fear ingrained in her body. But slowly, night after night in which the bolt protected her, day after day in which the house stayed a place of her own, the fear went on settling, went on becoming memory instead of present, and the house went on becoming what it had been. Dores relearned to be in her house without alarm, to sit at the window without her ear listening, to open the door without her heart leaping, because now it was she who decided who entered, it was she who had the bolt, and to have the bolt, to be able to shut it, gave her back the mastery of her own house that the fear had taken from her. And there was a day when Dores understood that she no longer had fear in her house, that the house had become a refuge again and not a stage of distress, and it was a day of a deep and simple joy, that of a woman who recovered her home, which is one of the most basic and most precious things there is, the right to feel safe between one's own walls, a right that the fear had robbed her of and that the bolt had given her back.

The house, first. The house that had been a home and had become a place of fear became a home again, because the bolt drawn to the drink gave it back its safety. Dores ceased to dread the doorbell, ceased to live on alert, ceased to hide the money and to lock the things, because the door shut to the harm had made of the house, again, a refuge. And she relearned to dwell in her house with pleasure, to sit at the window without a start, to sleep at night without her ear listening for the dreaded ring, to receive whom she wanted to receive, the neighbours, Rosalina, people who came for the company and not for the request. The house, given back, became what it had been before the crossings of fear, and Dores, in dwelling in it in peace, felt a deep gratitude for that simple quiet, that of a woman safe in her house, which she had not had in years and which she had thought never to have again.

The sleep, before the life. Because the first thing that came back, after the bolt, was the sleep, and Dores understood, in recovering it, how much she had lost it. For years she had slept badly, with a light and startled sleep, always listening for the doorbell, always awaiting the night ring, and when the drawn bolt took from her the fear of the doorbell, the sleep came back, slowly, and with the sleep there came back a thing Dores had forgotten existed: rest. To wake in the morning rested, without the weight of a night of vigil, was such a great novelty that Dores almost wept the first time it happened to her, because she understood, by contrast, how much she had lived exhausted, how much the fear had robbed her of, for years, even of sleep. And the body, with the sleep back, went on remaking itself: Dores recovered a little of the weight she had lost, of the colour, of the strength, because a body that sleeps and that ceases to live in alarm remakes itself, and Dores's remade itself, slowly, and the village noticed, oh Dores has a better look about her, and she had, because a woman who recovers her sleep and her peace recovers her face, and Dores's face, which had gone about a shadow, became again that of a living person.

The life, afterward. With the house given back and the money that ceased to drain and the strength that ceased to be spent on savings, Dores discovered that she had, at last, a life to live, and she went on living it, slowly, in the manner of one who relearns a thing they had forgotten. She went back to caring for herself, which she had abandoned; she went back to having her small joys, her days, her people; she went back to being a woman and not only a worn saviour. And there was a strangeness in this, and a guilt, at first, the guilt of living well while her son sank; but Dores learned, with Rosalina and with the Keeper, that her life did not have to stop because her son's had stopped, that to drown with him did not save him, that she had a right to live even if he chose not to live, and that to live, she, was even the only good thing she could still offer her son: the example of a person who chose life, and the open door of a living mother for when he, one day, wanted to choose it too.

There was a thing Dores did not expect and that the new life brought her: the guilt, which she feared would pursue her forever after shutting the door, went on transforming itself, slowly, into another thing. At first, the guilt was enormous — the guilt of having shut the door to a son, of having let him fall, of living well while he sank —, and there were days when the guilt nearly made her retreat, nearly made her run to open the bolt. But with time, and with the help of Rosalina and the Keeper, Dores went on understanding that the guilt she felt was not the guilt of having done wrong, it was the guilt of having done a new thing, a thing against the habit of a lifetime, and that those two guilts resemble each other but are different: one is the voice of conscience telling us we err, the other is only the discomfort of changing, the protest of an old habit at being crossed. And Dores's guilt was of the second kind, it was the habit of saving protesting at having been crossed, and not the conscience accusing her of an error, because, thinking it through, she had done no wrong: she had protected herself, set a limit, left the door open to the sober son. There was no error there, only novelty, and the guilt went on dissolving as the novelty became habit, as Dores went on convincing herself that she had the right to do what she had done.

And there were, at last, moments of peace, that Dores had not known in years. An afternoon sewing at the window without a start. A night of whole sleep without her ear listening for the doorbell. A Sunday with Rosalina and others, laughing, living. Small things, but that for Dores were enormous, because they were the proof that life, even with the son still sunk, even with the uncertainty of his outcome, could have peace, could have joy, could be lived. And Dores clung to those peaces, not with guilt, but with gratitude, because she had learned that to have a right to her own peace was not to betray her son, it was the only solid base from which she could still love him and, if one day he came sober, could still help him — because a drowned mother helps no one, and a mother on her feet, with her house firm and her heart open, that one, yes, is ready for the day when the son, perhaps, wants to come back.

And Quim? Quim is the part this story does not close, because life does not close these stories, and it would be a lie to tell it in another way. Quim was not cured by magic when his mother drew the bolt; no one is cured so, and the drink is a deep and stubborn illness that does not go with a mother's limit. What happened was truer and more uncertain: Quim, without his mother's money cushioning his falls, began to feel the consequences of his acts, to touch bottoms that the mother had put off for him, and that was hard, very hard, for him and for Dores who watched him from afar; but it was, perhaps, the only thing that gave him a chance, because it is from the bottoms that the will to climb is sometimes born. Whether Quim would climb, Dores did not know, no one knew, and she had to learn to live with that uncertainty, to love her son without a guarantee of outcome, to keep the door open without knowing whether he would cross it sober one day. But she knew one thing: that while she had saved him, the son had had no chance at all to change, because he never reached the bottom; and that now, with the bolt drawn, he had at least that, the chance to touch the bottom and perhaps want to climb. The mother's limit did not guarantee the son's salvation; but it was, perhaps, the only thing that made it possible.

Dores became, with time, a voice for other mothers of the village in the same strait, as Rosalina had been for her. When she learned of a mother sinking to save a son who was destroying himself, she drew near to her, with the authority of one who had made the crossing, and told her what they had told her: that she was not responsible for her son's illness, that she did not cure it by sacrificing herself, that to drown with him did not save either of the two, that to set a limit was love and not abandonment. And some heard her, and others did not, as she herself at first had not heard Rosalina; but Dores said it all the same, because she knew the price of not hearing, and because to transform her pain into a warning for others was the way to give meaning to all she had been through, to make of her crossing of fear a light for those who came behind. And there was a silent community of these women, in Véspera, those who had learned to love from a limit, and they held one another on the hard nights, and Dores, who had worn herself out a lifetime in the solitude of one who saves, found in the end the company of those who share the same combat, and that company was one of the best things of her new life.

And there was, on one of those days of the new life, a visit from Glória, with the grandchildren, and it was a visit such as Dores had not had in years, a real family visit, with the house full of good people, without fear, without request, only the joy of being together. And Dores, watching the grandchildren play in the house that had become a home again, watching her daughter at peace, felt a happiness she had thought lost forever, and understood that in shutting the door to the son who sank she had not shut the door to life, she had opened it; that the house, free of the fear, could receive joy again; that there was still, in her life, much to live and to love, the grandchildren, the daughter, the friends, herself. And she understood that if she had gone on drowning with Quim she would have lost this too, this joy, these grandchildren, this daughter, this full house, because a drowned woman receives no joy at all, and that in saving herself she had also saved her capacity to live all the rest, all the good that life still gave her and that the drowning with her son would have robbed her of.

And one night, already in that new life, Dores made the gesture that became hers, and said aloud what she had learned, for herself and for the house given back:

The door of my house shuts itself to the drink, and to the asking, and to the fear; to my son, when he comes sober, it is always open, and I will do everything. But I die no more to save him. I love him from a bank, with the door open and the bolt at hand, and I stay alive, because my life counts too, because I too have a right to a house without fear, because a drowned mother saves no one, and the best thing I can be, for myself and for him, is a living mother, firm on the bank, with my heart open and my feet on the ground.

And there came, with time, a peace Dores did not know, and that was not the peace of one who has resolved everything — because nothing was resolved, the son went on sunk, the future went on uncertain —, it was the peace of one who has done what they had to do, of one who has put their part in order and handed over to the son his part, of one who has ceased to carry what was not theirs. It is a strange peace, that, that of accepting that one cannot save the one we love, that of laying down the weight of a responsibility that was not ours, and Dores learned that it is not a peace of giving-up nor of coldness, it is a peace of truth, that of one who has accepted the limits of their power and stopped torturing themselves trying the impossible. She went on loving Quim, went on suffering for him, went on wishing that he would save himself; but she no longer carried his salvation on her back as though it were hers to achieve, and that difference, between loving and wishing the good of the son and carrying the responsibility of saving him, was all the difference between a tortured life and a life at peace, and Dores, in making it, found, in the midst of a situation that went on painful, a peace that the situation had not changed but that she, in changing her way of carrying it, had won.

And she drew the bolt, that night, not with the pain of the first time, but with the serenity of one who had learned, at last, that to shut the door to the harm is the way to keep the house, and the life, and the heart, open to what is good — and that that, and not the drowning, is the love that lasts.
