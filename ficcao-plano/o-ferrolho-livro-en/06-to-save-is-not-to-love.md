## 6. To save is not to love

There was resistance, of course, and the resistance had a name Dores did not use but felt: the pride of a saviour. Because Dores had, without admitting it, a certain pride in being the one who gave, the one who endured, the one who did not give up, and that pride was linked to her idea of herself as a good mother, a good person, a woman of worth. To accept that her saving was not a virtue, that perhaps it did more harm than good, was to wound that pride, was to have to come down from the pedestal of the self-denying mother to the ground of a woman who had perhaps been mistaken, and that cost, because the pedestal, however uncomfortable, gave her a dignity, a meaning, an identity. And it took Dores accepting to lose that pedestal — that of the mother who sacrifices herself, whom the village admired and lamented — to be able to change, because while she clung to the image of the self-denying mother she could not do what seemed the contrary of it, to shut the door. She had to accept to seem, in the eyes of the village and her own, a less self-denying mother, to be, in truth, a better mother; she had to come down from the pedestal of sacrifice to the ground of good sense, and it was a humiliating but necessary descent, because it was on the pedestal of sacrifice that she was sinking, and it was on the ground of good sense that she could save herself.

Rosalina's sentence worked in Dores for weeks, and led her, slowly, to a discovery that called into question everything she had always believed about love: that to save and to love, which she had always taken for the same thing, could be, after all, different things, and sometimes even contrary.

This discovery — that to save and to love could be different things — was perhaps the most important of Dores's whole story, because it was the one that changed everything, and it was also one of the hardest to accept, because it went against a certainty so old and so deep that to call it into question was like calling into question the ground. Everyone had taught her, her whole life, that a good mother saves her child, that a mother's love is to give everything, that a mother sacrifices herself; and the whole village went on teaching it to her, every day, applauding the mothers who sacrificed themselves and censuring those who set limits. Dores had, around her, almost no one who told her the contrary, except Rosalina and the Keeper; everyone else, and her own inner voice, told her that to save was to love, that to give was the duty, that to shut the door was unnatural. And so the discovery that perhaps it was the other way around was so solitary and so hard, because Dores had to make it almost against everyone, against the village, against what she had learned, against her own inner voice, clinging only to the evidence of the facts and to the words of two women who had seen deeper, and to go against everyone is one of the hardest things there is, above all when what is at stake is the idea we have of what it is to be a good mother.

Dores had learned, as a girl, that to love was to save, and had lived sixty-some years without ever calling that equation into question. To love her father was to save him, to love her husband was to save him, to love her son was to save him: love, for Dores, was always said in the verb to save, and a mother who did not save her child was not, in her head, a mother in earnest. So Rosalina's idea cost her so much, that of not saving; because not to save seemed to her, simply, not to love, and she loved Quim more than anything, and therefore she had to save him, and that was that.

There was a proof that weighed more than any reasoning, and Dores saw it with her own eyes, one morning, at the door of the shop. She was coming out with the bag of bread when Tó passed in the street, on the other side, Rosalina's son, with a sack of cement on his shoulder and the firm step of one going to a job, and he greeted her from afar, with his hand, good day, Dona Dores, without turning his eyes away, without the air of one who flees, and went on his way with the cement on his back. And Dores stayed still at the door, with the bag of bread in her hand, watching him move off, and there came to her memory, over the figure of Tó at his work, the other figure, that of Aurélio, Encarnação's son, saved to the end, deprived of everything by the mother who killed herself saving him, whom she had seen, the last time, leaning against a wall in the square, at midday, with his face swollen and his eyes on the ground, begging from those who passed. The two sons, before her, in the same village: the one the mother had let fall, carrying a sack of cement; the one the mother had saved until she died, leaning against a wall. If saving saved, it ought to be the other way around, and it was so, and Dores, at the door of the shop, with the bread in her hand, had before her eyes, alive, the proof that her path led to the sinking of both and the other perhaps to the salvation of both, and it was that image, more than any reasoning, that shook her faith of a lifetime, because one thing is to hear oneself told that one is wrong, another is to see Tó with the cement and Aurélio against the wall on the same day.

But Rosalina's sentence had opened a chink in that certainty, and through that chink an uncomfortable light began to enter. Dores began to look at what her saving did to Quim, and what she saw did not confirm the equation, it contradicted it. Her saving did not better her son; it kept him the same, or worsened him. Each time she got him out of a strait, Quim did not come out of the strait nearer to changing, he came out nearer to making the next strait, because he had learned that there was always the mother to get him out, and one who learns that there is always a net has no reason to stop falling. Dores's saving had become, without her wishing it, a net that allowed her son to go on falling without consequences, and without consequences there is no change, because it is from the consequences of their acts that a person learns, and Dores, in taking from her son the consequences, took from him, without knowing it, the only thing that perhaps would make him change.

And she understood, with pain, that Rosalina was right: that she did not love her son in saving him, or not only; in saving him, she was putting off his bottom, cushioning his fall, allowing him to sink comfortably, and that, however much it came from love, was not love in its effect, it was the contrary, it was a soft push to go on sinking. And she understood too the other half of the truth, the one that hurt her even more: that part of what made her save her son was not even his good, it was her fear — the fear of seeing him fall, the fear of being responsible, the fear of not being needed, the fear of ceasing to be the saviour. She saved her son, in part, to spare herself the anguish of seeing him fall, and that, however human it was, was not a clean love, it was a love with fear inside, and her fear was costing her son the chance to touch the bottom and perhaps climb.

It cost her, this discovery, because it called into question not only what she did now with her son, but everything she had been her whole life. If to save was not to love, then what had her life been? A whole life of saving — the father, the husband, the son —, a whole life of thinking that that was love, and to discover now, old, that perhaps she had loved badly, that perhaps her saving had done more harm than good, was to call into question the meaning of everything, it was a kind of mourning of what she had thought was her greatest virtue. And Dores resisted that discovery, as one resists a truth that undoes us, because it was easier to go on believing that to save was to love, even if it was killing her, than to accept that she had been mistaken her whole life about what it is to love. But the truth, when it is the truth, does not let itself be kept away, and that of save and love being able to be different things went on imposing itself on Dores, against her will, proof after proof, until she could no longer not see it.

And there was the question of the register, the question of the shelf, the one Dores had never heard but that governed her life: what am I carrying that is not mine? And the answer, for Dores, was clear as water, now that the light entered through the chink: she carried her son's illness, her son's recovery, her son's bottom, as though they were hers to carry, when they were his. Quim's drink was Quim's; the decision to stop, if it came, would have to be Quim's; the bottom that perhaps would make him change was Quim's to touch. None of that was Dores's to carry, and Dores carried everything, took everything off her son's back onto hers, and in doing so not only crushed herself but prevented her son from carrying what was his, which was, after all, the only thing that perhaps would save him: the weight of his own choices.

She thought much, in that time, of the difference between helping and saving, because she understood that she had confused the two things her whole life. To help, she understood slowly, is to give someone what they need to rise on their own feet; to save, in the sense in which she saved, is to do for the other what only they can do for themselves, is to carry them instead of letting them walk, and that, far from raising them, keeps them fallen, dependent, incapable. A mother helps a son when she gives him the conditions to get himself out; a mother saves a son badly when she gets him out herself, time after time, from the consequences of his acts, because so she takes from him what he needs to change, which is to feel the weight of what he does. Dores did not help Quim; she saved him, in the bad sense, did for him what only he could do, took from him the consequences, and in doing so kept him fallen, because a man who is always saved never has a reason to rise. And to understand this difference was, for Dores, a turning point, because it showed her that her saving, which she took for the height of love, was in truth the contrary of help, was a carrying that prevented her son from learning to walk, and that to help him in earnest was perhaps, paradoxically, to stop saving him.

And there was a hard humility in this discovery, that of admitting that she did not have the power to save her son, that that power was not in her hands however much she sacrificed herself. Dores had always believed, at bottom, that if she gave enough, loved enough, sacrificed enough, she would manage to save her son, as a kind of exchange: so much sacrifice, so much salvation. And she had to accept, now, that it was not so, that there was no exchange, that she could give everything and sacrifice herself to death and the son not be saved, because his salvation did not depend on her sacrifice, it depended on his choice, and that was the hardest thing to accept for a mother who had made of sacrifice her religion: that the sacrifice bought no one's salvation, that one could give one's life for a son and the son be lost all the same, that a mother's love, however great, did not have the power to save one who did not want to save themselves.

She could not, on understanding this, change at once, because between understanding and managing there is an abyss, above all when what one has to do goes against the instinct of a lifetime and against the deepest fear of a mother. She argued much with herself, in that time, about what it was to be a good mother, because her whole idea of a good mother was bound to the saving, and to unbind it hurt like unbinding a part of herself. A good mother does not abandon her son, the old voice told her; a good mother gives everything for her son; a good mother never shuts the door. And Dores had to answer that voice, with the help of Rosalina and the Keeper, that one thing is to abandon and another is to set a limit, that to give everything for the son does not mean to give everything the son asks, that to shut the door to the drink is not to shut the door to the son. She had to dismantle, piece by piece, her idea of a good mother, and build another, in which to be a good mother could include saying no, setting a limit, letting the son face the consequences, protecting herself. And it was a new idea, and strange, and it went against everything she had heard her whole life about what a mother is, but it was, she understood slowly, a truer idea, because the old idea, that of the mother who gives everything and never shuts the door, led to Encarnação, to the consumed mother and the son sunk all the same, and the new one, that of the mother who loves and sets limits, led to Rosalina, to the living mother and the son with a chance. And between an idea that killed and another that gave life, Dores had to choose the second, however much the first was the one she had learned, because the proof was in plain sight of which of the two was after all the true love.

But the equation had broken, and that was already much: Dores could no longer believe, as she had always believed, that to save her son was to love him; she began to suspect the contrary, to suspect that she loved him badly in saving him, and that perhaps she would love him better, and herself too, if she learned the thing Rosalina had done and that still seemed to her impossible: to stop saving him, to let him touch the bottom, and to love him from a bank instead of drowning with him.
