## 4. Always to give

It had not always been so, and Dores remembered, with longing, a time when the relationship with her son was another thing, when Quim came to see her for pleasure, when they talked, when they laughed, when he told her of his life and she of hers, a relationship of mother and son like the others, with its joys and its normal frictions. That time had gone on sinking with the son, as the drink took him, and the relationship had gone on shrinking, losing the conversations, the laughter, the pleasure, until there was left only the request, only the transaction, only Quim coming to fetch money and the mother giving it. And Dores wept, within, that impoverishment of the relationship, the passage from a full relationship to a relationship reduced to a request, because it was like watching die, slowly, not only the son, but the bond between the two, emptied of all the good it had had until there was left only the dry bone of the request. And perhaps it was also that longing for the relationship that had been, as much as the love and the fear, that bound her to the giving, because giving was what remained of the bond, and to stop giving seemed to cut the last thread, however poor, that still linked her to her son.

Dores's relationship with her son had reduced itself, over the years, to a single dynamic, repeated without end: Quim asked, and Dores gave. And it was in that dynamic, in the always-giving, that one saw what Dores's love had become, and the harm that love, with the best of intentions, did to the two.

It must be said, to understand Dores and not to judge her, that Quim's descent was slow, and that each yielding of hers, seen in isolation, always made sense. No one decides, from one day to the next, to finance the ruin of a son; one comes there one step at a time, and each step, in the moment, seems reasonable. The first time Quim asked, it was a real emergency, and what mother does not attend to a son's emergency? The second, it was another, and one could no longer let fall whom one had helped before. And so, request by request, each one reasonable in itself, Dores went on descending a stairway whose bottom she had never decided to reach, without there ever being a step on which it was evident that she had to stop there, because each step was only a little lower than the previous. That is how these things happen, not by one great wrong decision, but by a succession of small reasonable decisions that added together give a ruin, and Dores, like so many, had descended the stairway one sensible step at a time, and only there at the bottom, on looking up, did she understand how far from the surface she had come, and that none of the steps, alone, had taken her there, but all of them together, yes.

At first, when Quim began to sink, the requests were sporadic and had an air of real emergency: a late rent, a debt that got him into trouble, a serious strait. And Dores attended, because she was the mother, because she was not going to leave her son in the street, because any mother would attend. But the requests, once begun, did not stop; they multiplied, spaced out less and less, and Dores went on finding herself giving more and more often, more and more money, until the giving became the normal, until her son's visits were almost only that, requests, and until she herself counted, in the tight budget of her pension, on the part that went to get Quim out of one more hole.

There were things Dores sold, or pawned, or ceased to have, to give to her son, and each one was a small loss she did not tally but that added up. She sold some earrings that had been her mother's, one of the few things that remained to her of her mother, to plug a debt of Quim's, and was left without them, and it hurt her, but she gave. She ceased to put aside the little she put by for old age, for a strait, for an unforeseen thing, because the little went all to the son, and she was left with no net at all, with nothing set aside, at the mercy of the first unforeseen thing. She deprived herself of small things and of large things, without ever adding up the total, because to add up the total would be to see the size of what she gave, and to see the size would be to have to stop, and she did not want to stop, or could not. And so she went on stripping herself, piece by piece, of the little she had and even of the memories, like her mother's earrings, all to feed a well that did not fill, and when, later, she did the sums, she understood that she had given her son not only money, but pieces of herself, memories, security, future, all thrown into a bottomless well in the name of a salvation that never came.

And Dores's money was not much. It was a small pension, what had been left over from a life of straits, and Dores began to deprive herself to give to her son: she ceased to buy what she needed, cut into what was hers, went on living with less and less so as to have something to give Quim when he came to ask. She deprived herself of the necessary to feed her son's drink, though she did not say it so, though she said to herself that it was to help him, that it was to get him out of it; but the money went to the drink, directly or indirectly, and Dores, at bottom, knew it, and gave all the same, and deprived herself all the same, because to say no to her son was a thing she did not know how to do, that she had never learned, that seemed to her a betrayal.

There was one time, of the many, that stayed with Dores as an example of everything. Quim had come to ask, she had hesitated, and he had said to her a thing that disarmed her: if my mother does not help me, who helps me? You are my mother. And Dores, on hearing that, had given, of course, because it was exactly the key that made her yield, that of being the mother, that of if she did not help no one would help. But later, thinking, she understood what was crooked in that sentence, however true it seemed: that Quim used the fact of her being his mother as a lever, that he transformed her love into an obligation to give, that he said, at bottom, you are my mother, so you have to give to me. And it was not so, Dores understood slowly: to be a mother did not oblige her to give what sank him; to be a mother obliged her, rather, to want his good, and his good was perhaps, precisely, not to give. Quim's sentence, you are my mother, which seemed an appeal to love, was in truth a blackmail of love, and Dores had to learn to distinguish the two things, to refuse the blackmail without refusing the love, to say I am your mother, and it is for being it that I do not give you what sinks you, turning against the blackmail the very motherhood it invoked.

And there was, in Dores's giving, a thing she did not see and that made everything sadder: that the giving was, also, the only way left to her of being with her son. Because Quim no longer came for anything else; he did not come to see her, to talk, to be with his mother; he came to ask, and the request was the pretext of the visits, and Dores, at bottom, knew it, and gave in part to have her son there, even if to ask, because a son asking was better than an absent son, and the visit of the request was the only visit left to her. And there was in this a deep sadness, that of a mother who buys, with the money she gives, a few minutes of the presence of a son who otherwise would not come, that of a love reduced to a transaction, in which the coin of exchange for the son's presence was the mother's money. Dores would never say it so, would never think it so, but it was so, and perhaps it was that, more even than the fear, the deepest reason why it cost her so much to close the tap: because to close the tap was to risk losing even that, even the visits of the request, even the bought minutes, was to risk being left without her son at all, and that, for a mother, is a fear that defeats almost all the others.

There was a time when Quim asked her for a large sum, too large, a serious debt, and Dores did not have it, and instead of saying I do not have it, son, I cannot, she did a thing that would haunt her afterward: she borrowed, she herself, to give to her son. She got into debt to pay Quim's debt, put herself into a strait to get him out of a strait, and Quim took the money and his debt was paid and hers was left unpaid, and months later Quim was in another strait the same, because the debt was not the problem, the problem was the drink, and to plug the debt without touching the drink was to plug a hole in a wall that went on opening holes. Dores understood, vaguely, with that debt she took on for herself, that she was doing a thing that had no end, that she could give everything she had and more, and the son would go on sinking, because she was feeding the sinking and calling that saving him.

She began, in that time, to hide things in the house, and it was a sign of how far the thing had come. She hid the money, in different places, moved it from place to place, because Quim, when he came and she did not give, or when he came and she was not there, searched, and sometimes took. She hid the things of value, the little she had, the gold of a lifetime, because she was afraid it would disappear. And there was a deep humiliation in this, that of a mother who has to hide her goods from her own son, that of living in her own house as one who defends themselves from a thief, and that thief being the son she had raised, whom she had nursed, whom she had kept watch over as a child. Dores did it with shame, without telling anyone, and each hiding place was a stab, because it was to admit, in gestures, what she could not admit in words: that her son had become someone from whom she had to guard her things, someone she could no longer trust inside the house, and that the drink had robbed her not only of her son but of the trust, the quiet, the very safety of the home.

And even so she gave. Even hiding, even distrusting, even hurt, when Quim asked to her face, Dores ended up giving, because the no stuck in her throat would not come out, because the image of the son going hungry or cold or worse was too much for her to bear, because a whole life of saving does not come apart in a no. And she was left, after giving, with the bitter sensation of one who has done a thing they know they should not have, the momentary relief of having attended to her son mixed with the deep certainty of having fed what destroyed him, and that mixture, the relief and the guilt in the same gesture, was the taste of each yielding, and Dores knew it well, because she felt it time after time, without managing to stop.

But even understanding, she could not stop, and the reason was simple and terrible: she was afraid of what would happen if she stopped. She was afraid that, without her money, Quim would fall deeper, do himself a disaster, end up in the street, or worse; she was afraid that, if she closed the tap, she would be responsible for what happened to him. And Quim, without knowing how to say it or knowing it too well, used that fear, came with stories that stoked it, if his mother did not help him this and that would happen, and Dores, terrified of being the cause of a disaster of her son's, gave, always gave, hostage to the fear that not to give would be worse. She did not see, then, what the story would show her: that in always giving she was not preventing her son's bottom, she was putting it off indefinitely, and that a bottom put off forever is a whole life sinking slowly, without ever touching the ground that perhaps, only perhaps, would make the son push himself upward.

There was a word for what Dores did, though she did not know it nor needed to know it: she helped her son to go on drinking. Not because she wished to, of course, but in effect, because each yielding took from Quim a consequence that perhaps would make him stop. When Quim spent his on drink and was left with no money for the rent, the natural consequence would be the strait of the unpaid rent; but the mother came and paid, and the consequence disappeared, and Quim was left free to spend the next on drink again. When Quim got into a debt because of the drink, the consequence would be having to face it; but the mother came and plugged it, and the consequence disappeared. And so, yielding after yielding, Dores went on removing from her son's path all the stones the drink put there, all the consequences that perhaps would make him stop to think, and in removing them she left the path smooth for him to go on drinking without ever stumbling on anything. She called this helping him; it was, in effect, helping him to drink, because it took from him the reasons to stop, and a man from whom the reasons to stop are taken does not stop. Dores did not see this, or saw it vaguely and pushed it away, because it was unbearable, it was to admit that her love served her son's illness, that she was, without wishing it, complicit in what destroyed him; but it was true, and the story was going to force her to see it.

And while she put off her son's bottom, Dores dug her own. Each yielding took a little more from her, of the money, of the strength, of the peace, of the house, and Dores went on wearing away, went on ruining herself, went on filling with a debt that was not only of money, it was of life, and the worst is that she saw no way out, because the only way out she could conceive, to stop giving, seemed to her impossible, a betrayal, an abandonment, and so she went on, caught in the wheel of the always-giving, sinking with her son in the name of saving him, with no one yet to tell her that there was another way to love him that was not to sink with him.
