## 7. The night of fear

There was a night that changed everything, the night when the fear ceased to be a shadow and became a concrete thing, the night when Dores was afraid of her son in her own house, afraid in earnest, and when she understood, in her body and not only in her head, the bottom she had reached.

It had been a bad day, that one, with a foreboding since the afternoon, because Dores had learned to sense the bad nights as one senses a storm, by a change in the air. She had known, by the village's means, that Quim was worse, that he had got into trouble, that he drank more than ever, and she had spent the afternoon with her heart tight, waiting, because she knew that an afternoon like that usually ended in a night ring. And it ended.

It was the night ring, the ring she knew and dreaded, the finger on the button without letting go, and Dores, on the other side of the door, with her heart leaping, knew at once that Quim came in a bad way, came drunk, came in the shadow. And she hesitated, as she always hesitated, between opening and not opening, and she opened, as she always opened, because he was her son, because she was afraid of what would happen if she did not open, because never, in her whole life, had she managed to leave a door shut to a child. And Quim came in, and he was Quim and he was not, he was the drink with her son's face, and he came to ask, and he came worse than ever, and when Dores, that time, hesitated to give, when Rosalina's sentence gave her, for an instant, the courage to hesitate, Quim changed, became another, and Dores saw, that night, in the son she loved, a thing that terrified her.

There had been signs, before that night, that things were going that way, and Dores, looking back, saw them, but had not wanted to see them in the moment, because to see them was to have to act. Quim had come, other times, with the drink making him harsher, more demanding, and Dores had noticed the fear growing, slowly, visit after visit, but had told herself that it was her son, that he would do her no harm, that she exaggerated. A mother finds it very hard to admit that she is afraid of a son, and Dores had put off that admission as much as she could, finding excuses, minimising, telling herself that it was the drink and not the son, that it passed, that it was nothing. Until the night when it was something, when the fear ceased to be a suspicion she could minimise and became a certainty she could not deny, and it was that passage, from the deniable fear to the undeniable fear, that made of that night the night that changed everything, because what can be denied lets itself be put off, and what cannot be denied forces one to act.

There is no need to tell what happened that night, and there are things that are better told by what they leave than by what they are. It must be said, with the care the subject asks, that the Quim of that night was not a bad man, he was a sick man, and that the fear Dores felt was not fear of a wicked one, it was fear of what the drink made of a son who, sober, was incapable of frightening her. And that, far from easing the fear, made it more terrible, because it was a fear without hatred, a fear with love inside, the fear of a mother who sees the drink take hold of her son and transform him into someone she does not recognise and from whom she has to defend herself. Dores could not hate the Quim of that night, because she saw, beneath the man who frightened her, the boy she had raised, and that impossibility of hating, that love that persisted beneath the fear, was what made everything so harrowing: it would be easier if she could hate, if she could make of her son an enemy and cast him from her heart; but she could not, she loved him, and she had to learn to defend herself from someone she loved, to protect herself from a son without ceasing to love him, and there is no balance harder than that, that of one who has to guard themselves from one they love.

It stayed that Dores was afraid, physically afraid, afraid in her own house, of her own son; it stayed that she shut herself in a room with her heart galloping, waiting for it to pass; it stayed that, when Quim at last went, or at last fell asleep, or the night at last ended, Dores stayed sitting in the dark, trembling, understanding that she had come to a place she had never thought she would come to: to be afraid of a son, to be a hostage at home, to live in dread of the doorbell and the door, she, in the house that was hers, in the home she had made with so much love.

There is a particular solitude in the mother who is afraid of a son, and Dores came to know it that night in all its bitterness. Because to whom does a mother tell that she is afraid of her own son? To whom does she say it, without betraying him, without exposing him, without condemning him in the eyes of others? Dores could not say it to anyone, because to say it was to accuse her son, was to put him into the mouth of the village as a man of whom his own mother is afraid, and her instinct of a lifetime, that of protecting, that of covering, prevented her from exposing him even now, even with fear. And so Dores carried alone that monstrous fear, the fear of a loved son, without being able to share it, without relief, and that solitude of the silent fear was perhaps the worst of all, because the fear that can be said divides itself and eases, and the fear that has to be kept silent stays whole, gnawing within, and Dores had kept it silent for a long time, smiling for the village and trembling at home, defending her son out there and dreading him in here, split in two between the love she showed and the fear she hid.

When at last the night passed, and the house went silent, and Dores dared to leave the room where she had shut herself, she walked through the house in the dark, still trembling, and went on noticing the wreckage that night had left, and each sign was a stab. It does not matter what was in disorder or broken; it matters that Dores, recomposing the house at dawn, alone, with her hands trembling, understood that she was doing what she had done so many times with her husband, years before: recomposing, at dawn, in silence, alone, the harm of a man whom the drink had unhinged, erasing the signs, putting the house in order as though nothing were the matter, as though she could, by tidying the house, tidy also what went on within. And it was the repetition of the gesture, recomposing the house at dawn as she had recomposed her husband's, that gave her, more than anything, the measure of where she had come to: that she was living, with her son, the same night she had lived with his father, redoing the same gestures of distress and of covering-up, caught in the same cycle, condemned, it seemed, to recompose forever the houses that the men she loved unhinged, first the husband, now the son, always she, always at dawn, always alone, always tidying harm that was not hers.

And it was on that night, in the dark, after the fear, that Dores touched the bottom, and the bottom was this perception: that her love for her son, her saving without end, her always-giving, had brought her there, to being afraid of him at home, to being his hostage, to giving what she did not have to a man who frightened her. It was not the son who had brought her there, or not only; it was she, it was her saving, it was the door she always opened, it was the money she always gave, it was the refusal to set a limit, that had created, over the years, this situation in which she lived terrified in her own house of a son who had learned that he could do anything because the mother endured anything. Her love without limits had created a son without limits, and a son without limits, in the shadow of the drink, was a son one could be afraid of, and Dores was, and the fear was the bitter harvest of a life of not knowing how to say no.

The most terrible of that night, and what most marked her, was to discover that she was afraid of a person she loved, and that those two things — the love and the fear — could live at the same time in a mother's breast, directed at the same person, without cancelling each other. Dores had always thought that love kept away fear, that to a mother who loves her son the son cannot give fear; and she discovered, that night, that it was not so, that one could love and dread at the same time, that the love for her son did not prevent her from dreading what the drink made of him, and that to live with those two things together, the love and the fear for the same person, was a particular torture, because the fear told her to flee and the love told her to stay, and she was left split between the two, unable to flee from one she loved nor to love without fear one she dreaded. And it was that splitting, the love and the fear pulling her to opposite sides, that the night put before her with a clarity she could no longer ignore, and it was from that unbearable clarity that there was born, at last, the will to change, because a person can live a long time in a bad situation while they manage not to see it, and ceases to be able to bear it the instant they see it whole, and Dores, that night, saw hers whole.

She thought, that night, of Aniceto, of the husband who had also frightened her, in his time, when he drank, and she understood, with a cold horror, that she was living for the second time the same night, first with the husband and now with the son, a hostage at home of a man she loved and whom the drink transformed, and that her life had been, at bottom, this: two crossings of fear, separated by a short rest, always in the same water, always saving and always dreading, always giving everything to men who sank and who, in sinking, dragged her with them. There was, in the midst of the fear of that night, an instant of a cold lucidity that marked her: on seeing herself shut in a room of her own house, waiting for the danger to pass, Dores thought, with a clarity that froze her, this is not right. She did not think I have to help my son, nor what will become of him; she thought, for the first time, about herself: this is not right, I should not be like this, shut in a room, afraid, in my own house. And it was a new thought, because for the first time in her whole life Dores put herself at the centre of a thought, judged her own situation from the point of view of what she, Dores, deserved or did not deserve, instead of judging it from the point of view of her son. And that small revolution, that of thinking this is not right for me instead of what can I do for my son, was the first sign of a new Dores, the first time her own person entered the reckoning, and it was from it, from that tiny this is not right thought about herself, that all the rest was born, because a person who begins to think that something is not right for them is a person who has begun, at last, to count themselves.

And she asked herself, for the first time, whether she deserved that, whether a life had to be that, whether she, Dores, would not have a right, at least at the end, to a house without fear, to a home where the doorbell was not a threat, to an old age that was not one more crossing of dread.

It cost her, in the days after that night, to look at her son and admit what she felt, because what she felt was a thing a mother does not want to feel: fear, and beneath the fear, shame, and beneath the shame, a touch of a thing resembling anger, and Dores had a horror of that anger, because she thought that a mother cannot have anger at a son, that that was a sin, an unnaturalness. But the anger was there, dull, the anger of one who is treated badly by one they love, the anger of a whole life of giving and being paid with fear, and Dores had to learn, with much difficulty, that that anger did not make her a bad mother, that it was a human and just reaction to an intolerable situation, and that to deny it, to push it down as she had pushed down everything her whole life, only poisoned her more. The anger, she understood slowly, was the part of her that knew that that was not right, that she did not deserve to live with fear at home, that there was a limit there being violated; and instead of condemning it as a sin, perhaps she should hear it as a warning, the warning that she had come to a place where no mother should come and from which she had to get out.

And it was that question, born at the bottom of that night of fear — would I not have a right to a house without fear? —, that began to turn Dores, slowly, from saving toward saving herself, because it was the first time in her life that she put herself into the equation, the first time she thought of her own right to a life without terror, the first time the saviour asked herself whether she did not need, she too, and before all, to be saved — and that perhaps, that time, the only person who could save her was she herself, by shutting at last a door she had opened her whole life.
