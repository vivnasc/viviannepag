## 11. To love from a limit

There is an idea, very widespread, that to love is to have no limits, that true love gives everything, endures everything, forgives everything, and Dores had believed that idea her whole life, and it was that idea she had to unlearn after the bolt, because it was that idea that nearly killed her. Love without limits, she understood, is not the highest love; it is, often, the most dangerous, because a love that endures everything ends up enduring the unendurable, and a love that gives everything ends up giving even one's own life, and a love that forgives everything ends up permitting everything. True love, Dores learned, has limits, not for being less love, but for being more wise: it knows that there are things that should not be endured, that there are requests that should not be satisfied, that there are behaviours to which one has to say no, precisely out of love, because to say yes to everything does not help the one we love, it sinks them. And it was hard, for Dores, to trade the romantic idea of love without limits for the harder and truer idea of love with limits, because the first is beautiful and the second seems, at first sight, cold; but Dores learned, at her own cost, that the beautiful one killed and the cold one saved, and that real love, the one that lasts and the one that helps, is not the one that has no limits, it is the one that knows where to set them.

After the bolt, Dores had to learn a new and hard thing, which was to love her son from a limit, to love him without saving him, to keep him in her heart and outside the door of the drink, and she went on learning it, slowly, in that late spring of her life, with relapses and with firmness, because to set a limit once is hard and to keep it is harder still.

To learn to love from a limit was, for Dores, like learning a new language when old, with an accent, with mistakes, slowly. Everything in her was trained for the other language, that of giving without limit, and she had to watch herself at every moment so as not to fall into it, so as not to go back to the automatic of saving. It was a constant, conscious, tiring effort, because the instinct always pulled toward the old side, and Dores had to correct it time after time, like one who corrects an accent, and sometimes the accent won and she yielded, and had to begin again. But, as in any language, the practice gave her the knack, and what at first was an enormous and conscious effort went on becoming, slowly, a little more natural, and Dores went on learning to say the no without it costing her her life, to set the limit with less trembling, to distinguish the son from the illness more quickly. It never came to be easy, because these things do not become easy; but it became possible, it became livable, and that was already an immense victory for one who had set out from a total inability to say no.

Because Quim did not give up asking at once, of course. He came back, came back several times, with the stories, with the straits, with the requests, testing the bolt, to see whether his mother yielded as she always had, because a whole lifetime had taught him that his mother always yielded. And Dores had to keep the bolt drawn time after time, had to repeat the no many times, had to endure her son's rage, the guilt he threw at her, the accusations of an unnatural mother, of a mother who abandons her son, and it was very hard, because Quim knew where to touch her, knew the words that wounded her, and threw them at her, and Dores had to endure without yielding, clinging to the truth she had learned: that to yield was not love, it was sinking, and that her no, however much it hurt the two of them, was more love than her old yes.

The daughter, Glória, on learning that her mother had at last set the limit, came back, and came back in a way that moved Dores: she came back to support her. She telephoned more, came more, told her mother that she was proud of her, that she knew how much it cost, that she was there to hold her on the days when she wavered. And Dores, who in saving Quim had lost Glória a little, recovered her in shutting the door to Quim, because Glória, who could not bear to see her mother sinking, bore well, and even with joy, to see her mother saving herself, and drew near again. And it was an immense relief for Dores, to have her daughter near again, to have someone to support her, because to keep the limit alone was very hard, and to have Glória on her side, holding her on the bad days, reminding her why she did it, was one of the things that allowed her to endure. And there was here a lesson Dores kept: that in saving the son who sank she was losing the daughter who had got free, and that in stopping saving the one who sank she recovered the one who had got free, and that her sacrifice for Quim had had, all those years, a hidden cost she had never tallied, that of estranging her from Glória, and that that cost, now, in shutting the door, turned into gain.

But at the same time that she kept the bolt shut to the drink, Dores kept, with the same firmness, the door open to her son. She telephoned him, sent word that she loved him, that the door was open for when he wanted to come sober, for when he wanted real help; she never renounced him, never struck him out, never ceased being a mother. The difference, now, is that her love had a new form, a form with a limit: she loved the son, but not the drink; she received the son, but not the request; she had the door open to the person, and the bolt drawn to the harm. Quim, in striking against the shut bolt time after time, went on changing his way of coming, and that was revealing. At first he came with the usual stories and requests, awaiting his mother to yield as always; on seeing that she did not yield, he passed to accusations, to rage, to knives; and then, on seeing that not even the accusations opened the door, he went on drawing away, came less, because a son who came only to ask, on ceasing to get what he asked, ceases to have a reason to come. And there was in this a pain for Dores, that of seeing her son come less, that of understanding that part of what brought him was the request and that, the request shut, the son became rare; but there was also a clarity, that of seeing, at last, without illusions, what their relationship was, reduced to the request, and that of understanding that a son who came only for the mother to yield was not a relationship, it was an exploitation disguised as a visit, and that the hard truth of seeing him come less was better than the lie of having him there asking. And Dores endured this too, to see her son become rare on understanding that the door did not yield, clinging to the hope that, the door of the request shut, perhaps one day the door of the healthy son would open, that of the son who came not to take but to be.

And that form of love, with a limit, was much harder to live than the love without limit of before, because the love without limit was simple, it was to always give, and the love with a limit demanded discernment at every moment, to distinguish the son from the illness, the person from the request, and to keep open one and shut the other, which was a constant and painful exercise, but which was, Dores understood, the only love that did not kill her and that perhaps, only perhaps, helped the son.

There were days when she wavered, when she nearly opened the bolt, when the mother's heart nearly defeated the decision. There was a time when she learned that Quim was in a serious strait, bad in earnest, and that everything in her cried out to attend to him, and Dores wept the whole night clinging to the decision, with the telephone in her hand, and did not yield, and it was perhaps the hardest night after that of the bolt, because one thing is not to give when the request is the usual one, another is not to give when one truly fears for the son. And Dores had to learn that the limit is not a victory won once, it is a choice remade every day, and sometimes many times a day, and that each time it costs, and that firmness is not not feeling the temptation to yield, it is feeling it and not yielding. She went on becoming firmer with time, not because it ceased to hurt, but because she went on accumulating the proof that she endured, that the sky did not fall when she did not yield, that she and her son went on alive on the far side of each no, and that proof, accumulated, went on giving her the serenity that the decision alone did not give.

The village, which had seen her always give and had censured her for it, saw her now set the limit and censured her too, because the village always censures, and what before was oh Dores who always gives to that son became oh Dores who shut the door on her son, poor thing, what a mother. And Dores had to learn to endure this too, the village's censure for the limit, after having endured the censure for the yielding, and she understood a freeing thing: that the village would censure her whatever she did, that there was no way to please the village, that if she gave they censured her and if she did not give they censured her, and that therefore it was not worth governing herself by the village, it was worth governing herself by what was right. And that discovery, that she would never manage to please everyone and that therefore she was free to do what was right without minding what they would say, was one more liberation, because Dores had lived her whole life attentive to what they would say, doing what was expected of a good mother, and to learn to detach herself from what they would say, to do the right thing even censured, was to learn a freedom she had never had, that of one who ceases to live for the approval of others and comes to live for their own conscience.

And there was a change in Dores, as she kept the limit, that the village noticed: she went back to living. She recovered the house, which ceased to be a place of fear, because the bolt drawn to the drink gave her back the safety of the home; she recovered her sleep, which came back when she ceased to live in dread of the doorbell; she recovered the little money she had, which ceased to drain into the bottomless well of the requests; and she recovered, slowly, a life of her own, because a woman who stops drowning saving others discovers, suddenly, that she has time, and strength, and even the will, to live. Dores, who had worn herself out a whole life saving men who sank, discovered, in the end, with the bolt drawn, the strange lightness of a life that was hers, of days that were not of saving nor of fear, of a house that was at last a refuge and not a stage of distress.

She learned too, in that time, to distinguish what was love from what was fear in her own impulses, and it was a fine learning, because the two disguised themselves as each other. When the impulse came to her to yield, to open the bolt, to give, Dores learned to stop and to ask herself: is this love for my son, or is it fear of mine, fear of seeing him suffer, fear of the guilt, fear of losing him? And she discovered, often, that what she took for love was fear, was her own distress wanting relief, and that to yield, in those cases, was not for the good of the son, it was to calm her fear. And she learned that to yield to fear is not to love, it is only to flee the discomfort, and that true love, sometimes, is to endure the discomfort — to endure seeing the son suffer the consequences, to endure the guilt, to endure the fear — in the name of a good greater than the immediate relief. It was a distinction hard to make in the heat of the moment, with the son at the door and the heart leaping, but Dores went on training it, and each time she managed to distinguish the love from the fear and to act out of love and not out of fear, she became a little firmer, a little freer, a little more master of herself.

She thought much, in that time, of Rosalina, and understood that Rosalina, whom the village had called an unnatural mother, had been after all the wisest and most courageous mother she had known, because she had done, before her, what was hardest and most loving: to stop drowning, to set a limit, to love from a bank. There was, in the midst of this learning, a day that was worth many, and it was a day when Quim appeared sober. Not to ask; sober, or almost. He came by day, sober, with a shamefaced air, and stayed at the door without asking anything, and said to his mother some awkward words, that were not a request for money nor a story, they were another thing, they were perhaps the beginning of a thing. And Dores, who had the bolt drawn to the drink and to the asking, opened the door wide, because it was exactly this the son to whom the door was always open, the sober son, the son who came to talk and not to ask. And she received him, and gave him food, and they were, mother and son, a few moments as they had not been in years, with no request in between, with no fear, only a mother and a son at table. It was not a cure, it was not a promise, Quim would sink again perhaps many times still; but it was a glimpse, it was the proof that the bolt drawn to the drink had not shut the son, it had perhaps opened a chink through which, now and then, the sober son could peer, and Dores clung to that day, kept it, because it was the living proof that to shut the door to the drink and to open it to the sober son was not a contradiction, it was the most sensible and most loving thing she could do, and that her limit, far from driving the son away, was perhaps what made possible these rare days of a sober son at her table.

And she understood that the village was mistaken in calling love the drowning and unlove the limit, because it was the contrary: the drowning, the always-giving, the saving without end, had as much of fear and of need as of love, and the limit, the bolt, the no that cost, was a higher love, a love that put the true good of the son — the bottom that perhaps would save him — above the immediate relief of the mother in seeing him sheltered. Dores had learned, in the end, to love so, in the hard way, and though it cost her every day, she knew, now, that it was the right way, and that her old love, the one without limit, however well-intentioned, had been a love that killed.
