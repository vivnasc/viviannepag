## 9. The bottom

Dores had never been a person to give up, and her difficulty in shutting the door did not come from weakness, it came, paradoxically, from her greatest strength: the stubbornness of one who does not give up on anyone. Her whole life she had been the one who endured, the one who stayed to the end, and that stubbornness, which had been her greatness in raising her children alone and in holding the house in her husband's time, was now her trap, because the same stubbornness that had made her never give up was the one that prevented her from giving up on saving her son. And she was going to have to do, with her greatest strength, a thing that went against her own self: to stop not giving up, which for a woman who had made of not-giving-up her identity was almost like ceasing to be herself.

But between deciding and doing there is a path, and before Dores drew the bolt there was still her own bottom, the days when, determined to stop, she discovered how hard it was to stop, and when she almost retreated.

She had left the House of the Register determined, with the Keeper's strength in her breast; but the decision did not hold firm in the days that followed. In the morning, rested, it seemed to her right; at night it seemed to her impossible. And it was on one of those nights, the third or the fourth after the Keeper had spoken to her, that Dores nearly yielded for good.

It had been a bad day, with news of Quim arriving by the village's means, that he was worse, that he had got into a serious strait. And at night Dores found herself in the kitchen, with the telephone in her hand, dialling half her son's number to tell him that after all she would give, that after all she would open, that after all she could not. She dialled the first numbers, slowly, and stopped, with her heart leaping and her finger over the next digit, and stayed so, suspended, perhaps a minute, perhaps five, with the telephone beeping softly on the other side awaiting the rest of the number. And it was in that minute, suspended between yielding and enduring, that she understood the size of the prison she lived in: that the fear of a disaster of her son's held her hostage, that that fear would make her give forever. She laid down the telephone. She did not lay it down for good, she laid it on the table, there at hand, and stayed looking at it, and went to fetch a chair and sat down in front of the telephone, as one who keeps watch on an enemy, and spent the rest of the night sitting there, guarding the telephone from herself, and that was the first thing she did alone, at her bottom: to guard herself from her own finger.

The next day, still with the night of the chair in her eyes, she went to Rosalina's house, because she could no longer bear it alone. She knocked, and Rosalina opened, and Dores, at the door, without going in, said to her I cannot, Rosalina, I am afraid he will do himself a disaster if I shut the door. How did you manage? And Rosalina pulled her inside, sat her at the table, set a coffee in front of her, and told her the hardest and most freeing truth of the whole story: Dores, he can do himself a disaster even if you go on giving, and he can straighten out even if you shut the door, because what he does does not depend on you, it depends on him. His life is not in your hands, it is in his, and that is the hardest thing to accept. You can give him everything and he sink; you can shut the door on him and he save himself; or the contrary; you do not know, no one knows. What you decide is whether you sink with him or whether you stay alive. Only that is in your hands: your life, not his. And if you shut the door and he, even so, sinks, the fault is not yours, Dores, because the illness is his and the choice to treat it is his, and you are not God to save him against his will.

And Dores, on the other side of the cup, heard that, and said, with her voice hoarse, but how do I endure it, Rosalina, on the nights? Last night I sat the whole night keeping watch on the telephone so as not to call him. And Rosalina got up, went to the drawer of the dresser, took out a paper and a pencil, and laid them in front of Dores, and said, write. Write here the reasons why you are shutting the door, in your own hand, and have this paper at hand, in your apron, on the bedside table, wherever, and on the nights when your heart cries out at you to open, you take out the paper and read it, to remember, with your head, what your heart makes you forget. It is what saved me. I had my paper pinned on the inside of the pantry door, and when I went there, without meaning to, with my hand on the way to the shelf, I came face to face with the paper and stopped. And Dores took the pencil, and wrote, slowly, by her own hand, Rosalina dictating nothing to her, only waiting: that to give does not save him, it sinks him; that in saving him I take from him the bottom that perhaps would save him; that I have a right to a house without fear; that I am not responsible for his illness; that a drowned mother saves no one; that the door is open to the sober son. And she folded the paper in four, and put it in the pocket of her apron, and went away with it, and had it at hand from then on, and read it on the nights of weakness, and it was an anchor to her, a voice of the head holding her when the heart wanted to drag her.

But the reckoning of the head was not enough, and Dores knew it, because the head said one thing and the mother's heart cried out another, and in the moments of distress it was the heart that commanded. And there was, at Dores's bottom, a thing that made it harder to cross: the solitude of the decision. Because the decision to shut the door was hers, hers alone, and no one would be at the door with her on the night she had to keep to it.

It was Glória who eased it for her, without knowing it, on a night when she telephoned. Dores was at the table, with Rosalina's paper open before her, reading it for the umpteenth time, when the telephone rang, and it was her daughter. Mother, said Glória, I know what you are trying to do, Rosalina told me, and I called only to tell you that I am proud of you, and that you are not alone. And Dores, who had never told her daughter a naked truth in all her life, said to her, softly, I am sitting here with a paper of Rosalina's in front of me, Glória, so as not to go to the telephone and call your brother to tell him I'll give. For an hour I have done nothing but not call. And on the other side there fell a silence different from all the silences there had ever been between the two of them, and then Glória's voice came, changed, hoarse: stay there, Mother, with the paper in front of you, and do not hang up, I'll stay on the telephone with you until the urge passes. And they stayed, mother and daughter, on the telephone, talking of nothing, of the rain, of the grandchildren, of a recipe, and Glória laughed almost weeping, look at the two of us, Mother, years arguing, and after all this was what I wanted, to be on the telephone with you while you do not call Quim. And Dores, that night, at the third bottom of a distress, let herself be held by her daughter, and it was the first time in her life that a woman who had worn herself out saving others let someone save her a little, and it was so strange and so good that she understood how much she had lived without it.

Glória said to her still, before hanging up, already at daybreak, a thing Dores kept: he gets out of it, Mother, or he does not, but that is no longer in your hands. It is you who never saw him get himself out, because you never gave him time. You always arrived first. And when they hung up, there was already light behind the serra, and the telephone stayed on the table, silent, and Dores had not called him.

It was not the only night. There were others, and on one of them, of the worst, Dores did the sums of her life, and the sums shook her. She put herself at the table, with a paper, not Rosalina's, another, and began to add up, more or less, how much she had given Quim over the years. She did not know for certain, no one adds up these things while they give them, but she went on pulling from her memory, a debt here, a rent there, the loan she had taken on for herself, her mother's earrings she had sold, and the number that came out at the bottom of the paper was enormous, was almost all she had had in a lifetime. And alongside, in another column, she tried to add up what all that money had given the son, and the answer was nothing, or worse than nothing, because it had all been to feed what destroyed him. And in a third column, the hardest, she tried to add up what she had given herself in those same years, what she had spent on herself, on her rest, on a thing of her own, and she was left with the pencil in the air, because there was nothing to add up, the column stayed blank, and to see, so, the column of herself blank beside the column of the son full, was for Dores a balance-sheet of a life, and a bitter balance-sheet, that of a woman who had given everything to one who was not saved and nothing to herself, and who came to the end with empty hands and an empty earring case in a drawer. And it was perhaps that paper of the three columns, cold and clear, more than anything else, that pushed her toward the decision, because it showed her, in numbers, that the path she had walked led nowhere but to the ruin of two, and that it was necessary, at last, to change, if only so that one of them, she, might be saved.

No one saw this combat, because, as always, Dores hid everything. From outside, the village saw a woman who got by, who did her duty, who did not complain; no one imagined her nights, the chair in front of the telephone, Rosalina's paper in her apron, the three columns. And it was Rosalina, once more, who taught her this too: that she did not have to wage it alone, that she could lean on someone. She came to see her, in the late afternoon, with a pretext in her hand, a bunch of cabbages to exchange, a recipe, and she sat down, and drank the coffee, and kept watch on her friend over the cup for half an hour of no conversation at all, and on the way out, already at the gate, she said only, with no great thing, you are doing well, Dores. And that it costs, I know too. If you need company to do nothing, I'll do nothing with you. And she went away, and Dores stayed at the gate understanding, slowly, that she had just been sheltered, she, for the first time, and that to receive that, instead of giving it, was in its small way the first time she let someone save her a little, and she began to undo, without noticing it, the oldest knot of all, that of her being only to give and never to receive.

Rosalina's words, his life is not in your hands, it is in his, were the hardest to accept of the whole story, because they went against the deepest feeling of a mother, that of being responsible for the child. A mother feels, to the bone, that the child's life falls to her; and that feeling, just while the child is a child, becomes a trap when the child is an adult who makes their own choices. And Dores had to make the hardest crossing of all, that of passing from a mother of a child, responsible for it, to a mother of an adult, whom she loves but does not control, and that crossing, that of letting go of the responsibility for the adult child, goes against thirty, forty years of instinct. But it was the one she had to make, because to go on feeling herself responsible for the choices of an adult was to condemn herself to an infinite guilt, and was, at bottom, not to acknowledge the son as an adult, not to give him back the dignity of his own life.

And Dores, hearing Rosalina and Glória, understood at last that she carried a responsibility that was not hers, that of her son's fate, and that that impossible responsibility was what crushed her. To let it go, to accept that she was not responsible for his choice, that she could not save him against his will, that she could only save herself, was the hardest and most freeing step of her bottom, because it was to lay down a weight she had carried since girlhood, that of thinking herself responsible for saving everyone. And in laying it down, in the midst of the fear and the pain, with the paper of reasons in the pocket of her apron and the column of herself still to be filled, Dores felt a thing new and light: the possibility of a life that was not to drown saving others, the possibility, at last, of staying alive.
