## 9. The bottom

It should be said that Dores had never been a person to give up, and that her difficulty in shutting the door did not come from weakness, it came, paradoxically, from her greatest strength: the stubbornness of one who does not give up on anyone. Her whole life she had been the one who did not give up, the one who endured, the one who stayed to the end, and that stubbornness, which had been her greatness in raising her children alone and in holding the house in her husband's time, was now her trap, because the same stubbornness that had made her never give up was the one that prevented her from giving up on saving her son. And it was one of the hardest things for Dores to understand, at the bottom: that sometimes the virtue that saved us in one phase of life is what binds us in another, that the stubbornness of not giving up, so good for raising children, was bad for letting them face the consequences, and that she was going to have to do, with her greatest strength, a thing that went against her own self: to give up, not on the son, but on saving him, to let go, not the love, but the control, to stop not giving up, which for a woman who had made of not-giving-up her identity was almost like ceasing to be herself.

But between deciding and doing there is a path, and before Dores drew the bolt there was still her own bottom, there were the days when, determined to stop, she discovered how hard it was to stop, and when she almost retreated, because the fear of not saving her son was, in Dores, as deep as the instinct to breathe.

It should be said that the decision, taken at the House of the Register with the Keeper giving her strength, did not hold firm in the days that followed; it wavered, oscillated, almost came apart many times, because a decision taken in a moment of clarity has to be remade in each moment of weakness, and the moments of weakness, for Dores, were many. In the morning, rested, with a cool head, the decision seemed to her right and possible; at night, or when she thought of her son, or when she imagined what could happen to him, the decision wavered, and Dores almost telephoned to say that after all she would give. And it was that coming-and-going, between the clarity and the weakness, that made the days of the decision so hard, because it was not enough to have decided once, it was necessary to re-decide every hour, against a heart that changed its mind according to the fear, and Dores understood that it was going to be always so, that to set a limit was not a decision one takes and it stays, it was a decision one remakes every day, and sometimes many times a day, against the current of one's own heart, and that firmness was not not wavering, it was wavering and going back to the decision, wavering and going back, as many times as needed.

She had decided, at the House of the Register, that she gave no more, that she set the limit, that she shut the door to the drink and to the asking. But to decide at the House of the Register, with the Keeper giving her strength, was one thing; to keep to it at home, alone, when Quim rang the doorbell with his story and his strait and his if my mother does not help me a disaster will befall me, was another. And Dores spent the days that followed in a combat with herself, between the decision of the head and the terror of the heart, between what she knew she had to do and the fear of what would happen to her son if she did it.

Because that was the knot of Dores's bottom: the fear that, in stopping saving her son, she would be responsible for his disaster. And if he, without her money, did something mad? And if he ended up in the street? And if the worst befell him, and she, afterward, had to live with the guilt of not having given, of having shut the door, of having let him fall? That fear, that of being the cause of her son's disaster by not saving him, was what had bound her her whole life, what had made her always give, and it was a terrible fear, because it played with the deepest thing of a mother, the responsibility for her child, and Dores, alone at home in the days of the decision, was many times nearly giving up, nearly concluding that it was safer to go on giving, that at least in giving she had the conscience of having done everything, and that the alternative, to shut the door and risk a disaster, was too much for her heart to bear.

There was a night, in those days of the decision, when Dores nearly yielded for good, when she was on the point of telephoning her son to say that after all she would give, that after all she would open, that after all she could not, so great was the terror of letting him fall. She took the telephone, dialled half the number, and stopped, with her heart leaping, and it was in that moment, suspended between yielding and enduring, that she understood the size of the prison she lived in: that the fear of a disaster of her son's held her hostage, that that fear would make her give forever, that while the fear commanded her she would never be free nor would the son have a limit. And she understood that she had to choose between two fears: the fear of a disaster of her son's if she shut the door, and the fear of a whole life, hers and his, sinking if she did not shut it. Both gave fear; but one was a risk, and the other was a certainty, because to go on giving guaranteed the sinking of both, whereas to shut the door was only a risk, with a chance, at least, of another end. And it was that reckoning, cold and terrible, between the risk and the certainty, that helped her not to yield that night, to lay down the telephone, to endure.

But the reckoning of the head was not enough, and Dores knew it, because the head said one thing and the mother's heart cried out another, and in the moments of distress it was the heart that commanded, not the head. She needed more than a reckoning; she needed an anchor, something to cling to when the heart cried out to yield. And it was Rosalina who gave it to her, in telling her to make a list, written, of the reasons why she was shutting the door, and to have it at hand, and to read it in the moments of weakness, to remember, with the head, what the heart made her forget. And Dores made the list, wrote, by her own hand, the reasons: that to give does not save him, it sinks him; that in saving him I take from him the bottom that perhaps would save him; that I have a right to a house without fear; that I am not responsible for his illness; that a drowned mother saves no one; that the door is open to the healthy son. And she had that list at hand, and read it on the nights of weakness, and it was an anchor to her, a voice of the head holding her when the heart wanted to drag her, and it was also that, a simple list written by her own hand, that helped her to endure, because in the moments when we forget our reasons it is precious to have them written, by hand, to reread them and remember who we are when the distress makes us forget.

And it was Rosalina, again, who held her at the bottom, because Dores, in the despair of the decision, went to her, went to ask her for help, went to tell her I cannot, Rosalina, I am afraid he will do himself a disaster if I shut the door, how did you manage. And Rosalina told her the hardest and most freeing truth: Dores, he can do himself a disaster even if you go on giving, and he can straighten out even if you shut the door, because what he does does not depend on you, it depends on him, and that is the hardest thing to accept, that his life is not in your hands. You can give him everything and he sink; you can shut the door on him and he save himself; or the contrary; you do not know, no one knows, because it is not you who decide his fate, it is he. What you decide is whether you sink with him or whether you stay alive. Only that is in your hands: your life, not his. And if you shut the door and he, even so, sinks, the fault is not yours, Dores, because the illness is his and the choice to treat it is his, and you are not God to save him against his will. Free yourself of that guilt that is not yours, and stay alive, which is the only thing you can still do, for yourself and, who knows, for him.

And there was, at Dores's bottom, a thing that made it harder still to cross: the solitude of the decision. Because the decision to shut the door was hers, hers alone, and no one could take it for her, and no one would be at the door with her on the night she had to keep to it. Rosalina's and the Keeper's counsel gave her strength, but in the decisive moment, with the son at the door, she would be alone, and the weight would be hers alone. And that solitude frightened Dores as much as the decision itself, because she, who had worn herself out a lifetime at the service of someone, found herself now before the most solitary thing one can do, to decide alone to shut the door to a son. But she went on learning, at the bottom, that there are decisions that are truly only ours, that no one can take for us, and that to take them, however solitary, is what makes us masters of our life, and that the solitude of the decision was, after all, the other face of a good thing: that her life was hers to decide, at last, and not others' to dictate to her.

It should be said that no one saw this combat of Dores's, because, as always, she hid everything. From outside, the village saw a woman who got by, who did her duty, who did not complain; no one imagined her nights, the telephone in her hand, the struggle not to yield, the terror of a disaster of her son's, the guilt, the doubt. Dores waged this combat alone, as she had waged all, because to ask for help was a thing she did not know how to do, and it was Rosalina, once more, who taught her this too: that she did not have to wage it alone, that she could lean on someone, that there were people who had been through the same and who could hold her. And it was perhaps that, the discovery that she could be helped, one of the most important of Dores's bottom, because a woman who had worn herself out a lifetime helping others and who had never let them help her, in letting herself at last be supported by Rosalina, began to undo, without noticing it, the oldest knot of all, that of her being only to give and never to receive, and to receive Rosalina's support was, in its small way, the first time Dores let someone save her a little, and it was so strange and so good that she understood how much she had lived without it.

Rosalina's words — his life is not in your hands, it is in his — were, perhaps, the hardest to accept of the whole story, because they went against the deepest feeling of a mother, that of being responsible for her child, that of the child being hers to protect. A mother feels, to the bone, that her child's life falls to her, that if something happens to them the fault is hers, and that feeling, which is just and good while the child is a child, becomes a trap when the child is an adult who makes their own choices, because a mother cannot be responsible for the choices of an adult, however much she loves them, however much they are her child. And Dores had to make the hardest crossing of all, that of passing from a mother of a child, responsible for them, to a mother of an adult, whom she loves but does not control, for whom she worries but does not answer for their choices, and that crossing, that of letting go of the responsibility for the adult child, is one of the hardest there is, because it goes against thirty, forty years of being responsible, and against the deepest instinct of a mother, which is to protect the offspring. But it was the crossing she had to make, because to go on feeling herself responsible for the choices of an adult was to condemn herself to an infinite guilt and weight, and was, at bottom, not to acknowledge the son as an adult, not to give him back the dignity and the responsibility of his own life.

And Dores, hearing Rosalina, understood at last that she carried a responsibility that was not hers, that of her son's fate, and that that impossible responsibility was what crushed her and what prevented her from shutting the door. There was, at that bottom, a night when Dores did the sums of her life, and the sums gave her a thing that shook her. She calculated, more or less, how much she had given her son over the years, in money, and the number was enormous, was almost everything she had had; and she calculated what all that money had given the son, and the answer was nothing, or worse than nothing, because it had all been to feed what destroyed him. And she calculated then another thing, harder to put into numbers: how much she had given herself, over those same years, how much she had spent on herself, on her rest, on her peace, on her life, and the answer was almost zero, because everything had gone to the son, to the husband before him, to others always. And to see, so, in sums, that she had given everything to one who was not saved and nothing to herself, was for Dores a kind of balance-sheet of a life, and a bitter balance-sheet, that of a woman who had worn herself out entire saving others and who came to the end with empty hands, without having saved anyone and without having given herself anything, and it was perhaps that balance-sheet, cold and clear, that pushed her toward the decision, because it showed her that the path she had walked led nowhere but to the ruin of two, and that it was necessary, at last, to change paths, if only so that one of the two, she, might be saved.

To let it go, to accept that she was not responsible for the son's choice, that she could not save him against his will, that she could only save herself, was the hardest and most freeing step of her bottom, because it was to lay down a weight she had carried since girlhood, that of thinking herself responsible for saving everyone, and in laying it down Dores felt, in the midst of the fear and the pain, a thing new and light: the possibility of a life that was not to drown saving others, the possibility, at last, of staying alive.
