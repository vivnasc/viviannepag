## 2. The husband who drank

To understand Dores one must go back to the husband, to Aniceto, because the son she now saved was the second time Dores made that crossing, and the first she had made with his father, and it was with Aniceto that Dores learned, as an adult, what she already carried from girlhood: that her place in the world was to save those who were sinking.

She had met Aniceto young, and loved him, it is just to say so, because it would be easy to tell this story as though Aniceto had always been only the drink and the shadow, and he had not. There had been an Aniceto Dores had fallen in love with, a man with charm, with a knack, with a way of making her laugh, and Dores kept that Aniceto somewhere beneath all the bad years, as she kept the boy Quim beneath the Quim of now. And perhaps it was for that, for having loved the man beneath the drink, that Dores endured so much, because a woman does not endure years on end a man who drinks if she has not loved, beneath, the man he was; she endures out of love for that man, in the hope that he will come back, that the drink will let go of him and return him. And that hope, that the loved man would come back from within the drink, was what bound Dores to Aniceto as it was, afterward, what bound her to Quim: the hope, always put off and always renewed, that the loved one would save himself and come back, a hope that made her endure one more day, and one more, and one more, awaiting a return that, in Aniceto's case, never came.

Aniceto drank. He had always drunk, since before they married, but Dores, young, had thought it a thing of a young man, that it would settle with the marriage, with the children, with life; and it did not settle, it worsened, and Dores found herself, still young, married to a man who drank, and she did what seemed to her the duty of a good wife and what she had learned to be love: she held on. She covered for her husband. She excused him to the family, to the neighbours, to the boss when he missed work. She erased the harm, paid the debts the drink made, kept the house standing and the children fed at the cost of what she saved where she could, and she did all this in silence, without complaining, because to complain was to expose her husband, and to expose her husband was to fail in her duty to protect him, and Dores had been raised to protect, not to expose.

They were hard years, Aniceto's, and Dores crossed them with a resistance she herself did not know she had, because it is astonishing what a person endures when they convince themselves that it is their duty to endure. She worked, raised the children, held the house, and at the same time managed a husband who drank, with everything that brings: the nights of waiting without knowing at what hour he would come nor how; the days after, of bowed head and silence; his promises that he would stop, repeated and broken so many times that they ceased to mean anything; the contained shame before the village; the money that was always lacking because a part went on the drink. And Dores did not complain, or complained little and low, because she had grown up thinking that a woman endures what falls to her, and what fell to her, she thought, was that, a husband who drank and a house to hold, and it fell to her to hold it, cost what it might, and she held it.

And above all she protected the children. Her greatest effort, in Aniceto's years, was that the children would not see, or would see as little as possible, that they would grow up with the illusion of a normal house, of a normal father, and Dores wore herself out in that, in pretending, in covering up, in setting the table as though nothing were the matter on the nights when Aniceto came home in a bad way, in sending the children to bed before he arrived, in building around them a rampart of normality that it exhausted her to keep standing. She thought she protected them so. And she did, in part; but she did not see, then, a thing she would only understand much later: that the children saw all the same, that children always see, and that what they learned, in seeing their mother cover for and save their father a whole lifetime, was exactly the lesson that would lose Quim — that in a family someone drinks and someone saves, that that is the arrangement, that that is how things are.

The children were two, Quim and Glória, and it is revealing how they drew opposite lessons from the same house. The two grew up seeing their mother save their father, cover for him, endure; and Quim drew from that, without deciding it, that a man could sink because there was always a woman to save him, and Glória drew that a woman sacrifices herself for the men of the house, and each carried their lesson into life, Quim into his sinking awaiting to be saved, Glória into her own tendency to sacrifice herself. Not that the house had condemned them; but it had given each one a mould, and it took, for each one, a struggle against theirs not to fulfil it. Glória, far from Véspera, had struggled against hers and got free in part; Quim, in his father's shadow, had lost the struggle and fulfilled the mould. And Dores, in seeing her two children, understood, with pain, that part of what Quim was came from there, from the house she had kept, from the example she had given without wishing it — a woman saving a man who drank —, an example the son had learned to repeat, only on the side of the one who drinks.

What Dores never forgave herself, at bottom, and what she only learned with the story to see in another way, was not having managed to save Aniceto. Because she tried, tried everything, gave everything, and he died all the same, and Dores was left, after his death, with a dull guilt, the guilt of not having been able, of not having done enough, of having let him die despite all her devotion. And that guilt, that of not having saved her husband, was perhaps one of the things that made her throw herself with such force into the saving of her son: as though she wanted, in the son, to achieve what she had failed in the husband, as though Quim's salvation could redeem the defeat with Aniceto. But it was a trap, that, and Dores would only see it much later: that of wanting to save the son to compensate for not having saved the husband, that of putting upon Quim the weight of a redemption that was not his, that of transforming the love for her son into a combat against an old guilt. And while she did not see this, while she carried the guilt of not having saved her husband, Dores was condemned to try to save her son with the desperation of one who plays a second and last game, and desperation, in saving, is a bad counsellor, because it makes one give without measure and without judgement, and that is what Dores did, she gave without measure to her son, in part to silence the guilt of not having saved his father.

Aniceto died of it, in the end, as those die who drink without stopping, slowly and then suddenly, and Dores cared for him until the last day, saved him to where there was no longer salvation, and when he died she wept for him with a tired love and felt, beneath the pain, a thing she was ashamed of: a relief, the relief of one who lays down a weight they have carried too many years. And she thought, then, naively, that her crossing was over, that she had borne her cross, that from then on she would have a lighter old age. She did not know that the crossing was going to begin again, and with the son, because Quim, who had grown up seeing his mother save his father, had become a man who drank like his father, and who was going to need to be saved like his father, and who was going to find in his mother, trained by a whole lifetime, the saviour ready to begin again.

There was, between Aniceto's death and Quim's sinking, an interval, a few years of rest, and Dores recalled them now as the only time of her adult life in which she had not had to save anyone, and she understood, in recalling them, a thing that disquieted her: that even in that time of rest she had not quite known what to do with herself. Accustomed to a life of saving, when there was no one to save Dores felt herself, strange as it seems, a little lost, without a course, as though something were lacking, and looking back she understood that that emptiness, that lack of a sinking one to shelter, had perhaps predisposed her to throw herself with such readiness into the salvation of her son when he began to sink, because the saving gave her again a course, a place, a reason. Not that she wanted her son to sink, of course not; but when he sank, a part of her, the part trained to save, found its function again, and Dores gave herself to it with a readiness that came not only from love but also from that old need to have someone to save, to be again the indispensable one, the saviour, because it was in that role that she knew how to exist.

It was an uncomfortable truth, and Dores would only come to it much later, but it was there: that part of what made her save was not the good of the other, it was her need to save, the hunger of a girl who had learned that she was only worth something if she carried someone. And while she did not see this, while she thought she saved only out of love, Dores was condemned to repeat the furrow, to look for sinking ones to shelter, to make of her life a succession of rescues that consumed her, because what moved her was not only love for others, it was also a wound of her own that only the saving seemed to silence, and a wound is not cured by feeding it, however much feeding it brings momentary relief.

It is hard to say when Quim's sinking began, because these things begin slowly, without a date, and when the family notices them they already come from far. There were a few years when Quim drank as many drink, without it seeming an illness; then there were a few when he drank too much, and the family said it would settle; and when it was understood that it did not settle, that it was like his father, it already came from far, it was already installed. Dores watched it arrive, that sinking, with her heart tightening, because she recognised the signs, she had seen them in her husband, and to have to see them again, now in her son, was one of the cruellest things of her life, like watching an illness arrive that had already taken someone from her, knowing what was coming and powerless to halt it. And perhaps it was for recognising the signs, for having already made that crossing with her husband, that Dores threw herself with such readiness into saving her son: because she knew what was coming, and wanted, that time, with the experience of her husband, to achieve what she had failed, to save, and she threw herself into it with the desperation of one who plays the second and last game of a game they have already lost once.

And Dores began again, without even deciding, because it was what she knew how to do. When Quim began to sink, Dores did with him what she had done with his father: covered, excused, paid, saved. It was automatic, it was the furrow of a lifetime, it was the only response she knew to the sinking of a man she loved: to place herself between him and the harm and absorb it. Dores's children were two, Quim and a daughter, Glória, who left Véspera young and made her life far away, and Glória, unlike Quim, had not inherited her father's thirst but had inherited another thing, her mother's tendency to sacrifice herself, and that Dores saw with grief, because she recognised in her daughter her own furrow. But Glória was far away, and saw little of what went on between her mother and Quim, and when she came, and saw, she scolded her mother, mother, you have to stop giving him money, do you not see you are sinking him, and Dores heard from her daughter what she heard from Rosalina, but from her daughter it cost her more, because it was the daughter telling her she was doing harm to the son, and there was in that an added pain, that of the two children, the one who sank and the one who watched from outside, each weighing on her in their own way. Glória, in fact, had drawn away a little because of this, because it cost her to see her mother consume herself saving her brother and she could not change her, and that drawing-away of the daughter was another price Quim's salvation charged Dores: while she wore herself out saving the son who sank, she went on losing, in another way, the daughter who had got free, who could not bear to see her mother in that and who went on drawing back. And only much later would Dores see that, in shutting the door to Quim, she would also recover Glória, because the daughter, on seeing her mother at last putting herself to safety, would draw near again, relieved at no longer having to watch, powerless, her mother's sinking.

And only much later, with the help of the story that was going to happen to her, would Dores understand that she was not only saving her son, she was repeating with her son the role she had learned with her father and trained with her husband, the role of saviour, the role of one who wears themselves out sheltering those who sink, and that that role, far from saving anyone, had consumed her first with her husband and consumed her now with her son, and that perhaps, only perhaps, it itself, the role of saviour, was part of the problem and not of the solution.

But that was for the end. At the beginning, with Quim as before with Aniceto, there was only the love and the duty, and Dores gave herself to saving her son with the same devotion with which she had given herself to saving her husband, without seeing that she was entering, for the second time in her life, the same river that had nearly drowned her the first, and that this time, older, more worn, with less strength, the river might carry her off for good.
