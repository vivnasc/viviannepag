## 2. The husband who drank

To understand Dores one must go back to the husband, to Aniceto, because the son she now saved was the second time Dores made that crossing, and the first she had made with his father.

There was, in the chest of drawers in the bedroom, inside a sewing box lined with a cloth long since drained of colour, a small portrait from when they married, and Dores still took it out from there, now and then, in the afternoon, alone, and what she saw in the portrait was not the Aniceto of the drink, it was the other one, the one from before, the one from the day he made her laugh at the chapel door to the point that the priest had to cough to call them to order. Because there had been that Aniceto, and Dores kept him as she kept the boy Quim, beneath all the bad years, and it was that Aniceto she had loved, a man with charm, with a way of resting his arm on the windowsill and saying to her come and see this, woman, it's already gone for me but for you it may still come, over nothing, over a swallow, over a cloud with the shape of a thing. And perhaps it was for having loved that man beneath the drink that Dores endured what she endured, because a woman does not endure years on end a man who drinks if she has not loved, beneath, the man he was; she endures out of the hope, always put off and always renewed, that the drink will let go of him and return him to her, and that hope, in Aniceto's case, was what bound her as, afterward, it bound her to Quim.

Aniceto had drunk since before they married, and Dores, young, had thought it a thing of a young man, that it would settle with the house, with the children. She remembered a night, in the early times, when he came home late and she waited for him at the window, and when he came in, with that walk, and made his excuses with his companions, with the cold, with the road, Dores said nothing, made him his supper, and that was the first time of a whole life of suppers made at the wrong hours, though she did not then know that it was the first of so many. The next day she went to the shop, and Benvinda, who was young like her, and who did not yet have the ledger of debts under the counter but already had the eye, lowered her voice and said to her, without Dores having asked her anything, Dores, your man stayed at Lúcio's last night till closing. And Dores, who was meant to keep quiet, answered what she would answer her whole life: he's tired, Benvinda, it's the work. And Benvinda looked at her over the bread she was setting out, and said no more, but Dores carried that look home, and it was the first time she covered for her husband in front of someone, and she did it without thinking, because she already carried from girlhood that her place was that, to put herself between her own and the shame.

And she went on covering. She excused Aniceto to the family, to the neighbours, to the boss of the sawmill when he missed work on a Monday morning, and it was she who went, in her apron, to tell Senhor Firmino that Aniceto had woken with a fever, and Senhor Firmino, who knew, pretended to believe, out of respect for her, and Dores saw in the man's pretending that he knew, and blushed, and went off quickly. She erased the harm, paid the debts the drink opened, and kept the house standing and the children fed at the cost of what she saved where she could, and she did all this in silence, because to complain was to expose her husband, and to expose her husband was to fail in the duty to protect him, and Dores had been raised to protect, not to expose.

And above all she protected the children. Her greatest effort, in Aniceto's years, was that they would not see, or would see as little as possible. She sent them to bed early, on the nights when the air carried the foreboding, and told them stories on the stairs so that they would not hear the key missing the lock down below; she set the table in the morning as though nothing were the matter, with the bread cut and the milk steaming, and on the worst mornings she sang, softly, some little song, to fill the kitchen with a thing that was not the laden silence. She thought she protected them so. And she did, in part; but she did not see, then, what she would only understand much later: that the children saw all the same, that children always see, and that what they learned, in watching their mother cover for and save their father a whole lifetime, was the lesson that would lose Quim, that in a house someone drinks and someone saves, and that that is how things are.

She remembered one morning, in particular, that stayed with her and that only now, with the story working on her, she understood. Quim would have been seven or eight, and Aniceto had come home in the middle of the night in a bad way, and tripped on the dish bucket, and made a noise, and in the morning there was a broken chair in the corner of the kitchen. And Dores, before the children came down, leaned the broken chair against the wall, in such a way as to make it look whole, and when Quim came down and looked at the chair, Dores said, quickly, don't lean on that one, it's wobbly, son, and Quim looked at the chair and looked at his mother, and Dores saw, in the boy's eyes, that he had heard everything in the night, and that he knew the chair did not wobble, it had broken; and that he was learning, right there, at seven or eight years old, to look at a broken chair and call it a wobbly chair. It was so, without words, that the son learned the trade of covering up, and Dores taught it to him in the pretence of protecting him.

The children were two, Quim and Glória, and it is revealing how they drew opposite lessons from the same house. Glória was the elder, and was the first to help her mother in the pretending; Quim was the little one, and was the one who kept the father most within him. There was an afternoon, years later, Glória married now and living far away, when she came to spend a few days, and the two of them were folding sheets in the kitchen, and Glória, out of nowhere, with the sheet stretched between the two of them, said: Mother, do you know what I most want to tell you and never have? That I fled Véspera not because of my father. It was because of you. It was so as not to be you. And Dores was left with the sheet in her hands, and Glória went on, with her voice trembling a little but firm, I watched you save him, Mother, every night, and I swore to myself that I would never marry a man who needed saving, because I did not want your life. And Quim stayed, Mother. Quim stayed and wanted my father's life. We both drew lessons from this house, he and I, and they were opposite. And Dores folded the sheet to the end, slowly, without answering, because there was no answer, and because the daughter had said, in three sentences, at the edge of an unmade bed, what the village had taken thirty years not to say.

What Dores never forgave herself was not having managed to save Aniceto. She tried everything, gave everything, and he died all the same, and Dores was left, afterward, with a dull guilt, that of not having been able, of not having done enough. And it was perhaps that guilt that made her throw herself with such force into the saving of her son, as though she wanted, in Quim, to achieve what she had failed in the husband, as though the salvation of one could redeem the defeat with the other. But it was a trap, and Dores would only see it much later.

Aniceto died of it, in the end, as those die who drink without stopping, slowly and then suddenly. Dores cared for him until the last day, and on the eve of his death, when he could barely speak, he asked her for water, and she gave it to him by the spoon, and he gripped her wrist with a strength he should no longer have had and said to her a thing Dores kept and never told anyone: you saved me my whole life, Dores, and look what it served me for. You should have let me fall once, to see if I learned to get up. And he shut his eyes, and Dores was left with the spoon in her hand, and did not understand, then, what the man had said to her; she thought it was the fever speaking, or the repentance, and only years later, with the son on the other side of a shut door, did that sentence come back to her whole, said to the spoon, by a dying man who had seen, before her, what she did not yet want to see.

When he died, Dores wept for him with a tired love, and felt, beneath the pain, a thing she was ashamed of: a relief, that of one who lays down a weight they have carried too many years. And she thought, naively, that her crossing was over, that she had borne her cross. There was, between Aniceto's death and Quim's sinking, an interval, a few years of rest, and Dores recalled them now as the only time of her adult life in which she had not had to save anyone. And it disquieted her, in recalling them, a thing: that even in that time she had not quite known what to do with herself. Accustomed to a life of saving, when there was no one to save, she felt herself, strange as it seems, a little lost. She remembered a neighbour, Aurora, who lived at the bottom of the street and who had been left lame from a fall, and how Dores had offered to do her shopping and scrub her house, and how Aurora, after a few months, had said to her half-laughing, oh Dores, I manage now, woman, let me manage, for you seem more distressed that I don't need you than that I'm lame. And Dores had laughed too, but she carried that home, because Aurora had touched, without malice, on the bone of the thing: that she needed to be needed, that the emptiness of having no one to shelter left her without ground. Not that she wanted her son to sink, of course not; but when he sank, the part of her trained to save found its function again, and gave itself to it with a readiness that came not only from love but from that old hunger to have someone to save, to be again the indispensable one.

It is hard to say when Quim's sinking began, because these things begin slowly, without a date. There were a few years when he drank as many drink; then a few when he drank too much, and the family said it would settle; and when it was understood that it did not settle, it already came from far, it was already installed. Dores watched it arrive with her heart tightening, because she recognised the signs, she had seen them in her husband. She remembered the exact afternoon when she understood that it was the same: Quim had come to dinner, he still lived with her in those days, and in the middle of the dinner he went to the pantry, and Dores heard, from the kitchen, a sound she knew by heart, the sound of a bottleneck striking a glass in a hurried way, in secret, and she went to the pantry and found her son with his back to her, and he turned too quickly, with the glass already empty and the air of one who was doing nothing, and said, I came to see if there was bread, Mother, and Dores looked at the shelf where the bread was not and where the bottle was, and it was the first time she saw, in her son, the father's gesture, the drinking in secret in the pantry, and it was in the son's pantry that the second sinking began, for her, in the same place, in the same house, with the same sound.

And Dores began again, without even deciding, because it was what she knew how to do. When Quim began to sink, she did with him what she had done with the father: covered, excused, paid, saved. It was automatic, it was the furrow of a lifetime. And Glória, who was far away and saw little, when she came and saw, scolded her mother, and scolded her in that way of hers of touching the depths without raising her voice. She came one time, and the mother, in front of her, gave money to Quim, who had appeared with a story, and when Quim went out Glória stood still in the middle of the kitchen and said only, softly, you have just paid my brother for this week's drink, Mother, and you call that love. And Dores defended herself, he is your brother, Glória, and Glória answered, I know he is my brother, Mother, what I do not know is whether I still have a mother or only a woman drowning to save him, because every time I come here you are thinner and he is the same. And she went away earlier than she had meant to stay, and Dores was left watching the car go down the street, and understood, with a dull pain, that each time she threw herself into the water after Quim she was losing Glória in another way, the daughter who had got free and who could not bear to see her mother sink. And only much later would Dores see that, in shutting the door to Quim, she would also recover Glória.

At the beginning, with Quim as before with Aniceto, there was only the love and the duty, and Dores gave herself to saving her son with the same devotion with which she had given herself to saving her husband, without seeing that she was entering, for the second time in her life, the same river that had nearly drowned her the first, and that this time, older, more worn, with less strength, the river might carry her off for good. It was the hands that knew, and her hands, like those of all the women of those houses, went to the pantry before the head commanded. But that was for the end, and the end still came far off, and for now the doorbell rang, and Dores opened, and gave, and made the supper at the wrong hours, now for the son, as before for his father, in the same corner of the same kitchen, awaiting a return that with Aniceto had never come and that she swore, against all evidence, with Quim would still come.
