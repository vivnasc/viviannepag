## 3. The man who stays

Justino had for Rosário a thing that had no name because it had never been said, and the things that are not said, in Véspera, live a long time, because there are no words to wear them out.

They were of the same age, had been at the same school, played in the same largo, and there was a photograph somewhere, in someone's box, of a row of children in the churchyard, and Rosário was in it, with a plait, and Justino was beside her, and already in that photograph, said whoever had seen it, the boy looked at the girl and the girl looked at the camera. It was a portrait of their whole life: he looking at her, she looking elsewhere.

There was a time, when young, when Justino and Rosário almost. They were sixteen, seventeen, and there was between them, at the school and in the largo, that thing there is between two young people who have known one another for ever and who one day, without warning, begin to look at one another in another way. Justino, as a boy, even spoke to her, at the riverside, trying to say what he felt, and Rosário, as a girl, felt something too, a beginning, a possibility. But it was at that same time that there appeared, in Rosário's life, the first men of passage, the fair-folk, those who came and went, and Rosário discovered in them the fever, the longing, the rapture of arrivals and departures, and beside that fever the calm feeling for Justino seemed to her little, lukewarm, dull. She chose the fever. She always chose the fever. And Justino, who was of staying, stayed: he stayed in the village, stayed single longer than the village thought proper, stayed watching her fall in love with one and another and at last with Aniceto, stayed beside her her whole life in the one place he knew how to occupy, that of one who is there, and that she never saw.

Justino had stayed in the village, as the men who love the place where they were born stay. He worked the river. It was what he knew, of the waters, of the fords, of the currents, of the fish; he was among the men the council called when the Bridge needed work, and in the winters of the floods it was he who did not sleep, who measured the water by the piers, who knew, better than anyone, when the Bridge held and when the crossing had to be cut off. The village trusted him with the Bridge as one trusts a man with the most important thing one has, and the Bridge was, in a village cut by a river, the most important thing there was: it was what linked the two banks, what let the bread and the dead and the bridegrooms pass, and one who cared for the Bridge cared for the whole village without the village noticing, which is how the essential things are cared for.

Justino was so: essential and invisible, like the Bridge. He was always there. And what is always there is not seen.

He had a dry humour that was only noticed at the second look, a way of saying right things slowly, without giving them importance, and hands that knew the river as others' know a trade of a closed room. He was not a dim man, though Rosário thought him dim: he was a whole man who made no noise, and in the village, as in the world, much is the noise confused with life, and the silence with the lack of it.

The village had, about Justino and Rosário, an opinion that it never said to them but commented on amply: that Justino was a fool to go so many years silent for a woman who had eyes only for the man of the waters, and that Rosário was blind not to see the man she had at her side. The old women above all, who see everything and forgive little, spoke of this at the fountain and the churchyard: that boy, said one, would make a husband such as there are no longer, serious, hard-working, with hands of gold, and there she is wasting away for a vagabond of the river who has not even a house on this side. And another answered: leave her, for each one pulls toward her own fate, and the fate of that one comes from her mother, who was just the same with the carrier. And so the village went on weaving, around the three of them, the web of its observations, and was, as almost always, right in everything and with power over nothing, because people's hearts do not obey the fountains nor the churchyards, and Rosário was going to take her time, and make her way, and only life, and not the village, was going to turn her to the right side of the river.

That winter, with Aniceto again at Rosário's house, Justino did what he did in all the floods: he cared for the Bridge and cared, from afar, without her knowing, for Rosário. It was he who opened the channel that saved her threshing-floor, on the day Aniceto told stories by the fire. And it was he who, days later, when the water rose still more and threatened the wall of her house, appeared at night, alone, with sacks of sand, and reinforced the wall in the rain, without knocking at the door, without saying anything, and went before she woke, and Rosário, in the morning, saw the reinforced wall and the sacks of sand and never knew for certain who had put them there, and thought vaguely it would have been the council, and thought no more of it, because she had Aniceto at home and her head was not up to noticing sacks of sand.

There was a story of Justino that the village told and that Rosário knew without ever having given it weight. Years before, in one of the great floods, some boys of the village had taken to the river in a boat, on a dare, at the height of the waters, imitating what Aniceto did, and the boat had capsized, and two of them had gone into the current, and there would have been deaths had it not been for Justino, who knew the river, throwing himself into the water at the right place, not where they fell, but lower down, where the current was to carry them, and waiting for them, and pulling them out. He had saved two men that night, and had told no one, and the village had learned it from the boys themselves, and for a few days Justino had been a hero, and then the village had forgotten, as it forgets, because what Justino had done had no brightness: he had not thrown himself into the river laughing, as Aniceto crossed, he had done it in silence, with calculation, with the science of the river, and the things done with science and in silence do not stay in the stories, unlike those done with bravado and noise. Aniceto crossed the river to be seen, and the village saw him and clapped. Justino had crossed the river to save two men, and had done it in such a way as almost not to be seen, and the village had forgotten. And there was in this, Rosário understood much later, all the difference between the two men, and all the difference between what she had learned to admire and what was worth the trouble: one risked himself to shine, the other risked himself to serve, and she had spent her life confusing the brightness with the worth.

Rosário, years later, was to put together one by one the times Justino had been present in her life without her registering it, and the count gave her vertigo. It had been he who, when the roof of her house threatened to fall, one winter, had appeared with tiles and mended it in a day, and she had scarcely thanked him. It had been he who, when her mother needed to be taken to the doctor outside, in an emergency, had hitched the cart and taken them, and waited hours, and brought them back, and never spoken of it. It had been he who, every year, without fail, on her birthday, left at the door, without a note, a small thing: a basket of fruit in season, a bunch of wild flowers, once a wooden stool he had made with his own hands and that was still in her kitchen, and on which she sat every day without knowing she sat on a present of Justino's. Twenty years of anonymous presents, of silent rescues, of presences she had attributed to chance or to the village or to no one, and that were all his, all of the man who stayed and who cared for her in a way so without noise that she had never noticed she was being cared for. Aniceto, in fourteen winters, had never given her anything that was not a story and a promise. Justino, in twenty years, had filled her life with concrete things, and she had eaten the fruit, and sat on the stool, and warmed herself by what he mended, without ever linking the comfort of her life to the man who gave it her, because what he gave made no noise, and she, like everyone, only saw what made noise.

There was, that winter, a night when the three of them crossed paths, and that stayed.

It was in the largo, on a night when the water began to fall and the village came out of their houses, relieved, and there was a small festival, improvised, of those that are held in Véspera when a flood passes without carrying anyone off. Aniceto, of course, was the centre: he told the crossing of the boat, enlarged already with each telling, danced, drank, made them laugh, was the most alive man of the largo, and Rosário, at his side, shone with the reflected brightness of one who loves the sun. And at a certain point, Aniceto pulled Rosário to dance, in the middle of the largo, and they danced, and it was beautiful, and the village clapped, and it was the portrait of what everyone thought Rosário's love was: that glittering man and that lit-up woman, dancing in the largo after the flood.

And at the edge of the largo, leaning against a wall, with a glass in his hand that he did not drink, was Justino, watching. Not watching with envy, or not only: watching with a mild sadness, that of one who knows a thing that the person they love does not yet know. And Rosário, dancing, crossed for an instant her gaze with Justino's, over Aniceto's shoulder, and in that instant there was something, a thing she did not know how to read, a discomfort, like when someone looks at us from a place where we did not expect to be seen. And Rosário looked away, and went on dancing, and said to herself that Justino was there as he always was, watching the village, which was his way, and she forgot the instant.

What there was in Justino's look, on that night of the festival, and that Rosário did not know how to read, was a thing simple and rare: it was the look of one who loves expecting nothing in return. Justino did not look at Rosário as Aniceto looked at her, with the brightness of one who possesses and displays. He looked at her as one looks at a thing one loves and that one knows one will perhaps never have, and that one loves all the same, without collecting, without claiming, content only to see her exist and to be able, from time to time, to open her a channel or reinforce her a wall. It was a love without demand, and so Rosário did not recognise it as love, because the love she knew, that of Aniceto, was all demand and rapture, all arrival and departure, all me, and that of Justino was all you, all her good, and a love that is all the other's good is so silent that it is confused with the landscape. Rosário would take a long time to understand that Aniceto's brightness was, at bottom, a form of asking — asking for admiration, asking for an audience, asking to be loved —, and that Justino's silence was a form of giving, and that she had spent her life confusing one who asked with one who loved, because one who asks makes noise and one who gives does it silently.

But the instant stayed, against her will, in a deep place, and was going to come back.

At the end of the night, when the festival broke up and Aniceto, drunk on wine and on applause, went ahead toward home singing, Rosário stayed behind to help gather a few things, and found herself beside Justino, the two of them putting out the last lights of the largo, and it was the first time that winter they had been alone, and Rosário felt, without knowing why, a kind of peace that bothered her, because it was different from the fever she felt with Aniceto, and she only knew how to recognise love by the fever.

"It was a good flood," said Justino, rolling up a rope. "It carried no one off. There were worse years."

"The Bridge held."

"The Bridge always holds, if there is someone to watch it." He looked at her, and said, with no weight at all, a thing she would only understand much later: "It is like everything, Rosário. The things that stay hold, if there is someone to care for them. But no one claps a bridge. One claps the one who crosses it by boat in the middle of the flood. The bridge, which is there every day, no one heeds. And yet it is the bridge that holds the village, it is not the boat."

Rosário looked at him, and for a second, only a second, understood that he was not speaking of bridges. But it was late, and she was tired, and Aniceto was singing in the distance, and she said good night, Justino, and went after Aniceto's song, toward home, and from Aniceto's song she could hear nothing more of what Justino had said, because Aniceto's song was loud and Justino's truth was low, and in Véspera, as in Rosário's heart, what was loud always covered what was low.

Justino stayed in the largo finishing rolling up the rope, alone, without haste, and then went to check the Bridge once more, out of habit, before sleeping, because the water was still falling and a water that falls can also betray, and he stayed a while on top of the Bridge, in the dark, listening to the river, the man who stayed, and whom no one saw, and who loved a woman who could only see men when they were about to depart. And the river passed beneath his feet, going away, always going away, which is what rivers do, and Justino, who knew the river better than anyone, knew a thing about it that Rosário did not yet know: that what makes a river is not the water that passes and goes away. It is the bed that stays, and that bears it passing. Without a bed, the water is not a river: it is a flood, it is a disaster, it is water lost over the fields. It is what stays that gives meaning to what passes. But these were the thoughts of a man alone on top of a bridge at night, and the thoughts of such men rarely reach in time the ears of those who needed them.
