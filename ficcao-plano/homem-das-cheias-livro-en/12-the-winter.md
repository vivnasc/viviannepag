## 12. The winter

A year later, the great waters came again, because rivers learn nothing and do the same every winter, and it was in that fidelity of the river that was measured how much Rosário had changed.

It rained on the serra, and the Fundo swelled, and the Bridge was with the water lapping the piers, and the village came out to the windows, and everything was as it had always been, everything but one thing: Rosário did not go to the window. She was in the kitchen, with Justino, making the supper of the two of them, and when the village began to talk of the waters, and when someone passed by saying that a boat could be seen crossing against the current, Rosário knew, without going to the window, that it was Aniceto, faithful to his fate as the river to his, coming with the floods. And she did not go to the window. She went on peeling what she was peeling, beside Justino, and the only thing she felt was a distant tenderness, the kind one has for a person who no longer governs us, and a serene astonishment at no longer lighting up, she who for fourteen years had lit up entirely just from divining that boat on the water.

Justino noticed, of course, for he noticed everything, and said nothing, for it was his way of honouring great things, but he put his hand on her back an instant, on passing, and in that instant fit everything there was to say.

There was, that winter, a moment when Rosário measured how much she had changed, and she measured it by the absence of a thing. When she knew that Aniceto had arrived, she waited for the fever. She waited for the melting, for the heart in the throat, for the wish to run to the window. And it did not come. She waited still, almost with curiosity, like one who presses a place that used to hurt to see whether it still hurts, and the place did not hurt. The fever, which for fourteen years had come faithful to every flood, did not come. And Rosário understood, with a serene astonishment, that she was cured, or almost, and that the cure did not have the form she had imagined: it was not having come to hate Aniceto, nor having ceased to find him beautiful, nor anything dramatic. It was only this, this small and enormous thing: that her body, on knowing that he had arrived, no longer lit up. The alarm had switched off. And a woman whose alarm has switched off is a free woman, because the alarm was the chain, was what pulled her to the window every winter for fourteen years, and without an alarm there was no window, there was no waiting, there was no fate. She was free. It had taken her thirty-five years and a whole crossing, but she was free, and the freedom made no noise at all: it was only the absence of a fever, the silence of an alarm that did not sound.

Later, with the supper made, Rosário went to the door, not to the window, to the door, which is different: the window is to look from within, the door is to be able to go out. And from the door she saw the largo, and saw, in the distance, on the near bank, Aniceto coming out of the boat, wet, laughing, seeking with his eyes a window that this time was dark. And Rosário, from the door, felt pity for him, a clean pity: pity for a man who was going to spend his life crossing rivers to ports that were going out one by one, as the women of the windows, one here another there, went on learning, as she had learned, to look at the right side of the river. Aniceto was going to grow old on the waters, crossing floods ever more tired, seeking windows ever darker, and was going to die, perhaps, in the middle of a crossing, and the village was going to say what a brave man, he died in the river he loved, and no one was going to say the truth, which was sadder: that he had died alone, crossing, for never having learned to stay on a bank.

She did not go to speak with him. It was not needed, and it would have been almost a cruelty, the darkened port explaining to the boat why it had gone dark. She let him seek the dark window, and closed the door, slowly, and went back inside, to the lit kitchen, to Justino, to the near bank.

"He came," said Justino, without asking.

"He came," said Rosário. "And he goes. As always." She sat at the table, in front of him. "The difference is that this time the one who stays is me."

It was known, later, that Aniceto, that flood, on finding Rosário's window dark, went about the village asking after her, and that someone told him that Rosário had settled with Justino of the Bridge, and that he shrugged his shoulders, and said some quip or other, and went off with the waters as always, and did not seek her again. He was no man to fight for what he lost: he was a man to move on to the next flood, to the next bank, to the next window. And Rosário, on knowing that he had not sought her in earnest, felt no grief: she felt the confirmation of what she already knew, that for Aniceto she had always been a bank among banks, and that a bank that goes dark is replaced by another that lights up, up the river or down the river. What for her had been the love of a lifetime, for him had been a port among ports. And that asymmetry, which in another time would have shattered her, left her now only with a serene pity, and with a strange gratitude: gratitude for having seen in time, for not having crossed the river with him, for not having gone to be, on the other side, a lit-up window in a row of windows waiting for a man of all of them.

They ate, and it was a common supper, without fever, and Rosário, who for fourteen years had thought that common suppers were what was left over from life, understood in that one that common suppers were life, the true one, the one the mother had never had because she had spent the nights at the window instead of at the table. And she thought of the mother, and it was not with anger nor only with pity: it was with a kind of difficult gratitude, because the mother, in warning her, in telling her do not do as I did, had given her the one thing she had to give and that was worth more than all the inheritance: the key to her own cell.

There was, at the end of that winter, a morning when Rosário made peace with the window. Not the mother's window, for that one had stayed empty in the other house, but her own, that of the house where she now lived with Justino. She had come to fear it, the window, after everything: she passed by it and looked away, as one looks away from an old vice. And one morning she realised that she was making the window an enemy, and that that was still to give it power. So she sat at the window, on purpose, on a sunny morning, and looked out. And what she saw, from the window, was not the other bank, was not the road down which came one who went away: it was the village, the largo, the life happening, the neighbours, the day. She understood that the window was not the problem. The problem was where one looked from it. The mother had always looked at the road, at the way out, at the absence. And Rosário, there, that morning, looked from the window at the village, at the present, at what was there, and the window, which had been for four generations the post of the waiting, became another thing in her hands: a window like the others, from which one sees life instead of waiting for it to arrive from afar. She cured the window, that morning, by changing what she looked at from it. And she thought that perhaps that was the only way to cure inheritances: not to throw them away, for that cannot be done, but to change the use one puts them to.

The next day, with the rain stopping and the river beginning to draw back, Rosário did a thing she decided to do every winter from then on, like one who keeps a vow: she went to the Bridge with Justino, in the middle of the flood, to help him watch the water. Not to the window to wait for whom the flood brought: to the Bridge, to care for what linked the banks. And she stayed there with him, in the cold, watching the river pass, going away, always going away, and the Bridge beneath their feet, firm, holding, and Rosário understood, that morning, beside the man who stayed, with the river passing and the Bridge staying, the last thing she had left to understand: that she had spent her life wanting to be the water, the flood, what passes and sweeps away and goes, because she had admired that in the mother without knowing it and in Aniceto knowing it, and that after all what she wanted to be, what made her happy with that flat and deep happiness, was not the water. It was the Bridge. To stay. To link. To bear the water passing and to make of it a river instead of a lost flood. To be what stays, and not what goes away.

They married the following spring, without a great feast, because neither she nor Justino were for great feasts, but the village went, and the village, which had rooted for that for years at the fountain and the churchyard, rejoiced in a deep way, as one rejoices when a thing that should have happened long ago happens at last. The old women wept, and said it was from the contentment, and it was, but it was also another thing they did not say: it was the relief of seeing one of the women of the village escape the fate of the windows, marry one who stayed, turn at last toward the near bank. Each woman of the village who settled with a man who stayed was a small victory of all of them, of those who had stayed at the windows and of those who still ran the risk, and so the wedding of Rosário and Justino had, beneath the simple joy, a gravity of an important thing, of a fate broken, of a bad luck driven away from a house.

And in the middle of the Bridge, on the day of the great waters of that winter, at the highest point, Rosário stopped, as she had stopped a year before, and looked at the two banks. And this time there was no choice to make, because the choice was made, and what there was was only the peace of one who has already chosen: the far bank, in the distance, beautiful, empty, the bank of those who pass; and the near bank, hers, that of the man at her side, that of the lit house, that of the present life. And the river passing underneath, going away, and she staying, and the staying no longer tasting to her of little, no longer tasting to her of the waiting room, tasting, at last, of what it had always been without her seeing it: home.

And Rosário, on the Bridge, at his side, did what the mother had asked her at the window: she looked at Justino's hands. His hands, that morning of the flood, measured the water by the piers, tightened a knot in a rope, made the small and sure gestures of one who cares for what links the banks, and Rosário looked at them, on purpose, as the mother had taught her, and loved him for them, for the hands, for what they did and what stayed, and it was a love with no fever at all and no longing at all and no window at all, a love of looking at the hands of one who stays instead of looking at the road down which goes one who goes away, and Rosário understood, on feeling it, that she had learned. That the mother had died leaving her the key, and that she had opened the door. That she was, at last, the first woman of that house, in four generations, to look at the hands of the man at her side instead of at the point on the road. And she thought of the mother, and wished, with a tenderness without bitterness, that wherever the mother was, she might be at last looking at a pair of hands, and not at an empty road.

She thought, from the height of the Bridge, of all the women who had preceded her in that fate. Of the great-grandmother at the seaside waiting for the man of the sea. Of the grandmother at the door waiting for the man of the army. Of the mother at the window waiting for the man of the fairs. Four generations of women looking at horizons down which went the men they loved, four generations confusing love with longing, life with waiting, and all of them dying, or on their way to dying, with their eyes set on a road, on a sea, on a door, waiting for one who did not stay. And she, Rosário, was the fifth, and could have been one more, one more woman of that house dying at the window. And she was not going to be. She was going to be the first to turn, the first to look at the hands instead of at the road, the first to stay on the bridge instead of at the window, the first to break the fate. And she did it not only for herself: she did it for all those who came behind, for the girl who one day, in that village, would fall in love with one who went away, and to whom someone could say, look at Rosário, that one turned in time, that one escaped, do as she did. She was going to write, in life, with life, the thing the Keeper had said: something worth being copied. And what was worth being copied was not the fever, was not the brightness, was not the adventure of the waters. It was this: to stay. To see. To love what is there. To look at the hands of the man at one's side instead of at the point on the road.

Justino put his hand on her shoulder, on the Bridge, in the cold, and said nothing, and it was not needed. And Rosário looked at the river going away and felt no longing at all to watch it pass, because she had learned, at the end of thirty-five years, the simplest and hardest thing of all: that one does not have to run after the water. The water passes. One who stays on the bridge sees it all, the one that comes and the one that goes, and loses none of it, and does not get wet, and has, at the end of the day, a house to go back to and a man who is there, and that, which the village never claps and which gives no stories to tell at the fairs, that, Rosário discovered on the height of the Bridge that winter, that was, after all, and from the beginning, love.
