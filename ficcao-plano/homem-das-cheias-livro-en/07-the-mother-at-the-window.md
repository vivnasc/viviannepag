## 7. The mother at the window

Rosário went to the mother at the end of the summer, and she went with a question, and it was the first time in her life that she went to the mother with a real question, of those that are not made with the mouth but that one carries in the breast waiting for the occasion to let them out.

It was strange, and Rosário thought of this on the way, that having spent her whole life beside the mother she had never put to her the question she was going to put. They had lived together, mother and daughter, years and years, and had spoken of everything, of the weather, of the kitchen-garden, of the village, of the dead, and never, not once, had they spoken of that: of love, of the way of loving, of the window. The most important things, in Véspera as in almost every house, were the ones least spoken of, and the window, which governed the two lives, had never been named between them, because it was the water in which they swam, and fish, they say, are the last to know of the water. Rosário was going now, late, with the mother already halfway to the other world, to ask the question of a lifetime, and she asked it with the fear of one who touches a thing that has never been touched, and with the urgency of one who knows that if she does not ask it now she never will, that the mother was going out and would take with her, if Rosário did not tear it from her in time, the one thing that could save the daughter: the truth about the way the women of that house had loved, and the confession that that way was no good.

Lembrança was, as always, at the window. She was ever farther from the present world, but at the window she went on, with her eyes on the road, and there were days she confused Rosário with a dead sister, and days she knew her, and Rosário had learned to wait for the days the mother knew her, as one waits for the tides.

That day, the mother knew her.

"Sit down, daughter," said Lembrança, and there was in her a late-afternoon lucidity, of those that come to the old when the day falls, as though the soul, before the night, made a last effort to be whole. "Sit here by the window, for today I am here, and the days I am here are fewer and fewer. You have a thing to ask me. You have carried it in your face since you came in."

Rosário sat by the mother, at the window, the two of them looking at the road of the other bank, and the question, that she had carried in her breast the whole summer, came out at last, low, afraid of the answer:

"Mother. Father. Did you love him?"

Lembrança was silent a while, looking at the road, and when she answered, she answered slowly, with the naked truth of those who no longer have time to dress it.

"I did. I loved him like a madwoman. But I loved him badly, daughter, and I loved him badly my whole life, and only now, old, on the edge of going to find him wherever he is, do I see how badly I loved him." She laid her hand, dry and light, on top of Rosário's. "I loved the road he went down, more than I loved him. I loved the donkey drawing away, the point he became in the distance, the longing that filled my house when he disappeared. That, that I loved with all my soul. The man, the man who stayed five days and ate and snored and complained of the business, that one I scarcely knew, daughter, and sometimes, God forgive me, I could scarcely bear him, because he spoiled my longing with his presence. I did not love your father. I loved his lack. They are different things, and I took my whole life to confuse them, and when he died I wept little, and the village thought me hard, and I was not hard, daughter: it was that the man who died had already been, for me, an absence for forty years, and I knew how to love absences so well that death changed almost nothing. I went on at the window. I go on. Do you see me? An old woman at the window waiting for a dead man. This is what comes of loving the lack instead of the presence. It ends so."

Rosário had her eyes full of water, because each word of the mother's was a portrait of herself, of the fever for Aniceto, of the longing that filled the house when he departed, of the tedium she felt sometimes on the days he was present and was only a common man.

"And why, mother?" she asked. "Why did you love so? Why could you not love what was there?"

"I do not rightly know, daughter." Lembrança looked at the road, and her voice fell. "My mother waited too. And her mother. In this family, the women always loved men who went away: sailors, carriers, men of fairs, men of war. There was never, that I know of, a woman of this house who loved a man who stayed. Perhaps it is a thing that passes from mother to daughter, like the colour of the eyes. The girls are taught, without meaning to, to look at the road. I taught it you, daughter, I know I taught it you, and it is the thing I most regret, because I gave you the only thing I knew how to give, which was the waiting, and the waiting, Rosário, the waiting eats the life and gives nothing in return but more waiting."

"Do you regret it?" asked Rosário, and it was a cruel and necessary question. "Having loved father so? Having spent your life at the window?"

Lembrança took her time to answer, and when she answered it was with an honesty that only the very old have, because they no longer have anything to defend.

"I regret one thing only, daughter. I do not regret having loved your father, for I loved him as I knew how, and what is loved as one knows how is not regretted. I regret never having tried the other way. Never, in forty years, having turned from the window to the room, to see what there was in the room. There was your father, on the days he was there, and there was you, always, and there was the village, and there was a whole life happening in the room while I was at the window looking at the road. I lost the whole room, daughter, by being at the window. That is my regret: not the man I chose, but the life I did not live by waiting for him. The hours. All the hours I spent at the window, and that I could have spent living. If I could go back, I would not choose another man, for perhaps I would choose the same, for the heart is stubborn. But I would love him another way: I would love him on the days he was there, and on the days he was not I would go and live, instead of waiting. It was only that. It was only to turn the window chair inward. It seems little, and it is the whole of life."

And Rosário kept that as the most precious thing the mother gave her: not that she should choose another man, but that, whatever the man, she should turn the chair inward, to the room, to the life, instead of leaving it turned to the window, to the road, to the waiting. To turn the chair. It was the image that was going to stay with her, simple and exact: a woman turning the window chair to the room, and in that turning of a chair the difference between a life lived and a life waited.

They were silent, at the window, and the sun was falling over the other bank, and the river, down below, passed in its summer thread.

"I have come to know a man who stays, mother," said Rosário, at last, and it was the first time she said that aloud, to anyone. "You shall come to know him. He is called Justino. He is of the Bridge. He is always there, mother. He never goes away. And I... I do not know whether I know how to love him, because he does not give me the lack. He is always there, and I am afraid that, if I stay with him, I will stop seeing him, as you stopped seeing father when he was at home."

The mother was silent a while, and then told a thing she had never told, and that Rosário kept as the deepest of all. She told that, when young, before Vado, there had been a boy who wanted her, one of the village, one who stayed, a serious one, hard-working, similar, Rosário realised with a shiver, to Justino. And that she, Lembrança, had had him within reach of her hand, and had refused him, because he had seemed to her little, dull, without the brightness of those who go away. That boy, said the mother, married another, and they were happy, and I went to the window to wait for Vado for forty years, and many times, daughter, many times, at the window, I remembered that boy I thought little, and asked myself how my life would have been if I had had the sense to want him. But I did not. I was twenty and I wanted brightness, and brightness, daughter, brightness is paid for dear, it is paid for with a life at the window. And she said, low: you are now at the same crossroads where I was at twenty. You have one who shines and goes away, and one who does not shine and stays. I chose the one who shines, and I regretted it my whole life. I do not ask you to do the contrary out of obeying me. I ask you to do it because I have already been there, daughter, and I have already seen the end of the road you are beginning, and the end of the road is this window, and this old woman, and this looking at an empty road. Do not come to this window. It was to tell you this that I am still here, today, with my wits. It was only to tell you this.

Lembrança turned to her daughter, and in her eyes there was, suddenly, an urgency, a strength she should no longer have, the strength of a mother who sees her daughter on the edge of repeating the error of a lifetime and who has a last occasion to warn her.

"Hear me, daughter, hear me well, for today I am here and tomorrow I may not be." She squeezed her hand hard. "That man who stays, that Justino, hold on to him with both hands and do not let him go, do you hear? Do not commit my error. I loved a door that opened, your father, who came and went, and the door opening at least gave me his comings, the days he was there. You, daughter, you love worse than I, you love a door that closes, that man of the waters, who only gives you the departure, who does not even give you the true comings, because he does not come to stay, he comes to go. I loved what went away. You love the going-away. It is worse, daughter. It is much worse." And she said, low, looking at the road, the phrase that Rosário took home and that was the key to everything: "Do not do as I did. I loved a door that opened. You love a door that closes. Turn to the man who has no door at all, daughter, the one who is always in the same room as you. That is love. The rest is the disease of our house, and I pray every night, with the little wit that is left me, that it may end in you, for it goes already on four generations of women at the window."

"But how, mother?" asked Rosário, and the question was that of a whole lifetime. "How does one love one who stays? I do not know. You did not teach me, no one taught me. I know how to wait, I know how to long, I know how to light up when he arrives and die when he departs. That I know by heart. But to be with a man every day, in the same house, without the fever, without the longing, to see him in the morning and at night and the next day again, and not to go out, and to go on seeing him... that, mother, that I do not know, and I am afraid not to learn it, and to spoil him and myself for not knowing."

Lembrança was silent, and for an instant Rosário thought the mother had gone again to the far place. But no. The mother was thinking, with the enormous effort of the old who want to leave a thing said before departing.

"I do not know either, daughter," she said, at last, and it was of an honesty that hurt. "I cannot teach you what I never knew. But I am going to tell you what I learned to see, now that it is of no use to me any more, which is what is left to the old, to see late what they needed to know early. Your father, in the five days he stayed, did things, daughter. He cut firewood, mended the fence, worked the kitchen-garden. I never looked at that. I looked at the road, waiting for him to go, so I could love him from afar. If I had looked at his hands cutting the firewood, instead of looking at the road waiting for the departure, perhaps I would have loved him from near, daughter. The love of near is not in the fever. It is in the hands of the man doing the small things, every day, beside us. One must look at the hands, and not at the road. I looked at the road my whole life. You look at the hands, daughter. The hands of your Justino, what do they do?"

"They care for the Bridge," said Rosário, low. "They hold the river. They save people in the floods. They mend what is broken."

"Then look at them," said Lembrança. "Look at his hands doing those things, every day, and love him by that, by the hands, by what they do and what stays. For the love of near, daughter, is that: it is to look at the hands of one who stays, instead of looking at the road down which goes one who goes away. I took eighty years to understand this, and I understood it too late for me. You are thirty-four. Look at the hands, Rosário. Promise me that you will look at the hands."

And Rosário promised, at the window, hand in the mother's hand, and it was the most important promise she made in her life, because it was the promise to break a fate of four generations by a thing as simple as changing the direction of her gaze: from the road to the hands, from the departure to what stays, from what goes away to what is there.

Rosário embraced the mother, at the window, and wept, and the mother let herself be embraced, and they stayed so a long time, and when Rosário went, as it grew dark, Lembrança had already gone back to the far place where she now spent her days, and was looking at the road waiting for Vado, and did not know her daughter at the farewell, and called her by the name of the dead sister. But she had been there long enough. She had said what she had to say. And Rosário took home, on the night of late summer, the true inheritance of the mother, which was neither the window nor the waiting: it was the warning, said late but said, of a woman who had lost her life at the window and who did not want the daughter to lose hers too, and who left her, instead of the fate, for the first time in four generations, a way out.
