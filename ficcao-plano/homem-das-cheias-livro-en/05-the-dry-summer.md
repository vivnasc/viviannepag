## 5. The dry summer

The summer, in Véspera, was the time of the low river, and the low river was the time of Rosário absent from herself.

Without rain on the serra, the Fundo drew back to a thread, and there were stretches where one crossed on foot, from stone to stone, without wetting the knees, and the Bridge stood high and dry and almost useless above a water that no longer frightened anyone. And so was Rosário in the months without floods: low, withdrawn, crossable, with no strength either to frighten or to light anyone up. The village watched her pass with her slow step, tending her things with no soul in them, and said, low, there goes Rosário counting the days until winter, and it was true, she counted the days, lived the whole summer like a waiting room, waiting for the waters that brought her Aniceto and life.

The summer, in Véspera, had its labours and its festivals, and Rosário, in the summers of always, crossed them like a sleepwalker: the threshing, the nights in the churchyard, the popular saints' days with the bonfires and the sardines, all of that passed through her as the landscape passes by the window of one who travels with their head elsewhere. She went to the festivals because one went, danced little, laughed little, and came home, to the window, to the count of the days. The village had grown used to seeing her so at the festivals, present and absent, and no one drew her to dance any more, because it was known that Rosário, outside the floods, was a woman half asleep.

That summer, for the first time, the festivals entered her. She went to a bonfire, at night, in the churchyard, and instead of staying on the margin she stayed by the fire, and the fire warmed her, and the music was of a guitar and a cavaquinho, and Rosário, without noticing, found herself marking the beat with her foot, and at a certain point one of the women pulled her into the circle, and she danced, and danced with no fever at all, only for the pleasure of dancing, and it was the first time in her life she danced for pleasure and not for being lit up by a man. And she noticed that Justino was there, on the margin, as always, watching, and that he saw her dance, and that he smiled, his dry smile, and that he did not come to fetch her to dance, because he was no man for that, but that he stayed watching her with a calm pleasure, and Rosário, dancing, felt his look and was not bothered, on the contrary: she danced a little for that look, and it was a new and good thing, to dance for the look of a man who was there, instead of wasting away for the absence of one who had gone.

But that summer was different, and it was because of the thought of the Bridge, which had not gone away, and which during the dry months had been growing inside her as a root grows, slowly, in the dark, making no noise.

The low river, in summer, was a place where the village lived. The women went to wash the clothes on the flat stones, the boys went to bathe in the deep pool, the old men went to sit in the shade of the willows watching the water run in its thread, and there was, at the edge of the summer river, a slow and sweet life that Rosário, in the summers of always, had never lived, because she spent the summers shut up at home or at the window, waiting for the winter. That summer, without quite knowing why, she began to go down to the river. She went to fetch water, went to wash a thing, went for no reason, and stayed, and heard the women talk, and laughed at the jokes of the boys in the pool, and went on mixing, slowly, into the life of the near bank, that life that had always been there, beside her, and that she had never lived for having her eyes on the other side. She discovered that she liked it. She discovered that the village, seen from within and not from the window, was a place full of small good life, of conversations, of mutual help, of festivals, and that she had spent thirty-four years on the margin of that life, present in body and absent in soul, and that she was now, that summer, entering it for the first time, like one who enters a warm water and realises it had been there waiting the whole time.

Rosário began to notice what she did. She caught herself, one morning, at the window, looking at the road of the other bank, and instead of giving herself to the waiting, she asked herself what she was doing there, in the middle of a summer, looking at a road down which no one came. She caught herself counting the days until winter and asked herself what life that was, to live eleven months waiting for one. She caught herself, above all, living the summer as though it did not count, as though the months without Aniceto were only the interval between the things that counted, and she asked herself, for the first time, perhaps it is that my life is this, is the summer, is the low river, is the near bank, and the winter is only a flood that passes, and I have been living for the flood and throwing away the life.

They were new questions, and they hurt, and Rosário did not know what to do with them, and it was in that discomfort, in that summer of questions, that Justino, without doing anything special, went on gaining contour in her eyes.

It was not suddenly. It was as one sees a thing that was always in front of one: one day one notices it, and then one can no longer not see it. Rosário began to notice Justino. She noticed that he passed by her house more often than his road asked. She noticed that, when there was heavy work in the village, it was he who appeared, silent, and did what was needed, and went without fuss. She noticed his dry humour, the right things he said slowly, and that she, for years, had not heard because her ears were full of the noise of Aniceto. She noticed his hands, which knew the river. She noticed that Justino was always there, and for the first time in her life the being-always-there, instead of making him invisible, began to make him visible, because Rosário, with the new questions, began to suspect that perhaps the being-always-there was not the contrary of love, but a form of it she had never learned to read.

Benvinda, of the Grocery, was the first to notice the change, because Benvinda noticed everything from the height of her counter, which is among the best observation posts of a village. Rosário went to the Grocery, that summer, and instead of coming in and going out like a ghost, as she came in and went out in the summers of always, she began to linger, to talk, to laugh at one thing or another, and one day Benvinda, weighing out her sugar, said, without raising her eyes from the scale:

"You are different this summer, Rosário. You are here. Other summers you were always in the winter that was to come. This one you are here, in the summer." And she raised her eyes, and looked at her over her glasses, with that way she had of seeing people. "I do not know what it is, and I do not ask, for it is not my affair. But whatever it is, it does you good. You have the colour of one who lives in the month they are in, and not in the one that is lacking."

Rosário did not know what to answer, because she herself scarcely knew what was happening to her, but Benvinda's phrase stayed with her: to live in the month one is in, and not in the one that is lacking. It was exactly what she had never done. She had lived her whole life in the month that was lacking, in the winter to come, in the Aniceto to arrive, and the months she was in, those, she had thrown away, lived them looking at another, and the sum of all those thrown-away months was the greater part of life, was almost the whole of life, because the months of Aniceto were few and the others were almost all. She had thrown away almost the whole of life waiting for the small part of it that had Aniceto in it. And she was beginning now, that summer, without quite knowing how, to pick up off the ground the months that were lacking, to live in the month she was in, and each lived month was a month she recovered from a life she had been wasting at the window.

And there was, that summer, a difference in Rosário's body that she herself marvelled at. In the summers of always, her body wasted away waiting for the winter: she ate little, slept badly, went about slowly. That summer, without noticing, she ate better, slept better, had a firmer step. Benvinda said she had gained colour, and it was true, and Rosário did not know that it was the living in the present that gave her the colour, that it was the leaving the waiting that gave her back her body. She had spent her life treating the summers as a disease, as time to endure until the cure of the winter, and she discovered now that it was the contrary: the winter, with its short fever and its long voids, was what made her ill, and the summer, lived instead of waited, was what cured her. She had been, her whole life, calling health the disease and disease the health, calling life the weeks of Aniceto and death the months without him, when it was in his weeks that she spent herself in fever and it was in his months that she could, if she wished, live. Everything turned about. And the summer of Justino was, slowly, turning the things back, teaching her that the present was not the waiting room of life: it was the life, and the only one there was.

There was one afternoon, in the dry summer, when they crossed paths at the river.

Rosário had gone to the river to fetch water for the kitchen-garden, at the low stretch, and Justino was there, sitting on a stone, mending a net, and Rosário, instead of doing what she would do in another summer, which was to greet him from afar and go about her life, stopped. She sat on a stone beside him, without quite knowing why, and the two of them stayed by the low river, in the warm afternoon, and Justino mended the net and Rosário looked at the water, and there was no fever at all, no longing at all, none of the delicious alarm of the imminent loss that she felt with Aniceto. There was only an afternoon, a river, a man mending a net, and a peace that Rosário did not know where to put, because peace, in her catechism, was not love: it was the lack of it.

"Do you not tire?" asked Rosário, suddenly, and did not even quite know what she was asking.

"Of what?"

"Of always being there. Of caring for the Bridge, for the river, for the village. Of always being here, in the same place, doing the same things. Does it not make you want to go? To see other lands, other rivers?"

Justino mended one more knot of the net before answering, slowly, as he answered everything.

"I have already seen other rivers, Rosário. I went, when young. I went about outside a few years. And do you know what I learned? That rivers are all alike, and that what changes is not the river, it is who is at the edge of it with us." He looked at her, without weight. "I do not stay for not having seen the world. I stay because I saw, and because I chose. There are those who think that to stay is a lack of courage, that the brave one is the one who departs. I think the contrary. To depart, anyone departs: it is only to put one foot in front of the other and not look back. To stay is the difficult thing. To stay is to look at the same thing every day and not give up on it, not to think it little, not to flee to the next. To stay gives much more work than to depart. But no one claps that, and I have grown tired of wanting claps."

Rosário was silent. It was the first time in her life that someone put the staying to her as a difficult and brave thing, and not as what is left over to one who has no courage to depart. The whole village admired Aniceto for crossing the river by boat in the flood, and no one, ever, had admired Justino for being every day on the Bridge holding it up. And Rosário, that afternoon, by the low river, began to suspect that the village, and she with the village, and her mother before the village, had been admiring the wrong thing, clapping the boat and forgetting the Bridge, calling love the flood that passes and not seeing the bed that stays and that bears the water passing.

Justino taught her, that summer, to read the river, and the first lesson was about the fords. He took her to a stretch where the water, even in summer, ran deeper, and showed her how one sees, by the surface, what goes underneath: where the water makes that smooth ripple it is deep, he said, and where it ruffles it is shallow, there is stone; the river says everything to one who knows how to read it, only it speaks low, and one has to learn its language. And Rosário, who foresaw the floods because of Aniceto, in a rapture of the body, discovered that there was another way of knowing the river, a patient way, of attention, of years of looking, and that that way, Justino's, was surer and deeper than hers. She knew the river by emotion; he by science. She knew when the flood was coming because her body told her Aniceto was coming; he knew because he read the serra, the clouds, the colour of the water, the signs. And there was in the two ways all the difference between the two loves: hers, emotional, rapturous, that came in waves and left her at their mercy; his, attentive, constant, that knew the loved thing from within, from so much looking at it, and that for that could protect it. Rosário understood, in that lesson of the fords, that she wanted to learn to know things in Justino's way, by attention and not by rapture, because attention stays and rapture passes, and that perhaps that was adult love, after all: not the rapture, which anyone feels, but the attention, which few sustain.

"I do not know how to stay," said Rosário, low, and it was the truest thing she had said in a long while, and she said it to a man mending a net, at the edge of an almost dry river, in a summer she had always thought did not count. "I learned to wait, Justino. I learned from my mother. But to stay, to stay I do not know. I am afraid that, if I stay with a thing, it will go out. That only what is going away lights me up."

Justino laid down the net. He looked at her, and there was in him a tenderness so great and so silent that Rosário, for an instant, felt a thing that was neither fever nor longing, and that frightened her more than the two, because it was new and it was deep and it had no alarm at all: it was the beginning of the peace of one who is seen.

"Then it is learned," said Justino, and took up the net again, without making this a moment, which was his way of caring for fragile things, by not giving them too much importance. "To stay is learned like everything. Slowly, and with someone who stays with you while you learn." And he added, already mending, without looking at her, the phrase that Rosário took home that summer and that was worth all the stories of Aniceto: "I am not going anywhere, Rosário. I have been here for thirty-four years and here I will be. It is not a promise. Promises are made by one who goes away. It is only a piece of information, in case it suits you to know it."
