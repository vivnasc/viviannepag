## 8. The flood out of season

Aniceto came out of season that year, and it was the proof that fate, when it wants to teach a thing to someone, has no shame in being obvious.

Rosário knew, afterwards, that she owed her cure to that flood out of season, because it was the one that forced her into the confrontation she would have put off for ever. If Aniceto had come only in the winter, in his season, he would have found Rosário still in the middle of the summer of Justino, still halfway through the change, and perhaps the fever, coming at the right time, would have dragged her back. But Aniceto came out of season, at the end of summer, with the summer of Justino still warm, and that cruel coincidence put the two men and the two lives side by side, at the same moment, and forced Rosário to see them together, to compare them with the two before her, to choose with her eyes open. Fate, or chance, or the river, did Rosário the favour of not letting her choose in the dark. It put the fever beside the peace, the one who comes beside the one who stays, and said to her, without words: now see the two, and choose. And it was for having seen the two at the same time, and not one at a time separated by months, that Rosário managed, at last, to see the difference, and to choose, and the flood out of season, which seemed a misfortune, a temptation sent to make her fall, was after all the final test, the one that saved her.

It rained on the serra at the end of the summer, a great downpour, out of season, of those that come when no one expects them and make the river swell suddenly, and the Fundo, which was at a thread, swelled in a night, and the waters rose, and the Bridge was again with the water lapping the piers, and the village, which no longer reckoned on floods until winter, came out to the windows surprised. And with the waters, of course, faithful to his name and his fate, came Aniceto, by boat, against the current, laughing at the river, in the middle of an end of summer that had been, all of it, Justino's.

Rosário watched him cross, from the window, and her body did what it always did: it lit up. It lit up against her will, against everything she had thought and understood that summer, because the body had fourteen years of habit and the habit does not undo itself with a conversation at the mother's window. Aniceto leapt to land, wet, laughing, and sought with his eyes her window, and made her the gesture of always, the open hand in the air, and Rosário felt the old melting, and hated herself for feeling it, because now she already saw him, already knew what he was, and even so the body responded, as a dog responds to the whistle even after learning that the whistle brings no food at all.

But this time there was a difference, and the difference was called Justino, and was there, in the village, present, real, owner of Rosário's end of summer. And for the first time in fourteen years, Rosário was going to have the two men on the same bank at the same time, and was going to have to see them side by side, and was going to have to choose with the two before her, and not in the comfortable dark of the absence of one.

The village noticed, of course, the flood out of season and the coming out of season of Aniceto, and it noticed more: that Rosário, this time, did not light up as the other times. The old women of the fountain, who had a clinical eye for these things, noted that Rosário, with the man of the waters at home, went about the village with a face that was not the usual one, not the lit-up face of the seasons of Aniceto, but a pensive face, of one who is wrestling with a decision. And one of them said to the other: that one is different this year, she is falling out of love with the man of the waters, do you see? She is looking at Justino. And the other, older and wiser: let us wait and see, for these things take their time and sometimes go awry at the end. But if she settles with Justino, she is the first woman of that house in I do not know how many not to die at the window. And she crossed herself, without one knowing whether it was for the soul of Rosário's mother, or for her own, for she too, it was said, had had her man from afar when young.

Aniceto settled in at her house, as always, and filled it with his noisy presence, and told the story of the downpour and the crossing, already enlarged, and was charming, was everything he always was. But Rosário, this time, heard him from another place. She heard him with the ears Justino had opened for her in the summer, and noticed things she had never noticed: that all Aniceto's stories were of himself, that he asked little after her, that his brightness needed an audience, and that she, for fourteen years, had been above all that, the audience, the lit-up window, the public of a man who loved himself through the love she had for him. He was no monster, Aniceto. He was only a man who needed to arrive and depart to feel alive, and who needed someone who stayed so that he could depart, and Rosário had been that someone, the fixed port from which the boat sets to the water, the near bank that gives meaning to the crossing.

The days of that flood out of season were, for Rosário, the hardest of the whole story, because it was the first time she had Aniceto present with her eyes open. In the fourteen previous years, she had always received him blind, melted, not seeing. In that flood, she received him seeing, and seeing cost. She noticed things and could not stop noticing: the way he turned all the conversations toward himself; the way he did not ask after her, after her life in the months he was far off, as though her life only existed when he was there to light it; the way he spoke of the near bank with a light disdain, still, small, and Rosário, who had learned that summer to love the near bank, felt now, beneath his charm, a lack of respect for everything that was staying, for everything that was hers. Aniceto did not despise only the village. He despised, without knowing it, what she was becoming. And there was still the worst: she noticed herself, what she became at his side. She became again the audience, the lit-up window, the woman who existed to reflect his brightness. She felt herself shrink to fit the part he gave her, and she had spent a whole summer growing at Justino's side, and now, in three days of Aniceto, she felt herself shrink again, and it was that shrinking, more than anything, that frightened and woke her.

There was, in those days, a supper that Rosário never forgot, and that was the point where the balance tipped for good. Aniceto, in her house, told, as always, and in the middle of the supper Justino knocked at the door, who came to warn that the water was going to rise in the night and that it was better they gathered what they had on the threshing-floor. Rosário, on an impulse, invited him in, to supper, and Justino came in, and the three of them supped at the same table, and it was the most revealing thing Rosário lived in fourteen years. Because at the same table, the two men showed themselves whole, side by side, and the difference shouted. Aniceto spoke, shone, filled the table with himself, and did not ask Justino a single thing about the Bridge, about the river, about the village, because for Aniceto nothing that was of staying had interest. Justino spoke little, but the little he said was to ask, to listen, to take interest in the other, and at a certain point he asked Aniceto a simple and terrible question, with no malice at all, with genuine curiosity: and you, Aniceto, where is your house? Where do you go when you go? And Aniceto, for the first time in front of Rosário, hesitated, and gave a vague answer, the world is my house, the river is my house, and Justino nodded, serious, and said nothing more, but the question stayed on the table, and Rosário saw, for the first time, that Aniceto had no answer for it, that a man who has the world for a house is a man who has no house at all, and that she, for fourteen years, had loved a man with no ground and had called his shelterlessness freedom. Justino had a house. Justino was of a place. And at the table, that night, Rosário understood that she preferred a man who knew how to say where his house was to a man who had the whole world and no place to take her.

The three of them crossed paths, at last, one afternoon, at the edge of the Bridge.

Aniceto and Rosário were going to cross to the other side, for a walk, and Justino was on the Bridge, at his work of the flood, watching the water. And the two men looked at one another, and it was a short and deep look, the look of two men who both know what is at stake, though neither of them says it. Aniceto greeted Justino with the ease of one who sees no rival, with the confidence of the brilliant man before the silent man. And Justino answered with his head, serene, without diminishing himself, and said only, to Rosário, with the voice of always:

"The water is still rising. If you cross, cross now, and come back early, for at the end of the afternoon the Bridge may not hold."

That was all. But in that all was the portrait of the two: Aniceto, crossing the Bridge without looking at the water, confident, making of the crossing an adventure; Justino, caring for the Bridge, warning of the danger, thinking of those who cross, not of himself. One crossed. The other held the crossing so that one could cross. And Rosário, in the middle of the Bridge, between the two men, saw, with a clarity that made her stop, the whole of her life: she had spent fourteen years admiring those who cross and not seeing those who hold the Bridge, and was going, that end of summer, to have to decide on which side of the river she wanted to live.

That night, lying beside Aniceto, Rosário did not sleep. And Aniceto, who felt the wind change as the men of the waters feel everything that changes, spoke, in the dark, and said a thing that in another year would have been heaven and that in that one was the beginning of the end:

"Come with me, Rosário. This time come with me. Leave this, leave the near bank, this still village, these people who count the days. Come to the other side, to my life, which is a real life, of waters, of roads, of the world. You rot here waiting for the floods. Come with me to live all the floods, in all the rivers. I will take you."

There was, in that offer of Aniceto's, a thing Rosário only understood afterwards, and that was decisive: that he invited her to go with him, but not to stay with him. They were different things, and Rosário, who had learned from Justino to hear words in earnest, heard the difference. Aniceto did not say I stay with you, he said come with me, and come with me, in the mouth of a man who does not know how to stay, means come with me in my departures, come and be, on my side, what you are on yours, the bank from which I depart, only now a bank that travels. He was not offering her the end of the waiting: he was offering her a waiting in motion, a window on wheels, the same love of absences but now without even the fixed house in which to wait. And Rosário understood that to go with Aniceto would not cure her of the window: it would worsen the disease, because at least on the near bank she had the village, the mother, the river she knew, and on his side she would have only him, him and his absences, she alone in a strange land waiting for a man who would depart all the same, because the inability to stay is not cured by changing banks, it goes with the man wherever the man goes.

And fourteen years of Rosário wanted to say yes. Fourteen years of window, of waiting, of longing, threw themselves at the offer like one who throws themselves into the water. To go with him. To end the waiting, to turn into the flood, to cease being the bank and to be the boat. It was everything she had always wanted, on the surface. And Rosário opened her mouth to say yes.

And she did not say it. Because beneath the fourteen years, deep, was the summer of Justino, was the near bank she had learned to see, was the voice of the mother at the window saying do not do as I did, and was, above all, a new question that lit up in the dark with the light of a lightning flash: if I go with him, do I cease to long for him? And if I cease to long for him, do I still love him? Or what I love in him is exactly this, the departure, the offer to depart, and if I accept the offer and stay with him every day, he is going to turn into what father turned into for mother, a common man who snores, and I am going to be left on the other side of the river, far from everything I know, loving the longing for a village I left, at the window of a new house, waiting for some other thing that goes away?

And while she hesitated, in the dark, with Aniceto's offer shining before her as everything that is of departure shines, Rosário did a thing she had never done: she compared. She put side by side, in her head, the two men and the two lives. With Aniceto, she would have all the floods, in all the rivers, the adventure, the movement, the never stopping. But she would also have what she had seen on the far bank without yet knowing she had seen it: she would be one of his banks, a window in a row of windows, a woman who would always wait for him, because a man who does not know how to stay does not stay even when we take him with us, he carries his inability to stay into the new house, and Rosário would end up, even on the other side of the world, at the window, waiting for an Aniceto who would leave the house for the next flood, wherever that house was. His disease and her disease fit so well that they were going to feed one another for ever: he departing, she waiting, the two of them calling love the dance of the absences, until they died, each at the edge of his river, looking at the other side.

And with Justino she would have the near bank, the still village, the days alike, no adventure, no flood, no story to tell at the fairs. But she would have a thing she had never had: a man who would be there in the morning, and at night, and the next day, and whom she could learn to see, on purpose, every day, and who would not give her the fever but would give her the ground, and who would let her, at last, leave the window and live in the room. And Rosário understood, in the dark, that the choice was not between the adventure and the tedium, as Aniceto put it. It was between two ways of spending one's life: one looking at the road, the other looking at the hands. One loving what goes away, the other loving what stays. And that she, for the first time, wanted the second, wanted it with a calm and deep wanting, and that the calm wanting, after all, was stronger than the fever, only slower to arrive.

"Let me think," said Rosário, in the dark, and it was the first time in fourteen years she said that to Aniceto, and Aniceto marvelled, because Rosário never thought, Rosário gave herself, and the let me think was, though neither of them yet knew it, the first brick of the Bridge she was building, slowly, for herself, in the middle of the river, between the two banks, the Bridge from which, a little later, she was going to be able to see both and choose, at last, with her eyes open, on which of them she wanted to spend the rest of her life.
