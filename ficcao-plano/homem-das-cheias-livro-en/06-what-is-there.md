## 6. What is there

The dry summer, that Rosário had always lived as a waiting room, filled that year with small things, and the small things all had the same form and the same name: Justino.

It was not a courtship. Rosário would not know how to say what it was, because she had no measure for that: everything she knew about love had the shape of Aniceto, the fever, the coming, the departure, and what happened that summer had nothing of that shape, and so Rosário, for a long time, did not call it anything, lived it without a name, as one lives a thing of which one does not yet know whether it is disease or cure.

It began with a thing of nothing, as great things almost always begin. Justino appeared at her door, on a summer late afternoon, with a basket of fish he had caught too many of, and said only, it is the afternoon's fish, it does not keep, you eat it for I alone cannot get through it. And he went, without coming in. It was his way of caring: to leave at the door what was needed and to go, without collecting thanks nor company. Rosário ate the fish, and it was good, and the next day, when she saw him at the river, she thanked him, and stayed a while talking with him, and out of the while of talk came an invitation from him to show her where that fish was caught, and out of the invitation came an afternoon at the river, and out of the afternoon came others, and it was so, fish by fish, afternoon by afternoon, with nothing resembling a declaration, that there was woven between the two what was woven. Justino never said to her I love you, that summer. He never took her hand. He limited himself to being there, to appearing, to teaching, to leaving fish at the door, to being a good and constant presence in her life, and Rosário, without noticing, went on getting used to that presence, went on awaiting it, found herself looking at the river waiting to see him, and that waiting, she noticed one day with a start, was different from the waiting for Aniceto: it did not hurt. It was a mild waiting, contented, with no fever nor distress, the waiting of one who waits for a good thing they know is coming, and not the anguished waiting of one who waits for a thing that may not come. It was the first happy waiting of Rosário's life, and it was for a man who stayed, and that, if she had known how to read the signs, said everything.

Justino taught her to read the river. He took her, on summer afternoons, to the places of the Fundo she had never looked at for always having her eyes on the other bank, and showed her how one knows the depth by the colour of the water, how one knows where the fish is by the way the current breaks, how one reads the serra to divine the rain. Rosário, who foresaw the floods in her body because of Aniceto, discovered that Justino foresaw them by the science of the river, and that his science was surer than her body, and more beautiful, because it was not made of longing: it was made of attention. Justino paid the river an attention that Rosário had never paid to anything, and Rosário began to understand that that attention was also a form of love, perhaps the deepest of all: to look at a thing every day until one knows it from within, instead of loving it only when it arrives or when it departs.

He taught her to catch the fish of the near bank, which was a fish of little fame but of a good pot, and they ate it together, a few times, at the riverside, at the end of the afternoon, and they were the first meals of Rosário's life in which she was not waiting for anything. She ate the fish, looked at the water, heard Justino say little and right, and found herself, at the end, astonished, realising that she had spent a whole afternoon without counting the days until winter, without looking at the other bank, without the fever and without the longing, and that it had been, against everything she thought possible, a happy afternoon. A strange happiness, flat, without peaks, a happiness that did not hurt, and so Rosário distrusted it, because she had learned that true love hurts, and that did not hurt, therefore that could not be love, therefore what was that.

There was one afternoon when Justino told her of his time outside, the years he had gone about the world, when young, before coming back and staying. He had gone to the city, and then farther off, to work on the building sites, on the great rivers, on the dams, and he had seen things, and earned money, and could have stayed out there, as so many of the village had stayed. And he had come back. Rosário, who had always thought Justino stayed for not knowing anything else, discovered that he stayed for having known something else and having rejected it. She asked him why, and Justino said, at the riverside, slowly: because out there I was no one, Rosário. I was one more, in a city of many, doing a work that was not mine, in a river I did not know, beside people who did not know me. And here I am Justino of the Bridge. I know every stone of this river, and the river, in a way, knows me. The people I save in the floods are people with a name, whom I saw born. Out there I had more money and was no one. Here I have less and am someone. And I preferred to be someone in a small place to being no one in a great one. There are those who call this a lack of ambition. I call it knowing what is worth the trouble. And Rosário kept that, because it was the exact reverse of everything she had admired in Aniceto, who was precisely a man of nowhere, known by all and of no one, brilliant and alone, and she understood that there were two ways of living, that of being a flood that passes everywhere and stays nowhere, and that of being a river that has a bed and a bank and a name, and that she had always admired the first and was beginning now to wish for the second.

She came to know the near bank for the first time. She discovered that the bank where she had lived for thirty-four years had places she had never seen, nooks of the river, an old willow leaning over the water, a deep pool where the boys bathed in summer, a whole land that had been there her whole life, on her side, and that she had never looked at for having her eyes always turned to the far side. And she understood, with a new sadness, that she had done to the near bank what she had done to Justino: she had treated it as landscape, as a thing that is there and so does not count, and she had lost it, all these years, by being turned toward a bank where only the absence lived.

Justino, for his part, did all this without ever pressing her, and Rosário only much later understood how difficult that was and how much it was love. A man who had loved her for years, and who had her at last at his side, all summer long, and who did not take advantage, did not push, did not ask, did not collect any of the attentions he gave her. He took her to the river and taught her, and did not turn the afternoons into courtship, let them be what they were, afternoons of two people learning each to be with the other. Rosário, used to Aniceto, who was all onslaught and rapture, marvelled at that patience, and at a certain point almost distrusted it: a man who does not press, is he not just lukewarm? And it was Benvinda, again, who gave her the key, on a day when Rosário, without saying names, asked her whether a man who does not press, who does not run after one, who waits, whether a man like that loves or does not love.

"It depends," said Benvinda. "There is the one who does not run after because he does not want to, and that one does not love. And there is the one who does not run after because he knows that what is caught running flees running, and that what is worth the trouble is waited for, and that one, Rosário, that one loves better than all who run. Those who press, many times, it is themselves they press, it is their urgency they want to kill, it is not you they want. The one who waits for you without pressing is the one who wants you, and not the relief of having you. But this, daughter, is hard to tell from outside, and is only felt from within, and takes time, for the good things always take longer to recognise than the ones that shine."

There was, in the middle of that summer, a small scene that Rosário kept and that was, without her knowing it, the first time she chose presence over absence, even if in a tiny thing. There had come, to the Grocery, a carrier passing through, a man of fairs as her father had been, and the man, at the counter, was charming, told of lands, made them laugh, and had that brightness of those who go about the world and stop only for a few days. And Rosário, who her whole life would have turned to that brightness as the plant to the sun, found herself, that time, finding him amusing and feeling nothing, no pull, no fever, and understanding, with a start, that the man would only interest her if he were about to go away, that it was his imminent departure that would have made him, in another time, irresistible, and that without that departure he was only a man telling stories at the counter. And that same afternoon, on leaving the Grocery, she crossed paths with Justino, who was coming from mending the wheel of an ox-cart, dirty, tired, with no brightness at all, and she felt for him, for that dirty and tired and present man, a warm tenderness that the brilliant carrier had not lit in her. It was the first time that presence tasted better to her than promise, even if in a small thing, and Rosário took that home like one who carries a proof: the proof that she was changing, that her taste was turning, slowly, from what departs to what stays, from brightness to warmth, from the story to the hands.

There was one afternoon when she almost.

The two of them were by the deep pool, at the end of the day, and a good silence had fallen, and Justino looked at Rosário in a way that left no doubt, and drew near, slowly, with the delicacy of one who approaches an animal that may flee, and Rosário felt his body near, and his warmth, and for an instant she wanted, wanted in a flat and deep way that had nothing of the fever of Aniceto and that was perhaps truer than all the fever, and then the fear came.

The fear came, which was the thought of always turned inside out: if I stay with this man, if I let this be, he is going to turn into landscape. He is going to turn into a thing that is there and so does not count. The fever is not going to come, because he does not go away, and I only know how to love what goes away. I am going to have him every day and I am going to stop seeing him, as I stopped seeing the near bank, and I am going to end up at the window waiting for another who goes away, like my mother. And Rosário drew back. She got up, quickly, said something about it being late, about the kitchen-garden, and went, almost fleeing, leaving Justino by the pool, alone, with the afternoon ending.

Justino did not follow her. He did not insist. He stayed by the pool, and let her go, because he had learned, from the river, that there are things that are not forced, that water is not pushed, that one waits for it to find its way. And Rosário went home almost running, with her heart leaping, and the heart leaping was, for the first time in her life, not for the coming of a man nor for the departure of a man, but for the nearness of one who was there, of one who did not go away, of one who offered her exactly what she was most afraid of, which was to stay.

But before the flight, in the instant when Justino drew near, by the pool, there was a thing Rosário kept and that was to be, later, one of her anchors: she saw his hands again. Justino, on drawing near, laid his hand on the trunk of the willow, beside her face, and Rosário saw that hand close up, the hand that knew the river, that had saved men, that had opened the channel and reinforced the wall, and the hand was there, still, a span from her, without touching her, waiting, and Rosário felt for that hand a thing that was neither fever nor longing and that she did not know how to name, and that was, though she did not know it, the beginning of the love of near, the love by the hands that the mother would speak to her of afterwards. She wanted to take that hand. She wanted to lean her face against that hand. And it was the wanting, more than anything, that frightened her, because it was a calm wanting, deep, without the delicious alarm of the loss, a wanting of one who wants to stay, and not of one who wants to be swept away, and Rosário had no muscle for that wanting, she had never exercised it, and the calm wanting, for being strange to her, gave her more fear than all the fever of Aniceto together.

At home, at the window, in the dark, Rosário tried to understand what she had fled from. She had not fled from Justino. Justino had nothing to flee from: he was good, he was whole, he was the best man she knew, and she knew it now, after that summer. She had fled from what Justino showed her about herself: that she was capable of wanting a present man, that there was in her a place where peace fit, where the flat happiness fit, and that her whole life she had locked that place and thrown away the key, because she had learned at the mother's window that that place was worth nothing, that the love that is worth something is the other one, that of the fever, of the longing, of the absence.

And for the first time Rosário felt anger at the mother. Not a great anger, a small and deep anger, the anger of one who realises they have inherited a fate and that the inheritance is costing them their life. She had felt anger at Aniceto other times, in the bad longings, but never at the mother, and the anger at the mother was different, because the mother had not abandoned her: the mother had taught her, without malice, with love even, to love in a way that condemned her to the same window, to the same waiting, to the same small life lived waiting for floods. And to see, there, at the window, that perhaps the mother had bequeathed her not a love, but a disease of love, was the hardest thing of that summer, and was also, though Rosário did not yet know it, the beginning of the cure, because one does not cure a disease one does not see, and Rosário, for the first time, was seeing it.
