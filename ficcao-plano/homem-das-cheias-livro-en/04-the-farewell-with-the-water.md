## 4. The farewell with the water

The great waters do not last. That is their nature, and that was, though Rosário never confessed it, the reason she loved them: because they ended.

At the end of a week, or two, or three, according to the winter, the rain of the serra stopped, the Fundo began to draw back into its bed, the Bridge appeared again, first the muddy deck, then the walkways, and the village went back to sewing the two banks together, and everything went back to its custom. And with the waters Aniceto drew back, because Aniceto, as he himself said, was the man of the floods: he came with them and went with them, and when the river went back to its bed he went back to the other bank and to the world over there, and Rosário stayed on the near bank with the empty bed and the months to be walked through.

The farewell was always the same, and Rosário, without knowing it, lived the whole year waiting for it, because the farewell was the peak of everything.

It is a hard thing to explain to one who has not felt it, and Rosário took her life feeling it without knowing how to explain it, but it was so: the most intense moment of her love for Aniceto was not the reunion, when he arrived, nor that of the days they were together. It was the farewell. It was the instant when he went, when the loss was certain and imminent, when the heart tightened in the anticipation of the empty months. In that instant, and only in it, Rosário felt love in a pure state, concentrated, almost unbearable for how great it was. It was as though she could only feel the size of what she had at the exact moment of losing it, like one who only sees the worth of a thing when it slips from their hands. And there was in this a terrible trap, that Rosário had never seen: if the peak of love was the loss, then she needed to lose in order to love, needed the departure in order to feel, and a love like that is condemned always to seek the departure, to feed on absences, to flee from the presence that, having no loss in sight, does not give the peak, does not give the fever, does not give the enormous thing. It was for that that Justino's presence, with no imminent loss, no departure in sight, seemed to her lukewarm: because there was not in it the delicious terror of losing him, and Rosário, addicted to that terror, confused its lack with a lack of love, when it was only a lack of fear.

The farewell of that winter was, like all of them, the peak.

Aniceto began to look at the other bank two or three days before, exactly as Vado, Rosário's father, had begun to look at the road. He grew restless, told the stories with less wit, slept badly, and Rosário knew the signs and her heart tightened, and it was that tightening, that anticipation of the loss, that made those last days the most intense of all. They loved one another more in the last days. They spoke more. He promised more, promised to come back in the next floods, promised that one day, a day that was always on the far side of something, he would stay for good. And Rosário kept the promises like one who keeps seeds, and did not notice that she sowed them every winter and that nothing was ever born, because Aniceto's promise was not to fulfil: it was to sweeten the departure, and it fulfilled its end, which was to sweeten the departure, and nothing more was asked of it.

That winter, the farewell was on a morning of weak sun, with the river already almost in its bed and the Bridge again passable, and it was there, watching him cross the Bridge on foot, like common people, instead of crossing the river by boat against the current, that Rosário had, for the first time in fourteen years, a thought that left her cold.

She thought: he crosses on foot. The waters have already fallen. He could have come on foot two days ago, and could stay two more, for the Bridge is good. It is not the river that takes him. The river already lets him stay. It is he who goes. It was always he who went. The river never brought him nor ever took him. The river was only the excuse, and I clung to the excuse fourteen years so as not to have to see the man.

It was a short thought, and Rosário pushed it away, as she had pushed away the one of the first night, because it was a thought that dismantled the whole of her life and she was not ready to have her life dismantled on a morning of weak sun. But the thought, that, did not go away: it stayed, as true thoughts stay, waiting, on the inside, working slowly.

Aniceto, in the middle of the Bridge, turned and made her the gesture of always, the open hand in the air, and Rosário made him the gesture back, and stayed watching him draw away along the Bridge, and then along the road of the other bank, until he was a point, and it was there, at the exact moment he became a point, that she felt the enormous thing, the thing that hurt, the thing the mother had described at the window: love filling her whole house just as the man disappeared. She loved him more gone than she had loved him in three weeks of presence. And this time, because of the thought of the Bridge, instead of giving herself to the enormous thing as she always gave herself, Rosário was frightened by it. She looked at her own heart lighting up with the departure and was afraid, as though she had caught a disease doing a thing she had always done for her health.

Before Aniceto departed, that morning, there was still a conversation, and it was a conversation that Rosário, afterwards, went over many times, because it was the first in which she did not play her part. Aniceto, packing his things for the departure, spoke of the next floods, of what they were going to do, of that one day when he would stay for good, and reeled off the promise of custom with the lightness of custom. And Rosário, instead of receiving it as a seed, as she had always received it, heard herself ask, and the question came out of her on its own, from the place where the thought of the Bridge was working:

"That one day, Aniceto. That one day when you stay. When is it?"

Aniceto stopped, and laughed, his easy laugh, and said it was when life settled, when he saw to his things, when, and he went on threading the whens one into another, and Rosário, for the first time, heard the whens for what they were: not dates, but postponements, not promises, but his way of never saying never. And she insisted, with a calm that surprised even herself:

"But if I asked you to stay now. This flood. Not to go with the waters, to stay here, on the near bank, with me, to live. Would you stay?"

And Aniceto looked at her, and for an instant the easy laugh hesitated, and in the hesitation Rosário read the whole truth before he said it. Aniceto did not say no. He said things, said that she knew how he was, that he was no good for a still life, that he would rot on a single bank, that he loved her too much to give her a small and unhappy version of himself, and he said all this with charm, with tenderness even, but what he said, beneath everything, was no, was never, was I do not stay, neither for you nor for anyone, because to stay is a thing I cannot do and do not wish to learn. And Rosário, who fourteen years had waited for that one day, heard at last that the one day did not exist, that it had never existed, that it was only the sugar of the farewell, and she heard it without the drama she would have made in another year, with a kind of sad calm, the calm of one who confirms an old suspicion.

"All right," she said only. "It was just to know."

She went home, after watching him depart, and the house was darkened, as it always was after he departed. And Rosário did what the mother would do: she went to the window. She sat at the window facing the river and the road of the other bank, and stayed there, at the post of the waiting, beginning the months of wasting away, and it was a gesture so old, so inherited, that she did not even notice she was doing it, until she saw herself in the glass, reflected, a woman of thirty-four years sitting at a window looking at the other bank, and behind her reflection, in memory, she saw the mother, in the same position, at the other window, waiting for Vado. Two women of the same house, two windows, the same waiting. And Rosário got up from the window, suddenly, like one who gets up from a bed where they fell ill, and did not know why, and stayed in the middle of the darkened house not knowing what to do with a body that only knew how to wait.

She got up from the window, yes, but the body, that, did not know what to do away from the window. It was a body trained to wait, and a body that waits, when it is taken from the waiting, is lost, as the hands of one who has left a trade of many years are lost. Rosário went about the house without aim, took up things and laid them down, and understood, with a kind of panic, that she did not know how to live the days without the waiting organising them. The waiting gave shape to the days: there were the days nearer the flood and the days farther off, there was the count, there was the meaning. Without the waiting, the days were only days, alike, with no top nor bottom, and Rosário did not know what to do with days like that, because she had never had them: she had always had the days turned toward a flood. It was the first time she felt the size of what she was going to have to learn, if she wished to change: it was not only to cease waiting for Aniceto. It was to learn to live days that were not the waiting for anything, whole days lived for themselves, and that, she understood with vertigo, was almost to learn to live anew, from the beginning, like one who relearns to walk.

The months without Aniceto, in the other years, all had the same form, and Rosário knew them by heart as one knows a chronic disease. There was the first week, the worst, in which the house still smelled of him and each thing reminded her of him, and in which the longing was a physical pain, in the chest, that made her lose appetite and sleep. There were then the middle weeks, in which the pain abated into a dull weight, a background sadness that tinged everything, and in which Rosário went about life like a sleepwalker, present in the gestures and absent in the soul. And there were, as the winter drew near, the weeks of hope, in which the longing turned into expectation, in which she began to look at the sky in search of rain on the serra, to count the days, to revive herself in the anticipation of the next flood. It was a cycle, and the cycle had eleven months, and Rosário had spent fourteen of them, fourteen years of eleven months each, in that going and coming between the pain of the absence and the hope of the coming, without ever, not once, having simply lived a month for the month, without referring it to Aniceto, without measuring it by the distance to the next flood.

That first month after the farewell in which she had had the thought of the Bridge, the thing went differently, and the difference was small but Rosário noticed it: the first week, that of the physical pain, was shorter. The pain came, but on the third or fourth day, instead of feeding it as she had always done, giving it hours at the window, Rosário found herself wanting to do another thing, wanting to go out, wanting to go to the river, and the pain, unfed, shrank more quickly than usual. It was the first proof, still without her quite understanding what it proved, of a thing Justino would tell her later: that the longing, like the fire, lives on what we throw on it, and that a longing to which one gives no hours at the window goes out much more quickly than a fed longing.

She went to the door. She opened it. Out there, the common day, the village sewing itself together, the river drawing back. And on the Bridge, in the distance, she saw Justino, with the other men, cleaning the deck of the mud the flood had left, preparing the Bridge for the months without waters. The man who stayed, caring for what linked the banks, on the day the man who came had gone. And Rosário stayed at the door watching him work, and it was the first time in fourteen years that, on the day of a farewell, she looked at the near bank instead of the far one. She felt nothing special. Justino was Justino, the Bridge was the Bridge. But she looked. And to look, after fourteen years of always looking at the other side, was already a new thing, even if she did not know the name of what she was doing.

The village, in the days after Aniceto's departure, treated Rosário with a special care, the care one has with the sick and with the bereaved, because everyone knew that Rosário, after the man of the floods went, entered her dark season. The neighbour women appeared with some pretext or other, a dish of food, a message, only to see her, to draw her into conversation, not to let her sink entirely. Benvinda sent word for her to come to the Grocery, always found her a delivery to make, anything that would force her to leave the house. It was an invisible net the village spread beneath Rosário every year, at the beginning of each season without waters, and Rosário, in the other years, scarcely noticed it, so closed she went in her wasting away. That year she noticed. She noticed the village's care, and was moved, and understood a thing she had never understood: that while she spent her life turned toward the other bank, waiting for a man who came and went, there was a whole village on the near bank that loved her in a silent and constant way, that was always there, that held her up in her dark seasons with no fuss at all. The whole village loved her as Justino loved her: staying, caring, without brightness. And she, turned toward the absence, had never seen either the village or Justino, so busy was she loving the one who was not there.

That night, alone, she did a thing she had never done on a first day of absence: she did not weep with longing. She tried. She sat at the window, waiting for the good longing, the enormous thing that filled her when Aniceto departed, and the longing came, but it came mixed, for the first time, with another thing, a thing small and uncomfortable that was almost anger, almost weariness, the low voice of the thought of the Bridge saying: he crossed on foot, Rosário. The waters already let him stay. It was he who went.

And Rosário, at the window, in the dark, found herself asking a question she had never asked in fourteen years, and the question was simple and terrible: and what if I have been loving wrong? And what if this that I call love, this fever of the comings and the departures, this longing that fills me, what if this is not love, but only the way my mother taught me to wait? And what if there is another love, one that does not hurt, one that stays, and what if I do not know it, and what if I pass by it every day, on the near bank, and do not see it, because it is always there, because it does not go away, because it does not give me the longing by which I learned to recognise that I love?

The question stayed in the house, with her, the whole night. And out there the river fell, and the Bridge appeared, and on the Bridge, until late, a man worked in the dark preparing the crossing for the winter without waters, without knowing that on the other side of the village a woman had, that night, for the first time, turned her face from the far bank to the near bank, and asked herself whether she had not been, for fourteen years, calling love the disease of not knowing how to stay.
