## 10. Learning to see

To see, Rosário discovered, was not an instant: it was a trade, and like all trades it was learned slowly, with knack and with relapses, and no one taught it for free.

It was a trade of months, and the first gesture of it was to say no to Aniceto.

There were those, in the village, who marvelled at the quickness with which Rosário left the man of the waters and settled with Justino, and those who said, at the fountain, that that was not love, that it was spite, that Rosário had grown tired of waiting and had grabbed what was at hand. And Rosário knew it was said, because in the village everything is known, and she had, at first, a fear that it might be true: a fear of going to Justino not out of loving him, but out of weariness of Aniceto, out of age, out of fear of being alone, out of all the small reasons that lead women to settle with what is left over. It was a doubt that bit her for weeks, and that she took, one night, to Justino himself, because she had learned to say things aloud. She told him: I am afraid of being with you out of being tired of the other, and not out of loving you, and that would be unjust to you, who deserve to be loved for yourself and not as a leftover. And Justino heard, and answered with his calm: and what harm is there in that, Rosário? Everyone arrives at a love by some path, and the paths are almost always not beautiful. Some arrive by weariness, others by solitude, others by age. The path does not matter. What matters is what one does after arriving. If you, after arriving by weariness, go on learning to love me in earnest, then the weariness was only the door, and the door one forgets when one is inside the house. Do not distress yourself about the door you came in by. Distress yourself, rather, about what you do inside the house. And Rosário was left with that, and was at ease, because it was true: it mattered less why she had come, and more what she did now that she had come, and what she did, day by day, was to learn to love that man on purpose, and that, whatever the door, was real love, or was becoming such.

She told Aniceto, when he came back from up the river, that she was not going with him, and it was a conversation that Rosário kept, because it was the first time she spoke to Aniceto as an equal, without the spell, without the fever, with her eyes open. Aniceto did not understand, or did not want to understand, and tried the arguments of custom, the charm, the adventure, the real life of the waters, and at a certain point, on seeing that it did not catch, he changed his tone and said a thing he perhaps meant to be cruel: you are going to regret it, Rosário, you are going to stay there bound to that man of the Bridge and to that still village, and one day, old, you are going to remember me and what I offered you, and you are going to see that you had life within reach of your hand and chose the slow death. And Rosário, who in another time would have trembled at that, heard him with a calm that surprised even herself, and answered: perhaps you are right, Aniceto, and one day I will regret it. But do you know what is worse than regretting a choice? It is never having chosen anything. It is spending your life waiting, like my mother, without ever deciding anything, letting the waters decide for you. I prefer to choose and regret it than to wait and not live. At least the choice is mine. The waiting was never mine: it was my mother who gave it me, and her mother her, and I do not want to give it to anyone else. And Aniceto did not know what to answer to that, because he was a man who had also never chosen anything, who let himself be carried by the waters as Rosário let herself stay at the windows, and for an instant, only an instant, before he put on again the easy laugh, there was in his eyes a thing that looked like fear, or envy, or the sudden suspicion that that woman he was going to lose had just done a thing he had never known how to do in his life: decide.

Aniceto departed with the waters, as always, and made the gesture of the open hand in the air, in the middle of the Bridge, and Rosário made him the gesture back, and stayed watching him become a point on the other bank. And she felt the old enormous thing, the longing wanting to fill the house. But this time she recognised it by its name, and instead of giving herself to it, she said to it, low, as one speaks to an old and stubborn dog: I know. I know you hurt. But you are not love, you are only the habit of calling you love. And the longing, named, lost half its strength, because things, when one gets their name right, shrink.

With Justino, it was another learning, and harder, because it was from near.

She went back to seek him, after Aniceto departed, and told him everything, of the far bank, of the middle of the Bridge, of what she had understood. And Justino heard, with no triumph at all, with no I-told-you-so, because he was no man for that, and at the end said only: then stay. And they began, slowly, to learn one another from near, and it was there that the relapses came, and they were many, and Rosário learned not to give up on account of them.

There were the days when the peace of being with Justino tasted to her of tedium, and the tedium frightened her, because her old catechism told her that if there is no fever there is no love, and Rosário had to learn, day by day, that peace is not the lack of love: it is love without the alarm of the loss. That the fever she felt with Aniceto was not the measure of love, it was the measure of the fear of losing him, and that a love one is not afraid to lose gives no fever at all, and that that, far from being little, is everything, is the rest of a heart that spent its life in alarm. But it cost. There were afternoons when Rosário, at Justino's side, missed the fever, and had to hold herself back not to go and fetch it, not to invent a drama, a departure, a longing, only to feel the old delicious alarm. It was like one who gives up a vice: the body asked for the poison, and she had to tell it no, every day, and some days she said no easily and others with her hand trembling.

There was the day she caught herself at the window. It was in the first winter after that summer, when the great waters came and she knew Aniceto would come, and the body, treacherous, took her to the window, to the old window, to look at the other bank, waiting. And Rosário found herself there, at the window, at the mother's post, with her heart beating waiting for the waters, and she had a fright, and then a sadness, and then, slowly, a decision: she got up from the window, went out into the street, crossed the village, and went to Justino, who was on the Bridge watching the flood, and stayed at his side, on the Bridge, watching the water rise, instead of watching it from the window waiting for whom it brought. And it was a small gesture and it was enormous: to trade the window for the Bridge, the post of the waiting for the place from which one cares for what links, the looking at the other bank for the being at the side of what stays. Aniceto crossed by boat in that flood, as always, laughing at the river, and Rosário watched him cross from the height of the Bridge, at Justino's side, and did not light up. She saw a man she loved in passing, with the tenderness one has for one who no longer governs us, and she went home, that night, with Justino, by choice, and the house, for the first time in a winter of great waters, neither lit up nor went out: it stayed only lit, with an even light, without peaks, the light of the houses where one lives.

There was the greater relapse, and Rosário told it afterwards to Justino, because she had learned that the hidden relapses rule and the spoken ones shrink. It was on a winter night, without a flood, when there came to her a longing for Aniceto so strong, so physical, that she nearly went out of the house to go to the bank, to see whether the waters rose, to see whether he came. Her whole body asked her for the fever, the coming, the rapture, and the peace of Justino, that night, tasted to her of ash, of little, of small life. And Rosário was on the edge of doing what she had done her whole life: inventing a reason for the fever, going to fetch the poison. She even dressed to go out. And she went to the door, and opened it, and the cold of the night struck her face, and it was the cold that woke her: she understood that she was about to throw away a man who stayed for a longing for a man who went away, and she understood that the longing, however physical it was, was only the body asking for the old vice, and that to yield to it was not love, it was relapse. She closed the door. She went back inside. And instead of going to fetch the fever, she went to fetch Justino: she woke him, and told him, in the dark, that she had been nearly going out to the bank in search of a flood, and that she was afraid of herself, and that she needed him to know, to help her not to fall. And Justino, in the dark, instead of being hurt or frightened, did the right thing: he took her hand, and said only, sleepy and sure, then next time that wanting comes to you, you wake me, and the two of us stay awake until it passes, for those wantings all pass if one is not alone with them. And it passed. The two of them stayed awake, hand in hand, in the dark, until the longing passed, and it passed, and Rosário learned that night that the secret of not relapsing was not to have strength alone: it was not to be alone with the wanting, it was to have a man who stayed and whom one could wake in the middle of the night to say I am afraid of myself.

There was a relapse that Rosário never told anyone except, years later, Justino himself, laughing at it by then. One winter, alone one afternoon, she found herself doing an absurd thing: inventing a quarrel with Justino, out of nothing, over a tiny thing, only to feel the drama, the tension, the possibility of the loss. Aniceto had given her fourteen years of drama, of departures and returns, of intensity, and there was in her body a vice of that drama, a hunger for storm, and the peace of Justino, for having no storm at all, left that hunger unsatisfied. And Rosário, that afternoon, was nearly provoking an artificial storm, getting up a reason for a quarrel, only to feel, afterwards, the sweetness of the reconciliation, which was the thing she had most loved in Aniceto, the reconciliations after the departures. She stopped in time. She understood what she was doing: she was trying to turn Justino into an Aniceto, trying to put storm into a man who was of calm waters, and she understood that that would be not only unjust to him but the ruin of everything, because what she loved in Justino was exactly the absence of storm, the peace, and to want to put storm into him was to want to destroy the thing that had saved her. She learned, that afternoon, to distinguish two hungers: the hunger for love, which Justino satisfied, and the hunger for drama, which was the old vice, the after-effect of fourteen years of Aniceto, and which was no love at all, it was only the body asking for the poison it had got used to. And she learned to feed the first and to leave the second to starve, until it, slowly, over the years, fell silent.

"I am afraid of ceasing to see you," said Rosário to Justino, another night, because she had learned from him to say things aloud. "Of having you every day and ceasing to see you, as I ceased to see the near bank. It is what most frightens me of all. More than losing you. I am afraid of winning you and then ceasing to look at you."

And Justino, who knew the river and so knew the things that last, answered with the wisdom of one who has cared for the Bridge for thirty-four years:

"Then look at me on purpose, Rosário. Seeing, when a thing is always there, does not come on its own, as it comes when it goes away. You have to do it on purpose, every day, as I go to check the Bridge every day even when there is no flood, because a bridge no one looks at is a bridge that falls. The things that stay are not seen by miracle. They are seen by care. Your mother saw your father because he went away: it was the going-away that showed him to her. You are going to have to see me without my going away. It is harder, and it is better, because what is seen by care lasts, and what is only seen by longing always ends at the window." He smiled, his dry smile. "I will help you. I will remind you, from time to time, that I am still here. Not as a promise. As information."

They learned one another by the seasons, which is how the things that last are learned. In the winter, Rosário went with Justino to watch the Bridge, and learned the names of the floods, to read the serra, to know when the water was to be feared and when it was only to be respected. In the spring, they planted together, and Rosário discovered that there was a deep pleasure, that she had never known, in putting a seed in the earth and coming back every day to watch it grow, slowly, without haste, a pleasure that was the exact opposite of the fever of Aniceto, a pleasure of one who stays to watch it grow, and not of one who passes and sweeps away. In the summer, they went to the low river, to the deep pool, and bathed like the boys, and laughed, and Rosário understood that the flat happiness, the one she had distrusted, the one that had seemed to her tedium, was after all the real happiness, the one one could have every day, unlike the fever, which was rare and short and always left, behind it, the long months of void. In the autumn, they sat at the door, at the end of the afternoons, watching the village, and it was on those afternoons that Rosário realised she had stopped counting the days. She did not count the days until winter, until the waters, until Aniceto. She lived the days she was in. Benvinda was right: she lived in the month she was in, and not in the one that was lacking, and each day, for that, was a whole day, and not half a day spent waiting for the other.

There was a wedding, in the village, that year, and Rosário went with Justino, and in the middle of the feast, watching them, Benvinda said to another woman, without knowing that Rosário heard, look at Rosário, who would have thought, she settled in the end after all, she went about years after that man of the waters and she went and settled with Justino, with the one who was at hand the whole time, women take so long to see what they have in front of them. And Rosário, who in another time would have been offended by that settling, by that the one who was at hand, heard it now and smiled, because she knew a thing that Benvinda did not know: that she had not settled, in the sense of having contented herself with little. She had cured herself. And that the one who was at hand the whole time was not little: it was the much she had not known how to see, and that only a whole crossing, to the far bank and back, had at last taught her to look at.

And Rosário laughed, and it was a good laugh, flat, without fever, the laugh of one who is learning, slowly, with relapses, the hardest thing there is: to see what stays, to love what is there, to live on the near bank with her feet planted, instead of at the window with her eyes on the far one.
