## 2. The mother's window

To understand Rosário one must know the window, and the window is the mother's, and the mother was called Lembrança, which was an apt name like almost all those of Véspera, because Lembrança spent her life living more on what she remembered than on what she had before her.

Lembrança's house, on the near bank, had a window facing the river and the road that came from the fairs, and that window was, for forty years, the post of a woman waiting. The husband, Vado, was a carrier, a man of fairs and of roads, who loaded the donkey with cloths and crockery and set out along the road to sell from land to land, and came back with the seasons, now with the purse full now empty, now with presents now with debts, but he always came back with the same thing: with the absence all spent, ready to begin again. Vado did not know how to stay. On the third day at home he began to look at the road, and on the fifth he already had the donkey loaded, and Lembrança took her leave of him at the door and went at once to the window, and from the window watched him draw away along the road until he was a point, and stayed there, and at that exact moment, the moment in which he became a point and disappeared, Lembrança loved him more than she had loved him in the five days he had been at home.

Rosário grew up seeing this, and what is seen in childhood is not learned: it sticks, becomes bone, becomes the natural form of things.

She saw the mother at the window. She saw the mother light up when in the distance the father's donkey was sighted arriving, and she saw the mother waste away on the days the father was at home, because the father at home was a common man, who ate and snored and complained of the business and had nothing of the brightness he had when he was a longing. And she saw the mother light up again when the father departed, as though the departure gave her back the true husband, the one from afar, the one who fit whole in the window and in the waiting. The mother loved a man who only existed between the comings. The man of the comings, the one of flesh, that one almost bothered her, as reality bothers one who loves a portrait.

"Your father," the mother said to her once, Rosário already a young girl, the two of them at the window at the end of the afternoon, "your father only loved me well from afar. From near we were two strangers sharing a bed. But when he went along the road, and I stayed here watching him go, daughter, then yes, then I felt a thing so great it hurt. Then he was all mine, more mine than if he were here beside me. Strange, is it not? I loved him better going than staying."

She said it without sadness, almost with pride, like one who tells of a talent. And Rosário, a young girl, kept that as a truth of the world: that real love is the one from afar, that of longing, the one that hurts; and that presence, the man who stays and eats and snores, is the poor version, the landscape, what is left over when the longing goes. She learned, without a word of teaching, that to love is to wait, and that the peak of love is not the embrace: it is the point on the road, the moment in which the beloved becomes absence and the absence becomes longing and the longing fills the whole house with a thing that presence never managed.

Rosário kept, from childhood, a scene that was the summary of everything, and that only much later she understood to be the summary of everything. It was one of the father's comings. The donkey had been sighted in the distance, on the road, at the end of the afternoon, and the mother, at the window, had lit up entirely, and had said, with a voice that Rosário never heard from her at other times, your father is coming, he is coming, and had set to arranging her hair and the house, in a flutter, and Rosário, a little girl, infected, had gone to the window beside the mother to watch the father arrive, and the two of them had stayed there, mother and daughter, in a rapture, watching the point on the road grow and become a donkey and become a man. And it was while the father was still a point, and then a shape, and then almost a man, that the mother was happiest. Rosário remembered that clearly: the mother's happiness rose as the father drew near, and reached its peak when he was still a hundred steps off, recognisable but still to arrive, and then, when he really arrived, when he came through the door, real, tired, smelling of the road and of sweat, with the complaints of the business already in his mouth, the mother's happiness fell. It did not disappear, but it fell, as a tide falls, and the mother who received the man at the door was no longer the lit-up mother of the window: she was a woman tending a tired husband. And the little Rosário, without knowing why, felt vaguely cheated, as though the best part had been the waiting, and the arrival were already the beginning of the next farewell.

Years later, a grown woman, Rosário understood what she had seen that afternoon: she had seen the mother love the father more arriving than arrived, and had learned, without a word, that the best of love is the approach, the point that grows, the imminence, and that presence, the arrival in fact, is already a kind of loss, because it ends the unbearable sweetness of the waiting. She had learned to love the eve of things more than the things. And Véspera, the village, lived up to its name in her: Rosário always lived on the eve of love, in the imminence, in the approach, and fled, without knowing it, from the arrival, because the arrival spoiled the best part for her.

Rosário had one memory of the father present, one only that she liked, and it was revealing precisely for being one only. It was of an afternoon when Vado, at home between two fairs, had taken her, a little girl, to the river, and had taught her to make little boats of pine bark, and they had set them sailing on the current, and the father had laughed, and had been, for an afternoon, a whole father, present, hers. Rosário kept that afternoon like a treasure, and for years thought she kept it for having been happy. As a grown woman, she understood another thing: that she kept it because it had been rare, because it had been the one time the father had really been there, with her, without his head on the road. And she understood, with sadness, that the mother had no such afternoons to keep, because the mother did not go to the river with Vado: the mother stayed at the window waiting for him to come back from the river, and lost, for being at the window, the afternoons of the river. The little girl, who had not yet learned to stay at the window, had had the afternoon of the little boats. The mother, who already only knew how to wait, had not. And there was in this a lesson that Rosário only put together much later: that the waiting does not steal only the years, it steals the afternoons, it steals the little boats of pine bark, it steals the small good hours that only one who is present has, and that the mother, at the window, had traded a whole life of possible afternoons for the enormous and sterile thing of loving a point on the road.

The father died at a fair far off, and was buried far off, and Lembrança did not even go to the burial, because it was far and she was already old, and the village found it strange that a widow should not weep much for her husband. It was Rosário who went, alone, in the name of the mother who could not go, and it was the first time she left Véspera, and it was strange: the father's grave was in a land he had loved more than the house, because it was a land of passage, of fair, of road, and Rosário understood, at the edge of the grave, that the father had died where he wished to die, on the way, and not at home, and that that, which to the mother had seemed a final abandonment, had been perhaps, for Vado, the only fidelity of which he was capable: fidelity to the road. But Rosário understood, and never said it to anyone: the mother did not weep for the husband because, for the mother, the dead husband was not very different from the living husband. The living husband had already been, for forty years, an absence with short comings. The dead husband was an absence with no comings at all, but Lembrança knew how to wait for absences better than anyone, and so she went on at the window after he died, waiting for a man who no longer came, and the village said poor thing, she has lost her wits, and it was no wits she had lost: it was only that the waiting, for Lembrança, had never needed an object. To wait was her way of loving, and one could wait for a dead man as well as for a living one, better even, because the dead one did not spoil the waiting with disappointing comings.

Rosário had all this inside her, without knowing how to name it, when Aniceto appeared, at her twenty years, with the first great waters she remembered as a woman.

And Aniceto fit into the wound like the key in the lock, because Aniceto was the father made a lover: a man who came and went, who lit up at the arrival and at the departure, who filled the house with noisy presence for a few days and then became a point on the water, absence, longing, real love according to the catechism Rosário had learned at the mother's window. With Aniceto, Rosário could love in the only way she knew: waiting. The months without floods were the months of the mother's empty road, and she lived them as the mother had lived them, at the window, wasting away and keeping the longing like a treasure, and the days of the great waters were the father's comings, festival and disappointment mixed, presence that was never as good as the longing for it had been.

The women of Lembrança's house came from far back in the fate, and Rosário knew it in pieces, over her life, joining what the mother and the aunts and the old women let fall. Lembrança's grandmother, Rosário's great-grandmother, had married a man of the sea, who went to the fisheries of the north and came back once a year, and had died in a shipwreck, and the great-grandmother had waited for him years after knowing him dead, at the seaside, they say, in a coastal land from which the family had come to Véspera. Lembrança's mother, Rosário's grandmother, had married a man of the army, who departed for the wars and the barracks and came on leave after leave, and that one too had been more loved in the absences than in the presences. And Lembrança had married Vado, the carrier, and Rosário was now going to fall in love with Aniceto, the man of the waters. Four generations, four men who went away: the one of the sea, the one of the army, the one of the fairs, the one of the river. It was as though the women of that house had, generation after generation, a nose for men of departure, an instinct that always led them, among all the possible men, to choose exactly the one who did not stay. It was not chance. It was inheritance. Each mother taught the daughter, without words, to find beautiful what goes away, and the daughter grew up finding beautiful what goes away, and went to choose, among all, the one who went away most, and so the fate passed, intact, from window to window, from woman to woman, for four generations, waiting for one of them, at last, to break it.

The difference, and Rosário was only to understand it much later, is that the mother, at least, had had a man who came to stay a few days and departed to work and came back. Vado departed out of necessity, it was his trade, and he loved badly but he loved from afar by force of the fairs. Aniceto did not. Aniceto departed out of nature. There was no fair that took him; there was only the inability to stay, dressed as adventure, and Rosário had inherited the mother's fate and had even worsened it, because she had chosen, without knowing it, a man who absented himself not out of duty, but out of being who he was, and who therefore never, never, unlike Vado, was going one day to hang up the donkey and stay.

The mother's window was not only a window: it was an institution of the house, a furniture of the soul. It had a chair of its own, the window chair, with the rush worn in the place where Lembrança sat, and it had a sill where the paint was gnawed from so many elbows, from so many hours of waiting leaned there. When Rosário was small, the window was the mother's; when the father died and Rosário grew up, the window went on being the mother's, but Rosário, without noticing, had gone on arranging her own window, in her own house, facing also the river and the road of the other bank, and the chair, and the sill, and the hours. She had inherited the furniture of the soul as one inherits the colour of the eyes: without choosing, without noticing, by being born in that house. And there was in that inheritance an irony that Rosário would only see much later: the mother had taught her to wait at the window, and the daughter, obedient, had got herself a man, Aniceto, who justified the window, who gave the waiting an object, because a woman who has learned to wait needs, so as not to go mad, someone to wait for, and Aniceto was perfect for that, he came and went enough to keep the window always justified, always with a reason, always with a road to look down.

That winter, with Aniceto again at home and the waters at their height, Rosário went one afternoon to visit the mother, who lived now with a neighbour who looked after her, old and ever more absent from the world. She found her, as always, at the window. Lembrança scarcely spoke of present things any more, but at the window she went on, with her eyes on the road, waiting.

"He is arriving," said the mother, when Rosário came in, without taking her eyes from the road. "Your father. He is coming. I feel it."

"Father died, mother," said Rosário, gently, for it had to be said from time to time.

"I know," said Lembrança, and for an instant the lucidity came back to her eyes, an old and deep lucidity. "I know he died, daughter. But I go on feeling him arriving. Forty years feeling a man arriving are not unlearned by a small thing like death." And she turned her face to the daughter, and looked at her, and said, with that clear-sightedness the old have in flashes: "And you? Is yours here? That one of the waters?"

"He is, mother."

"Just so." Lembrança turned back to the window. "Enjoy him while he is here, daughter, for it is little. And then come here, to the window, with me, to wait. The two of us know how to wait. It is what we know how to do." And she said, low, to the road, a thing that Rosário took home and that did not let her sleep: "It is a pity. It is the only thing we know how to do well, and it is the one that gives the least life."
