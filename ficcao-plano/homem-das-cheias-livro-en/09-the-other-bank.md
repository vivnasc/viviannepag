## 9. The other bank

Rosário crossed to the other bank, but she did not cross with Aniceto: she crossed alone, on purpose, on a day when he had gone to see to his things up the river, and she went to see, with her own eyes, the life he offered her, before deciding whether she accepted it.

She crossed by the Bridge, on foot, on a day when the waters had fallen enough, and to cross the Bridge alone, without Aniceto, was already a strangeness to her: fourteen years watching Aniceto cross the river by boat, against the current, and she herself had never crossed except in one way, waiting for him on one side or the other. To cross the Bridge with her own feet, by her own decision, to go and see the life of a man instead of waiting for it, was already, in itself, a new act, and Rosário felt, in the middle of the Bridge, a thing she had never felt: the small and enormous power of one who decides to go and see instead of staying to imagine. The imagination had fed her fourteen years: she had imagined the other bank, the life of Aniceto, the one day when he would stay. The imagination is the food of one who waits. And she was now going to trade the imagination for the sight, was going to see with her eyes what she had imagined with her longing, and she knew, at bottom, that to see was going to be harder than to imagine, because the imagination beautifies and the eyes do not, but she chose to see, because she had understood, that summer of Justino, that to live on imagination is to live at the window, and that she wanted, at last, to leave the window, even if what there was to see on the other side broke her heart.

It was the first time in her life she set foot on the other bank of her own will, and not at his call. The other bank, which she had loved from afar for fourteen years, which had been the horizon of all her windows, the far side where love lived, was, seen close up, a bank like the near one: fields, houses, people working, a river that from there looked the same but in reverse, with her village on the other side, small, in the mist, in the exact way she had always seen the far bank. And it was that the first lesson of the crossing: that the far bank, for one who lives on the far one, is the near one, and that her village, seen from there, was someone's other bank, the horizon of another window, the far side of another waiting. There was no magic bank. There were only banks, and the magic was in the distance, and the distance undid itself on crossing.

She sought the life of Aniceto, and found it, and it was the second lesson, and that one hurt.

She asked after him, on the far bank, and the people knew him, of course, everyone knew Aniceto, and spoke of him with the same mixture of charm and reserve with which the near village spoke: Aniceto, ah, that one is always about to depart, that one does not stop, that one is of the waters. And Rosário, joining here and there, understood what she had never wanted to understand: that she was not Aniceto's woman. She was one of Aniceto's banks. That he had, up the river and down the river, other ports, other lit-up windows, other women who waited for him and whom he visited with his seasons, and that for Aniceto life was that, a series of arrivals and departures among a series of ports, and that she, Rosário, his Rosário of the great waters, was one of those ports, perhaps the most beloved, perhaps not, but a port, a bank, a window among others.

It was not out of malice that Rosário knew. It was because she asked, for the first time, instead of waiting. Fourteen years of waiting, and she had never known; one day of asking, and she knew everything. And she understood, with the cold clarity of the far bank, that the waiting is not only a way of loving: it is also a way of not wanting to know. That she had stayed at the window fourteen years in part because the window spared her the truth, because while she waited she did not need to ask, and while she did not ask she could go on thinking herself the woman of a man of whom she was only one of the banks.

She sought the house of Aniceto, and found it, and it was the third lesson. She expected to find a house, a home, anything that was his place in the world, the nest from which he set out for the floods. She found no house. Aniceto had no house, on the far bank nor anywhere: he had a rented room here, a cot in someone else's house there, slept where it fell out, kept his things in others' houses, and his life, seen close up, was not the glorious adventure he told: it was the life of a man with no ground, of a man who at forty-odd years old had nowhere to drop dead, who lived on expedients and on boats and on the goodwill of those who took him in for a few days, charmed by his stories, and who moved on when the charm ended or when the season changed. Aniceto was not free, Rosário understood, with a deep pity. He was shelterless. The freedom she had so admired in him, the not being bound to anything, the coming and going like the river, was, seen close up, the condemnation of a man who had never learned to make a house, to put down a root, to stay, and who disguised the inability as adventure, and the shelterlessness as freedom, and the solitude as independence. And Rosário, who had envied that life fourteen years, saw it at last for what it was: sad. An old age arriving with no house nor anyone, a man crossing rivers ever more tired toward ports ever darker, and some end or other, alone, in a borrowed bed of a strange land. There was nothing there to envy. There was, rather, someone to pity, and to thank, perhaps, for having taught her, even by the way of disillusion, what she did not want for herself.

She sat at the riverside, on the far bank, looking at her village on the other side, small in the afternoon, and stayed there a long time, letting the truth settle. And the truth did not make her weep as she would have expected. It made her, rather, feel an enormous weariness, the weariness of fourteen years of waiting that suddenly showed themselves for what they were: fourteen years loving the lack of a man who was made of lacks, filling the house with the longing for one who had never intended to stay, living from winter to winter like the river, full and dry, waiting for a flood that always brought her the same: a charming man who came to go.

There was, on the far bank, a woman Rosário saw, and who was the mirror she needed. She was a young woman, at the door of a house, with a child in her arms, and Rosário, on asking after Aniceto, understood by the manner of the people that that woman was, or had been, or still was a little, another of Aniceto's banks, another lit-up window on the other side of another piece of river. And the woman looked at Rosário, and Rosário looked at the woman, and the two understood one another, without a word, with that mute understanding of those who love the same absent man and discover, on seeing one another, that they are not rivals: they are colleagues of one same waiting, partners of one same fate. And Rosário saw, in the face of that young woman with the child in her arms, her own face a few years from now, or the mother's face a few years from now, the face of all the women Aniceto left on all the banks, lit up and waiting, raising alone what he left them on passing. And she had, more than jealousy, a shiver of recognition and of fear: it was that she was going to be, if she went on. A window in a row of windows. A bank in a series of banks. A woman at the door with a child in her arms, looking at the water, waiting for a flood that would bring, for a few days, a man who was of all and of none.

She did not speak with the woman. It was not needed. They exchanged only a look, and in that look Rosário knew everything, and thanked that stranger, in silence, for having shown her, better than any mirror, what awaited her on the far side of the river.

And it was looking at the near village, from the other side of the river, that Rosário saw Justino.

He was on the Bridge, of course, at his work, small in the distance, but she knew him by his way of being there, by the calm with which he moved, and she had, on seeing him from all that distance, from the far bank, a thing she had never had: she saw him as one sees what is far. She saw Justino at a distance, and at a distance Justino lit up in her, gained the brightness that far things have, and Rosário understood, with a start, the whole trick of her disease: it was not that she could not love Justino. It was that she could only love at a distance, and as Justino was always near, she had never had the distance to love him. It was enough to put him on the other side of a river for him to light up. And that, instead of being a misfortune, was a hope, because it showed that the problem was not Justino: it was the distance she needed, and the distance, unlike a man, could be changed. One could learn to love from near. One could build, within oneself, the right distance, the one that lets one see without needing to lose.

Before going back, she did one more thing, and it was to speak with the young woman of the child in her arms, at the end, the shame of both overcome. She drew near, and asked her, without preamble, how long she had known Aniceto, and the woman, who understood at once who Rosário was and what she came to ask, answered with a sad frankness: a few years, she said, he comes with the waters, as to all of us, and goes with them. I have had him a few years as you have him, I suppose, and as the one at the mill has him, up the river, and perhaps others I do not know of. And she looked at the child in her arms, and Rosário did not ask, but the woman answered the question that was not asked: it is his, yes, but he does not know it exists, or knows and pretends, which comes to the same, for he is no man for children, he is a man of waters. And Rosário looked at that child, daughter of Aniceto, whom he did not raise, whom he had left on a bank as he left everything, and saw, in that child, the possible future of a choice she could make: to go with Aniceto, or to stay waiting for him, and to end up so, with a child in her arms on some bank, waiting for a man who came with the waters and who of the children made what he made of everything, left them on passing. She thanked the woman, and the woman said to her, at the farewell, a thing that Rosário took across the river: you are still in time, for you have no children of his to bind you; I no longer am, I stay, because I have this one, but you, if you have sense, flee while you can, flee to one who stays. And she was the second woman, after the mother, to tell Rosário flee from your fate, and it was said by one who could no longer flee from hers, and that gave it a weight that the mother, from within the family, had not managed to give: the weight of a stranger, in the same fate, warning her from the edge of the well.

She got up. And she did, then, the thing that gives the chapter its name and that was the most important gesture of her life until then: she crossed the river again, alone, on purpose, back to the near bank. Not fleeing, not summoned: by choice. And in the middle of the Bridge, at the highest point, she stopped.

She stopped in the middle of the Bridge, between the two banks, and looked to one side and to the other. To the far bank, Aniceto's, the one of the waters, the one of the love that came and went, that she had loved for fourteen years and that she saw now for what it was: beautiful from afar, empty up close, a bank of passage. And to the near bank, hers, Justino's, the one of the village, the one of the love that stays, that she had never known how to see for being always turned to the other side, and that she saw now, from the Bridge, with new eyes: the bank where one lives, not the one one dreams.

And she understood, in the middle of the Bridge, with the river passing beneath her feet, going away, always going away, that she did not have to choose one bank against the other. She had to choose where to live. And that to live, unlike to love at the window, is done from one side only, with the feet planted, on the bank where one stays. The water that passes is beautiful, and it passes, and one does not live in the water: one lives on the bank, and the bank where she wanted to live, she saw at last, from the height of the Bridge, at the end of that summer, was the near one, the one of the man who stayed, the one of the bed that bears the water passing and that for that makes of it a river and not a lost flood.

She stayed a long time in the middle of the Bridge, that day, and it was perhaps the most important time of her life, because it was the time in which she undid, alone, the metaphor that had governed her since she was a girl. She had always thought that the other bank, Aniceto's, was the place of love, and the near one, hers, the place of the waiting. She understood now that she had turned everything about: the other bank was not love, it was the flight; and the near one was not the waiting, it was the life, only she had lived it as waiting by being always turned that way. Love was in neither of the banks, after all. Love was the Bridge: it was what linked, what stayed, what bore the water passing. And she had spent her life at the two wrong ends — at the window of the near bank looking at the far one, or in the bed of the far bank dreaming of the near one — and never, never had she stopped in the middle, on the Bridge, in the one place from which the two are seen and one can choose. Justino lived on the Bridge, was of the Bridge, was the Bridge-man, the one who links, who stays, who bears. And Rosário, in the middle of the Bridge that day, understood that to choose Justino was not to choose the near bank against the far one: it was to choose the Bridge against the two banks, it was to choose to link instead of to wait or to flee, it was to choose to be, she too, a woman who stays and links, and not one who waits or flees.

She finished crossing. She set her feet on the near bank. And it was the first time in her life that to set her feet on the near bank tasted to her, not of the waiting room, not of the place from which one looks at the other side, but of home, of the arrival, of the one side of the river where life, hers, the true one, the present one, the one without fever and without longing and without window, finally awaited her, patient as Justino, for thirty-four years, asking her only that she see it.
