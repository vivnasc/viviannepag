## 11. The name in the register

Lembrança died at the window, on a winter late afternoon, looking at the road, and the neighbour who looked after her said that she had been more lucid than usual all day, and that the last thing she had said, low, looking at the road, had been he is coming, and that she had smiled, and that then she had gone still, and that only at the end of a while did the neighbour understand that the stillness was another.

Rosário never knew whether the mother, at the end, was waiting for the dead Vado or whether she was waiting for another thing, a greater arrival, the last. She wanted to believe that the mother, on saying he is coming, with that smile, had at last her coming, the true one, the one that does not disappoint, the one that does not depart again. And they buried Lembrança on the eastern slope, beside what was left of the family, and the village went to the burial, and said poor thing, she waited her whole life, and Rosário, behind the coffin, thought that the village was right and was not: the mother had waited her whole life, yes, but at the end she had left the daughter, instead of the waiting, the warning, and that, for Rosário, made of Lembrança not a poor thing, but a woman who, in the dark of her fate, had still found a way to light a lamp so that the daughter would not fall into the same well.

In the days before the mother died, Rosário watched over her, and it was on those nights of watching that there came, between the two, the last reconciliation, though the mother was already almost not in the world. Lembrança spent the days and the nights looking at the road, even from her bed, with the bed turned toward the window as Rosário would come to know is done in the houses of Véspera when someone is departing. And on one of those nights, lucid for an instant, the mother took the daughter's hand and said, low, yours, the one of the waters, do you still come to the window because of him? And Rosário said no, mother, I no longer come, I got myself one who stays. And the mother smiled, a smile of the end, and said only it is well, and closed her eyes, and Rosário was left with that it is well as the last thing the mother gave her with her whole wits: the approval of a woman who had lost her life at the window and who died at ease for knowing that the daughter was not going to lose hers in the same way. It was the true inheritance, repeated at the end: not the window, but the blessing of leaving it.

She went to the House of the Register to see to the mother's papers, and it was there that the Keeper gave her the last piece.

The Keeper set before her the records of the family: that of the marriage of Vado and Lembrança, that of the birth of Rosário, that of the death of Vado, far off, at a fair, and now that of the death of Lembrança, at the window, at home. And Rosário read them, the records of the whole family, and saw that they were dry, exact, and that they said nothing of what mattered: they said that Vado and Lembrança had married, but did not say that he had loved her from afar and she his lack; they said that Rosário had been born, but did not say that she had been born in a house where the girls were taught to look at the road; they said who had died when, but did not say that a woman had spent forty years at the window waiting for a man who had never learned to stay.

"The records say little," said Rosário, almost to herself.

"They say what can be written," said the Keeper, putting away the books. "Who was born, who married whom, who died. The rest does not fit in a book. Who loved from near and who loved from afar, who waited their whole life and who learned not to wait, there is no pen that records that." She looked at Rosário, with that look that read people like pages. "But it is written all the same, you know? Not here. It is written in the way one lives. Your mother wrote forty years of waiting, and it is in no record, and yet it is written, it is written in you, in the way you looked at the other bank your whole life. The records are what the village remembers. The rest, what matters, is written in the body of one who comes after. Your mother wrote herself in you. The question is what you are going to write in whoever comes after you."

"And if I have no children?" asked Rosário, because it was an old pain, hers, that of not knowing whether she would. "In whom is it written, then?"

"It is not only in children that one writes, daughter," said the Keeper, without raising her eyes from the book. "One writes in whoever comes after, and whoever comes after is not always the children. It is the niece who sees us, the new neighbour, the girl who learns from us without our knowing we are teaching. Your mother wrote in you, who were a daughter, but she wrote also in all those who saw her at the window and learned, without meaning to, that it is so that one loves. And you, on leaving the window, are going to write in others, are going to be, for some girl of this village who is now falling in love with one who goes away, the proof that one can change banks, that the fate is not destiny. You do not need children to leave a mark, Rosário. You need to live in sight, and to live right, for in a village one always lives in sight, and what one does, whether one has children or not, stays written in those who look." And she added, with that dryness that was her way of saying great things: "Write something worth being copied. It is the most a human being can do for those who come after."

Rosário was left with that. *What you are going to write in whoever comes after.* They were four generations of women at the window, the mother had said. And Rosário, there, at the House of the Register, with the mother's papers in her hand, understood that she held in her hands more than an inheritance: she held a choice about what she was going to pass on. She could inherit the window and bequeath it, as the mother had bequeathed it to her, without malice, with love even, to a son or a daughter she did not yet have, and who would learn, without words, to look at the road. Or she could break it, there, in herself, be the first woman of the house in four generations to turn from the window to the room, and bequeath to whoever came, instead of the waiting, the Bridge.

There was, in the conversation with the Keeper, a moment when Rosário asked a thing she had carried crosswise since the burial:

"Was my mother happy? You, who see everything, who record everything, tell me: did my mother have a happy life, or did she waste it at the window?"

The Keeper laid down her pen, a rare thing, and looked at Rosário with that attention she put into the questions that deserved it.

"Both things, daughter. Almost always it is both things. Your mother had a life with love in it, and that is already more than many people have, and in that sense she did not waste it. But she loved in a way that ate half her days in waiting, and that half, yes, that she lost, and it is a pity, because it was half of a life, and there is only one life. She was not unhappy, your mother. She was incomplete. She lived half a life, the half of the comings, and waited the other half, that of the absences. And what I wish for you, and what she wished for you, is not that you have more love than she, for love she had. It is that you have the whole of life, and not half. That you do not give half your days to waiting. That you live the absences as whole as the presences, instead of spending them at the window. That is the difference between your mother and what you can be: not more love, more life."

And Rosário understood, at last, exactly what was the inheritance she wanted to refuse and what the one she wanted to keep. She did not want to refuse the mother's love, which had been true. She wanted to refuse the waiting, the lost half, the chair turned toward the window. And she wanted to keep, of the mother, the warning, the lucidity of the end, the courage of a woman who, in the dark of her fate, had found a way to tell the daughter do not do as I did. It was that the good inheritance, and Rosário was going to keep it, and was going to pass it on, to whoever came, instead of the window.

"I want a copy," said Rosário. "Of my mother's record. But not to keep her waiting. To remind me not to repeat it."

The Keeper almost smiled, and made the copy, and on handing it over said a thing that Rosário took home:

"There are those who ask for the copy of their parents to imitate them, and there are those who ask for it to do the contrary. The two are love. But only the second frees one." And she added, already going back to her register: "You have a man on the Bridge, they say. One who stays. You do well. This village is full of women who waited at the windows. It lacks women who have stayed on the bridges. Be one of those, for it gives more work and gives more life."

There was, in the mother's effects, a thing Rosário kept and that was worth the whole inheritance: an old notebook, of oilcloth, where Lembrança, when young, before marrying, had noted things, and on one of the pages, in the still firm hand of a girl, was written a list of suitors, the boys who had wanted her before Vado, and beside each name a note, and Rosário read them with her heart tight, because they were the story of a choice that had decided four generations. Beside one name, *good boy, but always stays, dull*. Beside another, *hard-working, but no charm, I do not miss him when he is not there*. And beside Vado, underlined, *this one always goes away and I cannot stop thinking of him, it is this one*. The mother had chosen Vado exactly for his going away, for her missing him, for his not letting her stop thinking of him, and had discarded those who stayed because they did not hurt. She had chosen the fever, as Rosário had chosen the fever, and the note *good boy, but always stays, dull* was, word for word, what Rosário had thought of Justino at seventeen years old. Four generations, the same choice, the same error, written in an oilcloth notebook: to choose what goes away, to discard what stays, to call love what hurts and tedium what quiets. And Rosário, on reading that, understood that she held in her hands not only the mother's inheritance, but the map of the trap, and that to know the trap was the only way not to fall into it: she knew now, in writing, in the mother's hand as a girl, what was the voice within her that said this one always stays, dull, and she could, the next time that voice spoke, recognise it and not obey it.

Rosário came out of the House of the Register with the copy folded in her pocket, against her breast, and crossed the largo, and went to the Bridge, where Justino was, as always, and stayed at his side looking at the river, and said to him, without preamble, because she had learned to say things:

"My mother died at the window waiting for a dead man. I do not want to die so, Justino. I do not want to die at the window. I want to die on a bridge, or in a room, or in a kitchen, beside one who stayed. And you are the only one who stays. So I am going to tell you a thing, and it is not a question, it is information, as you say: I choose the near bank. I choose you. Not because you give me fever, for you do not, and it is well, I have already had fever enough for a lifetime. I choose you because I see you, and I want to learn to see you every day, on purpose, as you see the Bridge. If you will have me, I stay. And if you will not have me, I stay on the near bank all the same, for the near bank is already mine, I learned it this summer, it is not yours, it is mine, and I do not go back to the window even if you send me away."

Justino heard it all without interrupting her, for he was a man who let people reach the end of what they have to say, and when she finished he was a moment silent, looking at the river, and Rosário, in his silence, had a tightening, the fear that it might be late, that a man waits years but does not wait for ever, that she might have taken too long to look at the right side. But Justino had not given up. The men who stay do not give up; it is that which makes them men who stay.

"I know all that, Rosário," he said, at last, slowly. "I know about your mother, I know about the man of the waters, I know that you went your whole life turned toward the other side of the river. It was no news to me. The whole village knew, and I knew better than the village, because I was here, on this side, in front of you, all those years, being what you did not see. And I am going to tell you a thing, and it is the only complaint I make to you and I make it no more: it cost. It cost me to see you light up for that man and go out in front of me. It cost me to save your threshing-floor and your wall and you not even to notice me. It cost me to watch you dance with him in the largo. But I stayed, and I did not stay out of being a saint, I stayed because I know you, and because I knew that one day, if life gave you time, you would look at the right side, because you, Rosário, at bottom, were always of this side, you were always of the near bank, you only went about with your eyes deceived." And his dry smile opened wider than she had ever seen it open, and he said, in his way, the right thing slowly: "It was not before time. I was here. I was always here. It was only to wait until you looked at the right side of the river."

And that afternoon, on going home, Rosário made a small gesture that was the gesture of a whole lifetime and of four generations. Her house had, like the mother's, a chair turned toward the window, toward the river, toward the road of the other bank — the chair of the waiting, that she had got herself without noticing, by being born in the house she was born in. Rosário took that chair, and turned it. She turned it inward, toward the room, toward the hearth, toward the place where a man who stayed would sit at the end of the days. It was only to turn a chair, and it took an instant, and it was the hardest and most important thing she did in her life, because in that turning of a chair went the end of a fate: a chair that had been turned toward the window for four generations of women, from the great-grandmother at the seaside to the mother at the edge of the road, turned at last, for the first time, toward the inside of the house, toward the life one has instead of the one one waits for. The mother had asked her, at the window, to turn the chair inward. Rosário turned it. And she sat in it, turned toward the room, and was the first woman of her house, in more years than the records knew how to count, to sit with her back to the road and facing what she had.
