## 9. The bottom

The conversation with the daughter came in the winter, and it came because it had to come, because a thing put off for sixty years always ends by bursting, and it burst on account of a trifle, as great things burst.

There had accumulated, over that autumn and winter, a tension between the two that neither knew how to name, and that came from Plácida changing and from Belmira not yet knowing what to do with the new mother. Because Plácida, after the illness and the grandson and the Keeper, had begun to try to ask of the daughter, clumsily, and the first requests came out crooked, half-made, wrapped in so many apologies that they seemed more like refusals, and Belmira, who did not know that the mother was in training, did not understand what was happening: she saw the mother draw near and draw away, sketch a request and undo it, need and deny that she needed, and she interpreted that as a confusion, a strangeness, perhaps a beginning of an illness of the head, mother is being odd, she said to her husband, now she asks me for a thing now she says that after all no, I do not understand her. And the tension went on growing, the daughter not understanding the mother, the mother not managing to explain to the daughter that she was learning a thing, and it was that accumulated tension that burst, as things burst, by the trifle that was at hand.

It was on account of a coat. Belmira had bought a coat for the mother, as a present, a warm coat for the winter, and Plácida, on receiving it, did what she always did with what she received: she thanked too much, made light of herself, said it was not needed, that the old one still served, that Belmira should not have spent money on her, that there was so much more useful. And she said it with the delicacy of custom, thinking she was being modest, not wanting to give trouble even with a present. But there was, in the refusal of the coat, a thing that Belmira felt deeper than the refusal of a coat, and that was this: in refusing the present, in making light of it, in saying it was not needed, the mother was, without knowing it, refusing the daughter. Because a present is a form of love, and one who refuses the present refuses the love of one who gives it, and Belmira had spent her life watching the mother refuse her presents, her helps, her cares, always with the same delicacy, always with the same it-is-not-needed, and each refusal, over a lifetime, had been a small door shut in the face, a small your-love-I-do-not-need, and Belmira had her heart full of all those small shut doors, and the coat was the last, the one that made the cup of a lifetime overflow. And Belmira, who had gone about for months with the splinter of the festival and the fright of the illness and everything that had been accumulating, exploded, and the explosion was the bottom of the book, and was also the beginning of the climb.

"Stop, mother!" said Belmira, and the voice came out of her with a force that frightened the two of them. "Stop refusing! Stop saying it is not needed! I bought you a coat, mother, a coat, and you do not even let me give you that in peace, you have to make light of it, you have to say it was not needed, you have to take from me even the pleasure of giving you a thing! You do this your whole life, mother! Your whole life! I never saw you ask me for anything, I never saw you need me, I never saw you accept a thing without undoing it! And I grew up so, mother, beside a mother who never needed me for anything, and do you know how that is? Do you know how it is to be the daughter of a mother who never needs us?"

And Plácida, caught, without a wall, was silent, and Belmira, now weeping, poured out what she had carried a lifetime:

"It is to feel that we are not needed, mother. It is to grow up thinking we are good for nothing, because the person who should most need us, our mother, never needed us. You gave me everything, mother, everything, you clothed me, raised me, served me my whole life. But never, not once, did you let me give you anything. Never let me care for you. Never asked me for help, never showed me a weakness, never said to me Belmira, I need you. And I wanted to, mother! I wanted so much to be needed by you! I wanted you to lean on me once at least, to ask me for a thing, to let me be your daughter in earnest and not only a person you serve! And you never let me! You preferred to burn with fever alone than to wake me! You prefer the old coat to letting me give you a new one! You love me, I know you love me, but you love me in a way that leaves me out, mother, that does not let me in, that makes of me a stranger in the life of my own mother! And I am sick of it! I am sick of having a mother who does not need me! I would rather a mother who gave me trouble, who asked me for things, who pestered me, who weighed on me, than this perfect mother who never needs anything and who leaves me, me, with nothing to give her!"

And she fell silent, weeping, and there fell an enormous silence in the kitchen, and Plácida, with the coat in her lap, realised, in that silence, the most terrible and most freeing thing of her life: that her not-bothering, which she had always taken for love, for a gift, for care, had been, for the daughter, an abandonment. That in refusing to weigh on the daughter, she had not spared her: she had deprived her. That in never asking, never needing, never letting herself be cared for, she had not been a good mother who gave no trouble: she had been a mother who never let the daughter love her, who had shut to her own daughter the door by which one enters a person, which is the door of needing. And that Belmira had grown up, because of that, feeling herself superfluous, useless, not-needed, exactly as Plácida, as a child, had felt herself invisible by another way. The wound had passed from mother to daughter, changed in form but the same at bottom: Plácida's mother had had no hands to see her; and Plácida, by needing no hands at all, had not given the daughter hands to take hold of her. The two daughters, in two generations, had grown up thinking they were not needed by the mother. And Plácida was now going to have to choose whether she passed the same thing on, to Bento, through Belmira, or whether she broke it there, in herself, in that kitchen, with a coat in her lap and the daughter weeping before her.

There was, in that silence of the kitchen, before Plácida spoke, a time in which the two women looked at one another truly, perhaps for the first time in their lives, mother and daughter, without the wall of the not-bothering between them. And Plácida saw, in the daughter's face, things she had never let herself see because she had never looked in earnest: she saw the little girl Belmira had been, the little girl who had grown up trying to wring from the mother a need, a request, a weakness, anything that would give her a place as a daughter, and who had never managed it, because the mother was impenetrable in her self-sufficiency. She saw the times when Belmira, as a small child, must have offered her help and heard the it-is-not-needed, I-manage, and must have kept, each time, the small wound of not being needed. She saw a whole life of a daughter knocking on a door that never opened, and realised that her goodness, her not wanting to weigh on the daughter, had been, on the daughter's side, a door always shut, a mother always complete, always strong, always above, who never came down to the place where a daughter can love the mother, which is the place of shared frailty, of mutual needing. And Plácida realised, with a pain that was almost physical, that she had done to the daughter, with her goodness, a harm that no malice would have done: she had denied her the mother. Not the mother who serves — that one, Belmira had had to spare. The mother who needs, the mother who leans, the mother who lets the daughter be a daughter. That one, Belmira had never had, and it was that one that was now, in the kitchen, weeping for her, claiming her, at the end of a lifetime.

She chose to break it, and the choice began with a gesture small and enormous, the first of a new life: Plácida laid down the coat, got up, and instead of consoling the daughter as she consoled everyone, instead of seeing to taking from Belmira the weight of that explosion, she did the contrary of everything she had done her whole life. She took the daughter's hands, and said, with her voice trembling, the hardest thing she had ever said, the thing that was the reverse of sixty-six years:

"You are right, daughter." She breathed. "And I am going to ask you for a thing, now, and it costs me so much that you cannot imagine, but I am going to ask, because you deserve that I ask you." She squeezed her hands. "Help me, Belmira. I do not know how to do this. I do not know how to need anyone, I never knew, I learned as a child not to need and I never again unlearned it, and I am old, and I am afraid, and I need you to help me learn. I need you, daughter. There. I said it. I need you. It is the first time in my life that I say this to anyone, and I say it to you, and it is well that it is to you. Help me learn to need, daughter, for alone I cannot, and I no longer have a life to do it alone."

And Belmira, who had asked her whole life, without knowing it, that the mother need her, heard at last the mother say I need you, and came undone, and embraced her, and the two of them wept in the kitchen, embraced, and it was the deepest embrace of their lives, because it was the first in which the mother leaned on the daughter instead of supporting her, the first in which Belmira held the mother instead of being held, the first in which the two of them were, at last, on the same side, the side of those who need one another, which is the only side where two people really come to touch.

There was, at the end of that explosion, a silence in which the two of them, exhausted from having said so much, stayed looking at one another with the clean eyes of people who have wept everything, and Plácida said to the daughter a thing that was the key to everything, and that came out of her without her preparing it, from the bottom: you know, daughter, I always thought I was sparing you. My whole life, in not asking you for anything, in not weighing on you, I thought I was doing you a good, leaving you light, not burdening you with a mother. I thought that was a good mother, a mother who does not weigh. And now I realise I did you the contrary of a good. I realise that a mother who does not weigh is a mother who does not let herself be loved, and that you grew up wanting to love me and not letting yourself, because I gave you no way in. Forgive me, daughter. Forgive me for having spared you so much that I left you out. Forgive me for having been such a good mother that I did not let you be my daughter.

And Belmira, on hearing that, realised that the mother had, at last, understood, and that that understanding was the present she had waited for her whole life, greater than any coat, and embraced the mother, and it was in that embrace, and not in the words, that the thing was sealed. Because there are understandings that need words and there are others that only the body knows how to say, and that embrace, the first in which the mother let herself be supported by the daughter, said more than everything the two of them had shouted: it said that the door, shut for a lifetime, had opened, and that from then on Belmira was going to be able to enter, and be a daughter, and care for the mother, and that the mother was going to let her, and that that was the greatest thing two women can give one another, that of letting themselves be needed.

The days after that night were strange for the two of them, as the first days of anything new are strange, and they were also, little by little, among the happiest they had lived together. Belmira began to appear more, not to watch over the mother as one watches over a sick woman, but to be with her as one is with one one loves, and Plácida, with an enormous effort, went on obliging herself to let her in: to accept that the daughter did a thing for her without saying it is not needed, to let her see to a task that she herself would do, to allow herself the luxury, which seemed to her a sin, of being served by the daughter. And each time she let herself be served, she saw in Belmira's face a joy that disarmed her, the joy of a daughter who at last could care for the mother, and she realised, again, what she was learning on all sides: that in letting herself be cared for she took nothing from the daughter, she gave her everything, she gave her the place of daughter that she had denied her her whole life.

There was one afternoon when Belmira, tidying the mother's kitchen, stopped and said, without turning, a thing that came from very deep: you know, mother, when I was little I used to pray for you to fall ill. Not much, not a real illness. Only a little. Only for you to need me, for me to be able to make you a tea, bring you a blanket, be useful to you. The other girls had mothers who needed them, who asked them for things, and I had a mother who never needed anything, and I felt myself superfluous, mother, my whole life I felt myself superfluous beside you, because you did not let me be needed by you. And Plácida heard that, the daughter confessing that she had prayed, as a child, for the mother to fall ill only to be able to be useful to her, and it was among the most painful things she heard in her life, because she measured in it the size of the harm her goodness had done: she had made her own daughter wish for her illness, only to have a place in her life. And she realised that Belmira, as a child, and she, as a child, had wished for the same thing by opposite ways: Belmira had wished that the mother need her; and she, Plácida, had wished, at bottom, beneath the championship, to be needed, to be carried as the noisy brother was. The two of them, mother and daughter, with the same hunger to be needed, and the two of them dying of that hunger side by side, because neither had known how to ask the other to satisfy it.
