## 9. The bottom

The conversation with the daughter came in the winter, and it came because it had to come, because a thing put off for sixty years always ends by bursting, and it burst on account of a trifle, as great things burst.

There had accumulated, over that autumn and winter, a tension between the two that neither knew how to name. Because Plácida, after the illness and the grandson and the Keeper, had begun to try to ask of the daughter, clumsily, and the first requests came out crooked, half-made, wrapped in so many apologies that they seemed more like refusals. She would sketch a look, daughter, perhaps, and right after no, leave it, after all it is not needed, I manage, and Belmira, who did not know that the mother was in training, did not understand what was happening. She saw the mother draw near and draw away, need and deny that she needed, and said to her husband, at night, mother is being odd, now she asks me for a thing now she says that after all no, I do not understand her, can it be her head? And the tension went on growing, the daughter not understanding the mother, the mother not managing to explain to the daughter that she was learning a thing, and it was that accumulated tension that burst by the trifle that was at hand.

It was on account of a coat. Belmira arrived at the mother's house one cold afternoon, with a parcel, and undid the paper on the kitchen table: it was a coat of wool, warm, dark, good for the winter.

"Here, mother. I bought you this. Yours is already worn at the elbows, I saw. This one is warm, try it."

And Plácida did what she always did with what she received. She took the coat, felt it, and at once:

"Ah, daughter, it was not needed, it was not needed at all. Why did you go and spend money on me? The old one still serves very well, look, it is good, it is only the elbows. You should have bought a thing for yourself, or for Bento, who grows and needs it. On me, no, daughter, on me it is money thrown away."

And she said it with the delicacy of custom, thinking she was being modest, not wanting to give trouble even with a present. But Belmira stayed on her feet, with her hands empty where the coat had been, and there was, in the refusal, a thing she felt deeper than the refusal of a coat. Because a present is a form of love, and one who refuses the present refuses the love of one who gives it, and Belmira had spent her life watching the mother refuse her presents, her helps, her cares, always with the same it-is-not-needed, and each refusal, over a lifetime, had been a small door shut in the face. And the coat was the last, the one that made the cup of a lifetime overflow. And Belmira, who had gone about for months with the splinter of the festival and the fright of the illness and everything that had been accumulating, exploded.

"Stop, mother!" she said, and the voice came out of her with a force that frightened the two of them, and her eyes were already filling. "Stop refusing! Stop saying it is not needed! I bought you a coat, mother, a coat, and you do not even let me give you that in peace, you have to make light of it, you have to say it was money thrown away, you have to take from me even the pleasure of giving you a thing! You do this your whole life, mother! Your whole life! I never saw you ask me for anything, I never saw you need me, I never saw you accept a thing without undoing it! And I grew up so, mother, beside a mother who never needed me for anything, and do you know how that is? Do you know how it is to be the daughter of a mother who never needs us?"

And Plácida, caught, without a wall, with the coat fallen in her lap, was silent, and Belmira, now weeping, poured out what she had carried a lifetime:

"It is to feel that we are not needed, mother. It is to grow up thinking we are good for nothing, because the person who should most need us, our mother, never needed us. You gave me everything, mother, everything, you clothed me, raised me, served me my whole life. But never, not once, did you let me give you anything. Never let me care for you. Never asked me for help, never showed me a weakness, never said to me Belmira, I need you. And I wanted to, mother! I wanted so much to be needed by you! I wanted you to lean on me once at least, to let me be your daughter in earnest and not only a person you serve! And you never let me! You preferred to burn with fever alone than to wake me! You prefer the old coat to letting me give you a new one! You love me, I know you love me, but you love me in a way that leaves me out, that makes of me a stranger in the life of my own mother! And I am sick of it! I am sick of having a mother who does not need me! I would rather a mother who gave me trouble, who pestered me, who weighed on me, than this perfect mother who never needs anything and who leaves me, me, with nothing to give her!"

And she fell silent, weeping, and there fell an enormous silence in the kitchen, only with the ticking of the clock and the kettle whistling low on the stove, and Plácida, with the coat in her lap, realised, in that silence, the most terrible and most freeing thing of her life: that her not-bothering, which she had always taken for love, had been, for the daughter, an abandonment. That in refusing to weigh on the daughter, she had not spared her: she had deprived her. That in never asking, never letting herself be cared for, she had not been a good mother who gave no trouble: she had been a mother who never let the daughter love her, who had shut to her own daughter the door by which one enters a person, which is the door of needing. The wound of Plácida's mother had passed to Plácida, and from Plácida was passing to Belmira, changed in form but the same at bottom. And Plácida was now going to have to choose whether she passed the same thing on, to Bento, through Belmira, or whether she broke it there, in that kitchen, with a coat in her lap and the daughter weeping before her.

She chose to break it, and the choice began with a gesture small and enormous. Plácida laid the coat on the chair, got up, and instead of consoling the daughter as she consoled everyone, instead of seeing to taking from Belmira the weight of that explosion, she did the contrary of everything she had done her whole life. She went to her, took her by the two hands, and said, with her voice trembling, the hardest thing she had ever said, the thing that was the reverse of sixty-six years:

"You are right, daughter." She breathed deep. "And I am going to ask you for a thing, now, and it costs me so much that you cannot imagine, but I am going to ask, because you deserve that I ask you." She squeezed her hands. "Help me, Belmira. I do not know how to do this. I do not know how to need anyone, I never knew, I learned as a child not to need and I never again unlearned it, and I am old, and I am afraid, and I need you to help me learn. I need you, daughter. There. I said it. I need you. It is the first time in my life that I say this to anyone, and I say it to you, and it is well that it is to you. Help me learn to need, for alone I cannot, and I no longer have a life to do it alone."

And Belmira, who had asked her whole life, without knowing it, that the mother need her, heard at last the mother say I need you, and came undone, and embraced her, and the two of them wept in the kitchen, on their feet, embraced, and it was the deepest embrace of their lives, because it was the first in which the mother leaned on the daughter instead of supporting her, the first in which Belmira held the mother instead of being held, the first in which the two of them were, at last, on the same side, the side of those who need one another, which is the only side where two people really come to touch.

There was, at the end of that explosion, a silence in which the two of them, exhausted from having said so much, stayed looking at one another with the clean eyes of people who have wept everything, and Plácida said to the daughter a thing that came out of her without her preparing it, from the bottom:

"You know, daughter, I always thought I was sparing you. My whole life, in not asking you for anything, in not weighing on you, I thought I was doing you a good, leaving you light. I thought that was a good mother, a mother who does not weigh. And now I realise I did you the contrary of a good. That a mother who does not weigh is a mother who does not let herself be loved, and that you grew up wanting to love me and not letting yourself, because I gave you no way in. Forgive me, daughter. Forgive me for having spared you so much that I left you out. Forgive me for having been such a good mother that I did not let you be my daughter."

And Belmira, on hearing that, was a moment speechless, and then took up the coat that lay fallen on the chair, the coat of the discord, and held it out to the mother again, slowly, like one who repeats a gesture to do it well:

"Then put on the coat, mother. Do not say it is not needed. Put it on, for I want to see it on you."

And Plácida, with her hands trembling, put on the coat, there in the kitchen, and did not say it was not needed, did not make light of it, let the daughter see it on her and say that it suited her well, and it was the first time in her life that she received a present from the daughter with her face on show, without undoing it. And she realised that the mother had, at last, understood, and that that understanding was the present that Belmira had waited for her whole life, greater than any coat, and she embraced the mother again, with the new coat between the two of them, and it was in that embrace, and not in the words, that the thing was sealed.

The days after that night were strange for the two of them, as the first days of anything new are strange, and they were also, little by little, among the happiest they had lived together. Belmira began to appear more, not to watch over the mother as one watches over a sick woman, but to be with her.

There was the first afternoon when Belmira, on arriving, went straight to the stove and said:

"Today it is I who make the supper, mother. Sit there."

And Plácida felt the old tightening, her hand wanting to go to the apron, the phrase of custom already in her mouth, leave it, daughter, I will do it, you must be tired from work. But she bit her tongue, remembered the I-need-you, and sat down. She sat at the table of her own kitchen watching the daughter cook, with her hands in her lap, and it cost her as a penance costs, to see another person working in her kitchen while she was seated. Twice she got up to go and peel a potato, and twice Belmira said to her:

"Sit down, mother. I know how to peel potatoes. I learned from you."

And at the third Plácida stayed seated, and let herself be, and when the supper came to the table, made by the daughter's hands, and she ate without having cooked, she felt herself almost to be sinning, and at the same time saw Belmira's face, glad, proud to have made the supper for the mother, and realised that that discomfort of hers was worth that joy of the daughter's. She went on so obliging herself, day after day, to let her in: to accept that the daughter did a thing for her without saying it is not needed, to allow herself the luxury, which seemed to her a sin, of being served by the daughter. And each time she let herself be served, she saw in Belmira's face a joy that disarmed her.

There was an afternoon when Belmira, tidying the mother's kitchen, with her back turned, her hands in the dishwater, stopped, and said, without turning, a thing that came from very deep:

"You know, mother, when I was little I used to pray for you to fall ill." She was a moment silent, with the tap dripping. "Not much, not a real illness. Only a little. Only for you to need me, for me to be able to make you a tea, bring you a blanket, be useful to you. The other girls had mothers who needed them, who asked them for things, and I had a mother who never needed anything. And I felt myself superfluous, mother. My whole life I felt myself superfluous beside you, because you did not let me be needed by you."

And Plácida heard that, the daughter confessing, with her back turned, with her hands in the dishes, that she had prayed as a child for the mother to fall ill only to be able to be useful to her, and it was among the most painful things she heard in her life, because she measured in it the size of the harm her goodness had done: she had made her own daughter wish for her illness, only to have a place in her life. And she realised that Belmira, as a child, and she, Plácida, as a child, had wished for the same thing by opposite ways: Belmira had wished that the mother need her; and she, Plácida, had wished, at bottom, beneath the championship, to be needed, to be carried as the noisy brother was. The two of them, mother and daughter, with the same hunger to be needed, and the two of them dying of that hunger side by side, because neither had known how to ask the other to satisfy it.

And Plácida, who was seated at the table, got up, went to the daughter at the sink, and did a thing she had never done: she leaned her head against Belmira's shoulder, there on her feet, and let herself stay, doing nothing, only leaning, needing, and it was Belmira, with wet hands, who supported her.
