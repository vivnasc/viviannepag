## From the register of Véspera

The Keeper of this time says that there was a year, in the village, when a woman no one saw learned to ask, and in asking, gave herself at last to be seen.

Plácida, widow of Brando, mother of Belmira, grandmother of Bento. The one who had served the village her whole life and who fit, in my books, in four lines, three of them borrowed from others. The one who sat at the end and served herself last and went away from the festivals without anyone noticing her absence.

At sixty-six years old, taught by a grandson of eight, she did the hardest thing that could be asked of her: she asked. She asked for the bread at the table, asked for her place in the middle, asked the daughter to let her need her, asked a friend of sixty years to listen to her. And she broke, in asking, a fate that came from three generations of women who effaced themselves so as not to weigh.

And she left in the custom of the village one more place at the long table, always set, for those who serve, and that is her large line, and it is in life, and it is not erased.

The register says who was born, who married whom, who departed. It does not say who learned, late, that to exist is not a bother, and that to ask is the way of giving others a place in our life. For that, books like this one are needed. And of all of it a record was made.
