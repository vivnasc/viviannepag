## 5. The silenced illness

The illness came in the autumn, and Plácida treated it as she treated everything that was hers: in secret, alone, with modesty, as though to fall ill were a rudeness that had to be hidden from the visitors.

It began with a weariness that did not pass with rest, and some pains she attributed to age, and a lack of appetite she thought was the heat ending. Plácida had, for her own symptoms, the same generous blindness she had for her own needs: she did not see them, or saw them and made light of them, because to give them importance would be to give herself importance, and to give herself importance was the thing she did not know how to do. So she went on with life as though nothing were the matter: she got up, tended the house, went to the largo. And she even went to visit Idalina, who had fallen and twisted a foot and was in bed, and took her a chicken broth in a pot wrapped in a cloth, and climbed her stairs with the pot trembling in her hands, not from the weight, but from the fever she herself was hiding.

"Ah Plácida, you are an angel," said Idalina, from the bed. "To come all this way in this cold, and bring me broth besides. And you, are you well? You seem to me a little yellow."

"I am fine, woman," said Plácida, and set down the pot, and went to the kitchen to heat the broth, and came back to serve it to her in the bed, and pulled the blanket up over her feet, and arranged the pillow at her back, she, burning with fever, tending a sick woman, and the sick woman not dreaming that the woman who put the blanket on her feet was more ill than she. "Do not worry about me. Eat the broth while it is hot, for I manage."

And she went down the stairs slowly, holding the rail, with her head going round, and went home, and lay down without eating anything, because all the strength she had she had spent heating broth for another.

The daughter did not know. Belmira lived nearby, and appeared, and Plácida took care that, when the daughter arrived, she was composed. There was a Sunday when she heard her knock at the gate and was lying down, in a sweat, with the fever high, and she did what she did: she got up from bed with effort, arranged her hair at the mirror, dressed, pinched her cheeks to give them colour, and went to the door on her feet, smiling.

"So, daughter? How good that you came."

"Mother, are you well?" asked Belmira, looking at her. "You have an odd look."

"I? I am fine, woman. Do not worry about me, see to your life, for I manage. Come, come in, will you have a coffee?"

And she kept up the performance the whole length of the visit, on her feet, looking the picture of health, and the moment Belmira left she collapsed again into bed, exhausted from the fever and the performance combined. She spent, in hiding the illness from the daughter, the little strength she had, and she spent it gladly, because to hide the illness was to protect the daughter from the trouble of treating her, and to protect the daughter from trouble was, in Plácida's crooked head, the highest form of a mother's love: not to weigh on her even when ill. It did not occur to her, burning with fever performing health, that she was doing exactly the contrary of what she thought: that in sparing the daughter the trouble of treating her, she spared her also the love of treating her, and that a daughter spared of love is not a protected daughter, she is a daughter pushed away.

The friends did not know. Felizarda appeared, as always, to pour out her things, and sat at Plácida's table, and complained an hour of a cold she had caught, of her dripping nose, of her laden chest.

"Ah, Plácida, this cold will not leave me. I am ground down. You do not know what a cold like this is at my age."

And Plácida, on the other side of the table, more ill than Felizarda had ever been in her life, listened to her, and consoled her, and went to fetch her a lemon tea for the chest, and did not say a word of her own ill, and Felizarda went away relieved, without knowing that she had spent an hour complaining of a cold to a friend who burned with fever and who hid it from her so as not to worry her.

And the illness, untreated, unspoken, unasked, worsened, as the things one does not attend to worsen. There was a week when Plácida scarcely left her bed, alone, at home, dispatching the fever with teas and with stubbornness. And what sustained her, in that stubbornness, was not only the habit of not bothering: it was a kind of secret, perverse pride, that she would never confess. Each day she endured alone was a small victory in the only championship she knew how to play. I am ill and I gave no one any trouble. I burn with fever and no one knows. I do not weigh, I do not call, I do not bother, even ill.

And there was a night when she got so bad that she was afraid. She woke at dawn drenched, with the room going round and her heart beating in her ears, and her mouth dry, and a weakness in her legs she did not know, and she had, in the dark, a fear of dying there alone, a true fear, cold, of those that rise from the stomach. She thought, with the lucidity of fever, that if she died that night no one would notice it until days later, when Belmira found the silence strange, or when the cat mewed at the door with hunger, and that they would find her there, in the bed, cold, and would say poor thing, she died alone, without bothering anyone, even in dying she gave no trouble.

She stretched out her hand to the bedside table, took the telephone, and was left with it in her hand, in the dark, with the daughter's number known by heart, her finger hovering over the keys. She had only to dial. She had only to say Belmira, I am bad, come. But the finger did not come down. She thought: it is the hour of Belmira sleeping, and the boy has school tomorrow, and if I call I wake the whole house, and she comes here in the middle of the night, in this cold, for a thing that is perhaps nothing, and tomorrow she goes about worn out at work on my account. Better to endure until morning. If in the morning I am the same, then we shall see.

And she set the telephone down on the bedside table, slowly, like one who sets down a temptation, and stayed with her eyes open in the dark, burning, with her hand still near the device but not touching it, counting the hours by the neighbour's cockerel and by the far-off bells of the church. And when, at the breaking of day, the fever fell a little and she realised that she was not, after all, going to die that night, she felt not relief, but a kind of terrible pride: she had managed, once more, not to give trouble, not even dying.

The most terrible thing of that week was the naturalness with which Plácida lived it. For her there was nothing heroic nor tragic in burning with fever alone: it was simply what one did, what she had always done, the correct way of falling ill. She heated the tea, drank it, lay down, got up to tend the cat and the pots because the cat and the pots were not to blame for her being ill, and went back to bed, and if someone asked her in those days how are you, Plácida would answer well, without hesitating, not out of lying, but because her well no longer had to do with the state of the body: her well was not to be giving trouble, and as long as she gave no trouble she was well, even if she was burning.

It was Bento who gave the alarm, and gave it without knowing he gave it, as children do great things.

He came with his mother, one Sunday, to visit the grandmother, and Plácida, of course, got up, composed herself, made a point of looking well. But Bento, who knew her better than the adults because he looked at her another way, with the total attention of children to one they love, stayed looking at her while she went to fetch the biscuits, and saw her slower, whiter, saw a grimace of pain that she disguised on bending down. And he said, aloud, without filter, turned to his mother:

"Mother, grandmother is ill. Grandmother has the face we have when we are ill."

And Belmira, alerted by the son, looked at the mother seriously, for the first time in months, with the eyes of one who was forced to see, and saw. She saw what had been there for weeks and that she had not seen because the mother hid it from her and because it was comfortable not to see: an ill woman, thin, finished, pretending to be well. And the fright came out of her in scolding, as the fear of those who love comes out:

"Mother! What is this? How long have you been like this? Why did you say nothing to me?"

And Plácida, caught, without the wall of the phrases working because Bento had broken through it, lowered her eyes like a child caught in a fault, and said, low, the only truth she had:

"I did not want to give you trouble, daughter. You have so much to do. I managed here."

They called the doctor from outside, the one who came to the village when there was a serious case, a young woman, of few words and sure hands, who had already treated half of Véspera and who had, with the patients of the land, the patience of one who learned that people's bodies always tell more than they say. She examined Plácida, at length, palpated her belly, listened to her chest, measured her fever, and what she had was not, fortunately, the worst that was feared: it was an infection that had spread for lack of care, a thing that, treated in time, was resolved, but that, left to go on as Plácida had left it, could have come to very bad. And she said it with clarity, in front of Belmira, on purpose, putting her things away in her bag:

"You were lucky," she said. "A few more days like that and it would be another conversation. And what worries me, dona Plácida, is not the infection, for that is treated. It is your having gone weeks with this and not having called anyone. That, yes, that is what kills, more than the illness: the not-calling. I see a lot of this, you know? Above all in the women of your age, those who served everyone their whole lives. They fall ill and keep silent. They think that to ask for help is to give trouble, and they prefer to risk their lives than to bother. And they die, sometimes, of things that were treatable, for not having called in time. They die of the delicacy, dona Plácida. It is a disease, that, as serious as any other, and harder to treat, because there is no syrup for it. The only remedy is for you to learn to call. To say I am ill, come. To ask." She looked at her, serious, with the bag now shut. "Promise me one thing: next time, at the first sign, you call. You do not wait. You do not manage alone. You call. For your daughter, here looking at you, would a thousand times rather have the trouble of treating you than the grief of losing you for your not having wanted to give trouble."

And Belmira, at her side, nodded, with her eyes full, and the doctor went, and there stayed in the room that word, they die of the delicacy, hovering over Plácida like a diagnosis deeper than that of the infection. Plácida had nearly died of hers, alone, in the dark, with the telephone in her hand and the daughter's number known by heart, for not wanting to wake anyone. And it was there, in that phrase, I did not want to give you trouble, that Belmira understood, suddenly, with a clarity that made her have to sit on the edge of the bed, the size of the thing. It was not an illness hidden out of carelessness. It was a mother who had preferred to burn with fever alone, afraid of dying, than to wake the daughter. And Belmira, looking at the mother shrunken, apologising to her for being ill, felt a thing she had never felt for the mother, a mixture of love and of anger and of a deep sadness, and said, with her voice trembling, a phrase that was the beginning of everything the two of them would have to say to one another, later, to the bottom:

"You would rather die alone than bother me. Is that it, mother? You love me so badly that you would rather die than need me?"

And Plácida did not know how to answer, because she had never thought the thing so, had never seen that her not-bothering, which she took for love, for delicacy, could be seen, from the other side, as its contrary: as a refusal to let others love her, as a shutting the door in their faces, as a way, the most polite of all, of telling them I do not need you. And she stayed silent, in bed, with the daughter at her side weeping from anger and from love, and the grandson at the door watching, and it was the first time in her life that Plácida's illness gave trouble to someone, and it was also the first time she realised, vaguely, in the midst of the fever, that perhaps to give trouble, sometimes, was not to take anything from one one loves: it was to give them a thing. It was to let them in. It was to tell them you are needed. And that she, her whole life, in giving no one any trouble, had not been generous: she had been, without knowing it, the most miserly of women, the one who had never given anyone the present of needing them.
