## 5. The silenced illness

The illness came in the autumn, and Plácida treated it as she treated everything that was hers: in secret, alone, with modesty, as though to fall ill were a rudeness that had to be hidden from the visitors.

It was, besides, the relationship Plácida had with her own body from the beginning: that of a demanding mistress with a maid from whom she does not want to hear complaints. Her body, over her life, had been the tool of her serving, and she had treated it as one treats a tool, without attention to the pains, without rest, without coddling, demanding of it that it endure, that it give no trouble, that it not bother her with needs. She had never complained of a pain, never stopped from weariness, never given herself the luxury of an acknowledged illness, of a few days in bed with the right to be cared for. The body, for Plácida, was one more thing that should give no trouble, and she had learned not to hear it, to silence its complaints, to treat it as she treated everything that was hers: with the recommendation that it manage alone. And the body, silenced for sixty-six years, ordered to be silent, gave at last, that autumn, the cry she had never let it give: it fell ill in earnest, to a point of not being able to be ignored, as though the body, tired of being ordered to be silent, had decided to cry so loud that the owner would be obliged, at last, to hear it. There are bodies that fall ill to be heard, said the doctor from outside, and Plácida's was one of them: she had spent her life swallowing complaints, and the illness was the complaint it could no longer swallow.

It began with a weariness, a weariness that did not pass with rest, and some pains she attributed to age, and a lack of appetite she thought was the heat ending. Plácida had, for her own symptoms, the same generous blindness she had for her own needs: she did not see them, or saw them and made light of them, because to give them importance would be to give herself importance, and to give herself importance was the thing she did not know how to do. So she went on with life as though nothing were the matter: she got up, tended the house, went to the largo, visited the sick friends — she, ill, visiting the sick friends —, and to no one did she say that she was not well, because to be unwell was to give trouble, and to give trouble was the one thing Plácida, in sixty-six years, had never learned to do.

The daughter did not know. Belmira lived nearby, but Plácida took care that, when the daughter appeared, she was composed, on her feet, doing something, looking well, and she had for the daughter the phrases of custom, I am fine, do not worry about me, see to your life for I manage here. And there was an enormous effort in that, an effort no one saw: Plácida, burning with fever, heard the daughter arrive and got up from bed, arranged her hair, dressed, put on an air of health, and received the daughter on her feet, smiling, and kept up the performance for the length of the visit, and the moment the daughter left she collapsed again into bed, exhausted from the fever and the performance combined. She spent, in hiding the illness from the daughter, the little strength she had, and she spent it gladly, because to hide the illness was to protect the daughter from the trouble of treating her, and to protect the daughter from trouble was, in Plácida's crooked head, the highest form of a mother's love: to spare the child of herself, not to weigh on her even when ill, to be a mother who never, in any circumstance, gives trouble. It did not occur to her, burning with fever performing health for the daughter, that she was doing exactly the contrary of what she thought: that in sparing the daughter the trouble of treating her, she spared her also the love of treating her, and that a daughter spared of love is not a protected daughter, she is a daughter pushed away. And Belmira, who had her life and her children and her work, believed, because it was comfortable to believe and because the mother had always been well, and went away, time after time, without seeing that the mother was wasting away, because the mother had made of the art of hiding her state a second nature, and deceived the daughter with the same skill with which she deceived the village and deceived herself.

The friends did not know. Felizarda appeared, as always, to pour out her things, and sat at Plácida's table, and spoke an hour of her pains and her ailments, without noticing that the woman who listened to her, on the other side of the table, was more ill than she had ever been, and Plácida listened to her, and consoled her, and did not say a word of her own ill, and Felizarda went away relieved, without knowing that she had spent an hour complaining of a cold to a friend who burned with fever and who hid it from her so as not to worry her.

And the illness, untreated, unspoken, unasked, worsened, as the things one does not attend to worsen. There was a week when Plácida scarcely left her bed, alone, at home, without calling anyone, dispatching the fever with teas and with stubbornness. And what sustained her, in that stubbornness, was not only the habit of not bothering: it was a kind of secret, perverse pride, that she would never confess. Each day she endured alone was a small victory in the only championship she knew how to play. I am ill and I gave no one any trouble. I burn with fever and no one knows. I do not weigh, I do not call, I do not bother, even ill. There was in that a sickly satisfaction, the satisfaction of the champion defending the title, and Plácida, in the midst of the fever, fed on it, without realising that she was confusing a disease of the soul with a virtue, that she was putting the title above life, that she was, literally, preferring to die a champion of not-bothering than to live having given trouble.

And there was a night when she got so bad that she was afraid, a fear of dying there alone, and not even that night, afraid of dying, could she do the thing that would have saved any other person, which was to call, to ask for help, to tell someone I am bad, come. She took the telephone, in her hand, in the dark, with the daughter's number known by heart, and did not call, because it was the hour of Belmira sleeping, and to call would be to wake her, and to wake her would be to give trouble, and better, thought Plácida in the dark, with the high fever, better to endure until morning than to wake the daughter in the middle of the night for a thing that was perhaps nothing. And she endured. She endured the whole night, alone, afraid, burning, so as not to wake the daughter, and when in the morning the fever fell a little, she felt not relief, but a kind of terrible pride: she had managed, once more, not to give trouble, not even dying.

The most terrible thing of that week was the naturalness with which Plácida lived it. For her there was nothing heroic nor tragic in burning with fever alone: it was simply what one did, what she had always done, the correct way of falling ill, which was to fall ill without bothering. She heated the tea, drank it, lay down, got up to tend the cat and the pots because the cat and the pots were not to blame for her being ill, and went back to bed, and all this alone, with the house shut, and if someone asked her in those days how are you, Plácida would answer well, without hesitating, not out of lying, but because her well no longer had to do with the state of the body: her well was not to be giving trouble, and as long as she gave no trouble she was well, even if she was burning. She had separated, over her life, her well-being from her real state, and measured herself not by what she felt, but by what she weighed, and as long as she weighed nothing she was, by her own definition, well. It was an account of the soul so crooked that it led her to call well a near-death, as long as that near-death was silent, and hers, and no one else's.

It was Bento who gave the alarm, and gave it without knowing he gave it, as children do great things.

He came with his mother, one Sunday, to visit the grandmother, and Plácida, of course, got up, composed herself, made a point of looking well, but Bento, who knew her better than the adults because he looked at her another way, with the total attention of children to one they love, saw that the grandmother was different. He saw her slower, whiter, saw a grimace of pain that she disguised, and Bento, instead of accepting the grandmother's I am fine as the mother accepted it, did what children do, said the truth he saw, aloud, without filter: mother, grandmother is ill. Grandmother has the face we have when we are ill. And Belmira, alerted by the son, looked at the mother seriously, for the first time in months, with the eyes of one who was forced to see, and saw. She saw what had been there for weeks and that she had not seen because the mother hid it from her and because it was comfortable not to see: she saw an ill woman, thin, finished, pretending to be well.

And Belmira was frightened, and the fright came out of her in scolding, as the fear of those who love comes out:

"Mother! What is this? How long have you been like this? Why did you say nothing to me?"

And Plácida, caught, without the wall of the phrases working because Bento had broken through it, lowered her eyes like a child caught in a fault, and said, low, the only truth she had:

"I did not want to give you trouble, daughter. You have so much to do. I managed here."

They called the doctor from outside, the one who came to the village when there was a serious case, a young woman, of few words and sure hands, who had already treated half of Véspera and who had, with the patients of the land, a patience of one who learned that people's bodies always tell more than they say. She examined Plácida, at length, and what she had was not, fortunately, the worst that was feared: it was an infection that had spread for lack of care, a thing that, treated in time, was resolved, but that, left to go on as Plácida had left it, weeks, alone, without telling anyone, could have come to very bad. The doctor said it with clarity, and said it in front of Belmira, on purpose, because she had understood the case at once, that it was a case she knew well, that of the women of the land who fall ill in silence.

"You were lucky," said the doctor, putting away her things. "A few more days like that and it would be another conversation. And what worries me, dona Plácida, is not the infection, for that is treated. It is your having gone weeks with this and not having called anyone. That, yes, that is what kills, more than the illness: the not-calling. I see a lot of this, you know? Above all in the women of your age, those who served everyone their whole lives. They fall ill and keep silent. They think that to ask for help is to give trouble, and they prefer to risk their lives than to bother. And they die, sometimes, of things that were treatable, for not having called in time. They die of the delicacy, dona Plácida. It is a disease, that, as serious as any other, and harder to treat, because there is no syrup for it. The only remedy is for you to learn to call. To say I am ill, come. To ask." She looked at her, serious. "Promise me one thing: next time, at the first sign, you call. You do not wait. You do not manage alone. You call. For your daughter, here looking at you, would a thousand times rather have the trouble of treating you than the grief of losing you for your not having wanted to give trouble."

And Belmira, at her side, nodded, with her eyes full, and the doctor went, and there stayed in the room that word, they die of the delicacy, hovering over Plácida like a diagnosis deeper than that of the infection. They die of the delicacy. Plácida had nearly died of hers, alone, in the dark, with the telephone in her hand and the daughter's number known by heart, for not wanting to wake anyone. And it was there, in that phrase, I did not want to give you trouble, that Belmira understood, suddenly, with a clarity that made her have to sit down, the size of the thing. It was not an illness hidden out of carelessness. It was a mother who had preferred to burn with fever alone, afraid of dying, than to wake the daughter, because to wake the daughter was to give trouble. It was a mother who put the not-bothering above her own life. And Belmira, looking at the mother shrunken, ill, apologising to her for being ill, felt a thing she had never felt for the mother, a mixture of love and of anger and of a deep sadness, and said, with her voice trembling, a phrase that was the beginning of everything the two of them would have to say to one another, later, to the bottom:

"You would rather die alone than bother me. Is that it, mother? You love me so badly that you would rather die than need me?"

And Plácida did not know how to answer, because she had never thought the thing so, had never seen that her not-bothering, which she took for love, for delicacy, for care toward others, could be seen, from the other side, as its contrary: as a refusal to let others love her, as a shutting the door in their faces, as a way, the most polite of all, of telling them I do not need you. And she stayed silent, in bed, with the daughter at her side weeping from anger and from love, and the grandson at the door watching, and it was the first time in her life that Plácida's illness gave trouble to someone, and it was also the first time she realised, vaguely, in the midst of the fever, that perhaps to give trouble, sometimes, was not to take anything from one one loves: it was to give them a thing. It was to let them in. It was to tell them you are needed. And that she, her whole life, in giving no one any trouble, had not been generous: she had been, without knowing it, the most miserly of women, the one who had never given anyone the present of needing them.
