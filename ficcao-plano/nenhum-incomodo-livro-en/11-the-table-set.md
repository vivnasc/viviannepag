## 11. The table set

The summer came, and with it the great festival of the churchyard, that of the saint of the village, that of setting up the long table, the same festival at which, a year before, Plácida had sat on a stool in the corner because she had not put her place. And it was that festival that Plácida chose, without saying it to anyone, for her final examination, for the greatest test of the trade she had spent a year learning.

The year that had elapsed between the two festivals had been the fullest year of Plácida's life, and it was curious that it should be so, because on the outside it had been a common year, without events: the whole difference had been within, in the slow and painful and comic learning of asking. Plácida was sixty-seven years old now, she had turned them that winter, and they had been the first years of her life in which, on the day of her birthday, instead of making a point of no one remembering, so as not to give trouble, she herself had told the daughter and the grandson, look, my birthday is on such a day, come here, I want to spend it with you. It had been the first great request of her calendar of requests: to ask that they remember her birthday, to ask for company to celebrate it, instead of letting it pass in an undertone as she had let dozens pass. And Belmira and Bento had come, and had made her a small party, and Plácida, who her whole life had made the birthdays of others and never her own, had blown out the candles of a cake for the first time in she did not know how many years, and had wept, and Bento had asked why are you crying, grandmother, and she had said from gladness, my treasure, I cry from gladness, which is a thing I did not know existed, to cry from gladness, and I am learning that too. It was so the whole year: a woman of sixty-seven learning things that are learned at five, to ask, to receive, to celebrate herself, to cry from gladness, and each of those small learnings was, for her, a mountain, and each conquered mountain left her nearer the final test, which was the festival of the saint and the place at the table.

Because a year of small requests had prepared her for the great request, and the great request was this: to ask for her place. Not to wait for them to put it for her, not to get a stool when they did not put it, not to serve herself last of what was left over. To ask, aloud, in front of those who set up the table, for her place, in the middle, at the table, with the village, like people who belong.

She went to Felizarda, who was among those who directed the setting up of the table, days before, and said, with her voice trembling but firm, a thing that Felizarda had never heard from her in sixty years:

"Felizarda, this year I want to ask you a thing. I want my place set at the table. In the middle, not at the end. I want a plate of my own, with my name, if need be, in the middle of the table, with the families, with everyone. I do not want this year to serve and sit at the end. I want to be at the table, like you, like all. I know it is strange to hear me ask this, that I never asked for anything, but I am learning to ask, and this is the most important request I have to make, and I make it to you, who are my oldest friend: put me the place, Felizarda. This year I want to have a place."

And Felizarda, astonished, moved, because she realised, with that request, everything she had not realised in sixty years, said yes, of course yes, Plácida, what a shame of me never to have put you the place in the middle, I always let you go to the end and thought it was your liking, and Plácida said the truth, that for sixty years it had indeed been her liking, or what she took for a liking, and that only now, old, had she learned that it was no liking, it was fear, and that she was ceasing to be afraid, and that so she asked. And the two friends, in that conversation, grew closer than they had been in six decades, because for the first time they were speaking the truth to one another, the truth of the place, the truth of the fear, the truth of a whole life of one serving and the other being served, that at last could be said and therefore at last could be changed.

The request of the place was not, however, a request only of a chair. Plácida realised, in preparing it, that to ask for the place in the middle of the table was to ask a much greater thing: it was to ask the village to come to see her another way, to take her from the place of one who serves and put her in that of one who belongs, and that that obliged the village to an effort, because the village had sixty years of the habit of seeing her one way only. It was easier, for all, that Plácida went on at the end serving, as always; to ask for the middle was to give the village trouble, to oblige it to change its mind, and Plácida realised that it was exactly that the point, that to exist is always to give some trouble to others, that a person who gives no trouble at all is a person who obliges no one to count on them, and that she, in asking for the place, was at last obliging the village to count on her, to make her weigh in the heads of others enough to keep her a place. To weigh in the heads of others, to occupy space in another's attention, to give the trouble of having to count on us: it was this, Plácida realised, that she had refused her whole life, and it was this, exactly this, the belonging. One does not belong to a table without weighing on it. One who does not weigh does not belong: one passes.

There were those who marvelled, in the village, when they knew. Plácida asking for a place, Plácida wanting to be in the middle of the table, what fly must have bitten her, it was said, always so modest, so much one for not bothering, and now these demands. And Plácida, who a year before would have drawn back just to imagine that this was said of her, bore the marvelling, because Bento had taught her, and the Keeper had confirmed it, that the marvelling of others is the price of a person beginning to exist, and that it is worth paying, that it is better to be marvelled at for occupying a place than to be forgotten for occupying none.

But the greatest place Plácida took, that year, was not that of the table: it was that of the daughter. Because after the conversation of the coat, after the I-need-you, Plácida had set herself a thing, and she kept it: to let Belmira in, to let the daughter care for her, to ask her for things, to need her, to give her at last the place of daughter that she had denied her her whole life. And it was the most beautiful thing of that year, to see the two of them, the mother and the daughter, learning a new relationship, in which the mother asked and the daughter gave, in which the mother leaned and the daughter supported, in which the mother, at last, weighed on the daughter, and the daughter, instead of feeling burdened, flourished, because to be needed by the mother was what Belmira had waited for her whole life. Plácida asked the daughter to fetch her, to help her with a thing, to keep her company, and each request was a present she learned to make, and Belmira received each request as one receives a gift, and the two of them, at the end of a life of distance, came near, and the distance that neither had known how to name undid itself, not because the mother had come to give more, but because she had come, at last, to ask.

The reconciliation with Felizarda, on that way toward the festival, was another of the things Plácida built, and she built it by asking, which was her new trade. She went to the friend, and said to her a thing she had never said in sixty years: Felizarda, I complained of you within, all these years, without ever telling you. I complained that you spoke and I listened, that you had pains and I consoled, that you never asked me of my things. And the fault was not yours, it was mine, who never gave them you to know, who never asked you to listen to me, who taught you to count on me only to give. But now I want to change that, if you will, and I want to ask you that, from now on, we be friends of the two and not only of one. That sometimes it be you who listens to me. May it be? And Felizarda, who was a good woman beneath the queen, wept, and said yes, and that she was ashamed, and the two of them, in that conversation, remade in half an hour a friendship that had lived sixty years crooked, and Plácida realised that it was never too late, that even a friendship of sixty years could be straightened, it was enough for one of the two to have the courage to say the truth and to ask for the change, and that she, at the end of a life of asking for nothing, was discovering that to ask did not spoil relationships, as she had always feared: it straightened them, deepened them, made them true. Everything she had feared of asking was a lie. The asking did not drive away: it drew near. It did not weigh: it linked. It did not bother: it honoured, because to ask of someone is to tell them that we trust them to the point of showing them our lack.

And there was one afternoon, before the festival, when Belmira said to the mother a thing that closed the circle of the three generations:

"You know, mother, what makes me happiest in all this? It is not your asking me for things. It is what that teaches Bento. Bento sees you asking, now, sees you needing me, and learns that that is normal, that it is good, that it is so that people who love one another do. Bento is not going to grow up as I grew up, mother, thinking that to need is bad, that to ask is to bother. He is going to grow up seeing the grandmother and the mother asking of one another, needing one another, and he is going to be, for that, freer than I was, and than you were. You, in learning to ask at sixty-six years old, did not save only yourself, mother. You broke a thing that came from far back. Bento is no longer going to inherit it."

There was, in the preparation of that festival, a scene that Plácida kept, and that showed how much the village, set to look, knew after all how to see. When it was known that Plácida had asked for the place in the middle, and when the first marvelling passed, there were women in the village — the older ones, those who had served their whole lives like her — who came to Plácida, in an undertone, one by one, with a thing resembling gratitude. Because Plácida, in asking for her place, had opened a door for all of them, the other invisible ones, the others who served and sat at the ends, and each one who came to Plácida said, in her way, the same: you did well, Plácida, I too, all my life at the end, I too never asked, and Plácida realised that she was not the only one, that the village was full of women like her, silent champions of giving no trouble, each on her stool in the corner, each thinking she was the only one, and that in asking for her place she had become, without meaning to, the voice of all, the first to say aloud what all kept silent. And that gave her request a weight she did not expect: it was not only Plácida's place that was at stake, it was the place of all those who served, and Plácida, who had spent her life not wanting to weigh, realised that that was a weight worth the trouble, a weight one carries for many, a weight that frees.

There was, in the preparing of the festival, a scene between Plácida and Bento that sealed the alliance of the two. Bento, who had been the general of the campaign of the requests, realised that the festival was the final battle, and took it seriously. On the eve, he sat with the grandmother and rehearsed with her, like one who rehearses a pupil for the examination: well then, grandmother, what are you going to do at the festival? And Plácida recited, like a lesson: I am going to arrive on time, not before. I am going to sit in my place, in the middle. I am going to serve myself in my turn, not last. And if I want a thing, I ask for it. And Bento corrected, added, grandmother, and if you feel like going away early, do not go in an undertone, take your leave, for people like to know that you were there. And Plácida noted the grandson's corrections, and there was in that a moving inversion of everything: the grandmother being prepared by the grandson, the old woman being taught by the boy, the master of one art making herself the apprentice of the contrary art with a teacher of eight years. And when, at the end of the rehearsal, Bento asked her, and are you afraid, grandmother?, Plácida said the truth, I am, my treasure, I have a fear you cannot imagine, and Bento, with his wisdom, said, I am afraid of things too, grandmother, and what I do is do them all the same with the fear, for the fear always comes with us, it is no use waiting for it to go away because it does not go. And Plácida kept that, do them all the same with the fear, and it was with that phrase of eight years in her pocket that she entered, the next day, the festival that was going to be her crossing.

And Plácida, on hearing the daughter, wept, because it was the most important thing she could have done with her life, and she had done it late but she had done it: she had broken, in herself, a fate that came from the full house of her childhood, from the mother without hands, from the compliment that had been a cell, and she had broken it in time for the grandson not to inherit it. Three generations of women not knowing how to need one another, and she, the old woman, the champion of not-bothering, had been the one who, in the end, broke the chain, by learning, at sixty-six years old, from a boy of eight, the simplest and hardest thing in the world: to ask.
