## 11. The table set

The summer came, and with it the great festival of the churchyard, that of the saint of the village, that of setting up the long table, the same festival at which, a year before, Plácida had sat on a bench in the corner because she had not put her place. And it was that festival that Plácida chose, without saying it to anyone, for her final examination, for the greatest test of the trade she had spent a year learning.

The year that had elapsed between the two festivals had been the fullest year of Plácida's life, and it was curious that it should be so, because on the outside it had been a common year, without events: the whole difference had been within, in the slow and painful and comic learning of asking. Plácida had turned sixty-seven that winter, and they had been the first years of her life in which, on the day of her birthday, instead of making a point of no one remembering, she herself had told the daughter and the grandson. She telephoned Belmira some days before, with her voice trembling as in all the requests:

"Daughter, look, my birthday is on such a day. And I wanted to ask you a thing. Would you come here, you and Bento, to spend the day with me? I feel like spending it with you."

And Belmira and Bento came, and brought a walnut cake bought in the village, and put a candle on top, one only, and lit it, and sang her happy birthday the two of them, there in the small kitchen, and Plácida, seated at the head of her own table, a thing she never did, stayed looking at the flame of the candle not knowing what one did, because her whole life she had made the birthdays of others and never her own, and no one had ever sung her happy birthday in that way, only for her.

"Blow out the candle, grandmother!" said Bento. "And make a wish. But do not say which, or it does not come true."

And Plácida blew out the candle for the first time in she did not know how many years, and made the wish in silence, and the wish was to learn to stay, and her eyes filled.

"Why are you crying, grandmother?" asked Bento, distressed, lowering his fork. "Are you sad? Did you not like the cake?"

"From gladness, my treasure. I cry from gladness." And she laughed with her eyes full, and wiped them on her apron. "It is a thing I did not know existed, to cry from gladness. The grandmother spent her life thinking one should not cry in front of anyone, so as not to give trouble with the sadness. And now she discovers that there is a weeping that is not sad, and that one can cry from gladness in front of one one loves. I am learning that too, Bento. I am learning everything backward, now, in old age."

It was so the whole year: a woman of sixty-seven learning things that are learned at five, to ask, to receive, to celebrate herself, to cry from gladness, and each of those small learnings was, for her, a mountain, and each conquered mountain left her nearer the final test, which was the festival of the saint and the place at the table.

Because a year of small requests had prepared her for the great request, and the great request was this: to ask for her place. Not to wait for them to put it for her, not to get a bench when they did not put it, not to serve herself last of what was left over. To ask, aloud, in front of those who set up the table, for her place, in the middle, at the table, with the village, like people who belong.

She went to Felizarda, who was among those who directed the setting up of the table, days before, and said, with her voice trembling but firm, a thing that Felizarda had never heard from her in sixty years:

"Felizarda, this year I want to ask you a thing. I want my place set at the table. In the middle, not at the end. I want a plate of my own, in the middle of the table, with the families, with everyone. I do not want this year to serve and sit at the end. I want to be at the table, like you, like all. I know it is strange to hear me ask this, that I never asked for anything, but I am learning to ask, and this is the most important request I have to make, and I make it to you, who are my oldest friend: put me the place, Felizarda. This year I want to have a place."

And Felizarda, astonished, moved, because she realised, with that request, everything she had not realised in sixty years, was a moment speechless and then:

"Of course, Plácida. Of course. What a shame of me never to have put you the place in the middle. I always let you go to the end and thought it was your liking."

"For sixty years it was indeed my liking, sister," said Plácida. "Or what I took for a liking. Only now, old, have I learned that it was no liking, it was fear. And I am ceasing to be afraid. So I ask."

And the two friends, in that conversation, grew closer than they had been in six decades, because for the first time they were speaking the truth to one another, the truth of the place, the truth of the fear, the truth of a whole life of one serving and the other being served.

The request of the place was not, however, a request only of a chair. Plácida realised, in preparing it, that to ask for the place in the middle of the table was to ask a much greater thing: it was to ask the village to come to see her another way, to take her from the place of one who serves and put her in that of one who belongs, and that that obliged the village to an effort, because the village had sixty years of the habit of seeing her one way only. It was easier, for all, that Plácida went on at the end serving; to ask for the middle was to give the village trouble, to oblige it to change its mind, and Plácida realised that it was exactly that the point: that to exist is always to give some trouble to others, that a person who gives no trouble at all is a person who obliges no one to count on them. To weigh in the heads of others, to occupy space in another's attention, to give the trouble of having to count on us: it was this that she had refused her whole life, and it was this, exactly this, the belonging. One does not belong to a table without weighing on it. One who does not weigh does not belong: one passes.

There were those who marvelled, in the village, when they knew. Plácida heard it in passing, in the largo, from the mouth of two women who did not see her pass behind them:

"Plácida asking for a place in the middle of the table, have you seen? What fly must have bitten her. Always so modest, and now these demands."

And Plácida, who a year before would have drawn back just to imagine that this was said of her, and would have run to undo the request so that no one should think her demanding, bore the marvelling, and went home with it, and did not draw back, because Bento had taught her, and the Keeper had confirmed it, that the marvelling of others is the price of a person beginning to exist, and that it is better to be marvelled at for occupying a place than to be forgotten for occupying none.

And there was a thing Plácida did not expect. When it was known in the village that Plácida had asked for the place in the middle, and when the first marvelling passed, there began to appear at her door, in an undertone, one by one, women of her age, the older ones, those who had served their whole lives like her. Idalina came, with a pretext of a recipe for marmalade that she had no need to ask anyone, and stayed at the door without coming in, with the empty jar in her hands, wringing it, and at a certain point, without looking at Plácida, said:

"I heard you asked for the place in the middle."

"I did, Idalina."

Idalina was silent a moment, looking at the jar.

"I, my whole life at the end," she said, at last, low. "Sixty years serving the teas and washing the dishes of others, and it never crossed my mind that I might be of those who sit. I thought it was of my nature, you know? That some were born for the parlour and others for the kitchen, and I was of the kitchen." She raised her eyes, and they were wet. "And now I see you, who are the same as I, asking for the middle, and I caught myself thinking, well, and why not I? And I frightened myself with the thought, Plácida, what a shame. But I cannot undo it. You did well." And she went, with no more, with the empty jar, upright and shut, down the street.

And another came, and another, each with a pretext in her hand and the same thing in her mouth, and one of them, the oldest of all, a widow who had served the village as much as Plácida, sat in the kitchen and stayed weeping low at the table, with no great motive, only saying it is late for me, Plácida, I am no longer of an age to learn those things, and Plácida took her hand and told her that it was never late, that she herself was learning at sixty-seven, and that if one could, all could. And she realised, on taking leave of the old woman at the gate, that she was not the only one, that the village was full of women like her, silent champions of giving no trouble, each on her bench in the corner, each thinking she was the only one, and that in asking for her place she had become, without meaning to, the voice of all, the first to say aloud what all kept silent. And that gave her request a weight she did not expect: it was not only Plácida's place that was at stake, it was the place of all those who served, and Plácida, who had spent her life not wanting to weigh, realised that that was a weight worth the trouble, a weight one carries for many, a weight that frees.

But the greatest place Plácida took, that year, was not that of the table: it was that of the daughter. Because after the conversation of the coat, after the I-need-you, Plácida kept what she had set herself: she let Belmira in, let the daughter care for her, asked her for things, needed her. And it was the most beautiful thing of that year, to see the two of them learning a new relationship, in which the mother asked and the daughter gave, in which the mother leaned and the daughter supported, and the daughter, instead of feeling burdened, flourished. And there was an afternoon, before the festival, when Belmira said to the mother a thing that closed the circle of the three generations:

"You know, mother, what makes me happiest in all this? It is not your asking me for things. It is what that teaches Bento. Bento sees you asking, now, sees you needing me, and learns that that is normal, that it is good, that it is so that people who love one another do. Bento is not going to grow up as I grew up, mother, thinking that to need is bad, that to ask is to bother. He is going to grow up seeing the grandmother and the mother asking of one another, needing one another, and he is going to be freer than I was, and than you were. You, in learning to ask at sixty-six years old, did not save only yourself, mother. You broke a thing that came from far back. Bento is no longer going to inherit it."

And Plácida, on hearing the daughter, wept, because it was the most important thing she could have done with her life, and she had done it late but she had done it: she had broken, in herself, a fate that came from the full house of her childhood, from the mother without hands, from the compliment that had been a cell, and she had broken it in time for the grandson not to inherit it.

There was, on the eve of the festival, a scene between Plácida and Bento that sealed the alliance of the two. Bento, who had been the general of the campaign of the requests, realised that the festival was the final battle, and took it seriously. He sat with the grandmother at the kitchen table, with the little notebook open, and rehearsed with her, like one who rehearses a pupil for the examination:

"So, grandmother. What are you going to do at the festival? Say it all."

And Plácida recited, like a lesson:

"I am going to arrive on time, not before. I am going to sit in my place, in the middle. I am going to serve myself in my turn, not last. And if I want a thing, I ask for it."

"Good." Bento noted, serious. "But one is missing. If you feel like going away early, do not go in an undertone, grandmother. Take your leave. For people like to know that you were there."

"You are right. I take my leave." And Plácida noted the grandson's corrections.

And when, at the end of the rehearsal, Bento asked her:

"And are you afraid, grandmother?"

Plácida said the truth:

"I am, my treasure. I have a fear you cannot imagine."

And Bento, with his wisdom, shrugged:

"I am afraid of things too, grandmother. And what I do is do them all the same with the fear. The fear always comes with us, it is no use waiting for it to go away, because it does not go."

And Plácida kept that, do them all the same with the fear, and it was with that phrase of eight years in her pocket that she went to bed, on the eve of the festival that was going to be her crossing.
