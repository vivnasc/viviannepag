## 8. The register

It was on account of a paper for Brando's grave that Plácida went to the House of the Register, that winter, already recovered from the illness, and it was a banal errand that became, without her expecting it, one of the deepest of her life, because the Keeper, on looking for her in the books, took her time, and in the taking of time told Plácida a truth no one had ever told her.

The House of the Register smelled of old paper and of wax, and the Keeper received her at the long table at the back, among the high shelves of the books, with a lamp lit even by day, because the house was dark. Plácida came in with her hat in her hand, and the first thing she said, still at the door, was:

"Forgive me for coming to bother you, Keeper. I know you have much to do. It is only a little thing of nothing, the paper for my Brando's grave, it is dealt with in an instant, I do not want to steal your time."

And the Keeper, who heard that phrase from half the village, set down her pen and looked at her over the books with an attention Plácida did not expect.

"You apologise for existing, Plácida," said the Keeper, without malice, but direct, as she spoke when it was worth her while. "Do you notice? You came in and the first thing you said was sorry to give trouble. It is my trade, woman, to attend to the village, you give me no trouble at all, you give me the work I am here for. But you apologise all the same, for being here, for taking up my time, for breathing the air of this house. And it is not only you. There is a lot of people in this village who do this, who apologise for existing, and they are almost all women, and they are almost all of your age, and they are almost all those who served their whole lives. I have noticed: those who ruled in life do not apologise for anything, they come in here as though the house were theirs. Those who served apologise for everything, they come in here as though they were stealing the air. And you, those who apologise, are the ones with least to apologise for, and the others, those who apologise for nothing, are the ones who most should. The world is turned about, Plácida, and you are the proof: the woman who weighed least in the whole village is the one who most apologises for weighing."

And Plácida was left with that, because it was true, and because no one had ever said it to her so, and because to apologise for existing was, in fact, what she did, everywhere, at every hour, without ever having noticed she did it, as one does not notice the breathing.

The paper for the grave was seen to, which was simple. And then Plácida, out of a new curiosity, of those that Bento had opened in her, asked a thing she had never asked: she asked to see her own record, that of her birth, and those of her life. She wanted to see herself in the book. It was a small request, but it was a request, and Plácida noticed that she made it, and noticed that it cost her, and made it all the same, because she had been training, with Bento, to ask for small things, and that was one.

The Keeper went to the books, and looked, and took her time. She took longer than Plácida expected, and Plácida, at first, thought it was the age of the books, the difficulty of finding. But the Keeper, on coming back, with little in her hand, said a thing that explained the delay and that stayed:

"It was hard to find you, Plácida." She laid down the books, and opened them at the places. "Look. Here your birth, one line. Here your marriage to Brando, one line. Here the birth of your Belmira, and there you are, as a mother, one line. Here the death of Brando, and there you are, as a widow, one line. And nothing more. Sixty-six years, Plácida, and you fit in four lines. There are people in this village who fill pages, from so much they got involved, asked, claimed, gave others to talk about, weighed. You fit in four lines, and three of them are on account of others, the husband, the daughter. By yourself, by a thing that was only yours, that you had asked for or done or claimed for yourself, there is nothing. It is as though you had passed through this village making a point of leaving no trace. And you managed it. You left almost none."

While the Keeper put things away, Plácida stayed looking at the books on the shelves, the rows and rows of records, the whole village of centuries kept there, and she asked a question that was born of the new curiosity:

"And are there many like me? Who fit in few lines?"

"There are two kinds who fit in few lines," said the Keeper, without stopping putting things away. "Those who died young, and those are God's pity, it is not their fault. And those who lived long and effaced themselves, and those are another thing. You are of the second kind, Plácida. You lived sixty-six years and made a point of leaving no mark, and you managed it, and your effacement was so well done that it deceived even this book, which is made to record everyone." She closed a shelf. "Do you know what most costs me, in my trade? It is not to write the dead. It is to write the forgotten. It is to come to the hour of recording someone's death and to realise that that person passed through the whole village without anyone coming to realise that they existed, and that the only proof that they existed is I, here, writing their line. And almost always, Plácida, almost always, those people were not bad, were not anything. They were too good to impose themselves, too humble to ask, too delicate to weigh. They were, many of them, the best of the village. And they are the ones who least stay there. There is an injustice in this that pursues me, and that I cannot correct with my book, because my book only records what people left, and one who left nothing, I have nothing to write."

Plácida looked at the four lines, and it was like looking at a cruel and exact portrait of herself. Four lines. A whole life in four lines, and three of them borrowed from others. The Keeper had put on paper, without malice, with the dryness of her trade, what Bento had said in his way, what the festival had shown her, what the illness had shouted at her: that Plácida had made of her life a work of effacement, and that the work was almost complete, and that the register, which records the whole village, scarcely recorded her, because she had scarcely let herself be recorded.

"And is that bad?" asked Plácida, low, and it was a real question, because a part of her still thought that to fit in four lines, not to weigh even in the register, was a kind of virtue, of lightness, of sanctity.

The Keeper put away the books slowly before answering, and when she answered it was with that authority she had, that of one who keeps the memory of the village and knows what lasts and what does not.

"It is neither bad nor good, Plácida. It is sad." She looked at her. "I have kept the records a long time, and I see the whole village pass through these books, and I have learned a thing: that the records do not measure the goodness of a person. They measure the presence. And there are two ways for a person to scarcely appear in my books. One is that of the selfish who only take and never give, and those I am rid of quickly, for they do not deserve pages. The other is yours, Plácida, and it is the one that most costs me: that of those who give everything and take nothing, that of those who efface themselves from so much not wanting to weigh. Those are the best of the village, and they are the ones who leave least trace, and there is in this an injustice that my book does not know how to correct: that the one who most served is the one who least stays written. You served this village sixty years, Plácida, and my book scarcely knows you. And when you die, and I write your line, it is going to be a small line, and whoever reads it a hundred years from now is going to think that there passed here a woman of no importance, when there passed one of those who most mattered and least let themselves matter. That is the sad thing. Not that you did wrong. It is that you did so much good in a way that does not let it be known."

There was, before Plácida left, an exchange that she took with her. The Keeper, on putting away the copy of the paper for Brando's grave, commented, without giving it importance, that Brando too had been one of the few-lines kind, one of those who had scarcely left a trace, and that the two of them, Brando and Plácida, must have made a quiet pair, two who did not weigh. And Plácida confirmed, and said, with a frankness that came to her more easily now, that yes, that they had been two quiet ones, and that perhaps it had been for that, at bottom, that the life of the two of them, being good, had always had a thing missing, a depth, a weight, that neither of them had known how to give the other because neither of them knew how to weigh. And the Keeper, who heard the confidences of the village like one who gathers rainwater, said a thing that stayed: the marriages of two who do not weigh are the most polite and the most alone, Plácida. Because love, at bottom, is to let weigh. It is to let the other weigh on us and to weigh ourselves on them. Two who do not weigh on one another live side by side and never come to touch, like two stones in the same wall that lean and never mix. You were so. It was not bad. But the weight was missing you, and the weight, Plácida, is what makes love deep. And Plácida took that, and realised, with a retroactive sadness, that what had been missing from her marriage to Brando was this, the weight, the mutual needing, and that they had been thirty years of delicacy without depth, and that if Brando were still alive, now that she was learning to weigh, perhaps they could have had, at the end, the depth they had not had at the beginning. But Brando had died, and that was one of the things she could no longer correct, and that stayed as grief, as a warning for what she could still correct: the daughter, the grandson, the friends, the village, all those who were still alive and on whom she was still in time to weigh.

And Plácida was left with that, and went away from the House of the Register with a thing new and heavy within her: the notion that her effacement was not humility, it was a waste, and not only of herself, but of those who loved her and who would be left, after she died, without even a trace to match what she had been. And at the door, before leaving, she did a thing that surprised even herself, and that was, after asking to see the record, the second request of the day, and the boldest of her life until then:

"Keeper," she said, and her voice trembled a little. "When my hour comes, and you write my line... do not make it small. Write that I served. Write that there was here a woman who served the whole village her whole life and whom no one noticed. Not to boast. So that it stays. So that someone, a hundred years from now, may know that I existed. It is the first thing I ask for myself in sixty-six years, and I ask it of you, who keep what stays."

And the Keeper, who heard many requests, looked at that woman who asked, for the first time in her life, that they not forget her, and there was in her face, which almost never changed, a thing that changed, and she said, laying her hand on the book:

"That line, Plácida, I write it with pleasure. But I am going to tell you a thing better, which is of my trade and so I tell you only once: do not wait for the line. The line is after you die, and it is little, make it I large or small. If you want to be noticed, Plácida, do not ask it of my book, which only writes the dead. Ask it of the living, while you are living. Go to your village, and to your daughter, and to your friends, and instead of serving them in silence, let them notice you. Make yourself noticed, woman. Weigh a little. Ask. For the best way to stay in my books is not a large line when you die: it is a life in which the living have seen you. The records keep who has died. But those who really see us are the living, and of those you have to ask, while you are here, that they look. Go. Go and ask that they look at you. You have already lost sixty-six years. Do not lose any more."

At the door, on leaving, Plácida crossed paths with another woman coming in, one of the old women of the village, one of those who serve, one of the few-lines kind, who came with a kerchief on her head and a paper in her hand, and who said, on entering, exactly as Plácida had said on arriving:

"Forgive me for bothering you, Keeper, it is only a little thing of nothing, it is dealt with in an instant..."

And Plácida stopped on the threshold, and stayed listening, and it was like hearing herself from outside, her own voice in another's mouth, the same apology for existing, the same haste not to weigh, and she looked at the Keeper over the woman's shoulder, and the Keeper looked at her, and between the two of them there passed a thing without words, an understanding, and Plácida saw the Keeper set down her pen, and draw a chair up for the newcomer, and say, sit down, woman, do not hurry, tell me of your life while I look. And Plácida went out into the street thinking of the phrase, ask it of the living while you are living, and the phrase accompanied her the whole way, and went on mixing with that of the grandson, to ask is to notice, and with that of the doctor, they die of the delicacy, and with everything that that year was teaching her, and from the sum of all those phrases there went on forming in her a new conviction, the first of her life that went against everything she had learned as a child: that she had the right to be seen. Not the obligation to serve, for that she had always had, but the right to be seen, to be noticed, to occupy a place in the attention of others, to weigh enough that they noticed her. It was a conviction so simple and so revolutionary, for one who had spent sixty-seven years thinking that her place was to have no place, that Plácida had to repeat it to herself several times, on the way, like one who memorises a new prayer: I have the right to be seen. I have the right to ask. I have the right to weigh. I was not born only to relieve others of my weight; I was born also to weigh on them enough that they love me, because one only truly loves what weighs, what stays, what is noticed by its absence.

And the Keeper, from that conversation, was also changed, in her way, and Plácida knew it some time after: the Keeper, who saw the whole village pass through her books, began, from that day, to notice the others, the other invisible ones, the others of whom there was scarcely anything to write, and to do with them a small thing that was within her trade and that changed some lives. When one of those effaced women went to the House of the Register to see to a paper, the Keeper, instead of dispatching her, lingered, asked her of her life, gave herself the trouble of listening to her, made her, for a quarter of an hour, be seen. It was little, but it was what the Keeper could do from her post, and she did it because Plácida had opened her eyes to a thing that she, from so much recording the dead, had ceased to see in the living: that there are people who die of not being seen, slowly, years before dying in earnest, and that a quarter of an hour of true attention is, for those people, more than all the wax that is lit over the grave.
