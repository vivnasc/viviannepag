## 10. The list

To ask, Plácida discovered, was a trade, and like all trades it was learned slowly, from the beginning, with knack and with relapses, and Plácida, who her whole life had been a master of the contrary art, had to make herself an apprentice at sixty-six years old of the hardest art of all: that of bothering.

She learned it in her way, which was the only one she had: with method, with a list. She had always been a woman of lists, but her lists had always been of others, of what to do for others, of what to take to whom, of what to see to for whom. Now she made a new list, the first of her life that was for herself, and it was a list of requests: small things she resolved to ask, one a day, like one who takes a bitter remedy by spoonfuls. And Bento, who had sealed the alliance with her, helped: he had made of this a game, a game he took very seriously, and every day he asked the grandmother, with the importance of a general, well then, grandmother, have you asked today? whom did you ask? and Plácida had to render accounts to the grandson of her requests, as a pupil renders accounts of the homework.

Bento's game had rules, which he established with the seriousness of one who invents a law: one request a day, at the least; it had to be a real request, a thing she truly needed, not a pretend request; it was not allowed to ask and undo; and at the end of the day accounts were rendered, the grandmother told the grandson what she had asked and of whom, and the grandson, who had got a little notebook for it, noted, in the big crooked hand of eight years, the grandmother's requests, one per line, like one who records the progress of a patient. And that little notebook of Bento's, with the grandmother's requests noted in pencil, went on becoming, over the weeks, the most precious document of Plácida's life, because it was the proof that she was changing, line by line, request by request, and there were days when Plácida, on lying down, thought of what she would be able to tell the grandson she had asked, and that anticipation, that of rendering accounts to the grandson, gave her courage to make requests she would not make alone, because she did not want to reach the end of the day and tell Bento today I asked nothing, did not want to disappoint the little general of her own liberation. And there was an enormous tenderness in that, a boy of eight leading the grandmother by the hand along the way that all the adults of her life had not known how to lead her, and Plácida realised that Bento managed what no one had managed not for being wiser, but for being a child, for not yet having learned that to ask is shame, and so being able to teach the grandmother, with the naturalness of one who has not yet been spoiled, the thing that the adults, all of them already spoiled like her, did not know how to teach her.

And the first requests were ridiculous for how small they were, and they cost her as though they were enormous, because for Plácida they were enormous. The first was to the neighbour, of whom she went to ask a cup of sugar, having sugar at home, only to train the gesture, and her hand trembled on knocking at the door, and her voice came out small, and the neighbour lent the sugar without giving it the least importance, of course, woman, take it, and Plácida went home with the cup of sugar like one who comes back from a battle, exhausted and victorious, and told Bento, who clapped.

There was the comic tragedy of the sugar, that Plácida and Bento told one another many times afterwards, laughing. For, having asked for the sugar only to train and having sugar at home, Plácida was left with the neighbour's cup of sugar and did not know what to do with it, and her conscience would not let her keep sugar asked for as training, and she, instead of using it, kept it to return it, and then thought that to return borrowed sugar was odd, and went about for days with the neighbour's cup of sugar on the table, distressed, not knowing how to undo a favour she had asked for without needing, until Bento, practical, said to her, grandmother, use it and be done, or let us make a cake with it, and they made a cake, and took half to the neighbour, and the neighbour was glad, and Plácida learned, with the episode of the sugar, that to ask puts a person into a web of exchanges, of returns, of shared cakes, of comings and goings, and that that web, which she had always avoided for seeming to her a tangle of obligations, was after all the very fabric of the neighbourhood, the mesh of small favours and small debts that links people to one another, and from which she had kept herself outside her whole life, by never asking, and into which she now entered, at sixty-seven years old, by the door of a cup of sugar.

There was the request of the bench, which was among the hardest, because it was to ask a thing that truly gave trouble. Plácida needed to move a heavy piece of furniture, and for weeks she tried to move it alone, in bits, at the risk of hurting herself, before obliging herself to do what any person would do at the first: to ask a neighbour to help her. And she asked, with her heart in her mouth, and the neighbour came, and moved the furniture in an instant, and even asked her whether there was anything more to move while he was there, and Plácida, who would have said no, thank you, do not trouble yourself, heard herself say, with a superhuman effort, look, while you are at it, if you do not mind, there is another thing there, and the neighbour moved the other thing too, and went away satisfied at having helped, and Plácida was left with the furniture in place and with a deep lesson: that the neighbour had come out of her house happier than he had gone in, that to help had given him pleasure, that she, in asking, had not taken his time nor bothered him, she had given him the small joy of being useful to a neighbour, and that that joy, that of being useful, was the same that she herself felt when she helped others, and that therefore, by never asking, she had denied others her whole life the joy she most liked to feel. The second was to ask a man of the largo to reach her a thing from a high shelf, a thing she would reach with a stool, and the man reached it without thinking, and Plácida thanked too much, and the man marvelled at the disproportionate thanks, and Plácida realised that she still had to learn to ask without making of the request an event, to ask with naturalness, as Bento asked, as though it were the most normal thing in the world, which is what it is, for everyone except her.

And there were the relapses, many, and Bento was implacable at catching them. There was the day when Plácida asked a thing of a friend and, the instant after, undid the request, ah no, leave it, after all it is not needed, I manage, and Bento, who witnessed it, said, grandmother, you asked and then you took it back, that does not count. There was the day when Plácida, ill again of a small cold, again said nothing to anyone, out of habit, and it was Bento who obliged her to telephone Belmira to say I have a cold, can you come and see me, and Plácida made that telephone call like one who leaps from a precipice, and Belmira came, and was glad to come, to be called, and Plácida saw the daughter glad that she had called her, and realised, again, what Bento had taught her: that to ask was not to take, it was to give; that in calling the daughter, she had not bothered her, she had given her a joy, that of being needed.

There was a request, among the small ones, that Plácida never forgot, because it was the one that most cost her and the one that most taught her. She decided to ask Belmira to take her somewhere, one afternoon, with no motive of usefulness at all, only because she felt like it: to ask the daughter for an outing, a company, a bit of her time, not to see to anything, only for pleasure. And it cost her dreadfully, because to ask someone for time, time without usefulness, was for Plácida the height of bother: to take from the daughter an afternoon only to be with her, with no profit at all, seemed to her an abuse, a selfishness. But she asked, with her voice trembling, Belmira, will you take me one of these days to see the sea? I feel like seeing the sea, it is so many years since I saw the sea, and I feel like seeing it with you. And Belmira, on hearing the request, was a moment speechless, and then her eyes filled, because it was the first time in her life that the mother asked her for time, company, a thing only for pleasure, and she realised the size of what that request cost the mother and therefore the size of the love it contained. And they went, the two of them, to see the sea, one afternoon, doing nothing, only looking at the water, and it was one of the happiest afternoons of the lives of the two of them, and they did nothing useful, and it was exactly for doing nothing useful that it was so happy, because it was the first time they were together only to be, mother and daughter, without the mother serving nor seeing to anything, only two women looking at the sea because one of them, at last, had asked.

Slowly, the hand of asking went on loosening, as a stiff joint loosens, with pain at first and then with a growing relief. And Plácida went on discovering things along the way, things everyone knows and that she, by never having asked, had never learned. She discovered that people like to be asked, that a request is a form of trust, of intimacy, that to tell someone I need you is to tell them I trust you, I choose you, you are important to me. She discovered that in asking, she did not go down, as she had always feared: she came near. That the wall of it-is-not-needed, I-manage, which she had taken her whole life for delicacy, was after all what had kept her alone, on the inside, and that each request was a brick of that wall falling, a door that opened, someone who entered. She discovered, at last, that to exist and to ask were, at bottom, the same thing: that a person who never asks anything of anyone is a person who refuses to occupy a place in the world of others, and that to occupy a place in the world of others, to weigh a little in the life of those who love us, was after all what she had spent sixty-six years calling bother and what was, in truth, simply, to live among others, to take part, to belong.

And there was a request, among the small ones, that was great, and that Plácida kept. She asked Felizarda, the friend of sixty years, a thing she had never asked her: she asked her to listen to her. She went to Felizarda's house, sat at the table where it had always been she who listened, and said, with her voice trembling, Felizarda, today it is I who need to unburden myself, today I need it to be you who listens to me, can you? And Felizarda, astonished, disconcerted, because that had never happened in sixty years, was a moment not knowing how to be on the other side of the relationship. But she was a good woman, Felizarda, and the disconcert passed, and she did what she had never done: she listened to Plácida. She heard her speak of her pains, of her solitude, of her fear, of her whole life of not-bothering, and at a certain point Felizarda too was weeping, and said, Plácida, forgive me, sixty years talking to you of my things and I never asked you of yours, what a friend was I? And Plácida said the truth, that it had not been Felizarda's fault, that it had been she, Plácida, who had never given the friend the chance to listen to her, who had taught Felizarda, as she had taught the world, to count on her only to give. And the two of them, that afternoon, sixty years late, were for the first time friends in earnest, friends on both sides, because Plácida, at last, had asked, and in asking had given Felizarda the chance to be, she too, a friend who gives, and not only one who receives.

Not all the requests went well, and Plácida learned also from those that went badly, because to ask, she discovered, brings with it a thing she had spent her life avoiding: the possibility of no. One who never asks never hears no, and Plácida had realised, without knowing it, that part of what had led her to never ask was the fear of the no, the fear of asking a thing of someone and that person refusing, and of the humiliation she imagined in that refusal. And when, in her training, a request was refused — she asked a thing of an acquaintance who was in a hurry and the acquaintance said she could not, not now, Plácida — Plácida felt the old terror, the wish to melt into apologies, to swear she would not ask again, to withdraw again into the shell of not-bothering. But Bento, to whom she told the no, gave her the lesson that was missing: and so what, grandmother? She said no, and then? Did something fall on you? No. She said no, and you ask another person, and that is that. The noes do no harm, grandmother. I ask for things and sometimes they tell me no, and I ask another person or I ask another day. It is part of it. And Plácida realised that the grandson had, at eight years old, a wisdom she did not have at sixty-six: that to ask implies accepting the no, that the no is not a humiliation, it is only an answer, and that a person who cannot bear to hear no is a person condemned never to ask, and therefore condemned never to receive, because one only receives who risks the no. To learn to ask was, also, to learn to bear the no without coming undone, and Plácida trained that too, and it was among the most freeing things, to realise that the sky did not fall even when they told her no.

"Have you asked today, grandmother?" asked Bento, that night.

"I have, my treasure," said Plácida, and smiled, a smile that came to her from a new place. "I asked the greatest thing I have ever asked. I asked a friend of sixty years to listen to me. And she listened to me, Bento. For the first time in sixty years, someone listened to me." And she pressed the grandson against her. "And the one who taught me was you, my treasure. It was you who taught the grandmother to ask. What a thing, a boy of eight teaching an old woman of sixty-six the most important thing there is. Thank you, Bento. And look, this that I said to you now, this thank you, is also a thing I never knew how to say truly, and I am learning it, and I am learning it with you."
