## 10. The list

To ask, Plácida discovered, was a trade, and like all trades it was learned slowly, from the beginning, with knack and with relapses, and Plácida, who her whole life had been a master of the contrary art, had to make herself an apprentice at sixty-six years old of the hardest art of all: that of bothering.

She learned it in her way, which was the only one she had: with method, with a list. She had always been a woman of lists, but her lists had always been of others, of what to do for others, of what to take to whom. Now she made a new list, the first of her life that was for herself, and it was a list of requests: small things she resolved to ask, one a day, like one who takes a bitter remedy by spoonfuls. And Bento, who had sealed the alliance with her, helped, with the importance of a general, and had got a little notebook where he noted, in the big crooked hand of eight years, the grandmother's requests, one per line, like one who records the progress of a patient. Every afternoon accounts were rendered:

"So, grandmother? Have you asked today? Whom did you ask?"

And there were days when Plácida, on lying down, thought of what she would be able to tell the grandson she had asked, and that anticipation gave her courage to make requests she would not make alone, because she did not want to reach the end of the day and tell Bento today I asked nothing, did not want to disappoint the little general of her own liberation.

The first request was to the neighbour, and it was ridiculous for how small it was, and it cost her as though it were enormous. It was to ask for a cup of sugar, having sugar at home, only to train the gesture. She knocked at the door with her hand trembling, and when the neighbour opened, her voice came out small:

"Neighbour, forgive me for bothering you, it was only... perhaps you might have a little sugar there to lend me? It is that I am in the middle of a thing and..."

"Sugar? Of course, woman, take it, take all you like," said the neighbour, and filled her cup without giving it the least importance, and Plácida went back home with the cup of sugar like one who comes back from a battle, exhausted and victorious, and told Bento, who clapped.

And there was the comic tragedy of the sugar, that the two of them told one another many times afterwards, laughing. For, having asked for the sugar only to train and having sugar at home, Plácida was left with the neighbour's cup and did not know what to do with it. Her conscience would not let her use it, because it was sugar asked for as training, and she, instead of spending it, kept it to return it, and then thought that to return borrowed sugar was odd, and went about for three days with the neighbour's cup of sugar on the table, distressed, not knowing how to undo a favour she had asked for without needing, until Bento, practical, raised his eyes from the little notebook and said:

"Grandmother, use it and be done. Or let us make a cake with it."

And they made a cake, and took half to the neighbour, and the neighbour was glad, and Plácida learned, with the episode of the sugar, that to ask puts a person into a web of exchanges, of returns, of shared cakes, of comings and goings, and that that web, which she had always avoided for seeming to her a tangle of obligations, was after all the very fabric of the neighbourhood, the mesh of small favours that links people to one another, and from which she had kept herself outside her whole life, and into which she now entered, by the door of a cup of sugar.

There was the request of the furniture, which was among the hardest, because it was to ask a thing that truly gave trouble. Plácida needed to move a heavy chest, and for a week she tried to move it alone, in bits, with a blanket beneath it, at the risk of hurting her back, before obliging herself to do what any person would do at the first. She went out to the street, with her heart in her mouth, to Antero, the neighbour from next door, who was tending the vegetable plot.

"Antero, forgive me... might you give me a hand to move a piece of furniture? It is that it is heavy, and I alone cannot..."

"I am coming, dona Plácida," he said, wiping his hands on his trousers, and came, and moved the chest in an instant, and even looked around the room and asked: "Do you want me to move anything else, while I am here? Would not that chest of drawers be better over there?"

And Plácida, who would have said no, thank you, do not trouble yourself further, heard herself say, with a superhuman effort:

"Look, Antero, while you are at it, if you do not mind, there is that chest of drawers that..."

And Antero moved the chest of drawers too, and went away satisfied at having helped, and Plácida was left with the furniture in place and with a deep lesson: that the neighbour had come out of her house happier than he had gone in, that to help had given him pleasure, and that she, in asking, had not taken his time, she had given him the small joy of being useful, which was the same that she herself felt when she helped others. By never asking, she had denied others her whole life the joy she most liked to feel.

There was, among these, the request of the man of the largo. Plácida needed to reach a thing from a high shelf of the pantry, a bottle of brandy she had kept from Brando's wake, and that she would have gone to fetch with a stool, at the risk of falling. But she was in training, and instead of the stool she resolved to ask, and asked the man who passed in the largo with a hoe over his shoulder, Saturnino, who was tall:

"Saturnino, will you do me a service? I have a thing on a high shelf that I cannot reach, and you are tall."

"How should I not," said Saturnino, and came in, and reached the bottle in one gesture, and Plácida thanked him, and thanked him, and thanked him again, ah thank you so much, forgive the bother, God repay you, and the man marvelled at so much thanks for a thing of nothing:

"But what the devil, woman, it was only to reach up there. There is no need for so much."

And he left, and Plácida was left realising that she still had much to learn, that to ask was not only to say the request, it was to ask without making of it an event, without melting into apologies, as Bento asked, with the naturalness of one who has not yet been spoiled, as though to ask were the most normal thing in the world, which is what it is, for everyone except her.

And there were the relapses, many, and Bento was implacable at catching them. There was the day when Plácida asked a thing of a friend and, the instant after, undid the request:

"Lend me your big pan, for I have people to... ah no, leave it, after all it is not needed, I manage with the small one, do not trouble yourself."

And Bento, who witnessed it from the kitchen bench, with the little notebook in his lap, said:

"Grandmother. You asked and then you took it back. That does not count. I have to cross it out."

There was also the relapse of the cold, which was the most revealing of all. Plácida caught a chill, of the kind that puts one to bed, and did out of habit what she had always done: she said nothing to anyone, lay down with a tea, and was going to dispatch it alone. But Bento, who was at her house that day, saw her sniffle and shiver under the blanket, and planted himself at the edge of the bed with the little notebook in his hand:

"Grandmother, you are ill. You have to telephone my mother."

"No, my treasure, this is nothing, it is a cold, it passes. I am not going to bother your mother for a cold."

"Grandmother." Bento crossed his arms, with seven spans of gravity. "The doctor said it. At the first sign, you call. And you promised. And it is a point in the championship."

And Plácida, cornered between the doctor and the general, took up the telephone, and dialled the daughter's number with her finger trembling, and when Belmira answered, she said, quickly, like one who leaps from a precipice:

"Belmira... look, it is nothing grave, do not be frightened. I only have a cold. But... could you come and see me? If you can."

And from the other end, instead of the bother Plácida had so feared, came a glad voice:

"I am coming, mother. I will bring you a syrup and make you a broth. Do not leave the bed, do you hear? I am coming."

And Belmira came, and made the broth, and stayed the afternoon, and Plácida saw in the daughter's face, on setting the bowl in her lap, a joy that disarmed her, and realised, again, what Bento had taught her: that in calling the daughter she had not bothered her, she had given her a joy, that of being needed. And Bento, in the corner, opened the little notebook and noted the point, very serious, with his tongue out.

There was a request, among the small ones, that Plácida never forgot, because it was the one that most cost her and the one that most taught her. She decided to ask Belmira to take her somewhere, one afternoon, with no motive of usefulness at all, only because she felt like it: to ask the daughter for an outing, a company, a bit of her time, not to see to anything, only for pleasure. And it cost her dreadfully, because to ask someone for time, time without profit, was for Plácida the height of bother. But she asked, by telephone, with her voice trembling:

"Belmira... look, I wanted to ask you a thing. But it is a silly thing, do not laugh. I feel like seeing the sea. It is so many years since I saw the sea, daughter. Will you take me one of these days to see the sea? And I feel like seeing it with you."

And from the other end there fell a silence, and then Belmira, with her voice changed:

"You want to see the sea, mother? With me?"

"I do, daughter. If you can."

"We will go on Sunday. We will go, of course we will go."

And they went, the two of them, to see the sea, one Sunday, and sat on a low wall looking at the water, with a kerchief on their heads against the wind, and stayed silent a good while, only watching the waves, and at a certain point Plácida said:

"Do you know how many years it is since I saw the sea, daughter? Since before you were born. Your father brought me here once, when we were young. And never again. I always thought it was far, that it was trouble, that it was not worth a person going to see water only to see it."

"And does it turn out to be worth it?" asked Belmira.

"It is worth it, daughter." And Plácida stayed looking at the sea, with her eyes shining from the wind or from another thing. "It is worth it, and I deprived myself of it my whole life, and I deprived myself of everything that was only for enjoyment, because I thought a person like me had no right to enjoy anything, only to serve. And after all I had. What a shame, daughter. What a shame to have taken sixty-six years to ask you to bring me to see the sea."

And Belmira took her hand, there on the low wall, and said nothing, and the two of them stayed watching the water, and it was one of the happiest afternoons of the lives of the two of them, and they did nothing useful, and it was exactly for doing nothing useful that it was so happy, because it was the first time they were together only to be, mother and daughter, without the mother serving nor seeing to anything, only two women looking at the sea because one of them, at last, had asked.

Slowly, the hand of asking went on loosening, as a stiff joint loosens, with pain at first and then with a growing relief. And Plácida went on discovering things that everyone knows and that she, by never having asked, had never learned: that people like to be asked, that a request is a form of trust, that to tell someone I need you is to tell them I trust you, I choose you, you are important to me. That in asking, she did not go down, as she had always feared: she came near.

And there was a request, among the small ones, that was great, and that Plácida kept. She asked Felizarda, the friend of sixty years, a thing she had never asked her: she asked her to listen to her. She went to Felizarda's house, sat at the table where it had always been she who listened, and before Felizarda began to pour out her things, she said, with her voice trembling:

"Felizarda, today it is different. Today it is I who need to unburden myself. Today I need it to be you who listens to me. Can you?"

And Felizarda, astonished, was a moment with the cup in the air, not knowing how to be on the other side of the relationship, because that had never happened in sixty years.

"You, Plácida? You want to unburden yourself? But... has something happened to you?"

"My whole life has happened to me, Felizarda. And I never told you. Sit down, for today I speak and you listen."

And Felizarda, who was a good woman beneath the queen, shut her mouth and listened, for the first time in sixty years. She heard Plácida speak of her pains, of her solitude, of her fear, of her whole life of not-bothering, and at a certain point Felizarda too was weeping, and said:

"Plácida, forgive me. Sixty years talking to you of my things and I never asked you of yours. What a friend was I?"

"It was not your fault, sister," said Plácida. "It was I who never gave you the chance. I taught you, as I taught the world, to count on me only to give. The fault, if there is one, is of the one who never asked."

And the two of them, that afternoon, sixty years late, were for the first time friends in earnest, friends on both sides, because Plácida, at last, had asked.

Not all the requests went well, and Plácida learned also from those that went badly, because to ask, she discovered, brings with it a thing she had spent her life avoiding: the possibility of no. One day she asked a thing of an acquaintance who was in a hurry in the largo, and the woman answered:

"Ah, I cannot now, Plácida, I am in a great hurry, leave it for another time."

And Plácida felt the old terror, the wish to melt into apologies, to swear she would not ask again, to withdraw again into the shell of not-bothering. But she told the no to Bento, that afternoon, still shaken, and Bento, with the little notebook in his hand, gave her the lesson that was missing:

"And so what, grandmother? She said no, and then? Did something fall on you?"

"No, my treasure. Nothing fell on me."

"There. She said no, and you ask another person, and that is that. The noes do no harm, grandmother. I ask for things and sometimes they tell me no, and I ask another person or I ask another day. It is part of it."

And Plácida realised that the grandson had, at eight years old, a wisdom she did not have at sixty-six: that to ask implies accepting the no, that the no is not a humiliation, it is only an answer, and that the sky did not fall even when they told her no.

"Have you asked today, grandmother?" asked Bento, that night, pencil in the air.

"I have, my treasure," said Plácida, and smiled, a smile that came to her from a new place. "I asked the greatest thing I have ever asked. I asked a friend of sixty years to listen to me. And she listened to me, Bento. For the first time in sixty years, someone listened to me." And she pressed the grandson against her. "And the one who taught me was you, my treasure. A boy of eight teaching an old woman of sixty-six the most important thing there is. Thank you, Bento. And look, this thank you is also a thing I never knew how to say truly, and I am learning it, and I am learning it with you."

And Bento, very serious, opened the little notebook, wet the pencil on the tip of his tongue as he saw the men do, and noted one more line.
