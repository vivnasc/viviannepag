## 1. The one who does not bother

Plácida had a talent, one only, but she had carried it to perfection: she knew how to give no trouble as others know how to sing or to heal or to make bread. It was her art, the only one in which she was a master, and she practised it with a deftness no one saw, because the perfection of that art lay precisely in not being seen.

She was the perfect guest. When she went to someone's house, she arrived on time, always brought something, ate of what there was without preferences, did not make a mess, did not ask, and went away a little before she began to weigh, with that exact instinct she had for the eve of the bother. The mistresses of the houses said of her that she was a delight of a visitor, that one did not even notice her, and they said it as a compliment, and Plácida received the compliment as the greatest of prizes, because her whole life she had competed for it.

She was the perfect friend. She listened to the friends, all of them, hours on end, their pains, the husbands, the children, the diseases, and had for each one the right word, the lap, the patience, and never, never, put on them her own pains, because a friend who gives pains is a friend who weighs, and Plácida did not weigh. The friends went away from her lighter, relieved, and loved her for it, and for that very reason never asked her how she was, because Plácida was always well, Plácida never had anything wrong, Plácida was the one who listened, not the one who needed to be listened to.

She was the perfect patient, in the rare times the village knew she was ill, which were few, because even to fall ill Plácida had modesty. She fell ill in an undertone, dealt with the fever alone, and when someone visited her, ill, it was she who melted into apologies for giving trouble, she in bed apologising to one who came to visit her, and the visit went away with the sensation of having been well received by a sick woman, which is a thing that should not exist and that Plácida had invented.

And at the table, at the long table of the festivals, Plácida sat at the end. She always sat at the end, in the corner, in the place from which it is easiest to get up to fetch, to serve, to help, and she served herself last, when she served herself, from what was left over, and she did it naturally, with pleasure even, because at the end, serving, helping, Plácida felt herself in her place, which was the place of one who has no place, the place of one who serves the others' table without occupying a place at it.

This art, like all of them, had its rules, and Plácida kept them without ever having written them, because she carried them in her body since childhood. The first rule was to arrive to help and not to be served: to a house, to a festival, to a wake, Plácida always arrived a little before, to catch the work, so as not to arrive at the hour of sitting down like one who comes to be waited on. The second rule was to have no preferences: one who prefers gives trouble, because they oblige others to know what they want, and Plácida wanted nothing special, ate of everything, sat where it fell out, said to everything it is fine like this, and her lack of preferences was so complete that people, at the end of years, no longer even asked her, because Plácida was well with everything, Plácida was not one for demands. The third rule, the most important, was to go away before weighing: there was, in every visit, in every festival, an exact point from which presence begins to cost, and Plácida had for that point an infallible nose, and she always got up an instant before it, with the excuse ready, and went out, and left behind her the best of impressions, that of one who never weighed.

To keep these rules her whole life had cost her a thing she never added up: it had cost her her whole life. Each anticipated arrival, each swallowed preference, each hurried departure, was a little bit of Plácida that did not happen, a wish that was not said, a taste that was not exercised, and the sum of all those little bits, at the end of sixty-six years, was a woman almost wholly unlived, a woman who had served all the festivals and been at none of them, who had listened to all the friends and been listened to by none, who fit in every corner by occupying none. But this Plácida did not add up, because to add up what one has not lived is too painful, and she had, for her own life, the same delicacy she had for that of others: she did not bother herself with accounts that weighed.

There was still, in this art of Plácida's, a deeper deftness, which was that of turning her own not-having into virtue. When someone, rarely, noticed some lack of hers and offered to supply it, Plácida always had, on the tip of her tongue, the answer that gave back the care before receiving it: it is not needed, I manage, do not trouble yourself, leave it, I will see to it. They were the phrases of her life, said so many times that they came out of her on their own, without her thinking, as the breathing comes out. It is not needed. I manage. Do not trouble yourself. Leave it. Four phrases, and with them Plácida had built a wall around herself, a wall courteous and impassable that kept everyone on the outside of her needing, and that she took for delicacy and that was, seen from outside, the best-mannered solitude one can imagine: that of one who never lets anyone in to help, and stays, for that, for ever, on the inside, alone, managing.

That summer, there was a great festival in the churchyard, of the kind where the long table is set up, and Plácida went, and did what she always did: she helped set the table, served, filled glasses, carried and brought, sat at the end when she sat, ate what was left over, and in the middle of the afternoon, when the festival was at its height and no one needed her any more for anything, she felt the first weariness, the weariness of one who has served the whole afternoon, and did what she always did: she went away, in an undertone, without taking leave of each one so as not to interrupt the festival, with the lightness of one who leaves a room without the door creaking.

Brando, the husband, in life, had been the only one who sometimes checked her. He was a meek man, of the fields, of few words, and of him Plácida kept above all this: that from time to time, at a festival, he took her by the arm and said to her, low, sit down, woman, rest, for you serve everyone and do not stop. And Plácida sat down, for him, a little, but the moment he was distracted she got up again, because seated she did not know how to be, seated she felt herself weighing, and rest, for her, was a discomfort greater than the weariness. Brando had died some years before, meek even in dying, without giving trouble, and Plácida had wept for him in an undertone, and had widowed as she had lived, without asking anything of anyone, and since he had died there was no longer, at any festival, anyone to take her by the arm and say to her sit down, woman. No one any more noticed that she did not stop. Brando had been the last to notice, and had taken with him, on dying, the only pair of eyes that saw, in Plácida, the woman beneath the help.

She went home, at the end of the summer afternoon, alone, and at home she did what she did, fed the cat, watered the pots, and sat at the door to watch it grow dark, rested from the duty fulfilled, the woman who had served the festival and withdrawn before weighing. And it was there, at the door, in the growing dark, that the thought came to her that was going to change everything, and it came to her not as a pain, but as a curiosity, almost amused at first: she asked herself whether anyone, at the festival, would have noticed her absence.

And she knew the answer at once, and the answer did not hurt her in that instant, because it was an answer so old, so much her own, that it was already part of her: no. No one would notice her absence. The festival would go on the same, with or without Plácida, because Plácida was not at the festival a presence that was noticed by its absence. She was a help, and helps, when they go, leave more work for the others, but they leave no absence. No one, at the end of the festival, would say where is Plácida, what became of Plácida. At most, the next day, someone would say Plácida helped so much, poor thing, and it would be the compliment of custom, the one of always, the one she had earned her whole life: Plácida gives no trouble at all, one does not even notice her.

One does not even notice her. The phrase came to her mouth, at the door, in the dark, and it was the first time in her life she heard it not as a compliment, but as what it was: an exact description of a woman who, by dint of not wanting to weigh, had achieved the terrible feat of not existing.

There had been, at the festival, before she went away, a small moment that said everything about her place in the world. In the middle of the afternoon, one of the women of the village, on seeing her pass with a platter, said, without malice, with the familiarity of one who orders about one who is used to being ordered about: oh Plácida, bring me more wine to this end, if you please. And she said it without even looking at her, in the distracted way one asks a thing of one whom one knows will do it, and Plácida brought the wine, of course, and the woman did not even thank her, turned already to the conversation. It was all so natural, so much of always, that no one noticed. But Plácida, in that year of the new eye, noticed: she noticed that they had asked her for the wine as one asks a servant, without looking, without thanking, and that she had served as a servant serves, and that that was her place at the festival, and that that place, which she had always thought was that of goodness, was after all, seen from outside, the place of one who does not count. And there hurt her, for the first time, a thing that for sixty-six years had not hurt her, because for sixty-six years she had thought that was her place and she was well in it: there hurt her to suspect that no one is born to be the wine that is asked for without looking, that she deserved, like everyone, to be looked at when a thing was asked of her, and thanked when she did it, and that not to be looked at nor thanked, over a lifetime, was no humility at all: it was a small injustice repeated thousands of times, and she had collaborated in all of them, by accepting them in silence.

There was, however, at that festival, an exception, and the exception was eight years old and was called Bento.

Bento was the grandson, son of Belmira, and he was, of all the people in the world, the only person who noticed Plácida. At the festival, he had sought her out ten times: grandmother, come and see this, grandmother, help me with that, grandmother, stay here beside me. Bento asked her for everything, chose her among all the adults of the festival, and Plácida, with the grandson, was another: she was sought, she was needed, she was chosen, and there was in those requests of the grandson a thing that warmed her in a way she could not name, and that was, though she did not yet know it, the thing most like existing that she felt. When Plácida went away from the festival, in an undertone, it was Bento, and only Bento, who saw her leave, and who ran after her to the gate of the churchyard, and who asked her, with the frankness of eight years: grandmother, are you going already? And why do you not take leave of anyone?

And Plácida, crouched beside the grandson, at the gate of the churchyard, gave him a kiss and said the thing she always said, the thing that was the motto of her whole life, and said it with a smile, without understanding, in that instant, what she was confessing to a child:

"It is not needed, my treasure. So as not to bother. Grandmother goes without bothering, which is the best thing grandmother knows how to do."

And she went, in the growing dark, and Bento stayed at the gate watching her go, and had, with the sometimes terrible wisdom of children, a serious face, of one who did not quite understand but understood enough to be left sorry, without knowing of what. And at home, at the door, watching it grow dark, it was that face of the grandson that stayed with Plácida, more than the question about the absence that no one would notice: the face of the only being in the world who noticed her, watching her go away alone, sorry for a thing he did not yet have a name to name, but that Plácida, that night, for the first time in sixty-six years, began, very lightly, to have.

And there was, still that night, a small moment that contained everything. Plácida set the table for supper, and set it, by the habit of a lifetime, for one person, with a single plate, hers, at the corner of the big table, and sat down to eat alone, quickly, without ceremony, because to cook and set a table properly for herself would be to give herself trouble, and she gave no one trouble, not even herself. She had always eaten so since Brando died: standing many times, or sitting at the corner, of what was left over, in a hurry, like one who dispatches a formality, because she thought that a woman alone does not deserve the trouble of a meal set as it should be, that that is for one who has company, for one who counts. And it had never occurred to her, in years of widowhood, to set herself a beautiful table, to light a candle, to sit down with time, to treat herself with the care with which she treated others, because to treat herself would be to give herself importance, and to give herself importance was, in Plácida's dictionary, the greatest sin. She ate at the corner of her own table as she sat at the corner of others' tables: everywhere at the end, everywhere not occupying a place, even in her own house, even alone, even with no one to efface herself for. She had become so good at not weighing that she effaced herself even when she was alone, that she served herself last even when she was the only one at the table. And that, perhaps, was the deepest proof of the championship she had won: that she no longer knew how to exist even for herself, that not even for herself did she give herself the trouble of setting the table as it should be, because even herself she treated as she treated everything that was hers — as a thing not worth the bother.
