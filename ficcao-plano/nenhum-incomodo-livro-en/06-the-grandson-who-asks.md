## 6. The grandson who asks

For Plácida to convalesce, it was decided — and it was Belmira who decided, with a new firmness, because the fright of the hidden illness had given her a firmness she had never had with the mother — that Bento would stay a few days at the grandmother's house, to keep her company and watch over her, with the pretext that it was the grandmother minding the grandson, but with the truth, which both women knew and neither said, that it was the grandson minding the grandmother.

The pretext, besides, was chosen with care, and was Belmira's choice, who was learning quickly to deal with the new mother: she realised that Plácida would never accept Bento staying to mind her, because that would be to give the grandson trouble, to make herself out ill, to weigh on a child; but that she would accept, gladly, Bento staying for her to mind him, because that was to serve, was her place of always. So Belmira wrapped the thing the other way round: she told the mother that she needed her to keep Bento a few days, that she, Belmira, was busy, that it would be a great favour. And Plácida, of whom a favour had been asked, to whom a place of usefulness had been given, accepted at once, glad, without suspecting that the favour was the other way round, that it was she who was being cared for under the appearance of caring. And there was in this a tenderness and an irony that Belmira savoured: that to make the mother let herself be cared for, she had had to lie to her that it was she who was caring, had had to disguise the care as service, because the mother only knew how to receive what came wrapped in giving. It was the first of many wrappings that Belmira would have to invent, in that time, to make the mother accept care without noticing it, until the day, later, when the mother learned to accept it with her face on show, with no wrapping at all.

And they were the most revealing days of Plácida's life, because Bento, at eight years old, did to her, without knowing it, what no one had done to her in sixty-six: he needed her without stopping, asked of her without modesty, and in needing and asking, taught her, without a word of lesson, everything she had left to learn.

There was a difference between Bento's requests and everything Plácida had served her whole life, and that difference was what opened her eyes. When she served the village, the friends, the festivals, she served things that the others could do alone and chose to have her do: Felizarda could serve her own coffee, but let Plácida serve it; the village could tidy its festivals, but let Plácida tidy them. It was a service that relieved one who already sufficed unto themselves, and so it was a service that effaced the one who did it, because to serve one who suffices is to be dispensable, is to do what the other would do all the same without us. But Bento, at eight years old, truly needed her: he could not make the milk, did not know how to sew, was truly afraid of the dark. Bento's requests were of real need, and to serve a real need, Plácida realised, does not efface: it fulfils. To be needed by one who does not suffice unto themselves is to be irreplaceable, is to do what no one else does, is to exist in a way that the serving of those who suffice had never given her. And it was that difference, between being used by one who suffices and being needed by one who does not suffice, that taught Plácida to distinguish, at last, her old serving, the one that effaced her, from true love, the one that fulfilled her, and to realise that she had spent her life confusing the two, calling love the service that effaced her, and never having known, except with Bento and at the end with the ill Brando, the other, the one that made her be someone.

Bento asked the whole day. Grandmother, make me the milk with bread as you know how. Grandmother, tell me the story of the wolf and the fountain. Grandmother, come and see what I built. Grandmother, stay here beside me for I am afraid of the dark. Grandmother, I need you to sew this for me. Grandmother, grandmother, grandmother. It was a river of requests, without end, without ceremony, and Plácida, who her whole life had lived on the side of one who serves unasked, discovered a thing that left her astonished: that to be asked so, openly, without modesty, by someone who wanted her, was the sweetest thing she knew. Each grandmother, make me, each grandmother, I need you, was like a small light lit within her, a confirmation that she existed, that she was needed, that she had a place in someone's world. Bento, in asking of her, gave her existence, and Plácida, serving the grandson, did not feel as she felt serving the village, emptied, invisible: she felt full, seen, chosen, because Bento did not use her as the village used her, Bento chose her, wanted her, and there is all the difference in the world between serving one who uses us and serving one who chooses us.

There were, in those days, small scenes that Plácida kept, because each one taught her a thing. There was the morning when Bento, making a drawing, asked her to sit beside him only to watch him draw, doing nothing, only to be there, and Plácida, who did not know how to be without doing, sat down, uncomfortable, with her hands wanting to get up for some task or other, and Bento, without looking up from the drawing, said, grandmother, be still, I want you here only watching, and Plácida stayed, and discovered, at the end of a few minutes, that to be wanted only to be there, without serving for anything, was a thing she had never experienced, and that it was strange, and that it was good, and that it filled her eyes with water without her quite knowing why. Her whole life she had been wanted for what she did; Bento wanted her for what she was, sitting there, doing nothing, only grandmother, only presence. And that, for a woman who had built herself whole around usefulness, was a revelation: that one could be wanted without being useful, that there was a love that was not paid for with service, and that she had never known it because she had never let herself be wanted except serving.

There was the afternoon when Bento, playing, asked her to play with him, not to serve him, not to make his snack, but to play, to be, in the game, the mother-lioness and he the little lion, and Plácida, who had not played since childhood, since the full house where she had learned not to give trouble even playing, played, awkward at first and then given over, and laughed, and played the lioness, and was, for an afternoon, a child she had never been, because the child she had been had spent her childhood not weighing and not playing. And at night, lying down, she thought that the grandson was giving her back, at sixty-six years old, bits of a childhood she had never had, and that it was a strange and beautiful thing, a grandmother learning to be a child with the grandson, out of time, sixty years late, but learning.

And it was in serving the grandson's requests, and in letting herself be wanted by him, that Plácida arrived, alone, at the edge of the great discovery of the book, and it was Bento himself who put it in her hand, with a child's question, of those that pierce what the adults take a lifetime to skirt.

The two of them were at the table, one afternoon, Plácida sewing a tear in the grandson's shirt, Bento watching her sew, and Bento, out of nowhere, with the straight logic of his age, asked:

"Grandmother, I ask you for a lot of things. Everyone asks you for things, I see. One always asks you." He frowned, doing the sum. "But you, grandmother? Whom do you ask? I never saw you ask anyone for anything."

And Plácida stopped the needle in the air, because the grandson's question was the question of her life, said by a mouth of eight years, without malice, with pure arithmetic curiosity: if everyone asks the grandmother, the grandmother asks whom? And the truth, which Plácida did not say to the grandson but said to herself with the needle stopped in the air, was simple and devastating: no one. The grandmother asked no one. The grandmother was sixty-six years old and did not remember the last time she had asked something of someone, a help, a favour, a lap, a company, nothing. The grandmother was a well from which everyone drew water and into which no one ever threw anything, because the grandmother did not let them, because the grandmother had closed the mouth of the well with the courteous lid of it-is-not-needed, I-manage.

"The grandmother does not need to ask, my treasure," said Plácida, at last, taking up the needle again, with the phrase of custom. "The grandmother manages."

And Bento, who was eight years old but was no fool, looked at her with a face that Plácida never forgot, a face between astonishment and pity, and said a thing in which the whole book fits, a thing that only a child could say because only a child has not yet learned that there are truths one does not say:

"But grandmother, that is sad." He said it simply, like one who notes the weather. "To ask is good. I like to ask you for things, do you know why? It is not only because of the things. It is because when I ask you, you grow glad. I see it. You grow glad when I need you." He shrugged, with the disarming wisdom of his age. "If you never ask anything of anyone, grandmother, then you never let anyone grow glad for needing you. You are keeping that only for yourself. It is selfish, grandmother."

Selfish. The word, said by a boy of eight, struck Plácida with a force that no adult's sermon would have had, because it was the last word she would ever have applied to herself. Her whole life, Plácida had thought herself the contrary of selfish: the generous one, the one who gives, the one who does not weigh, the one who effaces herself for others. And the grandson, with the clean logic of eight years, had turned the thing inside out and shown her what she had never seen: that never to ask is not generosity, it is a form of avarice. That in refusing to need others, she denied them the joy of being needed, the joy she herself felt, and adored, each time Bento asked of her. That she was keeping only for herself the good side of the relationship, that of being asked, and denying others that same side, by never asking. That her goodness had, at bottom, a hidden selfishness: that of wanting always to be the one who gives and never the one who owes, always the strong one and never the frail one, always above and never below, because to be below, to need, to owe, to ask, gave her a fear that she had disguised her whole life as virtue.

There was still, in those days with the grandson, a scene that was the luminous reverse of the festival of the stool in the corner, and that showed Plácida what she might have been. Bento fell ill, a child's cold, and Plácida cared for him, and Bento, ill, did the exact contrary of what she did when ill: he asked for everything, complained of everything, called for the grandmother at every moment, grandmother, my head hurts, grandmother, bring me water, grandmother, stay here with me, grandmother, tell me a story, and he had, at no moment, the modesty of bothering, because a child healthy of soul does not know that modesty, knows that when one is ill one calls, one asks, one lets oneself be cared for, and that that is what there is to do. And Plácida, caring for the grandson who asked without modesty, saw, side by side, the two ways of falling ill: his, which called and was cared for and healed in the midst of everyone's love; and hers, which fell silent and hid and nearly died alone in the dark. And she realised that the grandson's way was the healthy one, the natural one, the one God or nature had made, and that hers, that of the silence, that of the not-bothering, was the ill one, the twisted one, the learned one, and that she had spent her life thinking that her way was the virtuous one and the grandson's would be, in an adult, a defect, a rudeness, when it was the contrary: the grandson's way was the sound one, and hers, the too-polite one, was the disease. Would that, thought Plácida watching over the grandson, would that I had known how to fall ill so, like this boy, asking without shame. Would that someone had taught me, when small, that one can be ill and call, instead of teaching me to be a little saint and not to complain. Perhaps I would have had another life. Perhaps I would have weighed, and stayed, and been seen. And she looked at the grandson asleep, after being cared for, serene, and promised him in silence that she would do everything so that he would never unlearn that, so that he would grow up knowing how to ask, knowing how to call, knowing how to weigh, and that if her life served for anything, from then on, it would serve for that: to keep, in the grandson, the health of one who knows how to need, that she had lost and was now, old, trying to regain.

That night, with Bento already asleep, Plácida stayed at the kitchen table, alone, with the sewn shirt in her lap, mulling over the grandson's word. Selfish. And she realised that the grandson was right, and she realised more: she realised that the grandson was the only one, in sixty-six years, who had really got inside her, and that he had got in by the only door there was, the door of asking. Bento loved her in a way no one else loved her, with an intimacy that not even the daughter had, and it was because Bento asked, and in asking he got in, and in getting in he saw her, and in seeing her he loved her and not her help. And Plácida realised that if she wanted to be loved so, seen so, by anyone besides the grandson — by the daughter, by the friends, by the village —, she was going to have to do the impossible thing, the thing the grandson had taught her without knowing it: she was going to have to open the door of asking, was going to have to let others need her by letting herself need them, was going to have to set down, at the end of a life, the lid of the well, and let someone, once at least, throw water in instead of only drawing it out.
