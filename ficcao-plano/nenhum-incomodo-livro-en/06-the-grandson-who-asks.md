## 6. The grandson who asks

For Plácida to convalesce, it was decided, and it was Belmira who decided, with a new firmness that the fright of the hidden illness had given her, that Bento would stay a few days at the grandmother's house, to keep her company and watch over her, with the pretext that it was the grandmother minding the grandson, but with the truth, which both women knew and neither said, that it was the grandson minding the grandmother.

The pretext was chosen with care. Belmira realised that Plácida would never accept Bento staying to mind her, because that would be to give the grandson trouble, to make herself out ill, to weigh on a child; but that she would accept, gladly, Bento staying for her to mind him. So she wrapped the thing the other way round:

"Mother, I need a favour. I am busy this week, with work and everything, and I have no one to leave Bento with. Will you keep him a few days? It would do me a great service."

And Plácida, of whom a favour had been asked, to whom a place of usefulness had been given, accepted at once, glad:

"Of course, daughter, of course. Leave the little one with me, I will see to him. Do not distress yourself."

And she did not suspect that the favour was the other way round, that it was she who was being cared for under the appearance of caring. And there was in this a tenderness and an irony that Belmira savoured: that to make the mother let herself be cared for, she had had to lie to her that it was she who was caring, because the mother only knew how to receive what came wrapped in giving. It was the first of many wrappings that Belmira would have to invent, in that time, until the day, later, when the mother learned to accept care with her face on show, with no wrapping at all.

On the first day, Bento almost spoiled the wrapping. The two of them were at the supper table, and the boy, whom Belmira had warned that he had to pretend, but whom Belmira had forgotten that children do not pretend well, said, eating his soup:

"Grandmother, my mother said I had to mind you because you were ill and..."

And he stopped, with the spoon in the air, on seeing the grandmother's face change, and amended quickly, with the cunning of eight years:

"...because you were going to mind me. I meant that. Mind me."

And Plácida, who for an instant had suspected, let herself be deceived gladly, because she wanted to be deceived, because the truth, that it was she who was cared for, gave her trouble to accept, and the lie, that it was she who cared, was comfortable as an old garment. And she said:

"That is so, my treasure. The grandmother minds you. Eat all your soup, for you are still growing."

And they were the most revealing days of Plácida's life, because Bento, at eight years old, did to her, without knowing it, what no one had done to her in sixty-six: he needed her without stopping, asked of her without modesty, and in needing and asking, taught her, without a word of lesson, everything she had left to learn.

Bento asked the whole day.

"Grandmother, make me the milk with bread as you know how."
"Grandmother, tell me the story of the wolf and the fountain."
"Grandmother, come and see what I built."
"Grandmother, I need you to sew this for me, I tore myself on the wall."
"Grandmother, stay here beside me, for I am afraid of the dark."
"Grandmother, give me your hand until I fall asleep."
"Grandmother, do not go yet, stay a little longer."

It was a river of requests, without end, without ceremony, and Plácida, who her whole life had lived on the side of one who serves unasked, discovered a thing that left her astonished: that to be asked so, openly, by someone who wanted her, was the sweetest thing she knew. Each grandmother, make me, each grandmother, I need you, was like a small light lit within her, a confirmation that she existed. And there was a difference between Bento's requests and everything she had served her whole life, and that difference was what opened her eyes. Felizarda could serve her own coffee, but let Plácida serve it; the village could tidy its festivals, but let Plácida tidy them. It was a service that relieved one who already sufficed, and so it was a service that effaced the one who did it. But Bento, at eight years old, truly needed her: he could not make the milk, did not know how to sew, was truly afraid of the dark. To serve a real need, Plácida realised, does not efface: it fulfils. And it was that difference, between being used by one who suffices and being needed by one who does not suffice, that taught her to distinguish, at last, her old serving, the one that effaced her, from true love, the one that fulfilled her.

There were, in those days, small scenes that Plácida kept. There was the morning when Bento, lying on his belly on the kitchen floor making a drawing, asked her a strange thing:

"Grandmother, sit here beside me."

"What for, my treasure? Do you want something? I will fetch you a juice."

"I do not want anything. I want you here only watching me draw. Be still. Do not do anything."

And Plácida sat, uncomfortable, with her hands in her lap wanting to get up for some task or other, and at the end of a few minutes, with nothing to do, feeling herself almost at fault, she discovered a thing she had never experienced: that to be wanted only to be there, serving for nothing, was strange, and was good, and that it filled her eyes with water without her quite knowing why. Her whole life she had been wanted for what she did; Bento wanted her for what she was, sitting there, doing nothing, only grandmother, only presence.

There was the afternoon of the milk. Bento wanted the grandmother to make him the milk with bread as she knew how, with the sugar burnt on top, and he sat on the kitchen bench watching her, his feet swinging, and in the middle he asked:

"Grandmother, did your mother make you the milk like this?"

And Plácida stopped, with the spoon of sugar in her hand, because no one ever asked her of her own mother, and she said:

"No, my treasure. My mother had many children and little time. The milk, in my house, was a pot in the middle of the table and each one served himself, and whoever came late went without. I always came late on purpose, so as not to take from the others."

"Then you went without milk?"

"Many times."

Bento stayed thinking about that, serious, and then said:

"Well now you drink it. Make two, grandmother. One for me and one for you. And sit down to drink with me, do not drink standing."

And Plácida made two, and sat beside the grandson, and drank her milk with bread, slowly, at the table, and it was the first time in sixty years that she did for herself what she did for others, and it was a boy of eight who ordered her to.

There was the afternoon when Bento, playing, asked her to play with him, not to serve him, not to make his snack, but to play, to be, in the game, the mother-lioness and he the little lion, and Plácida, who had not played since childhood, since the full house where she had learned not to give trouble even playing, played, awkward at first and then given over, and growled, and was the lioness after the little lion through the house, and laughed, and was, for an afternoon, a child she had never been. And at night, lying down, she thought that the grandson was giving her back, at sixty-six years old, bits of a childhood she had never had.

And it was in serving the grandson's requests, and letting herself be wanted by him, that Plácida arrived at the edge of the great discovery of the book, and it was Bento himself who put it in her hand, with a child's question, of those that pierce what the adults take a lifetime to skirt.

The two of them were at the table, one afternoon, Plácida sewing a tear in the grandson's shirt, Bento watching her sew, with his chin resting on his hands, and out of nowhere, with the straight logic of his age, he asked:

"Grandmother, I ask you for a lot of things. Everyone asks you for things, I see. One always asks you." He frowned, doing the sum. "But you, grandmother? Whom do you ask? I never saw you ask anyone for anything."

And Plácida stopped the needle in the air, because the grandson's question was the question of her life, said by a mouth of eight years, without malice, with pure arithmetic curiosity: if everyone asks the grandmother, the grandmother asks whom? And the truth, which Plácida did not say to the grandson but said to herself with the needle stopped in the air, was simple and devastating: no one. The grandmother asked no one. She was sixty-six years old and did not remember the last time she had asked something of someone, a help, a favour, a lap, nothing.

"The grandmother does not need to ask, my treasure," she said, at last, taking up the needle again, with the phrase of custom. "The grandmother manages. The grandmother always manages, alone."

"But why?" insisted Bento, who did not let things go. "Everyone needs. I need, my mother needs. Only you never?"

And Plácida did not know how to answer, and pushed the needle into the cloth to have her hands busy, because the truth did not fit in a phrase one could say to a boy.

And Bento, who was eight years old but was no fool, looked at her with a face that Plácida never forgot, a face between astonishment and pity, and said a thing in which the whole book fits, a thing that only a child could say because only a child has not yet learned that there are truths one does not say:

"But grandmother, that is sad." He said it simply, like one who notes the weather. "To ask is good. I like to ask you for things, do you know why? It is not only because of the things. It is because when I ask you, you grow glad. I see it. You grow glad when I need you." He shrugged, with the disarming wisdom of his age. "If you never ask anything of anyone, grandmother, then you never let anyone grow glad for needing you. You are keeping that only for yourself. It is selfish, grandmother."

Selfish. The word, said by a boy of eight, struck Plácida with a force that no adult's sermon would have had, because it was the last word she would ever have applied to herself. Her whole life, Plácida had thought herself the contrary of selfish: the generous one, the one who gives, the one who does not weigh, the one who effaces herself for others. And the grandson, with the clean logic of eight years, had turned the thing inside out and shown her what she had never seen: that never to ask is not generosity, it is a form of avarice. That in refusing to need others, she denied them the joy of being needed, the joy she herself felt, and adored, each time Bento asked of her. That she was keeping only for herself the good side of the relationship, that of being asked, and denying others that same side, by never asking.

There was still, in those days with the grandson, a scene that was the luminous reverse of the festival of the bench in the corner. Bento fell ill, a child's cold, small fever and a runny nose, and Plácida cared for him, and Bento, ill, did the exact contrary of what she did when ill: he asked for everything, complained of everything, called for the grandmother at every moment.

"Grandmother, my head hurts."
"Grandmother, bring me water."
"Grandmother, stay here with me, do not go away."
"Grandmother, tell me a story until I fall asleep."

And he had, at no moment, the modesty of bothering, because a child healthy of soul does not know that modesty, knows that when one is ill one calls, one asks, one lets oneself be cared for. And Plácida, caring for the grandson who asked without modesty, saw, side by side, the two ways of falling ill: his, which called and was cared for and healed in the midst of everyone's love; and hers, which fell silent and hid and nearly died alone in the dark. And she thought, watching over the grandson now asleep after so much asking: would that I had known how to fall ill so, like this boy, asking without shame. Would that someone had taught me, when small, that one can be ill and call, instead of teaching me to be a little saint and not to complain. And she looked at the grandson serene, and promised him in silence that she would do everything so that he would never unlearn that, so that he would grow up knowing how to ask, knowing how to call, knowing how to weigh.

That night, with Bento already asleep, Plácida stayed at the kitchen table, alone, with the sewn shirt in her lap, mulling over the grandson's word. Selfish. And she realised that the grandson was right, and she realised more: she realised that Bento was the only one, in sixty-six years, who had really got inside her, and that he had got in by the only door there was, the door of asking. Bento loved her in a way no one else loved her, with an intimacy that not even the daughter had, and it was because Bento asked, and in asking he got in, and in getting in he saw her, and in seeing her he loved her and not her help. And Plácida realised that if she wanted to be loved so, seen so, by anyone besides the grandson, by the daughter, by the friends, by the village, she was going to have to do the impossible thing, the thing the grandson had taught her without knowing it: she was going to have to open the door of asking, was going to have to set down, at the end of a life, the lid of the well, and let someone, once at least, throw water in instead of only drawing it out.
