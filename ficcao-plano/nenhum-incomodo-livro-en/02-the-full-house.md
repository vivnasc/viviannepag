## 2. The full house

To understand Plácida one must enter the house where she was born, and the house where she was born was full: full of people, full of hunger, full of everything but space and hands.

The siblings were many, so many that Plácida, a grown woman, no longer counted them by heart without erring, and she was the youngest, the last of a batch the mother had borne over twenty years. The mother was an exhausted woman before Plácida was born, a woman whose hands, two only, had never been enough for the children who were many, and who lived in the state of one who is always putting out fires, attending to what cries loudest, to what weeps, to what bleeds, to what falls. In a house like that, the mother's attention was a scarce good, the scarcest of all, scarcer than the bread, and the children disputed it as one disputes what is lacking: with shouts, with illnesses, with tantrums. All except Plácida.

Plácida, the youngest, arrived at a house where the war for attention was already in full battle, and the little girl, small, without strength to compete in the noise, did, without knowing she did it, the most intelligent and most tragic thing a child can do: she realised she could not win the mother's attention by competing for it, and won it by the contrary way, by asking for none. She became the one who gave no trouble. While the siblings shouted, she fell silent. And she discovered that that was the way, in that house, for a mother without hands to be able to love her: she loved her for not weighing, for being, in the midst of the weight of all, the only one who relieved instead of burdening.

And there was the day of the compliment, which was the day the fate closed, and Plácida remembered it her whole life, because it was the happiest day of her childhood and it was her condemnation, the two things in the same instant.

The house was full of visitors, an aunt on the father's side with people who came from away, and the mother served and apologised for the noise of the children. One wept clutching her skirt, another wanted bread and beat the table with his spoon, another threw a tantrum over a place, and the mother went from one to the other, with the kerchief crooked on her head, apologising to the aunt.

"You must forgive this house," she said. "It is so the whole day long, there is no rest. They are many, and each pulls his own way."

The aunt, at a certain point, rested her eyes from the noise and set them on the corner, where Plácida, small, was quiet, playing alone with a wooden spoon, asking for nothing.

"And that one, there?" she asked. "Whose is that one, so calm?"

And the mother, with a sigh of relief in the midst of the weariness, turned to look at the daughter and said the phrase that Plácida carried her whole life:

"That is Plácida, the youngest. That one, ah, that one gives me no trouble at all. One does not even notice her. Would that they were all like that, then I would have a life."

"What a fine girl," said the aunt, and smiled toward the corner. "So quiet."

And Plácida, in the corner, with the spoon in her hand, felt in that instant a thing she had never felt: she felt loved. She felt seen, praised, chosen, and understood, with the searing clarity of children, exactly what she had done to deserve it: she had not asked for anything, had not weighed, one had not noticed her. That was the path of love, in that house. Not to exist was the path of being loved.

The father was a figure of passage, a man who worked from sun to sun and who came home exhausted, with neither hands nor soul for more children than the ones he could barely greet, and at supper he had the custom of counting the children at the table, like one who checks an account, finger in the air, pointing head to head. There was a supper, when she was little, when he erred in the count. He went from one end of the table to the other with his finger, counted, counted again, and concluded:

"They are all here."

And a brother, one of the middle ones, raised his eyes from his plate and said:

"Plácida is missing, father. Plácida is over there."

The father turned his head to the corner where she was, and said:

"Ah, yes. Plácida." He counted her, with his finger, closing the account. "There, now they are all here."

And he went on eating. And the little girl, in her place, felt in that forgetting of the father not a sharp grief, but a lukewarm confirmation of what she already knew: that she was the one one forgot to count, the one who did not make a number, the one who was always there and so was not seen. And instead of that revolting her, it refined her art still more, because the little girl concluded, with the crooked logic of one who learns early to survive the lack of love, that if not even the father counted her, then the way was to go on giving no cause, to deserve by her quietness the little of the attention that was left over.

She learned early to serve, too, because to serve was the active form of not weighing: at seven, at eight years old, Plácida was already the one who took up the smaller ones, the one who set and cleared the table, the one who offered for what no one wanted to do. And the house grew used to that, and the village grew used to that, and the little Plácida became, before ten years old, the little helpful one, the one one orders about, the one who serves, and gained in that the only identity she had: not that of a girl with wishes and tastes, but that of a small and reliable usefulness that went about the house relieving the others.

And there was, in that house, a word that wounded more than the mother herself imagined, and it was Plácida's name used as a rod. When a brother complained, or threw a tantrum, or would not run an errand, the mother flung at him at once:

"See if you do as your sister Plácida, who does not complain. Plácida never gave me a grief. Learn from Plácida."

And the brothers, on hearing that, turned to the corner where Plácida was, and it was not love they had in their eyes: it was the resentment of one who is measured by a ruler and found wanting. Once, one of them, of those nearest her in age, said to her low, on going to bed, with a child's rancour:

"Because of you it is I who get it. You are the little saint. Would that you did not exist."

And Plácida, in the dark of the pallet she shared with two sisters, was left with that, and did not weep so as not to give trouble even with her weeping, but she understood, even small, the trap she was in: that the virtue that earned her the mother's praise took from her the affection of the brothers, who made her pay for being scolded on her account. She won the praise of the adults, which is distant and cold, and lost the warmth of her equals, and was left in the middle of the two, cold, in a cold that accompanied her her whole life and of which, faithful to herself, she never complained.

There was, in childhood, a brother who was the reverse of Plácida, and to whom she owed, without ever having told him, part of who she became. He was called Aurélio, was one of the middle ones, and was the one who made the most noise: the one who shouted, the one who got angry, the one who broke things, the one who ran away from home and came back, the one who gave the mother sleepless nights. Plácida remembered a night when Aurélio came back from a flight, soaked from the serra, and the mother, who had called him the whole day a wretch and her cross, the moment she saw him at the door dropped everything, went to him, took hold of him, scolded him weeping, rubbed his head with a cloth, and stayed with him at the hearth half the night, warming his feet, telling him what a boy, what a boy, you will be the death of me. And Plácida, in the dark, shrunk in her corner, saw that with the eyes that see everything in silent children, and saw a thing no one else saw: that it was Aurélio, the one who gave trouble, the one who weighed, who had all the mother's attention. The mother was with Aurélio in a whole way, though distressed, in which she was never with Plácida, because Plácida gave no cause. And the little girl learned, watching the brother at the hearth, the perverse lesson that was going to govern her whole life: that one who weighs is carried, and one who is carried is touched, and one who is touched exists; and that she, in choosing not to weigh, had chosen not to be carried, not to be touched, not to exist.

Aurélio died young, of a troubled life, and the mother wept for him years, and Plácida realised, without bitterness but with a sad clarity, that the brother who had given all the trouble had stayed in the mother's heart much deeper than she, who had never given any.

The mother had died long ago, and Plácida was beside the bed on the day she went, and it was she, besides, who saw to everything in the last days, who fed her by hand, who changed her sheets, who moistened her lips with a cloth, in silence, without giving trouble to anyone, while the brothers came and went and wept aloud. The house, full again, now of grown children come from far, and the mother, already at the end, with her voice failing and her eyes wandering over the ceiling, began to call.

"Aurélio," she said, low. "Aurélio, where are you?"

And Aurélio had been dead for years. And the mother went on, naming, one by one, the children who had made her exist as a mother, those who had shouted, those who had weighed, those who had given her sleepless nights and so had stayed engraved in the depth of her. And Plácida, who held her hand, who was the only one there holding her hand, waited. She waited for the mother to call her too, at least once, at least at the end. And the mother did not call her. She went on fading with the names of the others in her mouth, and Plácida's hand in hers, and she never said the name of Plácida, because the hand that held her was the hand of the one who had never weighed, and of that hand the mother was not even aware. And afterwards, in the parlour, at the hour of the coffee and the memories, someone told it as a joke, the mother did not even remember to call for Plácida, poor thing, who was the best of all, and the others laughed with tenderness, and Plácida laughed with them, because to laugh was to give no trouble, was not to weigh with her grief in a family conversation. But within, deep, in a place where she never went, the whole thing stayed with her: that the mother had called, at death, for the children who had needed her, for those she had carried, because it is those who weigh who stay in our hearts, and Plácida, who had never weighed, had not stayed in the mother's last memory. The compliment had been fulfilled to the end. Not even at death did the mother notice her.

The marriage to Brando, years later, did not break the pattern: it continued it, with another face. Brando was a meek man, of the fields, of few words, and the village said the two had chosen well, two quiet ones, two people who gave no one any trouble, and it was true, but it was a sad truth, because what had united them had been, at bottom, the same thing that effaced each of them: the art of not weighing. They lived thirty years so, two not asking, and it was a good life and it was a life in which a thing was always missing that neither of them knew how to name: the weight was missing, the asking was missing, that way of two people carrying one another that is, after all, what makes of a marriage a deep thing and not only a delicate coexistence.

Brando, at the end, ill, was the only one who came near to teaching her the contrary, because the illness obliged him, at last, to ask. Plácida remembered the last week, of him already without strength, and a night when he, with effort, held out his hand to her from the bed and said, with his voice in a thread:

"Sit here beside me, woman. Do not go. Stay."

And she sat, and stayed, and held his hand the whole night, and discovered, caring for the dying husband, a thing that surprised even herself: that she adored being needed, that there was in her an enormous hunger to be needed that a whole life of not-bothering had never satisfied. In those final days, Brando, who his whole life had given her no trouble, gave it to her at last, and Plácida cared for him with a giving that was almost happiness, and behind the grief there was a thing she was ashamed to feel and felt all the same: the deep pleasure of being missed. But Brando died, meek even in dying, and took with him the only person who, at the end, had come to need her truly, and Plácida was left again alone with her art, more alone than ever, because now she already knew, after caring for Brando, the pleasure it had been to be needed, and she had no one to be it for.

And so the art of giving no trouble, which Plácida had taken up as a child with the determination of one who finds the key to a safe, had run through her whole life, improved year after year to perfection, without her ever realising that the prize of that championship was terrible: the prize was to disappear. The better she played, the less she existed. Each time she did not ask, did not weigh, was not noticed, she won a little bit of the love-for-not-bothering that the mother had taught her, and lost a little bit of existence, and at the end of sixty-six years she had won the whole championship and lost the whole of life. And what in the mouth of the exhausted mother had been the greatest of compliments was, seen the whole of a life at once, the deepest of sadnesses.
