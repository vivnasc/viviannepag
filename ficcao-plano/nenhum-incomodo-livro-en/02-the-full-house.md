## 2. The full house

To understand Plácida one must enter the house where she was born, and the house where she was born was full: full of people, full of hunger, full of everything but space and hands.

The siblings were many, so many that Plácida, a grown woman, no longer counted them by heart without erring, and she was the youngest, the last of a batch the mother had borne over twenty years of marriage, between the work of the field and the work of the house and the work of bearing. The mother was an exhausted woman before Plácida was born, a woman whose hands, two only, had never been enough for the children who were many, and who lived in that state of one who is always putting out fires, attending to what cries loudest, to what weeps, to what bleeds, to what falls. In a house like that, the mother's attention was a scarce good, the scarcest of all, scarcer than the bread, and the children disputed it as one disputes what is lacking: with shouts, with illnesses, with tantrums, with tears, each making their noise to wring from the exhausted mother a little of the attention that was not enough for all.

All except Plácida.

Plácida, the youngest, arrived at a house where the war for attention was already in full battle, with the elder ones knowing all the tricks, and the little girl, small, without strength to compete in the noise, did, without knowing she did it, the most intelligent and most tragic thing a child can do: she realised she could not win the mother's attention by competing for it, and so she won it by the contrary way, by asking for none. She became the one who gave no trouble. While the siblings shouted, she fell silent. While the siblings demanded, she contented herself. While the siblings pulled the exhausted mother in all directions, she learned not to pull, to tend to herself, to resolve alone what she could, to hide what she needed, and discovered that that was the way, in that house, for a mother without hands to be able to love her: she loved her for not weighing, for being, in the midst of the weight of all, the only one who relieved instead of burdening.

And there was the day of the compliment, which was the day the fate closed, and Plácida remembered it her whole life, because it was the happiest day of her childhood and it was her condemnation, the two things in the same instant.

The house was full of visitors, some aunt with her people, and the mother, exhausted, served and apologised for the noise of the children, of the one who wept, of the one who got angry, of the one who wanted this and that, and the aunt, at a certain point, looked at the corner where Plácida, small, was still, asking for nothing, playing alone with a spoon, and asked whose was that one, so quiet, and the mother, with a sigh of relief in the midst of the weariness, said the phrase that Plácida carried her whole life:

"That is Plácida, the youngest. That one, ah, that one gives me no trouble at all. One does not even notice her. Would that they were all like that, then I would have a life."

One does not even notice her. Would that they were all like that. The mother said it with love, with relief, with the gratitude of an exhausted woman for having, in the midst of so many who weighed, one who did not weigh. And the aunt agreed, and looked at Plácida with approval, what a fine girl, so quiet, and Plácida, in the corner, with the spoon in her hand, felt in that instant a thing she had never felt: she felt loved. She felt seen, praised, chosen, and understood, with the searing clarity of children, exactly what she had done to deserve it: she had not asked for anything, had not weighed, one had not noticed her. That was the path of love, in that house. Not to exist was the path of being loved.

The father, in that house, was a figure of passage, a man who worked from sun to sun and who came home exhausted, with neither hands nor soul for more children than the ones he could barely greet, and Plácida, of him, kept even less than of the mother: she kept a tired man who, at supper, counted the children at the table like one who checks an account, and who noticed above all those who were missing or those who gave trouble, and from whom Plácida, always present and always without trouble, escaped as she escaped everyone, too light to be caught in the tired eye of a father. There was a supper, when she was little, when the father, counting the children, erred in the count and forgot her, and it had to be a brother who reminded him, Plácida is missing, father, and the father said ah, yes, Plácida, and counted her, and went on, and the little girl, in her place, felt in that forgetting of the father not a sharp grief, but a lukewarm confirmation of what she already knew: that she was the one one forgot to count, the one who did not make a number, the one who was always there and so was not seen. And instead of that revolting her, as it would revolt another child, that refined her art still more, because the little girl concluded, with the crooked logic of one who learns early to survive the lack of love, that if not even the father counted her, then the way was to go on giving no cause, not to weigh, to deserve by her quietness the little of the attention that was left over, instead of disputing it with a noise that she, the smallest, would never win.

She learned early to serve, too, because to serve was the active form of not weighing: instead of only giving no trouble, to give less trouble to others, to relieve them, to make herself useful. At seven, at eight years old, Plácida was already the one who helped the mother, the one who took up the smaller ones, the one who set and cleared the table, the one who offered for what no one wanted to do, and the house grew used to that, and the village, when there were gatherings, grew used to that, and the little Plácida became, before ten years old, the little helpful one, the one one orders about, the one who serves, and gained in that an identity, the only one she had: not that of a girl with wishes and tastes and needs, but that of a small and reliable usefulness that went about the house relieving the others. And when, years later, a grown woman, Plácida served the festivals of the village and sat at the ends, she was only continuing, on a large scale, what she had learned on a small one in that full house: that her place in the world was at the service of the others' place, and that love, for her, was won not by occupying space, but by freeing it.

And Plácida took up that path with the determination of a child who has found, at last, the key to the safe, and she walked it her whole life. She became the champion of giving no trouble, and she went on improving at it, year after year, to perfection, without ever realising that the prize of that championship was terrible: the prize was to disappear. The better she played, the less she existed. Each time she did not ask, did not weigh, was not noticed, she won a little bit of the love-for-not-bothering that the mother had taught her, and lost a little bit of existence, and at the end of sixty-six years she had won the whole championship and lost the whole of life, she had become so good at giving no trouble that she was almost no longer there, and no one noticed her, and that, which in the mouth of the exhausted mother had been the greatest of compliments, was, seen the whole of a life at once, the deepest of sadnesses.

There was a thing, in the full house, that Plácida only much later knew how to name, and that was the reverse of the scarcity of attention: the abundance of comparison. In a house of many children, the children are always compared one to another, measured one by another, and Plácida had been measured, her whole life, by her virtue of giving no trouble, always by contrast with the siblings who gave it. She was the example. See if you do as your sister Plácida, who does not complain, they said to the others, and the others hated her a little for it, for being the example, and Plácida, who wanted to be loved by the siblings as she wanted to be seen by the mother, realised that her virtue, besides effacing her in the eyes of the mother, drew her away from the siblings, who saw her as the goody, the little saint, the one who made them look bad. And so the art of giving no trouble, which she had chosen to be loved, left her doubly alone: invisible to the mother, who did not notice her for her not weighing, and disagreeable to the siblings, who saw her as the ruler by which they were measured and found wanting. She won the praise of the adults and lost the affection of her equals, and that trade, which she had made as a child without realising it, was a bad one, because the praise of the adults is distant and the affection of equals is warm, and she had traded the warm for the distant, and had been left cold, a cold that accompanied her her whole life and of which she, faithful to herself, never complained.

The mother had died long ago, and had died, it was said in the family, calling for the children who had given her the most trouble, for those who had shouted, for those who had weighed, and not for Plácida, and the family thought that natural and even told it as a joke, the mother did not even remember to call for Plácida, poor thing, who was the best of all.

There had been, in childhood, a brother who was the reverse of Plácida, and to whom she owed, without ever having told him, part of who she became. He was called Aurélio, was one of the middle ones, and was the one who made the most noise: the one who shouted, the one who got angry, the one who fell ill with a clamour, the one who broke things, the one who ran away from home and came back, the one who gave the mother sleepless nights and days of distress. The family called him a wretch, a cross, that boy who is the death of his mother, and Plácida, a child, heard that and saw, with the eyes that see everything in silent children, a thing no one else saw: that it was Aurélio, the one who gave trouble, the one who weighed, who had all the mother's attention. The mother exhausted herself with Aurélio, scolded Aurélio, wept on account of Aurélio, and in all that distress there was a thing that Plácida did not have and that she envied without daring to name: there was presence. The mother was with Aurélio in a whole way, though distressed, in which she was never with Plácida, because Plácida gave no cause. And the little girl learned, watching the brother, the perverse lesson that was going to govern her whole life: that one who weighs is carried, and one who is carried is touched, and one who is touched exists; and that she, in choosing not to weigh, had chosen not to be carried, not to be touched, not to exist. But it was late to change strategy, and besides her strategy yielded its little, yielded the praise, yielded the quietness, and Aurélio, with all the trouble he gave, was unhappy, and Plácida did not want to be unhappy like Aurélio; she wanted only, in a deep corner she covered, to be touched once as he was, even if by scolding.

Aurélio had died young, of a troubled life, and the mother had wept for him years, and Plácida had realised, without bitterness but with a sad clarity, that the brother who had given all the trouble had stayed in the mother's heart much deeper than she, who had never given any. It was the confirmation of everything: that one who weighs is engraved, and one who relieves is forgotten, and that she had chosen, as a child, without knowing it, the wrong side of that law, the side of one who is loved with the mouth and forgotten with the heart.

And no one, in telling the joke of the mother calling for the others and not for her, realised the cruelty it had within it: that the mother had called, at her death, for the children who had made her exist as a mother, for those who had needed her, for those she had carried, because it is those who weigh who stay in our hearts, it is those who need who make us needed, and Plácida, who had never weighed, who had never needed, who had never given trouble, had not stayed in the mother's heart at the hour of dying, because a person one does not notice in life is also a person one does not notice at death. The compliment had been fulfilled to the end. Not even at death did the mother notice her.

The marriage to Brando, years later, did not break the pattern: it continued it, with another face. Brando was a meek man, of the fields, of few words, and the village said that the two had chosen well, two quiet ones, two people who gave no one any trouble, and it was true, but it was a sad truth, because what had united them had been, at bottom, the same thing that effaced each of them: the art of not weighing. Plácida married as she had done everything, without demanding anything, without asking anything, content with what there was, and she was a good wife to Brando in the sense that she never gave him trouble, and Brando was a good husband in the sense that he never gave her trouble, and they lived thirty years so, two not weighing on one another, two not asking, and it was a good life and it was a life in which a thing was always missing that neither of them knew how to name: the weight was missing, the asking was missing, that way of two people carrying one another that is, after all, what makes of a marriage a deep thing and not only a delicate coexistence. Brando, at the end, ill, was the only one who came near to teaching her the contrary, because the illness obliged him, at last, to ask, to need, and Plácida cared for him with a giving that surprised even herself, and discovered, caring for the dying husband, that she adored being needed, that there was in her an enormous hunger to be needed that a whole life of not-bothering had never satisfied. But Brando died, and took with him the only person who, at the end, had come to need her truly, and Plácida was left again alone with her art, more alone than ever, because now she already knew, after caring for Brando, the pleasure it had been to be needed, and she had no one to be it for.

And Plácida, a grown woman, on hearing the joke of the family, had laughed with the others, because to laugh was to give no trouble, was not to weigh with her own grief in a merry conversation. But within, deep, in a place where she never went, the whole thing had stayed with her: that she had spent her childhood winning the love of a mother by not needing her, and that she had won it, and that the price had been not to have stayed, even, in the last memory of that mother, because one who does not ask does not stay, and one who does not weigh is not engraved, and one who wins the championship of not existing receives, in the end, the exact prize for which they competed: that of not having existed for anyone.
