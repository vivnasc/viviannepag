## 7. The championship of not existing

There are children's questions that stay, and Bento's — whom do you ask, grandmother? — stayed in Plácida and opened, over the weeks of the convalescence, a door backward, to the bottom, to the place where the whole thing had begun, and Plácida found herself, that autumn, doing a thing she had never done: looking at her own childhood face on, asking herself where that art of not existing had come to her from, and finding, at the bottom of memory, the day she had learned it.

It must be said, because it is the heart of everything, that Plácida never blamed the mother, and that she was right not to blame her, and that it was exactly that which made her wound so hard to see and to cure. The mother had not been bad. The mother had been an exhausted woman, with more children than hands, doing what she could with the little she had, and the compliment she had given Plácida — this one gives no trouble — had not been an act of cruelty, it had been a sigh of relief, the genuine relief of a drowning woman for having, in the midst of so many who pulled her down, one who did not pull. The mother had loved Plácida. She had only loved her badly, without knowing it, in the only way a woman so tired could love a daughter so convenient: by thanking her for the convenience, and therefore asking her, without knowing she asked, to go on being convenient, to go on not weighing, to go on not existing. And a child, Plácida realised, does whatever is needed for the mother's love, even to disappear, above all to disappear, if that is what the mother, in her weariness, thanks them for. Plácida's wound had no one to blame. It had only a tired mother and a daughter who loved her too much, and from those two innocent things had been born a whole life of effacement. And that is the hardest thing to cure: the wounds that have no one to blame, those that were born of love and not of malice, because malice we can oppose, and the love that wounded us we can only forgive and, slowly, disobey.

It had not been a bad day. That was the hardest thing to accept: that the thing that had ruined her life had not been born of a cruelty, of an abuse, of a noisy wound that one could point to and accuse. It had been born of a compliment. It had been born of the love of a tired mother for a daughter who did not weigh. And it is harder to cure a wound that was born of a compliment than one that was born of a blow, because the blow we can call a blow, and the compliment we call, our whole life, love, and one does not distrust what one calls love.

Plácida reconstructed the day, piece by piece, on those nights of convalescence, and told it, in bits, to Bento, turned into a story, because to Bento she could tell things she could not say to an adult, and Bento heard the grandmother's stories with the seriousness with which children hear what matters.

"There was a little girl," Plácida told the grandson, at the edge of his bed, in the dark, "in a very full house, with many siblings, and a tired mother who had no hands for all. And all the siblings made noise for the mother to look at them: they wept, shouted, broke things, fell ill. And the little girl, who was the smallest, realised a thing. She realised she could not make more noise than the siblings, who were bigger. So she did the contrary. She made herself the quietest of all, the one who gave no trouble at all. And do you know what happened?"

"The mother scolded her?" asked Bento.

"No, my treasure. It was worse. The mother praised her. She said, in front of the visitors, this one gives me no trouble at all, one does not even notice her, would that they were all like that. And the little girl, on hearing that, felt herself the most loved of all, because she was the only one the mother praised. And from that day, the little girl devoted her whole life to deserving that compliment. To giving no trouble. To not weighing. To not being noticed. And she won. She won the compliment a thousand times, her whole life. But there was a price, Bento, that the little girl only discovered when she was already an old woman." Plácida fell silent a moment, in the dark. "The price was this: that by dint of not being noticed, no one noticed her. That by dint of not weighing, she stayed nowhere. The little girl won the championship of giving no trouble, my treasure. And the prize of that championship is to disappear."

There was more, in the root-scene, that Plácida only that autumn dared to look at face on, and that she did not tell Bento because it was too much for a child, but that she told herself, lying in the dark after the grandson fell asleep. There was the day she had realised that the championship had a cost, and she had realised it as a child, and had chosen to pay it all the same. It was a day when she, small, fell ill, a child's illness, fever, and when, faithful to her part, she did not complain, did not call, gave no trouble, and stayed in her corner, quiet, waiting for someone to notice. And no one noticed. The full house went on with its day, the exhausted mother attended to those who made noise, and the little Plácida stayed the whole day with fever, alone, in her corner, without anyone noticing her, because she had given no trouble, because she had not called. And at the end of the day, when the mother finally noticed that the little one was hot, instead of scolding her for not having said, she praised her: ah this girl, so long-suffering, she did not even complain, what a little saint. And the little Plácida, with fever, received the compliment and realised, at that moment, the cruel rule of the game she was playing: that to be praised for giving no trouble, she had to go on giving no trouble even when she needed, even ill, even suffering; that the price of the compliment was the silent suffering; and that she was willing to pay it, because the compliment, the being a little saint, the being the sufferer who does not even complain, was the only form of love that house gave her, and a child pays any price for the only form of love it is given. It was on that day, with fever, in her corner, praised for suffering in silence, that the championship was sealed for ever, and that Plácida learned the lesson that nearly killed her sixty years later, alone, with another fever, in another corner, still waiting, without knowing it, for the old compliment: what a little saint, she did not even complain, did not even give trouble, one did not even notice her.

And Bento, in the dark, was silent a while, digesting the story in his way, and then said, with the clear-sightedness he had:

"But grandmother. That little girl is you."

"It is I, my treasure."

"And are you still in time to lose the championship?"

And Plácida laughed, in the dark, a wet laugh, because the grandson, without knowing it, had put the thing exactly as it was: that to save herself, she had to lose. That the thing she had been a champion of her whole life was the thing she had to give up. That her salvation lay in doing badly what she had always done well: in giving trouble, in weighing, in needing, in existing. And that that, at sixty-six years old, with a whole life of training in the contrary direction, was the hardest thing that would ever be asked of her.

Before falling asleep, that night, Bento asked the grandmother one more question that dismantled her, of those only children ask because only they do not yet know that there are questions that hurt. He asked: grandmother, and your mother, my great-grandmother, did she care more for the siblings who made noise than for you? And Plácida, in the dark, had to think a long time before answering, because the true answer was complicated and hard, and she did not want to give it wrong to a boy. And she said, at last, slowly: it is not that she cared more, my treasure. It is that she noticed more. There is a difference. My mother cared for me, surely she cared. But she noticed the others, because the others made themselves noticed, and me she did not notice, because I made a point of not making myself noticed. And a person, Bento, needs both things: needs to be cared for and needs to be noticed. To be cared for is not enough, if one is not noticed. I was cared for and not noticed, and I spent my life thinking that it was enough to be cared for, and it is not, my treasure, it is not. The noticing is needed. It was that which was missing my whole life: not the love, for that I had, but the noticing. And Bento, in the dark, processed that in his way, and said, with a simplicity that settled the whole thing: then it is for that that I ask you for things, grandmother. When I ask you for a thing, it is because I noticed you. To ask is to notice. And Plácida was left speechless, because the grandson, without knowing it, had given her the key to everything: that to ask is to notice, that when one asks of someone one is telling them I notice you, I know that you exist, I count on you, and that she, by never asking anyone, had never told anyone I notice you, and so no one had told her back, and the whole village cared for her and no one noticed her, because she had never taught anyone to notice, by never asking.

"I do not know, my treasure," she said to him, at last. "I am old. Championships, when they are won sixty years ago, are very hard to lose. But I am going to try. Look, I promise you I am going to try. And you will help me, yes? You, who know how to ask so well, will teach the grandmother."

There was, in that conversation of the championship, a moment when Bento did a thing Plácida never forgot, a thing of a child and so wiser than everything. He got up from bed, went to fetch a sheet and a pencil, and said, grandmother, let us do it the other way round. Your whole life you won points for giving no trouble, did you not? Well now let us count the other way round. Let us make a sheet on which you win points when you ask, when you give trouble, when you need. And each time you ask for a thing, you mark a point. And let us see whether you can win this new championship, that of asking, as you won the old one, that of not asking. And he drew, on the sheet, in his crooked hand, a title: CHAMPIONSHIP OF ASKING, and made a grid of points, and hung it on the wall of the grandmother's room, and it was the most moving and most comical thing of that year, a grid of points on the wall of a woman of sixty-six years, made by a grandson of eight, to help her win the championship contrary to the one that had ruined her life.

And Plácida, who her whole life had played the wrong championship with the determination of a champion, turned all that determination, with the grandson's help, toward the right championship, and there was in that a poetic justice that she savoured: that the same stubbornness that had made her a champion of not existing, turned inside out, could make her a champion of existing, and that she did not need to change her nature, only to change her goal, to put all her strength, the strength she had put into disappearing, at the service of appearing. And she won points, now, and Bento marked them on the grid, and the grid went on filling up, and Plácida, on looking at it on the wall, on seeing the points accumulating, felt the same pride she had felt as a child on being praised for giving no trouble, but it was a new pride, sound, the pride of one who learns to exist, and not of one who strives to disappear.

And Bento, all serious with the importance of the mission, promised that he would teach her, and it was so, at the edge of a child's bed, in the dark of a room, that the most important alliance of Plácida's life was sealed: that of an old champion of not existing and a boy of eight who knew how to ask, united in the enterprise of making her, at the end of a life, begin to be.

But that night, after Bento fell asleep, Plácida stayed still a long time awake, and the thing that kept her awake was not the hope of the alliance with the grandson: it was a deeper sadness, the sadness of one who adds up, at last, what the championship had cost. She thought of the mother, who had praised her and lost her, who had loved her for her not weighing and forgotten her at death for her never having weighed. She thought of Aurélio, the noisy brother, who had given all the trouble and had stayed in the mother's heart. She thought of Brando, the meek husband, with whom she had lived thirty years of mutual not-bothering, and of the deep life the two of them had not had for never having weighed on one another. She thought of the friends, who forgot her from the lists. She thought of the festival, of the stool in the corner, of the place she never put. And she thought of the daughter, of Belmira, and it was there that the sadness most tightened, because she realised that she was doing to the daughter what the mother had done to her, in reverse: the mother had taught her not to weigh, and she, Plácida, by never weighing on the daughter, by never asking her for anything, was making Belmira feel useless, superfluous, not-needed, and was, without meaning to, passing on to her, by another way, the same solitude. The wound went on three generations: the mother who had had no hands for all; she, who had learned to need no hands at all; and now the daughter, who had grown up with a mother who had never let her care for her, and who perhaps, in her turn, no longer knew how to let herself be cared for by Bento, when Bento grew up. Three generations of women refusing, each in her way, the weight of one another. And Plácida realised, that night, that if she wanted to break that, it was not enough to learn to ask of the village, of the friends, of the world. She had to learn to ask of the daughter. She had to let Belmira, at last, in, need, care. She had to give her, before dying, what her own mother had never given her: the chance to be needed by a mother. And that, Plácida knew in the dark, was going to be the hardest of all the things she had left to do, because it was the deepest, and because the fear of weighing on the daughter was the oldest and the most ingrained of all her fears.
