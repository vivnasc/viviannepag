## 7. The championship of not existing

There are children's questions that stay, and Bento's, whom do you ask, grandmother?, stayed in Plácida and opened, over the weeks of the convalescence, a door backward, to the bottom, to the place where the whole thing had begun, and Plácida found herself, that autumn, doing a thing she had never done: looking at her own childhood face on, and telling it, in bits, to the grandson, at the edge of his bed, in the dark, turned into a story, because to Bento she could tell things she could not say to an adult.

"There was a little girl," Plácida told the grandson, sitting on the edge of the bed, with her hand on his forehead, "in a very full house, with many siblings, and a tired mother who had no hands for all. And all the siblings made noise for the mother to look at them: they wept, shouted, broke things, fell ill. And the little girl, who was the smallest, realised a thing. She realised she could not make more noise than the siblings, who were bigger. So she did the contrary. She made herself the quietest of all, the one who gave no trouble at all. And do you know what happened?"

"The mother scolded her?" asked Bento, from the dark.

"No, my treasure. It was worse. The mother praised her. She said, in front of the visitors, this one gives me no trouble at all, one does not even notice her, would that they were all like that. And the little girl, on hearing that, felt herself the most loved of all, because she was the only one the mother praised. And from that day, the little girl devoted her whole life to deserving that compliment. To giving no trouble. To not weighing. To not being noticed. And she won. She won the compliment a thousand times, her whole life. But there was a price, Bento, that the little girl only discovered when she was already an old woman." Plácida fell silent a moment, in the dark. "The price was this: that by dint of not being noticed, no one noticed her. That by dint of not weighing, she stayed nowhere. The little girl won the championship of giving no trouble, my treasure. And the prize of that championship is to disappear."

There was more, in the root-scene, that Plácida only that autumn dared to look at face on, and that she did not tell Bento because it was too much for a child, but that she told herself, lying in the dark after the grandson fell asleep. She remembered a day when she, small, fell ill, a child's illness, fever, and when, faithful to her part, she fell silent, and stayed in her corner, quiet, shrunk in a blanket, waiting for someone to notice. And the full house went on with its day, the mother attending to those who made noise, and the little Plácida stayed the whole day with fever, alone, in her corner, without anyone noticing her. And at the end of the day, when the mother finally put a hand to her forehead and found her burning, instead of scolding her for not having said, she turned to the neighbour who was in the kitchen and said, with a half-smile of pride:

"Look at this girl. The whole afternoon with fever and not a peep. So long-suffering, poor little thing. What a little saint."

And the little Plácida, with fever, received the compliment and realised, at that moment, the cruel rule of the game she was playing: that to be praised for giving no trouble, she had to go on giving no trouble even when she needed, even ill, even suffering; that the price of the compliment was the silent suffering; and that she was willing to pay it, because the being a little saint, the being the sufferer who does not even complain, was the only form of love that house gave her. It was on that day, with fever, in her corner, praised for suffering in silence, that the championship was sealed for ever, and that Plácida learned the lesson that nearly killed her sixty years later, alone, with another fever, in another corner, still waiting, without knowing it, for the old compliment.

And Bento, in the dark, was silent a while, digesting the story in his way, and then said, with the clear-sightedness he had:

"But grandmother. That little girl is you."

"It is I, my treasure."

"And are you still in time to lose the championship?"

And Plácida laughed, in the dark, a wet laugh, because the grandson, without knowing it, had put the thing exactly as it was: that to save herself, she had to lose. That the thing she had been a champion of her whole life was the thing she had to give up.

"And why did no one warn her?" asked Bento, from the darkness, with the blanket up to his chin. "The little girl. Why did no one tell her, oh little girl, do not play that game, that game is bad?"

"Because no one saw that she played it, my treasure. That is the spell of the game. Whoever plays it well becomes invisible, and what is invisible is not warned." Plácida was a moment silent, smoothing the blanket over the grandson's chest. "And because everyone, around her, gained by her playing it. The mother rested, the others had more attention for themselves, the visitors praised her and went away satisfied. Her game suited everyone. The games that suit everyone, Bento, are the hardest to give up, because everyone, without knowing it, holds our hand to keep us playing them."

"I saw it," said Bento, serious. "I see you. When you sit at the end and say that it is all well, I see that it is not."

And Plácida was left speechless, in the dark, because it was true, and it was the grandson, and only the grandson, and she had to breathe deep so as not to weep over the boy.

Before falling asleep, that night, Bento asked the grandmother one more question that dismantled her, of those only children ask because only they do not yet know that there are questions that hurt:

"Grandmother, and your mother, my great-grandmother, did she care more for the siblings who made noise than for you?"

And Plácida, in the dark, had to think a long time before answering, because the true answer was complicated and hard, and she did not want to give it wrong to a boy.

"It is not that she cared more, my treasure," she said, at last, slowly. "It is that she noticed more. There is a difference. My mother cared for me, surely she cared. But she noticed the others, because the others made themselves noticed, and me she did not notice, because I made a point of not making myself noticed. And a person, Bento, needs both things: needs to be cared for and needs to be noticed. To be cared for is not enough, if one is not noticed. I was cared for and not noticed, and I spent my life thinking that it was enough to be cared for, and it is not, my treasure, it is not. The noticing is needed."

And Bento, in the dark, processed that in his way, and said, with a simplicity that settled the whole thing:

"Then it is for that that I ask you for things, grandmother. When I ask you for a thing, it is because I noticed you. To ask is to notice."

And Plácida was left speechless, because the grandson, without knowing it, had given her the key to everything: that to ask is to notice, that when one asks of someone one is telling them I notice you, I know that you exist, I count on you, and that she, by never asking anyone, had never told anyone I notice you, and so no one had told her back.

"I do not know if I am still in time, my treasure," she said to him, at last, tucking in his blanket. "I am old. Championships, when they are won sixty years ago, are very hard to lose. But I am going to try. Look, I promise you I am going to try. And you will help me, yes? You, who know how to ask so well, will teach the grandmother."

And Bento, instead of saying yes and falling asleep, did a thing of a child and so wiser than everything. He leapt from the bed, barefoot, went to the kitchen to fetch a sheet of paper and the pencil for the accounts, came back, and knelt on the bed with the sheet propped on his knee.

"Grandmother, let us do it the other way round. Your whole life you won points for giving no trouble, did you not? Well now let us count the other way round. Each time you ask for a thing, you mark a point. And let us see whether you win this new championship, that of asking."

And he drew, on the sheet, in the big crooked hand of eight years, a title, CHAMPIONSHIP OF ASKING, and made a grid of little squares beneath, and the next morning hung it with a tack on the wall of the grandmother's room, beside the crucifix.

"Now," said Bento, hands on his hips, looking at the grid on the wall, "the first point is missing. Ask me for a thing, grandmother. Now. In earnest."

And Plácida stayed still, with the grandson before her, and discovered that she did not know how. That she was sixty-six years old and did not know how to ask a thing of a child, which was the easiest thing in the world. She opened her mouth and shut it. And at last, with an effort that coloured her cheeks, she said, low:

"Bento... will you keep me company today, while I do the dishes? Not to help. Only to be beside me, for I like it."

And Bento opened a smile from ear to ear, and ran to the grid, and painted the first little square with all the strength of the pencil, and said:

"Point! First point, grandmother. You are winning."

And he stayed there keeping her company the whole afternoon, leaning against the counter, talking of everything and nothing, while she washed the dishes, and Plácida washed them with her eyes burning, because that first little square painted on the wall was, seen the whole of a life at once, the first thing she had asked for herself that was not a work nor a service, but company, presence, liking. And there was in that a poetic justice that she savoured: that the same stubbornness that had made her a champion of not existing, turned inside out, could make her a champion of existing, and that she did not need to change her nature, only to change her goal.

But that night, after Bento fell asleep for good, Plácida stayed a long time awake, and the thing that kept her awake was not the hope of the alliance with the grandson: it was a deeper sadness, that of one who adds up, at last, what the championship had cost. She thought of the mother, who had praised her and lost her. She thought of Aurélio, the noisy brother, who had given all the trouble and had stayed in the mother's heart. She thought of Brando, the meek husband, and of the deep life the two of them had not had for never having weighed on one another. And she thought of the daughter, of Belmira, and it was there that the sadness most tightened, because she realised that she was doing to the daughter what the mother had done to her, in reverse: the mother had taught her not to weigh, and she, Plácida, by never weighing on the daughter, by never asking her for anything, was making Belmira feel useless, superfluous, not-needed, and was, without meaning to, passing on to her the same solitude. The wound went on three generations: the mother who had had no hands for all; she, who had learned to need no hands at all; and now the daughter, who had grown up with a mother who had never let her care for her. And Plácida realised, that night, that if she wanted to break that, it was not enough to learn to ask of the village, of the friends, of the world. She had to learn to ask of the daughter. She had to let Belmira, at last, in, need, care. She had to give her, before dying, what her own mother had never given her: the chance to be needed by a mother. And that, Plácida knew in the dark, was going to be the hardest of all the things she had left to do.
