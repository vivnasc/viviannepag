## 4. The place that was not set

The great festival of the summer, that of the saint of the village, was the largest of all, the one that set up the long table in the churchyard, the table of planks on trestles that stretched from one end of the churchyard to the other and at which the whole village sat, and it was, at that table, that one saw better than anywhere else who was who in Véspera, because the long table had an order, and the order was that of each one's place in the village.

There were no marked places, officially. But there was the custom, which is stronger than any mark: in the middle of the table, in the wide part, sat the families, the couples, the children, the full life of the village; and at the ends, in the corners, sat those who served, those who got up at every moment to fetch, those who did not quite have a place of their own because their place was to be at the service of the others' place. And Plácida, every year, without anyone telling her, went to the end, because the end was her place, the place of one who serves, and she went there with the naturalness of one who comes home.

That year, she helped set up the table, as always. She was among the first to arrive at the churchyard, in the morning, to spread the cloths, to arrange the benches, to count the places, and it was she, with other women, who put the plates, one by one, along the long table, counting the families, this one for those of the Olive-Press, these for Felizarda's house, these for the Serranos who come down for the festival, and it was so, putting the places of the others, that the thing happened, and the thing was of a cruelty so without malice that only life knows how to make it: in counting the places and putting them, Plácida did not put hers. Not on purpose. Simply, in counting the village, she did not count herself, because she, in her head, was not one of the places of the table: she was the one who put the places of the table. She put the whole world at the table and forgot to put herself, as she had forgotten her whole life, and none of the other women noticed, because they too, for sixty years, had Plácida not as one of those who sit, but as one of those who serve, and one who serves needs no place.

The festival filled up. The village sat at the long table, the families in the middle, the full life, and Plácida went from one side to the other serving the beginning, and when the time came for her too to sit, she went to look for her place and there was none. The table was full, all the places occupied by the families she herself had seated there, and there was no room left over, because she had not put herself room. And Plácida, instead of doing the simple thing, instead of saying aloud my place is missing, someone bring me a plate, did what she always did, did what sixty-six years of championship had taught her: she managed. She went to fetch a stool, of those from the storeroom, and put it in the corner, at the end of everything, almost off the table, and went to fetch a plate from the kitchen, and sat there, on her improvised stool, at the edge of the festival, eating of what was left over, and she did it with such naturalness, with such skill, that no one noticed that Plácida was eating on a storeroom stool at the edge of the table because the table, which she had set, had no place for her.

It must be said, in fairness to the village, that no one did that out of ill will, and that it was that, precisely, which made the thing so deep. If someone had, on purpose, denied a place to Plácida, there would be anger, there would be amends, there would be at least the acknowledgement that Plácida was someone to whom a place could be denied, and to deny a place to someone is still to acknowledge that that someone should have had it. But no one denied anything to Plácida. They simply did not count her, and they did not count her because she, for sixty-six years, had trained herself not to be counted, to be the one who manages, the one who fits in any corner, the one who need not be put on the list because she appears and serves and disappears. The village did not take Plácida's place. The village learned from Plácida, over a lifetime, that Plácida had no place, and limited itself to treating her as she treated herself. It was the pupil repeating, faithfully, the lesson the mistress had given her. And there is nothing harder to fight than this, Plácida realised on her stool: not the malice of others, which one faces, but the fidelity of others to the image we ourselves gave them of us. The village saw her exactly as she had taught herself to be seen. To change the village, she would first have to change herself.

No one, except one.

Bento, the grandson, from the middle of the table, where he sat with his mother, with Belmira, in the full life of the village, noticed. The eyes of children go to what is wrong as those of birds go to brightness, and Bento saw the grandmother, alone, on a stool, in the corner, off the table, eating of what was left over, while he, her grandson, was in the middle, in a set place, with plate and glass and everything. And Bento, who was eight years old and had not yet learned that there are things one does not ask, got up from his place, and went along, down the long table, to the grandmother's stool, and asked her, and asked it aloud, with the clear voice of children, loud enough for the part of the table nearby to fall silent and listen:

"Grandmother, why are you there on that stool? Why do you not have a place at the table, like everyone?"

And there fell a silence, in that stretch of the long table, a silence of those who were caught in a thing they had not seen, and several faces turned, and saw what they had not seen the whole festival: Plácida, on a stool, in the corner, with no place. And Plácida, with the whole village of that stretch looking, with the grandson before her waiting for an answer, felt a thing she had never felt at a festival: she felt herself seen. And it was so strange, so unaccustomed, to be seen, that she did not know what to do with it, and she did what she always did, she set about taking away the bother, putting everyone at ease, not weighing even with her own non-place:

"I am very well here, my treasure," she said, with a smile, aloud too, for the whole table to hear and be at ease. "Here in the corner I am more at hand to serve, and I like it, for I like to serve. Go on, go to your place, the soup is getting cold."

Bento, however, did not laugh, and did not go back to his place at once. He stayed there, beside the grandmother's stool, with a serious face, and said, louder still, with the stubbornness of children who feel an injustice and do not let it go: but it is not fair, grandmother. You set the table. I saw you. You put the places of everyone, in the morning, and then you have no place. Whoever sets the table should have a place at the table. And that phrase of a child, said at the long table, stayed in the air with a truth that disarmed the adults for an instant, because it was simple and it was right and no one had an answer for it: whoever sets the table should have a place at the table. There was an uncomfortable silence, and Belmira, from the middle of the table, flushed, and got up to resolve it, come beside me, mother, come here, we will make room, and there was a shuffling of benches, a squeezing to open space, and Plácida, now worse than before, because what she most feared was exactly that, to give trouble, to make the table shuffle on her account, raised her hands to appease, it is not needed, do not trouble yourselves, I am well here, truly, and her distress at giving trouble was so great that the table, to spare her, let her stay on the stool, and the moment passed, and the full life resumed, and Plácida stayed in the corner with her heart leaping, not for the humiliation of the stool, but for the horror of having, for an instant, made the table stop on her account.

And it was that the part she took home and that most made her think: that when the table offered to open her a place, she refused. That they had, at last, offered her what she had not had, a place in the middle, and that she, out of habit, out of dread of bothering, had refused, and gone back to the stool of her own will. No one forced her to stay in the corner in that second moment: she chose the corner, when they offered her the middle, because to accept the middle gave the table trouble and to stay in the corner did not. And Plácida realised that her fate was not only that the village did not set her a place: it was that she refused it when they set it for her. That even if they offered her the middle, she would flee to the end, because the end did not weigh and the middle weighed, and that to change it was not enough that the village see her: it was she who had to let herself be put in the middle, to bear the trouble that her place gave the others, to tolerate the shuffling of benches made on her account. And that, she realised, was the hardest part, because it did not depend on the village, it depended on her, on her old dread of making the table stop.

Bento, unconvinced, went back at last to his place looking behind him, at the grandmother on the stool, with the same serious face with which he had seen her leave the other festival, the face of one who realises a thing is wrong and does not yet have words to say it. And Plácida stayed on her stool, in the corner, eating of what was left over, with the full long table before her, and on the outside everything was well, it was resolved, she had put everyone at ease as always. But within, deep, in that place where she never went and where now, since the previous festival, a new light beat, Plácida heard the grandson's question echoing, and the question was simple and was the question of her whole life: why do you not have a place at the table, like everyone?

Belmira, the daughter, was the only adult who noticed, though late and though badly. At the end of the festival, when she helped the mother gather the cloths — because Belmira helped, she had learned from the mother to help, and was also she, in her younger way, one who served —, she asked her, with a touch of distress: mother, is it true that there was no place for you? And Plácida, of course, set about reassuring her, that it was Bento's fancy, that she was very well on the stool, that she liked to stay at hand to serve. And Belmira accepted the excuse, because it was comfortable to accept it, because her whole life they had taught her that the mother was always well, that the mother needed nothing, that the mother was the one who tended to others, and it is hard for a child to suddenly see a frailty in a mother who built herself whole around having none. But it stayed with her, Belmira, a splinter, a small disquiet, the image of the mother on the stool in the corner, and that splinter was going to grow, over that year, until the conversation the two of them would have, much later, and that would be the deepest of their lives.

Because there was, between Plácida and Belmira, a distance that neither of them knew how to name, and that came exactly from here, from the not-asking. Belmira had grown up with a mother who never asked her for anything, who resolved everything alone, who never showed her a weakness, a doubt, a need, and Belmira had interpreted that, over her life, in the only way a child can interpret a mother who never needs them: as distance, as self-sufficiency, as an I-do-not-need-you. Belmira loved the mother and felt, beside her, vaguely useless, superfluous, because a mother who never asks for anything is a mother who does not give the child the chance to love her back, to care for her, to be needed by her. And Belmira, without knowing it, resented the mother for that, for never having let her in, for being so complete in her not-needing that there was no place left over, for the daughter, where to be a daughter. Bento, the grandson, had been the only one to break through that wall, because Bento was a child and children ask without modesty and do not accept the it-is-not-needed, and so Bento had with the grandmother an intimacy that Belmira herself had never had: the intimacy of one who asks and is served, which is, after all, the only door into a person like Plácida.

And the answer, which she did not give the grandson because to a grandson one does not give such answers, she gave herself, that night, and it was the hardest answer she had ever given herself: because I never put my place. My whole life, at every table, I put the place of everyone and forgot mine, and then, when there was no place for me, instead of asking for it, I got a stool in the corner and said I liked to be there. No one took my place at the table, Plácida realised, with the stool still warm beneath her. It was I who never put it. And no one is going to put it for me, because no one puts the place of one who never asks for it. If I want one day to sit at the table, in the middle, in the full life, with the families, I am going to have to do the hardest thing there is, the thing I never did in sixty-six years: I am going to have to ask for it.
