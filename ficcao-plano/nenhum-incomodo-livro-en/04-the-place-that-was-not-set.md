## 4. The place that was not set

The great festival of the summer, that of the saint of the village, was the largest of all, the one that set up the long table in the churchyard, the table of planks on trestles that stretched from one end to the other and at which the whole village sat, and it was, at that table, that one saw better than anywhere else who was who in Véspera, because the long table had an order, and the order was that of each one's place in the village.

There were no marked places, officially. But there was the custom, which is stronger than any mark: in the middle of the table, in the wide part, sat the families, the couples, the children, the full life of the village; and at the ends, in the corners, sat those who served, those who did not quite have a place of their own because their place was to be at the service of the others' place. And Plácida, every year, without anyone telling her, went to the end, with the naturalness of one who comes home.

That year, she helped set up the table, as always. She was among the first to arrive at the churchyard, in the morning, to spread the cloths, and it was she, with other women, who put the plates, one by one, along the long table, counting the families aloud with Felizarda at her side.

"These for those of the Olive-Press," Plácida went on saying, setting down the plates. "These for your house, Felizarda, for you are seven with the grandchildren. These for the Serranos, who come down for the festival. These for Belmira and Bento."

And it was so, putting the places of the others, counting the village house by house, that the thing happened, and the thing was of a cruelty so without malice that only life knows how to make it: in counting the places and putting them, Plácida did not put hers. Not on purpose. Simply, in counting the village, she did not count herself, because she, in her head, was not one of the places of the table: she was the one who put the places of the table. She put the whole world at the table and forgot to put herself, as she had forgotten her whole life, and none of the other women noticed, because they too, for sixty years, had Plácida not as one of those who sit, but as one of those who serve, and one who serves needs no place.

When the plates were all set, Felizarda passed her eyes over the long table, from one end to the other, checking, and said:

"Is it all there, Plácida? Count again, let no one be missing."

And Plácida counted, aloud, pointing with her chin, those of the Olive-Press, Felizarda's house, the Serranos, Belmira and Bento, and all the others, house by house, and on reaching the end she said:

"It is all there. No one is missing."

And Felizarda nodded and went to make herself ready, and neither of the two noticed that the count was right for the whole village and wrong by one only: by the one who counted. Plácida had put the whole world at the table and said, with her own mouth, no one is missing, and it was she who was missing, and not even she noticed, because she, in her head, did not enter the count of those who sit.

The festival filled up. The village sat at the long table, the families in the middle, and Plácida went from one side to the other serving the beginning, and when the time came for her too to sit, she went to look for her place and there was none. The table was full, all the places occupied by the families she herself had seated there, and there was no room left over, because she had not put herself room. And Plácida, instead of doing the simple thing, instead of saying aloud my place is missing, someone bring me a plate, did what she always did, did what sixty-six years of championship had taught her: she managed. She went to the lean-to to fetch a storeroom bench, of those of plank, and put it in the corner, at the end of everything, almost off the table, and went to the kitchen to fetch a plate, and sat there, on her improvised bench, at the edge of the festival, eating of what was left over, and she did it with such naturalness, with such skill, that no one noticed that Plácida was eating on a storeroom bench at the edge of the table because the table, which she had set, had no place for her.

No one noticed, except one.

Bento, from the middle of the table, where he sat with Belmira, in the full life of the village, noticed. The eyes of children go to what is wrong as those of birds go to brightness, and Bento saw the grandmother, alone, on a bench, in the corner, off the table, eating of what was left over, while he, her grandson, was in the middle, in a set place, with plate and glass and everything. And Bento, who was eight years old and had not yet learned that there are things one does not ask, got up from his place, and went along, down the long table, to the grandmother's bench, and asked her, and asked it aloud, with the clear voice of children, loud enough for the part of the table nearby to fall silent and listen:

"Grandmother, why are you there on that bench? Why do you not have a place at the table, like everyone?"

And there fell a silence, in that stretch of the long table, a silence of those who were caught in a thing they had not seen, and several faces turned, and saw what they had not seen the whole festival: Plácida, on a bench, in the corner, with no place. And Plácida, with the whole village of that stretch looking, with the grandson before her waiting for an answer, felt a thing she had never felt at a festival: she felt herself seen. And it was so strange, so unaccustomed, to be seen, that she did not know what to do with it, and she did what she always did, she set about taking away the bother, putting everyone at ease, not weighing even with her own non-place:

"I am very well here, my treasure," she said, with a smile, aloud too, for the whole table to hear and be at ease. "Here in the corner I am more at hand to serve, and I like it, for I like to serve. Go on, go to your place, the soup is getting cold."

Bento, however, did not laugh, and did not go back to his place at once. He stayed there, beside the grandmother's bench, with a serious face, and said, louder still, with the stubbornness of children who feel an injustice and do not let it go:

"But it is not fair, grandmother. You set the table. I saw you. You put the places of everyone, in the morning, and then you have no place. Whoever sets the table should have a place at the table."

And that phrase of a child, said at the long table, stayed in the air with a truth that disarmed the adults for an instant, because it was simple and it was right and no one had an answer for it. There was an uncomfortable silence, and Belmira, from the middle of the table, flushed, and got up to resolve it:

"Come beside me, mother. Come here, we will make room."

And there was a shuffling of benches, a squeezing to open space, and Plácida, now worse than before, because what she most feared was exactly that, to give trouble, to make the table shuffle on her account, raised her hands to appease:

"It is not needed, do not trouble yourselves, I am well here, truly."

And her distress at giving trouble was so great that the table, to spare her, let her stay on the bench, and the moment passed, and the full life resumed, and Plácida stayed in the corner with her heart leaping, not for the humiliation of the bench, but for the horror of having, for an instant, made the table stop on her account.

And it was that the part she took home and that most made her think: that when the table offered to open her a place, she refused. That they had, at last, offered her what she had not had, a place in the middle, and that she, out of habit, out of dread of bothering, had refused, and gone back to the bench of her own will. No one forced her to stay in the corner in that second moment: she chose the corner, when they offered her the middle. And Plácida realised that her fate was not only that the village did not set her a place: it was that she refused it when they set it for her. That even if they offered her the middle, she would flee to the end, because the end did not weigh and the middle weighed, and that to change it was not enough that the village see her: it was she who had to let herself be put in the middle.

Bento, unconvinced, went back at last to his place looking behind him, at the grandmother on the bench, with the same serious face with which he had seen her leave the other festival, the face of one who realises a thing is wrong and does not yet have words to say it.

Belmira, the daughter, was the only adult who noticed, though late and though badly. At the end of the festival, when she helped the mother gather the cloths, because Belmira helped, she had learned from the mother to help, and was also she, in her younger way, one who served, she stopped with the cloths folded in her arms and asked, with a touch of distress:

"Mother, is it true that there was no place for you? Bento was distressed about that."

And Plácida, of course, set about reassuring her.

"What nonsense, daughter. It was Bento's fancy. I was very well on the bench, I like to stay at hand to serve, I always have."

"But mother," insisted Belmira, with the folded cloths pressing against her chest, "Bento said you set the table in the morning, counted all the places, and then had none of your own. Is that true?"

And Plácida hesitated an instant, only an instant, because to lie was not in her nature, and then turned it aside:

"Daughter, I set everyone's table because I like to, and then I sit where it falls out. I make nothing of it. Come, help me fold these cloths, for it is growing dark and we still have all this."

And she turned the conversation to the cloths, as she always turned aside everything that might weigh, and Belmira, who had her life and her wearinesses, let herself be turned aside, because it was comfortable to accept the excuse, because her whole life they had taught her that the mother was always well, that the mother needed nothing, and it is hard for a child to suddenly see a frailty in a mother who built herself whole around having none. But it stayed with her, Belmira, while she folded the cloths beside the mother in the darkening churchyard, a splinter, the image of the mother on the bench in the corner, and that splinter was going to grow, over that year, until the conversation the two of them would have, much later, and that would be the deepest of their lives.

Because there was, between Plácida and Belmira, a distance that neither of them knew how to name, and that came exactly from here, from the not-asking. Belmira had grown up with a mother who never asked her for anything, who resolved everything alone, who never showed her a weakness, and had interpreted that, over her life, in the only way a child can interpret a mother who never needs them: as distance, as an I-do-not-need-you. Bento, the grandson, had been the only one to break through that wall, because Bento was a child and children ask without modesty and do not accept the it-is-not-needed, and so Bento had with the grandmother an intimacy that Belmira herself had never had: the intimacy of one who asks and is served, which is, after all, the only door into a person like Plácida.

And the answer to the grandson's question, which she did not give him because to a grandson one does not give such answers, she gave herself, that night, at the kitchen table, and it was the hardest answer she had ever given herself: because I never put my place. My whole life, at every table, I put the place of everyone and forgot mine, and then, when there was no place for me, instead of asking for it, I got a bench in the corner and said I liked to be there. No one took my place at the table. It was I who never put it. And no one is going to put it for me, because no one puts the place of one who never asks for it. If I want one day to sit at the table, in the middle, in the full life, with the families, I am going to have to do the hardest thing there is, the thing I never did in sixty-six years: I am going to have to ask for it.
