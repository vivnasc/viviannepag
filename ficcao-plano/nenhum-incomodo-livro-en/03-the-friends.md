## 3. The friends

Plácida had friends of a lifetime, and the oldest and the most beloved was Felizarda, and the story of the two was, without either of them ever having told it so, the story of one who gave and one who received, over sixty years, without the account ever being said or turned about.

They were friends since they were girls, of those who share everything, and they had grown up together, and married at near times, and raised the children abreast, and grown old side by side. Felizarda was what the name said, or seemed to say: a woman of luck, married to a man with means, owner of a house that was among the best of the village, and she had, on top of the luck, the security of people for whom life went well, that of one who feels herself, without thinking of it, at the centre of things, with a right to the middle place at the table. And she needed, as those of the centre need, someone on the margin to listen to her, and Plácida was that person, for sixty years, and the two fit so well, the centre and the margin, the one who speaks and the one who listens, that never, in sixty years, had either of them called into question the form of that fit.

There was, that summer, an afternoon that was the portrait of all the afternoons of the two, and that Plácida, for once, saw from outside, because the thought of the festival, that of no one noticing her absence, had opened an eye that had been closed.

Felizarda appeared at Plácida's door without warning, as she always appeared, with her face already laden with a thing that had happened to her, and she did not even sit straight at the table, she began at once:

"Plácida, you cannot imagine what that woman did to me. The daughter-in-law. I went there yesterday, with all good will, took her a sweet, and she receives me like one receives the rent-collector. She told me, in front of my son, that I meddle where I am not called. I! Who gave the house they live in!"

Plácida set her coffee before her, and the sugar, and the spoon, and sat on the other side, and Felizarda spoke. She spoke an hour, or two, of the daughter-in-law, of the quarrel, of what she said and what they said to her, and of what she should have said and did not, and Plácida listened, and went on saying what there was to say:

"That is so, Felizarda. You are right. It is not easy, a daughter-in-law like that. But look, it will settle, these things settle."

"You know best," said Felizarda, and went on unburdening herself, and went on relieving herself, and at a certain point, already light, already consoled, she got up to go, and at the door turned with the phrase of always, the one that was the seal of sixty years of friendship:

"Ah, Plácida, it is so good to unburden myself with you. You are the only one with whom I can speak at ease, for you never have problems, with you a person does not have to worry about anything, it is just to unburden oneself and come away light. I do not know what I would do without you."

And she went, light, consoled, without ever, in two hours, having asked a single time how Plácida was. And Plácida stayed at the door, with the cloth in her hands, watching her go down the street, and the most terrible thing, she realised that afternoon, with the new eye open, is that Felizarda's phrase was a compliment, and was sincere, and was even, in her way, a love: Felizarda loved her for her never having problems, for being the light friend, the one who only gives and never asks. Felizarda loved in her exactly that which effaced her. And Plácida knew, that afternoon, that if one day she said to Felizarda, look, today it is I who need to unburden myself, Felizarda would be disconcerted, perhaps even vaguely cheated, because that would spoil the form of the friendship, would change Plácida from a wall into a person, and people, unlike walls, give trouble.

There was a ritual, among the friends, that had lasted forty years: the teas of the month. Once a month they gathered at the house of one of them, by turns, and the house that received saw to everything. Only, whatever the house that received, it was always Plácida who saw to almost everything. That summer there was the tea at Idalina's house, and it was like all of them: Plácida arrived half an hour before, with an apron under her coat, and knocked at the door, and Idalina opened already with the air of one who is set at ease.

"It is well you came early, Plácida. I do not even know where to begin, with all this."

"Leave it, woman, leave it, I will see to it," said Plácida, and took off her coat, and tied her apron well, and went to the kitchen to cut the bread and set out the platters and make the coffee, while the others arrived in the parlour, dressed for the afternoon, with their earrings and their shawls, and sat, and conversed, and Plácida went from the kitchen to the parlour with the platters, and from the parlour to the kitchen with the empty glasses, and no one told her to sit down, because no one saw that she was on her feet. She served the tea, went from glass to glass, and when the conversation thickened in the parlour, she withdrew to the kitchen to wash the dishes, and heard from there the laughter of the friends, and said from afar yes, yes, when they called her to confirm a thing, and thought that well, that that was her place, there in the kitchen, with her hands in the hot water, listening to the friends laugh in the parlour. When she came back to the parlour drying her hands on her apron, with the dishes already all washed and put away, Idalina said, with no malice at all, turned to the others:

"Ah, Plácida is the one who is worth it. In a house where Plácida is, the mistress of the house can sit down. It is all washed already, just look. I did not even notice her."

And the others laughed and agreed, and Plácida smiled, and did not sit down, and did not even notice that she had heard again, from another mouth, the phrase of her whole life: I did not even notice her. In forty years of monthly teas, almost five hundred teas, she had been served, seated, doing nothing, perhaps a dozen times, the times she had been ill or away, and even those had left her uneasy, sitting at the table doing nothing, with her hands wanting to get up. And the most revealing thing is that for forty years Plácida had liked that, or thought she liked it. She thought it was there, in the kitchen, serving, that she felt well, and that the friends in the parlour were of another kind, that of those who sit, and that she was of the kind of those who serve, and that each kind had its place. It had never occurred to her, in forty years, that she might sit in the parlour conversing and leave another in the kitchen, that she might be served without the world ending.

And there, that afternoon of Idalina's tea, with the new eye, she began to suspect that that division between those who serve and those who are served was no natural thing at all, it was a thing that was learned, and that she had learned, when small, in the full house, and that she had been teaching, without knowing it, the friends and the whole village, for forty years, one tea at a time.

There was, in the group of friends, an exception that confirmed the rule, and it was Rosenda, a woman who complained of everything, who asked for everything, who weighed on everyone. At that same tea, the moment Rosenda left to go early, the others at once:

"Poor Rosenda, always with her complaints. Today it was the knee, last week it was the son-in-law. A person tires."

"And always needing something. What a nuisance."

And Plácida, hearing the friends speak ill of Rosenda, felt a confused thing, that only that summer she knew how to untangle: she felt, on one hand, the comfort of not being like Rosenda, of being the light one and not the tiresome one; and she felt, on the other, hidden, an absurd envy of Rosenda, because Rosenda, with all her complaints, was cared for, was attended to, occupied a place in the friends, even if it was the place of the one who tires, and no one, ever, forgot to invite Rosenda, because Rosenda made herself noticed, Rosenda weighed, and what weighs is not forgotten. And Plácida realised, ashamed of her own envy, that she would rather, in a deep corner, be the tiresome Rosenda, remembered, than the light Plácida, forgotten.

The seal of the thing came days later, and came with no malice at all, which made it worse. It was Felizarda's birthday, and there was going to be a lunch of all the friends, at the house of one of them, and Plácida learned of the lunch by chance, two days before, from the mouth of the butcher's wife:

"So, Plácida, are you going to Felizarda's lunch on Saturday?"

And Plácida was left with the bag in her hand, and felt a tightening, because no one had invited her. They had arranged the lunch in a conversation in which she was not present, and then each one thought another would have told her, and Plácida had slipped through the plans as she slipped through everything, too light to be caught on a guest list. That night Felizarda, on learning that Plácida knew, telephoned her distressed:

"Ah Plácida, what heads we have, forgive us, of course it is with you, you have to come, how could we forget you?"

And Plácida did what she always did: she forgave her before she had finished apologising.

"It does not matter, woman, do not distress yourself, I know how it is, it is so many things. I will come, of course I will come, do not think of it any more."

And she took from Felizarda the weight of the guilt, as she took from everyone the weight of everything, and she was left with the whole weight, that of having been forgotten and that of having to console the one who forgot her. And she went to the lunch, and arrived early, to help, and while the others arrived dressed for a festival, on time, to be received, Plácida was already in the kitchen, in an apron. At the table she sat where there was room left, which was at an end, and during the whole lunch she got up more times than all the others together, to fetch what was lacking, to fill the glasses, to bring the dessert, and no one said to her sit down, Plácida, leave it, because all had grown used, over a lifetime, to Plácida being the one who got up. And the conversation turned, as always, around the lives of the others. One told of a son who was going to marry, and all wanted to know of the bride, and the bride's mother, and the trousseau. Another told of a pain in the hips, and at once remedies and folk-cures were traded. Felizarda told of the daughter-in-law again, and all took sides. And at a certain point, in a pause in the conversation, Felizarda turned to the end where Plácida was stacking empty plates and said:

"And you, Plácida, pass me the sweet, if you please, it is there on your side."

And Plácida passed the sweet, and the conversation went on, and no one, in the whole lunch, asked her anything about her life, about Belmira, about Bento, about whether she was well or ill, and it was not out of ill will: it was because Plácida, in the imagination of those women, was not quite one of them, she was the one who listened to the lives of the others, the confidante, the one who serves the conversation as she serves the table. She was, at the table of the friends, the same thing she was at the table of the village: the one who passes the sweet and of whom nothing is asked.

And Plácida, at that lunch, saw herself from outside for the first time, and saw a thing that made her hurt: she saw that she did not have, in that group of a lifetime, friends in the sense of people who knew her, who knew her things. She had people whom she served and listened to, and who wanted her for the service and the listening, and who had no idea who she was within, because she had never given herself to be known. She had spent a lifetime surrounded by friends and was, in the midst of them, completely alone, and her solitude was the worst of all, that of the servant in the midst of the festival.

There was, at the end of that lunch, a detail that stayed with Plácida. When they got up from the table, Felizarda, already full of sweet and of conversation, stretched in her chair and said, to the parlour:

"Ah, what a fine afternoon. There is nothing like being with the friends."

And all agreed, and Plácida agreed too, from the sink, with her hands in the foam, and she realised, in that instant, that for Felizarda and for the others that had been an afternoon of being with the friends, and that for her it had been an afternoon of work, and that the same afternoon had been, for some, company, and for her, service, and that the whole difference lay in who sits and who washes the dishes, and that she had chosen, or had been taught to choose, or there had never even been a choice, to be always on the side of the one who washes.

But that night, at home, Plácida could not do what she always did, which was to swallow the thing and not let it weigh. The thing weighed. For the first time in sixty years, the thing weighed, and Plácida found herself at the kitchen table, alone, thinking a phrase she had never thought: they forgot me because I made a point of being forgettable. I taught them, my whole life, not to count on me needing, to count only on me giving, and they learned, and now they forget me from the lists and the places, and the fault, if there is fault, is as much mine as theirs. And there was, in that phrase, no anger at all at the friends, because Plácida had no knack for anger: there was only a deep sadness, and a new question, lodged crosswise, that was the same as that of the festival, now clearer: and what if I had weighed a little? And what if I, once in my life, had given trouble? Would it cost them so much, the people I love, that I, from time to time, needed them?
