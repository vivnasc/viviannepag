## 3. The friends

Plácida had friends of a lifetime, and the oldest and the most beloved was Felizarda, and the story of the two was, without either of them ever having told it so, the story of one who gave and one who received, over sixty years, without the account ever being said or turned about.

They were friends since they were girls, of those who share everything, and they had grown up together, and married at near times, and raised the children abreast, and grown old side by side, and the village held them inseparable, Plácida and Felizarda, as one says of two things that always go together.

Felizarda was what the name said, or seemed to say: a woman of luck, married to a man with means, mother of children who throve, owner of a house that was among the best of the village, and she had, on top of the luck, that security of people for whom life went well, the security of one who feels herself, without thinking of it, at the centre of things, with a right to the middle place at the table. And she needed, as those of the centre need, someone on the margin to listen to her, to serve her, to confirm her centre, and Plácida was that person, for sixty years, and the two fit so well, the centre and the margin, the one who speaks and the one who listens, the one who weighs and the one who relieves, that never, in sixty years, had either of them called into question the form of that fit, which seemed natural as everything that has lasted a long time seems natural. Plácida loved Felizarda, loved her truly, and Felizarda loved Plácida, in her way, the way of one who loves one who serves them; and the mild tragedy of the thing was that neither of them saw that that was not quite a friendship between equals, it was a court, with its queen and its lady, and that the lady, at the end of a life of serving the queen, had a heart full of things the queen had never asked her about, because of ladies one does not ask the things of the heart, one expects them to serve. But one who looked closely, and no one looked closely, would see that the friendship had a fixed form, always the same, repeated for sixty years: Felizarda spoke and Plácida listened; Felizarda had pains and Plácida consoled; Felizarda needed and Plácida attended. Never the contrary. Never, in sixty years, had Plácida arrived at Felizarda's house needing to be consoled, bringing a pain to pour out, asking attend to me. And Felizarda, who was not bad, who loved Plácida in her way, had grown used to that form as we grow used to what is comfortable: Plácida was the friend who did not weigh, the one who was always available, the one who listened without collecting, the one who attended without asking anything in return, and Felizarda leaned on her for sixty years with the innocence of one who leans on a wall without ever asking the wall whether it tires.

There was, that summer, an afternoon that was the portrait of all the afternoons of the two, and that Plácida, for once, saw from outside, because the thought of the festival, that of no one noticing her absence, had opened an eye that had been closed.

Felizarda appeared at Plácida's house, as she always appeared, without warning, overflowing with something or other that had happened to her, a quarrel with a daughter-in-law, and sat at Plácida's table, and Plácida put her coffee before her, and Felizarda spoke. She spoke an hour, or two, of the daughter-in-law, of the quarrel, of what she said and what they said to her, and Plácida listened, and consoled, and gave her right where there was right and tact where there was none, and Felizarda went on unburdening herself, went on relieving herself, and at a certain point, already light, already consoled, she got up to go, and said, at the door, the phrase of always, the phrase that was the seal of sixty years of friendship:

"Ah, Plácida, it is so good to unburden myself with you. You are the only one with whom I can speak at ease, for you never have problems, with you a person does not have to worry about anything, it is just to unburden oneself and come away light. I do not know what I would do without you."

And she went, light, consoled, without ever, in two hours, having asked a single time how Plácida was. And the most terrible thing, Plácida realised that afternoon, with the new eye open, is that Felizarda's phrase was a compliment, and was sincere, and was even, in her way, a love: Felizarda loved her for her never having problems, for with her a person not having to worry about anything, for being the light friend, the one who only gives and never asks. Felizarda loved in her exactly that which effaced her. And Plácida realised, on closing the door, that if one day she said to Felizarda, look, today it is I who need to unburden myself, today it is I who have a pain, Felizarda would be disconcerted, perhaps even vaguely cheated, because that would spoil the form of the friendship, would change Plácida from a wall into a person, and people, unlike walls, give trouble.

There was a ritual, among the friends, that had lasted forty years, and that was the living proof of Plácida's place in the group: the monthly teas. Once a month, the friends gathered at the house of one of them, by turns, for a tea, and the house that received saw to everything. Only, over forty years, whatever the house that received, it was always Plácida who saw to almost everything: she arrived earlier to help the hostess, stayed in the kitchen preparing, served the tea, and stayed afterwards washing the dishes while the others talked in the parlour. And at the teas at Plácida's own house, it was the same: Plácida saw to everything, and the friends arrived, were served, and went, and none stayed to help her tidy, because to tidy was Plácida's, was what Plácida did, was Plácida's place. Forty years of monthly teas, and Plácida once calculated, without meaning to, the number: almost five hundred teas, and in five hundred teas she had been served, seated, doing nothing, perhaps a dozen times, the times she had been ill or away, and even those had left her uneasy, sitting at the table doing nothing, with her hands wanting to get up.

And the most revealing thing is that Plácida, for forty years, had liked that, or thought she liked it. She thought it was her place, that it was there, in the kitchen, serving, that she felt well, and that the friends in the parlour, talking, were of another kind, the kind of those who sit and are served, and that she was not of that kind, she was of the kind of those who serve, and that each kind had its place and hers was the kitchen. It had never occurred to her, in forty years, that she might be of both kinds, or of neither, that she might, at a tea, sit in the parlour talking and leave another in the kitchen, that she might be served without the world ending. The division between those who serve and those who are served seemed to her as natural as the division between the day and the night, and she was on the side of serving, and she was well, and only that year, with the new eye, did she begin to suspect that that division was no natural thing at all, it was a thing that was learned, and that she had learned, when small, in the full house, and that she had been teaching, without knowing it, the friends and the whole village, for forty years, one tea at a time.

It had been so with all the friends, her whole life. Plácida was, in the group of women of her age, the confidante of all and the confidante of none. She knew the secrets of all, the pains of all, and none knew hers, because she had never given them, and none asked for them, because Plácida did not seem to have any. She was the one who organised, the one who remembered the birthdays, the one who visited the sick, the one who took the broth to one who needed it, the one who stayed to tidy after the festivals when the others had already gone, and she was, for all that, deeply beloved and deeply invisible, beloved for what she gave and invisible for what she did not ask, and the two things were, after all, the same, because in a friendship, as at a table, one only sees who occupies a place, and Plácida never occupied any place.

There was, in the group of friends, an exception that confirmed the rule, and it was Rosenda, a woman who complained of everything, who asked for everything, who weighed on everyone, and of whom the friends spoke ill behind her back, ah Rosenda, always with her complaints, always needing something, what a nuisance. And Plácida, hearing the friends speak ill of Rosenda, felt a confused thing, that only that summer she knew how to untangle: she felt, on one hand, the comfort of not being like Rosenda, of being the light one and not the tiresome one; and she felt, on the other, hidden, an absurd envy of Rosenda, because Rosenda, with all her complaints and her requests, was cared for, was attended to, occupied a place in the friends, even if it was the place of the one who tires, and no one, ever, forgot to invite Rosenda, because Rosenda made herself noticed, Rosenda weighed, and what weighs is not forgotten. And Plácida realised, ashamed of her own envy, that she would rather, in a deep corner, be the tiresome Rosenda, remembered, than the light Plácida, forgotten, and that that preference, which she would never confess to anyone, said everything about the price of her lightness: that to be easy had cost her to be no one, and that even the tiresome Rosenda, with her life full of complaints, was happier, because she was more seen.

The seal of the thing came days later, and came with no malice at all, which made it worse. It was Felizarda's birthday, and there was going to be a lunch, of all the friends, at the house of one of them, and they organised it among themselves, and Plácida learned of the lunch by chance, from another person, two days before, and realised, with a tightening, that they had forgotten to invite her. Not out of ill will. They had arranged the lunch in a conversation in which Plácida was not present, and then each one thought another would have told her, and Plácida, who never collected, who never claimed a place, slipped through the plans as she slipped through everything, too light to be caught on a guest list. And when Felizarda, on learning that Plácida knew, telephoned her distressed to apologise, ah Plácida, what heads we have, of course it is with you, you have to come, Plácida did what she always did: she forgave her before she had finished apologising. It does not matter, woman, do not distress yourself, I know how it is, it is so many things, I will come, of course I will come, do not think of it any more. And she took from Felizarda the weight of the guilt, as she took from everyone the weight of everything, and she was left with the whole weight, that of having been forgotten and that of having to console the one who forgot her, and she went to the lunch, and helped to serve, and sat at the end, and no one, not even she, noticed the irony of the forgotten one having gone to help at the festival of the one who had forgotten her.

The lunch of Felizarda's was, itself, a small lesson about Plácida's place in the group, and Plácida, with the new eye open, saw it whole. She arrived early, as always, to help, and while the other friends arrived dressed for a festival, on time, to be received, Plácida was already in the kitchen of the house where the lunch was held, in an apron, helping to serve. At the table, she sat where there was room left, which was at an end, and during the whole lunch she got up more times than all the others together, to fetch what was lacking, to fill the glasses, to bring the dessert, and no one said to her sit down, Plácida, leave it, because all had grown used, over a lifetime, to Plácida being the one who got up, the one who served, and to grow used to a thing is to cease seeing it. And the conversation of the lunch turned, as always, around the others, the lives of the others, the children of the others, the pains of the others, and Plácida was the one who listened, the one who gave them right, the one who laughed at the jokes, and no one, in the whole lunch, asked her anything about her life, about her children, about her pains, and it was not out of ill will: it was because Plácida, in the imagination of those women, was not quite one of them, one with a life and pains and children to tell of; she was the one who listened to the lives and the pains and the children of the others, the confidante, the one who is on the far side of the conversation, the one who serves the conversation as she serves the table. And Plácida, at that lunch, saw herself from outside for the first time, and saw a thing that made her hurt: she saw that she did not have, in that group of a lifetime, friends in the sense of people who knew her, who knew her things, who noticed her. She had people whom she served and listened to, and who wanted her for the service and the listening, and who had no idea who she was within, because she had never given herself to be known, because to give oneself to be known would be to weigh, and she did not weigh. She had spent a lifetime surrounded by friends and was, in the midst of them, completely alone, and her solitude was the worst of all, the solitude of one who is always accompanied and never seen, the solitude of the servant in the midst of the festival.

But that night, at home, Plácida could not do what she always did, which was to swallow the thing and not let it weigh. The thing weighed. For the first time in sixty years, the thing weighed, and Plácida found herself at the kitchen table, alone, thinking a phrase she had never thought: they forgot me because I made a point of being forgettable. I taught them, my whole life, not to count on me needing, to count only on me giving, and they learned, and now they forget me from the lists and the places and the festivals, and the fault, if there is fault, is as much mine as theirs, because it was I who taught them that Plácida fits in any corner, that Plácida need not be invited, that Plácida gives no trouble. I made myself forgettable with such skill that now they forget me, and then it is still I who console them for having forgotten me. And there was, in that phrase, no anger at all at the friends, because Plácida had no knack for anger: there was only a deep sadness, and a new question, lodged crosswise, that was the same as that of the festival, now clearer: and what if I had weighed a little? And what if I, once in my life, had given trouble? Would it be so bad? Would it cost them so much, the people I love, that I, from time to time, needed them?
