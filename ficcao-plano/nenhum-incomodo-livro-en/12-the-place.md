## 12. The place

The festival of the saint was set up in the churchyard, as every summer, with the long table from one end to the other, and Plácida went, and did a thing she had never done in sixty-six years: she arrived on time, as a guest, and not before, as a servant.

And to arrive on time she had to check her body the whole morning, because the body, on the morning of the festival, did what it had always done: it wanted to go early, wanted to catch the work of setting up the table, wanted to put the places of the others. Plácida, at home, dressed in her best, with the coat the daughter had given her, sat at the kitchen table and looked at the clock, and it was one of the hardest waits of her life. Each minute that passed was a minute in which she imagined the other women working in the churchyard without her, spreading cloths, counting places, and that, for a woman who had built herself whole around being the first to help, was almost unbearable, was like failing a sacred duty. Twice she got up to go, and twice she sat down again, and said aloud, alone in the kitchen, the phrase of the general of eight years:

"You do not go early, Plácida. If you go early you go back to serving and sitting at the end. You go at the hour."

In the middle of the wait, they knocked at her gate. It was a girl of the village, sent by the women of the setting-up, out of breath:

"Dona Plácida, are you not coming? We are full of work there in the churchyard, we are short of hands, and you arrive early every year. Will you come and give a hand?"

And Plácida felt her whole body rise from the chair, her hands leap, her mouth already saying yes, I am coming, wait while I put on my apron. It was the hardest moment of all the morning. But she held to the sides of the chair, and breathed, and said, with her voice trembling:

"Not today, my girl. Today I go to the festival at the hour, as a guest. Tell them that this year Plácida is going to sit down." And she added, lower, almost apologising for the contrary of what she had asked for her whole life: "But thank you for coming to call me. It does me good that you noticed my absence."

And the girl went, marvelling, and Plácida stayed in the chair trembling from the victory, with her heart leaping, because to say no to a work was, for her, harder than it had been her whole life to say yes.

And she stayed, with her hands wanting to work held by force in her lap, until the hour of the guests came, and only then did she go out, and she went to the festival not as one who goes to fulfil a duty, but as one who goes to a festival, which was a thing she, in sixty-six years, had never done, to go to a festival only to be at it.

She entered the churchyard as the guests enter, and went straight to her place, which was set, in the middle of the long table, with her plate and her glass, because she had asked for it and Felizarda had put it. And she sat in her place, in the middle, with the families, with the village, and it was the first time in her life she sat in the middle of a table, and the bench in the corner, that, stayed empty, leaning against a wall, like an old trophy of a championship she no longer played.

At first it cost her. Sitting in the middle, with nothing to serve, without being able to get up at every moment to fetch, Plácida felt herself naked, exposed, without the shield of service behind which she had hidden her whole life. Her hands did not know how to be still, they wanted to get up, to serve, to do. But Plácida held them in her lap, and stayed, and bore the discomfort of being simply seated at the table, of being one of the people of the festival, and not the shadow that serves the festival of others. She had beside her, in the middle of the table, the woman of the Serranos, who came down for the festival every year and whom Plácida had always seen only from afar, from the end, serving her her plate. And the woman, at a certain point, turned to her and said, like one who strikes up conversation with an equal:

"So, dona Plácida, this year you are not serving? I was surprised to see you seated."

And Plácida, who would have answered with an excuse, with an I am coming, with a leave it, I will see to it, breathed, and said the truth:

"This year I resolved to stay seated, dona. I learned, late, that I too have the right to eat at the table like everyone."

"And you do very well," said the woman, and laughed. "Serving for us is enough by now. Look, try this one, for my sister-in-law makes it like no one."

And she put a piece on her plate, served Plácida, and Plácida let herself be served, and ate, and they conversed, of the sister-in-law, of the serra, of the year that was passing, and it was the first conversation Plácida had at a festival that was not to arrange who served what, and she realised, in the middle of it, that this was what the families did in the middle of the table for sixty years, while she went about jug in hand: they conversed. They were. They belonged.

And slowly, over the afternoon, the discomfort passed, and gave way to a thing she had never felt at a festival: the pleasure of being at it. Of laughing at the jokes of the middle of the table, that she, from the end, had never heard well.

And when, at a certain point, she wanted the bread, which was on the other side of the table, Plácida did the thing she had trained a whole year for, the thing that was the final examination: she asked for it. Aloud, at the table, with naturalness, as one who belongs asks:

"You at the end, pass me the bread, if you please."

And someone passed her the bread, without giving it the least importance, as one passes the bread to one who asks for it at a table, and Plácida received it, and ate, and the sky did not fall, and no one thought her a bother for having asked for the bread, and it was the smallest and most immense thing of her life: to ask for the bread at a table and to receive it, like everyone, like one who has a place, like one who exists.

Bento was beside her, in his place, and saw the grandmother ask for the bread, and smiled at her a smile of an accomplice, of a general who sees the soldier fulfil the mission, and Plácida winked at him, and it was between the two of them, in that wink at the long table, that the victory of a year of work was sealed.

At a certain point of the festival, Plácida looked at the corner where, a year before, she had eaten on a storeroom bench, at the edge of the table, alone, and the bench was there, leaning against the wall, empty, and Plácida looked at the empty bench as one looks at an old version of oneself, with a mixture of pity and of tenderness. She had spent sixty-six festivals on that bench, or on benches like it, at the edge of all the tables of her life, calling the exile humility. And she realised now, from the height of her place in the middle, that the bench had never been her true place: her place was this, that of the middle, beside the daughter, beside the grandson, and she had had it always waiting for her, sixty-six years, and had only not occupied it because she had never asked for it, and no one occupies a place they do not ask for.

She thought of Brando, the husband, who had died without ever seeing her at the table, always at the end, and she wished that he could see her now, in the middle, and she remembered that Brando, the meek one, had been the only one, in life, who had said to her sit down, woman, rest, and that now, finally, she was seated, and rested, and in the middle. And she thought of the mother, of the exhausted mother of the full house, who had praised her for giving no trouble and lost her for it, and she wished, without bitterness, that the mother too could see her, and that she might realise that the daughter she had taught not to exist had learned, late, to exist, and that that was not a betrayal of the mother's love, it was the cure of a wound that the mother, without meaning to, had opened in her.

And there was, at the end of the afternoon, a moment that Plácida kept for ever. Felizarda got up for a toast, as was done at the festivals, and toasted the saint, and the village, and then, to the surprise of all and above all of Plácida, raised her glass higher and said, aloud, to the whole long table:

"And I want to toast a person who is today, for the first time I remember, sitting with us in the middle of the table, and not at the end serving us. To Plácida. Who served us all her whole life, and whom no one, I the first, knew how to see as we should. This year she asked me for her place, and I was ashamed of never having put it for her. To Plácida, who is here, in the midst of us, where she always should have been. Welcome to the table, Plácida, at the end of sixty years. It was not before time."

And the long table toasted Plácida, the whole village, and all the faces turned toward her, and Plácida, in the middle of the table, felt a thing she had never felt at a festival, nor anywhere: she felt herself seen. Seen by everyone, at the same time, not as the one who serves, not as the shadow, but as a person, one of the people of the table, with a name and a place. And her eyes filled with water, and this time she did not lower them, did not see to putting anyone at ease, did not say it is not needed: she let herself be seen, with her eyes full, at the long table, and she raised her glass, and said, with her voice trembling but loud, for the whole table to hear, the first thing she asked for herself in public in sixty-six years, which was simply the right to be there:

"Thank you. And yes, I stay. I stay in the middle. I learned late, but I learned: my place is also here."

And there was, at Felizarda's toast, a thing Plácida did not expect and that marked her: around the table, when the village toasted, there were women, the others who served, the others of the ends, who got up too, and who looked at Plácida with a thing in their eyes that was recognition and was hope, because Plácida, in taking her place in the middle, had opened for all the possibility that they too, one day, might ask for theirs. And one of them, an old woman who had served the village as much as Plácida, raised her glass in her direction and said, low, only for the part of the table nearby to hear, but with an emotion that spread:

"Bless you, Plácida. For all of us."

And Plácida realised, in that bless-you-for-all-of-us, that her small gesture, that of asking for a place at the table, had been after all a great gesture, a gesture for many, and that in learning to exist she had not existed only for herself: she had given leave to all the other invisible ones of the village to exist too.

The festival went on, and Plácida stayed at the table until the end, and she did not go in an undertone, as she had always done, in the middle, without taking leave: she stayed until the end, and when she went, she took leave of each one, made herself noticed leaving. At the end of the festival, when the village began to clear up and the long table went on emptying, Plácida got up to help, by the habit of a lifetime, and Felizarda, who noticed, put her hand on her arm:

"Sit down, Plácida. Not today. Today there are others to clear up, today you are a guest."

And Plácida, who a year before would have insisted on helping, on being the last to leave after everything was tidied, heard the friend and did the hardest thing: she let herself be served. She sat down, and let others clear up, and stayed at the table conversing, and it cost her, it cost her dreadfully to see people working while she was seated, her hands wanting to get up. But she stayed. She bore the discomfort of being served, which was, for her, a discomfort greater than that of serving, and she realised that to learn to let herself be served was the other half of learning to ask, and perhaps the harder one, because to ask is still an act, and to let oneself be served is a surrender, is to stay still while others do for us.

And she went home with Bento and Belmira, the three of them together along the darkening way, and Bento went in the middle, holding the hands of the two, and at a certain point, swinging their arms, he said:

"You won the championship, grandmother. Today you did it all. You asked for the bread, you stayed until the end, you took your leave, and you let yourself be served. Four points in a day. It is your record."

"I won, my treasure," said Plácida, and squeezed his hand. "But the championship does not end, you know? This of existing is not won once and that is that. It is every day, it is one point at a time, until the end. And you are going to have to go on marking me the points, for alone I forget and go back to the end."

"I will mark them, grandmother," said Bento. "Always."

And Belmira, on the other side, heard that, and looked over the head of the son at the mother, and the two women smiled at one another in the dark, without saying anything, because there were things that were no longer needed to be said. And they went so home, the three of them, the woman who had learned to weigh, in the end, enough to stay, with the daughter on one side and the grandson marking her the points on the other.

Days later, she went to the House of the Register, for a last thing. She did not go to ask that they write her a large line when she died, as she had asked in the winter: the Keeper had taught her that that was for the dead, and Plácida was now busy existing among the living. She went for another reason. She sat at the long table at the back, and said to the Keeper:

"I come to ask you to record a new thing. It is not a death nor a birth. I spoke with the council, and it was decided: the long table of the festival of the saint will have, from now on, one more place, always set, always in the middle, for those who serve. So that one who serves the festival may also have a place at it, and not stay at the end nor on the bench in the corner. I want that to stay in the book, Keeper. Not for me. For what it means."

And the Keeper recorded it, and looked at Plácida with that thing in her face that almost never came to her, and said:

"This, Plácida, is a large line. And it is in life. There is none better. The dead have the lines I write them. The living have those they write themselves, by what they change. You changed the table of the village, woman. A hundred years from now, whoever sits in the place of those who serve, and does not know why that place exists, is sitting in a thing you made, by learning, old, to ask for yours. There is no larger line than that. That one is not erased."

And before Plácida left, the Keeper said to her one more thing, which was the last lesson of the book:

"You know, Plácida, what you did this year was not only to learn to ask. It was more. This village says a thing and does not think of it: that to serve is the greatest of virtues, and that the less a person weighs, the better person they are. And it kills people with that idea, slowly, by effacement, above all the women. You, in asking for your place, in having one more chair put at the table for those who serve, gave the lie to it. You showed that to serve does not oblige one to disappear, that one can serve and have a place, give and receive. You changed, with a chair, an idea that killed. There is no law I can write that does as much as a chair that makes one think."

And Plácida came out of the House of the Register into the sun of the largo, and crossed it, and from the largo the churchyard could be seen, where the long table was set up at the festivals, and Plácida looked at the churchyard and imagined, from then on, all the festivals with one more place, always set, in the middle, for those who serve, and she knew that she had made, with her life, a thing that stayed, not a small line of a woman no one noticed, but one more chair at a table, for ever, so that never again, in that village, would someone who serves have to eat on a bench in the corner for not having learned, as she had not learned for sixty-six years, that one who serves also has the right to a place, and that to ask for that place is not to bother: it is, simply, and at last, to exist.
