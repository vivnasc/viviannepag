## 12. The place

The festival of the saint was set up in the churchyard, as every summer, with the long table from one end to the other, and Plácida went, and did a thing she had never done in sixty-six years: she arrived on time, as a guest, and not before, as a servant.

And to arrive on time, and not before, she had to check her body the whole morning, because the body, on the morning of the festival, did what it had always done: it wanted to go early, wanted to catch the work of setting up the table, wanted to put the places of the others. Plácida, at home, dressed in her best, had to sit down and wait for the hour to come, and it was one of the hardest waits of her life, because each minute that passed was a minute in which she imagined the others working in the churchyard without her, and that, for a woman who had built herself whole around being the first to help, was almost unbearable, was like failing a sacred duty. But Bento had warned her, in the rehearsal, grandmother, do not go early, for if you go early you go back to serving and sitting at the end, go at the hour, and Plácida obeyed the general of eight years, and stayed at home suffering the wait, watching the clock, with her hands wanting to work, until the hour of the guests came, and only then did she go out, and she went to the festival not as one who goes to fulfil a duty, but as one who goes to a festival, which was a thing she, in sixty-six years, had never done, to go to a festival only to be at it.

It was the hardest thing of all, and Plácida had to decide it with firmness, because the body, out of habit, wanted to arrive early, to catch the work, to set the table of the others. But she checked the body, and arrived at the hour of the festival, dressed in her best, with the coat the daughter had given her, and entered the churchyard as the guests enter, and went straight to her place, which was set, in the middle of the long table, with her plate and her glass, because she had asked for it and Felizarda had put it. And she sat in her place, in the middle, with the families, with the village, and it was the first time in her life she sat in the middle of a table, and the stool in the corner, that, stayed empty, leaning against a wall, like an old trophy of a championship she no longer played.

At first it cost her. Sitting in the middle, with nothing to serve, without being able to get up at every moment to fetch, Plácida felt herself naked, exposed, without the shield of service behind which she had hidden her whole life. Her hands did not know how to be still, they wanted to get up, to serve, to do. But Plácida held them in her lap, and stayed, and bore the discomfort of being simply seated at the table, of being one of the people of the festival, and not the shadow that serves the festival of others. And slowly, over the afternoon, the discomfort passed, and gave way to a thing she had never felt at a festival: the pleasure of being at it. Of conversing with the one she had beside her without it being to serve. Of laughing at the jokes of the middle of the table, that she, from the end, had never heard well. Of being a part.

And when, at a certain point, she wanted the bread, which was on the other side of the table, Plácida did the thing she had trained a whole year for, the thing that was the final examination: she asked for it. Aloud, at the table, with naturalness, as one who belongs asks:

"You at the end, pass me the bread, if you please."

And someone passed her the bread, without giving it the least importance, as one passes the bread to one who asks for it at a table, and Plácida received it, and ate, and the sky did not fall, and no one thought her a bother for having asked for the bread, and it was the smallest and most immense thing of her life: to ask for the bread at a table and to receive it, like everyone, like one who has a place, like one who exists.

At a certain point of the festival, Plácida looked at the corner where, a year before, she had eaten on a storeroom stool, at the edge of the table, alone, and the stool was there, leaning against the wall, empty, and Plácida looked at the empty stool as one looks at an old version of oneself, with a mixture of pity and of tenderness. She had spent sixty-six festivals on that stool, or on stools like it, at the edge of all the tables of her life, and she had thought, all that time, that that was her place, that it was there that she fit, that the table, the middle, the full life, were of the others, of the people who weighed, of the people who belonged, and not of her, who served. And she looked now at the stool from the height of her place in the middle of the table, and realised that the stool had never been her true place: it had been the place where she had exiled herself, out of fear, calling the exile humility. Her true place was this, that of the middle, beside the daughter, beside the grandson, in the middle of the village, and she had had it always waiting for her, sixty-six years, and had only not occupied it because she had never asked for it, and no one occupies a place they do not ask for.

She thought of Brando, the husband, who had died without ever seeing her at the table, always at the end, and she wished that he could see her now, in the middle, and she thought that perhaps he saw her, from wherever he was, and that perhaps he rejoiced, because Brando, the meek one, had been the only one, in life, who had said to her sit down, woman, rest, and that now, finally, she was seated, and rested, and in the middle. And she thought of the mother, of the exhausted mother of the full house, who had praised her for giving no trouble and lost her for it, and she wished, without bitterness, that the mother too could see her, and that she might realise, from wherever she was, that the daughter she had taught not to exist had learned, late, to exist, and that that was not a betrayal of the mother's love, it was the cure of a wound that the mother, without meaning to, had opened in her, and that the mother, if she loved her truly, would want to see healed.

Bento was beside her, in his place, and saw the grandmother ask for the bread, and smiled at her a smile of an accomplice, of a general who sees the soldier fulfil the mission, and Plácida winked at him, and it was between the two of them, in that wink at the long table, that the victory of a year of work was sealed: the old woman who had learned to ask and the boy who had taught her, side by side, in the middle of the table, in the middle of the village, in the middle of life.

And there was, at the end of the afternoon, a moment that Plácida kept for ever. Felizarda, the friend of sixty years, got up for a toast, as was done at the festivals, and toasted the saint, and the village, and then, to the surprise of all and above all of Plácida, said:

"And I want to toast a person who is today, for the first time I remember, sitting with us in the middle of the table, and not at the end serving us. To Plácida. Who served us all her whole life, and whom no one, I the first, knew how to see as we should. This year she asked me for her place, and I was ashamed of never having put it for her. To Plácida, who is here, in the midst of us, where she always should have been. Welcome to the table, Plácida, at the end of sixty years. It was not before time."

And the long table toasted Plácida, the whole village, and all the faces turned toward her, and Plácida, in the middle of the table, felt a thing she had never felt at a festival, nor anywhere: she felt herself seen. Seen by everyone, at the same time, not as the one who serves, not as the shadow, but as a person, one of the people of the table, with a name and a place. And her eyes filled with water, and this time she did not lower them, did not see to putting anyone at ease, did not say it is not needed: she let herself be seen, with her eyes full, at the long table, and she raised her glass, and said, with her voice trembling but loud, for the whole table to hear, the first thing she asked for herself in public in sixty-six years, which was simply the right to be there:

"Thank you. And yes, I stay. I stay in the middle. I learned late, but I learned: my place is also here."

There was, at Felizarda's toast, a thing Plácida did not expect and that marked her: around the table, when Felizarda spoke to Plácida, and the village toasted, there were women, the others who served, the others of the ends, who got up too, and who looked at Plácida with a thing in their eyes that was recognition and was hope, because Plácida, in taking her place in the middle, had taken a little of the place of all of them, had opened for all the possibility that they too, one day, might ask for theirs. And one of them, an old woman who had served the village as much as Plácida, raised her glass in her direction and said, low, only for the part of the table nearby to hear, but with an emotion that spread: bless you, Plácida, for all of us. And Plácida realised, in that bless-you-for-all-of-us, that her small gesture, that of asking for a place at the table, had been after all a great gesture, a gesture for many, and that in learning to exist she had not existed only for herself: she had given leave to all the other invisible ones of the village to exist too, and that, which she had never planned, was perhaps the most beautiful thing her crossing had produced, that a woman, in learning to ask for her place, taught a whole village of silent women that they too had the right to one.

The festival went on, and Plácida stayed at the table until the end, and she did not go in an undertone, as she had always done, in the middle, without taking leave: she stayed until the end, and when she went, she took leave of each one, made herself noticed leaving, gave trouble to those who accompanied her to the gate, and went home with Bento and Belmira, the three of them together, the woman who had learned to weigh, in the end, enough to stay.

At the end of the festival, when the village began to clear up and the long table went on emptying, Plácida did a thing that was the exact measure of how much she had changed. She got up to help clear up, by the habit of a lifetime, and Felizarda, who noticed, said to her, sit down, Plácida, not today, today there are others to clear up, today you are a guest. And Plácida, who a year before would have insisted on helping, on serving to the end, on being the last to leave after everything was tidied, heard the friend and did the hardest thing: she let herself be served. She sat down, and let others clear up, and stayed at the table conversing, being one of those who stay conversing while others tidy, and it cost her, it cost her dreadfully to see people working while she was seated, her hands wanting to get up, her conscience weighing on her. But she stayed. She bore the discomfort of being served, which was, for her, a discomfort greater than that of serving, and she realised that to learn to let herself be served was the other half of learning to ask, and perhaps the harder one, because to ask is still an act, and to let oneself be served is a surrender, is to stay still while others do for us, is to tolerate the debt, is to accept that one receives without paying at the same instant. And Plácida, at that festival, received: she received the place, received the toast, received the service of others, received the being seen, and each thing received was a new muscle hurting, the muscle of receiving, that she had atrophied for sixty-seven years and that was beginning, at last, slowly, with pain, to move.

Days later, she went to the House of the Register, for a last thing. She did not go to ask that they write her a large line when she died, as she had asked in the winter: the Keeper had taught her that that was for the dead, and Plácida was now busy existing among the living. She went for another reason. She went to ask the Keeper to record a new thing, a thing she wanted to stay in the book of the village, not for herself, but for what she had learned: she asked that it be recorded that the long table of the festival of the saint would have, from then on, by a decision taken with the council, one more place, always set, always in the middle, for those who served, so that one who serves the festival might also have a place at it, and not stay at the end nor on the stool in the corner. It was the first thing Plácida left in the register that was not a line of birth or of death: it was a change in the custom of the village, one more place at the table, for those who serve, and the Keeper recorded it, and looked at Plácida with that thing in her face that almost never came to her, and said:

"This, Plácida, is a large line. And it is in life. There is none better. The dead have the lines I write them. The living have those they write themselves, by what they change. You changed the table of the village, woman. A hundred years from now, whoever sits in the place of those who serve, and does not know why that place exists, is sitting in a thing you made, by learning, old, to ask for yours. There is no larger line than that. That one is not erased."

Before leaving, the Keeper said to her one more thing, and it was the last lesson of the book. She said: you know, Plácida, what you did this year was not only to learn to ask. It was more. You were the proof of a thing this village says and does not think, which is that to serve is the greatest of virtues, and that the less a person weighs, the better person they are. This village is full of that idea, and it kills people with it, kills them slowly, by effacement, above all the women. And you, in asking for your place, in having one more chair put at the table for those who serve, gave the lie to that idea, showed that to serve does not oblige one to disappear, that one can serve and have a place, give and receive, help and be helped. You changed, with a chair, an idea that killed. There is no law I can write that does as much as a chair that makes one think. And Plácida took that, and realised that her small victory was not only hers nor only of those who served: it was against an idea, an old and cruel idea that confused goodness with disappearance, and that she, without being a thinker or anything, only an old woman who had learned late to ask for the bread, had begun to give the lie to, with her life, which is how cruel ideas are given the lie, not with speeches, but with lives that prove them wrong.

And Plácida came out of the House of the Register into the sun of the largo, and crossed it, and from the largo the churchyard could be seen, where the long table was set up at the festivals, and Plácida looked at the churchyard and imagined, from then on, all the festivals with one more place, always set, in the middle, for those who serve, and she knew that she had made, with her life, a thing that stayed, not a small line of a woman no one noticed, but one more chair at a table, for ever, so that never again, in that village, would someone who serves have to eat on a stool in the corner for not having learned, as she had not learned for sixty-six years, that one who serves also has the right to a place, and that to ask for that place is not to bother: it is, simply, and at last, to exist.
