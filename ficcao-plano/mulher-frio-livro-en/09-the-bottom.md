## 9. The bottom

But the thaw is not a straight line, it is like that of the serra, with advances and setbacks, and before Serafina could be, at last, cared for, there was the setback, there was the relapse, there was the bottom, and the bottom nearly killed her.

The bottom of a cure is a strange thing, because it comes often after one has already begun to get better, and it is for that more treacherous: because it is when the person has already tasted a bit of thaw, has already felt a bit of the warmth, that the fear of the thaw tightens with more force, and the temptation to draw back into the known ice becomes stronger than ever. Serafina, over the weeks of illness and care, had begun to melt: she had accepted the blanket, let herself be cared for, felt the warmth, and all that, instead of reassuring her, had frightened her more and more, because each bit of ice that melted was a bit of fortress that went, and the fortress was all she knew how to be. And there is a point, in every cure, when the person has to choose between the unknown that the cure offers them and the known that the illness guarantees them, and many people, at that point, choose the known, even though the known kills them, because the unknown is more frightening than death. It was to that point that Serafina came, and it was that choice that nearly killed her: the choice to draw back into the known fortress instead of advancing into the unknown thaw.

It was when the hip seemed to get a little better. Serafina, the moment she felt she could get on her feet, tried to be again who she was: she wanted to get up, wanted to see to things, wanted to put an end to the being cared for, that that had already lasted too long, had already given the granddaughter too much trouble. And she was not well, she was far from well, but the fortress, the moment it felt a remnant of strength, wanted to go back to its post, because to be cared for was more unbearable to her than the illness, and she preferred to risk relapsing than to go on depending. She got up, against Neves's will, wanted to go and tend the animals in the cold, and Neves argued with her, grandmother, you cannot, you are not yet well, and Serafina, stubborn, went all the same, and into the cold, with the hip still bad, with the cough still deep, and she fell again, and got worse again, and the thing, which was getting better, worsened again, dangerously, and one night Serafina burned with fever again, worse than ever, and the snow had closed the serra again, and Neves, alone on the height with the grandmother burning, was, for the first time, truly afraid that the grandmother would die.

Serafina's setback was not only stubbornness: it was panic, the panic of the fortress that felt the ice melting and that was frightened by the thaw. Because the thaw, for one who built themselves entirely of ice, is not a relief: it is a threat to one's very identity. Serafina had felt, in the weeks of illness and care, the ice giving way, had felt herself melting, feeling, needing, depending, and that, instead of relieving her, had terrified her, because if she ceased to be the fortress, who was she? The fortress was all she knew how to be, for sixty-three years; without it, Serafina did not know who she would be, and the fear of not knowing who she would be without the ice was so great that she preferred to freeze again, even if to freeze killed her, than to risk discovering who she was without the ice. It was for that that she got up, that she went into the cold, that she relapsed: not out of stubbornness, but out of a desperate flight back to the known ice, fleeing the unknown thaw that the granddaughter brought her. The fortress, feeling itself crumbling, did what fortresses in panic do: it tried to rebuild itself in haste, at any cost, even at the cost of its own life.

And Neves realised this, that night, and it was for realising it that she grew angry, because she saw that the grandmother was fleeing back into the prison that was killing her, and that if she did not check her the grandmother would die there, not of the illness, but of the ice, of the desperate return to the ice. And it was that night, with the grandmother burning and the snow closing everything and the fear tightening, that Neves lost her patience, and the loss of patience was, after all, what was missing, because there are truths that only come out in anger.

"You are killing yourself!" said Neves, and her voice came out trembling with fear and with anger. "Do you understand, grandmother? You got up ill, you went into the cold ill, you relapsed, and now you are here burning with fever because you were not able to let yourself be cared for a few more days! Your strength, grandmother, your strength that saved your siblings, is now killing you! It is killing you! Because it is good for nothing any more, that strength, there is no longer anyone to save, your siblings have been raised for fifty years, and you go on enduring, refusing, not needing, as though you still had five mouths to feed, as though someone still died if you stopped! But no one dies any more, grandmother! It has passed! Sixty-three years ago it passed, and you are still in that winter, still being the girl of eleven years who cannot feel the cold or they all die, and no one dies any more, grandmother, only you die, you are the only one who can still die of this, of your own strength, of not letting yourself be cared for! I did not come to watch you die stubborn! I came to spend a winter with my grandmother and I want to take my grandmother alive into the spring, and for that you have to stop, you have to let yourself be cared for, you have to let the girl of eleven years rest at last!"

And she fell silent, trembling, with tears on her face, and there fell a silence in the house of the height, with the snow falling and the fire burning, and Serafina, in bed, with the fever, looked at the granddaughter weeping from fear for her, and it was perhaps that image, that of the granddaughter weeping from fear of losing her, that broke through all the ice at last, because Serafina, who had never wept in sixty-three years, felt, on seeing the granddaughter weep for her, a thing rise from the bottom, a thing she had not felt since the eleven years, a thing warm and terrible and long frozen, and said, low, with her voice breaking:

There was a silence after Neves's anger, a silence in which Serafina, in bed, with the fever, looked at the granddaughter as she had never looked at her, and Neves was afraid that the grandmother would shut herself away for good, offended, that she would tell her to be silent, that she would put her in her place. But what happened was the contrary: Serafina looked at the granddaughter and saw, for the first time, someone who loved her enough to grow angry with her, someone who did not treat her as the untouchable saint that the whole serra saw, but as a stubborn person who was doing herself harm and to whom it was necessary, out of love, to say stop. And that novelty — to be treated not with reverence but with the harshness of true love, of the kind that dares to oppose — opened in Serafina a thing that sixty-three years of admiration had never opened. Because admiration keeps the distance, and anger, the anger of love, draws near, and Serafina, who had lived sixty-three years at the respectful distance of everyone's admiration, felt, in the granddaughter's anger, someone drawing near, entering, treating her from up close, and it was that nearness, more than the words, that disarmed her.

And she said, low, with her voice breaking: you are right, my girl. — And it was the first time in her life that Serafina said to anyone you are right about a thing of that size. — You are right. I am still in that winter. I never left it. I am seventy-four years old and I am still up there, in the snow, at eleven years old, waiting for my parents, enduring so that my siblings do not die, and no one needs me to endure any more, and I do not know how to stop, my girl, I do not know, they taught me to endure and no one ever taught me to stop, and now I am old and tired and still enduring, and you are right, this no longer saves me, this is killing me. I am so tired, Neves. I have been tired for sixty-three years. I never could say this to anyone: I am tired. I am so tired of being strong.

And in saying I am tired of being strong, Serafina said the most forbidden thing of her whole world, because on the serra, and in her life, to say oneself tired of being strong was like a soldier saying himself tired of defending the frontier: it was to confess that the defence that had sustained everything was at last crushing them. Everyone, her whole life, had told her you are so strong, Serafina, how I admire you, and she had never been able to answer what she felt beneath the compliment, which was: I am tired of being strong, let me rest, I do not want to be admired, I want to be cared for. Sixty-three years being praised for the strength and wishing, in secret, that someone would see, beneath the strength, the tiredness, and tell her, instead of how strong you are, you must be so tired, rest, now I hold. And it was exactly that the granddaughter had just told her, in the anger, you can let go for I hold, and it was for being exactly what she had waited for sixty-three years without knowing she waited for it that that disarmed her for good.

There was, before the weeping, a last tightening of the knot, because the bottom, to be the bottom, has to reach the end of the breath. Serafina, in bed, with the fever, after saying you are right, tried still to draw back, to do what she always did, to shut again the door that had opened: she told the granddaughter not to mind her, that it was the fever, that in the morning she would be well, that Neves should go to bed for she would manage. It was the last assault of the fortress, the attempt to go back to the steel after having shown the crack, and Neves knew it, and did not let her. Instead of obeying, instead of going to bed and letting the grandmother shut herself away again, Neves did a thing she had never done: she lay down beside the grandmother, on the narrow bed, dressed, over the blankets, and embraced her, and said, I am not going to bed in my room, grandmother, I am going to stay here with you tonight, and you are not alone, do you hear, you are not alone, and you can let go, for I hold. And it was that gesture, the granddaughter lying down beside her, embracing her, telling her you can let go for I hold, that broke through the ice for good, because it was exactly what no one had done for her at eleven years old, that winter: no one had lain down beside her telling her you can let go for I hold, because there was no one bigger than her, she was the biggest, and the girl of eleven years had had to hold everything alone, with no one to hold her. And now, sixty-three years later, there was. There was a granddaughter, in the bed, holding her, telling her the words no one had told her, you can let go, I hold, and the fortress, on hearing at last those words, on feeling at last someone holding her, ceased to have to hold itself, and what had been held for sixty-three years, loosed at last, collapsed.

The words Neves had said, in the anger, had been hard, and Neves was afraid, for an instant, of having gone too far, of having been cruel to an ill old woman. But they were the right thing, and she realised it by the effect: Serafina needed, not more sweetness, for sweetness everyone gave her, but a hard truth said out of love, of those that only one who loves us truly dares to say. Everyone, her whole life, had been sweet with Serafina, had admired her, spared her, treated her as the saint one does not contradict, and all that sweetness had been, at bottom, a way of leaving her in peace in her ice, of not touching her fortress. No one, in sixty-three years, had loved her enough to tell her the hard truth: your strength is killing you, let it go. And Serafina, at bottom, knew it, knew that that anger of the granddaughter was the proof of a love greater than all the reverence of the serra, because the reverence left her in peace and the granddaughter risked losing her to save her, and there is in being loved so, to the point of someone telling us the truths that hurt, an intimacy that Serafina had never known: that of being loved not as a symbol, not as a fortress, but as a person, a person one loves enough not to leave them in peace in their prison.

And in saying I am tired of being strong, Serafina began, at last, to weep, and it was a small weeping at first, hesitant, of one who lost the knack or never had it, but it was a weeping, the first in sixty-three years, and Neves, instead of staunching it, instead of consoling her and taking the weeping from her as everyone had always done to Serafina, did the contrary: she went into the bed beside the grandmother, embraced her, and told her, weep, grandmother, weep what you have to weep, I am here, I hold you, weep freely for I do not let you go, and Serafina, held by the granddaughter, let herself weep, and the thaw, which had come slowly since the first blanket, broke through at last for good that night of fever and of snow, and the water that had been held for sixty-three years began, with pain and with relief, to run.
