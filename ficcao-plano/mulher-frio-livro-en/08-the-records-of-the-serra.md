## 8. The records of the serra

When the snow abated, in February, and the paths opened a little again, Neves went down to the village for provisions and for medicine for the grandmother, and she went, on her own account, to the House of the Register, because she wanted to understand a thing the grandmother had told and that had stayed hurting her: that Serafina's parents had never had a grave.

The descent to the village was, itself, a thing for Neves, because it was the first time she had gone down since she had come up, and the village, seen by one who had spent weeks on the height, seemed to her another: full, warm, easy, with the houses together, everyone's hearths, life happening in common, so different from the solitude of the house of the height. And Neves realised, on going down, a thing about the grandmother: that to live on the height, alone, was also part of the ice, was a choice of isolation, because Serafina could have gone down to the village long ago, as so many old serra folk went down for the company and the warmth of others, and she had never done it, she had stayed on the height, alone, in the house of the parents, guarding a house that no longer needed to be guarded, because to go down would be to surrender, would be to need others, would be to cease being the fortress of the height. The very geography of Serafina's life, up there, far off, alone, was a portrait of the ice, and Neves realised that part of what she could do for the grandmother was to bring her more down, more near to the others, more to the warmth of the village, even if she did not change her house, even if it were only to make her go down more often, receive more visits, let the whole serra draw near to her.

The Keeper received her, and Neves explained whose granddaughter she was, and what she came to seek, and the Keeper, who knew the story of the Serranos as she knew the whole village, went to the old books, those of the time of the Great Winter, and showed Neves what was there, which was little and was much.

The House of the Register of the serra was smaller than that of the village, but it had the same books, the same wax, the same smell of old paper and of kept time, and the Keeper who served it was, like all of them, a woman of no certain age, with clean hands and a look that read people. And Neves, on entering, felt what everyone feels on entering a House of the Register: that she was entering the other serra, the one that neither works nor dies, the one that stays, the serra of paper where all the Serranos, living and dead, were kept in columns, and that what she came to seek there, the story of the great-grandparents lost in the snow, was in that place as everything that happened is, written, waiting for one who would go and read it.

There was the death of Serafina's parents, recorded in one line, with the date of the Great Winter, and beside it, in the margin, in the hand of the Keeper of then, a note that Neves read and that tightened her throat: *they were lost on the serra; there were no bodies to bury.* There was no grave, no headstone, no sepulchre where Serafina could have gone, over sixty-three years, to weep her parents, to take a flower, to do the mourning that is done. The snow had taken the parents and never given them back, and Serafina had been left not only an orphan, but an orphan without a grave, with a mourning that had nowhere to be done, and perhaps it was also that, Neves realised, part of the ice: that one cannot bury one who has no body, one cannot weep over a grave that does not exist, and that Serafina's mourning had stayed, like everything in her, frozen, waiting for a body that never came, for a grave that there never was, for a farewell that the snow had made impossible.

"Your grandmother," said the Keeper, putting away the books, "is, of all the people who passed through these records, among those the village most owes and those who least let the village give to them. I know the story. The whole serra knows it. That woman saved five siblings at eleven years old, and from those five came half the serra, people and people, generations. If I were to count how many people exist today because Serafina, at eleven years old, did not let her siblings die that winter, it would fill a page of these. And yet, her line, when the hour comes, is going to be small, because Serafina never asked for anything, never claimed anything, never let anyone do anything for her. She is the strongest woman this serra has given, and the loneliest, which are often the same thing."

The Keeper, on seeing the girl's interest, went to fetch yet another book, older, and showed Neves a thing she did not expect: the record of birth of Serafina herself, and beside it, in the following years, the births of the siblings, and then, in the year of the Great Winter, the death of the parents, and right after, in the same hand, a rare note, of those the Keepers only put when a thing touched them enough to break the dryness of the trade: *there remained five orphans on the height; the eldest, of eleven years, raises them.* A single line, put sixty-three years ago by a Keeper who had already died, recording, in the midst of the births and the deaths, the extraordinary fact of a girl of eleven years having been left to raise four siblings on a serra that had just killed her parents. And Neves looked at that line, *the eldest, of eleven years, raises them*, and realised that the grandmother's heroism was there, recorded, in a book, for sixty-three years, and that yet the grandmother had never seen it as heroism, had always seen it as what had to be done, and that that was, perhaps, the mark of true heroes: not knowing that they are such, thinking they only did what was necessary, not asking for it either recognition or care or rest, and being left, for that, with a small line in a book and a whole life of steel.

"The Keeper who wrote this," asked Neves, touching the line, "did she know my grandmother?"

"She knew her," said the Keeper. "And she wrote to me, when she passed me the book, that that girl of eleven years was the strongest and saddest thing she had seen on this serra, and that she pitied one thing: that no one, in that time, had thought of caring for her, instead of letting her care for everyone. That everyone, that winter and in those that came, marvelled at the girl's strength, and no one, not one, took her in their arms and told her, you can rest, now I care for you. And the old Keeper wrote to me that that was the greatest injustice she had seen, and that she stayed waiting, she said, for one day someone to come up to the serra and do to Serafina what no one did for her sixty years ago: care for her." The Keeper looked at Neves, with that attention she put into the things that mattered. "I reckon that someone is you. You took your time, but you came. Better late."

And Neves asked the Keeper a thing, before going up, a thing that had been born in her there, before the line without a grave:

"And if I wanted to do a thing for her parents? A grave, a sign, anything where my grandmother could, at last, go to weep them? Can it be done? Even without bodies?"

And the Keeper looked at the girl with a serious approval, and said yes, that it could: that one could put, in the cemetery of the serra, a stone with the names of the parents, a grave without a body, of the kind one puts for those the sea or the snow does not give back, and that that, though it had no body beneath, had a name above, and that a name above a stone, sometimes, is all a mourning needs to be able, at last, to be done. And Neves decided, there, that she was going to do that, that she was going to give the grandmother, at the end of sixty-three years, a grave where to weep her parents, and that that would be the present of the winter, greater than all the blankets, because a blanket warms the body and a grave warms, at last, the frozen mourning.

She went up to the serra with the provisions, the medicine, and the plan of the grave in her heart, and at night, by the hearth, she told the grandmother what she had seen in the records, the line without a grave, the note in the margin. And Serafina heard, and stayed a long time silent looking at the fire, and Neves realised that she had touched a deep place, the deepest of all, and was afraid of having gone too far. But Serafina, at the end of the silence, said, low, with a voice now without any ice, with a voice of a girl that Neves had never heard from her:

"I never had anywhere to weep them, my girl. My parents. There was never a grave. The snow took them and never again gave them back, and I, my whole life, did not have a place to go and put a flower, to go and say them a thing, to go and weep them. And perhaps it was also for that that I never wept them, you know? Because one does not weep in the air. One needs a place, a stone, a name. And I had none. My parents did not die, for me, they never finished dying, they stayed there, lost in the snow, sixty-three years, without a grave, without an end, and I with them, lost in the snow with them, unable to weep them because there was nowhere, unable to finish losing them because there was never a body, a grave, an end."

"And why did you never have a grave made, grandmother?" asked Neves, carefully. "Over so many years. You could have had a stone put with their names, even without bodies. Why did you never do it?"

And Serafina was a while silent, and then said: because to make a grave was to admit that they had died, my girl. And as long as there was no grave, as long as they were only lost in the snow, there was a part of me, the part of eleven years, that still waited for them, that still thought that one day they might appear, come down from the serra, come back. It is foolishness, I know. Sixty-three years. But the grave made the death definitive, and I never had the courage to make it definitive, I preferred to leave them lost in the snow, in a place where they could still, in my girl's head, come back. Not having the grave made was my way of never finishing losing them. And it was also, I realise now, my way of never finishing weeping them, because one does not weep one who can still come back, and as long as I did not make them the grave, they could still come back, and I still did not have to weep them, and the mourning stayed there, waiting, sixty-three years, like them, in the snow.

And Neves realised that the grave she was going to have made was not only a beautiful gesture: it was to help the grandmother do the thing she had never had the courage to do alone, which was to make the death of the parents definitive, to accept that they were not coming back, and to be able, at last, with the death accepted, to weep them and let them go. The grave was not a monument: it was a permission, the permission for the mourning the grandmother had put off sixty-three years for not being able, alone, to give the parents the end the snow had denied them. And Neves realised, with the clarity of one who sees a deep thing, that it was not the grandmother who was going to need courage for the grave: it was she, Neves, who was going to have to have the courage to have it made for the grandmother, to take that decision the grandmother had never taken, and to take the grandmother there, because there are things a person cannot do for themselves and that they need someone to do for them, with them, beside them, and to give a grave to the parents was, for Serafina, one of those things.

And Neves, on hearing this, realised that the grave she was going to have made was no ordinary beautiful gesture: it was the thing that was missing for the thaw to complete itself, it was the grave where Serafina was going to be able, at last, sixty-three years later, to lay down the parents and the mourning and the girl of eleven years who was still up there, in the snow, waiting for them to come back.

The Keeper, at the farewell, gave her still a counsel that was of the trade of one who cares for those who care. She said: you are going to go back up, my girl, and you are going to want to cure your grandmother in one winter, and you are going to grow frustrated, because a thing that took sixty-three years to freeze does not melt in one season. Go calmly. Do not want to melt all the ice. Melt what lets itself be melted, and be content with that. A blanket accepted is a victory. A weeping is a victory. Do not cure your grandmother: warm her. Give her the warmth she never had, the time that remains to her, and let the ice melt what it wants to melt beside that warmth. For with very frozen people, what one can do is not melt them entirely: it is to give them, in the end, a bit of the warmth they lacked their whole life, and that bit, even small, even late, is often everything. And Neves took that, and it was perhaps the most useful counsel she received that winter, because it freed her from the haste to cure and taught her to content herself with warming, which is, in the end, what one can do for those one loves: not to cure them, for that is not in our hands, but to warm them, to be there, to give them the warmth, and to let each one melt, beside that warmth, the ice they can.
