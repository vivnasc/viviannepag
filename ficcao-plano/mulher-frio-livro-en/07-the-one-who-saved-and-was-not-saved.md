## 7. The one who saved and was not saved

After the night when Serafina told the winter of the eleven years, the story went on coming out, now more easily, as the water comes out when the ice that covered it is broken, and Neves went on understanding, over January, the grandmother's whole life by the light of that scene, and the grandmother's whole life was a single phrase repeated in a thousand ways: the one who saved and was never saved.

Because after the winter of the eleven years, Serafina did not become a girl again. She leapt, that winter, from childhood into the charge, and never again left the charge. She raised the siblings: she clothed them, fed them, taught them, defended them from the cold and the hunger and the world, played the mother without ever having finished being a daughter. She married them off: she saw to the weddings of each one as a mother does, gathered the possible dowries, received the daughters- and sons-in-law, was godmother to the nephews and nieces as though they were grandchildren. She buried those who went: because in a large family and on a hard serra one dies, and Serafina buried siblings, and buried them as she had done everything, on her feet, without weeping in sight, tending the living while she wept within for the dead, if she still managed to weep within, which perhaps not even that. She held the house of the height for sixty years: the house of the parents, that she did not let fall, that she kept standing against every winter, as though to keep the house standing were to keep standing the memory of the parents the snow had taken.

And in sixty-three years, Neves realised it and it hurt her, no one had ever cared for Serafina. No one. Because she had never allowed it, of course, but also because no one had thought it necessary, because Serafina was the fortress, and to fortresses it does not occur to anyone that they might need care, and so, over a lifetime, everyone received from Serafina and no one gave to her, everyone leaned on her and no one supported her, everyone treated her as the wall that holds the house and no one remembered to ask the wall whether it was tired of holding.

There was, in those afternoons of convalescence, a conversation about Serafina's marriage that opened another layer. Because Serafina had married, when young, and been widowed early, and had children, and Neves, who was the granddaughter of one of those children, knew this but had never thought about what it had meant, and she asked. And Serafina told that she had married not out of love, but out of practical sense: she had married a man of the serra, a good man, who helped her raise the siblings, because with a man the house of the height held up better, and that the love, if there was any, came afterwards, slowly, or did not come at all, for she no longer even knew, because she had never allowed herself to love the husband as the enamoured love one another, she had always treated him as she treated everything, with the practicality of one who cannot feel. And that when the husband died, early, she wept him as she wept everything, on her feet, within, without tears in sight, and went on, raising the siblings and now also the children, the fortress enduring one more loss without allowing herself to feel it. And Neves realised that Serafina's ice had not frozen only the pain of the parents: it had frozen the whole capacity to love, it had made of Serafina a woman who cared for everyone and never loved anyone with the abandon of one who gives themselves, because to give oneself was to feel, and to feel was the luxury she could not allow herself, and so Serafina had crossed life fulfilling all the duties of love — to raise, to sustain, to protect, to care — without ever allowing herself the pleasure and the risk of love, which is to feel, to need, to depend, to give oneself. She had been the best of carers and had never been a lover, nor a daughter, nor even, at bottom, a sister: she had always been, and only, the one who endures.

There was a day when Neves found, in a chest, some old photographs, of the few there were in the house, and noticed a thing: that in none of them, of any age, did Serafina smile. She was always straight, serious, with a firm chin, with the face of one who endures, and Neves went through them all in search of one in which the grandmother smiled and found none, and realised that that absence of a smile over a lifetime was the exact portrait of the ice: a woman who had never allowed herself the lightness of a smile, because to smile was to lower one's guard, and she only knew how to show herself firm. She showed them to the grandmother, and asked why she never smiled, and Serafina looked at them, surprised, as though she had never noticed, and said, I do not know, my girl, to smile for a camera seems to me a thing of people who have an easy life, and I never had an easy life. And Neves promised herself that, before going down from the serra, she would take a photograph of the grandmother in which she smiled, and that that photograph, an old woman at last smiling, would be the proof that the winter had been worth it.

"You were never a girl, grandmother," said Neves, one afternoon, combing the grandmother's white hair, who now let herself be combed, one more care accepted. "You were a mother at eleven years old, without ever having finished being a daughter. You skipped a whole part of life, grandmother. The part of being cared for, of being small, of being able to weep, of having someone to tend to you. You never had that."

"I did not, my girl," said Serafina, and she already said these things without the dryness of before, with a new sadness that was, itself, a sign of the thaw, because to have sadness one must first feel, and Serafina was beginning to feel. "But I do not complain. There were those who had less. I at least had the siblings, had the house, had a work to do. There are those who are left truly alone. I was left alone with five mouths, which is a way of not being alone, even if it is the most tiring of all."

There was, in those conversations, a thing Serafina said that put together for Neves the grandmother's whole life in one image. She said: you know, my girl, I never felt like the other women. My whole life, at the festivals, at the weddings, at the funerals, beside the other women of the serra, I felt myself different, of another kind. They spoke of their husbands, of their childbirths, of their loves, of their weaknesses, of their longings, and I had none of that to say, or I had it and did not know how to say it, and I felt myself like a man in their midst, or like a thing that was neither quite woman nor quite man, a thing that was only strength, only work, only enduring. I never belonged to the world of women, Neves, with their confidences and their shared weaknesses, because I did not share weaknesses, I had no weaknesses, or did not let myself have them, and that always put me outside, always on the margin, admired but alone, like one who is at a banquet serving and never eating. And Neves realised, in that confidence, that Serafina's ice had cost her not only the feeling, but the belonging: that in not having weaknesses to share, Serafina had never entered the warm circle of women who confide their weaknesses to one another, had never had friends in the deep sense, only admirers, and that her solitude was also that, that of one who is too strong to belong to the lukewarm world of those who share their weaknesses.

"But you were cold, grandmother," said Neves, slowly, combing. "Your whole life. You were cold and could not feel it, and that is a way of being alone that is perhaps worse than being alone truly. To be surrounded by people who owe you their lives and to have no one who asks you whether you are cold. To be the one who warms all and the one whom no one warms. I think that, grandmother, is the deepest solitude there is: the solitude of one who is so strong that no one remembers that she too is a person."

And Serafina was silent, letting herself be combed, and at the end of a while said a thing, low, that was almost a confession and almost a surrender:

Neves noticed, in those weeks, a thing that said everything about the grandmother's life: that Serafina had, in the house, not a single photograph of herself, nor a single object that was only for pleasure, for delight, for comfort. There were photographs of the siblings, of the nephews and nieces, of the children; there were useful objects, tools, work clothes; but of Serafina, of Serafina the person, of the Serafina who might like a beautiful thing, have a caprice, a treat, a pleasure, there was no trace at all, as though that woman had never had tastes of her own, never wanted anything for herself, never been, at bottom, a person with desires, but only a function, a usefulness at the service of others. And Neves, one day, asked her: grandmother, what do you like? Not what is necessary to do, not what is useful. What do you like, only for liking it? And Serafina was left still at the question, genuinely still, as though she had never been asked it, as though the question were in a language she did not speak, and at the end of a long time she said, I do not know, my girl. I never thought of it. There was never time to like things. To like things is for one who can choose, and I never chose anything, I always did what was necessary. And Neves realised that that was perhaps the saddest part of all: that the grandmother, by dint of only doing what was necessary, had lost the very capacity to want, to like, to desire, that the fortress had not only frozen the pain, it had frozen also the liking, the pleasure, the desire, everything that makes of a life a life and not only a survival.

And Neves decided, from that day, that part of the work of the winter was that too: not only to teach the grandmother to be cared for, but to help her rediscover that she had the right to like things, to want things, to have small pleasures. She began with tiny things: she brought from the village a tea that smelled good, and made it for the grandmother, and asked her whether she liked it, and obliged her to notice whether she liked it, instead of drinking it for the sake of drinking. She put on music, the few times there was a way, and asked the grandmother whether it pleased her. She made food not only to feed, but to taste good, and asked the grandmother which she preferred. And she went on teaching the grandmother, sip by sip, dish by dish, to do a thing she had unlearned sixty-three years ago: to prefer, to like, to want, to have a taste of her own, which is the most basic form of existing as a person and not as a function, and that Serafina, the fortress, had sacrificed, along with everything else, in that winter of the eleven years.

"Do you know what I envied, my whole life, without ever saying it to anyone? I envied my siblings, when they were small and wept, and I warmed them. I envied them for being able to weep, for being able to be cold, for being able to be warmed. I, warming them, envied them, and I was ashamed of that envy, because I was the eldest, I was the strong one, I had no right to envy the small ones. But I envied them, my girl. Each time I warmed a weeping sibling, a part of me wanted to be that sibling, wanted to be small, wanted to weep, wanted there to be someone bigger than me to warm me. And there was not. I was the biggest. I was the only one. And the girl I was swallowed that envy with the rest, and never again let it out, and look, sixty-three years later, it is the first time I say it aloud: I envied my small siblings for being able to be cared for. What an ugly thing for a sister to say."

There was, in that afternoon of combing, a new intimacy between the two of them, and Neves realised that to comb the grandmother was, itself, a cure: that the touch, the simple physical care, the hands of one tending the hair of the other, did more than a thousand conversations, because Serafina's body, which had been sixty-three years without being touched with tenderness, without fuss, without affection, only with the utilitarian touch of work, rediscovered, under the granddaughter's combing hands, the touch that asks nothing, the touch that only cares, the touch that warms. And Serafina let herself be combed, with her eyes closed, and there was in her face an abandon that Neves had never seen in her, the abandon of one who gives themselves to the care of another, and Neves realised that that old body had a hunger for touch accumulated of sixty-three years, a hunger to be touched with tenderness that no one had satisfied because no one dared to touch the fortress with tenderness, and that to comb her hair, to make a fuss of her, to take her hand, were as important as all the words, because the ice was not only in the head, it was in the body, and the body thawed with touch, with affection, with hands that care, and not with reasons. And Neves came to touch the grandmother more, to embrace her, to take her hand, to comb her hair, and each touch was a little bit of ice melting, because there is a part of us that does not understand words, only understands hands, and that part, in Serafina, had been frozen sixty-three years for lack of hands that touched her not to put her to work.

"It is not ugly, grandmother," said Neves, and stopped combing, and embraced the grandmother from behind, with her face in her white hair. "It is not ugly at all. It is the most human thing there is. It is only a girl of eleven years wanting, at last, to be cared for. And it is time, grandmother. It is more than time. Let yourself be cared for now. I am here. Let yourself."
