## 12. I was always cold after all

The spring comes late to the serra, but it comes, and that year it came like a promise fulfilled, with the snow melting from the peaks to the valleys, with the water running everywhere, with the serra stripping off the white and dressing in green, and the house of the height, which had crossed the winter shut and cold, opened to the sun and to the thaw, and Serafina, within it, did the same.

She was another woman, and she was the same. She was the same Serafina, strong, of the serra, matriarch of the height; but she was a Serafina who had learned, that winter, to feel, to weep, to be cared for, and that new Serafina was, at the same time, more frail and more whole than the old one, more frail because she felt, and more whole because she was no longer only half a person, the half that endures, but the whole person, the one who endures and the one who surrenders, the one who cares and the one who is cared for, the one who gives warmth and the one who receives it.

And there was, that spring, a thing Neves realised about herself, and that was the other half of the story. She had come up to the serra frozen of her own ice, the misencounter of the city, the undone love, the failed work, the stuck life, and she had spent the winter so busy thawing the grandmother that she had almost forgotten her own cold. But the thaw of the grandmother had thawed her too, without her noticing, because in seeing the grandmother learn to feel, to ask, to let herself be cared for, to weep, Neves had learned the same things, and had realised that she, at twenty-six, was going by the same road as the grandmother, the road of one who endures alone and does not ask and does not weep and does not let themselves be cared for, and that she had there, in the grandmother, a warning, a mirror of what a person who spends their life freezing what they feel becomes: they become a fortress alone on a serra, admired and frozen, strong and unhappy. And Neves decided, that spring, not to reach seventy-four years old as the grandmother had reached them, needing a granddaughter and a whole winter to feel at last; she decided to learn now, young, what the grandmother had only learned old, and it was with that decision, more than with anything else, that Neves went down from the serra changed, having received from the grandmother the greatest of inheritances: not a house, not money, but the living warning of a whole life of ice, and the proof that the ice could melt, at any age, if there was someone to bring the warmth and patience to wait for the thaw.

The siblings came, in the spring, and Serafina told them what had happened that winter, and told them of the parents' grave, and took them there, and it was the first time the siblings, whom she had raised, wept the parents with the sister, all together, before the stone, and it was also a cure for them, who had grown up without parents and without a grave, and who discovered, in that grave Neves had had made, a place where they could at last go to weep those who had made them. And Amaro, the youngest, the one Serafina had practically borne, said to the sister, before the grave, a thing that changed the relationship of the two: let us care for you now, Serafina. Your whole life you cared for us, and you never let us pay. Let us now. Not as a debt. As love. Let your siblings, whom you raised, care for you in your old age. It is the one thing we want, and that you never let us do. And Serafina, who her whole life had refused the care of the siblings out of thinking that it was she who had to care for them, said yes, at last, and it was a yes that freed the siblings as much as her, because it gave them, at the end of a life, the possibility of loving the sister not only with distant gratitude, but with the near care of one who tends to someone who lets themselves be tended.

The siblings, and the nephews and nieces, and the children, organised themselves, that spring, without Serafina asking, to care for her, because Neves, before going down, spoke with them, and told them what she had realised: that the grandmother did not need to be left in peace in her strength, as they had always done, but to be cared for, and that to care for her was the only way to pay her what they owed her, and that she, now, after that winter, at last let them. And the family, which for sixty-three years had wanted to care for Serafina and had never known how, discovered, with relief, that after all it was simple: it was to come up to the house of the height, light her the fire, bring her food, keep her company, and let Serafina, at last, be cared for. They arranged turns, without fuss, so that the grandmother would never spend a winter alone again, so that there would always be someone on the height with her, and Serafina, who would have refused that tooth and nail a year before, accepted, because she had learned, that winter, that to accept the care of those who loved her was not to give them trouble: it was to give them the joy of loving her back, it was to close the circuit of a whole life in which she had only given and never received.

And Amaro, the youngest brother, the one she had practically borne, took for himself the task of coming up more often, and the two old ones, the sister and the brother she had raised, came to sit together by the hearth, on the winter afternoons, and Amaro, at last, instead of treating her as a saint, treated her as a sister: he told her things, scolded her when she did not wrap up, brought her his wife's socks and watched her put them on, and the two laughed at old things, and it was, for the two of them, a new old age, the old age of two siblings who at last treated one another as equals, after a life in which one owed the other a debt that separated them. The debt, at the end of sixty-three years, had at last turned into simple love, into company, into siblinghood, because Serafina, in letting herself be cared for, had freed the brother of the impossible debt and had given him, in exchange, the thing he had always wanted: a sister, and not a saint.

And there was, in the end, the phrase. The phrase the whole book walked toward, and that came one spring night, by the hearth, without anyone announcing it, in the way great things come, slowly and suddenly.

Serafina and Neves were by the hearth, on the last night before Neves went down to the city, the winter ended, and it was still cool, for on the serra the spring is cool, and Neves, out of the habit of the winter, put a blanket on the grandmother's knees. And Serafina, this time, did not refuse it: she took it, arranged it, kept it, and stayed a while looking at the fire, with the blanket on her knees, warm, cared for, and said, low, without drama, with a serenity that was that of one who arrived at last home after a long journey:

"You know, my girl, I said my whole life that I was never cold. And everyone believed it, and I myself believed it." She looked at the granddaughter, and there was in her eyes a new peace, and a brightness that was of tears but was not of sadness. "And after all I was always cold, Neves. Always. I was cold at eleven years old, in the snow, waiting for my parents. I was cold every winter of this serra. I was cold my whole life, within, a cold that never ended, and I never said it to anyone, not even to myself, because to say that I was cold was to admit that I was a person, and I did not let myself be a person, I let myself be only a fortress. And fortresses are not cold. But I was not a fortress, my girl. I was a woman. I was always only a woman, cold, like everyone. After all I was always cold. And it is well that I can say it to you, at last, before dying. It is well that I lived enough to thaw and to be able to say a thing so simple and that cost me seventy-four years: I am cold. Pass me the blanket, my girl. I am cold."

And after that phrase, by the hearth, on the last night, the two of them were silent a while, and Neves realised what the phrase had of immense: that to say after all I was always cold was not, for Serafina, a confession of weakness nor a late complaint. It was a reconciliation. It was the fortress making peace at last with the girl of eleven years who had always been cold and had never been able to say it; it was the old woman embracing the child she had been, the frozen child, and telling her, at last, it is all right, you could have been cold, you could have been afraid, you could have needed, you were not weak for that, you were a girl, and girls are cold, and you deserved that someone had warmed you. To say after all I was always cold was Serafina warming, at last, sixty-three years later, the girl she had been, giving her the blanket no one had given her at eleven years old, telling her the truth that would have saved her then: that to be cold is no shame, that to need is no weakness, that one can be strong and cold at the same time, that to endure and to feel are not enemies. The phrase was not the end of Serafina's strength: it was its completion, the strength becoming at last whole, capable not only of resisting but also of feeling, not only of enduring but also of resting, not only of warming others but also of letting herself be warmed.

And Neves passed her the blanket, and arranged it on her shoulders, and embraced the grandmother, and the two of them stayed by the hearth, on the last night, the old one warmed and the young one warming her, and outside the serra thawed in the moonlight, and the water ran, and everything that had been frozen, on the serra and in Serafina, came back to running, to living, to feeling, after a long winter, after a long life, after sixty-three years.

On the morning of the farewell, before Neves went down, the two of them had breakfast together, by the hearth, and it was a silent and full breakfast, of those in which one need not speak because everything has been said, or because what is left to say does not fit in words. And Serafina, at a certain point, got up, went to a chest, and took from it a thing she had kept, and gave it to Neves: it was a shawl, of serra wool, woven by herself long ago, a thick and warm shawl, of those that warm even the cold of the serra. And she said: take it, my girl. For the city. For when you are cold. — And she added, with a new smile, the smile she had learned that winter: — And when you are cold, Neves, hear me well, do not do as your grandmother did for sixty-three years: do not say you are not. Put on the shawl. Accept the warmth. Call on those who want to warm you. Do not wait for the seventy-four years to learn to let yourself be warmed, for it is too late, and life, my girl, is too long to spend cold pretending one is not. And Neves took the shawl, and embraced the grandmother, and realised that that shawl was the true inheritance, more than the house, more than anything: it was the grandmother's warmth made an object, and it was also the grandmother's warning made a present, take the warmth, accept it, do not do as I did. And she took it, and she was to use it in the city, and each time she put it on she would remember the grandmother and the lesson of the winter, and she would call on those who wanted to warm her instead of hiding shivering, because the grandmother had given her, in that shawl, not only warm wool, but the permission, which the grandmother herself had only received at seventy-four, to be cold and to let herself be warmed.

Neves went down to the city the next morning, and she went down changed, having received from the grandmother, in that winter when she had come to care and had stayed to be cared for too, more than she gave: having learned, from a woman of seventy-four years, that the strength that does not let itself be cared for is not strength, it is a prison, and that to ask, to feel, to surrender, to let oneself be warmed, is not weakness, it is the hardest courage of all, that of becoming a person again after a life of being stone. And she took to the city, to her own ice, to her own misencounter, the lesson of the grandmother: that one is always cold, that everyone is cold, and that life is not to be without cold, it is to have someone to whom one can, at last, say pass me the blanket.

Before going down, on the last morning, Neves did with the grandmother one last thing, and it was to go back to the crag of the first week, to the place on the slope where the parents used to come up, where Serafina passed silent for sixty-three years without ever stopping. That time they stopped. And Serafina, by the crag, with the granddaughter, looked at the path where the parents had never finished coming up, and instead of going on, as she had always done, she stayed, and said to them, to the path, to the serra, a thing low, a farewell she had put off her whole life, and Neves drew away a few steps to give her her moment, and saw the grandmother, from afar, speaking to the serra, speaking to the parents, making at last, in that place, the peace she had never made. And when Serafina came back to her, her face was wet and serene, and she said only, we can go, my girl, it is done, I have taken my leave, I can pass by here now without fleeing. And they went down, and Neves realised that the grandmother had, that morning, freed the last frozen place, the path of the parents, and that from then on she could pass by there, and through the whole serra, not fleeing the memory, but living with it, which is what mourning done allows: to live with the dead in peace, instead of fleeing them in ice.

And the serra, which had made of Serafina the legend of the woman who did not feel the cold, learned, that spring, of the change, and received it in a way that surprised Neves: with relief, and with a new tenderness. Because the serra, which had admired Serafina from afar sixty-three years, without ever knowing how to draw near to her, discovered, on learning that Serafina at last let herself be cared for, that it could at last draw near, that it could come up to the height and bring her a broth and stay an afternoon, that the fortress had at last opened the door. And they went on coming up, the neighbours, the women of the serra, those who owed her and those who only admired her, and the house of the height, which had been for sixty-three years the solitary house of the woman of steel, filled, that spring and in those that came, with people, with visits, with company, with warmth, and Serafina, who had lived so alone, discovered, at the end of her life, what it was to be surrounded, to be visited, to be part of the serra and not only its distant symbol. And it was, Neves realised, as though the whole serra had been sixty-three years waiting to be able to care for that woman, and had only not done it because she did not let them, and now that she let them, the serra rushed in, with the haste of one who has a love kept long and at last finds where to put it.

And in the house of the height stayed Serafina, older and more whole, letting herself be cared for by the siblings and the nephews and nieces and the serra, and lighting, from then on, the hearth for herself too, and sitting by it, and being cold, and letting them warm her, the woman who after all had always been cold and who learned, in the end, late but in time, the simplest and most human thing there is: to let the warmth, which had always been there at hand, at last reach her.
