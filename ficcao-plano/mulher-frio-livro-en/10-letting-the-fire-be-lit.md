## 10. Letting the fire be lit

After the night of the fever and the first weeping, Serafina healed, slowly, and the convalescence was, itself, the learning of the book: because it was in convalescing, gesture by gesture, that Serafina learned, at seventy-four years old, to be cared for, and it was the granddaughter who led her, as one teaches a child to walk, by the hand, one step at a time.

And the steps were small, and each one cost Serafina a world, and each one, conquered, melted one more bit of the ice.

And there was, in that learning, a joy that Neves did not expect, and that relieved what the subject had of heavy: because to see the grandmother learn, gesture by gesture, to let herself be cared for, was also to see born in her small joys that she had never allowed herself. There was the joy, almost childlike, of the first time Serafina, by the hearth, warmed, with the blanket, closed her eyes and said, low, this feels good, and it was the first time in her life that she admitted that a thing felt good to her only for feeling good, without being useful, without being necessary, only for pleasure. There was the joy of the grandmother discovering the taste of a sweet thing, of a warm tea drunk slowly, of the warmth of the fire on her feet, tiny pleasures that everyone knows from childhood and that Serafina knew for the first time at seventy-four years old, with the astonishment of one who discovers, old, that the world had all these small sweetnesses and that she had spent her life refusing them out of thinking they were not for her. And Neves realised that she was witnessing a second childhood, or the first that the grandmother had never had: an old woman learning to like things, to allow herself pleasures, to discover that life was not only to endure, that it had, beneath the enduring, sweetnesses she had never tasted, and that she was still in time, in the winter of life, to taste some.

She learned to let the granddaughter light her the hearth. It was a tiny gesture, but for Serafina, who had sixty-three years lit the fire for others and never for herself, to let them light the fire for her, and to sit by it, and to warm herself, was an enormous surrender. There was one morning, of the first ones, when Neves lit the fire and called the grandmother to it, and Serafina came, and sat down, and at the end of a minute got up, out of habit, to go and see to something or other, and Neves told her, with sweetness but with firmness, sit down, grandmother, there is nothing to see to, today the only thing you have to do is warm yourself. And Serafina sat down again, and got up again, and Neves sat her down again, and it was almost comic, the two of them in that game, the old one wanting to get up to serve and the young one sitting her down to warm her, and at the end of a few days of that game Serafina began, at last, to manage to stay seated by the fire more than a minute, and it was a victory as great as any battle, a woman of seventy-four years learning to stay seated by the fire without getting up to serve, learning that she had the right to simply warm herself, without doing anything in return, without deserving it through work. At first she sat by the fire with a visible guilt, like one who usurps a place that is not theirs, and Neves had to reassure her, stay there, grandmother, warm yourself, the fire is for you, today the fire is for you. And Serafina, slowly, went on learning to stay by the fire, to let herself be warmed, and Neves saw, day after day, the grandmother draw nearer to the fire, draw away from the cold window, surrender to the warmth, and each centimetre the grandmother drew nearer to the fire was a centimetre of ice melted.

She learned to keep the blanket. The same blanket she had refused in December, in January she had accepted in bed out of illness, and in February, convalescent, she began to accept it awake, by the hearth, and to keep it, and to like it, and there was one afternoon when Neves, on coming into the room, found the grandmother asleep in the chair, by the hearth, with the blanket on her knees, and stopped at the door looking, moved, because that image, an old woman sleeping warm with a blanket by the fire, was the most banal image in the world in any other house, and was, in that house, in the house of the woman who was never cold, a miracle, the proof of a thaw, a fortress that at last let itself be warmed and rested.

And she learned, at last, to put on the socks. The socks that Amaro had brought, and that Serafina had laid aside as she had laid aside sixty-three years of the siblings' offerings, Neves went to fetch them, one cold night, and instead of offering them and hearing the usual no, she knelt by the grandmother, by the hearth, and put them on her herself, slowly, without asking leave, and Serafina, who would have refused if they had offered them to her, did not know how to refuse feet that were already being shod, and let her, and was left with the brother's socks on her feet, warm, and Neves told her, they are from uncle Amaro, grandmother, he brings them to you every year and you never put them on, and he goes away sad to see the socks laid aside. Put them on, grandmother. It is not for you, it is for him. Let him warm you, even if it is only your feet, which is what he knows how to do, which is the way he has of paying you the life you gave him. And Serafina, on hearing that to put on the socks was a present she gave the brother, and not only a care she received, managed, at last, to accept them, because it was still easier for her to accept a care if it was, at the same time, a way of giving, and Neves realised that and used it, and it was so, by the door of giving the brother the joy of warming her, that she managed to get the grandmother, for the first time in sixty-three years, to wear the socks they brought her. And she sent word to Amaro, by his son's telephone, that the sister was wearing his socks, and old Amaro, in the village, on learning it, wept from gladness, because it was the first time in his life that a present of his to the sister had been accepted, and it was, for him, like paying at last a little of the impossible debt, and it was, for Serafina, one more crystal of ice melted, and it was, for Neves, the victory she had promised herself: the grandmother with the socks on, the circuit of love at last closed.

She learned, also, to accept the food Neves made her without correcting it, without saying it is good but my mother made it another way, without getting up to remake it her way. And it was harder than it seems, because the kitchen had been, for sixty years, the last redoubt of Serafina's control, the place where she ruled absolutely, and to let another person cook, and to eat what that person made, and to like it, or at least not to correct it, was to surrender one more parcel of the kingdom. Neves cooked her way, the city way, things the grandmother did not know, and at first Serafina ate with distrust, and said that in her time one did not eat so, but slowly she went on surrendering to the new flavours, went on allowing herself to like things she had not made herself, and it was, that too, a thaw: to let another person feed her, nourish her, give her food, which is the most ancient and deepest form of care, the first a mother gives a child, and the one Serafina, who had fed half the serra, had never again received since she had a mother, since before the eleven years. To let the granddaughter give her food was to let return a thing from before the Great Winter, from before the ice, the warmth of being fed by one who loves us, and Serafina, sip by sip, dish by dish, went on allowing herself that old warmth, and went on remembering, without saying it, a thing she had forgotten for sixty-three years: the taste of being a daughter.

She learned to let the granddaughter see to things. The house of the height, which had been for sixty years the kingdom of Serafina, where only she ruled and only she worked, became, in that convalescence, a house of two, and Serafina had to learn the very hard thing of seeing another person doing her tasks and not getting up to take them from her hands. She had to learn to let the granddaughter cook, and to eat what the granddaughter cooked without saying it is good but I made it another way. She had to learn to let the granddaughter tend the animals, the firewood, the house, and to stay seated, by the hearth, with the blanket, being cared for, and it was among the hardest things for her, because to stay seated while another worked was, for Serafina, a betrayal of everything she had been, but she went on obliging herself, with the granddaughter's help, and each time she stayed seated and let the granddaughter see to things, she melted one more bit of the ice.

And there went on happening, over February, the thing Neves had foreseen on the first night of snow, the deep thing: as Serafina let herself be warmed from outside, she began to feel, for the first time in sixty-three years, the cold she had within. Because the ice, in melting, does not melt only outward: it melts inward too, and thaws what was frozen, and Serafina, in learning to feel the warmth of the hearth and the blanket and the granddaughter's care, began also to feel, by contrast, the enormous cold she had carried her whole life without knowing it, the cold of sixty-three years without being warmed, the cold of the girl of eleven years in the snow, the cold of a whole life of ice. And that feeling hurt, hurt more than the ice, because the ice was anaesthesia and the thaw was the pain coming back, and there were days when Serafina, on feeling at last the cold within, almost preferred the ice, almost wanted to freeze again, because to feel hurt. But Neves was there, and held her, and reminded her that the pain of the thaw was good, that it was life coming back, that it is worth more to feel the cold and be able to be warmed than to feel nothing and be, within, dead. And Serafina, slowly, with relapses, with days of wanting to freeze again, went on enduring the thaw, went on learning to feel, went on letting life, with its pain and its warmth, come back to a body that had switched it off sixty-three years ago.

There were setbacks, of course, because the thaw is never straight. There were days when Serafina, ashamed of how much she had let herself melt, hardened again, got up to see to things, refused the blanket, said she was already well, that this of being tended had already lasted too long. And Neves learned not to despair at the setbacks, to understand them as part of the thaw, like the snow that melts by day and freezes again by night before melting for good, and instead of fighting the grandmother's setbacks, she accompanied them, waited for them to pass, and when they passed, she offered the care again, without censure, without giving importance to the relapse, with the patience of the snow. And she went on learning, herself, from those setbacks of the grandmother, a thing about the cure that was going to serve her for her own life: that no one cures in a straight line, that the cure is made of advances and setbacks, that to draw back is not to fail, it is to rest from the effort of advancing, and that what matters is not to not draw back, it is, after each setback, to advance again, to try again, to let oneself be cared for once more. The grandmother drew back and advanced, drew back and advanced, and with each cycle she advanced a little more than she drew back, and it was so, in that going and coming, that the ice of sixty-three years went on, slowly, melting.

And Neves realised, in those setbacks, a new tenderness for the grandmother and for herself: that to be hard on oneself in the setbacks is the worst thing there is, because the shame of having drawn back makes the person shut themselves away still more, and that the only way to help someone cure themselves — or to cure oneself — is compassion for the setbacks, patience with the relapses, the offering of the care again without censure after each time it is refused. The grandmother, who had been hard on herself her whole life, was learning, with the granddaughter, to be gentle with herself in the setbacks, and Neves, who was hard on herself in the city, was learning the same, and the two of them, in that learning to be gentle with their own weaknesses, met, the old one and the young one, in the middle of the road of the thaw.

"Do you know what is strangest, my girl?" said Serafina, one afternoon, by the hearth, with the blanket, feeling the warmth of the fire on her face. "It is that this, this warmth, was always here. The hearth, the blanket, the care. It was I who did not let myself draw near. I had all this in front of me, sixty years, and stayed at the cold window, looking from afar, thinking it was not for me. And after all it was only to draw near. It was only to let. It was only to say yes to the warmth that was here the whole time, waiting for me, and that I refused my whole life out of thinking I had no right to it. What a waste, Neves. What a waste of warmth, a whole life refusing the warmth that was here at hand."
