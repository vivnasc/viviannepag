## 11. The weeping of sixty years

The grave was made in March, when the snow began to abate and the paths opened, and it was Neves who saw to it, in secret at first, with the Keeper and with a stonemason of the village, and it was a simple stone, in the cemetery of the serra, with the names of Serafina's parents engraved, and beneath them a line that Neves chose and that the Keeper approved: *they were lost on the serra they loved; here remain their name and the longing of their own.* A grave without bodies, but with names, a grave where, at the end of sixty-three years, Serafina was going to be able, at last, to weep her parents.

It cost Neves to take the grandmother to the cemetery, and it cost in a way she did not expect: the grandmother, the moment she realised where they were going, wanted to draw back, with a resistance that was almost panic, I do not go to the cemetery, my girl, I have nothing there, my parents have no grave, what would I go to the cemetery for. It was the last defence of the fortress, the refusal to go to the place where the mourning awaited her, and Neves had to be firm and sweet at the same time, to tell her, they have a grave now, grandmother, I had it made, they have a stone with their names, come and see, come only to see, and if you do not want to stay, we will come away at once. And she took her, by the hand, up the serra, to the cemetery, and the grandmother went trembling, and it was not from cold, it was from fear, the fear of facing at last the loss she had put off sixty-three years, and Neves realised that she was taking the grandmother not to a stroll, but to a surgery of the soul, and held her hand more tightly, and said, I go with you, grandmother, I do not leave you alone, and they went.

And Serafina, on arriving, on seeing the stone, on reading the names of the parents engraved, on realising what the granddaughter had done for her, stood a long time still, in silence, before the grave she had never had, and Neves, beside her, held herself back so as not to speak, because she realised that that was the moment the whole winter had walked toward, and that it should not be spoiled with words.

And Serafina wept.

She wept as she had never wept, as she had not wept at eleven years old, in the snow, because at eleven she could not; as she had not wept in sixty-three years, because she had not known how nor allowed herself; she wept at last, before the stone with the names of the parents, the mourning of a whole life, and it was a weeping that seemed to have no end, a weeping that came from very deep, from very far, from very long ago, the dammed-up weeping of sixty-three years bursting all at once, and Serafina, standing before the grave, bent with the force of that weeping, and would have fallen if Neves had not held her, and Neves held her, embraced her, supported her, and it was that the image that sealed the book: the woman who had saved everyone, who had endured everyone, who had held everyone for sixty years, at last held by one, at last supported, at last weeping in someone's arms instead of enduring on her feet and alone.

"Father," said Serafina, before the stone, through the weeping, and she said it with the voice of a girl of eleven years who had waited sixty-three to be able to say it. "Mother. I waited for you. I stayed up there waiting for you, and you did not come, and I held the little ones, I did everything you would do, I did not let them die, I raised them, married them off, held the house, I did everything, father, I did everything you would have wanted me to do, and I never wept you because there was nowhere, and now there is, now there is a stone with your names, and I can at last weep you, and tell you that I missed you, sixty-three years of missing you, and that I was cold, father, I was so cold that winter and in all those that came, and I never could say it to anyone, and I say it now to you: I was cold. Your girl was cold her whole life, and she was strong as you would have wanted, but she was cold, and she is tired, and she came here, at last, to tell you that she weeps for you, and that she forgives you for having died, and that she forgives herself for having survived, and that it is time to rest."

And there was, in the midst of the weeping, before the stone, the moment when Serafina told the parents the thing she had carried for sixty-three years, the guilt, and laid it there, on the grave, where it should be laid. Forgive me, she said, through the weeping, it was I who sent you down, it was I who threw the tantrum, it was I, and I carried this my whole life, I thought I had killed you, I thought I deserved no warmth for having sent you into the snow. And she was a moment silent, and then, as though she heard an answer that only she heard, or as though she at last gave herself the answer she needed, she said, lower: but I was a girl. You were the parents. It was not my tantrum that killed you, it was the snow, and the snow has no fault, and neither had I, I was eleven years old and I wanted a thing, like all children. I forgive myself. At the end of sixty-three years, I forgive myself. And you, from where you are, will want me to forgive myself, for no parent wants a child punishing themselves their whole life over a tantrum. There. It is done. I have laid it here, the guilt, beside you. I no longer carry it. And Neves, hearing the grandmother forgive herself at last before the grave, realised that that was the true thaw, deeper than the tears: it was the fortress letting go not only of the pain, but of the guilt that had sustained it, the penance of cold she had imposed on herself for a crime she had not committed, and that in forgiving herself, at last, Serafina authorised herself, for the first time in sixty-three years, to deserve warmth, to deserve rest, to deserve to be cared for, because she no longer had to punish herself, because the guilt, the absurd and crushing guilt of the girl of eleven years, had at last been laid there, on the stone, beside the parents, where it always should have been.

And Neves, holding the grandmother before the grave, wept too, and the two women stayed there, embraced, weeping, the old one and the young one, and the cemetery of the serra, with the last snow melting in the March sun, was witness to the oldest and most put-off mourning of the whole serra, the mourning of a girl of eleven years who, at seventy-four, at last could do it.

And there came also, to the cemetery, without being called, Amaro, and the others who still remained of the siblings, or the children of those who had already gone, because the news that a stone had been put for the parents ran the serra, and the family, who had never had anywhere to weep the grandparents and great-grandparents lost in the snow, gathered there, that March morning, around the new stone, and it was the first time the whole family wept together those the snow had taken, and the mourning, which had been Serafina's alone for sixty-three years, became at last a mourning of all, shared, and therefore lighter, because the shared pain is the only one that can be borne. And Amaro, beside the sister, before the stone, took her hand, a thing he had never done, and instead of treating her as a saint he treated her, at last, as a sister, we wept together, sister, did we? Today we weep together, for you held us all when they went and we never let you weep, and today it is we who hold you. And Serafina, held by the hand of the brother she had raised, let herself weep before him, and it was the first time in sixty-three years that the siblings saw Serafina weep, and instead of that terrifying them, as it had terrified the three-year-old Amaro on that old night, it comforted them, because they were no longer children who needed to see her strong, they were old men who needed, at last, to be able to support her, and the weeping of Serafina, which at eleven years old would have taken the hope from the siblings, at seventy-four gave them, at last, the whole sister, the sister who felt, the sister who let herself be loved.

The weeping lasted a long time, and Neves let it last, because she realised that that weeping had sixty-three years to come out and that it should not be hurried. And as it lasted, Neves noticed a thing: that Serafina's weeping went on changing, that it had begun as a weeping of pain, deep, torn, the weeping of the lost parents, and that it went on becoming, over the minutes, a different weeping, lighter, almost of relief, the weeping of one who lets go at last of a weight they have carried their whole life. It was as though, in weeping the parents, Serafina were also weeping everything else, all the mournings she had never done, that of the husband, that of the siblings she had buried, that of the girl who never was, that of the whole life of ice, and that that great weeping, in bursting, carried with it not only the pain, but the ice itself, melting it, washing it, leaving in its place a new thing, a gentleness, a humanity, a woman at last capable of feeling. And Neves realised that she was witnessing a late birth: the birth, at seventy-four years old, of the person Serafina had never let herself be, the person who feels, who weeps, who lets herself be supported, and that that birth, like all of them, came with pain and with water, but that it was, at bottom, a joy, the joy of a life that begins again when it already seemed too late.

The siblings, on learning of it, came, and the news that Serafina had wept, the Serafina who never wept, ran the serra and was received not with pity but with a kind of collective relief, because the serra, which had made of Serafina the legend of the strength that does not bend, felt, on learning that she had at last bent, that she had at last wept, not that its heroine had fallen, but that she had at last become human, and that that, at bottom, was a liberation for all, because if even Serafina could weep, then to weep was not a weakness, it was a thing that even the strong do, and the whole serra, without saying it, breathed a little better on learning that the woman who was never cold had at last admitted it, because it gave them all, the other strong ones of the serra, the others who endured in silence, the permission for them too, one day, to be able to feel.

When they came back to the house of the height, that afternoon, Serafina was exhausted like one who made a great journey, and it was true, she had made the greatest of journeys, that of sixty-three years of return to a pain she had never been able to feel. And at home, by the hearth, with the blanket, she let herself be cared for by the granddaughter with no resistance at all, for the first time with no guilt at all, and there was in her a new thing, a lightness, the lightness of one who has laid down at last a weight they had carried so long they no longer even knew they carried it. She had wept the parents. She had done the mourning. She had said I was cold. And the ice of sixty-three years, which had been protecting and imprisoning her, had at last melted, and what was left in its place was not weakness, as Serafina had always feared: it was a rediscovered humanity, a woman who came back to feeling, with all that feeling has of pain and of warmth, after a whole life anaesthetised.

It was at that time, in the weeks after the weeping in the cemetery, that Neves fulfilled the promise she had made herself: she took a photograph of the grandmother in which she smiled. It was not on purpose, it was not posed; it was one afternoon by the hearth, with Amaro up there telling an old story, a joke from the time when they were small, and Serafina laughed, laughed truly, with her whole body, a thing Neves had never seen in her, and Neves, who had the camera at hand, caught her: the grandmother laughing, by the hearth, with the blanket on her knees, at seventy-four years old and a new lightness in her face, the first photograph of Serafina's life in which she smiled. And when Neves showed it to her, Serafina stayed looking at her own face laughing, astonished, like one who sees a stranger, and said, is this me? I never saw myself so. And Neves said, it is you, grandmother, you laughing, and I am going to take this to the city, and I am going to put it in a place where I see it every day, to remind me that one can learn to laugh even old, even after a whole life without laughing. And Serafina asked for a copy, a thing she had never asked of anything, she asked for a copy of the photograph of her laughing to put at home, and it was one more request, one more crystal of ice melted, and it was, for Neves, the photographic proof of the winter: the before and the after of a woman, from the ice to the smile, in a portrait.

"Thank you, my girl," said Serafina to the granddaughter, that night, by the hearth. "For the grave. For this winter. For having taught me, old as I am, a thing I should have learned as a girl and that no one taught me: that I too can be cold. That I too can be cared for. That I too am a person, and not only a fortress. You came to the serra thinking you came to be cared for by me, and it was you who cared for me, and you made me, at the end of seventy-four years, become a person again. There is no way to pay a thing like that. It is the greatest debt of my life, and it is the first I am glad to owe, because it is a debt of love, and the debts of love, I learned this winter, are not paid: they are thanked for, and carried in the heart."
