## 3. The siblings

Serafina's siblings, the ones she had raised that winter and in those that followed, were now old, scattered across the serra and the village and the world, and from time to time one of them came up to the house of the height to visit the eldest sister, and Neves witnessed one of those visits, and it was there that she realised a thing about the grandmother that the legend did not tell: the price of having saved.

Before describing the visit, it must be said what those siblings were, for Serafina: they were the work of her life, the proof that it had been worth it, the reason for everything. Serafina had not had, in life, projects of her own, dreams of her own, ambitions of her own; she had had the siblings, and then the children, and her work had been to keep them alive, to raise them, to see them grow, and when she looked back, at sixty-three years of life, what she saw was not her life, it was theirs: the lives she had saved and made grow, the branches that had sprouted from those four small ones she had not let die in the snow. And there was in this a grandeur and a sadness at the same time: the grandeur of a woman whose life had been entirely for others, and the sadness of a woman who, for that, had not had a life of her own, who had diluted herself in such a way in the others that she scarcely existed any more as a separate person, only as the root from which the others came. The siblings were Serafina's pride and they were also her sink: in her, they had fed, and in feeding them she had spent herself, given, drained, until only the fortress remained, the function, the root, without the tree of her own that she could have been if someone, some day, had let her grow for herself instead of only for others.

The youngest of the siblings came up, the one Serafina had practically borne, who at the date of the Great Winter was three years old and who of a mother knew only the sister. He was called Amaro, was now an old man of sixty-odd, and he came up to the house of the height with his son, who brought him by car as far as the car reached, and did the rest on foot, slowly, leaning on a stick, to see the sister. And the visit was, for Neves, a lesson about a thing she had never thought about: about how hard it is to love one who saved us.

Because Amaro loved Serafina, one could see, he loved her with an intensity that was almost painful to see. But he did not love her as one loves a sister: he loved her as one loves a divinity to whom one owes one's life, with reverence, with gratitude, with a kind of respectful fear, and that way of loving, Neves realised, isolated Serafina instead of warming her. Amaro, before the sister, was not a brother: he was an eternal debtor, someone who owed her his very existence and who did not know how to pay a debt like that, and who therefore treated her with the respectful distance with which one treats a sacred creditor. He did not make a fuss of her. He did not play with her. He did not treat her with the confidence and the equality of siblings. He treated her as a saint, and to saints one prays, one does not take them in one's arms.

"Without you, Serafina," said Amaro, at a certain point, with the wet eyes of an old man, and he said it like one who repeats a phrase said a thousand times over a lifetime, "without you we would all have died that winter. I do not remember, I was small, but the others told me. You were eleven years old and you held us all. We owe you our lives, sister. All of us. All the children we had, the grandchildren, all these people who came from us, exist because you, at eleven years old, did not let us die. It is a debt that is not paid."

And Serafina, on hearing this, did what she always did: she shrugged her shoulders, made light of it, said she had done what anyone would do, that it was nothing, that he should leave that be. But Neves, who was observing, saw a thing cross the grandmother's face, quick, at once hidden: she saw that that debt, that gratitude, that "we owe you our lives", instead of warming Serafina, saddened her, isolated her still more, because it put her, once more, in the place of the one who saves and never in the place of the one who is saved, of the one who gives and never of the one who receives, of the one who is above and never beside. Amaro, in thanking her for his life, was, without knowing it, confirming her in her exile: telling her you are the one who saved us, and therefore you are not one of us, you are another thing, you are the fortress, and to fortresses one gives thanks, one does not take them in one's arms nor ask them whether they are cold.

During the visit, Neves noticed a thing that moved her: that Amaro, old as he was, had brought the sister a present, and that the present was a pair of thick wool socks, made by his wife, so that Serafina would not be cold on her feet in the winter. And that Serafina thanked him, and laid the socks aside, and did not put them on, and that Amaro, on taking his leave, looked at the laid-aside socks with a small sadness, the sadness of one who brought a present he knew was not going to be used, but who brought it all the same, every year, because it was the only way he had of caring for a sister who did not let herself be cared for. Neves realised, in that detail of the socks, the silent tragedy of that family: that the siblings wanted to care for Serafina, wanted it with all their soul, brought her socks, wraps, food, wanted to pay her a little of the much they owed her, and that Serafina, in refusing everything, in not putting on the socks, in not using the wraps, not only deprived herself of the care but deprived the siblings of the one thing they wanted, which was to care for her. Serafina's refusal did not hurt only Serafina: it hurt the siblings, who were left with the love ungiven, with the socks unput-on, with the debt unpaid, condemned to a gratitude that could never become care because the sister did not let it.

And there was, in the laid-aside and unput-on socks, a portrait of sixty-three years: how many socks, how many wraps, how many broths, how many offers of care the siblings would have made Serafina over a lifetime, and how many she would have laid aside, with a dry thank you, without ever using them, and how much love would have stayed so, ungiven, between a sister who only knew how to give and siblings who only wanted, once in their lives, to repay. Neves took the socks, after Amaro went, and kept them, and promised herself that before the end of the winter she would see the grandmother with those socks on, and that that, to make the grandmother put on her brother's socks, would be a victory as great as any other, because it would be to close, at last, a circuit of love that had been open for sixty-three years, the love of the siblings that never reached Serafina because Serafina did not let it reach.

When Amaro went, at the end of the afternoon, leaning on his son and on the stick, Neves stayed at the door with the grandmother watching him go down, and dared a question:

"Grandmother, they thank you so much. Everyone owes you so much. But... did anyone ever care for you? You cared for them all. But you, grandmother, who cared for you?"

And Serafina was a moment silent, watching the brother go down the serra, and then said, with a naturalness that was the saddest thing Neves had heard, because it had in it no complaint at all, only the noting of an old fact:

"Me? My girl, I never needed anyone to care for me. I am the one who cares. I always was. One cannot be both things. One who cares, cares; one who is cared for, is cared for. I happened to fall on the side of caring, and it is well so, for someone had to be it."

"But why can one not be both things, grandmother?" insisted Neves, slowly. "I care for people and I let them care for me. Most people do both things. They give and receive. Why do you think you can only give?"

And Serafina was a moment thinking, and it was the first time Neves saw her really think about the question, instead of dispatching it with a set phrase. And then she said, slowly: because one who receives, my girl, is left at the mercy. When a person needs another, they are left in their hands, depend on them, and if that person fails, or dies, or does not come — and her voice hesitated an instant on that does not come —, the one who needed is left helpless. I learned, when small, that to need is dangerous, that to depend on someone is to hand that person the power to destroy us, because if they fail, and people fail, my girl, people go down to the serra and do not come back, the one who depended on them dies. And so I decided not to depend on anyone, ever, so as never again to be left at the mercy of someone failing me. To care is safe: one controls it. To be cared for is to give oneself, and to give oneself is to risk being failed, and I was already failed once, at eleven years old, and I did not risk it again. It is for that that I only give, Neves. It is not virtue. It is fear. It is a way of never again being left waiting for someone who may not come.

And in that answer, the longest and the deepest the grandmother had given until then, Neves realised the whole mechanism: that Serafina did not refuse the care out of thinking herself superior, but to protect herself, that her not-needing was an armour against the possibility of being abandoned again, that each refused blanket was the girl of eleven saying I am not going to give myself to anyone who can fail me as my parents failed me. And she realised that for the grandmother to learn to be cared for she would have to learn, first, to risk, to trust that this time the one who cared for her was not going to go down the serra and not come back, and that that, the trust, was the hardest thing to give back to one who had lost it so early and in so definitive a way.

There was, after that conversation, a thing Neves did not say to the grandmother but thought about much: that the grandmother, in not letting anyone care for her, was not only protecting herself from the risk of being failed — she was also, without meaning to, doing to the siblings and the family exactly what had most hurt her. Because what had most hurt Serafina, at eleven years old, had been no one caring for her, no one asking her whether she was well, having been left alone with all the weight. And Serafina, in refusing now the care of the siblings, was leaving them with a similar weight: the weight of a debt they could not pay, of a love they could not give, of a gratitude that had nowhere to become care. She was doing to those who loved her what had been done to her: leaving them out, not letting them in, condemning them to love her from afar without ever being able to reach her. And Neves realised that the cure of Serafina was not only for Serafina: it was for the whole family, for the siblings who needed to be able to care for her as much as she needed to be cared for, because the love that has nowhere to give itself hurts the one who has it to give, and the whole serra had gone, for sixty-three years, with love for Serafina and nowhere to put it, because Serafina did not let it reach.

And she went into the house, into her cold house with the window open, and Neves stayed at the door a while more, watching Amaro's shape disappear into the serra, thinking of that "someone had to be it", and realising that the grandmother truly believed that, believed that there were two kinds of people, those who care and those who are cared for, and that she was irremediably of the first, and that to be cared for was not a thing that had been denied her: it was a thing she thought did not belong to her, that was of another kind, of the kind of the weak, of the small, of those who can stop. And Neves realised that the work of the winter, if there was work, was not to convince the grandmother to let herself be cared for: it was to convince her, first, that she had the right to be, that she was not of a separate kind, that the fortress was also a person, and that people, all of them, even the strongest, even those who saved others, have the right to, once in their lives, be saved.

That night, after Amaro's visit, Neves stayed thinking of the brother's phrase, we owe you our lives, it is a debt that is not paid, and realised what was most cruel in that debt: that a debt that is not paid frees no one, it binds both sides. It binds the debtor, who carries for ever a gratitude they have no way of settling; and it binds the creditor, who is left for ever above, untouchable, in the position of one who gave without ever receiving. The only way to free the two, Neves realised, was for Serafina to let, at last, the siblings pay her a little of the debt, not in money nor in words, but in care: to let them care for her in old age was the only coin with which that debt could, at last, begin to be paid, and in refusing their care, Serafina was not being generous, she was keeping the siblings bound to an eternal debt, denying them the relief of being able, at last, to repay. And Neves decided that part of the work of the winter was that: to make the grandmother realise that to let herself be cared for was neither weakness nor selfishness, it was a present she gave the siblings, the present of freeing them, at last, from a debt of sixty-three years, by letting them pay it in the only way a debt of life is paid, which is with cared-for love, with hands that tend, with the opportunity, at last, to be the ones to warm the one who warmed them.
