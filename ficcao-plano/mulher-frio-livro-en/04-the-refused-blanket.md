## 4. The refused blanket

The first real snow of that winter fell in December, at night, and Neves woke in the morning to the serra white, with the whole world covered and silent in the way only the snow silences the world, and she had, at first, the wonder of city folk before the snow, and then, over the day, as the cold sharpened, the frightened respect of one who realises that the beautiful snow is also the snow that kills, the same that had taken the grandmother's parents sixty-three years before.

Neves spent that first day of snow at the window, in wonder, watching the white world, and Serafina, at a certain point, came to stand beside her, and Neves expected the grandmother to share the wonder, but Serafina looked at the snow with other eyes, the eyes of one who knows it from death, and said, low, it is beautiful, the snow, my girl, but do not trust it. The snow is the most beautiful and most treacherous thing there is. It covers everything in white, everything becomes lovely, silent, it seems a postcard, and beneath all that white is death waiting for one who trusts it. The snow kills smiling, Neves. It kills people who let themselves be deceived by its beauty. So, on the serra, one learns to distrust what is too beautiful, because what is too beautiful, on the serra, is usually what kills. And Neves kept that, the snow kills smiling, and realised that the grandmother had learned, in that old winter, not only not to feel the cold, but to distrust beauty, comfort, sweetness, everything that was soft, because on the serra the soft deceives and the hard saves, and Serafina had made herself hard to survive a land where the soft was a trap. And she realised that part of the grandmother's ice was this: a deep distrust of everything that was good, sweet, warm, comfortable, because all that, in the winter of the eleven years, had been the siren song of the snow, the invitation to lower one's guard, to lie down in the white and let oneself go, which is how the snow kills, giving a good desire to rest. Serafina had learned to refuse the good desire to rest, because that desire, in the snow, is death, and she had never again managed to distinguish the good desire to rest that kills from the good desire to rest that cures, and so she refused both, that of the snow and that of the blanket, with the same old fear.

And it was on that first night of snow that the gesture happened that was the heart of the whole winter, and it was a small gesture, a blanket.

The two of them were by the hearth, at night, the snow falling outside, and Neves, even near the fire, was cold, the damp cold of the serra that gets into the bones, and she went to fetch a blanket for herself, and then, on seeing the grandmother sitting a little apart from the fire, in short sleeves, as always, near the window where the cold came in, she had an impulse of a granddaughter, an impulse of simple tenderness: she went to fetch another blanket, and laid it on the grandmother's knees, slowly, carefully, with love.

And Serafina, the moment she felt the blanket on her knees, took it off. Not brusquely, not with anger: with sweetness, but with a firmness of steel, like one who pushes away a thing that does not belong to them, that is in the wrong place.

"I do not need it, my girl," she said, and folded the blanket, and held it back out to Neves. "Keep it for yourself, for it is you who are cold. I am well."

"But grandmother, it is so cold. You are there in short sleeves, by the open window. Keep the blanket, do it for me."

"I was never cold, Neves. I have told you. The blanket is for one who feels the cold. I do not feel it. It would only be a hindrance on my knees. Keep it yourself."

And Neves was left with the blanket in her hand, not knowing what to do with it, and looked at the grandmother, and realised, in that instant, that she was not before an old woman's stubbornness, a tantrum, a whim. She was before a thing deeper and sadder: the grandmother could not accept the blanket. It was not that she did not want to: she could not. To receive that care, to let the granddaughter warm her, was for Serafina a thing as impossible as to feel the cold, and for the same reason, because they were the same thing: the grandmother had switched off, at eleven years old, at the same time, the feeling of the cold and the letting herself be warmed, the needing and the receiving, and one could not be switched back on without the other. The refused blanket and the unfelt cold were the same gesture, the gesture of a girl who had learned that she could not need anything, that she had to be the one who warms and never the one who is warmed, and who had kept that gesture sixty-three years with a fidelity that was, at the same time, admirable and heartbreaking.

There was, before Neves gave up for that night, a small exchange that stayed with her. Neves, with the blanket in her hand, asked the grandmother, slowly, not insisting, only wanting to understand: grandmother, but if you are cold and do not admit it, and go about in short sleeves to prove you are not, does that not tire you? Is it not more work to pretend not to be cold than simply to accept a blanket? And Serafina was a moment silent, and then said a thing that was the key to her whole life: it tires me, my girl. It tires me very much. But it is the tiredness I know. I learned to live tired so long ago that I no longer know how to live another way. To accept the blanket would be to rest, and I do not know how to rest, Neves, the rest frightens me more than the tiredness, because the tiredness I know and the rest I do not, and one, when old, clings to what one knows, even if what one knows kills one, because what one does not know is more frightening than death. And Neves kept that, the rest frightens me more than the tiredness, and realised that the grandmother did not refuse the blanket out of pride nor out of stubbornness, but out of fear, the old fear of resting, of lowering one's guard, of stopping, the fear of the girl of eleven who had learned that to stop was to die, and who at seventy-four still thought, in her body, that if she rested, if she accepted the blanket, if she let herself be warmed, something terrible would happen, even though there was no longer anything terrible to happen.

It was the fortress speaking: the logic of one who built themselves to resist and who, before the invitation to surrender, feels not relief but panic, because surrender, for one who only knows how to resist, seems death. And Neves realised that she was going to have to go slowly, very slowly, that one does not ask a fortress to surrender from one day to the next, that what she could do was offer, every day, a little bit of care, and let the grandmother grow used, little by little, to the care not killing her, to the rest not bringing the catastrophe, to being able to lower the guard and the sky not falling. It was a work of patience, of snow melting one crystal at a time, and Neves, who had come to the serra impatient, with the haste of the city, learned there, with the grandmother, the first lesson of her own thaw: patience, the serra art of waiting for things to happen in their time, for the ice to melt when the sun comes, without forcing, without haste, with the certainty that the spring always comes, even to the serra, even to the most frozen hearts, if there is someone to wait for them as long as it takes.

Neves did not insist any more, that night, because she realised that to insist was useless, that one does not convince a fortress with a blanket. She kept the blanket, and stayed watching the grandmother mend by the light of the fire, in short sleeves, in the cold, and felt a new powerlessness, the powerlessness of one who loves someone and cannot care for them because they do not let her. And she went to bed, with that powerlessness, and it was in bed, in the cold room, that she heard the thing that changed everything: she heard, from the room next door, the grandmother coughing. A low cough, muffled, of one who coughs against the pillow so as not to wake anyone, so as not to give trouble, so as not to admit. A cough of one who is cold and does not say it.

On one of the following nights, Neves got up, and went without making noise to the grandmother's room, and found her sleeping, but sleeping badly, curled up in spite of everything, with the window ajar letting in the cold of the serra, and Neves, carefully, closed the window a little — not entirely, for the grandmother would notice and be angry, but enough for the cold to abate — and went to fetch the blanket the grandmother had refused, and laid it over the sleeping grandmother, slowly, because the sleeping grandmother could not refuse, and the grandmother, in her sleep, instead of taking it off, gripped it, pulled it to her, nestled into it, and Neves realised, in that gesture of the sleeping grandmother gripping the blanket, the truth that the waking grandmother denied: that Serafina's body wanted the blanket, needed the blanket, gripped the blanket the moment the vigilant head fell asleep and ceased to forbid it. By day, the fortress refused the warmth; by night, the fortress asleep, the girl of eleven who still lived in that body gripped the blanket with the avidity of one who had been cold a whole lifetime. And Neves stayed at the door watching the grandmother sleep gripping the blanket, and was certain, then, that the thaw was possible, that beneath the ice there was a child who wanted to be warmed, and that her work, that winter, was to help the vigilant head lower the guard enough to let that child, at last, accept by day, awake, without shame, the blanket she gripped by night in secret.

In the morning, Serafina woke with the blanket over her, and Neves waited for the anger, but the grandmother said nothing, folded the blanket, laid it down, and pretended it had not happened, and Neves realised that that silence was already a small surrender, that the grandmother knew she had gripped the blanket in her sleep and was ashamed of it, and that the saying nothing was her way of not having to admit that she had been cold. But the blanket had been accepted, even if in sleep, even if without confession, and Neves kept that first blanket accepted in secret as the first sign that the ice, beneath, was beginning to want to melt.

And Neves, lying in the dark, hearing the grandmother cough low in the room next door, realised the whole truth of the winter she had before her: that the grandmother was cold. That the woman who was never cold was cold, of course she was, everyone is cold, the cold is of the body, it does not switch off truly, only the feeling of it switches off, the admitting of it, the complaining of it. The grandmother was cold like everyone, and coughed of it low in the dark, and by day swore she did not feel it, and went about in short sleeves proving it, and refused the blanket, and all that was a performance of sixty-three years, a performance she did so well that it deceived the village and almost deceived herself, but that did not deceive the body, which was cold all the same, and which coughed, at night, the truth that the owner did not let it say by day.

That night, hearing the grandmother cough, Neves thought also of her own cold, and it was the first time she linked the two things. Because she too had a blanket she refused, in her city way: she had friends who offered to listen to her and to whom she said she was well; she had a mother who asked her what was the matter and to whom she answered that it was tiredness; she had, after the undone love, refused all the hands that had reached out, with the same it-is-not-needed, I-manage that the grandmother said to the blanket, and she had gone to hide on the serra so as not to have to accept anyone's care, to lick her wounds alone, like an animal that draws away from the pack to suffer apart. And she realised, lying in the dark, that the grandmother was her fifty years from now, if she went on: a woman who had learned so well not to need anyone that she ends up alone on a serra, admired and frozen, refusing blankets and coughing low at night. The grandmother was not only the grandmother: she was a warning, a mirror of the future, and Neves realised, with a shiver, that she had come up to the serra thinking she came to help the grandmother and that perhaps she had come, at bottom, to see herself at the end of the road she was beginning, and to frighten herself enough to change roads while she was still in time.

And she decided, that night, lying in the dark hearing the grandmother cough, a thing she did not say to anyone: that she was going to spend that winter trying, slowly, without forcing, with the patience of the snow that melts one crystal at a time, to make the grandmother accept a blanket. Not to warm her, or not only: to give her back, gesture by gesture, blanket by blanket, the right to feel the cold, to admit it, to be warmed, which was the right the grandmother had lost at eleven years old and that no one, in sixty-three years, had tried to give her back, because everyone, dazzled by the fortress, had thought the fortress did not need it, and no one had heard, as Neves heard now in the dark, the low cough of the girl of eleven who was still cold, in there, beneath all that steel, and who never, in sixty-three years, had had leave to say it.
