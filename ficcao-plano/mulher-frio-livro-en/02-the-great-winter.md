## 2. The Great Winter

Serafina's story began to be dug up as things are dug up on the serra: by layers, slowly, with the first layer being the easiest, the one on the surface, the one everyone knows.

The first layer was the legend, and the legend Neves had already heard, in pieces, in the city, told by her father with that pride mixed in of one who comes from people who endured things: grandmother Serafina had been orphaned very young, one terrible winter, and had raised the siblings alone, and was for that a woman of iron. It was the summary, the postcard version, the one told at family dinners as proof of the temper one comes from. And Neves, at the beginning of the winter, asked the grandmother to tell it her, because she wanted to know, and because she sensed that there, in that story, was the key to the woman who did not feel the cold.

Serafina told it. But she told the legend, not the story, and Neves only over the winter realised the difference.

She told it one night by the hearth, with the dryness of one who tells a thing of another person, a thing read in a book, and not a thing lived in one's own skin. She told that there had been, long ago, sixty-three years ago, a winter the serra called for ever the Great Winter, the worst in memory, with a snowfall that closed the serra for weeks, and that in that winter her parents had gone down to the village for provisions, before the great snowfall, and that the snowfall had fallen before they came up, and that they had not come back, and that they had been lost in the snow, and that no one ever saw them again, and that she, who was eleven years old, had been left with the small siblings in the house of the height, and had raised them. She told it so, in half a dozen sentences, like a summary, like a legend, and at the end she shrugged her shoulders and said it was so, my girl, it was life, in those times the serra was hard, and went back to mending the sock, and Neves realised that the grandmother had told everything and had told nothing, that she had given the facts and kept the truth, that the story had come out of her as the water comes out of a frozen fountain: on the outside, and dry within.

The legend of the Great Winter, Neves discovered over that winter, was not only the family's: it was the whole serra's. The Serranos told it from generation to generation, and it had become the founding legend of the height, the story that explained who they were: hard people, people who endure, people the serra did not bend. And at the centre of the legend was Serafina, the girl of eleven years, transformed by the collective memory into a kind of mythical figure, the heroine of the height, the living proof that the Serranos were made of a matter that resisted everything. And Neves realised that that legend, which honoured Serafina, also imprisoned her, because a whole serra that makes of a woman the symbol of the strength that does not bend leaves her not much room to bend, to weep, to be weak, to be cared for. Serafina was not only a prisoner of her own ice: she was a prisoner of the legend the serra had made of her, of the expectation of a whole community that needed her to be the woman who was never cold, because if she, the strongest of all, were cold, then what hope was there for the others. The serra had leaned on Serafina as the siblings had leaned, as the whole village had leaned: it had made of her a symbol, and symbols are not permitted to be people.

And there was a cruel irony in that, that Neves only put together later: that the legend that celebrated Serafina's survival was, itself, part of what prevented her from living. Each time someone on the serra told the story of the girl of eleven who saved her siblings, they were reinforcing Serafina's prison, reminding her that her role was to be strong, making it more impossible, more unthinkable, that she might one day lower her guard. The serra's admiration was a chain of gold, but it was a chain, and Serafina had carried it for sixty-three years, the weight of being the legend, the weight of not being able to disappoint a whole serra that needed to believe that there were people who did not bend.

Because there was, in the way Serafina told it, an absence that shouted: the absence of the pain. A woman tells the death of her parents at eleven years old with pain, with her voice changing, with something crossing her face, even sixty years later. Serafina told it like one who tells the price of linen in the year of the wedding, with a neutrality that was, itself, the proof that something, there, had been frozen, put in a place where it no longer hurt because it was no longer felt. And Neves, who was young but was no fool, and who was herself learning, in the city, that the things one tells without pain are often the ones that most hurt, kept that dryness as the first sign, the first layer of a digging that was going to last the winter.

Neves noticed that the grandmother, in telling the legend, always used the same words, in the same order, like one who recites a thing memorised, and realised that that too was a defence: that Serafina had told that story so many times, over sixty-three years, always in the same dry way, that the story had become a formula, a set of worn words that no longer touched what they had beneath. It was like a river stone, polished from so much rolling: the story, from so much being told the same way, had lost its edges, and to tell it no longer cost, because it was no longer the thing that had happened, it was a summary of the thing, a shell of the thing, and Serafina hid in that shell as one hides who does not want to go back to what the shell keeps. And Neves realised that to reach the truth she was going to have to break the shell, to take the grandmother out of the memorised formula, to make her tell the story in a new way, with new words, because only with new words would the old thing have edges again, would touch again, would hurt again and be able, at last, to be felt and wept.

And she realised that that was going to take time, and that it was not done by force, and that the only way was to go on living together, to go on gaining the grandmother's trust, to go on being there, day after day, until one day, in a moment that could not be forced, the grandmother let go of the formula and told the truth. And Neves resolved to wait for that day, however long it took, because she had realised, in those first days on the serra, that the haste of the city was of no use there, that the things of the serra, and those of the heart, have their time, and that her work that winter was not to wring the truth from the grandmother, but to be there, present, patient, until the truth could come out on its own.

"And the grandmother, at that time, at eleven years old, alone with the siblings... were you not afraid?" asked Neves, trying to break the legend.

"Afraid?" Serafina seemed to find the question strange, almost without sense. "There was no time for fear, my girl. There were the siblings to warm, there was the fire to light, there was food to find. One who has all that to do has no time for fear. Fear is a luxury, Neves. Fear is for one who can stop. I could not stop."

And she went back to the mending, and did not realise that she had said, in that phrase, more about herself than in the whole legend: that at eleven years old she had learned that to feel — the fear, the cold, the pain — was a luxury she could not allow herself, because to feel was to stop, and to stop was for them all to die, and that therefore she had switched off the feeling, there, that winter, to be able to do what was needed, and that she had never again switched it back on, because life had never again given her, or she had never again given herself, the permission to stop. The woman who did not feel the cold was a woman who at eleven years old had decided not to feel, because to feel killed, and who had kept that decision sixty-three years, with a fidelity of steel, without ever realising that the danger had passed, that the siblings were already raised, that she could already, long ago, feel again, and that she went on not feeling only out of habit, out of old fear, for the girl of eleven who still ruled her and who still thought that, if she felt the cold, they would all die.

There was, that night, after the grandmother told the legend, a silence by the hearth that Neves did not know how to fill, and it was the grandmother who filled it, with a thing she said not to the granddaughter but to the fire, low, like one who lets an old thought escape. She said: the serra is just, you know, my girl. The serra does not kill out of malice. It kills one who underestimates it, who goes down without wraps, who does not respect the cold. My parents knew the serra like no one, and the serra took them all the same, and I, for years, did not understand why, I thought it had been a punishment, an injustice, and I took a long time to understand that it was nothing of the sort, that it was only the serra being the serra, the cold being the cold, and that the cold has neither fault nor intention, the cold only is. And one who learns that, my girl, one who learns that the cold only is, ceases to fear it and comes to respect it, and one who respects the cold does not die of it. That was what I learned that winter: to respect the cold instead of fearing it. And Neves heard, and kept it, without realising yet that the grandmother was speaking of two things at the same time, of the cold of the serra and of the other cold, that within, and that that serene philosophy of the cold that only is was also Serafina's way of having made peace with the loss of the parents without ever having wept it: by turning the pain into a law of nature, into the cold that only is, into a thing without fault and without tear, and therefore into a thing one does not weep, one only respects.

But there was a flaw in that philosophy, and Neves, young as she was, sensed it without knowing how to say it: it was that the parents were not the cold. The parents were people, were loved, and their death was not a law of nature to respect, it was a loss to weep, and Serafina, in treating the death of the parents as the cold that only is, had spared herself the pain but had also spared herself the mourning, and a mourning that is not done does not disappear, it stays, frozen, waiting, and it was that frozen mourning, more than the cold of the serra, that kept Serafina in short sleeves and with the window open for sixty-three years, because to admit the cold would be to admit the pain, and to admit the pain would be, at last, to weep the parents, and to weep the parents was the thing that Serafina, since she was eleven, most feared, because she had learned, that winter, that if she began to weep perhaps she would not stop, and that to stop to weep was to die.

There was, in that first telling of the legend, a detail that Serafina let escape and that Neves caught and kept. The grandmother, telling how the parents had gone down to the village for provisions before the snowfall, said, without giving it importance, it was I who told them to go down. And she went on with the story, but Neves stayed gripping that phrase: it was I who told them to go down. She asked, carefully, how so, grandmother? And Serafina, realising she had said too much, tried to undo it, it was a manner of speaking, and Neves did not insist, but kept it, and over the winter would come to realise the weight of that phrase: that Serafina, a girl, had had something to do with the parents' decision to go down, perhaps she had asked for something that had to be fetched from the village, perhaps she had insisted, a thing of a child, and that she carried, beneath all the ice, a guilt, the absurd and deep guilt of children who think the death of their parents was on account of them, that if they had not asked for that, if they had not done that, the parents would not have gone down and would not have died. And Neves realised that part of Serafina's ice, perhaps the deepest part, was not only the pain of losing the parents: it was the guilt of thinking she had sent them to their death, a guilt of eleven years, irrational but crushing, that Serafina had carried for sixty-three years without ever having told it to anyone, and that perhaps it had also been to punish herself for that guilt that she had condemned herself to a whole life of cold, of sacrifice, of not deserving warmth: because one who thinks themselves guilty of the death of their parents does not think they deserve to be warmed, they think they deserve to be cold for ever.

Neves said none of this, of course. She was twenty-six years old and was no one to tell the grandmother what the grandmother was. But she kept it. She kept the dryness, kept the "fear is a luxury", kept the image of the girl of eleven alone in the snow with her siblings, and at night, lying in the cold room of the house of the height, with the grandmother sleeping with the window open in the room next door, Neves stayed thinking of the grandmother as a girl, and felt, for the first time, not the frightened respect the village had for Serafina, but a deeper and more tender thing: an enormous pity for a girl of eleven years who had never been afraid because she could not allow herself that luxury, and who had become, by dint of not allowing herself that luxury, a woman who at seventy-four still did not know that she had the right to feel the cold.
