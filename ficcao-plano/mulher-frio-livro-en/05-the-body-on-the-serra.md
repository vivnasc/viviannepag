## 5. The body on the serra

The low cough became, in January, a real illness, and the illness came accompanied by the snow, and the snow closed the serra, and it was so, the two of them isolated in the house of the height, that Serafina was obliged, for the first time in sixty-three years, to need.

It must be said that Serafina fought against the illness as she fought against everything: by denying it. At the beginning of the symptoms, she did what she had always done, dispatched them, went on with life, tended the animals, chopped firewood, refused to stop, because to stop for an illness was to give it importance, and to give importance to an illness of her own was a thing Serafina did not do. And it was that refusal to stop in time that worsened the illness, because a cold treated in time is a cold, and a cold ignored and added to the cold and the work and the seventy-four years becomes another thing, and it was into that other thing that Serafina's illness turned, out of stubbornness, out of that old stubbornness of not admitting that the body had limits, of treating the body as she treated everything else, with the order that it endure. The body, for seventy-four years, had endured, faithful to its owner; but the body, at seventy-four, was no longer that of eleven, and that order to endure, which in other times the body fulfilled, that time it could not fulfil, and the body gave way, and Serafina fell into bed, defeated not by the illness, but by her own refusal to treat it in time, by the very fortress that, in not letting her stop, had pushed her into an illness much worse than the one she would have had if she had allowed herself, at the first sign, the weakness of stopping.

It began with a fall. Serafina, stubborn, had gone out into the cold to tend the animals and the firewood, as she always did, refusing Neves's help, I will see to it, my girl, you are not used to the serra, and on one of those outings she slipped on the ice and fell, and hurt a hip, and dragged herself back home alone, and said nothing, of course, dispatched the fall as she dispatched everything. But the hurt hip, added to the cough, added to the cold, added to the seventy-four years, was too much even for the fortress, and in a few days Serafina was in bed, with fever, scarcely able to move, and the snow outside falling without stopping, closing the paths, leaving the two women alone on the height, with no doctor, no neighbours near, no one else: the old woman who would not let herself be cared for and the granddaughter from the city who was going to have to care for her by force.

And it was the hardest thing Neves did in her life, to care for a woman who did not let herself be cared for.

Because Serafina, even in bed, even with fever, even unable to move, fought against the care with a force that commanded respect. She tried to get up to see to things, and Neves had to lay her down again. She refused the food Neves brought her, said she was not hungry, that it was not needed, that Neves should not trouble herself. She apologised, from the bed, for giving trouble, ah my girl, you came to spend the winter and you end up tending an old woman, this is not what I wanted. She wanted to send Neves down to the village, despite the snow, so as not to have to be cared for, I would rather stay here alone, my girl, go away before the snow closes for good. And Neves had to be firm, firmer than she had ever been with anyone, had to tell the grandmother, with an authority she did not know she had, grandmother, you are ill, I am not going anywhere, I am going to care for you, and you are going to let me, because there is no one else here and you cannot treat yourself alone, and whether you like it or not, this winter, it is I who care for you.

And the serra, with the snow, was Neves's accomplice, because it took from Serafina all the ways out. There was nowhere to send the granddaughter, no way to flee the care, no way not to depend. The snow closed the two women in the house of the height and obliged the fortress to what sixty-three years had not managed: to surrender, to let herself be treated, to depend on another person to eat, to be warmed, to get up. And it was terrible for Serafina, Neves saw it, it was almost more painful for her to be cared for than to be ill, because the illness was of the body and the being cared for attacked the thing that defined her, the fortress, the identity of sixty-three years, the woman who does not need, who does not weigh, who does not feel the cold.

The days of isolation were, in spite of everything, a kind of gift, and Neves realised it later: the snow, in closing the serra, took from Serafina not only the physical ways out, but the excuses. In a house full of visitors, in a village with neighbours, Serafina would always have been able to send the granddaughter to see to something else, to make herself out busy, to hide the illness behind the bustle. The two of them shut on the height, with no one else, with nothing to do but the illness and the care of it, there was no way to hide, no way to deflect, no way not to depend. The snow obliged the two women to an intimacy that neither of them would have chosen: the old one obliged to be cared for, the young one obliged to care, and in that forced intimacy they went on knowing one another in a way that sixty years of polite visits had never allowed. Neves learned to read the grandmother's body, the signs of the fever, what she needed before she asked it, because the grandmother did not ask. And Serafina, against all her will, went on getting used to the granddaughter's presence by the bed, to the granddaughter's hand on her forehead, to the granddaughter's voice asking how do you feel today, and that presence, at first unbearable, went on becoming, day after day of fever, less unbearable, and then almost bearable, and then, in one moment or another that she would never admit, almost good.

Because there is in being cared for, Serafina discovered in bed, with the fever, a sweetness she had forgotten existed, or that perhaps she had never known, because the last time anyone had cared for her had been before the eleven years, before the Great Winter, when she still had a mother, and of those memories almost nothing remained, only a vague sensation of warmth and of hands and of a voice, so old that it seemed more a dream than a memory. And now, at seventy-four, ill, with the granddaughter caring for her, Serafina felt return, very lightly, beneath the discomfort and the shame of giving trouble, that old sensation, that warmth of being cared for that she had not felt since she was a girl, and it was a sensation so strange, so unaccustomed, that at first she did not even recognise it, and when she recognised it she was frightened, because to feel that warmth was to open a door she had shut at eleven years old, the door of being cared for, and through that door, she sensed, came not only the warmth, but everything she had locked away along with it: the pain, the longing, the mourning, the cold of a whole lifetime.

"I never gave anyone trouble, my girl," said Serafina, one night, in bed, with the fever, and there was in her voice something Neves had never heard from her, something resembling despair. "In seventy-four years, never. And now I am here, giving you trouble, making you care for me, taking your winter from you. This, for me, is worse than the illness, Neves. You do not know what this costs me. I would rather..." — and she stopped, because she was going to say a terrible thing, and she said it all the same, low, the fever loosening what health silenced — "...sometimes I would rather die here alone than give you all this trouble. It is not out of not wanting you. It is out of not knowing. I do not know how to be cared for, my girl. I never knew. No one taught me. I learned to care at eleven years old, by force, but to be cared for no one ever taught me, and now I am old and I do not know, and I am ashamed of not knowing a thing so simple."

There was a night, at the worst of the fever, when Neves was truly afraid, and when the serra left her completely alone with that fear. The snow fell without stopping, the paths were closed, there was no telephone that caught a signal on the height, no way to call the doctor from outside, no way to go down, and Serafina burned, and was a little delirious, and Neves, alone, at dawn, by the grandmother's bed, felt what the grandmother must have felt at eleven years old: the total responsibility, with no one to turn to, the life of another person in her hands and no one else to help. And it was that night that Neves understood the grandmother truly, not with her head but with her body, because she lived, for a few hours, what the grandmother had lived for a whole winter at eleven years old: the terror of being the only one, the impossibility of collapsing because there is no one else to hold, the necessity of being strong not by choice but because there is no alternative. And Neves, that night, did not collapse: she endured, cared for the grandmother, brought down her fever with cloths of water, watched over her until morning, and in the morning the fever gave way, and Neves realised that she had just done, on a small scale, what the grandmother had done on a large one, and that she had, for that, earned the right to speak to her as an equal, because she now knew, on her skin, what it was to be the one who endures alone.

And in the morning, with the fever down, she said to the grandmother: grandmother, last night I was afraid of losing you, and I was here alone, unable to call anyone, and I realised a thing. I realised what you lived at eleven years old, a whole winter like that, alone, with lives in your hands and no one else. I lived one night and I almost could not endure it. You lived a winter, and then a whole life. Now I understand where your ice comes from, grandmother. It is not coldness. It is what is left of one who had to be strong alone too early. And Serafina, weak from the fever, heard the granddaughter say that, I understand where your ice comes from, and it was perhaps the first time in her life that someone told her they understood, that they saw beneath the fortress the wound, and Serafina's eyes filled with water, and she turned her face away so the granddaughter would not see, but Neves saw, and said nothing, and knew that one more crystal of ice had begun to melt.

And Neves, on hearing the grandmother confess, with the fever, that she would rather die alone than give trouble, that she did not know how to be cared for and that she was ashamed of it, felt her heart tighten in a new way, and realised that she was before not a stubborn woman, but a child of eleven years with seventy-four years of age, a girl who had never been cared for and who did not know how it was done, who had as much fear and as much shame of depending as another person would of a foreign language they never learned. There was, in caring for the ill grandmother, an inversion that moved Neves and that she kept: that she, who had come up to the serra to be, in a certain sense, the frail one — the granddaughter with the broken heart, the one who had come to be cared for by the strong grandmother —, had become, through the illness, the strong one, the carer, and that the grandmother, the fortress, had become the frail one, the cared-for. The roles had switched, and Neves realised that it was that switch that was curing the two of them: she, in caring for the grandmother, discovered that she had strength, that she was not only the frail one with the broken heart, that she was capable of holding another person, and that gave her a new dignity, took her from the role of city victim in which she had come wrapped; and the grandmother, in being cared for, discovered that she could be frail without disappearing, that there was someone to hold her, and that melted her ice. Each cured the other by switching place: the frail one made herself strong by caring, the strong one made herself frail by being cared for, and in the midst of that switch, in the sickbed, in the snow, the two of them met at last in the place where two people cure one another, which is the one in which each is, by turns, the one who holds and the one who is held.

And she said to the grandmother, with a firm tenderness, sitting on the edge of the bed, the thing that was going to be the beginning of the thaw:

"Then I teach you, grandmother. To be cared for. You taught your siblings to live, at eleven years old. I, now, teach you to let yourself be cared for. You do not have to know. I will show you. It is only to let. Begin with a small thing: let me put this blanket on you, and do not take it off. Only that, for today. Let me put the blanket on you."

"How does one let, my girl?" asked Serafina, and the question was genuine, it was the question of one who truly does not know, of one who never learned. "I do not know how one lets. Tell me how it is done, and I will try."

And Neves realised, in that question, that the grandmother was not resisting out of stubbornness, in that moment: she was asking for help, in her way, she was saying teach me, and that that, a fortress asking how one lets oneself be cared for, was already the beginning of the surrender. And she answered, with a sweetness that came to her from somewhere, because she herself, in the city, did not know how to let herself be cared for, and was teaching the grandmother a thing she had to learn too: one lets so, grandmother. One does nothing. To be cared for is not a thing one does, it is a thing one lets happen. I put the blanket on you, and you do not take it off. I make you the broth, and you eat. I stay here, and you let me stay. You do not have to do anything, grandmother. On the contrary: you have to not do. You have to stop doing, which is the hardest thing for one who has done their whole life. To be cared for is the one thing in the world one manages by doing nothing, only letting, only being, only receiving. And you, who know how to do everything, are going to have to learn the thing that is not done, that is only received. It is the contrary of everything you know. That is why it is so hard. But it is only to let, grandmother. Begin by letting the blanket.

And she put the blanket on her, slowly, and Serafina, in bed, with fever, without strength to refuse it, or perhaps without the will, for the first time, to refuse it, let it. She kept the blanket. And it was the first blanket Serafina accepted in sixty-three years, and it was little, and it was everything, and Neves, on seeing the grandmother keep the blanket, knew that the ice, at the end of a life, had begun, at a tiny point, to melt.
