## 6. The winter of the eleven years

The snow, in January, repeated the old scene, and it was the snow that opened the deep layer of the story, because there are things that are only told when the world outside resembles the day they happened, and the white and closed serra around the house of the height was, line for line, the serra of sixty-three years before, and Serafina, in bed, with the granddaughter caring for her, with the same snow falling, told at last, not the legend, but the winter of the eleven years from within.

She told it little by little, over several nights, as the fever and the blanket and the granddaughter's care went on melting her tongue, and Neves heard, without forcing, sitting on the edge of the bed, and what she heard was this.

That the parents had gone down to the village for provisions, before the great snowfall, because the winter announced itself hard and it was necessary to make a reserve, and that they had gone down both, a rare thing, because there was much to bring and the father alone could not manage it. That they had left the children in the house of the height, the five of them, with Serafina, the eldest, of eleven years, in charge of them until they came back, which was a matter of two days. That on the first day Serafina tended the siblings with the confidence of one who knows the parents come back tomorrow. That on the second day it began to snow, to snow in earnest, to snow as never before, and that the snow closed the serra, closed the paths, closed the world, and that the parents, who should have come up that day, did not come up. That Serafina, of eleven years, waited, at first without fear, they do not come up with this snow, they come up when it stops. That the snow did not stop. That it stopped days later, and that the parents did not come. That she realised, slowly, over those days of snow, with the small siblings asking for their mother and father, that something had gone wrong, that the parents had been caught by the snow on the way, somewhere between the village and the height, and that they were not coming, and that perhaps they would never come.

"And I realised, Neves," said Serafina, in bed, with the snow falling outside the same as then, and her voice, for the first time that winter, was no longer dry, it had something trembling beneath it, "I realised, at eleven years old, alone, with your little uncles weeping from hunger and from cold and from missing their mother, I realised a thing that decided my whole life. I realised that if I wept too, we would all die. That if I let myself go, if I felt what there was to feel, the fear, the cold, the lack of my parents, the desire to lie down on the floor and weep until I died, if I gave myself to that, no one lit the fire, no one made the food from the little there was, no one warmed the small ones, and we would all die in the snow, as my parents had died. And so I did not weep. I was eleven years old, my parents had just died on the serra, and I did not weep, because there was no time, because there were five mouths to warm and feed, and I was the only one left to do it. I lit the fire. I made the food. I warmed the small ones. And I kept everything I felt in a deep place, and I shut that place, and I told myself, at eleven years old, that as long as there were siblings to care for I could not feel, because to feel was to stop and to stop was to die. And I never again opened that place, my girl. Never again. The siblings grew, married, had children, and I went on not feeling, because I no longer knew how to do otherwise, because the girl of eleven never again left me, because she still today thinks that if I feel the cold, someone dies."

She told further, that night or another of the nights when the story came out, the details that made the scene a scene and not a legend, the details that hurt. She told that on the third or fourth day of snow, when she realised the parents were not coming, the hardest thing was not the fear nor the cold: it was the smaller siblings asking for their mother, and her having to answer them. She told that the smallest, Amaro, who was three years old, wept at night calling for his mother, and that she, of eleven, took him in her arms and told him that the mother was coming, that the mother was arriving, that it was only the snow to pass, and that she told him so knowing it was a lie, knowing already, at bottom, that the mother was not coming, and that that was the first time she lied out of love, that she learned that sometimes love is to lie to one one loves so they can endure, and that that lie, repeated night after night to a boy of three who called for his mother, was perhaps what most froze her, because to lie with firmness, to say the mother is coming without her voice trembling, she had to not feel, she had to set aside her own pain as a girl who also wanted the mother, and to be only the firm voice that boy needed to hear.

And she told the thing she had never told anyone, the deepest of all, and she told it looking at the snow, without looking at the granddaughter, because there are things one only says without looking at anyone: she told that there was a night, that winter, when she, of eleven years, with the siblings finally all asleep, allowed herself, a single time, to feel. She sat by the fire, alone, with all of them asleep, and let the pain come, the lack of the parents, the fear, the cold, and began to weep, low, so as not to wake the siblings, and wept a while, and it was then that the smallest, Amaro, woke and saw her weep, and was frightened, because if the big sister, who was the strong one, the one who said everything was going to be all right, if the big sister wept, then everything was lost. And Serafina, on seeing the brother's terror at seeing her weep, realised, at eleven years old, that night, the lesson that governed her whole life: that she could not weep, ever, because her weeping terrified those who depended on her, because as long as she did not weep they could believe that everything was going to be all right, and that her role, from then on, was to be the one who does not weep so that the others could have hope. And she stopped weeping, that night, swallowed everything, smiled at the brother, said it is nothing, it was only smoke from the fire in my eyes, sleep, and never again, in sixty-three years, did she allow herself to weep in front of anyone, because at eleven years old she had learned that her weeping took from the others their hope, and that she had to be, for ever, the one who does not weep.

And she stopped, and stayed looking at the snow through the window, the same snow, and Neves, on the edge of the bed, with her eyes full of water, realised that she was hearing the deepest thing of the grandmother's life, the scene where everything had begun, and realised, above all, a thing that filled her with an immense tenderness and an enormous pity: that the grandmother was not cold. That the grandmother had never been cold. That beneath the ice of sixty-three years was a girl of eleven years who had felt everything, all the fear, all the cold, all the pain of losing her parents, and who had had to freeze all of that, not out of being hard, but out of being the most courageous and most loving thing a girl of eleven years could do: out of love for her siblings, to save them, she had frozen herself. The fortress had not been born of coldness. It had been born of love. It had been an act of love, the greatest of all, a girl sacrificing her own capacity to feel to save her siblings, and the price of that love had been a whole life of ice, and no one, in sixty-three years, had seen that beneath the ice was the love, they had only seen the fortress and admired the fortress, without realising that the fortress was a wound, the deepest and the noblest of wounds, that of one who sacrificed themselves so early and so wholly that they forgot that they were, also, someone who deserved to be saved.

She told further how it had been afterwards, in the days and the weeks following, when the snow abated and the serra realised what had happened in the house of the height: how the neighbours came up, and wanted to take the siblings, to divide them among the houses, that a girl of eleven years could not raise four children alone, and how Serafina, of eleven years, planted herself at the door of the house of the height and said no, that the siblings were not to be separated, that she would raise them, that that was their house and that they were not leaving it. She told that the neighbours thought it was madness, that a child could not endure it, and that she endured it, out of stubbornness, out of love, out of a strength she herself did not know where it came from, and that she raised the four in the house of the height, alone, without letting any of them be separated, because she had decided that the one thing she could still save from that disaster was the whole family, together, in the house of the parents, and she saved it. And Neves realised that that decision, that of not letting the siblings be separated, had been the second great decision of that winter, after that of not weeping, and that the two together had made Serafina: the one who does not weep and the one who does not let go, the one who endures alone and the one who keeps everything together, the whole fortress, born in a few weeks, in a girl of eleven years, against the counsel of the whole serra.

"And did you never regret it, grandmother?" asked Neves. "Having taken all that on alone, at eleven years old? You had a whole life at the service of others, without ever being a child, without ever being cared for. Did you never regret it?"

And Serafina thought a long time before answering, and answered with an honesty that was itself a sign of the thaw: regret, no, my girl. I do not regret having saved them, I would do it all again. But... — and she hesitated, and said the hard thing — but sometimes, my girl, only sometimes, in sixty-three years, in a deep corner, a sadness came to me of a thing I did not have: a sadness came to me that no one ever asked me, that winter, whether I was well. Everyone asked me about the children, how are the children, do you need anything for the children, and no one, not one, asked me, and you, Serafina, how are you? I was eleven years old, I had lost my parents, I was raising four siblings alone, and no one asked me how I was, because I was the strong one, and the strong ones are not asked. And I, you know, I think I waited for that question my whole life, without knowing I waited for it. And you, my girl, were the first in sixty-three years to ask it of me. When you asked me, weeks ago, who cared for me, it was the first time someone wanted to know of me and not of the children. It took sixty-three years, that question. But it came."

And it was that night, or another near it, that Serafina said at last the thing she carried beneath everything, the guilt, and she said it with the smallest voice Neves had heard from her, the voice of the girl of eleven years: you know, my girl, what I never told anyone? It was I who sent them down. I wanted a thing, a foolishness, a thing of a child that was sold only in the village, and I threw a tantrum, and my mother said to my father let us both go, we will take the chance and bring all the provisions before the snow, and they both went down on account of me, on account of a foolishness I wanted, and they did not come back. If I had not thrown the tantrum, my girl, perhaps only one of them would have gone down, or none, or they would have gone down on another day, and perhaps they would not have died. I sent my parents into the snow, Neves. I was eleven years old, and I sent my parents to their death over a foolishness, and I raised my siblings my whole life thinking of this, that they were orphans on account of me, that I had killed their parents with a tantrum. I never told it to anyone. How does one say a thing like this? But it is this that I carry, my girl. It is not only the pain of losing them. It is the guilt of having sent them to their death. And it is for that, perhaps, that I never thought I deserved to rest, to be cared for, to have warmth: because one who kills their parents with a tantrum deserves no warmth at all, they deserve to be cold for ever, in penance.

And Neves, on hearing that, realised the deepest root of all, and did the most important thing she did that winter: she did not leave the guilt standing. Grandmother, she said, with firmness and with tears, you were eleven years old. Children throw tantrums, it is what children do, and it is the parents who decide, not the children. Your parents decided to go down, both of them, they were adults, they knew the serra, they knew the risks, and they went down because they thought they could manage it, not because you forced them. A tantrum of a child of eleven years kills no one, grandmother. The snow killed. The serra killed. You did not send your parents to their death: you were a girl who wanted a thing, like all girls, and you had the terrible misfortune that life punished you for it in a way no child deserves. The fault is not yours, grandmother. It never was. It was the snow's. And you carried for sixty-three years a guilt that was not yours, and punished yourself for sixty-three years with cold for a crime you did not commit. Set it down, grandmother. You have carried it too long. Do not condemn yourself to be cold for ever over a child's tantrum. Your parents would not want that. No parent wants their child to spend their life punishing themselves for a thing the child did not do.

"You were very brave, grandmother," said Neves, low, and took the grandmother's hand, in the bed. "At eleven years old. You saved them all. But, grandmother... the girl of eleven years can rest now. There is no longer anyone to die. Your siblings are raised, they have children, grandchildren. No one any more dies if you feel the cold. The girl can, at last, stop. She can weep her parents. She can be cold. It is safe now, grandmother. It has passed. There are only sixty-three years left of warning her that it has passed."

And Serafina did not answer, but she squeezed the granddaughter's hand, in the bed, with the snow falling, and it was the first time in sixty-three years that she held someone's hand not to warm them, but to hold on to herself, and Neves felt the grip, and knew that the girl of eleven years, deep down, beneath the ice, had heard, for the first time, that perhaps she could, at last, rest.
