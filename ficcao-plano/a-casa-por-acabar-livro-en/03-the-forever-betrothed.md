## 3. The forever betrothed

In Véspera, Esperança was the forever betrothed, and that was the fond name that hid the truth from her: because of a betrothed, even one of twenty years, no one asks when she gives up, one asks when she marries, and the question, asked with fondness, kept Esperança bound to the waiting with the complicity of the whole village.

It was said of her, in the square, at the church door, by the wash-houses: Esperança and Venturoso, those two, they'll still marry yet; when he finishes the house, it will be a beautiful wedding. And it was said with affection, with the liking the village had for a love so faithful, so old, so proof against time, and no one saw, or no one said, that the fidelity so praised was also the chain that held Esperança, because as long as the village treated her as the betrothed who is going to marry, and not as the woman who grows old waiting, Esperança could go on believing she was in an engagement, and not in an endless wait, on a road to marriage, and not in a place where she had been standing still for twenty years.

There was, at the wash-houses, a woman the village called Perpétua, and she was the oral memory of Véspera, the one who knew all the dates of the engagements and the births and the deaths without needing the Keeper's books. And one morning, wringing a sheet, in the presence of Esperança who scrubbed beside her, Perpétua said, without malice, with the naturalness of one who remarks on the weather, "Eh, Esperança, I remember the day you got engaged as if it were yesterday, it was the year of the great flood, when the river took the old bridge." And a younger woman, Açucena, who was at the next tank, raised her head and asked, "And the bridge, Perpétua, the bridge they rebuilt ages ago, didn't they?" And Perpétua said, "Oh, they rebuilt it, child, that same winter, for a place can't be without a bridge." And she did not see, Perpétua, and Açucena did not see, what that exchange of words did to Esperança, who went on scrubbing with her head down and her heart leaping: that the bridge which had fallen in the same year as her engagement had already been rebuilt, used, worn, and had even been rebuilt a second time; and that her house, begun in the same year, was still unfinished; and that the village had taken months to remake a bridge that served everyone, and twenty years not to finish a house that served the happiness of one alone, and that this said everything about what gets finished and what does not, about the works whose owner wants them done and the works whose owner prefers their dream. And Esperança wrung the sheet harder than was needed, and was left with that hammering in her, and said nothing, because she did not yet know how to say it, but she began, slowly, to do the sums the village did for her without noticing.

Because there are names that bind by being beautiful, and the forever betrothed was one of those. If the village had called Esperança the abandoned, the deluded, the one who loses her life with a man who does not marry, perhaps Esperança, wounded by the word, would have woken; but the village called her the faithful betrothed, the example of constancy, and against that name there is no waking, because it is a name that fills, that gives pride, that makes a virtue of the waiting, and Esperança clung to it, and made of her fidelity to Venturoso a thing to be proud of, without seeing that fidelity to a man who does not keep his word is not virtue, it is only fear of looking at the truth, dressed as constancy.

And there was, in the village's praise, a thing no one saw and that was the cruellest: that the village needed Esperança the forever betrothed as much as Venturoso did. Because Esperança and Venturoso, the faithful love proof against time, were, for Véspera, a beautiful story, one of those stories a village likes to be proud of, our love of twenty years, and no one wants a beautiful story to end, and so the village, without malice, went on keeping the story going, went on asking when the wedding would be, went on saying it would be beautiful, and in doing so held Esperança in the part of betrothed, because a village that needs a story of faithful love needs a woman who stays faithful in the waiting, and Esperança was that woman, and the village's applause for her fidelity was also, without her or the village knowing it, the rope that bound her to her own sacrifice.

And there was, at the wash-houses, a woman younger than Esperança, Idília, married to Telhinha of the Telharia people, living in the half-built house that three generations of men of the family had not finished, and Idília had, still young, the air of one who had already given up, drooping shoulders, a silence in the eyes, and she washed her linen in silence beside the others. And one day, Idília, without anyone drawing it out of her, said, to the tank, more to herself than to the others, "I married thinking the sheet-metal roof was for one year, that my man said he'd lay the tile in the summer; it's twelve years now the sheet metal's been there, and when it rains it's a hell of a din, and I don't even ask him for the tile any more, for asking tires you more than the rain." And there was a silence at the wash-houses, and the women bowed their heads, and Esperança, who scrubbed beside her, went cold, because she saw in Idília, twelve years younger, what she herself would be thirty years on, a woman worn out beneath a provisional roof that had become permanent, leaving off asking, growing used to the rain; and she understood that Idília and she were the same woman at different ages, bound to unfinished houses of men who do not finish, and that the village had, scattered across its wash-houses and its slopes, a whole harvest of these women, some beginning to give up like Idília, others already widowed and bowed like Sina, and she in the middle, the forever betrothed, the praised one, the one who still had time not to end like them if she woke in time.

And the unfinished house, on the slope, had the same part in the landscape that the engagement had in the talk: it was a thing that had always been there and that one stopped seeing. The people of Véspera, going up the road, passed Venturoso's unfinished house as one passes a tree, without noticing, and those from outside, they would sometimes ask, whose is that house under works with that view? and the people of Véspera would answer, it's Venturoso's, he's finishing it, and they said it without irony, he's finishing it, as if twenty years finishing a house were finishing it, as if the unfinishing were not, itself, the permanent state of that house, its true form, the form of a thing that is not going to be finished and that one goes on, from habit and from fondness, calling under works.

Venturoso, he, shone in the village's talk, and it was one of the things that hurt Esperança most when she began to see. Because Venturoso was a dear man, one of the dearest in the village, charming, well-spoken, generous with promises, and when he came into the café and spoke of the house he was going to finish, and of the life he was going to have with Esperança, and of all the plans, he filled the room, and everyone liked to hear him, and no one called him to account, because to call to account a man so likeable, so full of dream, seemed pettiness; and so Venturoso lived, for twenty years, on the infinite credit his likeableness gave him, a credit he never had to pay because no one charged it to him, and Esperança, beside him, was the guarantor of that credit, the one who paid, in life, what Venturoso owed in promises, without anyone, not even herself, doing the sum.

There was at that time an occasion that showed her all of this at once. A young girl married in the village, Rosalina, daughter of Açucena of the wash-houses, one Esperança had seen born and to whom she had even stood as godmother-in-arms at a christening, with a young man, Joaquim of the upper mill, who had asked for her a year before, and it was a simple wedding, with no new house and no great plans, just two young people who wanted each other and married and went to live in a rented room over their uncle's shop, awaiting nothing, with no house ready, with no life in earnest for next year; they married with the life they had, in the house they came by, and began to live. And there was, at Mass, before the wedding breakfast, a moment that stayed crosswise in Esperança for ever. Rosalina, in her veil, before going into the church, saw her on the terrace, and came straight to her, and embraced her, and said to her, with that unbuttoned joy of young brides, "Eh, godmother Esperança, and yours, when's it to be? Mind it has to be before my first daughter, for I want you for godmother again!" And she said it with such love, without a drop of malice, that it was worse, because malice Esperança would have known how to stop, and against love there is no defence. And Esperança smiled, and said the usual, "When the house is ready, child," and Rosalina laughed and went in to her life, light, in her veil, and Esperança stayed on the terrace watching her go in, and had, watching the young bride go in to her life with that young man who had asked for her and who had kept in one year what Venturoso had not kept in twenty, a thing she had never let herself have: she had envy. Envy of a girl who was going to begin to live, she, Esperança, who had been betrothed for twenty years and had never begun anything, and who had been called to be godmother of a life that was setting off, she who could not set off her own.

And she observed, at the wedding breakfast, a thing that made her think: that that young couple, with no house ready, with nothing in earnest, seemed happier, there, beginning their modest life, than she and Venturoso had ever seemed in twenty years of beautiful engagement. Because they had a thing she and Venturoso had never had: they had the present. They had today. They had a life, small, imperfect, but real, that had begun, that was happening, whereas she and Venturoso had only a promised life, great, beautiful, perfect, that was always to come and that never came, and Esperança understood, watching the young couple dance, that a small life that happens is worth more than a great life that is promised, because the small one is lived and the great one is only awaited, and the waiting, however beautiful the awaited thing, is not life, it is the eve of it.

And it was worse when, at the wedding breakfast, Venturoso, who was at the far table charming half a dozen men, stood up for a toast to the couple, and made it with grace, as he did everything, and wished Rosalina and Joaquim a full house and a great life, and said, raising his glass, with his easy laugh, "And we, Esperança, are next, for our house this year will be ready and there'll be a feast on the slope with the best view in the village!" and the room laughed and applauded, and drank to Venturoso and to the unfinished house as one drinks to an old and dear joke; and Esperança, from her place, watched the man toast the house he was not building with the same ease as he toasted the couple who had built theirs, and understood, with a chill, that for Venturoso the toast to the unfinished house and the toast to the others' finished house were worth the same, were both words in a happy mouth, and that she was the only one in the room for whom that house was not a joke, it was life. And right after, by the table of sweets, Perpétua, without malice, with her usual liking, leaned in to her and said, "Eh, Esperança, and yours, when's it to be? It was about time, no?" And Esperança heard the it was about time, and the when's it to be, and understood, in that when's it to be made with fondness, right after Venturoso's toast, that the whole village had been asking the same question for twenty years, and that for twenty years she had answered the same, when the house is ready, and that the answer, said for the thousandth time, in front of a young bride who had married in a year, sounded to her, for the first time, as what it was: an excuse. An excuse she had given for twenty years, with faith, and that was, seen from outside, in the light of a bride who had not waited, a polite way of saying mine does not marry, and I wait, and I call it an unfinished house so as not to call it what it is.

She came back from the wedding with that, and went home, and looked at the trousseau in the chest, and at her face in the mirror, the face of forty-three that had entered that engagement at twenty, and did, for the second time, the sum of the twenty years, and this time the sum came with the young bride's face over it, and with the village's when's it to be, and with the mother's phrase, and I who spent my life waiting, all of them saying the same thing: that she, Esperança, was the forever betrothed, and that the forever betrothed is a beautiful thing to say and a sad thing to be, because a betrothed who is betrothed for ever is not a betrothed: she is a woman who stopped at the door of her own life, with the veil on, awaiting to be let in, and who is always left for next year, and who grows old in her veil, at the door, without ever going in, awaiting a house that perhaps will never be finished, faithful to a man who perhaps will never marry, praised by the village for a constancy that is, seen up close, only the most patient and most beautiful way of losing a life.

And that night, for the first time, Esperança did not say when the house is ready. She stayed silent, in the dark, with the village's question hammering, when's it to be, Esperança, when's it to be, and instead of covering it with the usual phrase, she left it open, and it was an enormous discomfort, because the usual phrase was the roof that protected her from the rain of the truth, and without it the truth came in entire: that perhaps never, that perhaps the house would never be finished, that perhaps the when's it to be had no answer, that perhaps she was going to die betrothed, as her mother had died waiting, and that this, and not the beautiful wedding the village promised her, was her lot, if she did not do, at last, something that twenty years of hope had never let her do, which was to look at the house, and the man, and her own life, as they were, and not as they promised to be.
