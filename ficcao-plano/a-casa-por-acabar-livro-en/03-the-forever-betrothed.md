## 3. The forever betrothed

In Véspera, Esperança was the forever betrothed, and that was the fond name that hid the truth from her: because of a betrothed, even of twenty years, no one asks when she gives up, one asks when she marries, and the question, asked fondly, kept Esperança bound to the waiting with the complicity of the whole village.

It was said of her, in the square, at the church door, by the washing-troughs: Esperança and Venturoso, those two, they'll marry yet; when he finishes the house, it will be a beautiful wedding. And it was said with affection, with the sympathy the village had for a love so faithful, so old, so proof against time, and no one saw, or no one said, that that so-praised faithfulness was also the chain that held Esperança, because as long as the village treated her as the betrothed who was going to marry, and not as the woman who grows old waiting, Esperança could go on believing she was in an engagement, and not in an endless wait, on a road to marriage, and not in a place where she had stood still for twenty years.

Because there are names that bind by being beautiful, and the forever betrothed was one of those. Had the village called Esperança the abandoned, the deluded, the one who wastes her life on a man who does not marry, perhaps Esperança, wounded by the word, would have woken; but the village called her the faithful betrothed, the example of constancy, and against that name there is no waking, because it is a name that fills, that gives pride, that makes of waiting a virtue, and Esperança clung to it, and made of her faithfulness to Venturoso a thing to be proud of, without seeing that faithfulness to a man who does not keep his word is not virtue, it is only fear of looking at the truth, dressed as constancy.

And there was, in the village's praise, a thing no one saw and that was the cruellest: that the village needed Esperança the forever betrothed as much as Venturoso did. Because Esperança and Venturoso, the faithful love proof against time, were, for Véspera, a beautiful story, one of those stories a village likes to be proud of, our love of twenty years, and no one wants a beautiful story to end, and so the village, without malice, kept the story going, kept asking when the wedding would be, kept saying it would be beautiful, and in doing so it held Esperança in the role of betrothed, because a village that needs a story of faithful love needs a woman to stay faithful in the waiting, and Esperança was that woman, and the village's applause for her faithfulness was also, without her or the village knowing it, the cord that bound her to her own sacrifice.

And the unfinished house, on the slope, had the same role in the scenery that the engagement had in the talk: it was a thing that had always been there and that one stopped seeing. The people of Véspera, going up the road, passed Venturoso's unfinished house as one passes a tree, without noticing, and outsiders, they, asked sometimes, whose is that house under works with that view?, and the people of Véspera answered, it's Venturoso's, he's finishing it, and they said it without irony, he's finishing it, as if twenty years finishing a house were finishing it, as if the unfinishing were not, itself, the definitive state of that house, its true form, the form of a thing that is not going to be finished and that one goes on, from habit and from affection, calling under works.

Venturoso, he, shone in the talk of the village, and it was one of the things that most hurt Esperança when she began to see. Because Venturoso was a dear man, of the dearest in the village, charming, well-spoken, generous with promises, and when he came into the café and spoke of the house he was going to finish, and of the life he was going to have with Esperança, and of all the plans, he filled the room, and everyone liked to hear him, and no one charged him, because to charge a man so likeable, so full of dream, seemed meanness; and so Venturoso lived, for twenty years, off the endless credit his likeableness gave him, a credit he never had to pay because no one charged it, and Esperança, beside him, was the guarantor of that credit, the one who paid, in life, what Venturoso owed in promises, without anyone, not even she, doing the sum.

There was at that time an occasion that showed her all this at once. A young girl of the village married, one Esperança had seen born, with a young man who had asked for her a year before, and it was a simple wedding, without a new house or great plans, only two young people who wanted each other and who married and who went to live in a rented room, without waiting for anything, without a ready house, without an in-earnest life for next year; they married with the life they had, in the house they found, and began to live. And Esperança, at the wedding, saw the young bride, veiled, entering into her life with that young man who had asked for her and who had kept in one year what Venturoso had not kept in twenty, and had, seeing the young bride, a thing she had never let herself have: she had envy. Envy of a girl who was going to begin to live, she, Esperança, who had been a betrothed for twenty years and had never begun anything.

And she observed, at the reception, a thing that made her think: that that young couple, without a ready house, without anything in earnest, seemed happier, there, beginning their modest life, than she and Venturoso had ever seemed in twenty years of a beautiful engagement. Because they had a thing she and Venturoso had never had: they had the present. They had today. They had a life, small, imperfect, but real, that had begun, that was happening, whereas she and Venturoso had only a promised life, great, beautiful, perfect, that was always to come and that never came, and Esperança perceived, watching the young couple dance, that a small life that happens is worth more than a great life that is promised, because the small one is lived and the great one is only awaited, and the waiting, however beautiful the awaited thing, is not life, it is eve.

And it was worse when, at the reception, someone, without malice, with the usual sympathy, said to her: oh Esperança, and yours, when's it for? It was time, no? And Esperança heard the it-was-time, and the when's-it-for, and perceived, in that when's-it-for asked fondly, that the whole village had been asking the same question for twenty years, and that for twenty years she had answered the same, when the house is ready, and that the answer, said for the thousandth time, in front of a young bride who had married in a year, sounded to her, for the first time, like what it was: like an excuse. An excuse she had given for twenty years, with faith, and that was, seen from outside, in the light of a bride who had not waited, a polite way of saying mine does not marry, and I wait, and I call it the unfinished house so as not to call it what it is.

She came back from the wedding with that, and went home, and looked at the trousseau in the chest, and at her face in the mirror, the face of forty-three that had entered that engagement at twenty, and did, for the second time, the sum of the twenty years, and this time the sum came with the young bride's face over the top, and with the village's when's-it-for, and with the mother's sentence, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting, all saying the same thing: that she, Esperança, was the forever betrothed, and that the forever betrothed is a beautiful thing to say and a sad thing to be, because a betrothed who is a betrothed forever is not a betrothed: she is a woman who stayed at the door of her own life, with the veil on, awaiting to be let in, and is always put off till next year, and grows old veiled, at the door, without ever going in, awaiting a house that perhaps will never be finished, faithful to a man who perhaps will never marry, praised by the village for a constancy that is, seen up close, only the most patient and most beautiful way of losing a life.

And that night, for the first time, Esperança did not say when the house is ready. She stayed silent, in the dark, with the village's question hammering, when's it for, Esperança, when's it for, and instead of covering it with the phrase of always, she left it open, and it was an enormous discomfort, because the phrase of always was the roof that protected her from the rain of the truth, and without it the truth came in whole: that perhaps never, that perhaps the house would never be finished, that perhaps the when's-it-for had no answer, that perhaps she was going to die a betrothed, as the mother had died waiting, and that this, and not the beautiful wedding the village promised her, was her fate, if she did not, at last, do something that twenty years of hope had never let her do, which was to look at the house, and the man, and her own life, as they were, and not as they promised to be.
