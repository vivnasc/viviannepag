## 12. The life that begins

The village was surprised, of course, that Esperança and Venturoso had broken off after twenty years, and for a time nothing else was talked of, and there were those who said Esperança had done wrong, that a love of twenty years is not thrown away, that Venturoso was such a good man; but there were also those who understood, above all the older women, the ones who had waited for husbands who did not come, and those, on hearing, said nothing, but looked at Esperança with a thing in their eyes that was almost envy, the envy of one who did not have the courage to leave in time for one who did.

Esperança justified herself to no one, and went on living, and the village grew used to it, as it grows used to everything, and after some months Esperança and Venturoso broken off were already only one more thing of the village, and the unfinished house on the slope went on on the slope, and Venturoso went on saying he was going to finish it next year, now without a betrothed waiting, and perhaps he would finish it one day, for himself, or perhaps not, and Esperança no longer had a part in it, and it was good not to have, it was like setting down a weight she had carried twenty years without knowing she carried it.

And there were, it is true, bad days, days on which the waiting, which is a vice like any other, came to her mouth as the cigarette comes to one who has given it up, days on which Esperança, alone, caught herself thinking of Venturoso, feeling the lack of the dream, asking herself whether she had not done wrong, whether she had not been hard, whether a love of twenty years did not deserve more patience; and on those days she had to remember, by force, Sina, the house in the sun, the mother's sentence, and had to hold herself not to climb the slope, not to send word to Venturoso, not to go back to the eve, which was the place she knew how to be. Because to leave the waiting is like leaving any vice: one does not leave it all at once, one leaves it every day, and there are relapses, and there is longing for what did harm, and only with time, day after day of not waiting, does the real life win out over the dream, and the present become, slowly, better company than the promise.

And Esperança's life, the real one, the one she had begun to use, went on growing, slowly, in the way the lives that are begun late grow, without haste and with relish. She cared for the aunt, as always, but now not as one who passes the time awaiting the in-earnest life; she cared for her as one who lives, today, the life she has. She put more flowers at the window. She first-used the crockery she had kept. She went to places she had never been to because she kept the outings for the honeymoon that did not come. She took up again friendships she had let die because she lived all turned towards Venturoso and the house. And she discovered, with an astonishment that made her laugh alone, that life, that of today, the imperfect one, the one without a new house, the one without a husband, was full, was good, was much more life than the twenty years of eve, and that she had spent twenty years awaiting to begin to live when she could have lived the whole time, in the house she had, with the life she had, had she not put her heart in an unfinished house.

It is not known, and the book does not say, whether Esperança loved again, whether there came a man who was there instead of one who was going to, whether life brought her, late, a love of the present instead of one of the future. Perhaps yes, perhaps no, and it makes no difference, because Esperança's crossing was never about finding another man; it was about ceasing to live on the eve, about bringing her heart from the future to the present, about learning to love the real, any real, instead of the promise, and that crossing she made, and made it alone, and what comes after, come what may, will come to a woman who already knows how to live today, and who therefore is not going to put her life on hold for any unfinished house, for any next year, for any man who is going to.

And there was, after a time, a morning that was the reverse of all the mornings of the twenty years of eve, and that Esperança kept as she had kept, when young, the happy mornings when the house rose. She woke early, in her bed, in her first-used sheets, in her at-last-inhabited house, and instead of thinking, as she had thought twenty years, of the house that was going to be, of the life that was going to begin, of Venturoso, of next year, she thought of none of that; she thought of the day she had before her, of the day of today, of the small and real things she was going to do, and she rose, and went to the window, and opened it, and the sun came in, and there were the flowers she had put, and the small stone from the unfinished house, beside the flowers, reminding her not of the waiting, but of her going out of it, and Esperança stood at the window in the morning sun, in her real life, and felt a thing the mother had never felt, and that Sina had never felt, and that so many women of Véspera had never felt: she felt that her life had, at last, begun, and that it had not begun when a house was ready, nor when a man kept his word, nor when the in-earnest life came; it had begun on the day she stopped awaiting it, and took it, imperfect and real, with her own hands, and gave it its first use, today, in the house she had, which was, she discovered at last, the only one that had ever been hers: her own life, unfinished like all lives, but inhabited, but lived, but hers.
