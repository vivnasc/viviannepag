## 12. The life that begins

The village was taken aback, of course, that Esperança and Venturoso should have broken off after twenty years, and for a time nothing else was talked of, and there were those who said Esperança had done wrong, that a love of twenty years is not thrown away, that Venturoso was such a good man; but there were also those who understood, above all the older women, the ones who had awaited husbands who did not come, and those, on hearing of it, said nothing, but looked at Esperança with a thing in their eyes that was almost envy, the envy of those who had not had the courage to come out in time for one who had.

Esperança justified herself to no one, and went on living, and the village went on growing used to it, as it grows used to everything, and after a few months Esperança and Venturoso broken off were already only one more thing of the village, and the unfinished house on the slope went on on the slope, and Venturoso went on saying he was going to finish it next year, now with no betrothed waiting, and perhaps he would finish it one day, for himself, or perhaps not, and Esperança no longer had part in it, and it was good not to have, it was like setting down a weight she had carried twenty years without knowing she carried it.

And there were, it is true, bad days, days when the waiting, which is a vice like any other, came to her mouth as the cigarette comes to one who has given it up. There was one, in particular, a winter Sunday, when Esperança, alone at home with Florinda asleep, heard the Mass bell and found herself putting on her coat to go up to the slope, without thinking, from the habit of twenty years of Sundays, and was already with her hand on the door when she realised what she was doing, and stopped, with her heart leaping, and had to lean against the wall. And that Sunday it was the house in the sun that held her, the memory of the house in the sun and the question, and it was also, without her expecting it, Sina, because there was a knock at her door that same afternoon, and it was Sina, on her stick, who had come up with difficulty to her house, a thing she never did, and who said to her, without preamble, at the door, "Today at Mass I looked at your place and it was empty, and I looked at your face, and I know that face, child, it's the face of one who was going to go up the slope. So I came. I didn't come to tell you anything. I came only to sit with you so you wouldn't go up." And she sat, Sina, and Esperança made coffee in the grandmother's china, and the two stayed the whole afternoon talking of everything and nothing, and the slope was left unclimbed, and Esperança understood, that afternoon, that Sina, from whom the son had taken the daughter-in-law, had stayed her mother, and that there are ties that are not undone with the engagements, and that she was not so alone in her crossing as she had thought. And on the other bad days she had to remember, by force, Sina at the door, the house in the sun, the mother's phrase, and had to hold herself so as not to go up the slope, so as not to send word to Venturoso, so as not to go back to the eve, which was the place where she knew how to be. Because to leave the waiting is like leaving any vice: one does not leave it all at once, one leaves it every day, and there are relapses, and there is longing for what did one harm, and only with time, day after day of not waiting, does the real life win out over the dream, and the present become, slowly, better company than the promise.

And Esperança's life, the real one, the one she had begun to use, went on growing, slowly, in the way the lives that begin late grow, without haste and with pleasure. She cared for the aunt, as always, but now not as one who passes the time awaiting the life in earnest; she cared for her as one who lives, today, the life she has, and Florinda, in the last year of her life, lived it beside a niece who had taken the case down from the wardrobe, and died, unlike Idalina and unlike what would have been her lot, with no journey hanging above her head, and the last thing she said to Esperança, hand in hand, was, "It's not your mother's phrase, child, hear me well, mine is not your mother's phrase; mine is grateful, child, for you taught me to take down the case." And she was the first woman of that house to die without saying and I who spent my life waiting, and Esperança kept that as she kept the stone from the slope, as a chain that had at last been cut.

And there came, at the end of a year, what had to come, and it was through Rosalina. Because Rosalina, the young bride of the terrace, had her first daughter, and came to Esperança's house, with the little one in her arms, to keep what she had promised in her veil, "Eh, godmother Esperança, I told you I wanted you for godmother again, and here is my daughter waiting for you." And Esperança, who had refused when young to be the godmother of her own life, went to the font to hold Rosalina's daughter, and promised, at the brink of the water, to care for that life that was beginning, and it was, without anyone knowing it but her, the first thing she had promised in twenty years that depended only on herself to keep, a promise with no next year, a promise of today, and she kept it, and became, in the old age drawing near, the godmother of half the village, the Esperança who held the children at the font and took their small hands, she who had never had children because she had kept them, like everything, for the house that did not come. She put more flowers at the window. She used the crockery she had kept. She went to places where she had never been because she kept the outings for the honeymoon that did not come, and she went on foot to Santiago, the journey her mother had postponed for a year she did not have, and she made it for her mother, and lit there a candle to Idalina and another to Florinda and another to Sina, the three women in waiting, and a fourth, smaller, for herself, for the Esperança who waited and who stopped. She took up again friendships she had let die because she lived all turned towards Venturoso and the house, and met one Sunday old Custódio with his grandchildren, and this time she stopped to talk with him, and Custódio introduced his grandchildren to her, and said, with no grief at all, "You did well not to marry me, Esperança, for I was too little for you"; and Esperança answered, with an honesty that freed her, "No, Custódio, it was I who did not know how to see that a roof that keeps out the rain is worth more than a view that goes cold. You told me so, forty years ago, by the wash-house. It took me my life to understand." And Custódio laughed, astonished that she remembered, and said only, "We are what we are, woman," and they went each to their own life, in peace. And she discovered, with an astonishment that made her laugh to herself, that life, today's, the imperfect one, the one with no new house, with no husband, was full, was good, was much more life than the twenty years of eve, and that she had spent twenty years awaiting to begin to live when she could have lived the whole time, in the house she had, with the life she had, if she had not put her heart in an unfinished house.

It is not known, and the book does not say, whether Esperança loved again, whether there came a man who was there instead of one who was going, whether life brought her, late, a love of the present instead of one of the future. Perhaps yes, perhaps no, and it makes no difference, because Esperança's crossing was never about finding another man; it was about leaving off living on the eve, about bringing the heart from the future to the present, about learning to love the real, any real, instead of the promise, and that crossing she made, and made it alone, and what comes after, come what may, will come to a woman who already knows how to live today, and who therefore will no longer put her life on hold awaiting any unfinished house, any next year, any man who is going.

And there came, after a time, a morning that was the reverse of all the mornings of the twenty years of eve, and that Esperança kept as she had kept, when young, the happy mornings on which the house rose. She woke early, in her bed, in her used sheets, in her house inhabited at last, and instead of thinking, as she had thought for twenty years, of the house that was going to be, of the life that was going to begin, of Venturoso, of next year, she thought of none of that; she thought of the day she had ahead, of the day of today, of the small and real things she was going to do, and got up, and went to the window, and opened it, and the sun came in, and there were the geraniums she had put there, and the small stone from the unfinished house, by the flowers, reminding her not of the waiting, but of her coming out of it, and there was, beside the stone, in a clay pot, the fig tree. The fig tree she had sown from the figs of the unfinished house, and that had taken, and that was growing now at the window of her real house, still small, two hand-spans high, with the first tender leaves in the morning sun, the same height the fig tree of the slope parlour had been, twenty years before, on the day she had told Venturoso to pull it out before the floor. But this one, the one at the window, Esperança was not going to leave awaiting any floor; she was going to water it, and watch it grow, and eat its figs in summer, today and every today, in the house she had. And Esperança stayed at the window in the morning sun, with the new fig tree beside the old stone, in her real life, and felt a thing her mother had never felt, and that Sina had never felt, and that Florinda had only felt at the end, and that so many women of Véspera had never felt: she felt that her life had, at last, begun, and that it had not begun when a house was ready, nor when a man kept his word, nor when the life in earnest came; it had begun on the day she stopped awaiting it, and took it up, imperfect and real, with her own hands, and used it, today, in the house she had, which was, she discovered at last, the only one that had ever been hers: her own life, unfinished as all lives are, but inhabited, but lived, but hers, with a fig tree at the window growing in the sun instead of awaiting it.
