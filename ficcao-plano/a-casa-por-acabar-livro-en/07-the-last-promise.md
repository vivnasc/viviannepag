## 7. The last promise

Venturoso, who was charming but was not blind, felt Esperança drawing away, and did what those men do when they feel fleeing the thing that sustains them: he promised higher. And it was the highest promise of twenty years, and for a moment it nearly made her turn back, because hope, in one who is called Esperança, does not die without a fight.

He came to her house, one night, a thing he did not do much, and he came with an air of decision Esperança had not seen in him for years, and said to her, taking her hands, with that sharpness of his that made things seem already done: Esperança, I have thought a great deal. You are right. Enough of postponing. I am going to finish the house, and I am going to set the day. We marry at the end of summer. It is decided. I have already spoken to the men, I am going to put on the roof now, and the windows, and by the end of summer the house is ready and we marry. Enough of next year. This year really is the one, Esperança, and I promise you, look at me, I promise you, we marry at the end of summer.

And Esperança, hearing that, felt the old hope leap in her breast, warm, irresistible, because it was what she had awaited twenty years, it was Venturoso deciding, setting the day, promising an end and not only a beginning, and for an instant, a long and dangerous instant, Esperança nearly turned back on everything Sina and the Keeper had opened to her, nearly said yes, nearly threw herself again at the promise, because the promise, that time, came with a date, end of summer, and a date is a concrete thing, a date seems truth, and Esperança, starved of twenty years, clung to the date as one clings to a plank.

And it was in this, she perceived later, that the greatest danger of those men lay: not in promising little, but in promising, when they feel the person fleeing, exactly what she most needs to hear. Venturoso, for twenty years, had promised the house; but the moment Esperança threatened to go, he promised the marriage, the date, the end, because he felt, with the instinct of those who need someone waiting, that it was that which held her, and offered it, sincere, because the moment he said it he believed it, he saw himself finishing the house, saw himself marrying at the end of summer; and Esperança perceived that the greater promise was not a sign that he was going to change, it was a sign that he had felt her flee, and that a man who only promises in earnest when he is about to be left is a man whose promise is worth the fear of losing her, and not the will to keep it, and that the fear past, the person held again, the promise would loosen once more, as it had loosened for twenty years.

She had now, against the promise, things she had not had before: she had the mother's sentence, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting; she had Sina, he is not going to finish the house; she had the Keeper, the house already is as it is going to stay; she had twenty years of this-year-is-the-one; and she had, above all, a question she had learned to ask and that the promise, however high, could not answer. Instead of saying yes, Esperança, holding Venturoso's hands, asked the question: and if not, Venturoso? If the end of summer comes and the house is not ready, as it has not been for twenty summers, what do we do? Do we set it for the next summer? And Venturoso, caught, laughed, and said it won't happen, woman, this time it's in earnest; and Esperança insisted, with a firmness he had never seen in her: but if it does happen, Venturoso. Promise me a different thing. Do not promise me you'll finish the house. Promise me that, if you do not finish it by the end of summer, we marry all the same, in the house there is, in mine, in yours, in a rented room, as the young girl married. Promise me we marry with the life we have, and not when the house is ready. Promise me that, and I believe you.

And Venturoso stayed silent. And it was in that silence, more than in any promise, that Esperança had her answer. Because Venturoso, who promised so easily to finish the house, to marry at the end of summer, to give her the whole life, could not promise that, the one real thing she asked of him, to marry with the life they had, now, without a ready house; and across his face passed again the fright, and he stammered but Esperança, to marry without the house, to marry like that, in a room, with nothing ready, that is not life, I want to give you a real house, I want it to be beautiful; and Esperança perceived, in that stammering, the whole truth: that Venturoso did not want to marry her, he wanted to marry the promise, he wanted the in-earnest house, the in-earnest life, the beautiful, and that she, Esperança, the real one, the one of today, without a ready house, was not enough for him, because he, like her, loved the to-come and not the present, and that his marriage to her was, from his side too, a marriage in the waiting, awaiting an in-earnest life that would justify marrying, and that that in-earnest life did not come for him as it did not come for her, and that the two were going to stay, each on their own side, awaiting a life that neither of them knew how to begin.

This was the bottom of the book, and it was a silent bottom, without shouts: it was Esperança perceiving, in Venturoso's refusal to marry the real life, that the problem had never been the house. The house was the excuse. The problem was that neither of them knew how to live in the present, that the two loved the promise, and that as long as there was an unfinished house on which to lay the blame of the postponement, the two could go on postponing life and calling it the waiting for the house, and never have to look at the truth, which was that they were afraid, the two of them, to live the real life, with its flaws and its unfinishing, and that they preferred the dreamed life, perfect, of the end of summer that never comes, to the imperfect and real life of today, which is the only one there is. And it hurt Esperança to perceive that the fear was hers too, and not only his; that she could have married, in twenty years, could have forced the real life, could have said to Venturoso fifteen years ago what she was saying to him now, and had not said it, because she too had preferred the safe promise to the risky life, because the unfinished house protected her, too, from having to live, and that therefore the two were accomplices, not victim and culprit, two accomplices of one same fear of living, which they had dressed as faithful love and which had lasted twenty years.

And as Esperança had foreseen, though it hurt to foresee it, the end of summer came, and the house was not ready. Venturoso put on some tiles, at first, with enthusiasm, and then the tile-man was wanting, and then it rained, and then he thought the roof would after all be better another way, and the end of summer came with the house almost the same, and Venturoso, without great distress, said to Esperança what he had said for twenty years, with the phrase slightly changed: next year, Esperança. This year it didn't come off, but next year I'll finish it for sure, and we marry. And Esperança, hearing the next year come back, after the promise of the end of summer, after the date set, felt, instead of the hope of always, a thing new and cold and clear: she felt the end of hope. Not of the Esperança that was her, but of the hope that had bound her, of the faith that the house was going to be finished, of the faith that Venturoso was going to change, of the faith that the in-earnest life was going to begin next year. That hope, the bad one, the one that had kept her twenty years in the waiting, died in that next year repeated after the failed date, and Esperança was left, for the first time in twenty years, without it, and it was an enormous emptiness, but it was, she would perceive, the necessary emptiness, the emptiness that is made when a false hope falls and opens room, at last, for a true one, hope not in Venturoso, not in the house, but in herself, in the life she could still have if she stopped, at last, keeping it awaiting a house that was never going to be finished.
