## 7. The last promise

Venturoso, who was charming but not blind, felt Esperança drawing away, and did what those men do when they feel slipping from them the thing that sustains them: he promised higher. And it was the highest promise of twenty years, and for moments it almost made her step back, because hope, in one who is called Hope, does not die without putting up a fight.

He came to her house, one night, a thing he did not do much, and came with an air of decision Esperança had not seen in him for years, and said to her, taking her hands, with that sharpness of his that made things seem already done, "Esperança, I've thought a great deal. You're right. Enough of postponing. I'm going to finish the house, and I'm going to set the day. We'll marry at the end of the summer. It's decided. I've already spoken to the men, I'm going to put the roof on now, and the windows, and by the end of the summer the house is ready and we marry. Enough of next year. This year really is the one, Esperança, and I promise you, look at me, I promise you, we marry at the end of the summer."

And Esperança, hearing that, felt the old hope leap in her chest, warm, irresistible, because it was what she had awaited for twenty years, it was Venturoso deciding, setting the day, promising an end and not only a beginning, and for an instant, a long and dangerous instant, Esperança almost went back on everything Sina and the Keeper had opened in her, almost said yes, almost threw herself again at the promise, because the promise, that time, came with a date, end of the summer, and a date is a concrete thing, a date seems like truth, and Esperança, famished from twenty years, clung to the date the way one clings to a plank.

And it was in this, she understood it later, that lay the greatest danger of those men: not in their promising little, but in their promising, when they feel the person slipping away, exactly what she most needs to hear. Venturoso, for twenty years, had promised the house; but at the moment Esperança threatened to go, he promised the marriage, the date, the end, because he felt, with the instinct of those who need someone to be waiting, that that was what held her, and offered it, sincere, because at the moment he said it he believed it, saw himself finishing the house, saw himself marrying at the end of the summer; and Esperança understood that the greater promise was not a sign that he was going to change, it was a sign that he had felt her slipping, and that a man who only promises in earnest when they are about to leave him is a man whose promise is worth the fear of losing her, and not the will to keep it, and that, the fear past, the person held again, the promise would slacken once more, as it had slackened for twenty years.

And that night, after Venturoso went away whistling, light, with the finished house in his head and the date of the end of the summer set, Esperança almost relapsed entirely. She went to the chest, and took out the trousseau sheets, the yellow ones, and spread them on the bed, not to use them, for that would be later, but to have them at hand for the new house, to wash them and bleach them in the sun and leave them smelling of clean linen for the end of the summer, for the wedding he had set; and she found herself doing sums for the wedding, thinking of the dress, thinking of whom she should invite, dreaming, again, of the veranda at sunrise, and it was warm and good for her like twenty years of warm and good, because hope, in one who is called Hope, is the sweetest and most dangerous thing there is. And she stayed so an hour, dreaming the finished house with the sheets on the bed, and was falling asleep happy, when her aunt Florinda, who had got up to drink water, passed by the bedroom door, and saw her niece with the yellow sheets spread out and that air in her eyes, and stopped, and asked, without malice, "What's that, child, are you to be married?" and Esperança said, with all her faith back in her chest, "I am, aunt, Venturoso has set it, it's at the end of the summer, he promised." And Florinda stayed a moment looking at her niece, and then looked at the case on top of the wardrobe, instinctively, and Esperança followed her gaze to the case, and it was the case that saved her, the case of a promised journey that was not made, the travelling clothes ready of a woman who had awaited ten years a letter that did not come; and Florinda said nothing, she did not need to, she went back to bed, and Esperança stayed alone with the sheets spread out and the aunt's case on top of the wardrobe, and the sweetness of hope went cold in her hands, and she understood that she was doing what she had done for twenty years, clinging to a date, to a promise, to a for the end of the summer, as the aunt had clung to the letter of passage, and she folded the sheets again, slowly, and put them back in the chest, because it was not yet time to use them, but she put them away now with Florinda watching her from the depth of memory, and fell asleep, that time, not in Venturoso's promise, but in the question the aunt's case had left her: and how many years did you wait, Florinda, with your case packed, for a letter that did not come?

She had now, against the promise, things she had not had before: she had the mother's phrase, and I who spent my life waiting; she had Sina, he is not going to finish the house; she had the Keeper, the house is already as it will stay; she had Florinda's case on top of the wardrobe; she had twenty years of this year is the one; and she had, above all, a question she had learned to ask and that the promise, however high, could not answer. Instead of saying yes, Esperança, holding Venturoso's hands, asked the question: "And if not, Venturoso? If the end of the summer comes and the house is not ready, as it has not been for twenty summers, what do we do? Do we set it for the summer after?" And Venturoso, caught, laughed, and said "It won't happen, woman, this time it's in earnest"; and Esperança insisted, with a firmness he had never seen in her: "But if it happens, Venturoso. Promise me a different thing. Do not promise me you'll finish the house. Promise me that, if you do not finish it by the end of the summer, we marry all the same, in whatever house there is, in mine, in your mother's, in a rented room, as the young girl married. Promise me we marry with the life we have, and not when the house is ready. Promise me that, and I'll believe you."

And Venturoso stayed silent. And it was in that silence, more than in any promise, that Esperança had her answer. Because Venturoso, who promised so easily to finish the house, to marry at the end of the summer, to give her his whole life, could not promise that, the one real thing she asked of him, to marry with the life they had, now, with no house ready; and across his face passed the fright again, and he stammered "But Esperança, to marry without the house, to marry like that, in a room, with nothing ready, that's no life, I want to give you a house in earnest, I want it to be beautiful"; and Esperança understood, in that stammering, the whole truth: that Venturoso did not want to marry her, he wanted to marry the promise, he wanted the house in earnest, the life in earnest, the beautiful, and that she, Esperança, the real one, today's, with no house ready, was not enough for him, because he, like her, loved the to-come and not the present, and that his marriage to her was, on his side too, a marriage in waiting, awaiting a life in earnest that would justify marrying, and that that life in earnest did not come for him as it did not come for her, and that the two of them were going to stay, each on their own side, awaiting a life that neither of them knew how to begin.

This was the bottom of the book, and it was a silent bottom, with no shouts: it was Esperança understanding, in Venturoso's refusal to marry with the real life, that the problem had never been the house. The house was the excuse. The problem was that neither of the two knew how to live in the present, that both loved the promise, and that as long as there was an unfinished house to lay the blame of the postponement on, the two could go on postponing life and calling it waiting for the house, and never have to look at the truth, which was that they were afraid, both of them, of living the real life, with its faults and its unfinishing, and that they preferred the dreamed life, perfect, of the end of summer that never comes, to the imperfect and real life of today, which is the only one that exists. And it hurt Esperança to understand that the fear was hers too, and not only his; that she could have married, in twenty years, could have forced the real life, could have said to Venturoso fifteen years ago what she was saying to him now, and had not said it, because she too had preferred the safe promise to the risky life, because the unfinished house protected her, too, from having to live, and that therefore the two of them were accomplices, not victim and culprit, two accomplices of one same fear of living, which they had dressed as faithful love and which had lasted twenty years.

And as Esperança had foreseen, though it pained her to foresee it, the end of the summer came, and the house was not ready. There was a beginning, it is true, and the beginning was even beautiful, and that was what made it cruel. On the first Sunday after the promise, Venturoso went up the slope with two men and a cart of tiles, and Esperança went up too, and saw, with her heart wanting to believe, Venturoso on top of the half-built roof, in the sun, laying tile, and for one morning it was all as she had dreamed it, the man on top of the house finishing it, the tiles going up from hand to hand, and she below passing the water to the men, and Aprígio, the smith, who happened to be passing on the road and stopped to look, called up to her from below, with a genuine joy, "So this is it at last, Esperança? Shall I put up your gate?" and Esperança, her eyes burning, called back yes, that he should put up the gate, and Aprígio laughed and said about time too, I'll go sharpen the hinges. And by the end of that morning there was a course and a half of tile laid, and Esperança came down the slope feeling, for the first time in twenty years, the life beginning in earnest. But the next Sunday it rained, and there was no work, and the one after Venturoso had the district feasts, and the one after the tile man failed him, having got steadier work elsewhere, and when Venturoso came back to the roof, three weeks later, he stayed looking at the course and a half he had laid, head to one side, with that air Esperança had learned to dread, and said, "You know, Esperança, now that I look at it, I think the Roman tile is more beautiful than this, this is too common for the view the house has"; and Esperança, below, felt the ground go from under her, because she knew that head to one side, it was master Belmiro's and the window frame, it was that of the man who undoes what is made to dream it better; and Venturoso did not actually take out the course and a half, but neither did he lay another, he stayed awaiting the Roman tile that was going to be more beautiful, and the Roman tile never came, and the end of the summer came with the house almost the same, with a course and a half of common tile in the middle of the half-built roof, like a sentence begun and cut off. And the gate went on leaning against the wall, with Aprígio's tag, who had sharpened the hinges for nothing. And Venturoso, with no great distress, said to Esperança what he had said for twenty years, with the phrase slightly changed: "Next year, Esperança. This year it didn't work out, but next year I'll finish it for sure, and we marry." And Esperança, hearing the next year come back, after the promise of the end of the summer, after the date set, felt, instead of the hope of always, a thing new and cold and clear: she felt the end of hope. Not of the Esperança that was she, but of the hope that had bound her, of the faith that the house was going to be finished, of the faith that Venturoso was going to change, of the faith that the life in earnest was going to begin next year. That hope, the bad one, the one that had kept her twenty years in the waiting, died in that next year repeated after the failed date, and Esperança was left, for the first time in twenty years, without it, and it was an enormous emptiness for her, but it was, she would perceive, the necessary emptiness, the emptiness that is made when a false hope falls and opens space, at last, for a true one, the hope not in Venturoso, not in the house, but in herself, in the life she could still have if she stopped, at last, keeping it awaiting a house that was never going to be finished at all.
