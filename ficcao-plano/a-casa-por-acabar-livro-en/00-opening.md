## From the register of Véspera

In the register of the village, by the hand of the Keepers who passed through the book, is found:

Esperança — hope — daughter of people of the village, born in the spring, and the name was a promise, like almost all those of Véspera, but this one was of a particular kind: the mother, who had lived a whole life awaiting the life her husband was going to give her next year, set on her daughter the name of Hope not to bless her, though she thought she did, but because hope was the one thing the house had always had in abundance, and what a house has in abundance is what it passes to its daughters, even when it is what most harms them.

The register says who married whom, and sometimes, in the dates, one reads a whole life of postponement: there are marriages set down late, very late, after engagements of twenty years, and in Véspera it is known that behind a twenty-year engagement there is almost always a man who was going to marry next year, and a woman who waited for him, and a house that was going to be finished and was left unfinished, year after year, as the things of men who live on what they are going to do and never on what they do are left.

This is the fifth book of the shelf of the Bridge, the shelf of those who lose themselves when they love, and within it, it is the book of the one who loves what is to come and does not see what is. It tells the story of a woman who loves a man who for twenty years has been finishing the new house, and who loves in him not the man who exists, putting-off and charming and eternally in works, but the man he promises to be when the house is ready, when they marry in earnest, when they live in earnest, when they are happy in earnest, an in-earnest that is always next year and that never comes. It is not a book against hope, for hope is a good thing; it is a book about hope put in the wrong place, in the promise instead of the person, in the drawing instead of the house, and about the price one pays for loving a man to come while one's own life, that, the real one, goes by, waiting to begin.

And whoever, reading, recognises herself in some Esperança who loves someone's potential and does not see them as they are, who postpones her life awaiting the other to finish the house, change, arrive, be at last what they promise, let her know, from the first line, what it took Esperança a whole life not to wish to know: that one loves a person for what they are, not for what they are to be; that the house unfinished for twenty years perhaps will never be finished, because there are men whose whole work is the unfinishing; and that there is a day, in every life like that, on which one must look at the house in the sun, beautiful as a skeleton, and ask, at last, without fear of the answer, how much of one's life is walled up there, awaiting a life that perhaps will never come.
