## 2. The man of almosts

Esperança learned to wait in a house where one waited a whole life, and it was for that she learned to confuse waiting with living: she saw her mother do it, day after day, year after year, and took postponement for love, because that was what she was taught, without words, in her father's house, the man of almosts.

Esperança's father was an almost his whole life, and he was so in a way the village knew and that had an almost-tenderness, because the man was neither bad nor lazy, he was only a man who lived in what he was going to do and never in what he did. He almost emigrated, once, he had the papers seen to and the boat booked, and on the eve he thought that next year would be better, and stayed; he almost bought the plot next door, that would do so well, and while he thought it over another bought it; he almost changed his life, almost opened a business, almost a thousand things, and of all those lives he almost had he made a single real life, that of staying, that of postponing, that of always living on the eve of a better life that was always next year and that never came.

And what made Esperança's father hard to see, and therefore hard to leave, was that he was always right in his postponements. Each almost had its good reason: he did not emigrate because the year was not good for the boats, he did not buy the plot because the price was going to come down, he did not open the business because it was better to wait for the village to grow. The reasons were good, one by one, and it was for being good that they deceived, because a person who postpones with bad reasons is soon unmasked, but one who always postpones with good reasons seems a prudent man, and the whole house, and the village, and his own wife, treated Esperança's father as a prudent man, when he was, in truth, a man afraid, a man who used the good reasons as Venturoso would use the unfinished house, as an excuse never to have to live the real life, with its risks and its ends.

And Esperança's mother waited for him. She waited for the man he was going to be when he emigrated, when he bought, when he changed, when at last he set out with life; she waited for him engaged, she waited for him married, she waited for him with the small children and with the grown children, and she went on spending her life in that waiting, doing the sums of what they would have next year, keeping things for the life that was going to begin, postponing today's pleasure in the name of tomorrow's ease. She had, Esperança's mother, a chest where she kept the good things for when life improved: the fine china, which was for the visits that would come when they had a better house; the cut of good cloth, which was for a dress she would make when there was an occasion; and she kept, and kept, and life did not improve, because the father postponed it, and the fine china stayed in the chest gathering dust, and the cut of cloth rotted from being kept, and the mother died without having used either the china or the dress, awaiting an occasion her husband was going to give her next year and that never came.

She died young, Esperança's mother, of an illness that came quickly, and she died, said the village, awaiting the life her husband was going to give her next year, with the house he was going to improve unimproved, with the journey they were going to make unmade, with all he had promised unfulfilled, and the last thing she said, it was told in the village, was a sigh and a sentence: and I who spent my life waiting. And Esperança, who was sixteen and at the bedside, heard the sentence, and heard, beneath the sentence, the chest of fine china unused, and the cut of cloth rotted, and perceived, a girl, without yet being able to put it in words, that the mother was not dying only of the illness; she was dying also of a life she had kept for later and that the later had eaten whole.

Esperança was sixteen when her mother died, and she heard that sentence, and and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting was carved into her, and it should have warned her, should have set her on guard against waiting, and it had, for some years, that effect: the girl Esperança swore to herself she would not be like her mother, that she would not spend her life waiting for a man who postpones, that she would not keep the fine china for an occasion that did not come, that she would live, that she would demand, that she would have her life and not its eve. But what the child learns in the body is stronger than what the girl swears in the head, and Esperança, who had sworn not to wait, fell in love, at twenty-odd, precisely with a man who promised, with a Venturoso who was going to finish the most beautiful house in Véspera, and did not see, because her body did not let her see, that she was choosing the father, that she was marrying the waiting, that the man who had made her fall in love was of the same family as the one who had killed the mother with waiting, and that she was going to repeat, with the faithfulness of those who swear not to repeat, the exact life she had fled.

Because this is what houses do with their daughters, and Esperança was the proof: they teach them, without meaning to, to recognise love by the form it had at home, and if at home love had the form of waiting, the daughter will recognise waiting as love, and will fall in love with what makes her wait, because what makes her wait is what tastes to her of home, of love, of truth, and what does not make her wait, the man who is there, who keeps his word, who does not postpone, he will seem to her without spark, without shine, without that charm of the to-come that she learned to confuse with love. And Esperança, who had had, when young, suitors of that kind, men who were there, who kept their word, who offered a life now, had refused them all, without knowing why, finding them not enough, finding in them a lack of something she could not name and that was, she would perceive late, precisely the lack of the promise, the lack of the to-come, the lack of that charm of the almost that was, to her, the smell of love.

There had been one, in particular, a serious young man who had wanted her when she was young, one who already had his house, small but finished, and his work, and who had offered her a modest but real life, marriage now, house now, life now; and Esperança had refused him, and had given as her reason that she did not feel for him that rapture, that dream, she felt for Venturoso, and she was right, she did not feel it, because the serious young man did not promise her an in-earnest life for next year, he offered her a real life for today, modest and sure, and the real life for today, modest and sure, did not have, for Esperança, the shine of the house to come, of the veranda at sunrise, of the beautiful that Venturoso knew how to describe to her; and Esperança traded, without knowing it, the real for the promised, the ready for the to-come, the man who was there for the one who was going to be, and it was that trade, made at twenty-odd with the certainty of love, the trade that cost her the twenty years that followed.

And Venturoso was, in his way, more charming than Esperança's father, and for that more dangerous. Her father postponed without spark, with dry reasons; Venturoso postponed with poetry, with dream, with a capacity to see the house to come and to describe it that was almost a gift, and Esperança, who had inherited the waiting from her mother, found in Venturoso the perfect object of it, a man who not only made her wait but who made the waiting beautiful, who filled the waiting with verandas at sunrise and with hearths where they would grow old, and who made of twenty years of postponement not a desert, but a continuous dream, always postponed and always alive, and Esperança lived in that dream, and the dream was so beautiful that she had never wished to wake from it to the real house, in the wind, roofless, on the slope.

But the mother's sentence was there, at the bottom, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting, and the date on the door-stone had made it rise, and in those days, after seeing the yellow sheets, Esperança began to hear her mother in another way. She began to perceive that the mother's sentence had not been a complaint of the dying; it had been a warning, and a warning that she, Esperança, had received and disobeyed, because she had done exactly what the mother had told her not to do, she had spent her life waiting, and was now, at forty-three, in the same place where the mother had been, awaiting a life the man was going to give next year, with the trousseau yellowing as the mother had had the fine china gathering dust, with the house unfinished as the mother had had the life unbegun, and that if something did not change she would die, she too, with the same sentence in her mouth, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting, and would leave it, perhaps, to a daughter, if she had one, as the mother had left it to her, the chain of waiting passing from woman to woman, making of each one the eve of the next.

And it was that, in those days, the thing that most shook her: the idea that she was repeating the mother. Because a person can bear much, but it is hard to bear the discovery that one has become exactly that from which one fled, that the oath one made when young has come true in reverse, that the life one wished to avoid is the life one is living. And Esperança, who had sworn at sixteen not to be the mother, saw herself at forty-three being the mother point by point, and it was a painful astonishment, of the kind that does not let one sleep, and that opens, in people, either the door of change or the door of despair, and Esperança, lying with that, did not yet know which of the two would open for her, but she knew, for the first time in twenty years, that one had to open, that she could not go on living on the eve of life as the mother had lived, awaiting a house that time, and the door-stone, and the yellow sheets, and the mother's fine china unused, were telling her, ever louder, perhaps would never be finished.
