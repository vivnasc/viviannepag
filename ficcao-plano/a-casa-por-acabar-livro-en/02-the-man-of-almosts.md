## 2. The man of almosts

Esperança learned to wait in a house where one waited a whole life, and that was why she learned to confuse waiting with living: she watched her mother do it, day after day, year after year, and took postponement for love, because that was what she was taught, without words, in her father's house, the house of the man of almosts.

Esperança's father was an almost his whole life, and was so in a way the village knew and that had an almost-tenderness to it, because the man was neither bad nor lazy, he was only a man who lived on what he was going to do and never on what he did. He almost emigrated, once, he had the papers in order and the boat booked, and on the eve he thought next year would be better, and stayed; he almost bought the plot next door, which was going to do so well, and while he thought it over another bought it; he almost changed his life, almost opened a business, almost a thousand things, and of all those lives he almost had he made a single real life, the one of staying, of postponing, of always living on the eve of a better life that was always next year and that never came.

And what made Esperança's father hard to see, and therefore hard to leave, was that he was always right in his postponements. Each almost had its good reason: he had not emigrated because the year was not good for the boats, had not bought the plot because the price was going to fall, had not opened the business because it was better to wait for the village to grow. The reasons were good, one by one, and it was because they were good that they deceived, because a person who postpones with bad reasons soon unmasks himself, but one who always postpones with good reasons looks like a prudent man, and the whole house, and the village, and his own wife, treated Esperança's father as a prudent man, when he was, in truth, a man with fear, a man who used good reasons the way Venturoso would use the unfinished house, as an excuse never to have to live the real life, with its risks and its ends.

The father was called Aníbal, and in the village they called him Aníbal of the Eves, without malice, with that nickname Véspera set on people as one who reads their soul and sums it up in a word. Esperança kept of him one scene that was all the scenes. She was about eight, and it was a summer night, and her father came from the café full of enthusiasm, with a man from the big town, and sat at the table drawing on a paper, with a carpenter's pencil, a mill he was going to set up on the lower stream, and he drew it all, the wheel, the weir, the miller's house, and said to his wife, his eyes shining, "Idalina, this mill will give us our life; by next year, about this time, the wheel will already be turning," and the mother, who was called Idalina, looked at the drawing with a hope Esperança never forgot, the hope of a still-young woman, and said only, low, "So it's for next year, then, Aníbal?" and he said "It's for next year, woman, for certain," and folded the paper and put it away in the drawer of the chest. The mill never went beyond that paper. But the drawer filled, over the years, with papers just like it, the mill, the shop, the emigration, the plot, each one drawn on a night of enthusiasm and folded and put away, and Esperança, as a girl, once opened that drawer in secret and found it full of folded lives, dozens of papers with houses and mills and boats that her father had drawn and never made, and shut the drawer with a tightening she could not name, and which was this: the drawer of her father's chest was the same as the unfinished house on the slope, a place where whole lives were kept that never happened.

And Esperança's mother waited for him. She waited for the man he was going to be when he emigrated, when he bought, when he changed, when at last he set off with his life; waited for him through their engagement, waited for him married, waited for him with the children small and with the children grown, and went on spending her life in that waiting, doing sums of what they were going to have next year, keeping things for the life that was going to begin, postponing today's pleasure in the name of tomorrow's ease. Idalina had a trunk where she kept the good things for when life improved: the fine china, a service of twelve she had inherited from her grandmother, which was for the visitors who would come when they had a better house; the length of good cloth, a navy blue with a thread of silk, which was for a dress she would make when there was an occasion; and she kept, and kept, and life did not improve, because the father postponed it, and the fine china stayed in the trunk gathering dust, and the cloth rotted from being kept, and the mother died without having used either the china or the dress, awaiting an occasion her husband was going to give her next year and that never came.

Esperança remembered one time, just one, when her mother almost rebelled. Christmas was coming, and Idalina took the fine china out of the trunk, set it on the table, blew the dust off it, and said, more to herself than to her daughter, "This year we use the good china, for I am sick of eating Christmas off scratched plates awaiting visitors who never come." And she washed the twelve plates, and the twelve saucers, and the tureen, and set them to drain, and her hands shone with pleasure, and Esperança had never seen her so. But at night Aníbal came home, and saw the grandmother's china on the counter, and said, without malice, with that prudence that was the disguise of his fear, "Eh, woman, the good china at Christmas, no; if a piece breaks then it'll be wanting for when we receive in earnest; leave it for an occasion as it should be." And Idalina stood a moment, a plate in her hand, and Esperança saw the struggle in her face, and saw the struggle lose, and her mother set the plate down slowly, and said, "You're right, Aníbal, let's keep it for an occasion in earnest," and wrapped the twelve plates again in the paper and put them away in the trunk, one by one, and never took them out again in her life. And what stayed with Esperança from that night, and that she only understood decades later, was this: that her mother had held in her hands her own life, washed and shining, ready to be used, and that it had taken only a good reason from a man with fear to wrap it up again and put it away for an occasion in earnest that was, after all, the pretty name for never.

She died young, Idalina, of an illness that came quickly, in the space of a winter, and died, the village said, awaiting the life her husband was going to give her next year, with the house he was going to improve unimproved, with the journey they were going to make unmade, with all he had promised unfulfilled. And Esperança, who was sixteen, was at the bedside to the end, and kept those last days with the cruel sharpness with which one keeps the last days. She remembered her father coming into the room and, with no knack for death as he had had no knack for life, promising the dying woman still that, the moment she was up from this, they would make the journey to Santiago they had been putting off, and her mother, her voice already a thread, smiling at him with a tired tenderness and saying, "Leave it, Aníbal, we'll make the journey next year," and that being, said to death, the last irony of her life, the next year of a woman who no longer had any year at all. And she remembered that, on the eve of the end, her mother had called her, and asked her to open the trunk, and to take out the navy-blue cloth, and Esperança had taken it out, and the fabric had a kept smell and a stain of damp in one corner, rotted without ever having been worn, and her mother had taken it with her thin hands and stayed looking at it, and said, "You see, child, it was for a dress, and I never made it, and now it's good for nothing, not even to lay me out in, it's stained." And then she looked at Esperança with eyes the daughter carried all her life, and said, with the little voice she had, the phrase that was to be her testament: "Do not do as I did, Esperança. Do not keep your life for later. Later is a lie that men with fear tell us, and we believe it because it is easier to wait than to demand. Wear the cloth while it is cloth, child. Do not let it rot in the trunk awaiting an occasion." And the next morning, on a sigh, she said the last thing, which was a murmur and was a sentence, "And I who spent my life waiting," and died. And Esperança, sixteen, at the bedside, heard the phrase, and heard, beneath the phrase, the trunk of the unused fine china, and the cloth rotted in the dead woman's hand, and understood, a girl still, without yet being able to put it into words, that her mother was not dying only of the illness; she was dying also of a life she had kept for later and that later had eaten whole.

Esperança was sixteen when her mother died, and heard that phrase, and and I who spent my life waiting was engraved in her, and it should have warned her, should have put her on her guard against waiting, and it had, for a few years, that effect: the girl Esperança swore to herself she would not be like her mother, would not spend her life awaiting a man who postpones, would not keep the fine china for an occasion that did not come, would live, would demand, would have her life and not the eve of it. But what the child learns in the body is stronger than what the girl swears in her head, and Esperança, who had sworn not to wait, fell in love, in her early twenties, precisely with a man who promised, with a Venturoso who was going to finish the most beautiful house in Véspera, and did not see, because the body would not let her see, that she was choosing her father, that she was marrying the waiting, that the man who had made her fall in love was of the same family as the one who had killed her mother with waiting, and that she was going to repeat, with the fidelity of those who swear not to repeat, the exact life she had fled.

Because this is what houses do to their daughters, and Esperança was the proof: they teach them, without meaning to, to recognise love by the form it had at home, and if at home love had the form of waiting, the daughter will recognise waiting as love, and will fall in love with whatever makes her wait, because what makes her wait is what tastes to her of home, of love, of truth, and what does not make her wait, the man who is there, who keeps his word, who does not postpone, that one will seem to her dull, without shine, without that charm of the to-come she has learned to confuse with love. And Esperança, who had had, when young, suitors of that kind, men who were there, who kept their word, who offered a life already, had refused them all, without knowing why, finding them too little, finding in them the lack of some thing she could not name and which was, she would perceive late, precisely the lack of the promise, the lack of the to-come, the lack of that charm of the almost that was, to her, the scent of love.

There had been one, in particular, a serious young man who had wanted her when she was young, and he was called Custódio, and was a mason, of good hands, of those who set a wall plumb the first time. He already had his own house, small but ready, with the roof on and the water piped in, a house he had raised himself, and his steady work, and he had offered Esperança a modest but real life, marriage already, house already, life already. Esperança remembered an afternoon, by the wash-house, when Custódio, awkward, without Venturoso's poetry, had said to her what he knew how to say, which was little and was everything. "Esperança," he said, "I don't know how to promise you great things. I've no gift of the gab. My house is small, it has only two rooms, but it has a roof, and the rain doesn't get in, and it's ready today. If you want, we'll marry next month and you go to live in it now. I don't promise you the best view in the village. I promise you a house the rain doesn't get into." And Esperança, in her early twenties, had heard that and found it too little, found in it a lack of dream, and told him she was very fond of him but did not feel what one ought to feel, and Custódio had bowed his head, and said only, with a humility the mature Esperança would look back on with shame, "Well, I knew it already. You want the one up top, the one with the view. I'm the one down below, the one with the roof. But look, Esperança, mark one thing before you decide: a roof that keeps out the rain is worth more than a view that goes cold." And he had gone away, with no apparent grief, and married two years later another, a girl from the lower fields, and had children, and grandchildren, and Esperança saw them sometimes on Sundays, old Custódio walking his grandchildren by the hand, in a small life, full and finished; and it was that sight, years later, of Custódio with his grandchildren by the hand, that weighed on her most, more than any word, because it was the life she had refused, real and modest and ready, and which she had chosen to trade for the view of the unfinished house. Because the serious young man did not promise her a life in earnest next year, he offered her a real life for today, and the real life for today, modest and sure, did not have, for Esperança, the shine of the house to come, of the veranda at sunrise, of the beauty Venturoso knew how to describe; and Esperança traded, without knowing it, the real for the promised, the ready for the to-come, the man who was there for the one who was going to be, and it was that trade, made in her early twenties with the certainty of love, the trade that cost her the twenty years that followed.

And Venturoso was, in his way, more charming than Esperança's father, and therefore more dangerous. Her father postponed without grace, with dry reasons; Venturoso postponed with poetry, with dream, with a capacity to see the house to come and to describe it that was almost a gift, and Esperança, who had inherited the waiting from her mother, found in Venturoso its perfect object, a man who not only made her wait but made the waiting beautiful, who filled the waiting with verandas at sunrise and with hearths where they would grow old, and who made of twenty years of postponement not a desert, but a continuous dream, always postponed and always alive, and Esperança lived in that dream, and the dream was so beautiful that she had never wished to wake from it to the real house, in the wind, without a roof, on the slope.

But the mother's phrase was there, at the bottom, and I who spent my life waiting, and the date on the door-stone had made it rise, and in those days, after seeing the yellowed sheets, Esperança began to hear her mother in another way. She began to understand that her mother's phrase had not been the complaint of a dying woman; it had been a warning, and a warning she, Esperança, had received and disobeyed, because she had done exactly what her mother told her not to do, had spent her life waiting, and was now, at forty-three, in the same place where her mother had been, awaiting a life the man was going to give next year, with the trousseau yellowing as the mother had had the fine china gathering dust, with the unfinished house as the mother had had the life unbegun, and that if she did not change something she would die, she too, with the same phrase in her mouth, and I who spent my life waiting, and would leave it, perhaps, to a daughter, if she had one, as the mother had left it to her, the chain of waiting passing from woman to woman, making of each the eve of the next.

And there was a moment, in those days, when the repetition became flesh and bone for her, and it was before the mirror of her mother's chest of drawers, which had come to Florinda's house and that Esperança had used for years without thinking. She was putting a kerchief on her head to go to Mass, and looked at herself in the mirror, and the mirror gave back to her, for an instant, in a trick of the light and the tiredness, her mother's face, because she was now the age at which the mother had died, or near it, and had her features, and had the kerchief set the same way, and Esperança stood a moment looking at the mother who was her, or at herself who was the mother, and had a shudder, and went to the drawer of the chest, the bottom one, where Florinda had kept Idalina's things, and rummaged, and found there, at the back, wrapped in a cloth, the navy-blue cloth, the one the mother had shown her at the brink of death, rotted, with the stain of damp, kept for thirty years for a life that did not come, and Esperança took it, and unfolded it, and the fabric came apart a little in her hands, rotten, and she understood that she was holding, literally, in her hands, the life her mother had kept and lost, and that she had, in the drawer of her own chest, inherited the rotted cloth as she had inherited the waiting, and that if she did nothing she would leave it there another thirty years, and would leave it, perhaps, to another woman, with the same stain, with the same smell, with the same warning unheard. And it was that, in those days, the thing that shook her most: the idea that she was repeating her mother, and that she had the rotted proof in her hands. Because a person can bear much, but it is hard to bear the discovery that one has become exactly what one fled, that the oath one swore when young has been kept inside out, that the life one wished to avoid is the life one is living. And Esperança, who had sworn at sixteen not to be her mother, saw herself at forty-three being her mother point by point, and it was a painful astonishment, of the kind that does not let one sleep, and that opens, in people, either the door of change or the door of despair, and Esperança, lying with it, did not yet know which of the two was going to open for her, but knew, for the first time in twenty years, that one had to open, that she could not go on living on the eve of life as her mother had lived, awaiting a house that time, and the door-stone, and the yellowed sheets, and her mother's unused fine china, were telling her, ever more loudly, perhaps would never be finished at all.
