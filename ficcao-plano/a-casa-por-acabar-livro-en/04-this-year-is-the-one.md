## 4. This year is the one

Venturoso had a phrase that was sister to Esperança's, and the two, together, had built the whole engagement: she said when the house is ready, and he said this year is the one, and between the two phrases fit a life that never happened.

That Sunday, after Mass, Esperança went with Venturoso up to the house, as they did sometimes, and it was an afternoon like so many, and was, for that very reason, revealing, because Esperança went, for the first time, to see and not only to dream. Venturoso climbed the slope talking, as always, full of plans, this year I'll finish the roof, Esperança, this year is the one, I've already spoken to a man who'll make me the tiles at a good price, and for the spring I'll put in the windows, and in the summer, who knows, we can already put the bed in the bedroom, and by Christmas, God willing, we marry. And he described it all with that sharpness that was his gift, he saw the roof on, the windows, the bed in the bedroom, and described it to Esperança as if it already were, and Esperança, twenty years, had heard that and believed, because he believed; but that Sunday, for the first time, instead of hearing the words, Esperança heard the tense of the verbs.

I'll finish, I'll put, we can, we marry. All in the future. All in the this year, the for the spring, the by Christmas. Twenty years of futures, and Esperança, that afternoon, at the door of the unfinished house, understood that never, in twenty years, had she heard Venturoso speak in the present, in I did, in it is done, in here it is; Venturoso lived in the future indicative, it was his house, the future, and the present, the real, the house in the wind, without a roof, with the grass grown, that Venturoso did not see, or saw as a passing thing, an interval between what had been and what was going to be, without realising that that interval had lasted twenty years and was, itself, his whole life.

And Esperança remembered, there, at the door, a scene from some ten years before that she had always put away as proof of Venturoso's zeal and that now unfolded with another meaning. Venturoso had, that year, hired a carpenter from outside, master Belmiro, a dry man and good at the trade, to set the window frames for him, and Belmiro had come, and measured, and set the first frame in the wall of the sunrise, plumb, perfect, and Esperança remembered the joy she had felt to see, at last, a window set in that house, a window in earnest, with glass and all, the first. And Venturoso had come at the end of the afternoon, and looked at the set frame, and instead of liking it, had stayed looking at it with his head to one side, and said, "You know, Belmiro, now that I see it, I think the window's better more to the left, more towards the corner, it catches the sunrise better"; and Belmiro, who was a man of few words, had looked at him and said only, "Senhor Venturoso, the window's plumb and it's where the plan has it, and to move it now is to open the wall again and lose a day's work"; and Venturoso had laughed, charming, and said "But it's our house, Belmiro, it must be perfect, a day more is nothing"; and had had the set frame taken out. And Belmiro took it out, with a closed face, and opened the wall again, and never came back to that work, and Esperança learned later, in the village, that Belmiro had said at the café that he had worked on many a house in his life but that a house like that one, of a man who has you take out what is well made to dream it better, he would not set foot in again, for one does not work for those who do not want the work done, they want it undone. And Esperança, at the time, had been offended on Venturoso's account and thought Belmiro a grumbler; and now, at the door of the house, ten years later, with the wall of the sunrise still open and with no window at all, because the better window to the left had never come to be set, she understood that Belmiro had seen, in one day, what she had taken twenty years not to see: that Venturoso did not take out the set window for love of perfection; he took it out because a set window is a finished thing, and Venturoso could not bear finished things, he needed them unfinished, because as long as the house was unfinished he could go on dreaming it, and a plumb window, real, with glass, was a little bit of the future turned present, and the present, for Venturoso, was always less beautiful than the promise, and so he undid it, without knowing it, to give it back to the dream.

And there was a thing, in the way Venturoso looked at the house, that Esperança, that Sunday, saw for the first time and that moved her and frightened her at once: that Venturoso, looking at the walls in the wind, the roof half done, the grass grown, did not see the ruin; he saw the finished house. His eyes set, over the real skeleton, the house of the dream, with such force that he really saw it, there, in front of him, finished, and described it to Esperança pointing, here will be the veranda, see, and he looked at an opening without a wall and saw a veranda, and was sincere, he really saw it; and Esperança understood that Venturoso had a kind of gift and disease at once, that of seeing the to-come with such sharpness that he stopped seeing the present, and that it was that gift-disease that made him so charming and so incapable, because a man who sees the finished house where it is not never feels the urgency to build it, he has it already, in his head, and one who already has a thing in his head does not rush to make it with his hands.

And Esperança did a thing she had never done, and that cost her: instead of believing, she asked. "Venturoso," she said, and her voice trembled a little, "you told me this year is the one last year too. And five years ago. And ten. I remember, Venturoso. You say this year is the one every year. And the roof isn't on." And she asked him, with an honesty that opened between the two of them a crack that had never been there, "Do you really think you're going to finish this house?" And Venturoso stayed a moment without an answer, and it was the first time in twenty years Esperança had seen him without an answer, and across his face passed, for an instant, a thing that was not the usual charm, it was a fright, the fright of one caught, not in a lie, for Venturoso did not lie, but in a truth he himself had never let himself see, and then the charm came back, and he laughed, and said "Of course I'll finish it, woman, what talk is this, this year is the one, I promise you," and embraced her, and Esperança let herself be embraced, but for the first time the this year is the one did not settle her, because she had seen, beneath it, the fright, and the fright told more truth than the promise.

Because that was Esperança's discovery that afternoon, and it was hard for her: that Venturoso did not deceive her, and that this, instead of excusing him, condemned him still more. A man who lies knows he lies; a man like Venturoso does not lie, he believes, he sees the finished house in his head with such sharpness that for him to finish it is almost a detail, a formality the future will see to, and so he promises with truth, and so he is so hard to call to account, because there is nothing to catch, there is no lie, there is only a man who lives in the future and confuses seeing the house finished with building it, wishing with doing, this year is the one with it is done, and who goes through his whole life so, sincere, charming, postponing, without ever realising he postpones, because for him each postponement is only one more this year that has not yet come.

And Esperança understood, that afternoon, at the door of the house, a thing that broke her: that she loved precisely that. That what had made her fall in love with Venturoso, and had bound her twenty years, was exactly his capacity to see the house to come, to promise so beautifully, to live in the future indicative; that she loved the man of the future, the one of this year is the one, and that that man was, by nature, a man who never reached the present, who never finished anything, because to finish a thing is to bring it from the future to the present, and Venturoso did not know how to live in the present, only in the future, and that therefore to love him was to love the waiting, was to choose, with eyes shut, her mother's life, was to put her whole life into the veranda at sunrise he described and never into the real house where the grass grew.

And there was, before they came down, a small thing that said more than all the talk. Esperança, on an impulse, wanted to bring Venturoso into the present, there, that afternoon, and said to him, with a forced cheer, "Then what if we stayed here now to watch the sunset, Venturoso, today, you and I, in our house, even like this without a roof, with the view there is, and we opened that wine you brought and never drank?"; and Venturoso, who did not understand what she was asking of him, which was only a present, a real hour in the real house, laughed and said "Ah no, woman, not now, now this is under works, it's all earth, you can't be comfortable here; when the house is ready, that's when we'll watch the sunsets here, with the veranda made, with the chairs, that'll be a wonder." And Esperança fell silent, because there it was, whole, in a gesture: she had offered him an hour of the present, and he had refused it to keep it for a future that did not come, exactly as her father had kept the china and her mother the dress, and not even to watch a sunset could Venturoso come down to today, he wanted it too in earnest, with the veranda, with the chairs, next year; and Esperança understood that if she waited, to live, for that house to be ready, she would never see, with that man, a single sunset, because the sunset is today, and Venturoso only knew how to make appointments for later.

They came down the slope in silence, a rare thing, and Venturoso, feeling that something had changed, tried harder at the charm, talked more of the house, of the life they were going to have, and each word of his, which before had charmed Esperança, that afternoon hurt her, because she no longer heard them as promises, she heard them as what they were, as the future indicative of a man who lived in order not to reach the present, and the more he described the house to come, the more Esperança saw the unfinished house, and the more he spoke of the life in earnest next year, the more she felt the real life, today's, going by, like the sheets in the chest, yellowing awaiting a use that did not come.

And she thought, coming down the slope, of a thing terrible and symmetrical: that she and Venturoso were the perfect couple of postponement, made for each other like a lock for its wrong key. Because a man who postpones needs, in order to postpone his whole life without guilt, a woman who waits without charging it to him, and a woman who has learned to wait needs, in order to wait her whole life without seeing herself, a man who promises without keeping; and Venturoso had found in Esperança the betrothed who let him postpone for ever, and Esperança had found in Venturoso the betrothed who always gave her a good reason to wait, and the two fit so well in their shared disease that they had built, in twenty years, not a house, but a prison, perfect, comfortable, charming, proof against time, and which was called, in the village's mouth, the most faithful love in Véspera.

At the parting, at the door of her house, Venturoso, perhaps feeling the distance, said to her, with all his tenderness, and with the phrase he had said for twenty years, "Have faith, Esperança. The house will be finished. And we'll be happy. Next year." And Esperança looked at him, at that man she loved, charming, sincere, promising her happiness next year for twenty years, and instead of saying the I-have-faith of always, instead of receiving the promise and keeping it as she had kept twenty, she said a thing she had never said, low, more to herself than to him: "And if the year never comes, Venturoso?" And he did not hear, or made as if he did not hear, and went away whistling, down the slope, light, with the finished house in his head, and Esperança stayed at the door watching him go off, and understood, watching him go light with the house he was never going to build in his head, that Venturoso did not suffer with the unfinished house, that the unfinishing did not weigh on him, that he was going to be happy so, in the future, his whole life; and that the only one who suffered, the only one who paid the price of the unfinishing, the only one for whom the this year is the one was a torment and not a dream, was she, Esperança, who had her feet in the present and her heart in his future, and who had been splitting between the two for twenty years, and who was going to go on splitting, she understood it at that door, until she decided, at last, either to bring her heart into the present, into the real life, or to lose it for good, awaiting a house that the man she loved, she saw it now, was never going to finish, not from malice, but from being who he was, a man of the future, a man of this year is the one, a man unfinished, like the house.
