## 4. This year is the one

Venturoso had a phrase that was sister to Esperança's, and the two, together, had built the whole engagement: she said when the house is ready, and he said this year is the one, and between the two phrases there fit a life that never happened.

That Sunday, after Mass, Esperança went with Venturoso up to the house, as they did sometimes, and it was an afternoon like so many, and it was, for that very reason, revealing, because Esperança went, for the first time, to see and not only to dream. Venturoso climbed the slope talking, as always, full of plans, this year I'll finish the roof, Esperança, this year is the one, I've already spoken to a man who'll make me the tiles at a good price, and by spring I'll put in the windows, and in summer, who knows, we can put the bed in the room, and by Christmas, God willing, we marry. And he described it all with that sharpness that was his gift, he saw the roof on, the windows, the bed in the room, and described it to Esperança as if it already were, and Esperança, twenty years, had heard that and believed, because he believed; but that Sunday, for the first time, instead of hearing the words, Esperança heard the tense of the verbs.

I'll finish, I'll put, we can, we marry. All in the future. All in the this-year, in the by-spring, in the by-Christmas. Twenty years of futures, and Esperança, that afternoon, at the door of the unfinished house, perceived that never, in twenty years, had she heard Venturoso speak in the present, in the I-did, in the it-is-done, in the here-it-is; Venturoso lived in the future tense, it was his house, the future, and the present, the real, the house in the wind, roofless, with the grass grown, that Venturoso did not see, or saw as a passing thing, an interval between what had been and what was going to be, without noticing that that interval had lasted for twenty years and was, itself, the whole life.

And there was a thing, in the way Venturoso looked at the house, that Esperança, that Sunday, saw for the first time and that moved her and frightened her at once: that Venturoso, looking at the walls in the wind, the half-roof, the grown grass, did not see the ruin; he saw the house ready. His eyes put, over the real skeleton, the house of the dream, with such force that he saw it indeed, there, before him, finished, and described it to Esperança pointing, here will be the veranda, see, and he looked at an opening without a wall and saw a veranda, and he was sincere, he saw it indeed; and Esperança perceived that Venturoso had a kind of gift and of illness at once, that of seeing the to-come with such sharpness that he stopped seeing the present, and that it was that gift-illness that made him so charming and so incapable, because a man who sees the house ready where it is not never feels the urgency to build it, he already has it, in his head, and whoever already has a thing in their head does not run to make it with their hands.

And Esperança did a thing she had never done, and that cost her: instead of believing, she asked. Venturoso, she said, and her voice trembled a little, you told me this year is the one last year too. And five years ago. And ten. I remember, Venturoso. You say this year is the one every year. And the roof is not on. And she asked him, with an honesty that opened between the two a crack that had never been there: do you think, truly, that you are going to finish this house? And Venturoso was a moment without an answer, and it was the first time in twenty years that Esperança saw him without an answer, and across his face passed, for an instant, a thing that was not the usual charm, it was a fright, the fright of one caught, not in a lie, for Venturoso did not lie, but in a truth he himself had never let himself see, and then the charm came back, and he laughed, and said of course I'll finish it, woman, what talk is that, this year is the one, I promise you, and he embraced her, and Esperança let herself be embraced, but for the first time the this-year-is-the-one did not settle her, because she had seen, beneath it, the fright, and the fright told more truth than the promise.

Because that was Esperança's discovery that afternoon, and it was hard for her: that Venturoso did not deceive her, and that this, instead of excusing him, condemned him still more. A man who lies knows he lies; a man like Venturoso does not lie, he believes, he sees the house ready in his head with such sharpness that for him to finish it is almost a detail, a formality the future will see to, and for that he promises with truth, and for that he is so hard to charge, because there is nothing to catch, there is no lie, there is only a man who lives in the future and who confuses seeing the house ready with building it, wishing with doing, the this-year-is-the-one with the it-is-done, and who goes on so his whole life, sincere, charming, postponing, without ever perceiving that he postpones, because for him each postponement is only one more this-year that has not yet come.

And Esperança perceived, that afternoon, at the door of the house, a thing that broke her: that she loved precisely that. That what had made her fall in love with Venturoso, and what had held her twenty years, was exactly his capacity to see the house to come, to promise so beautifully, to live in the future tense; that she loved the man of the future, the one of the this-year-is-the-one, and that that man was, by nature, a man who never reached the present, who never finished anything, because to finish a thing is to bring it from the future to the present, and Venturoso did not know how to live in the present, only in the future, and that therefore to love him was to love the waiting, was to choose, with closed eyes, the mother's life, was to put one's whole life in the veranda at sunrise he described and never in the real house where the grass grew.

They came down the slope in silence, a rare thing, and Venturoso, feeling that something had changed, tried harder at the charm, spoke more of the house, of the life they were going to have, and each word of his, which before charmed Esperança, that afternoon hurt her, because she no longer heard them as promises, she heard them as what they were, as the future tense of a man who lived so as not to reach the present, and the more he described the house to come, the more Esperança saw the unfinished house, and the more he spoke of the in-earnest life for next year, the more she felt the real life, that of today, pass by, like the sheets in the chest, yellowing, awaiting a use that did not come.

And she thought, coming down the slope, a thing terrible and symmetrical: that she and Venturoso were the perfect couple of postponement, made for each other like a lock for its wrong key. Because a man who postpones needs, to postpone his whole life without guilt, a woman who waits without charging, and a woman who learned to wait needs, to wait her whole life without seeing herself, a man who promises without keeping; and Venturoso had found in Esperança the betrothed who let him postpone forever, and Esperança had found in Venturoso the betrothed who always gave her a good reason to wait, and the two fitted so well in their common illness that they had built, in twenty years, not a house, but a perfect prison, comfortable, charming, proof against time, and called, in the mouth of the village, the most faithful love of Véspera.

At the parting, at the door of her house, Venturoso, perhaps feeling the distance, said to her, with all tenderness, and with the phrase he had said for twenty years: have faith, Esperança. The house will be finished. And we will be happy. Next year. And Esperança looked at him, at that man she loved, charming, sincere, promising her happiness for next year for twenty years, and instead of saying the I-have-faith of always, instead of receiving the promise and keeping it as she had kept twenty, she said a thing she had never said, under her breath, more to herself than to him: and if the year never comes, Venturoso? And he did not hear, or made as if he did not hear, and went off whistling, down the slope, light, with the house ready in his head, and Esperança stayed at the door watching him go off, and perceived, watching him go light with the house he was never going to build in his head, that Venturoso did not suffer with the unfinished house, that the unfinishing did not weigh on him, that he was going to be happy so, in the future, his whole life; and that the only one who suffered, the only one who paid the price of the unfinishing, the only one for whom the this-year-is-the-one was a torment and not a dream, was she, Esperança, who had her feet in the present and her heart in his future, and who was split between the two for twenty years, and who was going to go on being split, she perceived at that door, until she decided, at last, either to bring her heart to the present, to the real life, or to lose it for good, awaiting a house that the man she loved, she saw it now, was never going to finish, not from malice, but from being who he was, a man of the future, a man of the this-year-is-the-one, a man unfinished, like the house.
