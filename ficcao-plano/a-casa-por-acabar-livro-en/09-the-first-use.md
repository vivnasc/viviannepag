## 9. The first use

The first thing Esperança did, when she decided to take her own life instead of awaiting it, was small and was enormous: she went to the chest, took out the trousseau sheets, the ones she had embroidered in her youth for the house that was not finished, and put them on the bed. On her own bed. That night.

For twenty years she had kept those sheets for the new house, for the in-earnest life, for the bed of the marriage that was going to happen when the house was ready; and they were yellow from being kept, with the creases marked, with the smell of being shut away, and they had been embroidered for a use that did not come, like she herself. And Esperança, that night, decided she was not going to keep them any longer. She took them out of the chest, spread them, and though they were yellow, though they were wedding sheets on a single woman's bed, she put them on her own bed, and lay down in them, and it was the first time in twenty years that she slept in the sheets she had embroidered, and it was strange to her, and it was painful, because they were sheets of a life that had not happened; but it was, above all, freeing, because it was the first time Esperança used, today, a thing she had kept for a tomorrow that did not come, and in using it, in giving the trousseau its first use on her single woman's bed of forty-three, she was telling herself, with a gesture, what her head still barely dared: that life was today, that she would wait no longer, that she would give, at last, the first use to her own life, on the bed she had, without a ready house, without a marriage, without Venturoso, if need be, but today.

And lying down in the yellow sheets, she remembered the mother, and the fine china the mother had kept for an occasion that did not come, and the cut of cloth that had rotted in the chest, and she perceived that she was doing, with the sheets, the opposite of what the mother had done with the china: the mother had kept the good for a later that life ate from her, and she, Esperança, was going to give the good its first use today, was going to use what she had kept, was going to refuse to die, like the mother, with the fine china unused in the chest. And she thought that perhaps that was the only way to honour the mother, to give meaning to the sentence of the end, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting: not to repeat, but to use, to live what the mother kept, to use the china the mother did not use, to sleep in the sheets life did not let her use, and to cut, in one gesture, the chain of women who keep their life for an occasion that death eats from them before it comes.

And she did not stop at the sheets. It was as if, the trousseau used, a door had opened, and Esperança began to do, one by one, the things she had postponed for twenty years awaiting the in-earnest life. She bought for her house, the single woman's one, the one she shared with the aunt, some beautiful things she had always wanted and had always left for the new house, some crockery, a quilt, some pots of flowers at the window, and in putting those things in her house of today she perceived a thing that moved her: that she had a house. That the unfinished house on the slope had never been her house; her house was this one, that of today, the one where she slept and ate and cared for the aunt for years, and that she had treated her whole life as a place of passage, as a provisional house where one waits until the in-earnest house is ready, and that by dint of treating it as provisional she had never made it hers, never made it beautiful, never truly inhabited it, because her heart dwelt in the unfinished house, in the promise, and not in the real house where her body lived.

And in making her house of today beautiful, in inhabiting it at last, Esperança did to her life what was lacking to be done to Venturoso's house: she finished it. Not with bricks, but with presence. She brought her heart back from the slope to the house where she lived, from the future to the present, from the promise to the real, and the real house, which had been ready for years and which she had never inhabited, filled suddenly with life, with flowers, with first-used things, and Esperança perceived, with an astonishment that was almost comic, that the ready house she had awaited for twenty years had always been there, beneath her feet, awaiting not tiles, but her ceasing to think it provisional and accepting it as her life. Venturoso's unfinished house was never going to be finished; but Esperança's house, the real one, the one of today, that was ready, had always been, and it only lacked her moving into it with her heart, which had been for twenty years on loan to the slope.

The aunt, old, who watched her niece change, asked her, one day, and Venturoso, Esperança, and the house on the slope, are you going to leave it all?, and Esperança answered a thing she herself heard with surprise, so clear: aunt, I do not yet know about Venturoso. But the house on the slope, that, I have left. Not the house, for that is his. I have left awaiting it. I have left putting my life there, awaiting him to finish it. My house is this one, aunt, and my life is today, and I have already lost twenty years awaiting to begin to live in a house that is not finished. I lose no more. And the aunt, who was old and had seen many people lose their life in the waiting, looked at her niece using the sheets and putting flowers at the window, and said only, with brimming eyes: good, child. Your mother did not know how. Good that you knew.

And at night, lying in the yellow first-used sheets, in her at-last-inhabited house, Esperança did a thing she had not done in a long while: she slept without awaiting anything. She did not fall asleep dreaming the house ready, did not fall asleep counting when the marriage would be, did not fall asleep in Venturoso's future; she fell asleep in her present, in her bed, with her sheets, in her life, and it was a sleep different from all those of twenty years, because it was the sleep of one who has stopped living on the eve and begun to live on the day, and discovered that the day, however imperfect it was, however it had no new house or marriage or in-earnest life, was better than the eve, because the day existed, and the eve, that, was only a beautiful way of postponing forever the existing.
