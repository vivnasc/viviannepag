## 9. The first use

The first thing Esperança did, when she decided to take up her own life instead of awaiting it, was small and was enormous: she went to the chest, took out the trousseau sheets, the ones she had embroidered when young for the house that was not finished, and put them on the bed. On her bed. That night.

For twenty years she had kept those sheets for the new house, for the life in earnest, for the bed of the marriage that was going to happen when the house was ready; and they were yellow from being kept, with the creases marked, with the shut-in smell, and they had been embroidered for a first use that did not come, like she herself. And Esperança, that night, decided she was not going to keep them any longer. She took them from the chest, spread them out, and though they were yellow, though they were wedding sheets on a single woman's bed, she put them on her own bed, and lay down on them, and it was the first time in twenty years she slept in the sheets she had embroidered, and it was strange to her, and it was painful to her, because they were sheets of a life that had not happened; but it was, above all, liberating, because it was the first time Esperança used, today, a thing she had kept for a tomorrow that did not come, and in using it, in giving the first use to the trousseau on her single woman's bed at forty-three, she was saying to herself, with a gesture, what her head still scarcely dared: that life was today, that she was not going to wait any more, that she was going to give, at last, the first use to her own life, in the bed she had, with no house ready, with no marriage, with no Venturoso, if need be, but today.

And the next morning she did a thing that was the reverse of her mother's, and that freed her for good from the length of cloth. She took it from the drawer of the chest, the rotten navy blue the mother had kept thirty years and she more she knew not how many, and instead of putting it away again, took it to the yard, and dug a hole by Florinda's rosebush, and buried it, the length of cloth entire, the life the mother had not worn, and threw earth over it, and said to it, low, "Stay there, mother, for I keep you no longer in the drawer to rot; go into the earth, which is where the things one let rot do rot, and may the rosebush eat you and make of you a flower, for a rose today is worth more than a dress for an occasion that does not come." And she planted beside it a new rosebush, and from then on, whenever the rosebush flowered, Esperança remembered her mother, not with the phrase of the end, but with the flower, and it was the way she had found to turn the inheritance of waiting into a living thing, to take the mother out of the drawer and put her in the sun. And in lying down, the night before, on the yellow sheets, she had already remembered the mother, and the fine china the mother had kept for an occasion that did not come, and the length of cloth that had rotted in the chest, and had understood that she was doing, with the sheets, the contrary of what the mother had done with the china: the mother had kept the good for a later that life ate from her, and she, Esperança, was going to use the good today, was going to use what she had kept, was going to refuse to die, as the mother had, with the fine china unused in the chest. And she thought that perhaps this was the only way to honour the mother, to give meaning to the phrase of the end, and I who spent my life waiting: not to repeat, but to use, to live what the mother had kept, to use the china the mother had not used, to sleep in the sheets life had not let her use, and to cut, in a gesture, the chain of women who keep life for an occasion that death eats from them before it comes.

And there was, in those first days, a gesture that was the brother of the sheets', and that made Florinda weep. Esperança remembered the grandmother's fine china service, the one of twelve pieces the mother had never used and that had come to the aunt's house with the mother's other things when the father sank, and that had been for years on top of a cupboard, wrapped in yellowed newspaper, awaiting the in-earnest occasion that had killed the mother. And Esperança took it down, piece by piece, unwrapped the twelve plates and the twelve saucers and the tureen with the lid chipped in one corner, washed them with care, and instead of putting them away again, set the table with them, the table of the aunt's house, on some weekday or other, with no visitors, with no occasion, only she and Florinda, to eat a soup. And Florinda, seeing her sister-in-law's china set on the table for a weekday soup, stayed a moment without understanding, and then understood, and her eyes filled, and she said, "But child, this china is for visitors, it's your grandmother's," and Esperança answered, with a gentle firmness that was new in her, "Today the visitors are us, aunt. My mother died awaiting an occasion for this china. I do not die. The occasion is today, it is the soup, it is the two of us." And they ate the soup off the grandmother's plates, the two of them, on a weekday, and Florinda, halfway through, set down her spoon and said, low, with a voice that came from very far away, "You know, I too have a thing kept for a journey I didn't make," and Esperança said "I know, aunt, the case on top of the wardrobe," and Florinda looked at her, astonished, "I didn't know you noticed," and Esperança said "I notice, aunt, I've slept beneath it for twenty years." And that afternoon, without arranging it, the two of them went up to the case, and Florinda opened it, after fifty years, and inside were, folded, the clothes of a young girl for a Brazil that was not, eaten by the moth, unrecognisable, and Florinda passed her hand over them and did not weep, and said only, "It's plain it's no good for the journey now," and Esperança said "The journey, no, aunt, but the case is good for you to take down from up there, for you no longer need it to remind you not to be a fool twice, now you remember through me." And they took the case down from the wardrobe, and Florinda slept, that night, for the first time in fifty years, without the luggage of a dead journey above her head.

And she did not stop at the sheets nor the china. It was as if, the trousseau once used, a door had opened, and Esperança began to do, one by one, the things she had postponed twenty years awaiting the life in earnest. She bought, for her house, the single woman's, the one she shared with the aunt, some beautiful things she had always wanted and had always left for the new house, some everyday crockery, a coloured bedspread, some pots of geraniums for the window, and in putting those things in her house of today she understood a thing that moved her: that she had a house. That the unfinished house on the slope had never been her house; her house was this one, today's, the one where she slept and ate and cared for the aunt for years, and that she had treated it her whole life as a place of passing through, as a provisional house where one waits until the house in earnest is ready, and that by dint of treating it as provisional she had never made it her own, never made it beautiful, never truly inhabited it, because her heart dwelt in the unfinished house, in the promise, and not in the real house where her body lived.

And in making her house of today beautiful, in inhabiting it at last, Esperança did to her life what was left to do to Venturoso's house: she finished it. Not with bricks, but with presence. She brought her heart back from the slope to the house where she lived, from the future to the present, from the promise to the real, and the real house, which had been ready for years and which she had never inhabited, filled suddenly with life, with flowers, with used things, and Esperança understood, with an astonishment that was almost comical, that the finished house she had awaited for twenty years had always been there, beneath her feet, awaiting not tiles, but her stopping to think it provisional and accepting it as her life. Venturoso's unfinished house was never going to be finished; but Esperança's house, the real one, today's, that one was ready, had always been, and only she was lacking, to move into it with her heart, which had been for twenty years lent to the slope.

And there was a gesture, in those days, that scandalised half the village and that was, for Esperança, a liberation. She invited people to her house. She set the table with the grandmother's china, made an oven rice and some sweets, and called Rosalina and Joaquim, and Perpétua of the wash-houses, and Sina, and a few more women, for a dinner on some Saturday or other, with no reason, with no feast, just for the pleasure of having people in the house, a thing she had never done in twenty years because she always kept the dinners and the invitations for when she had a house in earnest, a house to receive in. And Perpétua, who knew all the dates, said at the table, "Eh, Esperança, how many years since I last set foot in this house," and Esperança answered, with a frankness that made her laugh at herself, "Twenty, Perpétua, for I kept the house to receive in when I had the good one, the one on the slope, and I never received anyone awaiting it, and now I've understood that the good house is the one with people inside it, and the only one with people inside it is this one, today." And the table laughed, and they ate, and they sang, and Florinda's house, which had been for twenty years a house of two women waiting, filled with voices for the first time, and Esperança looked around her, at the people at the table, at the grandmother's china full of food, at the aunt laughing, and understood that it was that, exactly that, the life in earnest she had awaited for twenty years to come when the house was ready, and that it had come, after all, with no house at all, only because she had opened the door and set the table, today, in the house she had.

The aunt, old, who watched her niece change, asked her, one day, "And Venturoso, Esperança, and the house on the slope, are you going to leave it all?" and Esperança answered a thing she herself heard with surprise, it was so clear: "Aunt, I don't yet know about Venturoso. But the house on the slope, that, I've left. Not the house, for that is his. I've left off waiting for it. I've left off putting my life up there, awaiting him to finish it. My house is this one, aunt, and my life is today, and I've already lost twenty years awaiting to begin to live in a house that does not get finished. I lose no more." And the aunt, who was old and who had seen many a person lose their life in the waiting, looked at her niece using the sheets and putting flowers at the window, and said only, with brimming eyes, "Just as well, child. Your mother did not know how. Just as well that you knew how."

And at night, lying on the yellow sheets used at last, in her house inhabited at last, Esperança did a thing she had not done in a long time: she slept awaiting nothing. She did not fall asleep dreaming the finished house, did not fall asleep counting when the wedding would be, did not fall asleep in Venturoso's future; she fell asleep in her own present, in her own bed, with her own sheets, in her own life, and it was a sleep different from all those of twenty years, because it was the sleep of one who left off living on the eve and began to live in the day, and discovered that the day, however imperfect, however much it had no new house nor marriage nor life in earnest, was better than the eve, because the day existed, and the eve, that, was only a beautiful way of postponing for ever the existing.
