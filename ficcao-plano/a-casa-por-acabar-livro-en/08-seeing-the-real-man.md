## 8. Seeing the real man

When the false hope died, Esperança could, for the first time in twenty years, see Venturoso as he was, and it was a strange and painful thing, because to see the real man after loving the promise so long is like opening one's eyes in a room where one has lived in the dark: one sees what had always been there and was never seen.

For twenty years, Esperança had seen in Venturoso the man he was going to be: the one who would finish the house, who would marry, who would give the life in earnest. That man was beautiful, was trustworthy, was worthy of a life of waiting. But that man did not exist, he had always been a projection, a drawing Esperança had made over the real Venturoso and taken for the real Venturoso; and when the hope fell, the projection fell too, and beneath it stayed the man who was there, and Esperança looked at him, at last, with open eyes.

There was a Sunday, in those days, when she saw him whole in a small gesture, and it was a gesture she had witnessed a thousand times without ever seeing it. They were at the café, and Venturoso, in the midst of the ring of men, told, for the umpteenth time, and with his usual grace, the story of when he was going to emigrate to Switzerland, when he was young, and had the papers and the contract, and on the eve thought there'd be better next year and stayed; and he told it like an anecdote, like a cleverness of his, would you believe I nearly emigrated, and a good thing I didn't, for that's a dog's life; and the men laughed, and Venturoso shone. And Esperança, who had heard that story and laughed at it for twenty years, that Sunday did not laugh, because she heard, beneath the anecdote, what it was: it was the story of a man who had had a life within reach of his hand, with the papers in order, and had let it go on the eve for a next year, and had made of that flight a joke he told at the café, and so, turning the fear into an anecdote, never had to look at it full in the face. And she looked at Venturoso shining as he told the life he had not lived as if it were a cleverness, and saw, for the first time, that it was exactly her father's gesture folding the drawings of the mill and putting them away in the drawer, it was the same family of men, the one that makes a virtue of postponement and a joke of fear; and Venturoso, whom she loved, was the best teller of all, the one who made the flight so charming that no one, in twenty years, not even she, had noticed that that anecdote of Switzerland was a death entry, that of a life he had killed on the eve and came to show off, embalmed and laughing, at the table of the café.

And what she saw was not a villain, and that was what made it all the harder. She saw a good man, charming, sincere, generous, dear to all; but she saw also, now without the veil of the promise, a man who finished nothing, who lived in the future, who postponed by nature, who confused dreaming with doing, and who was going to be so his whole life, not from malice, but from being who he was. She saw a man who loved her, in his way, but who loved in her, as she in him, a promise: he loved the Esperança who would always wait, the faithful betrothed, the one who did not charge it to him, the one who accepted the next year; and the day she stopped waiting, she understood, he would perhaps stop loving her, because what he loved in her was the patience, was the not having to keep his word, was the permission to postpone without losing, and that, that mutual love of two promises, was not a love of two people, it was an agreement between two postponements, comfortable, eternal, and empty.

And it was strange to her, and almost comical it was so sad, to discover that she had spent twenty years loving a man she did not know. Because to know a person is to see them as they are, and she had never seen Venturoso as he was; she had always seen what he was going to be, and had loved that, the to-come, and the real man, the everyday one, had passed her by for twenty years without her once looking at him full in the face, always busy looking, over his shoulder, at the finished house, the wedding, the life in earnest. And now that she looked at him, at the real Venturoso, it was almost like looking at a stranger, a man who had been there twenty years and whom she was only now seeing, and she understood that one can share a whole life with someone and not know them, if what one loves in them is the promise and not the person, and that love of the promise is the loneliest of loves, because one loves alone, inside one's own head, a man who does not exist, while the one who exists stays outside, unknown, unloved.

And she saw him in yet another scene, and it was the one that moved her most and enlightened her most at once. There was in the village a poor young man, Telhinha, the grandson of the Telharia people of the unfinished house the Keeper had shown her, an awkward lad everyone laughed at, and Venturoso, one day, in the square, set himself to talking with him in front of everyone, and treated him as a man, and gave him importance, and promised to find him work with an acquaintance in the big town, and described the work to him, and the wage, and the life the lad was going to have, with that sharpness of his, and Telhinha went away shining, with his whole life drawn in his head by Venturoso, and everyone thought Venturoso a saint, that he had given hope to the poor lad. And Esperança, who before would have thought the same, saw, that time, another thing: she saw that the promised work did not exist, or that Venturoso was never going to lift a finger to arrange it, because Venturoso did not arrange work, he promised it, and that what he had given Telhinha was not a life, it was a drawing of a life, just like the one he had given her twenty years before, and that the lad was now going to live on the promise as she had lived, awaiting a telephone call that did not come, and that what the village called Venturoso's kindness was the same thing as the postponement, was the generosity of one who gives futures because the future costs nothing, and never presents, for those cost. And it hurt her to understand that the man she had loved dealt out across the whole village, smiling, the disease she had nearly died of: that of making people live on promises, and calling it love, and calling it kindness, and being, on the strength of it, the dearest man in Véspera.

It hurt her to see this, because it took from her love twenty years of nobility. Esperança had always seen herself as the faithful betrothed, the constant one, the one who loved with a rare fidelity; and now she saw that her fidelity had not been only love, it had been also fear, the fear of living, the fear of demanding, the fear of trading the safe promise, which does not fail because it never comes, for the real life, which fails because it happens. And she understood that she and Venturoso were, at bottom, of the same family as Sina and his father, as her mother and her father: people who loved the eve of life because they were afraid of life, and who made of waiting a nest where they hid from what frightens one in living, which is to live indeed, with its choices, its risks, its ends.

There was an afternoon, in those days, when Esperança realised she had never had a photograph of the real Venturoso. She had them, yes, in the album, of the young betrothed pair, but they were photographs from twenty years before, of the man he had been or had promised to be, and Esperança understood that in her head Venturoso was still that one, the one of the old portraits, the one of the beginning, and that she had never updated the image, never looked at the man of forty-odd years he was now, with his hair thinning and his belly growing and the same phrases as always in a face that was no longer a young man's. And she went to Mass the following Sunday on purpose to look at him, to see him as he was, today, and not as she kept him, and she looked at him through the whole Mass, Venturoso greeting everyone on the way out, laughing, promising this one and that one, shining, and saw, for the first time, a middle-aged man saying the same things he had said at twenty, promising the same house, living the same future, and grown old without changing, and it was strange and sad to her like seeing an actor play, at forty, the same leading-man part he had played at twenty, without realising that time had passed and that the part no longer fit him. And she understood that what had grown old, of the two of them, was she, who had the sums of the twenty years in her body, whereas Venturoso, he, had stayed still in the future, eternally young in the promise, eternally the betrothed of the beginning, because one who lives in the to-come does not grow old, he stays suspended, and that was, perhaps, the most terrible part of his charm: that he was going to be always the same, the man of this year is the one, from twenty to eighty, promising the same house to whoever would wait for him, and that the only thing that changed, around him, were the women who grew old waiting.

But to see the real man was not only loss; it was also, and Esperança did not expect this, a kind of peace. Because while she had loved the promise, she had lived in a permanent tension, the tension of awaiting a thing that did not come, of wanting Venturoso to be another, of postponing the disappointment; and when she saw him as he was, and stopped wanting him to be what he was not, a sad calm made itself in her, the calm of one who leaves off fighting against a truth and accepts it, and rests, at last, in the truth, however much it hurts, because to rest in the truth hurts less, in the end, than to fight against it a whole life. Esperança stopped awaiting Venturoso to finish the house, stopped awaiting him to change, stopped loving him for what he was going to be, and in stopping she felt, in the midst of the sadness, an enormous relief, the relief of setting down a weight of twenty years, the weight of keeping alive a hope that life kept denying her reason for.

And it was there, in the sad calm of seeing the real man, that Esperança asked the question the whole book had been preparing her for, and she asked it not about Venturoso, but about herself: if I stop awaiting the house, and stop loving what he is going to be, what is left? What is left is this man, real, charming and put-off, whom I can love as he is, without awaiting him to change, and with whom I can, perhaps, have a real life, in whatever house there is, today, if he wants; or what is left is a man I no longer want, because what I loved was the promise, and without the promise there is not, for me, life enough there. And the answer to this question, Esperança understood, was the true crossing of the book, and did not depend on the house, nor on Venturoso: it depended on her, on knowing whether she could love the real, any real, his or that of another life, or whether she was condemned, like her mother and Sina, only to know how to love promises, and therefore only to know how to wait.

She did not answer at once, because the answer did not come ready, and she did well not to answer at once, for these things are not decided in a day. But one thing changed, and it was everything: she stopped living awaiting the answer to come to her from Venturoso, or from the house, and began to seek it in herself. She stopped asking when the house will be ready, which was to ask Venturoso for her life, and began to ask what am I going to make of my life, which was to take it up with her own hands. And that change, from awaiting the life to making the life, was the beginning of the end of the waiting, and was also, though Esperança did not yet know it, the beginning of the end of the book, because from the moment a woman takes up her own life instead of awaiting it from a man, the story stops being about the man and becomes about her, which is how they must end, in order to end well, the stories of women who lost themselves in the waiting.
