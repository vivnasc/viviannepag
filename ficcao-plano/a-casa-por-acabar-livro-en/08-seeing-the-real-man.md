## 8. Seeing the real man

When the false hope died, Esperança could, for the first time in twenty years, see Venturoso as he was, and it was a strange and painful thing, because to see the real man after loving the promise so long is like opening one's eyes in a room one has lived in in the dark: one sees what was always there and never saw.

For twenty years, Esperança had seen in Venturoso the man he was going to be: the one who would finish the house, the one who would marry, the one who would give the in-earnest life. That man was beautiful, was trustworthy, was worthy of a life of waiting. But that man did not exist, he had always been a projection, a drawing Esperança had made over the real Venturoso and taken for the real Venturoso; and when the hope fell, the projection fell too, and beneath it was left the man who was there, and Esperança looked at him, at last, with open eyes.

And what she saw was not a villain, and it was that which made everything harder. She saw a good man, charming, sincere, generous, dear to all; but she saw also, now without the veil of the promise, a man who finished nothing, who lived in the future, who postponed by nature, who confused dreaming with doing, and who was going to be so his whole life, not from malice, but from being who he was. She saw a man who loved her, in his way, but who loved in her, as she in him, a promise: he loved the Esperança who would always wait, the faithful betrothed, the one who did not charge him, the one who accepted the next year; and the day she stopped waiting, she perceived, he would perhaps stop loving her, because what he loved in her was the patience, was the not-having-to-keep-his-word, was the permission to postpone without losing, and that, that mutual love of two promises, was not a love of two people, it was an agreement between two postponements, comfortable, eternal, and empty.

And it was strange to her, and almost comic for being so sad, to discover that she had spent twenty years loving a man she did not know. Because to know a person is to see them as they are, and she had never seen Venturoso as he was; she had always seen what he was going to be, and loved that, the to-come, and the real man, the everyday one, had passed beside her twenty years without her once looking at him head on, always busy looking, over his shoulder, at the ready house, the marriage, the in-earnest life. And now that she looked at him, at the real Venturoso, it was almost like looking at a stranger, a man who had been there twenty years and whom she only now saw, and she perceived that one can share a whole life with someone and not know them, if what one loves in them is the promise and not the person, and that love of the promise is the most solitary of loves, because one loves alone, inside one's own head, a man who does not exist, while the one who exists stays outside, unknown, unloved.

It hurt her to see this, because it took from her love twenty years of nobility. Esperança had always seen herself as the faithful betrothed, the constant, the one who loved with a rare faithfulness; and now she saw that her faithfulness had not been only love, it had been also fear, the fear of living, the fear of demanding, the fear of trading the safe promise, which does not fail because it never comes, for the real life, which fails because it happens. And she perceived that she and Venturoso were, at bottom, of the same family as Sina and his father, as her mother and her father: people who loved the eve of life because they were afraid of life, and who made of waiting a nest where they hid from what is frightening to live, which is to live indeed, with its choices, its risks, its ends.

But to see the real man was not only loss; it was also, and Esperança did not expect this, a kind of peace. Because while she had loved the promise, she had lived in a permanent tension, the tension of awaiting a thing that did not come, of wishing Venturoso to be another, of postponing the disappointment; and when she saw him as he was, and stopped wishing him to be what he was not, there came over her a sad quiet, the quiet of one who stops fighting against a truth and accepts it, and rests, at last, in the truth, however much it hurts, because to rest in the truth hurts less, in the end, than to fight against it a whole life. Esperança stopped awaiting Venturoso to finish the house, stopped awaiting him to change, stopped loving him for what he was going to be, and in stopping she felt, in the midst of the sadness, an enormous relief, the relief of setting down a weight of twenty years, the weight of keeping alive a hope that life denied reason for her whole life.

And it was there, in the sad quiet of seeing the real man, that Esperança asked the question the whole book had been preparing for her, and asked it not about Venturoso, but about herself: if I stop awaiting the house, and stop loving what he is going to be, what is left? There is left this man, real, charming and postponing, whom I can love as he is, without awaiting him to change, and with whom I can, perhaps, have a real life, in the house there is, today, if he wishes; or there is left a man I no longer want, because what I loved was the promise, and without the promise there is not there, for me, life enough. And the answer to this question, Esperança perceived, was the true crossing of the book, and it did not depend on the house, nor on Venturoso: it depended on her, on knowing whether she could love the real, any real, his or that of another life, or whether she was condemned, like the mother and Sina, only to know how to love promises, and therefore only to know how to wait.

She did not answer at once, because the answer did not come ready, and she did well not to answer at once, for such things are not decided in a day. But one thing changed, and it was everything: she stopped living awaiting the answer to come to her from Venturoso, or from the house, and began to seek it in herself. She stopped asking when will the house be ready, which was to ask Venturoso for her life, and began to ask what am I going to do with my life, which was to take it with her own hands. And that change, from awaiting the life to making the life, was the beginning of the end of the waiting, and it was also, though Esperança did not yet know it, the beginning of the end of the book, because from the moment a woman takes her own life instead of awaiting it from a man, the story stops being about the man and becomes about her, which is how the stories of women who lost themselves in the waiting must end, to end well.
