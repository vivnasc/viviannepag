## 10. The conversation

Esperança called Venturoso, one afternoon, and had with him the conversation that twenty years of hope had never let happen, the conversation in which the truth is said, and it was the hardest and the cleanest conversation of the two of theirs' lives.

They sat, not on the slope, at the door of the unfinished house, where they had always sat dreaming it, but in Esperança's house, the real one, the one she had at last inhabited, with the flowers at the window and the first-used sheets, and that alone told Venturoso, before any word, that something had changed. He came in, and looked about, and was surprised, without knowing why, at the betrothed's house being more alive, more full, more inhabited, and Esperança saw him be surprised, and perceived that he had never noticed it, her real house, because he too lived always turned towards the house on the slope, towards the to-come, and that the two of them had spent twenty years not seeing each other's real life, busy looking at the house that did not exist.

And Esperança said to him, without hardness, with the sad clearness of one who no longer waits: Venturoso, I am not going to wait any longer for the house to be ready. Not because I have stopped wanting you, but because I have perceived that I spent twenty years living on the eve of my life, awaiting a house that you, and I know this now, and your mother knows it too, are not going to finish. And I do not blame you. You are who you are, and I loved you twenty years for what you were going to be, and that was my error, not yours. But I cannot go on. My mother died awaiting a life my father was going to give her next year. I am not going to die so.

And Venturoso, who expected perhaps a scene, a reproach, heard that, and was shaken, because it was no reproach, it was a leave-taking, or nearly, and said, with the charm failing him for the first time, but Esperança, I promise you I'll finish the house, I promise, this year, and Esperança interrupted him, with tenderness, but firm: no, Venturoso. Do not promise me the house. You have promised me the house twenty years. I do not ask you for the house. I ask you for another thing, and it is the last time I ask you, and hear me well, because your answer is going to decide everything. I ask you for the real life, today. Marry me now, this week, in the house there is, in mine, in yours, in a rented room, without a ready house, with the life we have. Come to live with me the life of today, imperfect, without a veranda at sunrise, without the in-earnest house. If you come, I stay with you, and love you as you are, and we build the house slowly, or we never build it, and it makes no difference, because life is not the house, life is us, today. But if you do not come, if you go on wanting me only when the house is ready, then I go, Venturoso, with sorrow, but I go, because I no longer live on the eve. Choose. The real life with me, today, or the promise, alone.

And Venturoso stayed silent, and it was a long silence, and Esperança did not fill it, she left it whole, because it was a silence that had to be his, the answer had to be his. And across Venturoso's face passed, in that silence, all the things: the fright, the wish, the fear, the love, and at last, what Esperança already knew was going to pass, the flight, because Venturoso, at the end of the silence, instead of saying yes, let us go, we marry today, in the house there is, said, under his breath, with a true sadness that made him still more himself: Esperança, I cannot. To marry without the house ready, with nothing in earnest, in a room, that is not the life I want to give you. I want to give you the beautiful house, the beautiful life. Give me only one more year. This year is the one.

And there, in the this-year-is-the-one said as the answer to the request to live today, Esperança had her whole answer, and it was, despite everything, despite her awaiting it, a deep sadness, because it was the end of twenty years, and it was the end of a love that had been true, in his way and in hers. But it was also, and Esperança felt it beneath the sadness, a liberation, because Venturoso, in choosing the promise over her, in preferring the house to come to the real woman, had given her, without meaning to, the permission to go, had taken her guilt away, had shown her, once and for all, that there was not there, for her, a life, there was only a waiting, and that to go on was to choose the mother's life, and that to go, however much it hurt, was to choose to live. And she perceived, with a sad tenderness, that Venturoso could not give her the real life not from not loving her, but from having of a real life a fear as great as her own, and that the beautiful house he wished to give her was, at the bottom of him, the same thing the ready house had been for her: a way of postponing forever the living, dressed as wanting the best for the one one loves.

So this is it, Venturoso, said Esperança, her voice trembling but firm. You choose the house. I choose life. And there is no wrong in it, hear, I do not want to leave you thinking you are bad, for you are not; you are going to be happy so, in your next year, dreaming the house, your whole life, and perhaps that is your way of being happy, and I respect it. But it is not mine. I can no longer be happy on the eve. I want the day. And Venturoso, with true tears, because he loved her, in his way, asked, and we never marry?, and Esperança, with equal tears, answered: no, Venturoso. We do not marry. Because you only marry when the house is ready, and the house is not going to be finished, and I no longer wait. Stay with your unfinished house, Venturoso. I stay with my unbegun life. And perhaps it is better for the two of us, because you, with me charging you the real life, would be unhappy, and I, with you postponing it for me, would die waiting, like my mother. Go with God, Venturoso. And finish the house, if one day you wish. But for you. It is no longer for me.

And Venturoso went, down the slope, and Esperança stayed at the door of her house, watching him go off for the last time as a betrothed, and it was the hardest thing she had done in her life, to watch go the man she had loved twenty years; but it was also, she perceived as she watched him disappear on the road, the first wholly her own thing she did, the first real choice, the first day she did not postpone, and she stayed at the door a long time, after he disappeared, with her heart broken and, beneath the broken heart, a thing she had not felt in twenty years: the sense of being alive, upright in her own life, in the present, awaiting nothing from anyone, alone and whole, which is a sad thing and an enormous thing, and which was, for Esperança, after twenty years of eve, the first day.
