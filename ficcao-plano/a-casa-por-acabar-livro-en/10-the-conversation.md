## 10. The conversation

Esperança sent for Venturoso, one afternoon, and had with him the conversation that twenty years of hope had never let happen, the conversation in which one says the truth, and it was the hardest and the cleanest conversation of both their lives.

They sat, not on the slope, at the door of the unfinished house, where they always sat to dream it, but in Esperança's house, the real one, the one she had at last inhabited, with the flowers at the window and the used sheets, and that alone told Venturoso, before any word, that something had changed. He came in, and looked around, and was surprised, without knowing why, by the betrothed's house more alive, more full, more inhabited, and Esperança saw him be surprised, and understood that he had never noticed it, her real house, because he too lived always turned towards the house on the slope, towards the one to come, and that the two of them had spent twenty years not seeing each other's real life, busy looking at the house that did not exist.

And Esperança made coffee, and set the table with the grandmother's china, the fine plates, the little cups with the gold thread worn, and Venturoso noticed them, and these, yes, he noticed, and said, with the charm coming, first, before anything, "Look what beautiful china, Esperança, this would look fine in the dresser of the new house, in the dining room I told you about, with the cabinet turned towards the view"; and Esperança heard him, and almost laughed, and almost wept, because even the china she had at last used, today, in the present, Venturoso put at once into the house that did not exist, into the dresser that would never be, into the parlour unbuilt; he could not see a beautiful thing without moving it into the future, without taking it from the today where it was and putting it into the tomorrow where he dwelt. And Esperança said, with a sad sweetness, "This china is fine here, Venturoso, on this table, today. Drink your coffee." And it was with the grandmother's cup in her hand, and the coffee going cold, that she said what she had to say.

And Esperança said to him, without hardness, with the sad clarity of one who no longer waits: "Venturoso, I am not going to wait any longer for the house to be ready. Not because I've stopped wanting you, but because I've understood I spent twenty years living on the eve of my life, awaiting a house that you, and I know this now, and your mother knows it too, are not going to finish. And I do not blame you. You are who you are, and I loved you twenty years for what you were going to be, and that was my mistake, not yours. But I cannot go on. My mother died awaiting a life my father was going to give her next year. I am not going to die so."

And Venturoso, who perhaps expected a scene, a reproach, heard that, and was shaken, because it was not a reproach, it was a farewell, or almost, and he said, with the charm failing him for the first time, "But Esperança, I promise you I'll finish the house, I promise, this year," and Esperança interrupted him, with tenderness, but firm: "No, Venturoso. Do not promise me the house. You've promised me the house for twenty years. I don't ask you for the house. I ask you for another thing, and it's the last time I ask you, and hear me well, because your answer is going to decide everything. I ask you for the real life, today. Marry me now, this week, in whatever house there is, in mine, in yours, in a room, with no house ready, with the life we have. Come and live with me the life of today, imperfect, with no veranda at sunrise, with no house in earnest. If you come, I stay with you, and love you as you are, and we build the house slowly, or we never build it, and it makes no difference, because life is not the house, life is us, today. But if you do not come, if you go on wanting me only when the house is ready, then I go, Venturoso, with sorrow, but I go, because I no longer live on the eve. Choose. The real life with me, today, or the promise, alone."

And Venturoso stayed silent, and it was a long silence, and Esperança did not fill it, she left it whole, because it was a silence that had to be his, the answer had to be his. And across Venturoso's face passed, in that silence, all the things: the fright, the will, the fear, the love, and at last, what Esperança already knew was going to pass, the flight, because Venturoso, at the end of the silence, instead of saying yes, let's go, we marry today, in whatever house there is, said, low, with a true sadness that made him still more himself: "Esperança, I can't. To marry without the house ready, with nothing in earnest, in a room, that's not the life I want to give you. I want to give you the beautiful house, the beautiful life. Give me just one more year. This year is the one."

And there, in the this year is the one said as an answer to the request to live today, Esperança had her whole answer, and it was, for all that, for all that she had awaited it, a deep sadness, because it was the end of twenty years, and it was the end of a love that had been true, in his way and in hers. But it was also, and Esperança felt it beneath the sadness, a liberation, because Venturoso, in choosing the promise over her, in preferring the house to come to the real woman, had given her, without meaning to, the permission to go, had taken the guilt from her, had shown her, once and for all, that there was not there, for her, a life, there was only a waiting, and that to go on was to choose her mother's life, and that to go, however much it hurt, was to choose to live. And she understood, with a sad tenderness, that Venturoso could not give her the real life not for not loving her, but for having of a real life a fear as great as her own, and that the beautiful house he wanted to give her was, at his bottom, the same thing the ready house had been for her: a way of postponing for ever the living, dressed as wanting the best for the one one loves.

"So this is it, Venturoso," said Esperança, her voice trembling but firm. "You choose the house. I choose life. And there's no harm in it, listen, I don't want to leave you thinking you're bad, for you're not; you're going to be happy so, in your next year, dreaming the house, your whole life, and perhaps that is your way of being happy, and I respect it. But it is not mine. I can no longer be happy on the eve. I want the day." And Venturoso, with true tears, because he loved her, in his way, asked, "And we never marry?" and Esperança, with tears the same, answered: "No, Venturoso. We do not marry. Because you only marry when the house is ready, and the house is not going to be finished, and I wait no more. Keep your unfinished house, Venturoso. I keep my life unbegun. And perhaps it's better for us both, because you, with me charging you the real life, would be unhappy, and I, with you postponing it for me, would die waiting, like my mother. Go with God, Venturoso. And finish the house, if one day you want to. But for yourself. It is no longer for me."

And Venturoso went, down the slope, and Esperança stayed at the door of her house, watching him go off for the last time as a betrothed, and it was the hardest thing she had done in her life, to watch the man she had loved twenty years go; but it was also, she understood it as she watched him disappear on the road, the first wholly her own thing she had done, the first real choice, the first day she had not postponed, and she stayed at the door a long time, after he had disappeared, with her heart broken and, beneath the broken heart, a thing she had not felt in twenty years: the sense of being alive, standing in her own life, in the present, awaiting nothing of anyone, alone and whole, which is a sad thing and an enormous thing, and which was, for Esperança, after twenty years of eve, the first day.

And that afternoon, her eyes still undried, Esperança did a thing she had to do, and that cost her almost as much as the farewell: she went to Sina's house, to tell her. Because Sina was Venturoso's mother, and deserved to know it from her mouth and not from the village, and because Sina had given her the truth that had freed her, and it was right to give her back the news of what the truth had done. And she went in, and Sina, who was sewing by the window, with the plastic sheet of the unfinished room fluttering behind her, saw her face, and set down the sewing, and said only, "It's over." And Esperança said, "It's over, Sina, I told him I'd marry with the life we have, today, and he chose the house, he chose next year, and I came away." And Sina stayed a moment silent, and Esperança saw pass across her face the two things that had to pass, the mother aching for the son who was left alone, and the woman breathing in relief because a girl had escaped her life; and Sina, at last, got up, and went to a cupboard, and took from it a thing wrapped in a kerchief, and unwrapped it, and it was an old deck of cards, worn, the one of her fortune-readings of fifty years before, and held it out to Esperança, and said, "Take it, child, you keep it. I no longer need it to see what was coming, for you've already come out of the road where I saw you. And it's not for you to read anyone's fortune, hear, it's to remind you of one thing: that you, unlike me, unlike your mother, did not shuffle the cards until the lie you wanted came up. You kept the one that came. It was the bravest thing I've seen a woman do in this village." And Esperança took Sina's deck, and kept it at home beside the stone she would bring from the slope, and they were, those two objects, the stone and the cards, the markers of her crossing, one from the house that bound her, the other from the woman who helped her come out.
