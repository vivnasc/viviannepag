## 5. What Sina saw

Venturoso's mother, Sina, was the one person in Véspera who knew exactly what Esperança was living, because she had lived it herself, from beginning to end, with Venturoso's father, and it was she who told Esperança, at last, the truth the whole village saw and that no one had the courage or the right to say.

Sina had been widowed years before, and her husband, Venturoso's father, had been, in life, a man like the son: charming, full of plans, always going to do, never doing. He had promised Sina, when they were sweethearts, a life he never gave her; he was going to buy, going to build, going to change his life, going, going, going, and so his whole life went, in the going, and he died leaving Sina with a half-finished house, projects unfulfilled, and a whole life spent awaiting the life he was going to give her and that never came. And Sina, a widow, looked now at her son, and saw in the son the husband, point by point, the same charm, the same promises, the same unfinished house, the same betrothed waiting, and suffered for Esperança as one who sees a girl entering into a life she herself crossed and from which she came out with empty hands and a worn heart.

And there was, in Sina's suffering, one layer more, which was guilt, because Sina knew that she herself had made of the son what the son was. She had raised him hearing the father promise, seeing the father postpone, taking the postponing for a normal way of living, and had never told him, the son, what she should have told him, that a man is made by what he finishes and not by what he promises; and more, she had defended the husband to the son's eyes, had said to him your father is a dreamer, he has great plans, instead of telling him the truth, that the father was a man afraid to live, and so she had taught the son to admire in the father precisely the flaw that was going to make him the same, and Venturoso had grown up thinking it fine to be like the father, thinking that to promise great was better than to do small, and had become the father, and Sina saw it, and carried the guilt of not having cut, in the son, the chain that came from the husband, and of now watching it pass to Esperança, one more woman, one more life in the waiting.

That week, Esperança went to Sina's house to take her some things, for she cared for her as she cared for her aunt, and Sina, who felt in Esperança a change, a new sadness, made her sit, and said to her, without roundabouts, with the courage of old women who have nothing left to lose: Esperança, I am going to tell you a thing I should have told you twenty years ago, and that I did not from selfishness, and I beg your pardon for it. My son is not going to finish that house. Esperança lost her breath, because it was the first time anyone said it aloud, and the more so his mother, and Sina went on, her eyes burning with an old truth: and he will not finish it not because he does not love you, for he loves you in his way, nor because he is bad, for he is not; he will not finish it because he is like his father, and his father never finished anything, and I waited for his father my whole life, as you wait for the son, and his father died promising me a life for next year, and I was left with the house half done and the life unlived, and I look at you, my child, and I see myself at your age, with the same faith, with the same trousseau kept, awaiting the same man who does not come, and I cannot bear to watch you repeat my life in silence as I lived it.

And Sina said more, and it was what most hurt Esperança, because it was the confirmation of what she herself had begun to see: you love what he promises to be, Esperança. As I loved what his father promised. And they promise so beautifully, the two of them, that one spends a life loving the promise and never looks at the man. But the promise does not marry you, child. The promise does not warm you at night, does not give you children, does not grow old with you. What does that is the man, the real one, the one who is there, and the man who is there, in my case and in yours, is a man who postpones, and to postpone is what he is, it is not a phase, it will not pass, there is no this-year-is-the-one that cures it. I waited for it to pass fifty years. It did not pass. What passed was my life. And I do not want to see you, thirty years from now, a widow or a spinster, with the house still half done on the slope, saying what I say now: and I who spent my life waiting.

And there, in Sina's sentence, and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting, Esperança heard, again, the mother, her own dying mother, and perceived, with a shiver, that it was the same sentence, that her mother and his mother had died, or were going to die, with the same sentence in their mouths, because they had lived the same life, the life of women who love men of the future, and that she, Esperança, was on the exact road to saying the same sentence at the end, and that she had before her, there, in Sina, her own future, an old widow of a life she did not live, and that Sina was offering her what no one had offered her, which was to see the end of the road before walking all of it, to see where the waiting led, to be able, still in time, to leave it.

Because it is rare, and it is a grace, for a person to be able to see, in flesh and bone, what will become of them if they go on the road they are on, and Esperança had that grace in Sina: she saw in Venturoso's mother what she herself would be if she went on waiting, she saw the half-finished house that stays forever, she saw the life unlived, she saw the sentence of the end, and it was a mirror terrible and saving, terrible because it showed her what awaited her, saving because it showed it to her in time, with thirty years still to live, and not on the deathbed as to the mother, when there is no turning back. And there was a moment, in the talk, when Sina, without noticing, showed her the whole mirror: she rose to fetch something, and Esperança watched her cross the room slowly, bent, a widow, in the half-finished house her husband had left her, and had, for an instant, the sense of seeing herself thirty years on, crossing a room bent, a widow of a life she had not lived, and it was a shiver so deep that she nearly rose and left, because no one bears for long to look head on at their own end.

Why do you tell me this now, Sina, Esperança asked, her voice trembling, and Sina answered, with an honesty that honoured her: because I repented of not having told you before. When you became engaged, I saw at once what was going to happen, I saw his father in my son, and I held my tongue, from selfishness, because I wanted you for a daughter-in-law, because you were good to him, because you cared for him, and I thought, perhaps with her it will be different, perhaps she will make him change. And I did not make him change, child, no one changes those men, and what I did was let you enter into my life without warning you. And I carried that twenty years. And now that I see you beginning to see, I hold my tongue no more, because to hold it would be to let you lose your life as I lost mine, and that, child, I no longer do, though it cost me my daughter-in-law, for you are the only one who treats me as a daughter.

And Sina added, a thing Esperança carried with her as the greatest warning: look, child, I do not tell you to leave him. That is for you to know, and there are those who stay, with open eyes, and are happy, because they chose to stay knowing what they choose. What I tell you is another thing: do not wait any longer. Whether with him or without him, stop putting your life on hold for his house. If you stay with him, stay to live today, in the house there is, and not to wait for the one he does not finish. And if you cannot live with him without waiting, then it is better to go, because waiting kills, child, waiting kills slowly, and I am the proof, and your mother was the proof, and I do not want to be, after I am dead, one more old woman of whom it is said, poor thing, she spent her life waiting. Live, Esperança. That is all I ask of you. With him or without him, but live.

Esperança left Sina's house shaken as never before, because Sina had given her all of it at once: the certainty that Venturoso was not going to finish the house, said by the mouth of the one who knew him most and loved him most; the sight of her own future, if she went on, in a mirror of flesh; and the permission, almost the order, to live, with him or without him, but to wait no more. And she carried with her, down the slope, no longer the timid question of and-if-the-house-is-not-finished, but its hard version, with no escape: the house is not going to be finished, Esperança. Sina told you so, and Sina knows. The man you love is a man who postpones, and to postpone is what he is. So, now that you know, what do you do? Do you go on waiting, faithful to the promise, until you say at the end and-I-who-spent-my-life-waiting? Or do you stop waiting, and live, with him or without him, but today? And Esperança, for the first time, did not have the when the house is ready to cover the question, because Sina had taken her roof off, and the truth came in whole, cold and clear, as the wind came into the unfinished house on the slope.
