## 5. What Sina saw

Venturoso's mother, Sina, was the only person in Véspera who knew exactly what Esperança was living, because she had lived it herself, from beginning to end, with Venturoso's father, and it was she who told Esperança, at last, the truth the whole village saw and that no one had the courage or the right to say.

Sina had been called so as a nickname for so long that few still knew her christened name, which was Assunção. They called her Sina — fate — because, when young, she read fortunes in hands and in cards, and had been the best card-reader of the serra, of those to whom the girls went to learn whom they would marry and the women to learn whether their men came back from the fields far away; and the irony, which the whole village savoured with a half-fearful respect, was that Sina, who read everyone's fate, had been blind to her own, had chosen the worst of men for herself, and had read in the hands of others futures she had not known how to read in her husband's face. The husband, Venturoso's father, was called Belarmino, and had been, in life, a man just like his son: charming, full of plans, always going to do, never doing. He had promised Sina, when they were engaged, a life he never gave her; he was going to buy, going to build, going to change his life, going, going, going, and so his whole life went, in the going, and he died leaving Sina with a half-built house, projects unfulfilled, and a whole life spent awaiting the life he was going to give her and that never came.

And Sina told Esperança, that afternoon, a thing she had never told anyone, and that was the bottom of her shame. On the day Belarmino asked for her, she, who read fortunes, dealt herself his cards in secret, at night, alone at the kitchen table, to see what the man she was going to marry would give her. And the cards, said Sina to Esperança, her eyes lowered, "gave me what they always give when there is this affliction: they gave me a road that begins and does not arrive, a card of beginnings upon beginnings and never the card of the end, and I read it, Esperança, I read it with these eyes, I know how to read them, and do you know what I did? I shuffled again. I dealt again, looking for a better fate, and dealt again, and again, until one time a card came up that suited me, and I kept that one, and threw the others away. I did to my fate, Esperança, what you do to Venturoso, and what your mother did to your father: I had the truth in front of me, read in my own cards, and I shuffled until the lie I wanted came up. And I married the lie. And I lived the truth. That is why I tell you what I tell you, child, for I do not speak from outside, I speak as one who shuffled the cards so as not to see, and lost her life in it." And Sina, widowed, now looked at her son, and saw in the son the husband, point by point, the same charm, the same promises, the same unfinished house, the same betrothed waiting, and she suffered for Esperança as one who sees a girl going into a life she herself crossed and from which she came out with empty hands and a worn heart.

And there was, in Sina's suffering, one layer more, which was guilt, because Sina knew that she herself had made of the son what the son was. She had raised him hearing the father promise, watching the father postpone, taking the postponing for a normal way to live, and had never told him, the son, what she should have told him, that a man is made by what he finishes and not by what he promises; and more, she had defended the husband in the son's eyes, had told him your father is a dreamer, he has great plans, instead of telling him the truth, that the father was a man afraid to live, and so had taught the son to admire in the father precisely the fault that was going to make him the same, and Venturoso had grown up thinking it beautiful to be like his father, thinking that to promise great was better than to do small, and had become the father, and Sina saw it, and carried the guilt of not having cut, in the son, the chain that came from the husband, and of now seeing it pass to Esperança, one more woman, one more life in the waiting.

That week, Esperança went to Sina's house to take her some things, for she cared for her as she cared for her aunt, and Sina's house said, without words, all that Sina had to say to her. It was a house Belarmino had begun to enlarge forty years before, a new room for the children that kept coming, and he had raised three walls of the room, and the fourth, the one giving onto the rest of the house, had been left unraised, with a sheet of plastic nailed to a rafter standing in for a wall, and the plastic, after forty years, was black with mould and torn by the wind, and through it could be seen the room that had never been a room, with its earth floor and some boards stacked that Belarmino had bought for the floor and that had rotted there, awaiting the floor he never laid. And Esperança, on going in, saw the sheet of plastic where there should be a wall, and Sina saw her see it, and said, with a bitter smile, "It's your father-in-law's wall that never was, child. Forty years behind the plastic. In the beginning, every winter, I'd beg him, Belarmino, raise me the bedroom wall, a deathly cold comes in there, and he'd say I'll raise it in the spring, woman, and in the spring he'd say in the summer, that the work goes better in summer, and in summer there were the feasts, and so life went, and the wall is this plastic, and I ended up growing used to it the way you grew used to your unfinished house, and I left off seeing it, and only see it when someone comes from outside and looks at it with pity, the way you're looking now." And Sina, who felt in Esperança a change, a new sadness, sat her down with her back to the plastic, perhaps on purpose, and said to her, without preamble, with the courage of old women who have nothing left to lose: "Esperança, I'm going to tell you a thing I should have told you twenty years ago, and didn't, out of selfishness, and I beg your pardon for it. My son is not going to finish that house." Esperança lost her breath, because it was the first time anyone had said it aloud, and still more his mother, and Sina went on, her eyes burning with an old truth: "And he won't finish it not because he doesn't love you, for he loves you in his way, nor because he's bad, for he isn't; he won't finish it because he's just like his father, and his father never finished anything, and I waited for his father my whole life, as you wait for the son, and his father died promising me a life next year, and I was left with the half-built house and the life unlived, and I look at you, my child, and I see myself at your age, with the same faith, with the same trousseau kept, awaiting the same man who does not come, and I cannot bear to watch you repeat my life in silence as I lived it."

And Sina said more, and it was what hurt Esperança most, because it was the confirmation of what she herself had begun to see: "You love what he promises to be, Esperança. As I loved what his father promised. And they promise so beautifully, the two of them, that one spends one's life loving the promise and never looks at the man. But the promise does not marry you, child. The promise does not warm you at night, does not give you children, does not grow old with you. What does that is the man, the real one, the one who is there, and the man who is there, in my case and in yours, is a man who postpones, and to postpone is what he is, it is not a phase, it will not pass, there is no this year is the one that cures it. I waited for it to pass for fifty years. It did not pass. What passed was my life. And I do not want to see you, thirty years from now, widowed or a spinster, with the house still half-built on the slope, saying what I say now: and I who spent my life waiting."

And there, in Sina's phrase, and I who spent my life waiting, Esperança heard, again, her mother, her own dying mother, and understood, with a shudder, that it was the same phrase, that her mother and his mother had died, or were going to die, with the same phrase in their mouths, because they had lived the same life, the life of women who love men of the future, and that she, Esperança, was on the exact road to say the same phrase at the end, and that she had in front of her, there, in Sina, her own future, an old widow of a life she had not lived, and that Sina was offering her what no one had offered her, which was to see the end of the road before walking it all, to see where the waiting led, so as to be able, still in time, to come out of it.

Because it is rare, and it is a grace, that a person can see, in flesh and bone, what will become of her if she goes on in the road she is on, and Esperança had that grace in Sina: she saw in Venturoso's mother what she herself was going to be if she went on waiting, saw the half-built house that stays for ever, saw the life unlived, saw the phrase of the end, and it was a terrible and a saving mirror, terrible because it showed her what awaited her, saving because it showed it to her in time, with thirty years still to live, and not on the deathbed as to her mother, when there is no turning back. And there was a moment, in the conversation, when Sina, without realising it, showed her the whole mirror: she got up to fetch something, and Esperança watched her cross the room slowly, bowed, widowed, in the half-built house her husband had left her, and had, for an instant, the sense of seeing herself thirty years on, crossing a room bowed, widowed of a life she had not lived, and it was a shudder so deep that she almost got up and left, because no one can bear long to look full in the face at one's own end.

"Why do you tell me this now, Sina?" asked Esperança, her voice trembling, and Sina answered, with an honesty that did her honour: "Because I repented of not having told you before. When you got engaged, I saw at once what was going to happen, I saw his father in my son, and I kept quiet, out of selfishness, because I wanted you for a daughter-in-law, because you were good for him, because you cared for him, and I thought, perhaps with her it will be different, perhaps she'll make him change. And I did not make him change, child, no one changes those men, and what I did was let you into my life without warning you. And I carried that for twenty years. And now that I see you beginning to see, I keep quiet no longer, because to keep quiet would be to let you lose your life as I lost mine, and that, child, I no longer do, though it cost me the daughter-in-law, for you are the only one who treats me as a daughter."

And she added, Sina, a thing Esperança took with her as the greatest warning: "Look, child, I do not tell you to leave him. That you must judge, and there are those who stay, with eyes open, and are happy, because they chose to stay knowing what they choose. What I tell you is another thing: do not wait any more. Be it with him, be it without him, stop putting your life on hold awaiting his house. If you stay with him, stay to live today, in whatever house there is, and not to wait for the one he does not finish. And if you cannot live with him without waiting, then it is better you go, because waiting kills, child, waiting kills slowly, and I am the proof, and your mother was the proof, and I do not want to be, after I'm dead, one more old woman of whom they say, poor thing, she spent her life waiting. Live, Esperança. That is all I ask of you. With him or without him, but live."

And at the door, at the parting, Sina held her hand and made her see, in a last gesture, the whole mirror. She pointed, through the window, to Venturoso's unfinished house, there at the top of the slope, golden with the afternoon sun, and then pointed, there at her side, inside the house, to the black sheet of plastic where the bedroom wall should be, Belarmino's unfinished work, and said, "You see, child, they are the same thing, the house up there and the plastic here at my side, with forty years between them and the same owner inside; I look at my husband's plastic every day for forty years, and you will look at my son's walls thirty more, if you stay, and one day you too will have some plastic or other nailed to a rafter standing in for a wall in your life, and you will grow used to it, and you will leave off seeing it, as I left off. Unless you come out now, while the wall still hurts you to see it unmade." And Esperança looked from the plastic to the house on the slope, from the old work to the new, and it was a shudder, because she saw, indeed, the same thing repeated in two men and about to repeat in her, and she left Sina's house shaken as never before, because Sina had given her everything at once: the certainty that Venturoso was not going to finish the house, said by the mouth of one who knew him most and loved him most; the vision of her own future, if she went on, in a mirror of flesh and in a sheet of plastic; and the permission, and almost the order, to live, with him or without him, but to wait no more. And she took with her, down the slope, no longer the timid question of and if the house is never finished, but its hard version, with no escape possible: the house is not going to be finished, Esperança. Sina told you so, and Sina knows. The man you love is a man who postpones, and to postpone is what he is. So, now that you know, what do you do? Do you go on waiting, faithful to the promise, until at the end you say and I who spent my life waiting? Or do you stop waiting, and live, with him or without him, but today? And Esperança, for the first time, did not have the when the house is ready to cover the question, because Sina had taken her roof from her, and the truth came in entire, cold and clear, as the wind came into the unfinished house on the slope.
