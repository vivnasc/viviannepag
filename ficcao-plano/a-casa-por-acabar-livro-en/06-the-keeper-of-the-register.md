## 6. The keeper of the register

She went to see to a paper of her aunt's at the House of the Register, and it was so, for a thing of bureaucracy, that Esperança sat before the Keeper, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps the beginnings and the ends, and where, she would perceive on leaving, the postponements are kept too, in the dates that do not close.

The House of the Register was cool, of thick walls, and smelled of old paper and ink, and the shelves rose to the ceiling full of thick-spined books, each one its year, each year its harvest of beginnings and of ends. There was there, in those books, the whole memory of the village, the births and the deaths, the marriages and the engagements, and also, in the margins, the works that began, the houses that rose, because in Véspera, in the old days, everything was set down, even the beginning of a house, and the books kept, without meaning to, beside the beginnings, the silence of the ends that did not come, of the works that began on a date and that did not have, pages on, the date of finished.

The Keeper saw to the aunt's paper, and then, instead of dismissing her, stayed looking at her, with that look that reads people as it reads the entries, and said: you are Venturoso's betrothed. Of the house on the slope. Esperança said yes, with a new weariness in that yes, and the Keeper, who had heard that yes a thousand times over the years, always full of faith, noticed the weariness, and rose, and went to a shelf, and took a book, and searched, and turned it towards Esperança, and said: look. And it was the page of the engagements of some years before, and the Keeper passed her finger down a line, and said: here is the entry of your engagement. Not of the marriage, for that was not made. Of the engagement. It has the date. And Esperança looked at the date of the engagement, written in the hand of an old Keeper, and did, without meaning to, the sum she had already done on the door-stone, and the sum came to the same, twenty years, and the Keeper, seeing her do the sum, said, low: twenty years, Esperança. There are engagements that last two, three years, and end in marriage. And there are engagements that last twenty years because they already are the marriage. When an engagement lasts twenty years, it is no longer the waiting for a thing. It is the thing.

And the Keeper said a thing Esperança never forgot: this book is full of beginnings that had no end. Dates of works that began and that have no date of finished. Engagements that have no line of marriage. Children promised who were not born. And I, who set down the beginnings and the ends, learned to read, in the beginnings without an end, a thing the people who live them do not wish to read: that a beginning that has no end after many years is not a beginning that is taking its time. It is a state. Venturoso's house, Esperança, is not unfinished. It is finished. It is in the state in which it is going to stay. Finished at not being finished. And your engagement, child, is not awaiting to become a marriage. It already is what it is going to be. It already is the marriage you have: a marriage in the waiting, which is Venturoso's way of marrying, and your way of awaiting him, and which may last until you die, because it is complete so, it lacks nothing to be what it is.

Esperança heard this and it was hard for her as Sina's truth had been, but from another angle: Sina had told her he is not going to finish the house; the Keeper told her the house already is as it is going to stay, and your engagement too, and you have been living, for twenty years, the life you are going to have, only you call it waiting, you call it not-yet, so as not to have to see that it is already, that it is this, that this is the life, that of climbing to the unfinished house and dreaming it ready, that of embroidering sheets that yellow, that of saying when the house is ready to a village that asks when's it for. And Esperança perceived that the Keeper was right, that she had been living for twenty years thinking her life had not yet begun, that she was awaiting to begin, and that the truth is the life was not awaiting: it was passing, it was that, the twenty years of waiting were the life, not its eve, and that she was going to spend her whole life calling her own life an eve, and letting it pass awaiting another, if she did not wake to the fact that that one, the one of the waiting, was the only one she was having.

And there was a thing, in the book, that the Keeper showed her and that was the deepest. She leafed forward, and stopped at a page, and said: look, Esperança. Do you see this work? It began the same year as Venturoso's. And do you see here, three years later, the date of finished. And the marriage, after. And the children, after. This house was finished. The one who made it was a man who finished things. And do you see this other, that began ten years before Venturoso's? It has no end. It never had. The man who began it died, and the house was left half done, and the son inherited it half done, and never finished it, and will leave it half done to the grandson. There are whole families of unfinished houses, Esperança, generations inheriting a half-finished house, because they inherit, with the house, the man who does not finish it. And you were going to enter into one of those families. Venturoso's is one of those houses. And the children you gave him, if you gave him any, would inherit the half-finished house and the father who does not finish it, and the chain went on. It is this the entries teach me to see, child: that there are houses that are finished and houses that are not, and that it is not the house, it is the one who makes it, and that the one who makes it is so before laying the first stone and will be so after the last he will never lay.

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a keeper of books, showed her also the entries of Esperança's mother, and said: and look at this. Here is your mother. And here, see, her engagement to your father, and the date. And here, after, years later, the marriage, for that was made. But see the dates of the works of their house, which were also set down, and see how they began and have no end. Your mother married a man who was going to, as you court one who is going to. And she died waiting. And you, Esperança, inherited this, this page, that of the woman who waits for the man who is going to. You were not born waiting; you learned, in this house, with this mother. But what is learned can be unlearned, child, if the person sees it in time. Your mother did not see it in time. You are seeing it. You have that luck, which she did not have. The question is what you do with it.

Esperança left the House of the Register with the page of her mother and the page of her own engagement weighing on her together, and crossed the square, and looked at the fountain, and the fountain of Véspera, that day, said a thing to her she had never thought: that a fountain gives the water it has, today, to whoever passes, and that it does not keep the water for a greater flow that is to come, because if it kept it awaiting a better flow, it would run dry without ever having given anything; and that she, Esperança, was keeping her life, as the fountain does not keep the water, awaiting a greater life, the in-earnest one, that was to come when the house was ready, and that she was letting pass, awaiting that, the only life she had, that of today, the real one, that ran, like the water of the fountain, and did not come back.

And she went home, that afternoon, with the truth of the Keeper over that of Sina, the two saying, from different angles, the same thing: that the house was not going to be finished, and that the engagement already was the life, and that the waiting was not the eve of living, it was the not-living, and that Esperança had, at last, to choose, not to wait any longer for the house to be finished, for that did not depend on her and perhaps would never happen, but to choose what she herself was going to do with her life, which depended on her and which had been, for twenty years, awaiting, like the trousseau in the chest, a use that only she, and not Venturoso, and not the house, could, at last, give it.
