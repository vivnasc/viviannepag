## 6. The keeper of the register

She went to see to a paper of her aunt's at the House of the Register, and it was so, through a matter of paperwork, that Esperança came to sit before the Keeper, in the midst of the books where Véspera keeps the beginnings and the ends, and where, she would perceive on leaving, the postponements are also kept, in the dates that do not close.

The House of the Register was cool, of thick walls, and smelled of old paper and ink, and the shelves rose to the ceiling full of thick-bound books, each one its year, each year its harvest of beginnings and of ends. There was there, in those books, the whole memory of the village, the births and the deaths, the marriages and the engagements, and also, in the margins, the works that began, the houses that rose, because in Véspera, in the old days, everything was set down, even the beginning of a house, and the books kept, without meaning to, beside the beginnings, the silence of the ends that did not come, of the works that began on one date and that had not, pages on, the date of finished.

The Keeper was a woman of broad hands and few gestures, and she had, at her neck, a pair of spectacles hung on a cord, which she put on and took off according to whether she read near or far, and that was her measure, the putting on and taking off of the spectacles, as the midwife of the serra had her kerchief and the smith his apron. She still wrote with a pen, in a china inkwell, because she said that ballpoint ink faded in twenty years and the entries were to last longer than people, and she had on the fingers of her right hand a stain of old ink that no longer came off, the ink of a life writing the beginnings and the ends of the village. She received Esperança in that cool room, bade her sit on a long bench worn smooth by the elbows of generations, and saw to her aunt's paper with the calm of one who is never in a hurry because she deals with what stays. And then, instead of dismissing her, she put on her spectacles, and stayed looking at her, with that look that reads people as it reads the entries, and said, "You are Venturoso's betrothed. Of the house on the slope." Esperança said yes, with a new tiredness in that yes, and the Keeper, who had heard that yes a thousand times over the years, always full of faith, noticed the tiredness, and got up, and went to a shelf, and took out a book, and searched, and turned it round to Esperança, and said, "Look." And it was the page of the engagements of some years back, and the Keeper ran her finger along a line, and said, "Here is the entry of your engagement. Not of the marriage, for that was not made. Of the engagement. It has the date." And Esperança looked at the date of the engagement, written in the hand of an old Keeper, and did, without meaning to, the sum she had already done on the door-stone, and the sum came to the same, twenty years, and the Keeper, seeing her do the sum, said, low: "Twenty years, Esperança. There are engagements that last two, three years, and end in marriage. And there are engagements that last twenty years because they already are the marriage. When an engagement lasts twenty years, it is no longer the waiting for a thing. It is the thing."

And the Keeper said a thing Esperança never forgot: "This book is full of beginnings that had no end. Dates of works that began and that have no date of finished. Engagements that have no line of marriage. Promised children who were not born. And I, who set down the beginnings and the ends, have learned to read, in the endless beginnings, a thing that the people who live them do not wish to read: that a beginning that has no end after many years is not a beginning that is taking its time. It is a state. Venturoso's house, Esperança, is not unfinished. It is finished. It is in the state it will stay in. Finished at not being finished. And your engagement, child, is not awaiting to turn into a marriage. It already is what it will be. It already is the marriage you two have: a marriage in waiting, which is Venturoso's way of marrying, and your way of awaiting him, and which can last until you die, because it is complete so, it lacks nothing to be what it is."

Esperança heard this and it was hard for her like Sina's truth, but from another angle: Sina had told her he is not going to finish the house; the Keeper told her the house is already as it will stay, and your engagement too, and you two have been living, for twenty years, the life you are going to have, only you call it waiting, you call it not yet, so as not to have to see that it is now, that it is this, that this is the life, the going up to the unfinished house and dreaming it ready, the embroidering of sheets that yellow, the saying when the house is ready to a village that asks when's it to be. And Esperança understood that the Keeper was right, that she had been living for twenty years thinking her life had not yet begun, that it was awaiting to begin, and that the truth was that life was not waiting: it was going by, it was that, the twenty years of waiting were the life, not the eve of it, and that she was going to spend her whole life calling her own life the eve, and letting it go by awaiting another, if she did not wake to the fact that that one, the one of waiting, was the only one she was having.

And there was a thing, in the book, that the Keeper showed her and that was the deepest. She leafed forward, and stopped on a page, and said, "Look, Esperança. You see this work? It's the house of Justino Carvalho, in the lower fields. It began the same year as Venturoso's, mark the date, it's the same year. And you see here, three years on, in the old Keeper's hand, house finished, and the blessing. And his marriage to Aurora, after, and the children, one, two, three, set down over the years like the beads of a rosary. This house was finished. The man who made it was a man who finished things, and you know him, it's Justino who to this day goes to Mass on a crutch, with the house full of grandchildren. And you see this other one, three pages back? The house of the Telharia people, which began ten years before Venturoso's. It has no end. It never had. The old Telha who began it died, and here is the entry of his death, and the house was left half-built, and the son inherited it half-built, and here is the son, and he never finished it, and he died too, you see, and now it's the grandson, Telhinha, who lives in his grandfather's half-built house with a sheet-metal roof that was meant to be provisional and that's been there thirty years. Go up to the lower fields and look at the Telharia house, Esperança, and you'll see three generations of men who did not finish the same house, and inside it three women who waited, the old man's, the son's, and now Telhinha's, poor Idília, who though still young already has that air of one who has given up. There are whole families of unfinished houses, Esperança, generations inheriting a half-built house, because they inherit, with the house, the man who does not finish it. And you were going to enter one of those families. Venturoso's is one of those houses; here is his father, Belarmino, you see, house begun, and never the line of finished. And the children you might give him, if you gave him any, would inherit the half-built house and the father who does not finish it, and the chain would go on, and fifty years from now some Keeper or other would show your page to another girl on the brink of entering another house without an end. This is what the entries teach me to see, child: that there are houses that get finished and houses that do not, and that it is not the house, it is the one who makes it, and that the one who makes it is so before he lays the first stone and will be so after the last one, which he will never lay."

And the Keeper, who was a Keeper and not only a book-keeper, showed her also Esperança's mother's entries, and said, "And look at this. Here is your mother. And here, see, her engagement to your father, and the date. And here, after, years on, the marriage, for that one was made. But look at the dates of the works on their house, which were also set down, and see how they began and have no end. Your mother married a man who was going to, as you court one who is going. And she died waiting. And you, Esperança, inherited this, this page, that of the woman who awaits the man who is going. You were not born waiting; you learned it, in this house, with this mother. But what is learned can be unlearned, child, if a person sees it in time. Your mother did not see it in time. You are seeing it. You have that luck, which she did not have. The question is what you do with it."

On the way out, the Keeper went with her to the door, and before letting her go said one more thing, low, with her ink-stained hand resting on the lintel: "I set down the beginnings and the ends, Esperança, and there will come a day when I set down yours. Mind, it is in you whether I set you down as Venturoso's betrothed who waited, and put your line beneath your mother's among the lives in waiting, if that is how you wish to stay in the book; or whether I set you down some other way, which I do not yet know, but which you yourself will write me with your life, for me to copy. Go and think on it, child. For what stays in the book is what you did, not what you waited for." And Esperança left the House of the Register with that, and with the page of her mother and the page of her own engagement weighing on her together, and crossed the square, and stopped at the fountain to drink water from the spout, with her hand cupped, and the fountain of Véspera, that day, told her a thing she had never thought: that a fountain gives the water it has, today, to whoever passes, and does not keep the water for a greater flow that is to come, because if it kept it awaiting a better flow, it would dry up without ever having given anything; and that she, Esperança, had been keeping her life, the way the fountain does not keep its water, awaiting a greater life, the in-earnest one, that was to come when the house was ready, and that she was letting go by, awaiting that, the only life she had, today's, the real one, that ran, like the fountain's water in the cupped hand, and that did not come back, and that was drunk now or lost for ever down the slope.

And she went home, that afternoon, with the Keeper's truth over Sina's, the two saying, from different angles, the same thing: that the house was not going to be finished, and that the engagement was already the life, and that the waiting was not the eve of living, it was the not-living, and that Esperança had, at last, to choose, not to wait any more for the house to be finished, for that did not depend on her and perhaps would never happen, but to choose what she herself was going to make of her life, which did depend on her and which had been, for twenty years, awaiting, like the trousseau in the chest, a first use that only she, and not Venturoso, and not the house, could, at last, give it.
