## 11. The house in the sun

A few days after parting from Venturoso, Esperança went up, one last time, to the unfinished house, not to dream it, as she had gone up for twenty years, but to take her leave of it, and it was on that climb, and in what she saw up there, that there closed, within her, what the whole book had been opening.

It was morning, and the sun struck the slope, and the unfinished house was in the sun, with the walls golden with the light, the openings bare, the roof half done, the grass grown inside shining with dew, and Esperança, reaching the top and seeing it so, in the sun, had a thing she had never had in the thousand times she had gone up there: she saw it as it was. Not as it was going to be, with the veranda and the hearth and the life in earnest; she saw it as it was, and as it was in the sun, with the light crossing it through the windowless openings, with the sky coming in where the roof should be, it was, Esperança discovered with a tightening, beautiful. Beautiful as a skeleton is beautiful, with the clean beauty of the things that are not finished and that perhaps for that reason show their form better, the bones of the house in the sun, without the flesh of the plastered walls and the roofs and the windows, only the structure, the drawing, the promise in stone, in the sun, beautiful and empty.

And Esperança went in, by what was to be the door, and the first thing she saw, leaned against the wall of the sunrise, was the gate. Aprígio's iron gate, bought twenty years before for the main entrance, leaned twenty years against the wall, with the smith's tag still tied to the bar, now only a rusted rag, and the factory paint eaten by the rust, and Esperança went up to it, and took the tag, what was left of it, and read, still against the light, Aprígio's hand, her name and Venturoso's and the word entrance, written with the pleasure of a man who had made a gate for a house that was going to be beautiful. And she remembered Aprígio drunk on Saint John's night, a gate leaned against a wall is not a gate, it is a coffin stood on end, and remembered him sharpening the hinges for nothing in the summer of the course and a half of tile, and understood that that gate, bought to let life in, had spent twenty years leaned against a wall without ever having let anyone in, and that it was, there, her own portrait: a good thing, bought with pleasure for a beginning, set aside awaiting the hinges that never came, rusting on its feet. And it was with that in her chest that Esperança ran her hand along the jamb, along the stone with the date carved twenty years before, the beginning, and walked through the rooms that existed only on the floor, the kitchen, the bedroom, saw the course and a half of common tile still in the middle of the half-built roof, the sentence begun and cut off, and stopped in the opening that was to be the veranda at sunrise, and looked at the view, the best in Véspera, the village, the river, and at last looked at the whole house around her, in the sun, and asked, at last, the question for which the whole book had brought her, and asked it aloud, alone, in the unfinished house, in the sun: how much of my life is walled up here?

Because that was, she perceived it now, what that house had been for twenty years: not the house where she was going to live, but the house where she had put her life on hold, wall by wall. Every year she had waited was there, in those walls in the sun; every yellow sheet, every next year, every feast at which she had said when the house is ready, every time she had refused to live today because the house was not ready, all of it was there, walled up in that unfinished house, twenty years of Esperança's life immured in that promise of stone, in the sun, beautiful and empty, as her life had been beautiful and empty, full of waiting and hollow of life. The house was not unfinished; it was full. It was full of Esperança's life, of the twenty years she had given it, and that was why it was so beautiful in the sun and so empty at once: because it was a skeleton, yes, but it was a skeleton in which a woman was immured, alive, who now looked at her own walls and asked herself how she had left so much of herself there.

And she went back to the jamb, to the stone with the date carved, and ran her fingers along the grooves of the numbers, which Venturoso had opened there with a nail, twenty years before, saying this is the beginning of our life, and read the date again, and did the sum again, the same she had done on the first Sunday, twenty years, and this time the sum did not frighten her, it hurt her in another way, deeper and cleaner. Because she understood that that date, carved on the stone as a beginning, had been the only thing Venturoso had finished in that house in twenty years: the beginning. He had carved the beginning with a nail, in earnest, with pride, and left all the rest unmade, because that was what he knew how to do, beginnings, dates of setting off, this is the beginning, this year is the one, and the stone with the date was his portrait in stone, a man who carves beginnings and does not reach the ends, and the whole house around the date was the proof, twenty years of unfinished house around a well-carved beginning. And she did, there, a sum that made her tremble: twenty years of Sundays going up to that house to dream it; twenty years of nights falling asleep imagining it ready; twenty years of small gestures postponed, of journeys not made, of things not used, of a whole woman's life set aside, wall by wall, in that house that never received her. And she understood that if she had given that house half the love and the faith she had given it, and had given it to her own life instead, she would have today a full life, instead of an empty house; that she had put in the wrong place twenty years of a rare thing, of a faith, of a fidelity, of a capacity to love, and that those things, put into a promise of stone, had rotted like the sheets in the chest, when, put into the real life, they would have borne fruit. And it was not rage she felt, it was a mourning, the mourning of the twenty years, and she wept it there, in the house in the sun, but she wept it, and that was new, without putting anything more there, without promising that house one more year of her life.

And Esperança wept, in the house in the sun, but it was a weeping different from that of the farewell to Venturoso; it was a weeping for herself, for the twenty years, for the woman she had been, the forever betrothed, the one who waited, and it was a weeping of mourning, but also, beneath it, of liberation, because in seeing, at last, how much of herself was walled up there, Esperança was also, in the same gesture, taking herself out of there, un-walling herself, recovering for herself the years she could still live, which were fewer than those she had had ahead of her when young, but which were, still, a life, thirty years perhaps, thirty years she was not going to wall up in any unfinished house, that she was going to live, day by day, in the present, in her real house, with flowers at the window and the used sheets.

And it was in that moment, looking at the whole house, that Esperança noticed at last the fig tree, and the fig tree gave her the last lesson. It was there, in the middle of what was to be the parlour, great, in the sun, laden with ripe figs, its shade stretching across the earth floor, alive, full, finished; and Esperança looked at the finished fig tree inside the unfinished house, and understood that the tree had done, in twenty years, what Venturoso had not done: it had sprung up, grown, borne fruit, completed itself, without awaiting anything, with no next year, simply living what it had to live in the place where it happened to fall. The fig tree did not await the floor. It did not await the parlour ready to grow. It grew in the unfinished parlour, gave figs on the earth floor, made itself a whole tree in the middle of the skeleton, and was now the only finished and living thing in that house of unfinished things. And Esperança went up to it, and picked a fig, the first she had ever picked from that tree in twenty years, because she had always left them for when, had always thought that eating the figs of the parlour fig tree was for when the parlour was ready; and she ate it there, ripe and sweet and warm from the sun, standing in the unfinished house, and it was the sweetest fig of her life, because it was a fig of today, picked and eaten in the present, from the tree that had taught her, silent, for twenty years, what she only now understood: that one lives where one is, with what one has, now, and that one who awaits the floor in order to grow does not grow, and one who grows does not await the floor.

And before coming down, Esperança did two things that were the true farewell. First, she took from the unfinished house a small stone, loose, from the floor, one of those Venturoso had left there twenty years before for a wall that never rose, and kept it, to put in her house of today, at the window, by the flowers, and each time she saw it to remember not the house that was not finished, but the woman who un-walled herself from it. And then she picked a handful of figs from the parlour fig tree, and took them too, to eat at home, and to keep a seed, which she would plant at the window, in a pot, and which would grow in her house, the real one, the inhabited one, the fig tree of the unfinished house transplanted to the house yet to be lived; and she took the two things, the stone and the figs, down the slope, not as grief, but as remembrance, of the Esperança who went up to the house in the sun and asked herself how much of her life was walled up there, and who came down resolved to wall up nothing more, to live what she had, today, in the house there was, with the life there was, which is all there is, and which is enough, for one who learns, at last, to stop keeping it awaiting another that does not come.

She looked, from the middle of the slope, one last time at the house in the sun, beautiful and empty, and said to it, low, goodbye, as one says goodbye to a thing one loved and that cost one dear: "Keep what I gave you, house. Twenty years. I give you no more. And I don't wish you ill, for it's not your fault, the fault is not of unfinished houses, it's of those who put their life on hold in them. I put. And now I take it out. Stay in the sun, house, beautiful as you are, skeleton of my waiting. I am going to live." And she came down the slope, with the small stone in her hand, to her real house, to her life of today, and did not go up again, and the unfinished house stayed on the slope, in the sun, as it had always stayed, beautiful and empty, awaiting a roof that Venturoso, alone now, might put on next year, or might not, but that no longer had, walled up in its golden walls, the life of any woman, because the one who had been immured there for twenty years had come out, at last, into the sun, and come down the slope, alive, to begin, late but in time, her own life.
