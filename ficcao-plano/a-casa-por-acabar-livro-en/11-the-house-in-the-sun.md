## 11. The house in the sun

Some days after taking her leave of Venturoso, Esperança climbed, one last time, to the unfinished house, not to dream it, as she had climbed for twenty years, but to take her leave of it, and it was on that climb, and in what she saw up there, that there closed, within her, what the whole book had been opening.

It was morning, and the sun struck the slope, and the unfinished house was in the sun, with the walls golden from the light, the openings bare, the roof half done, the grass grown inside shining with dew, and Esperança, reaching the top and seeing it so, in the sun, had a thing she had never had in the thousand times she had climbed there: she saw it as it was. Not as it was going to be, with the veranda and the hearth and the in-earnest life; she saw it as it was, and as it was in the sun, with the light crossing it through the windowless openings, with the sky coming in where the roof was to be, it was, Esperança discovered with a tightening, beautiful. Beautiful as a skeleton is beautiful, with the clean beauty of things that are not finished and that perhaps for that show their form better, the bones of the house in the sun, without the flesh of the plastered walls and the roofs and the windows, only the structure, the design, the promise in stone, in the sun, beautiful and empty.

And Esperança went in, by what was to be the door, and passed her hand over the jamb, over the stone with the date carved twenty years before, the beginning, and walked through the rooms that existed only on the floor, the kitchen, the bedroom, stopped in the opening that was to be the veranda at sunrise, and looked at the view, the best of Véspera, the village, the river, and at last looked at the whole house around her, in the sun, and asked, at last, the question the whole book had brought her to, and asked it aloud, alone, in the unfinished house, in the sun: how much of my life is walled up here?

Because that was, she perceived now, what that house had been for twenty years: not the house where she was going to live, but the house where she had put her life on hold, wall by wall. Each year she had waited was there, in those walls in the sun; each yellow sheet, each next year, each feast at which she had said when the house is ready, each time she had refused to live today because the house was not ready, all was there, walled up in that unfinished house, twenty years of Esperança's life immured in that promise of stone, in the sun, beautiful and empty, as her life had been beautiful and empty, full of waiting and hollow of life. The house was not unfinished; it was full. It was full of Esperança's life, of the twenty years she had given it, and it was for that it was so beautiful in the sun and so empty at once: because it was a skeleton, yes, but it was a skeleton in which was immured a woman, alive, who looked now at the walls themselves and asked herself how she had left so much of herself there.

And she did, there, a sum that made her shudder: twenty years of Sundays climbing to that house to dream it; twenty years of nights falling asleep imagining it ready; twenty years of small gestures postponed, of journeys not made, of things not first-used, of a whole woman's life set aside, wall by wall, in that house that never received her. And she perceived that if she had given that house half the love and the faith she had given it, and had given it to her own life, she would have today a full life, instead of an empty house; that she had put in the wrong place twenty years of a rare thing, of a faith, of a faithfulness, of a capacity to love, and that those things, put in a promise of stone, had rotted like the sheets in the chest, when, put in the real life, they would have borne fruit. And it was not anger she felt, it was a grief, the grief of the twenty years, and she wept it there, in the house in the sun, but she wept it, and that was new, without now putting anything more there, without promising that house one more year of her life.

And Esperança wept, in the house in the sun, but it was a weeping different from that of the leave-taking of Venturoso; it was a weeping for herself, for the twenty years, for the woman she had been, the forever betrothed, the one who waited, and it was a weeping of grief, but also, beneath, of liberation, because in seeing, at last, how much of herself was walled up there, Esperança was also, in the same gesture, taking herself out of there, unwalling herself, recovering for herself the years she could still live, which were fewer than those she had had before her when young, but which were, still, a life, thirty years perhaps, thirty years she was not going to wall up in any unfinished house, that she was going to live, day by day, in the present, in her real house, with flowers at the window and the first-used sheets.

And before going down, Esperança did a thing that was the true leave-taking: she took from the unfinished house a small stone, loose, from the floor, one of those Venturoso had left there twenty years before for a wall that never rose, and carried it with her, down the slope, not as a grievance, but as a keepsake, to put it in her house of today, at the window, beside the flowers, and each time she saw it to remember not the house that was not finished, but the woman who unwalled herself from it, the Esperança who climbed to the house in the sun and asked herself how much of her life was immured there, and who came down resolved to wall up nothing more, to live what she had, today, in the house that was, with the life that was, which is all there is, and which is enough, for one who learns, at last, to stop keeping it awaiting another that does not come.

She looked, from the middle of the slope, one last time at the house in the sun, beautiful and empty, and said to it, under her breath, goodbye, as one says goodbye to a thing one loved and that cost one dear: keep what I gave you, house. Twenty years. I give you no more. And I do not wish you ill, for you are not to blame, the blame is not the unfinished houses', it is of whoever puts in them their life on hold. I put it. And now I take it. Stay in the sun, house, beautiful as you are, skeleton of my waiting. I am going to live. And she went down the slope, with the small stone in her hand, to her real house, to her life of today, and did not climb up again, and the unfinished house stayed on the slope, in the sun, as it had always stayed, beautiful and empty, awaiting a roof that Venturoso, alone now, would perhaps put on next year, or perhaps not, but which no longer had, immured in its golden walls, the life of any woman, because the one who had been walled there twenty years had gone out, at last, into the sun, and gone down the slope, alive, to begin, late but in time, her own life.
