## 9. The first account

The letter never came to be delivered. Benvinda kept it in the apron, beside the wholesaler's, and the next morning, on opening the shop, she understood that the letter was still the easy way, the way of one who collects without showing their face, the way from outside. And she was not from outside. She was of the Grocery, and at the Grocery things are said by mouth, at the counter, with eyes in eyes. So she tore up the letter, and decided to do the thing by the difficult door: to wait for Marçais to come, as he always came, and to collect from him there, standing, with the ledger in her hand.

Marçais had not come to the shop since he had learned of the closing, but the vineyard on the height needed sulphate, and the sulphate was sold at the Grocery, and the Estrada Nova was far and the city lorry charged double. So Benvinda knew that he would appear, sooner or later, pushed by his own convenience, and she prepared herself for that day as she had never prepared herself for anything.

Benvinda prepared herself that morning as she had never prepared herself. She got up even earlier than usual, and instead of opening the shop at once she stayed a while at the counter, alone, with the ledger open at Marçais's page, looking at the number, accustoming her eye to the number, saying it low to herself until it ceased to frighten her. She had understood, with the failed visits, that the number trembled in her mouth because it was the first time she said it; so she rehearsed it, as one rehearses a word of another language, until the tongue made it without stumbling. She said the number to the shelf of the salt cod, said it to the scale, said it to the lamp. And at a certain point the number ceased to be an accusation and became only a number, a truth, a thing that was so whether she trembled or not. And it was with the number already tamed in her mouth that she opened the door, and that she waited, the whole day, without haste, with the patience of one who waits for a flood they know will come, for Marçais to appear.

He came on a Tuesday morning, with his effrontery intact, as though he had never gone about saying through the village that the Grocery was no longer what it had been. He came in with the broad laugh, greeted Benvinda like one who greets an old friend, and asked for the sulphate, and how much it was, and took from his pocket, for the sulphate, cash on the spot, ready money, folded notes, because the sulphate was from outside and the supplier of the sulphate was the house, and Marçais paid the house for the sulphate in cash while he owed the same house eleven years of trust. It was that which gave Benvinda the strength she lacked: to see the ready money in his hand, the money he had, the money that paid in cash for what was from outside and got on trust without end what was from within.

"The sulphate is this price," said Benvinda, and said it firm, and Marçais laid down the notes, and she did not take them up at once. "But before the sulphate, Marçais, we have another account."

Marçais's laugh hesitated, only an instant.

"Another account?"

Benvinda took the ledger from under the counter, and opened it at his page, which she knew by heart, and turned it toward him, on the wood, beside the notes for the sulphate.

"This one," she said. "Yours. Eleven years, Marçais. It is all here, in my hand and in my father's before me, day by day, year by year. I never told you the number, because in this shop it was not said. I am going to tell it you now, once, aloud, and you are going to hear it, because you should have heard it long ago and the fault of your not having heard it is mine, who silenced it from you." And she said the number. She said it slowly, whole, without rounding, the exact number of eleven years of wine and tobacco and flour and old sulphates, and her voice trembled at the beginning and steadied at the end, because there are things that, once begun, sustain themselves. "This is what you owe me. I do not ask you for all of it at once. But I ask you for a part, now, of the ready money you have there, and the rest we will arrange, as you can, slowly. Because the shop is going to close, Marçais, and it is going to close because of accounts like yours, and I am no longer of an age nor of a mind to close my father's door in silence, pretending that no one owes me anything."

A silence fell in the shop. Marçais looked at the number in the ledger, looked at the notes for the sulphate, looked at Benvinda, and his face passed quickly through three things: the surprise, then the shame, and then, over the two, to cover them, the offence. He chose the offence, because the offence was the one that left him standing.

"Well, I never," he said, and the broad laugh turned into another thing, finer, more dangerous. "Well, I never, Benvinda. You come to me now, after so many years, with accounts? You? Abílio's daughter? Your father, may his soul rest, never in his life asked a penny of anyone. Your father gave the shirt off his back. And you, now, you have taken to this? To collecting from friends like a..." — he stopped in time so as not to say the word, but the word stayed in the air, and the word was *moneylender*, or *big house*, or any of those that are flung at one who collects — "...to collecting from friends at the door? You see? It is this. It is what comes of it. I well said to the village that the Grocery was no longer what it had been. The father giving, and the daughter collecting. Poor Abílio, if he saw this."

It was cruel, and it was cruel in the exact place, because it was the father's phrase turned against her, it was all her fear put into words by the mouth of a debtor. *You were always like your father, and now you have taken to this.* Marçais, without knowing it, had found the one weapon that could fell Benvinda: to accuse her of betraying the father. And Benvinda felt the ground flee from under her, felt the old will to draw back, to laugh, to say ah Marçais, you are right, never mind, take the sulphate, forget what I said. She felt her hand want to take up the notes for the sulphate and push the ledger under the counter and go back to being the good one, the beloved one, the one who never collects. It was the hardest moment of all her life, and it lasted a second, and in that second there passed through her head the big house fallen in the brambles, and the mother with her hand on the empty larder, and the sister in the rain saying you are not good, you are afraid, and the niece asking cared for you for what.

And she bore it.

She did not draw back. She did not take up the notes for the sulphate. She did not lower the ledger. She looked at Marçais, at his fine laugh, and said, with the voice that came out of her not trembling, but low, and firm, and sad, which is how the truth comes out when it costs:

"My father did not give the shirt off his back, Marçais. My father had fear, and I inherited his fear, and you, and the whole village, lived forty years on account of that fear, and called it goodness because it came cheap to you. But it is over. I am not betraying my father. I am doing what he never had the courage to do, which is to look at a man who has a vineyard and cattle and ready money in his pocket, and who has owed me for eleven years, and to tell him: pay. Not because I am the big house. But because I am not free, Marçais. The Grocery was never free. It always cost, only the one who paid was I, and my mother before me, in the dark, in the back room. The back room is over. Now the accounts are done here, in front, in the light, as honourable people do them. Pay what you can today, and we will arrange the rest. And if you do not wish to pay, take the sulphate all the same, for I refuse sulphate to no one. But take it knowing that you owe me, and that I know that you know, and that from now on, when you smile at me in the largo, the two of us are going to know the truth. That is all I ask of you, Marçais. That we stop pretending."

It was the father's phrase, turned inside out. Her whole life Benvinda had heard "a shop that collects is a shop that loses friends" as a law of the world. And there, at the counter, saying it to Marçais the other way round, she understood that the father's phrase had always hidden a question that no one in the family had had the courage to ask: and what friend is that who is lost by a shop collecting what it gave him? The father's phrase protected the Marçaises. It served those who took and did not pay, gave them a beautiful reason not to pay, made of the collecting a lack of friendship and of the default a form of fondness. It was a phrase made to the measure of the debtors, and the father, without meaning to, had spent his life reciting the excuse of those who abused him. Benvinda saw all this in a flash, at the counter, with Marçais before her, and it was that vision, more than the courage, that held her standing: she understood that to yield to Marçais was not to be good like the father, it was to go on giving voice to the lie that had sunk the house for three generations.

Marçais was left without his laugh. He looked at her, and for an instant, only an instant, the man beneath the welcoming was seen, and the man beneath was naked, and the man beneath knew that she was right. And it was perhaps for knowing that she was right that he chose, in the end, not to pay that day. He took up the sulphate, put the notes for the sulphate on the counter, said something or other about going to fetch the rest from home and coming back, a lie that the two of them knew was a lie, and went out, smaller than he had come in, without the laugh, without the effrontery, with the debt now said, named, set between the two of them for ever.

And when the door closed, Benvinda had to grip the counter, because her legs trembled, and her heart beat as though she had run, and her eyes burned. But she did not weep. She stayed there, gripping the counter, breathing, waiting for the sky to fall, waiting for the punishment, waiting for the shop to collapse because she had collected, because she had betrayed the father, because she had been the big house. And the sky did not fall. The shop did not collapse. Out there, in the largo, life went on the same, the dry fountain, the weak sun, an ox-cart creaking. She had collected, for the first time in her life, and the world, against everything she had feared for sixty-one years, went on exactly in the same place.

She stayed still a long time gripping the counter, and within all that trembling there came to her, slowly, another thing, deeper than the fear, deeper than the relief: an old anger, mild, with no recipient. Anger at having spent sixty-one years trembling at a thing that did not happen. Anger for the father, who had trembled seventy. Anger for the grandfather, who had signed the cross. Anger for that line of people of her house who had lived shrunken before a danger that after all, put to the test, had no teeth at all, that was only a shadow cast by a big house that had already fallen and that no one had had the courage to go and see was fallen. How much life wasted, thought Benvinda, gripping the counter. How much soup with water, how many turned skirts, how much sleep lost by the mother, how much fear, and all on account of a shadow. Marçais had gone out smaller, yes, but she felt no victory over Marçais: she felt mourning. Mourning for all the years in which she could have been just and had been only fearful, and had called it goodness, and had made of that a pride.

Aurora, who had heard everything from the top of the stairs and had not dared to come down, came down now, and found her aunt gripping the counter, white, trembling, and went to embrace her, and Benvinda let herself be embraced, she who always embraced others and was never embraced, and said to her in her ear, with a thread of voice, a thing that was pure astonishment:

"Nothing fell, my girl. I collected, and nothing fell. My whole life I was afraid the sky would fall, and after all the sky does not fall. Who would have told me. Sixty-one years afraid of a thing that does not happen."

"And did you hurt yourself with what he said?" asked Aurora, low, still embracing her. "That about your father. You were always like him, now you have taken to this."

"I did," said Benvinda. "I did, because he hit the place. But do you know what, my girl? While I was here, trembling, I understood a thing about that phrase. Marçais said it to fell me, and he nearly felled me. But the phrase, seen cold, is a lie. I am not being like my father. My father gave out of fear. I am doing the contrary of my father: I am collecting despite the fear. Marçais wanted to tell me that I was betraying my father, and what I am doing is the contrary of a betrayal: I am doing what my father had not the courage to do, and that, deep down, he perhaps wished he had done." She straightened up, slowly, freed herself from the embrace, wiped her eyes with the back of her hand. "The debtors, when one collects from them, always throw in our face the person we most love, to stop us. Marçais threw my father at me. It is their weapon. But it is only a weapon, Aurora. It is not a truth. And today I learned the difference, which is perhaps the most useful thing I learned in my life: when someone, so as not to pay you, tells you that you have changed for the worse, it is because what changed was only one thing, that you ceased to be of use to them for free. And that is not to change for the worse. It is only to cease being a fool."

And she laughed, through the trembling, a wet laugh, astonished, almost like a little girl's, the laugh of one who has done for the first time a forbidden thing and discovers that the prohibition was only fear, and that the fear, that, was the only thing falling, now, slowly, there at the counter, embraced by her niece, on the day the kindest woman of the largo ceased to be too kind and discovered, with her legs trembling and the whole sky in its place, that she went on standing.
