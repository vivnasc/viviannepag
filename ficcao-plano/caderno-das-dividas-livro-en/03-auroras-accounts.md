## 3. Aurora's accounts

Aurora stayed, and the Grocery, which had been of two people for a week, learned to be of two ways of seeing.

In the morning, it was the shop of always. Benvinda at the counter, the board ledger, the village coming in on trust, the scale leaning half a finger to the customer's side. In the afternoon, it was Aurora's shop: the niece settled in at the back table, where the oilcloth had the marks of three generations of lunches, with the clean notebook, the pen, and a small adding machine she had brought from the city and that made a dry noise at each number, tac, tac, tac, a noise Benvinda began to hear even when the machine was still, even at night, as though the whole house had taken to counting.

Aurora was not meddling in her aunt's life out of malice. She was meddling because she did not know how to be otherwise. It was her wound, though she called it method: where others rested, Aurora measured; where others accepted, Aurora verified. She had left the village at eighteen, the first to study away, and each thing she had won out there had turned at once into the step to the next, without ever an afternoon of arrival, without ever a this is fine as it is. She came back now to Véspera to sell her share of her father's house and go on her way, and the share of the house was only one more number to resolve, and the aunt was only one more account to close, and Aurora did not understand, yet, that she had come to the only place in the world where the numbers did not close on purpose.

She did accounts, Benvinda discovered on watching her work, in the same way that she herself gave: without being able to stop. It was a trade and it was a flight at the same time, like her generosity. Aurora put life into columns for city people, earned it well, said she was the best in the house where she worked, and fled, from column to column, from the one account she never did, that of herself: the years passing, the houses she did not buy for being always about to leave, the life put off until after the next step that was never the last. The girl who saw so clearly the numbers of others saw none of her own, and Benvinda, who recognised the flight because she had her own, said nothing, but kept it, and understood that the niece had not come to Véspera only to save the aunt's shop: she had come, without knowing it, to stop, even if it were for the first time in twenty-three years, at a place where someone would force her to look at a thing face to face to the end.

"Aunt, come and see this."

It was the third day of the accounting. Benvinda went, with the cloth in her hand, and sat down at the back table reluctantly, like one who sits in the dentist's chair.

Aurora had done the work in earnest. She had passed the board ledger into the clean notebook, page by page, and then had done what no one had done: she had organised the debtors. In one column, what each one owed. In another, for how long. In yet another, in a smaller hand, a note of her own: *pays sometimes*, *never paid*, *pays in kind*, *needs*. It was a portrait of the village that was frightening for how clear it was, the whole village reduced to who can and does not pay, who cannot and tries, who never could and never tried.

"Look," said Aurora, and pointed with the pen. "Marçais. He has owed you for eleven years. Eleven, aunt. And Marçais has the vineyard up on the height, has cattle, sells wine to the lorry that comes from the city. He is not poor. He was never poor. He pays you once a year, at the vintage, a crumb, and takes on trust the whole year. This is not need, aunt. This is habit. The man got used to the Grocery being free, and you taught it him."

Benvinda looked at the Marçais line. But she did not see the line: she saw Marçais, she saw Marçais's father, who had been a friend of her father's, she saw Marçais's mother dying and the Grocery sending the broth every day of that winter, she saw a web of things that did not fit in any column.

"You see a number," said Benvinda. "I see thirty years. That number, to you, is money that is lacking. To me it is the broth my mother sent to his mother when she was dying, and his wedding feast, at which my father gave the wine, and the day he came to the shop weeping because the cattle fell ill, and I gave him the whole feed on trust without noting half of it. How do I collect thirty years, Aurora? Where does one begin to collect from a person one watched grow up?"

"One begins with the present, aunt," said Aurora, with the new patience of one who had learned, in three days, not to trample. "One does not collect the broth of his mother. That was given, and what is given is not collected, it is given. One collects last week's tobacco and last month's wine, that he takes and could pay and does not pay. Your past with Marçais is yours, it is beautiful, it stays. But Thursday's tobacco is no funeral broth, aunt. It is tobacco. And he has the means to pay it. It is you who mix everything, who put the broth of the dead mother on top of Thursday's tobacco so as not to have to collect either the broth or the tobacco. Separate the things. The love of thirty years to one side, the account of Thursday to the other. One is not collecting the love. One is collecting the tobacco."

Benvinda was left silent, because the niece had said a thing that was almost wisdom, and that came from a girl she had thought only knew how to add up. To separate the love from the account. It was that the house had never known how to do, since the grandfather: either everything was mixed in the love and nothing was collected, or everything was mixed in the account, like the big house, and the love was collected along with the interest. And yet, thought Benvinda, perhaps the thing Aurora said was possible, perhaps there was a way of keeping the broth of the dead mother in one place and Thursday's tobacco in another, and of collecting one without undoing the other. Perhaps. But for that a firmness was needed that she did not have, and a faith she did not have, the faith that Marçais might know, he too, how to separate the things, and go on caring for her after paying the tobacco. And in that faith was the hole.

"Marçais lost his father young," she said. "The Grocery helped them at that time. There are things that are not collected."

"The Grocery helped them forty years ago, aunt. Forty." Aurora laid down the pen. "You are collecting from yourself a favour your father did to a man who is already dead, and paying it to his son, who has a vineyard. This is not goodness. I do not know what it is, but goodness it is not, because goodness does not leave a family hard up so that a vineyard may do well."

The phrase stayed. *A family hard up.* Benvinda did not answer at once, because the phrase had struck her in a place she had long protected, the place where the mother was turning the skirt, the Christmas cheese, the soup with water. The family hard up was not an abstraction. The family hard up had been her, as a girl. And now the family hard up was her again, alone, at the counter, without confessing to herself that she was hard up because a woman who gives to everyone cannot be hard up, that would be a contradiction that dismantled her whole life.

"Show me the ones from outside," she said, instead of answering.

Aurora turned the page. On the other side of the village was the city, and the city did not give on trust. The wholesaler who had supplied the Grocery since the Estrada Nova opened sent the bills on time, with the typed letters, and wanted his money on the marked dates, because for the wholesaler the Grocery was not a house of friends: it was a customer, a name on a list of many, and a customer who does not pay is a customer who is cut off.

"Here is the whole problem in one line," said Aurora, and her finger landed at the bottom of the page. "The village owes you this number. You owe the wholesaler this other one. The second is smaller than the first. You have, on paper, the means to pay the wholesaler three times over. But you have not the means to pay him even once, because what they owe you is all on trust and what you owe is in cash. You are rich in trust and poor in money. And the wholesaler does not eat trust, aunt. The wholesaler eats money, and when there is none, he cuts you off. And when he cuts you off, you close. And when you close, do you know what happens to the village that owes you all this number?" Aurora tapped her finger on the large number. "Nothing. Nothing happens to it. The village goes to the city lorry to buy dearer and forgets the Grocery in a month. The only one who sinks in this well is you."

Benvinda got up. Not out of anger: out of distress. Aurora's clarity was like a light too strong in a room where one had always lived by the lamp, and it hurt the eyes. She went to the counter, to the drawer she did not open, and took from it the bundle of the wholesaler's letters, and this time she did not lay it down: she opened the last one, there, standing, with the niece watching, and read the typed letters, cold, that did not know her by name, that called her *Dear Madam* and gave her a deadline, a real deadline, with a date, after which *we shall see ourselves forced to suspend supply*. The date was six weeks away.

She laid the letter on the counter, beside the board ledger. And there, on that counter, at the end of the afternoon of an October day, were the two truths of Benvinda's life, side by side: the ledger of the names, where the village owed her the world and she forgave everything, and the letter of the number, where someone from outside collected from her what was hers without forgiving anything. And between the two, she, who had learned to give like the father and to watch like the mother, and who had never, never learned the third thing, the thing that was lacking, the thing for which there was no master at the Grocery: to collect.

"Six weeks," said Aurora, low, reading over her aunt's shoulder. "Aunt, in six weeks you have to receive a part of what they owe you. Not all of it. A part. You have to ask for what is yours. Only that."

"Only that," repeated Benvinda.

And she laughed, a dry laugh, with no joy at all, the laugh of one who hears the hardest thing in the world called simple. Because for Aurora, who saw numbers, it was only that: to ask for what is yours. For Benvinda, who saw faces, it was to ask of the miller's widow, of old Aprígio, of Marçais whose father had died, of the whole village she had taught, over forty years, that the Grocery was the place where one came in empty-handed and went out with full hands and never, never heard a number said aloud. It was to undo, in a letter of six weeks, the house that three generations had built with open doors. It was to betray the father. It was to become the thing the father had most feared, and that she still did not know how to name, but that had a name, and a ground, and a story, and that she was going to have to go and dig up before she could take up the pencil for anything other than to strike out.

"Tomorrow," said Benvinda, and folded the letter, and kept it, this time not in the drawer she did not open, but in the pocket of her apron, against her body. "Tomorrow I am taking you to a place. Before I ask for what is mine of anyone whatsoever, my girl, you have to see what it cost this house, a long time ago, to owe to one who collects. You have to see where all this comes from. Otherwise you do not understand, and neither do I, and two women who do not understand what they are doing with money always make a mess, be it in giving, be it in collecting."

Aurora closed the clean notebook. For the first time since she had arrived, she did not have a number for her aunt. She had a question, and the question was new in her, who lived on answers.

That night, Benvinda heard her niece pacing the house. Aurora did not sleep: her steps could be heard on the floorboards of the room, from one side to the other, and in the middle of the night the kitchen light came on, and Benvinda, who also did not sleep, went down and found her at the table, with the clean notebook open and the adding machine, doing numbers at two in the morning.

"Are you not sleeping, my girl?"

"I cannot, aunt." Aurora did not even raise her eyes. "I am doing the accounts of the sale of father's house. And then those of what I am going to do with the money. And then those of what I am going to do after that. I always do accounts ahead, you see? For next year, for five years from now. If I stop doing accounts ahead, aunt, a distress comes over me that I cannot explain."

Benvinda sat down, and looked at her niece, and saw, beneath the girl of the accounts, a thing she recognised, because it was sister to her own: a fear. Aurora fled forward as she fled from collecting. They were two ways of the same inability to be still before what one feels.

"You run, Aurora," said Benvinda, low. "I noticed it the moment you arrived. You run as though there were a place to arrive at, and you never arrive, because the moment you arrive at one you are already doing the accounts of the next." And as the niece raised her eyes, surprised, Benvinda added, with tenderness: "We are of the same house, my girl. I do not collect, you do not stop. The two of us are afraid to be still looking at a thing face to face. I at a debt they owe me, you at a life you do not know whether you want. But that is your affair, and it is for another night. Tonight the problem is mine. Go and sleep, for tomorrow I need you with a fresh head."

"What place are you going to show me, then?"

"The big house," said Benvinda. "What is left of it. Come, go and sleep, for it is on foot and I want to get there before the heat."
