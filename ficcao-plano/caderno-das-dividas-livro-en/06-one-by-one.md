## 6. One by one

Benvinda decided to begin with the easiest, and discovered, at the first door, that at the Grocery there was no easiest at all.

She rehearsed at home, at the mirror of the room, like a little girl before the school recital. She had written on a paper, in her small hand, the words she was going to say, and she said them to the mirror, low, and to the mirror they came out well: look, it is on account of the wholesaler, who is pressing me, and I needed you to go on paying me a part of the arrears, slowly, as you can. It was a good phrase. It was just, it was gentle, it had no threat at all, it asked for a part and not all, it gave time. At the mirror, Benvinda said it and thought herself able. The problem was that between the mirror and the door of the miller's widow there was the whole largo, and the largo undid her phrase at every step.

The miller's widow was the first, because she was the first of every morning, and Benvinda thought that if she managed with the widow she would manage with all of them. She went to her house, at the end of the afternoon, with an excuse in her mouth, which was to bring her a jar of honey that was left over, and the excuse was already the first defeat, because one who goes to collect does not bring gifts. The widow received her with celebration, sat her down, made her tea with the sugar got on trust at the Grocery, and told her of the leg and the son, and halfway through Benvinda searched for the phrase of the mirror and the phrase was not there. There was the widow, the leg, the cold house, the portrait of the dead miller on the wall, and the phrase of the mirror, before all that, seemed an indecency. Benvinda drank the tea, heard the whole leg, and went away without saying anything, and on the way she realised that, on taking her leave, she had promised the widow to bring her the next day an old wrap she no longer used. She had gone to collect and had come out owing a jumper.

"I could not," she said to Aurora, on arriving.

"I know," said Aurora, who had seen her go out with the honey. "The widow truly needs it, aunt. That one, perhaps, is to forgive. Go to the ones who have."

And Benvinda went to the ones who had, and with the ones who had it was worse.

She went to the house of Marçais, the one of the eleven years of debt, the one of the vineyard on the height. And Marçais received her with even more celebration than the widow, because Marçais was a man of welcoming, fat and laughing, and had a bottle of his wine opened, the wine he sold to the city lorry, and filled two glasses, and toasted the Grocery and Benvinda's health and the memory of her father, who was a saint, he said, a man such as there are no longer. And he spoke of the vineyard, and of the frost that threatened, and of the price of the wine that was at death's door, and when Benvinda, with the glass of his wine in her hand, finally managed to draw out the phrase of the mirror and said, fearfully, it is on account of the wholesaler, you see, who is pressing me, Marçais was serious a moment, and then gave a great burst of laughter and clapped her on the shoulder and said:

"Ah those wholesalers, Benvinda, they are all thieves! They bleed us, they bleed us who are of the country! But do not you worry, woman, the Grocery does not close, the Grocery is eternal, it was your father's and your grandfather's, that never closes. Drink, drink, for this year the wine came out fine."

And he filled her glass again, and changed the subject, and Benvinda understood, with his wine warming her face, that Marçais had not said no to her. Worse: he had not even heard that she was asking. To Marçais, the debt to the Grocery was not a debt: it was a law of nature, like the frost and the price of the wine, a thing that had always been and always would be, and that a man does not pay because one does not pay what is eternal. Marçais was no swindler. He was worse, for Benvinda's case: he was a good man who had grown used to it, and to whom no one had ever said the number, and who was going to go on not hearing the number until the number was said in a way he could not confuse with the chatter of gossips.

She came out of Marçais's house with half a glass of wine too much and nothing collected, and had to sit on a stone seat by the way to get some air, and it was there, on the stone, that she understood what she had been doing wrong. She had been begging. And to beg, in her mouth, sounded like an apology, sounded like one who repents for bothering, and no one pays one who apologises for collecting. The father had never asked anything of anyone because the father gave. She, who had not learned to give in another way, tried now to collect with the voice of one who gives, and that was what came out: a woman offering honey and jumpers and drinking the wine of the debtors, coming out of each house more in debt than she had gone in.

The week was so. Benvinda visited seven houses and came back from all of them with her hands emptier. The mother of the twins paid her a crumb, in front of her, with a ceremony that was almost a humiliation, and took right afterwards twice as much on trust, as though paying gave her the right to take more, and Benvinda noted it, of course she noted it. Old Aprígio, when she touched on the matter of the eleven years of loaves, played deaf with an art of so many years that it was impossible to know whether it was deafness or strategy, and answered everything with that's it, that's it, and offered her a peach in syrup from last year, and Benvinda came out of there eating the peach and understanding that she had been defeated again, and this time with syrup.

There was one house, in the middle of the week, that went another way, and Benvinda kept it because it was the first to show her that perhaps the thing was not entirely impossible. It was the house of Firmeza, of the Serranos, a hard woman, from up the serra, who had for a motto to owe nothing to anyone and who always paid on time, double if she could. Firmeza did not owe the Grocery: on the contrary, she was among the few who paid everything in cash, with an almost gruff pride. But Benvinda knew a thing about Firmeza that no one else knew: that in a bad winter, years before, Firmeza had truly gone hungry, up there, alone, and that Benvinda had given her the food of that whole winter on trust without noting a single line, on purpose, so that Firmeza would not have to carry the weight of owing, because there were prides that had to be spared. They had never spoken of it. And that week, on learning that the Grocery was going to close, it was Firmeza who came down the serra, a rare thing, and came into the shop, and put on the counter an envelope, and said, gruff as she was:

"That winter. You pretended not to note it, but I know how to count, Benvinda, and I know what I ate and what I did not pay. Here it is. Do not tell me no, for I do not come down this serra twice for the same thing."

And Benvinda, who was going to refuse out of habit, out of that habit of not receiving that was the twin brother of that of not collecting, looked at Firmeza's face and understood that to refuse would be to rob that woman of the one thing she wanted: to pay, to be square, not to owe. She accepted. It was the first time in a long while that Benvinda received without discomfort, and she understood, on keeping the envelope, that to receive and to collect were of the same family, and that she was afraid of both for the same reason. Firmeza left without taking much leave, and Benvinda was left with the envelope and with a new idea: that there were people who wished to pay, who needed to pay, and that her goodness of not collecting, to those, did not spare them: it humiliated them, left them owing for ever a thing they wished to take off their shoulders. That not everyone was Marçais. That for some, to collect was a favour.

The house that most disarmed her was that of old Aprígio, the one of the eleven years of loaves. Benvinda went there determined, with the number in her head, and the old man received her with the celebration of those who live alone and rarely have a visit. The house was poor as a nail, cold, with a single good chair that he offered her, sitting himself on the lame stool. And Benvinda, on looking around, on seeing the poverty of that house, understood a thing that Aurora's column did not say: that old Aprígio did not pay for the loaves not out of vice, like Marçais, but because he truly had not the means. The eleven years of loaves were not an abuse. They were hunger, slow hunger, the hunger of an old man alone who is ashamed to say it and hides it behind a joke about the day of his pension. Benvinda came out of there without having collected anything, and that time it was not defeat: it was discernment. She learned, in Aprígio's house, the first half of what she lacked, which was to know whom not to collect from. The old man stayed on the list of those one forgives on purpose, and Benvinda struck out his eleven years that night, but she struck them out now in another way, not out of fear of asking him, but for having seen the cold house and the single chair, and for choosing, with open eyes, to give him that. The difference between the old striking-out and this one was invisible on the paper and was everything: one was blind, the other was seen.

On the eighth day, she told the results to Aurora, at the back table. In one column, what she had collected in the week: almost nothing, a crumb from the mother of the twins. In another column, what she had given in the week, in trying to collect: the jar of honey, the jumper, three afternoons of tea, half a day of the shop's work lost. She was losing money trying to receive money. The collecting, in her hand, cost more than the debt.

Aurora looked at the two columns and did not laugh, because she was no longer the girl who laughed at her aunt's accounts. She was serious, and said a thing that was half account, half another thing:

"Aunt, I understood a thing this week, watching you go out and come in. You are not afraid to collect. You are afraid that they will stop caring for you. They are different things, and I confused them, and so did you. To collect is only to say a number. You say numbers all day, you say the prices of the tinned goods without trembling. What you cannot do is risk that someone, on hearing the number, will look at you differently. It is that which paralyses you. It is not the money. It is the look."

Benvinda was silent. The niece was right, and her rightness was new, because it was not an account: it was a thing about the heart, said by a girl who lived fleeing from her own. And perhaps for being said by one who fled was it that she hit the mark: Aurora, who ran without stopping so that no one would see where she did not arrive, knew from within the fear of being looked at in the raw light, and saw it in her aunt as one sees in a mirror.

"And how does one learn that, my girl?" asked Benvinda. "To bear the look?"

"I do not know, aunt." Aurora was honest, for the first time in a long while, about a thing she did not know. "I never learned it. I flee. You, at least, stayed. You stayed your whole life at the same counter, bearing the whole village. Perhaps you know how to bear looks better than I do, only you have not yet tried to bear the worst of all: that of someone you collected from." She paused. "Perhaps it is not so bad as you think. Perhaps one who cares for you in earnest goes on caring. And one who stops caring on account of a number... that one, aunt, cared for you for what?"

The question stayed. *Cared for you for what.* Benvinda took it to bed that night, and the question did not let her sleep, but it was an insomnia different from the others, an insomnia that went forward instead of going round in circles. Because Aurora's question dismantled the father's law from within. The father said: a shop that collects loses friends. But if a friend is lost by a shop collecting what is owed it, then what was that friend, exactly? Was he a friend, or was he a free customer? And the others, those who would stay even after paying, those, whom the father had never come to know because he had never put them to the test, would those not be the true friends, the only ones worth the trouble, those who stayed for the person and not for the open larder?

There was, still, the visit to the mother of the twins, which was the one that most confused her, because the mother of the twins was among those who paid a crumb and took twice as much, and Benvinda never knew whether that was need or cunning. She went to her house, and the house was of one who governs with little and sews the little so it seems more: clean, mended, with the two twins studying at the table, by the weak light, with the school books that the mother paid for who knows how. And Benvinda understood, there, that the mother of the twins was neither cunning nor an abuser: she was a woman making the best of a bad job so that the children might study, paying the Grocery the crumb she could and taking on trust what she needed so that the children would not go through what she had gone through. It was not a case of collecting nor quite of forgiving: it was a case of going on giving on trust, with confidence, because that debt was an investment in the village, was the Grocery doing what the Grocery does best, holding up a family on its way up. Benvinda came out of there having learned the third thing, after learning whom to collect from and whom to give to: whom to go on giving on trust to. Because there were debts that were neither to collect nor to forgive: they were to maintain, with faith, as one maintains a wager on a person one knows will pay when they can, and who in the meantime needs someone to believe in them. And that, the faith, was the best thing a Grocery had to give, better than the trust and better than the forgiveness: the faith that that mother, with those two twins studying, was one day going to pay everything, and she was, and she paid, years later, when the children graduated, but that is already after the end of this book.

She fell asleep at dawn with an idea lodged crosswise, and the idea was simple and terrifying: that the only way to know who her friends were was to collect from them. That she had lived sixty-one years surrounded by people, beloved by the whole village, and that never, not once, had she known who cared for her and who cared for the shop, because she had never given them the occasion to choose. And that she was going now, with the shop closing and the knife of necessity at her back, to discover the deepest truth of her life, the one the father had died without knowing: who stays, when the Grocery, instead of giving, asks.
