## 4. The big house

They set out early, by the old road, the one the Estrada Nova had left behind. It was a way almost no one travelled any more, of dust and of brambles, climbing slowly toward the west, and Benvinda made it at her pace, without haste, with her shawl over her shoulders and her apron still on, because she had left the shop like one who leaves for an instant and the apron never came off her body again.

Aurora went behind, looking at the brambles with city eyes, and would have asked ten times where are we going if the aunt had not been ahead with that face of one who goes to a place that hurts. So she fell silent, and went counting the steps, which was what she did when she did not know what to feel.

Along the way, Benvinda had been telling her the village inside out, the village that Aurora, raised with her head in books, had never learned: that that fountain of the largo, dry for generations, had once been the only water of the land, and that the big house, up on the height, had its own spring and from it sent the water down or not down as it suited it, and that whoever ruled the water ruled everything; that the Estrada Nova, which now brought the wholesaler's lorry, had been the ruin and the salvation at the same time, because it had taken the customers to the city but had also broken, at last, the power of those of the big house, who lived on the village having no way out. Aurora listened, and went on joining to the portrait she carried of the land, that of a small and backward village, another, deeper, of a place where each stone had its story of who ruled and who obeyed, of who had and who was left owing, and where her family, hers, Aurora's, had a marked place, on the side of those who owed, of those who signed with a cross and lost their name.

The big house appeared after a bend, on the height, and Aurora stopped when she saw it, because she did not expect that: it was a ruin. It had been large, one could see: the master walls still standing, the worked stonework of the windows, an iron gate fallen sideways in the weeds, with a worn coat of arms that no longer said whose. But the roof had gone long ago, and inside grew a fig tree, of those that are born in dead houses, and the brambles had taken what had been the courtyard, and all of it had the deep silence of houses that were once powerful and no longer command respect of anyone except by the size of what fell.

"This was the big house," said Benvinda, standing before the fallen gate. "The people of the village called them only that: those of the big house. They had all the land on the west side, from the height to the river. Whoever worked, worked for them. Whoever wanted seed, asked them. Whoever needed money until the harvest, went to them. They were the bank of the village before there was a bank, my girl, and they were the bank as banks are when there is no other: they collected what they wanted, from whom they wanted, how they wanted."

Aurora looked at the ruin, and then at her aunt, and understood that that visit was not a stroll. It was a record. The aunt had brought her there to read her a page that was not in any ledger.

"Your great-grandfather," began Benvinda, and sat down on a stone of the wall, and Aurora sat beside her, "the father of my father, had land. Little, but he had it. And there was a bad year, a year of frost that burned everything, and he went to the big house, as everyone went, to ask for the means to sow the next year. And they lent. They always lent, that was their business: to lend to one who had nothing, so that one who had nothing was left owing, because one who is left owing is left bound, and the big house did not want the land of the poor, it wanted the poor bound to the land, working for them while pretending to work for themselves."

The sun rose, and the heat began to come from the stones. Benvinda spoke slowly, with her gaze on the ruin, like one who reads.

"The next year it rained in time, and the harvest came good, and my grandfather went to pay. He took the money all glad, for he was an upright man and had pride in paying on time. And when he got to the big house, they told him he owed more. That the interest was different. That he had understood wrong, that it was in the papers, and the papers were in their hand, and my grandfather did not know how to read. He had signed with a cross, my girl. A cross. And on top of his cross they wrote the number they wanted, and the number they wanted was more than the whole land was worth."

Aurora said nothing. The girl of the accounts, who read everything, was hearing what happens to one who does not know how to read the papers they sign, and there was in her, for the first time, something resembling fear, or shame, of one who understands suddenly that their clarity is a privilege that someone, behind them, paid dearly for.

"They took his land," said Benvinda. "All of it. And it was not enough, and he was still left owing, and the debt passed to my father, who was a boy. My father grew up owing the big house a debt he did not make, for a cross his father put on a paper he could not read. And he spent his childhood hearing those of the big house call his grandfather a swindler, a defaulter, people who ask and do not pay, when it had been the contrary, he had paid and they had robbed him on top of it. But the village believed them, because they were the big house, and it was the big house that said who was honourable and who was not. Whoever has the money has the right, my girl. It was the first thing my father learned, and the one that most marked him: that to be left owing is not only to lose the money. It is to lose the name. It is to let another write who you are, on top of your cross."

"And the name?" asked Aurora, low. "You said they ate his land and his name. The land I understand. The name how?"

"The name is eaten so, my girl." Benvinda picked up from the ground a broken roof-tile, of those that had been of that roof, and turned it in her hands. "When you are left owing to one who rules, the one who rules comes to say who you are. My grandfather was an upright man, and the big house, so as not to pay the shame of having robbed him, spread through the village that he was a defaulter, one who asked and did not pay. And the village believed it, because the village always believes one who has, against one who owes. And my grandfather died with that fame, that of defaulter, he who had taken the money on time and been robbed on top of it. His children, my father and the brothers, grew up being 'the sons of the one who did not pay'. You do not know what that is in a village, Aurora, you left early. In a village, the name is everything. There is no other wealth. And to let another write your name, on top of your cross, is the worst poverty there is, worse than that of the land, because the land recovers in a generation of work and the name takes three, or takes none."

She laid the broken tile on the stone of the wall, carefully, like one who lays down a bone.

"My father spent his life recovering the name. It was for that he gave so much. Each thing he gave was a stone in the wall of the new name: Abílio is a saint, Abílio gives the shirt off his back, Abílio never collected a penny from anyone. He built, by dint of giving, the fame contrary to that of his father. The father was 'the one who did not pay'; he wanted to be 'the one who never collected'. And he managed it, my girl, he managed it, he died loved, with the whole village at the funeral. But look at the price. To wipe out the grandfather's fame of defaulter, the son had to ruin himself by giving. We paid the shame of one generation with the hunger of two. And I, who neither made the debt nor suffered the shame, am still paying the bill, sixty years later, with the shop closing. This is the big house, Aurora. It is not that ruin. The ruin is the least of it. The big house is this that I carry inside me, this dread, this debt of blood that no one collects from me and that I pay every morning on opening the door without collecting from anyone."

She got up from the stone, and went to the fallen gate, and put her hand on the cold iron, on the rust, and Aurora saw in her back the same that the aunt had carried in her face since the day before.

"That is why my father never collected from anyone," said Benvinda, with her back turned. "You thought, with your accounts, that he was a slovenly man, or a saint, or a fool. He was none of that. My father had dread. He had dread of being the big house. Each time he collected from someone, he saw himself becoming the thing that had robbed his grandfather of his land and his name. And so he chose the contrary, he chose with all the strength of his life: to be the man who gives, who forgives, who strikes out, who never, never does to another what was done to him. And the village loved him for it. And he died poor and loved, and thought he had won, because he died loved and the big house died hated, look at it, fallen in the brambles, with no one to put a flower on it."

She turned to her niece, and her eyes were dry, but the voice came from deep.

"Only he did not leave to me the choice he made for himself. He left me the shop, yes. But he left me above all this: the fear. I do not collect, Aurora, and I spent my life thinking it was goodness, that it was my father's goodness going on in me. And yesterday, with your notebook, and today, here, I understood that it is no goodness at all. It is his fear. I have inside me, without ever having seen them, those of the big house, and I have such dread of becoming like them that I would rather sink than ask for what is mine. I would rather close the shop than resemble that ruin." She laughed, without joy. "I carry a fear that is not mine, for a debt I did not make, against people who have all died. Just like my grandfather, who paid a debt he did not owe. In this family, my girl, we always pay what we do not owe. It is our way. It is our cross on the paper."

They went down in silence, by the old road, with the sun already high. Halfway, Aurora, who was behind counting the steps, stopped counting them, and said, to her aunt, a thing that was not an account, and that cost her to say because she was not used to saying things that did not close:

"Aunt. One does not have to be the big house to collect what is theirs. There is a place in the middle. There must be."

And Benvinda, ahead, without turning, at her pace of always, answered what her mother might have answered, from the depth of the room where she watched over the accounts:

"There must be, daughter. But to that place in the middle no one took me. That place in the middle I am going to have to find alone, at sixty-one years old, with the shop closing in six weeks. And I have such a fear of seeking it that you cannot imagine, because the way to that place passes all through the inside of that house that lies fallen there, and I, each time I think of collecting, it is that ruin I see, and not my full shop."

Halfway down, Aurora stopped again, and this time it was not to say a thing to her aunt: it was to say a thing to herself, aloud, which was a thing she never did.

"I understand a thing now, aunt." Her voice was strange. "I fled this village at eighteen thinking I fled from its smallness. It was what I told everyone, in the city: that I had come from a small village, backward, and that I had fled to be someone. And I thought myself brave, the first in the village to study away, the one who broke out." She looked down, at the village below, at the dry fountain at the centre. "And after all, hearing you, I understand that I did not flee out of courage. I fled out of the same fear that binds you to the counter. You are afraid to collect and you stay; I was afraid to stay and I flee. But it is the same fear, is it not, aunt? The fear of stopping and looking at what one is. You set your fear to yield in interest you do not collect. I set mine to yield in kilometres and in diplomas. And neither of us got anywhere, because the place we had to get to was neither away nor at the counter: it was in here, and from that one does not flee, neither by staying nor by leaving."

Benvinda stopped too, and looked at her niece with a new tenderness, the tenderness of one who recognises their own disease in the body of one they love.

"Look at us, my girl," she said. "The old aunt who never left the shop and the niece who ran half the world, and the two of us come to the same place. It is in the family. I did not know that you had it too, the disease. I thought that you, with your studies and your accounts, had rid yourself of it. But there is no study that rids a person of what they bring from home. It only changes shape." She put her hand on her arm. "Perhaps it cures us both, this winter. Perhaps I learn to collect and you learn to stay. It would be beautiful, would it not? The two daughters of the same crooked house straightening up at the same time, each pulling the other along. Your grandmother, from where she is, would like it."

They reached the village at noon. The Grocery was shut, waiting, with the lamp out and the ledger under the counter. And Benvinda, on unbolting the door, made a gesture that Aurora noticed and kept: before going in, she looked at the largo, at the dry fountain, at all the houses that owed her the world, and it was a look that the niece did not know how to read, but that had inside it, mixed together, the love of a lifetime and the beginning, still small, still frightened, of a new thing: the will, lost somewhere between the father's fear and the ruin of the west, to be, once in her life, just. Neither saint nor thief. Just. And to discover whether the village, put to the test, went on wanting her.
