## 5. The bill

The wholesaler was called Adelino Sousa & Sons, and it was a printed name, not a person, and it was that, more than anything, that Benvinda never knew how to face.

With the village she knew how to speak. With the village it was all face, voice, story: the widow had a leg, Marçais had a dead father, old Aprígio had eleven years of loaves to pay for and a stubbornness that made one want to laugh. But the wholesaler was headed paper and typed letters, was a lorry that stopped at the door once a week and unloaded boxes, driven by a boy who barely spoke and was in a hurry, and was afterwards a bill that arrived by post with a number and a date and no face at all. There was no one there to whom to tell of the leg nor the dead father. There was only the number, and the date, and the cold phrase, and Benvinda, before that, felt like the grandfather before the papers in their hand: not knowing how to read the language in which the world collected from her.

Aurora tried to teach her their language. She sat her down one night at the table and wanted Benvinda to write, herself, a reply to the wholesaler, a letter of business people, proposing a payment plan. Benvinda took the paper and the pen, and stayed an hour before the blank sheet, and did not write a line. It would not come out. The words of the shop she knew them all; the words of the counter, the "come in, woman, it is cold," the "I am noting it," the "strike it out," those came to her without thinking. But the words from outside, those of to propose and to regularise and to settle, were of a language she did not speak, the language in which the grandfather had signed the cross, the language in which those above wrote over those below. To set herself to speak that language was, for Benvinda, to cross over to the side of the big house, and her hand refused, as the body refuses cold water.

"I cannot, my girl," she said, and laid down the pen. "It is not out of ill will, mind. It is that this hand is not mine. I know how to write 'strike it out'. I do not know how to write 'I hereby'. They are different languages, and the second I never learned, because in my house the second was the language of those who robbed us."

"Then do not write in that language, aunt," said Aurora, slowly, and it was among the wisest things she said in the whole winter. "Write in yours. I deal with their language, I speak to them on the telephone, I say 'I hereby' so well it comes out of me in my sleep. You do not have to speak the language from outside. You have to collect from the village, and to the village one speaks in the language of the village. Each one collects in the language they have. Yours is not worse. It is only from within."

The letter of the six weeks had stayed in the pocket of the apron, and from there it had not come out. Benvinda went about with it against her body the whole day, as Amparo had gone about with her envelope, and at night she put it on the bedside table and in the morning she put it back in the apron, and the letter went on creasing and softening from so much carrying, and the number, that, did not soften at all.

Aurora telephoned the wholesaler. She did it without asking leave, because that was how Aurora resolved things, face on, and because she had understood that the aunt would never telephone them, that the aunt would rather sink in silence than dial that number. She spoke with a gentleman of the accounts, a gentleman who called Aurora madam and was correct and was inflexible, the two things that city people manage to be at the same time and that in the village would seem contradictory. The gentleman of the accounts explained, with all politeness, that the house of Adelino Sousa & Sons had much regard for the Grocery, an old customer, but that the times were difficult, that there were many village groceries closing and leaving debts, and that the house could not go on supplying without receiving, that that was no way to do business, and madam will surely understand.

"I understand," said Aurora, who understood perfectly, because she spoke their language. "Six weeks, then."

"Six weeks to regularise the arrears," said the gentleman. "After that, with all pleasure, we resume the normal supply. It is nothing personal, madam. It is accounts."

Aurora heard it all with the telephone pressed to her ear and the aunt at her side, white, pretending to tidy the tinned goods but with both ears on the call. It was nothing personal. Aurora hung up and was left with the phrase, and went to say it to her aunt, and the aunt, on hearing it, made a strange face, almost of envy.

"Would that I could," said Benvinda. "Would that I could say that. It is nothing personal. What a rest, my girl, what a rest it must be to collect like that, from people one never saw, with no name, no face. The man of the accounts never knew the miller's widow, never heard her speak of her leg, never gave her the honey for her children's cough. To him the widow is a line. And a line is collected at no cost at all. I have no lines, Aurora. I have the whole widow, with the leg and the son who does not write and her face when she comes in of a morning with the cold. How do I tell a face that it owes me? The man of the accounts never found it hard to collect in his life, because he never collected from a person, he always collected from lines. The hard thing is not to collect, my girl. It is to collect from one you know. And I know them all. That is my misfortune and it was always that: I know them all."

"What luck theirs," said Benvinda. "To be able to say that. It is nothing personal. For me it is all personal, my girl. There is not in my shop a single line that is not personal. That is the difference between them and me, and it is for that they receive and I do not."

And it was so. That night, after closing, Benvinda did a thing she had never done: she sat at the back table, alone, with the two ledgers and the letter, and tried to see the Grocery with the eyes of the man of the accounts. She tried to look at the board ledger and see, instead of faces, numbers. She tried to read the name of the miller's widow and think of it as a line of debt, an asset, a thing to collect. She could not. Before the widow's name she saw the widow, the leg, the son who does not write, and the number dissolved into people. Aurora managed to do that, to turn people into numbers, and it was for that that Aurora suffered from another thing, that of turning everything into an account and not knowing how to rest. And Benvinda could not, and so she suffered from that one, that of not being able to collect even to save herself. Each one had her inability, Benvinda understood that night, and the two inabilities were sisters, and came from the same house: a family that had never known how to put money in its place, now giving it all, now counting it all, never looking at it face on, simply, as one looks at a tool.

The absurdity of the situation was all in an account Aurora made and laid on the counter, without comment, because she had already learned that with the aunt the comment spoiled it.

It was an account of three lines. On the first, what the village owed the Grocery. On the second, what the Grocery owed the wholesaler. On the third, the difference, which was in Benvinda's favour, wide, ample. On paper, Benvinda was a creditor. On paper, after everyone paid her and she paid everyone, money was left over, well over. The Grocery was not losing for being poor. It was losing for being uncollectable. It was a rich house dying of hunger with the larder full, because the owner did not know how to say to a single person, aloud, the number that person owed her.

There was, in those days, a sign that the world out there did not wait for her. The wholesaler's lorry, which stopped on Wednesdays, stopped at the right time, but the boy who drove it, who barely spoke, brought this time an extra paper, a notice by hand, and stayed waiting, leaning against the door, with a new haste, while Benvinda read. It was the confirmation of the deadline, in writing, with the phrase of always, and the boy, on receiving it back signed, said only, without ill will, with the indifference of one who carries many such messages to many shops:

"You are not the first. This year three have already closed, down the road. The house got tired of giving on trust to villages."

And he went, and the phrase stayed. *The house got tired of giving on trust to villages.* It was the world telling her, by the mouth of a hurried boy, that the time of the Groceries with open doors was ending not only in her village, but in all of them, that the Estrada Nova that had brought the lorry also took the customers to the city, and that a shop that gave on trust to whoever wanted, in a time when no one from outside gave on trust to anyone, was a flower out of season, beautiful and doomed. Benvinda was left with that hurting her in a way different from the letter: the letter was her debt, and this was her kind dying out, the way of the father and the grandfather dying with her if she did nothing.

Benvinda looked at the account of three lines a long time.

"Do you know what this is, my girl?" she said, at last. "This is the story of my mother. My father's larder was full, and we ate hungry, because what was in it all went to the front door and never stayed for the back table. The Grocery is my father's larder made larger. It is full, and I am going to close for lack of bread." She laid her finger on the third line, on the difference that was hers and that she could not touch. "I have this. This is mine. It is out there, in the village, scattered over sixty names, and it is mine, and I need only go and fetch it to pay the man from outside and the shop not close. And I would rather close. Look well at your aunt, Aurora, and learn, for you are of this house too: I would rather close the shop of my father and grandfather and great-grandfather than knock at the door of the miller's widow and say to her the number she owes me. I would rather lose everything than ask for what is mine. What disease is this, my girl? What breed of people are we?"

Aurora did not answer with an account. She was learning, slowly, with her aunt, that there are questions one answers by staying silent beside the one who asks them. She sat at the table, beside Benvinda, and for a moment the two women stayed looking at the account of three lines, the creditor and the accountant, the one who saw faces and the one who saw numbers, and neither of them managed, alone, to do the simple thing the account asked: to go and fetch what was theirs.

There was, in that conversation, a moment when Benvinda nearly gave up everything. She said to Aurora, with a small voice, that perhaps the simplest was indeed to close. To sell what was left, to pay the wholesaler with the sale, and be done. She was sixty-one, had no children, had no one to leave the shop to; perhaps it was time to rest, to close the door with dignity, as her friend Idalina said, and to go and live the years that remained to her without the weight of the shop on her back. She said it almost with relief, like one who finds a way out, and Aurora was silent a moment, and then said the thing that was needed, and that was not an account:

"You can close, aunt. It is your right, and no one takes it from you. But do not close for not being able to collect. Close, if you wish, after learning to collect, for then you close by choice, and it is different. If you close now, you are going to close fleeing, as your father gave fleeing from his fear. And you are going to take the fear with you into retirement, aunt, for the fear does not stay in the shop, the fear goes with the person wherever the person goes. You can retire from the Grocery. You cannot retire from yourself. That work, that of learning to ask for what is yours, you have to do, close the shop or not. Better to do it while you have the shop to practise on."

Benvinda looked at her niece, and understood that the girl of the accounts had, beneath the numbers, a wisdom she did not expect, and that that wisdom came, perhaps, from exactly the same place from which her disease came: from one who spent her life fleeing, and knows, better than anyone, that from people one does not flee. She did not speak again of closing. Not because she had ceased to be afraid, but because the niece had taken from her the easy way out, and sometimes that is all that is lacking to a person to do the difficult thing: that someone block, with love, their way of escape.

"We are missing a third person," said Aurora, low, at the end of a long time. "One who is neither you nor I. You see only the faces and forgive everything. I see only the numbers and would collect everything, and would collect badly, would collect like the man of the accounts, without looking at who. Neither of the two will do, aunt. For this one would need one who saw both things at the same time. The face and the number. The widow's leg and what Marçais has in the vineyard."

Benvinda looked at her niece. And she smiled, a tired smile, but the first true one since the letter had come.

"That third person, my girl," she said, "there is nowhere to go and fetch her. That person, if she exists, I am going to have to be the one to invent her, here, in these six weeks, with this pencil that only knows how to strike out. I am going to have to learn, at sixty-one, to be a thing I never saw anyone be in this village: neither my father giving everything, nor the big house taking everything. One who weighs fair." She took the pencil from behind her ear, and looked at it as at a stranger. "And I am beginning to fear, Aurora, that it hurts more to learn to weigh fair than it always hurt to give too much. Because to give too much, at least, made me beloved. And to weigh fair, in this land, is going to make me what?"

The question stayed in the air of the shop, above the two ledgers and the creased letter, and neither of them had an answer, because the answer was in no account: it was out there, in the village, in the sixty doors at which Benvinda was going to have to knock, and only by knocking at each one would it be known, one by one, who stayed and who fled when the kindest woman of the largo ceased, at last, to be too kind.
