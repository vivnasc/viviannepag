## 12. The open door

In the summer, the Grocery opened before the bell, as it had always opened, because the hunger of the fields went on not waiting for the hours, and that had not changed nor would it change.

What had changed was within, and was seen in small things. The scale, for example, no longer leaned half a finger to the customer's side: it weighed fair, true, and Benvinda gave to whom she wanted to give on purpose, with her hand and with the name, and not in secret, on the scale, with shame. The ledger went on under the counter, but now it had accounts that were collected and accounts that were given, and Benvinda knew how to distinguish one from the other, and the distinction was the soul of the new business: she gave on trust to one who needed, collected from one who could, forgave one whom she chose, and in none of the three things was she afraid. The village, after the fright of the closing, had stayed — the village that stayed. They were fewer than the hundred of the father's time, but they were of another mettle, they were the ones who paid and stayed friends, and the Grocery, with them, was enough to live on, was enough with ease, was enough without watching the accounts at night in the back room.

Aurora did not sell her father's house. She stayed, for now, and found work in the village with the numbers, for in a village too there is need of one who knows how to count, and she went on learning, slowly, beside her aunt, at the counter, the third thing she lacked: to stay. She was not cured, for such cures take more than a winter, but she had stopped running, and to stop was already halfway. Donato, the father, telephoned from the City marvelling that the daughter did not sell, and Aurora told him that she was not yet ready, and the father, who did not understand, said she should do as she saw fit, and Aurora hung up and stayed a while looking at her aunt's shop, and understood that it was the first time in her life she had put off a deal for a reason that did not close in any account, and that that, instead of distressing her, gave her a kind of new rest, the rest of one who, for the first time, is in a place without being already about to leave for the next.

And on a summer morning, Benvinda did the last thing she had left to do, and did it alone, early, before opening.

She took the board ledger, the old one, the one of the time of the father and the grandfather, with the corners rounded by three generations, and leafed through it one last time, from beginning to end. It was full. From the first page to the last, full: the whole village over a hundred years, the dead and the living, those who paid and those who fled, the father's hand and hers and the Amadeu of the Olive-Press House's and the Salvo's owing the same things in the same place, circles and circles of a village always owing the same. It was a full ledger, and it was a closed ledger: it was the history of a fear of three generations, and the fear, at the Grocery, had ended that winter, and Benvinda wanted the ledger of the fear to stay where the things that end and deserve to be kept stay.

Before taking it, she opened the new notebook, the one she had begun that winter, and looked at the first page, which she had written in her own hand, on the day she had decided to be just. It was not a page of debts: it was a page of rules, the rules of the new Grocery, that she had written for herself like one who writes an oath, so as not to forget in the relapses. *One gives on trust to one who needs. One collects from one who can and owes. One gives to one whom one chooses, aloud and on purpose, never in secret on the scale. The scale weighs fair. One who flees because they are collected from what they owe was no friend, was a customer. One is not ashamed to receive what is ours, nor vain to give what we do not lack. One is not the big house. One is not the father. One is just.* She had read them over many times, in those weeks, above all on the bad days, and they had held her as the rope holds one who learns to swim. Now she scarcely needed to read them over. She already knew them in her body, which is where the true rules live when they cease to be rules and become a way of being.

She took the old ledger to the House of the Register.

The Keeper was at the long table at the back, and received her without astonishment, as she received everything, and Benvinda laid the board ledger on the wood, beside the great books of the village.

"I bring you this one," she said. "It is neither record nor paper of law. It is the ledger of the debts of the Grocery, from the time of my father and grandfather. It is full, and it is closed, for I have opened a new one. And I do not want to throw it away, because the whole village of a hundred years is here, and I do not want to keep it at home, because at home it was my fear, and I no longer want to live with my fear in the drawer. I thought its place was here, with the things that stay. You are the one who knows whether it may be."

The Keeper drew the ledger to her, and leafed through it, slowly, and stopped at a middle page, and Benvinda saw her stop exactly at the page where the Amadeu's hand and the Salvo's were, as though the Keeper knew, without seeking, where each name slept.

"It may be," said the Keeper. "These ledgers are the ones most needed, and the ones no one keeps. The register says who was born and who died. But who owed whom, and what it cost one who forgave, and the fear that passed from father to daughter in a shop with open doors, that is in no record, and that is the village as true as the other." She laid her open hand on the board ledger, and looked at Benvinda. "I was told that you collected, this winter. The village spoke of it. Some spoke ill, others well."

"And you, what do you say?"

The Keeper almost smiled, with that thing in her face that, in another face, would be a smile.

"I do not say. I keep." And she laid the board ledger on the shelf, beside the great books, closed, in order, the history of a fear at last put in its place. "But I am going to tell you a thing, since you ask me, which is outside my trade and so I tell you only once. Your father brought me, many years ago, before dying, a thing like this. Not a ledger. A conversation. He came here one day, and sat where you are, and told me of the big house, and of the grandfather's cross on the paper, and told me that he was afraid to die and leave the daughter his fear. He was afraid to leave you the fear, Benvinda. He knew that he was leaving it you, and he was afraid. And I kept that, as I keep everything, and never told you of it, because it was not the time. Now it is. Your father died knowing that he left you the fear, and wishing that you would be braver than he. You were. You took your time, but you were. He would like to know it. Perhaps, from where he is, he knows."

Benvinda was left speechless. Sixty-one years thinking the father was the man without fear, the saint of the counter, and after all the father knew. He knew of the fear, called it by its name, and had died wishing that the daughter would conquer it. The beautiful phrase, *a shop that collects is a shop that loses friends*, had not been a wisdom he left her as an inheritance: it had been a confession he had not known how to make, a fear dressed as a law, and beneath the law, hidden, the wish that the daughter would not obey him. The father had not left her the phrase for her to fulfil. He had left it her for her to break, and had died without knowing how to say that, as so many people die, leaving the children the orders they would like them to have the courage to disobey.

Before going out, the Keeper said to her still a thing, apropos of the new notebook Benvinda had begun.

"And the new one, that you keep. The living ledgers stay with the one who writes them, for they still have a village to note down. Only bring me that one when it is full, and full in another way than this one. For this one, you see, is full of fear. What you write in the new one, I hope, will fill with another thing."

"With what?" asked Benvinda.

"With justice," said the Keeper, and went back to her register. "It is rarer than goodness, justice. Goodness anyone has who is afraid of displeasing. Justice takes courage. Go along, Benvinda. You have a shop to open."

She came out of the House of the Register into the sun of the largo with her hands empty and light, without the ledger of the fear, and crossed the largo at her pace, and stopped, as she stopped now every day, by the dry fountain, and laid her hand on its warm rim, and went on.

She opened the Grocery. She lit the lamp behind the glass, though it was already day, because it was the gesture, and she put Prudência's cloth on the counter, the whole and beautiful cloth, in sight of whoever came in. And the first to come in was the miller's widow, as on every morning, and she took the beans and the bacon and a candle, and went to look for the coins in the knotted handkerchief. And Benvinda, this time, let her look, and received the coins, and gave the right change, and to the widow, who needed it, she forgave all the same the part she knew she did not have, but she forgave it aloud, on purpose, saying the rest is on me, woman, for today I felt like it, and not in silence, on the scale, with shame. The widow thanked her and went, and the morning went on, the village coming in, some to pay, some to get on trust, the Grocery breathing its breath of always, that of a hundred years, but with the air cleaner, the air of a house where the money, at last, came in and went out by both doors, with no fear at one nor shame at the other.

Prudência came, at the end of the morning, as she came now often, and brought a dress. Not a hem: a whole dress, cut and sewn by her, from beginning to end, for Benvinda, for the shop, a work dress but beautiful, with a cut the seamstress had designed in her head without copying from anyone. It was the boldest thing Prudência had done in her life, and she did it for her sister, and Benvinda understood, on putting it on, that the sister had also crossed her crossing that winter, in parallel, on the other side of the village: that on seeing the elder learn to collect, the younger had at last learned to cut the whole dress, to risk its coming out too beautiful, to take up her size as a seamstress. The two daughters of the same fear had straightened up at the same time, each pulling the other along, as Benvinda had said at the fallen big house. And there was still Aurora, who had not sold her father's house and was staying on, and who began, slowly, to pass nights without counting anything, to look at the largo from the window without budgeting it. Three women of the same crooked house, straightening up each in her own way, in the same winter, on account of a wholesaler's letter and a niece who did not know how to be without doing accounts.

At the end of that morning, there came into the shop a young woman, from the far reaches of the serra, whom Benvinda did not know, with a child in her arms and the face of need, that old need that Benvinda knew from far off because it was that of the family of the hunger of the father's time. The woman asked for the cheapest, of the cheapest, and on paying she fell short, and made as if to give back half of what she took, with the shame of one who is used to giving back. And Benvinda, at that moment, did the whole thing of the book in a single gesture, without thinking, because she no longer needed to think: she did not give anything back to the bag, she noted in the new notebook what was lacking, in her hand, without ceremony, and said to the woman "take it, take it all, and the rest stays noted, pay when you can, and if you cannot it pays itself with time, for that is what a Grocery is for." And the woman went, with the child and the full bag, and Benvinda stayed watching her leave, and understood that she had just done, with that stranger, exactly what the father did: to give on trust to one who needs, without collecting, out of goodness. Only now it was different, and the difference was everything: the father gave on trust to all, out of fear of collecting from anyone; she gave on trust to that one, for having seen the face of need and having chosen. The gesture was the same. The soul of the gesture was the contrary. And it was in that, Benvinda understood, that the cure lay: not in ceasing to give, for to give was the soul of the house, but in giving by choice and not out of fear, in knowing how to distinguish the serra woman with hunger from the Marçais with a vineyard, and in having, for each one, the just answer — trust to one, account to the other — without ever again confusing the two things.

And in the middle of the morning, with the shop full, Benvinda took up the pencil, from behind her ear, to note another account. And she noticed, on doing it, that her hand no longer trembled, and that the pencil, which for sixty-one years had only known how to strike out, had learned, that winter, to do the other thing, the thing that was lacking, the hardest of all: to write a number and to say it aloud and to let it stand, in the light of day, at the counter of the father's house, without the sky, which she had sworn her whole life would fall, falling. It did not fall. It had never fallen. And the Grocery, which opened before the bell, was going to go on opening, now with the scale weighing fair and the door open and the owner, at last, knowing the difference between giving and being afraid, between being good and being trodden on, between the father's phrase and the truth the father had died wishing for her and had never known how to say.
