## From the register of Véspera

On the middle shelf, beside the great books of the village, there is kept now a board ledger with rounded corners, closed, with the hand of three generations of grocers. It is neither record nor paper of law. It is the ledger of the debts of the Grocery, and in it is written a whole village of a hundred years: who owed, who paid, who fled, and what it cost, one who forgave, to be afraid to collect.

The Keeper of this time, who received it from the hand of Benvinda, daughter of Abílio, says that it came here in the summer that the Grocery changed weight. There was a winter, in the village, when a good woman learned, late, to be just, and discovered that they were not the same thing, and that the second cost her more than all the goodness of her life, and made her, in the end, more whole.

The register says who was born and who died. It does not say who owes whom, nor what it costs one who pays, nor what is silenced by one who forgives, nor the fear that passes from father to daughter in a shop with open doors. For that there are other ledgers. This is one of them, and of all of it a record was made.
