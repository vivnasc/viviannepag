## 8. The night of the shop

The wholesaler cut off in the fifth week, before the deadline, because those from outside also break their own word when it suits them, and the lorry that stopped at the door on Wednesdays did not stop.

Benvinda waited for it the whole morning, with the shelves counting the days: the sugar running out, the rice near its end, the salt cod that was what most sold reduced to three sad pieces on the display. At noon she understood it was not coming, and in the afternoon Aurora telephoned, and the gentleman of the accounts, ever correct, ever inflexible, regretted very much but the situation had become unsustainable and the house had decided to suspend at once, and madam understands. Madam understood. Madam hung up the telephone and went to look at the shelves emptying, and understood, for the first time with her body and not only with her head, that the Grocery was truly going to close, that it was not a threat of paper, that it was rice running short and salt cod dwindling and a village arriving tomorrow morning, before the bell, at a shop that no longer had what to give.

And it was then that the village showed what it was, and showed it in every way at the same time, because a village is not a single thing.

It ran quickly, as everything runs in Véspera, that the Grocery was closing. And on its being known, three things happened at the same time, in the same days, and Benvinda saw them all from the counter, like one who witnesses, at last, the truth she had asked for without knowing she was asking it.

The shop emptying was a thing Benvinda lived with her body, day by day, and that hurt her more than Aurora's number. Because an empty shelf, in a Grocery, is not only a lack of goods: it is the house dying in sight. First it was the corner of the salt cod, then the shelf of the tinned goods, then the sack of rice that she scraped down to the bottom. And the village saw. The village came in and saw the shelves thinning, and each customer who came in and saw understood, without anyone saying it, that the Grocery was ending, and carried the news outside, and the news grew from mouth to mouth as the news of death grows: Benvinda is going to close, the Grocery is giving its last. And it was that news, more than any letter, that set the village moving, because one thing is a shop that gives on trust, eternal, always there; another is a shop dying, and before an announced death people do what they do before all deaths: some draw near, others draw away, and one discovers who is who.

There were those who appeared to pay. They came suddenly, with the shame stamped on their faces, taking from their pockets what they owed or part of what they owed, without anyone asking them, now that the shop was going to close. The mother of the twins came, who paid half the arrears at once, without ceremony this time, with her eyes low; a farmer from the lower fields came, who had owed for years and put the notes on the counter and said only sorry to have been so long, Benvinda, and went quickly, ashamed; people came whom Benvinda did not even know had shame, and who after all had it, and who had only needed to know that the shop was truly going to end to remember that what they took was not free, it was a woman's, and that that woman could sink. Those moved Benvinda in a way she did not expect, because they were the proof that the village was not bad, it was only distracted, and that the distraction of a whole village can sink a person as well as malice, but that sometimes a fright is enough for the distraction to wake.

There were those who vanished. Those hurt the most, because they were the ones of always, the ones of the welcoming, those who kissed Benvinda in the largo and asked after her health with a family voice. Marçais, he, disappeared. He did not come to pay nor came to show his face. It was known that he went about saying through the village that it was a pity, that the Grocery was no longer what it had been, that Benvinda had let herself go, that these village businesses are doomed, and that he, poor man, with the frost at the door, well needed his money for the vineyard. The man who had owed for eleven years, the one who had drunk wine with her toasting the father's memory, made of his debt a defect of the Grocery, and of his flight a fatality of the times, and never, not once, set foot in the shop to show his face. That one taught Benvinda, in a few days, more about who he was than eleven years of celebrations at the counter.

And there were the third ones, who were the most curious: those who came to give advice. They appeared, not to pay, not to flee, but to advise, and the advice was all polite ways of not paying. They told Benvinda that what she ought to do was take a loan from the bank in the City; that what she ought to do was sell the shop to one who knew how to modernise it; that what she ought to do was speak to the gentleman doctor at the town hall, that this matter of groceries perhaps had some support; they gave her a whole world of solutions, all far off, all with third parties, all in which no one there present had to put a hand in their pocket. They were the hardest to bear, because they came dressed as friendship and as concern, and Benvinda, who now knew the difference, saw beneath the concern the same thing as Marçais: the wish that the problem might be resolved by any path other than that of their paying what they owed.

The worst of the advisers was Benvinda's own friend, Idalina, a friend of forty years, of those of a kiss in the largo and a visit on Sundays. She came to the shop with a face of condolence, sat down, took Benvinda's hands as one takes the hands of a sick woman, and told her, with all the sweetness in the world, that what she ought to do was retire, poor thing, that she had already worked too much, that she should close the shop with dignity while there was time and go and rest, that the village would understand. And at no moment of the half hour of sweetness did Idalina, who owed the Grocery a reasonable account, of years, offer to pay a penny of what she owed. Her sweetness was all to push Benvinda toward retirement, toward the dignified closing, toward everything except the one thing that would save the shop, which was Idalina and all the Idalinas paying what they owed. And Benvinda, who before would have swallowed the whole sweetness and even thanked her for it, heard now, beneath it, the sound her sister had taught her to hear: the sound of one who would rather see her friend shut the door than have to open her purse. She took her leave of Idalina with the courtesy of always, but stayed, after she had gone, a long time looking at the empty chair, thinking that forty years of friendship were coming apart in her hands, and that perhaps they had never quite been what she had thought, and that that, hurt though it did, was a truth she had been buying on instalments for forty years, without knowing it, at the price of never collecting.

At the end of those days, one night, Benvinda sent Aurora upstairs, shut the shop, and stayed alone with the ledger.

She lit only the lamp of the counter, the father's, and opened the board ledger at the first page and leafed through it all, slowly, from beginning to end, page by page, name by name. It was the history of the village and it was her history. There were the dead and the living, those who had fled and those who had come to pay, those who needed and those who abused, all mixed in the same hand over the years, the father's hand at the beginning, hers afterwards, and even, she saw on leafing through, on a middle page, the hand of an Amadeu of the Olive-Press House, dead long ago, and beneath it, years later, on the same page, the hand of a Salvo, who was Amparo's son, owing the same things in the same place as the dead uncle, as though the whole village went in circles always owing the same, generation after generation, and the Grocery were the book where those circles stayed written.

And Benvinda understood, that night, by the light of the lamp, a thing about the ledger that changed everything: the ledger was not a record of debts. It was a record of fears. Hers, above all. Each line unstruck was a time she had been afraid to collect; each strike was a time she had been even more afraid, and had forgiven so as not to have to ask. The whole ledger, from the first page to the last, was the map of the fear of a family written over three generations, since a cross that a man put on a paper he could not read, up there, in the fallen big house.

And leafing so through the ledger, by the light of the lamp, Benvinda stopped at an old page, from the father's time, and her heart tightened: it was the account of a family she knew, people who had truly gone hungry one winter, and the father had given them everything on trust, the whole winter, and had never collected, and had done well, and Benvinda, on seeing that page, understood that there were in the father's ledger pages that were glory and pages that were disease, and that they were all mixed together, in the same hand, without distinction. The family of the hunger was glory: there, the not-collecting had been love, had been the Grocery doing what a Grocery should do in a village, holding up its own when the land fails. Marçais was disease: there, the not-collecting had been fear, had been letting himself be abused. And the father, and she after him, had treated the two pages in the same way, struck both out with the same pencil, without ever separating the glory from the disease, because to separate them required a thing the house lacked: discernment, the look that distinguishes one who needs from one who abuses, and that collects from one and gives to the other, and does not treat the two as one.

It was that, Benvinda understood that night, the true inheritance the father had left her, and the hardest to undo: not the not-collecting, but the not-distinguishing. The father had put in the same sack the family of the hunger and the Marçais of the vineyard, had called both things goodness, and had taught his daughter to treat them alike. And the cure was not to collect from all, for that would be to turn into the big house; it was to learn to look at each page face on and ask: is this glory or is it disease? Is this giving to one who needs, or is it fear of one who abuses? And to answer, page by page, with the pencil in hand, without the dread deciding for her.

She took up the pencil, from behind her ear.

And she did, that night, alone, by the light of the father's lamp, the thing she had never done in sixty-one years: she did not strike out. She took a clean sheet, of the wrapping ones, and in her small and straight hand, slowly, with her hand trembling and stopping and starting again, she wrote a letter. Only one, to try, for a single person, the one who best could and longest had owed: Marçais. *Marçais*, she wrote, and her hand trembled, *I ask you to come and pay me what you owe me, which is noted down and which I will tell you exactly if you wish to see. I do not ask you out of pity for myself. I ask you because it is mine, and because a shop that gives its whole life also has the right to receive, and because if I close the Grocery, I close it because of those who do not pay, and you are the one who owes most and has most. It is nothing personal, Marçais.* And she stopped, with the pencil in the air, on writing that last phrase, because she recognised it: it was the wholesaler's phrase, the phrase from outside, the cold phrase she had never known how to say. *It is nothing personal.* And she understood, with the lamp flickering, that she had lied in writing it, because to her it was all personal, it always had been. And so she struck out that last phrase, struck it out hard, and wrote over it, in her truth, the only one she knew how to say: *It is very personal, Marçais. It is the most personal thing I have ever said to you. That is why it costs me so much. Come and pay me, please, for I want to go on caring for you, and if you do not come I no longer know whether I can.*

She read over what she had written, by the light of the lamp, and understood that the letter said, beneath the words, a thing she had never said to anyone: that to collect cost her because she was afraid of ceasing to be loved. It was all there, in the phrase she had not managed to write in the cold language of the wholesaler: *I want to go on caring for you, and if you do not come I no longer know whether I can.* It was not a letter of collection. It was a declaration of love with a number inside it. And perhaps, thought Benvinda, that was the only way she had of collecting, and perhaps it was no worse than the other: to collect telling the whole truth, that money and affection had gone mixed at the Grocery from the beginning, and that to ask for the money back was also to ask that the affection be true, that it bear the test of a number. Those from outside collected by separating the money from the affection, *it is nothing personal*. She only knew how to collect by joining them, *it is all personal*. And perhaps, who knows, her way, however much it cost, was the more honest of the two: the one that did not pretend that money between people who know one another is a cold thing.

She folded the letter. She laid down the pencil. And she stayed a long time sitting at the counter, with the father's lamp going out slowly, with the first letter of collection of her life folded before her, and it was not a victory, and she knew it was not, because a letter is nothing, a letter is only paper, and the hard thing was not to write, the hard thing was to look Marçais in the eyes and say that to him with her mouth. But it was a beginning. It was the hand learning, by the light of the lamp, a gesture that was not to strike out. And out there, in the sleeping village, slept those who had paid and those who had fled and those who had come to advise, all mixed together, all yet to be separated, and Benvinda knew now that she was going to have to separate them one by one, with her voice and not with the letter, and that the letter was only the practice, the scale one plays before the music, the first time a woman of sixty-one takes up the father's pencil not to forgive a debt, but to say, at last, in the letters of her own hand: this is mine, and I want it back.
