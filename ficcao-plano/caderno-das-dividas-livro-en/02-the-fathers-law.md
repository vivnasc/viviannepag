## 2. The father's law

The Grocery of Abílio's time was the fullest house in the village, and the poorest, and Benvinda took fifty years to understand that the two things were the same thing.

Full, because the door never closed. Abílio, the father, had for that door a pride that was almost a fault: he boasted that the Grocery opened before the bell and closed after the lamp of the largo, and that in forty years he had never sent anyone away empty-handed. Whoever came without money took all the same; whoever came in a hurry was served slowly, because haste, said Abílio, is a thing of the city, and the Grocery was the contrary of the city. The men stopped there on the way to the fields, the women on the way to the fountain, the old ones on the way to nowhere, and Abílio had for each one an opened tin to taste, a counsel, a line in the ledger. The shop was the heart of the largo, and the heart, as is known, gives blood to all and keeps nothing for itself.

Poor, for that very reason.

Benvinda remembered the house within, behind the shop, and the house within told another story than the shop without. She remembered the mother turning the same skirt three winters, putting water in the soup when a visit came, hiding from the husband that this and that were lacking so that he need not know that his generosity at the counter was paid in the back room with what was not there. The mother did not scold the father in front of anyone: that was not done. But at night, lying down, with the little Benvinda on the mattress beside her pretending to sleep, the mother told low to the dark what the day had taken. Today he gave the whole ham to the ones from the Cistern, who came from the serra with the face of hunger, and at supper we ate what. Today he gave on trust to the Marçais for the third time, and the Marçais have a vineyard and have cattle, and we have not for the rent. The mother's voice in the dark was not of anger: it was of accounting, a murmured account that never closed, and the little one fell asleep to the sound of it as other children fall asleep to the sound of the rain.

The father, he slept at ease. And it was that, Benvinda understood much later, what had most marked her: that his generosity gave him sleep, and her account took the mother's sleep away. One gave, and slept. The other paid, and watched. At the Grocery, as in almost every house, there was one who made the beautiful gesture and one who was left with the bill, and the two places were divided, and the little one learned, without anyone teaching it to her in words, which was the place that was praised and which was the one that was silenced.

There was, in the way the village spoke of the father and the mother, an injustice that the little one felt without knowing how to name it, and that only as a grown woman she managed to put into words. The mother no one called anything. She was "Abílio's wife", and she mended, and she saved, and she watched over the accounts, and she died without the village putting an epithet on her, because the village has no beautiful names for one who pays, only for one who gives. The father they called the saint, the heart of the largo, the man who gave the shirt off his back. And the two of them made the same shop work, his giving and her enduring, but only one was left with the glory, and it was the one who had the easy part, that of opening the hand; the one who had the difficult part, that of closing the account in the dark so that the other's hand could open, that one was left with nothing, not even a name. The little one grew up seeing this without seeing it, and learned, without meaning to, that the world pays the glory to one who gives and the silence to one who sustains, and that if a woman wished to be loved in the village, it was better to open the hand like the father than to watch over the account like the mother.

The father's phrase she learned at the counter, on a day that stayed.

She would have been seven or eight, and was sitting on the inner step, playing with the buttons of the box, when in came a man from the lower fields, of those who paid badly, and took half a sack of flour. On going out, the man made a vague gesture toward the ledger, the gesture of one who says note it there, and Abílio noted it, and the man went without even thanking, for much giving on trust wears out the thanks as the water wears out the stone. And there was at the counter, at that time, a cousin of Abílio's, a man who had gone to the city and came back on a visit, in a suit and a watch, and who witnessed that and said, with the frankness of those who no longer live in the land:

"You, Abílio, if you collected half of what they owe you, you would be rich."

And Abílio, putting away the flour, without turning, said the phrase that was to govern three generations:

"A shop that collects is a shop that loses friends. I would rather have the shop full and the purse empty than the purse full and the shop empty. Money is kept in the bank and gives no warmth at all. It is friends who keep a man when he falls."

The cousin laughed, from the height of his suit, and said that those were village ideas, and went off to the city and never came back, and Benvinda, from the step, with the buttons in her hand, was left with the father's phrase whole, as one is left with the first phrases, cold, without understanding, but for ever. A shop that collects is a shop that loses friends. The father had said it with so much certainty, with so much peace, that the little one understood she was before one of those truths that are not argued, like the bell or the seasons. And she understood more, she understood the essential, which was this: that to be loved, a person gave; and that to be left alone, it was enough to collect. That love and the account were enemies, and that one had to choose, and that the father had chosen, and that the father's choice was the good one, because the father was the most beloved man of the largo.

That the mother watched at night paying for that choice was another thing, a thing that stayed on the inside, in the back room, with the turned skirt and the water in the soup. The accounts of the women of the Grocery were always done in the dark, and so was life, and Benvinda grew up thinking that so was life.

The mother, who in Benvinda's memory had almost no name, was only "the mother", taught her the account in secret, and taught it to her like one who passes on a vice, with shame and with need. On Sunday afternoons, when the father went to the churchyard to play quoits with the men, the mother sat the little one at the kitchen table and opened a little notebook that was not the shop's, a notebook of her own, with an oilcloth cover, where she noted what was lacking at home and what the father had given during the week, to know, she said, how far they could go. Not to check the father, for the father one did not check, but to know beforehand when she would have to put water in the soup and when she would have to turn the skirt, so that his generosity would not catch them off guard. It was an accounting of fear, and the little one learned it pretending to play, and learned above all the lesson underneath, the one that was not written in any notebook: that in a house there is always someone doing the account the other does not do, and that this someone, in their house, was always the woman, and was always in the dark, and was always silent.

"Do not tell your father that I show you this," the mother asked, every Sunday, and the little one promised, and it was the secret of the two of them, and the secret united them in a sad way, because it was a secret against the joy of the man both of them loved. "He is good, daughter. He is too good, and the too-good is a cross for one who lives with him. You, when you are grown, marry a man who knows how to say no. Better one no on time than a thousand yeses out of time. But do not say this to anyone either, for it is ugly for a woman to speak ill of her husband's goodness, and I do not speak ill, I only do the account."

And the little one kept the two things, the father's goodness and the mother's account, and grew up thinking they were two separate worlds, that of the bright shop and that of the dark kitchen, without understanding, until very late, that it was a single world, and that the light of one was paid for with the darkness of the other, and that she, Benvinda, was going to inherit both and try to live them at the same time, alone, with no mother to watch over the account nor father to sleep at ease.

There was one afternoon, only one, when she saw the thing whole, and that afternoon was the one that came back now, sixty years later, with Aurora's notebook on the counter.

It was winter, and there had come to the Grocery a family from the far reaches of the serra, people no one knew, with children whose feet were wrapped in rags. They asked by the day, for the cheapest, and Abílio, who could not say no even to those he knew, much less to those who came like that, not only gave them what they asked but sent the little Benvinda to fetch from the larder of the house, from their larder, the cheese the mother kept for Christmas. The little one went, because the father was not to be disobeyed, and brought the cheese, and the father gave it to those people with a broad gesture, beautiful, the gesture of a king, and the family went off weeping with gratitude, and Abílio stayed at the counter with his eyes wet from his own goodness, and said to his daughter: did you see? It is for this that a shop is good for.

And the little one saw, yes. But she saw also, at night, the mother's face on opening the larder and noticing the lack of the Christmas cheese. The mother said nothing. She stayed a moment with her hand on the empty shelf, the same gesture that Benvinda, sixty years later, was to recognise in herself without knowing where she had learned it, and then she closed the larder and went to set the table, and that Christmas they ate without cheese, and the father told the story of the family from the serra to all the relatives, at the table, like one who tells of a feat, and all thought Abílio a saint, and the mother smiled and served and did not say that the saint had worked the miracle with the bread that was lacking at home.

That was the inheritance, Benvinda understood now, lying in the same back room where the mother had watched, at sixty-one years old and with the niece asleep in the room next door and the large number waiting downstairs in the clean notebook. She had not inherited the shop. The shop was only the stage. She had inherited the two places and had been left with both: she was the father at the counter, giving with the broad gesture, and she was the mother in the room, watching over the account no one saw. She gave like the father and paid like the mother, and so she was always tired in a way that had no name, the generosity giving her face to the largo and the bill eating her sleep, the two things in the same body, with no mother to weep the account low nor father to sleep at ease over it.

The shop passed to Benvinda in the winter that Abílio died, and the passing was done without papers or testament, as the true things of a village are done: simply, on the morning after the funeral, Benvinda went down, opened the door before the bell, lit the lamp, and the village understood that the Grocery went on, that it had changed hands without changing soul. The brothers did not dispute it. Donato already had his head in the city, and Prudência had her sewing, and to Benvinda, who was the eldest and the one who had stayed single caring for the old father, the shop fell as the houses of the fathers fall to single women: by omission, by being there, by having no other life to dispute it.

Abílio's funeral was the largest the village remembered, and Benvinda, on receiving it, on seeing the churchyard full and the largo full and the line of condolences that did not end, felt, in the midst of the mourning, a pride that shamed her: the father had died loved as he wished, the whole village had come, and the proof of his life was there, in that flood of people. Only much later, with Aurora's notebook on the counter, was Benvinda to put the other half of the account beside that one: that of all those people who had filled the churchyard, half owed to the Grocery, and that none, none, in the week after the funeral, had come to pay what they owed to the dead man, and that many, those, went on getting on trust from the daughter as they had got on trust from the father, as though Abílio's death were only one more page to turn in the ledger of their perpetual debts. The village's love for the father was true. And it did not pay the accounts. The two things lived together, and Abílio had died seeing only the first, and Benvinda was going to have to learn, alive, to see both at the same time.

In the first week of the shop being hers, Benvinda found the father's ledgers, the old ones, kept in a drawer, and leafed through them, and had for an instant the impulse Aurora would have sixty years later: that of adding up, of seeing exactly how much the village owed. She did not add up. She was afraid of the number, and closed the drawer, and said to herself that to add up was a thing of the big house, and that she was the father's daughter, and that she was going to go on with the shop as he had left it. And she went on with it, forty years, without ever adding up. The number Aurora put on the counter was the number she had been afraid to see in the first week, and that had grown in the dark of that drawer for forty years, waiting for a niece from outside who was not afraid to look at it.

She got up in the middle of the night, as the mother used to get up, and went down to the shop in the dark, without lighting, for she knew each shelf by its smell. She went to the counter and put her hand on the black oilcloth of the ledger, in the dark, and stayed there a long time, the woman and the ledger, as the mother had stayed with her hand on the empty larder. And she understood, that night, a thing that did not let her fall asleep again: that the father's phrase, the beautiful phrase, the phrase she had repeated her whole life as one repeats a prayer, was not a truth of the world. It was the fear of a man, dressed as virtue. And that beneath it there was another thing, older, that the father had never told and that she was going to have to go and seek, because a man is not born with such dread of collecting: he learns it. Someone, somewhere, had done to the father what the father swore never to do to anyone, and that person, whoever it was, governed the Grocery still, from the ground where they lay, sixty years later, by the hand of a woman who had never known them and paid them interest every morning, on opening the door before the bell.
