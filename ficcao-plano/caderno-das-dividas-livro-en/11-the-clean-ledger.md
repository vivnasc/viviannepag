## 11. The clean ledger

The month closed, and it closed without fear, and it was the first time in more years than Benvinda cared to count.

They did the accounts together, at the back table, on a night of late winter, with the board ledger and Aurora's clean notebook side by side, and this time the two ledgers did not quarrel: they conversed. Aurora added, and Benvinda put the faces on the numbers, and between the two the village went on being counted in a new way, just, in which each name had its right weight, neither too much out of fear nor too little out of hardness. The wholesaler's arrears were paid in part and the rest arranged; the lorry had come back to stopping on Wednesdays; the shelves had once again rice and sugar and real salt cod. The Grocery was not going to close. And the most astonishing, for Benvinda, was that it was not going to close not for having given less, but for having received what was hers: the shop had saved itself by collecting, not by avarice, and those were two things she had learned, at the end of sixty-one years, to distinguish.

There was, before that, a night when Aurora did not do accounts. It was the first, and Benvinda noticed, because the house grew strangely quiet without the tac, tac of the machine. She found her niece sitting at the kitchen window, in the dark, looking at the largo, with no notebook, no pen, nothing in her hands. And she sat beside her, without speaking, for she had learned from her niece, that winter, that there are moments when to be beside is worth more than to say.

"I am trying to do what you do," said Aurora, at the end of a long time. "To be still. To look at a thing without turning it at once into a number. I am looking at the largo, aunt, and I am trying not to calculate anything. It is harder than it seems. My head wants to count the tiles of the roofs, wants to budget the mending of the fountain, wants to do something. It costs me an enormous labour just to stay here looking."

"To me it costs an enormous labour to collect," said Benvinda. "To you it costs an enormous labour to stop. Each one has her mountain, my girl. The good thing is that yours is earlier. You are forty-one. I only began to climb mine at sixty-one. You are twenty years ahead of your aunt."

And the two of them stayed at the window, in the dark, looking at the largo, the aunt and the niece, each doing the difficult thing of the other: Benvinda letting herself stay without getting up to tidy, Aurora letting herself stay without counting, and the largo out there, with the dry fountain at the centre, asking nothing of either of them, only letting itself be looked at.

"I have something left to tell you, aunt," said Aurora, later, closing the clean notebook, on the night of the accounts. "And it costs me, because I do not say such things, I say numbers. But you taught me a thing this winter, and it would be ugly of me to go away without telling you of it."

"Say it, my girl."

Aurora took her time. She fiddled with the pen, counted something on her fingers, did what she did when she did not know how to feel. And then she said, slowly:

"I do accounts so as not to feel, aunt. I always did. When a thing hurts me, I add something up, I count the steps, I do budgets in my head, and the pain passes behind the numbers. I came to Véspera to sell father's house, which is only an account, and I was going to go away, as I always go away from everywhere, running to the next place, which is always only the step to the other. And you, this winter, without meaning to, showed me a thing. You showed me that there are accounts that do not close by adding up. That your account with the village would not be resolved in my clean notebook, with the large number underlined. It was resolved by your going, house by house, with your heart in your mouth, to look each one in the eyes. I would have sent letters. I would have collected everything from everyone by post, all neat, and I would have received the money and lost the village. You received the money and were left with the friends, because you put your face where I put the paper." She raised her eyes, and they were wet, the girl of the accounts. "I have been fleeing for twenty-three years, aunt, and I never got anywhere, and I come from a family that either gives everything or counts everything, and I never learned the third thing, the one you learned this winter: to stay. To stay in front of a person and bear what one feels. You learned it at sixty-one. Perhaps I am still in time at forty-one. That is all I wished to tell you. Thank you."

Donato telephoned from the City, in those days, to ask after the sale of the house, and it happened to be Benvinda who answered. The brother, on the telephone, was the one of always: practical, hurried, with the city in his voice. He asked after the shop, and Benvinda told him, in a few words, what had happened that winter, the letter, the collecting, the shop saved. And Donato, on the other side, was silent a moment, and then said a thing Benvinda kept: "Look, sister, you did what I always told you to do. You only took forty years." And he laughed, and Benvinda laughed too, but beneath the laugh there was, in both of them, the awareness of a thing unsaid: that Donato had fled the shop and the village exactly so as not to have to wage that struggle, that he had moved to the city where money is dealt with without faces or guilt, and that perhaps he too, in his way, had spent his life fleeing from what the house had put in his body. Each of Abílio's three children had dealt with the inheritance of the fear in a way: Benvinda giving without collecting, Prudência shrinking, Donato fleeing to a city where no one asked him to be good for free. And the three of them, each in their way, were children of the same father and of the same cross on the paper, up there, in the fallen big house.

Benvinda reached her hand across the table, over the two ledgers, and took her niece's, the hand that added up, and squeezed it.

"You do not do accounts so as not to feel, my girl," she said. "You do accounts because no one ever taught you that to feel would not sink you. You are afraid of what you feel as I was afraid to collect: you think that if you let yourself feel, the sky falls on you. And the sky does not fall, Aurora. I learned that this winter, at the counter, with my legs trembling. The sky does not fall. One says the number, or weeps, or asks, or stays in front of the person, and the sky, that one swears will fall, stays in its place. It is the simplest and the hardest thing there is." She squeezed her hand again. "Do not sell your father's house yet. Stay a while longer. Not for the house. For yourself. Learn to stay in a place before selling it. It is the counsel of an old aunt, and old aunts, when they cease to be afraid, sometimes say right things."

Aurora did not answer, but she did not take away her hand, and they stayed so a while, the aunt and the niece, hand in hand over the ledgers, the two daughters of the same house, each unbolting, slowly, her version of the same old fear.

They spoke still, that night, of the mother, a thing that in that family was almost never done. Benvinda told Aurora the phrase Prudência had brought her on the night of the rain, the mother's phrase in bed, that of doing the account aloud, at the table, in front of everyone. And Aurora, who had never known the grandmother, heard it as one hears a letter arrived fifty years late, and said, slowly:

"So grandmother already knew all this. She already knew, in bed, dying, what you took sixty-one years to learn and I have not yet learned."

"She knew, my girl. Mothers know almost always. What they do not have is the means to force the children to do what they know. The mother told us the truth and died, and the truth stayed there, in the room, and we went out and did the contrary, because a truth said out of love forces no one. What forces is a wholesaler's letter." Benvinda smiled. "Look what a sad thing and what a comic thing, Aurora: your grandmother begged us on her knees, with love, and we did not hear her; a man from outside, who never saw us, threatened us with a bill, and we obeyed. Perhaps it is for that the world needs both things, the love and the bills. The love tells us what we ought to do. It is the bills that force us to do it."

Aurora kept that, because it was for her too. She lived fleeing from the love that forced one to stop, and perhaps she needed, she too, some bill or other, a marked date, a thing from outside that forced her to stay where the love, alone, could not hold her.

Prudência appeared a few days later, without warning, as she had appeared on the night of the rain, but this time she did not come to quarrel. She came with a parcel under her arm, and laid it on the counter, and there unfolded from it a cloth, a linen cloth, embroidered, of the festival kind, worked from top to bottom with a perfection that took the breath away.

"I made you this," said Prudência, gruff, like one who hands over their heart and is ashamed of it. "For the shop table. Since now the shop is of people who make themselves respected, at least let it have a cloth of people who make themselves respected, and not that black oilcloth that is of the father's time."

Benvinda took up the cloth, and understood, on touching it, what it was. It was not a cloth. It was a plea for pardon, and it was more: it was a whole cloth, embroidered from end to end, and not a hem sewn for the trade. Prudência, who was afraid to cut a whole dress and have it come out too beautiful, had embroidered a whole cloth, and beautiful, and had brought it to put on show, on the counter, in the shop through which the whole village passed. It was the younger sister doing, in her way, the same gesture Benvinda had done with the ledger: ceasing to shrink, taking up her size, risking that they would think her contrived so as to be able, at last, to be whole.

"It is the most beautiful thing I ever saw, sister," said Benvinda, and it was true, and the two of them understood they were not speaking of the cloth. "I am going to put it here, in front, where the whole village sees it. And when they ask who made it, and they will ask, I will say your whole name, aloud, and I will say the price it is worth, which is much, and I will not lower it a penny. Because you are not free either, Prudência. You never were. We are the two daughters of the same fear, and the two of us are, old as we are, learning the same thing at the same time, each on her own side. Look at us. We only took fifty years."

And the two of them laughed, and it was a wet laugh, of sisters, the first in a long time, and Aurora, from the corner, saw the two aunts laughing over a cloth and a ledger, and kept that too, because it was the portrait of the house curing itself, slowly, generation by generation, of the old disease that came from a cross on a paper, up there, in the fallen big house.

That week, Benvinda paid the wholesaler. Not the part: all she owed, at once, because she had collected enough and wanted to close that account from outside with the same firmness with which she had learned to close the ones from within. Aurora telephoned, and Benvinda, who had never spoken with the man from outside, asked her niece for the telephone and spoke herself, for the first time, with the gentleman of the accounts. And she told him the number she was going to pay, and the date, and said it without trembling, in their language, the cold language of the accounts that close, and the gentleman of the accounts called her madam and was correct, and at the end, before hanging up, Benvinda said to him a thing the gentleman of the accounts did not expect from a village grocer, and that perhaps he did not understand, but that she said all the same, because it was for herself that she said it:

"You taught me a thing this winter, without knowing it, and I thank you for it. You always collect from me what I owe you, on time, with no pity at all, and I for years thought you a hard man. But you are not hard. You are just. You collect what is yours, and so your house does not close. I did not collect what was mine, and mine nearly closed. I learned from you, from your coldness, more than I learned from all my father's goodness. Have a good afternoon. The money goes tomorrow."

The gentleman of the accounts, on the other side, was a moment not knowing what to answer, because he was not used to a customer thanking him, and much less for that. He said only, at the end, with a new strangeness in the correct voice of always, that the house of Adelino Sousa & Sons was very glad to go on supplying the Grocery, and that he hoped the business would prosper. And he hung up. And perhaps he thought no more of the matter, for city men have many calls in a day. But Benvinda was left thinking that she had done, in that call, a new thing: she had spoken as an equal with the world outside, with the modern big house, that of the telephones and the bills, and she had not trembled, and had not felt herself either the grandfather signing the cross or the father fleeing. She had felt herself what she was: a tradeswoman paying what she owed and collecting what was owed her, neither below nor above anyone. Just. The word was beginning to settle on her body as the clothes that are much worn settle.

And she hung up, and stayed with her hand on the telephone, and Aurora looked at her with her mouth open, and Benvinda shrugged, and said, putting away the ledger, with the peace of one who has closed at last the hardest account, which was the one with herself:

"There are teachers one never thanks, my girl, because they come dressed as enemies. The wholesaler was mine. The one who threatened to close me was the one who taught me to open."
