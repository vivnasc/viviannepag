## 10. Who stays and who flees

To collect, Benvinda discovered, was like learning to write with the other hand: at first everything came out crooked and cost a fortune of effort, and then, slowly, the hand went on finding the knack, and what had been impossible became only difficult, and the difficult only uncomfortable, and it never came to be easy, but it ceased to be a wall.

After Marçais, it was lighter. Not easy: lighter. She had proved, with her body, that the sky did not fall, and that proof was worth more than all Aurora's advice and all Prudência's truths, because it was a proof made by her, on her own skin. So she went on, house by house, but now in another way, not with the phrase of the mirror nor with honey nor with wine drunk, but with the ledger in her hand and the number in her mouth and her eyes in their eyes.

And in doing it, she discovered the thing the father had died without knowing, and that was the most important thing of the whole book of her life: she discovered who was who.

Because the village, put to the test, separated itself, like the wheat from the chaff in the sieve. There were those who paid and stayed. Those were the good surprise, and they were more than she feared. They paid what they could, arranged the rest, and, what was the marvel, they did not care for her less for it: they cared, if anything, more, with a new respect, the respect one has for one who makes themselves respected. The mother of the twins, after paying, came into the shop with her head more raised, as though paying had freed her from a shame she carried without knowing it. Old Aprígio, confronted at last with the eleven years of loaves, instead of playing deaf, wept a little, as old men do, and said that he was ashamed, and that he would pay with eggs from his hens, a dozen a week, and Benvinda accepted the eggs, and the eggs were few and they were everything, and the two of them were greater friends after the account than they had been in the eleven years of pretending it did not exist.

And there were those who fled. Marçais never came back to pay, and never came into the shop with an open face again, and took to buying from the city lorry, dearer, only so as not to show his face, and went about the village speaking ill of Benvinda, that she had become grasping, that age had soured her temper. And it hurt, at first, to hear that. But Benvinda learned quickly to read their flight the other way round: each one who fled because she had collected was one who had never been a friend, was a free customer dressed as a friend, and the flight, however much it hurt, was a truth being given her for free, the truth the father had paid for his whole life so as not to know. *A shop that collects is a shop that loses friends*, the father had said. And Benvinda amended him now, at the counter, low, to herself, with the bitter and freeing wisdom of the six weeks: a shop that collects is a shop that loses those who were not friends. And it is left with those who were. And a shop with ten true friends is better than a shop with a hundred customers who flee at the first number. The father had died surrounded by a hundred. She was going to be left with ten. And the ten were more than the hundred.

There was, among those who paid and those who fled, a third case that taught Benvinda the greatest of the subtleties, and it was that of her friend António, of the lower fields. António owed, and could pay, but not much at once, and when Benvinda told him the number, instead of fleeing like Marçais or taking offence, he did a simple thing: he flushed, lowered his eyes, and said "you are right, Benvinda, I know I owe it, let me go on paying as I can, for I will pay, just give me time, and forgive me for having abused your goodness." And he went on paying, slowly, a little a month, without fail, and at the end of a few months he was square, and the friendship of the two, instead of spoiling, became cleaner, because António, on paying, ceased to be a man who abused a good woman and became an upright man who settled what he owed. Benvinda understood, with António, that to collect did not spoil the good ones: it straightened them. That many of those who owed her had, beneath the habit of not paying, a silent shame, and that the collecting, far from driving them away, freed them of that shame, gave them the occasion to become upright again. That she, in not collecting, had not spared them: she had left them bound to a small permanent shame, eternal debtors of a woman who never gave them the chance to be square. Her goodness had made of the whole village a crowd of small ashamed debtors, and the collecting, now, was also a present she gave them: that of being able, at last, to look her in the eyes owing nothing.

The scene Benvinda kept for ever, of those weeks, was that of Vitória.

Vitória was the single mother of the two children, the one who took both heads of the table, the one who had sworn that they would lack nothing and accepted help nor trust from no one, out of pride, out of a stubbornness of not owing. But she owed, the Grocery she owed, because with two children and one wage there is no pride enough, and her debt was of those that shamed Benvinda to collect, because it was a debt of hunger, of pure need. Vitória came to the shop, in those weeks, to pay what she could, which was little, and she came with a hardness in her chin, ready to be humiliated, ready for Benvinda to say to her the whole number in front of the other customers who were in the shop.

And Benvinda did the contrary, and did it on purpose, and did it aloud, for the whole shop to hear.

"Vitória," she said, in front of three or four women who were at the counter. "You paid me what you could, and I saw you go without to pay it, and that, woman, that is worth more to me than the money. The rest of your account, I strike it out. Not out of pity, hear me well, for I have no pity for you at all, you are among the most whole women of this village. I strike it out because I want to, because it is mine and I do with it what I see fit, and what I see fit to do with your debt is to give it you, as a present, for your two children." And she struck out, there, in front of all of them, with the father's pencil, Vitória's debt, and it was the first strike of her life that was not fear: it was choice. "This is what to give is, Vitória. To give on purpose to one whom one chooses. The other, what I did my whole life, to give to all for not knowing how to say no, that was not to give. It was to be afraid. I learned the difference this spring, at my own cost, and now that I know it I do the two things as they should be done: I collect from one who can and owes me, and I give to one I choose to give to. The two with the same hand, and the two with my head raised."

The women at the counter heard, and the story ran the village that afternoon, and it did more for Benvinda than a thousand collections: because the village understood, from that scene, that Benvinda had not become the big house, had not become grasping nor sour as Marçais said. She had become another thing, a thing the village had never seen and for which it scarcely had a name: a just woman. Who collected from one who owed and could, and gave to one she chose, and who in the two things was the same, whole, without fear. And the village, which is distracted but is not a fool, understood that that woman was more to be trusted than the previous one, because the previous one gave out of weakness and this one gave out of strength, and what is given out of strength is not withdrawn, and what is given out of weakness flees at the first fright.

And there was, in the reverse of Vitória's scene, the scene of Idalina, the friend of the sweetness, and that was the hardest of all, because to collect from an enemy is easy, and to collect from a friend of forty years is an operation on living flesh. Idalina, who had come to advise the closing with so much sweetness, owed, and could pay, and Benvinda went to her house, and collected from her, with a firm voice and the number in her mouth. And Idalina passed through the three phases of Marçais at the speed of one who is finer: the surprise, the shame, and then, instead of the rude offence of the farmer, a lady's grief, tears in her eyes, an "ah, Benvinda, after so many years, you come to me with this, I who always cared so for you." And it was there that Benvinda nearly yielded, because the sweet grief of a friend fells more than the coarseness of a debtor. But she bore it, and said: "Idalina, if forty years of friendship cannot bear a number, then it was not friendship, it was an arrangement in which you took and I gave. Pay me what you owe me, and we go on being friends in earnest, for the first time. Or do not pay me, and you will know what we were." Idalina did not pay. She wept, took offence, and went away, and was never the same with Benvinda again, and drew apart slowly, with complaints through the village. And that loss hurt Benvinda more than that of Marçais, because Idalina was of forty years. But it was a truth, and the truths that hurt most are the ones that free most: Benvinda discovered, on losing Idalina, that she had lived forty years calling friendship an account in which one gave and the other took, and that true friendship, the one that stayed after the number, was another thing, and was rarer, and was better.

Vitória, she, came out of the shop saying almost nothing, with her throat tight, and at the door she turned, and said only, low:

"Thank you, Benvinda." And then, lower still, a thing that cost her, she who never accepted anything: "When mine can, they will pay you, not this one, for this one you gave me, but they will pay you with another thing, with what one pays to one who teaches us not to be ashamed of needing." And she went, straight, with the two children to raise and a debt the less and a thing the more that was in no ledger.

There were also the relapses, which Benvinda told Aurora at the end of the day, half laughing at herself, because she had learned that to laugh at the relapses was the way of not giving up on account of them. There was the afternoon she went to collect from a farmer and came out, again, owing him a favour, because the man began to speak of his sick daughter and Benvinda, without noticing, offered to mind his shop one afternoon and the relapse only undid itself when Aurora, at night, did her the account of the time lost. There was the morning she struck out a debt out of pure habit, the hand on its own, before the head decided, and had to write it again, ashamed, because she understood that she had struck it out of fear and not of choice. The hand had sixty-one years of the habit of striking out, and the habit does not undo itself in a season. But the difference, now, was that Benvinda noticed the relapses. Before, the relapse was life; now, the relapse was a relapse, a thing one noticed, named, corrected. And to know how to distinguish the relapse from the natural state, Benvinda understood, was already half the cure: the sick one who knows they are sick is halfway to the healthy.

That night, Benvinda told Aurora the accounts of the weeks, and this time there were good numbers. She had collected enough to pay a part to the wholesaler, and Aurora had already spoken to the gentleman of the accounts, who, set before real money, suddenly discovered much regard for the Grocery and a disposition to resume. But it was not of the numbers that they spoke, at the back table, that night.

"Do you know what I learned, my girl?" said Benvinda, with the ledger closed before her. "I learned that I did not know my village. Sixty-one years living in the midst of it, feeding it, thinking I knew it all, and I knew nothing, because I had never asked it for anything. One only knows a person when one asks of them, Aurora. In giving, we all stay equal, all good people. It is in asking that one sees who is who. I lived surrounded by people my whole life and only this year, collecting, did I learn who my friends were. Late, but I learned. And they are few, and they are good, and they are worth more than the whole village I thought I had and did not have."

"And do you not grieve for those you lost?" asked Aurora. "Marçais, your friend Idalina, all those people of so many years."

"I do," said Benvinda. "I grieve, of course I grieve. Forty years of friendship are not lost without hurting. But it is a clean grief, my girl, it is the grief of one who buries a thing that after all had been dead a long time and had only not been buried. Idalina did not stop caring for me when I collected from her. She had not cared for me for a long while, what she cared for was the open shop and the easy trust, and it was I who did not want to see it. The collecting did not kill the friendship. The collecting only showed me that the friendship was already dead, and that I had been putting flowers on it as one puts them on the graves, pretending it was alive. Now I know. It hurts, but I know. And better a truth that hurts than a lie that comforts, for that I learned late but I learned it well this winter."

Aurora heard, and kept it, because there was in that a lesson for her too, for her wound, that of running without stopping. One only knows a person when one asks of them. And Aurora, who never asked anything of anyone, who resolved everything alone running so as not to have to owe, understood, on hearing her aunt, that she too lived surrounded and alone, that she too knew no one because she had never risked asking. But that was her story, and her story was another book, and that night the story was the aunt's, who was sixty-one years old and had just made friends in earnest for the first time in her life, on losing, without grief, those who had never been such.
