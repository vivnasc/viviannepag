## 7. The sister

Prudência knew of the wholesaler's letter before Benvinda told her of it, because in Véspera things are known so, through the air, and because Aurora, who was niece to them both, did not have the care of the old generations with what is said and what is silenced.

She came to the Grocery on a rainy late afternoon, shut the door behind her, turned the sign to *closed* without asking leave, and went straight to the back table, where the sister and the niece had the ledgers open. Prudência was the youngest of Abílio's three houses, thirteen years younger than Benvinda, and had her own business, a small house of sewing and trousseaux that she had set up from nothing and that could have grown and never grew, always checked at the same invisible line, at the measure of the village, not a span above what their own people had had. The two sisters were, without ever having said it, the same wound turned to two sides: one did not collect what was owed her, the other did not collect from herself the right to grow. One gave too much to others, the other gave too little to herself. And perhaps for being so alike was it that they understood one another so badly.

"Show me the letter," said Prudência, without sitting down.

"Good afternoon to you too," said Benvinda.

"The letter, sister. I did not come for good afternoons."

Benvinda took the letter from the apron, creased, soft from so many days against her body, and Prudência read it standing, by the weak light of the shop, with her glasses on the tip of her nose, and her face went on closing, and when she finished she laid the letter on the table with a dry gesture.

"Six weeks. And you, what did you do with these two that have already passed?"

"I went to speak with people."

"To speak." Prudência said the word like one who spits out a stone. "You went to speak. You went to take honey to the miller's widow and to drink Marçais's wine, that is what you went to do, for the whole village knows it already, sister, the whole village is laughing that Benvinda now goes about paying calls. You go about apologising from door to door for having the nerve to want what is yours. That is what you went to do."

Benvinda flushed. That the village should be commenting on her visits was a thing that had not occurred to her, and it hurt her more than it should, because it confirmed the worst: that even in trying to collect she only managed to cut the figure of a kindly fool.

"And what would you have me do, Prudência? Go from door to door threatening people? Putting the debt in their faces? That is what the big house would do."

"The big house." Prudência at last pulled up the stool and sat down, slowly, and when she spoke again the voice had changed, had descended to a deeper and older place. "You and the big house. The father and the big house. It was always that the story of this family, and I am sick of it, sister, I have been sick of it for fifty years. The whole misfortune of this house fits in that excuse: we do not collect so as not to be the big house, we do not grow so as not to be the big house, we do not ask for what is ours so as not to be the big house. And do you know what we are, on account of not wanting to be the big house? We are poor. We are poor and we are trodden on, and we die poor and trodden on thinking that is virtue. The father died thinking that. You go by the same way. And I, God forgive me, too, with my sewing that never grows because to grow is ugly, because to have is to rob, because to stand out is to betray our own." She struck the table, once, without force, more from tiredness than from anger. "I am sick of it. I am sick of being poor on account of the fear of a man who is already dead."

Benvinda heard, and what the sister said was true, and it was for being true that it hurt, and it was for hurting that she answered badly, as one answers badly to the truths of sisters:

"Speak well of the father. The father was a good man."

"The father was a man with fear," said Prudência. "The two things do not quarrel, sister, they can live together, and they lived in him his whole life. He was good, yes. And he was so afraid of being like those who robbed him of his land that he preferred to have nothing rather than have to collect whatever it was. And he taught us that as one teaches a prayer, and we learned it, and look at us: you closing the shop so as not to collect, I tightening my life into a small measure so as not to grow. We are the daughters of his fear, Benvinda. We inherited all the fear and none of the land."

"The mother warned us," said Prudência, lower, and the rain on the glass filled the pause. "Do you remember? The mother, at the end, already in bed, called us both and told us not to be like her. Do you remember what she said? 'Do not spend your life doing the account your father does not do. Do the account in front of him, at the table, aloud, and let him learn to hear it.' It was the last thing she said to us properly, sister, and we went out of the room and the two of us did exactly the contrary. You went to go on giving like the father, I went to go on watching like her, silent, shrunken. Neither of us had the courage she asked of us, which was that of the middle: to do the account aloud. To put the number on the table, in front of everyone, without shame to give nor shame to collect." She laughed, without joy. "The mother died asking us that, and we, the two daughters, are the proof that she died without being heard. Fifty years later, sister, you have a letter from a wholesaler forcing you to what the mother asked of you for free. See if this time you do what she said. For her, if not for yourself."

It was hard for Benvinda to hear that, because it was true and because it came with the mother inside it, and the mother was the deepest place of all.

"I did not remember that phrase," she said, low.

"No, you did not. You kept the father's, the beautiful one, the convenient one. I kept the mother's, the difficult one, the one that obliged. Each of us was left with the inheritance that weighed on her less. But the mother's was the good one, Benvinda. The mother's was the one that could have saved us both, and we threw it away because it gave work."

They were silent. The rain beat on the glass, and Aurora, in a corner, said nothing, but heard everything, and kept everything, because that conversation between the aunts was also about her, about her endless running, about the third way the same wound turned: neither to give too much nor to shrink, but to run, to run so as never to arrive at the place where one would have to stop and look at what one is.

"You are not good, sister," said Prudência, at last, and said it without malice, slowly, as one delivers a heavy thing carried too long. "Forgive me, but you have to hear it, and there is no one else to tell you, because the whole village likes to think you good, it suits them that you be good, it comes cheap to them. But I am your sister and I tell you the truth: you are not good, you are afraid. And your goodness, all that goodness the village praises, is your fear well dressed. And the worst, sister, the worst of all, is that it is not only you who pays for that fear. You make the whole village pay for it, in interest that no one sees. Each time you forgive a debt out of fear of collecting, you teach one more not to pay. Each time you give too much, you teach one more to expect too much. You are not being good to the village, Benvinda. You are spoiling it, slowly, for forty years, teaching it not to respect one who gives. And when you close, and you are going to close at this pace, the village is going to say poor thing, and go to the city lorry, and in half a year no one remembers that there was here a Grocery where one came in empty-handed. That is going to be the prize of your goodness. The forgetting."

It was too much. Benvinda got up, and the anger, which she had contained for a long while, came out at last, and came out unjust, as true angers come out:

"Get out. Get out of my shop, Prudência. You come here, who never set foot here to help, and tell me that I spoil the village, I, who sustained half the village when you were sewing hems for the trade! Get out. Let each one mind her own measure, since you speak of measure. My shop is mine, and the fear is mine, and if I want to close with it I close, and I do not need my younger sister to come and tell me who I am."

Prudência got up. She did not answer the anger, which was worse, because with one who answers one can go on quarrelling, and with one who falls silent one is left hearing the echo of what one shouted. She went to the door, turned the sign again to *open*, and before going out she said, without turning, low, the phrase that Benvinda was to take to bed and that was going to be, though she did not yet know it, the one that would most push her forward:

"I sew hems for the trade, sister, because I am afraid to cut a whole dress and have it come out too beautiful and our own people think I put on airs. You and I have the same disease. The difference is that you still have six weeks to cure yourself, and the knife at your back to force you. I have no knife. I can go on sewing hems until I die, and no one forces me to anything, and that is my misfortune, not to have a wholesaler to threaten me. Make the most of yours, sister. Make the most of your threat. It is the best thing that has happened to you in your life, and you have not yet understood it."

And she put the shawl over her head, and opened the door to the rain, and before going out she turned once more, and this time the voice no longer had any harshness, it had only a sister's tiredness:

"I did not come here to quarrel with you, sister. I came because I was afraid. I was afraid that you would close the shop and die poor and beloved like the father, and leave me, alone, the last of this house, sewing hems until I die with the same fear. You are the eldest. If you do not conquer this, I have no one to go to for courage. Do you understand? It is not only your shop that is at stake. It is the possibility of someone of this family, once at least, in three generations, managing to look at money without fear. If you manage it, sister, perhaps I manage it too. If you close, I have closed too, there at home, with my small sewing. That is why I spoke hard to you. It was not to hurt you. It was to push you. You are my elder sister. You first."

And she went out into the rain, and the door slammed, and in the Grocery were left the elder sister and the niece, and the silence after the younger sister, which was the worst silence of all, the one that comes full of another's rightness. Benvinda sat down, and stayed a long time with her hand on the board ledger, and Aurora had the good sense not to say a word, not to do an account, only to be there. Through Benvinda's head passed, in that silence, the childhood of the two of them. Prudência had been born late, thirteen years after her, when the mother already thought herself without more children, and had been raised almost by Benvinda, who took her in her arms while the mother sewed and the father gave at the counter. The two had grown up in that same shop, in that same back kitchen, and had learned together, without words, the lesson of the house: that to give is beautiful and to collect is ugly, that to have is suspect, that to stand out is to betray. Benvinda had turned to the shop and Prudência to the sewing, but the lesson had been the same, and had given in both the same crooked fruit: a shop that did not collect and a sewing that did not grow, the two daughters making small, each in her own way, the life they could have had large. And the worst, thought Benvinda, was that they had drawn apart from one another exactly for being so alike: each saw in the other the mirror of her own disease, and no one likes to live beside the mirror that shows what one hides. That was why they quarrelled. They quarrelled because they recognised one another, and recognition, without cure, is a form of accusation.

And at the end of a long time Benvinda said, low, to the ledger, to the rain, to the sister who was already far off:

"The worst is that she is right." Benvinda passed her hand over the cover of the ledger, slowly, like one who strokes an old and guilty animal. "And the worst of the worst is what she said of the interest no one sees. I always thought that my goodness did harm to no one. That it was only mine, that it was only I who lost. And Prudência showed me that it was not so, that I, in giving too much, in always forgiving, spent forty years teaching the village not to respect one who gives. I spoiled them, Aurora. Little by little. Marçais was not born thinking that the Grocery was free: it was I who taught it him, on trust by on trust, line by line. If he has owed me for eleven years with no shame at all, the fault is not only his, it is mine too, who gave him eleven years' leave. My goodness was not only a cell for me. It was a slow poison for them. And that, my girl, is the hardest thing to swallow of all that my sister came to tell me: that I, thinking myself good, did harm to the whole village. That there are ways of being good that spoil more than much malice."

And at the end of a while more, lower still, a thing she had never said aloud in sixty-one years: "I am afraid, Aurora. I have a fear of collecting that makes my hands sweat. And I spent my life calling it goodness, because goodness was a name one could say to the mirror without shame. But it is fear. My sister took the dress off it and showed it to me naked, and now that I have seen it naked I can no longer dress it up as beautiful. And I do not know which is worse, my girl: whether to live with it dressed my whole life, or to have to face it naked in the six weeks that remain to me."
