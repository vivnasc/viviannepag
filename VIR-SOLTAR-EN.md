# vir.soltar
### returning: coming back to yourself
*Method VS · See and Release · Vivianne dos Santos*

> *You do not have to stay standing to be worthy of rest. The lap you return to has been open all along, waiting for you.*

*Here you do not come to stop caring. You come to see that your effort was the way you were loved, and to discover that you already are, before doing anything.*

## Who this book is for

This book is for you, if you carry everything and do not know how to put it down. If you always arrive first, anticipate what others will need before they know it themselves, solve things before they are asked, catch what is falling before it falls. If you do your part and then, quietly, do everyone else's part too, and still apologise for not having done more. If sitting down with nothing to do brings up an immediate restlessness, a dull guilt, as though rest were a debt you will have to repay later, with interest.

It is for you, too, if the moment silence settles in you rush to cover it. If you switch on the television before you have put down your keys, if you reach for your phone for no reason at all, if you fill your diary until there is not one minute left that is only yours and you call that having a full life. If you stay in relationships that no longer do you any good, only so as not to face the emptiness that would come once you let them go. If, beneath a life that from the outside may even look full, you carry a tiredness that sleep does not cure, and a hurry to always be doing that does not leave you even on your free days.

Perhaps you recognise yourself more in one of these two women than in the other. Perhaps you recognise yourself in both, and they seem to you, at first glance, two different things: one who never stops giving, another who cannot bear the silence. But this book is born of an intuition that perhaps no one has ever told you, and that changes everything. The two are the same movement. You do and you fill, without stopping, precisely so that you never have to stop and feel the emptiness that would lie beneath. The doing is the most applauded flight in the world. And there is a single gesture that frees you from both: to stop pushing, and to come back to yourself.

It is not a self-help book with easy promises, nor a manual of ten steps to a productive life. It would be ironic, in fact, to ask more effort of you, one more task at which you can fail, when it is precisely effort, and the flight from emptiness it hides, that we are going to look at here. This book asks you the opposite. It asks you to stop, slowly, and to discover something you may find hard to believe right now: that your worth was never at stake, that there was no account to settle, and that on the other side of the emptiness you feared there was not the abyss, there was a lap.

It is a short book, on purpose. It belongs to a larger family. The Method VS · See and Release has a map, *The Seven Veils*, and from that map are born three sister-books, three movements of a single crossing: to see, to come, to live. This is the second, the movement of **vir**, the return to yourself. It joins two veils that always travel hand in hand, the Effort and the Desolation, because they are in truth the two faces of a single knot. If you have never read the mother-book, that does not matter at all. This one stands on its own. It holds within it everything you need to recognise this movement in yourself, to see it with tenderness, and to begin to return, with a concrete method you can use at any time, in any place.

A word before we begin, because it is the heart of everything that follows. I do not write to you from a place of arrival. I write from a path still under way, my own. I am a woman who saw herself run all her life to be loved, and fill every silence so as not to hear what lived inside it, and who grew tired of judging herself for it, and went looking to understand. I study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as someone who collects certainties, but as someone who needs names for what she feels. Everything I share here passed through me first. I recognised it in myself, I sat with it, and only then did I find it, repeated, in what echoes in so many women who go round the same turns, convinced they are the only ones. So I do not invite you to follow me. I invite us to walk together, at the same pace, towards a rest that is already yours and that you may only have forgotten.

Read slowly. Do not rush to the end, which would be, precisely, the movement creeping into the reading itself. Do the practices, even the ones that seem too small to you, because it is the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here. And be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades, and generations, to weave.

One last thing, before we go in, because it is the condition without which none of this works: tenderness towards yourself. Most of us learned to change by force of hardness, of demand, of inner criticism. And for a woman like you, this is a double trap, because you run the risk of making the path of return itself into one more task at which you strive and at which you can fail. If you catch yourself scolding yourself for still not knowing how to rest, or for fleeing the silence again, stop, and recognise in that the old movement dressing itself up anew. One does not awaken by force. A frightened child does not calm with shouting, she calms with a lap, and there is in you a child like that, clever and tired, behind every gesture. Treat her as you would treat your best friend learning this. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself, and the rest becomes possible.

## The movement of coming

There are three movements in a whole crossing of return home. First, **to see**: to recognise the pattern, without guilt, to give a name to what had no name. Then, **to come**: to come back to yourself, to put down what you were carrying, to stay with yourself. And finally, **to live**: to inhabit life again, free. This book is the second of those movements, the middle one, the most bodily of all. Because seeing is no longer enough, and living has not yet arrived. Between the two there is a return to make, and it is that return this book is about.

To come is to return. It is the movement of one who spent years turned outward, attentive to what others need, busy with everything that must not fail, and who, at a certain point, turns inward and discovers that she stayed behind herself. To come is to turn around on the path and go and fetch the woman who was left forgotten somewhere, between the gestures and the days, beneath all that full life. It is not going anywhere new. It is returning to a place that was always yours and from which, without realising it, you went absent.

Notice that seeing, on its own, is not enough for this. You can see with all clarity that you carry too much, that you flee silence, that you wear yourself out pleasing, and go on doing it just the same, the next day, unable to stop. It is the complaint of so many lucid women: I know all this about myself, so why does it not change? Because between what you know and what you do there is a gap, and in that gap lives the body, which learned slowly and unlearns more slowly still. Awareness learns with a sentence. The body learns with repetition and with time. That is why coming is not an understanding, it is a gesture, repeated until the body knows it by heart. It is not enough to see that you are rowing against yourself. You have to let go of the oars, with your hands, many times, until they learn that they can rest.

Now let me show you why the Effort and the Desolation are, in truth, a single knot, and why I have to treat them together, in the same book, with the same method. At first glance they seem opposites. A woman of effort is always doing; a woman of desolation is always filling. But notice what lies beneath the two, and you will see that it is exactly the same thing. You do, and you do, and you do, not only because you learned that love is earned by giving, but also, and perhaps above all, because while you are doing you do not have to stop. And if you do not stop, you never reach the silence. And if you never reach the silence, you never have to feel the emptiness that terrifies you. The effort is the engine; the flight from emptiness is the secret destination. You carry everything so as never to have to stay with anything, with yourself, in the hollow you are afraid to visit.

That is why rest gives you such anguish. It is not only the guilt of not being useful, though it is that too. It is that, at the exact instant you stop doing, the silence you were covering with busyness opens up, and from that silence rises everything the doing served to keep you from feeling. The exhaustion and the fear of emptiness are, in the end, the same woman, caught between two fires: too tired to go on, too frightened to stop. She does so as not to feel the hollow; and she fears the hollow precisely because she never stopped doing long enough to discover what is in it.

What is in it, on the other side of the flight, when you finally stop and stay, is not the abyss you feared. It is your own presence, without interruptions. It is the fertile earth where something of yours, long postponed, was waiting for its time to germinate. It is, in the end, a lap. The hands that all your life caught others before the fall can rest, at last, on your own lap, and you, who all your life held everyone, can discover, for the first time, that there is someone to hold you. That someone is you. And it is not little. It is everything.

There is an image that may help you see the two movements as one. Imagine a woman who rows, without stopping, against the current, with all the strength of her arms, for so many years that she no longer remembers when she began. She rows because she learned that, if she stops rowing, the current takes her, and the current, to her, is abandonment, is emptiness, is ceasing to be loved. The effort is the arms that never rest. The desolation is the fear of where the current would carry her if she stopped. And what no one told her, and what she herself could never discover because she never let go of the oars long enough, is that the current does not carry her to the abyss. It carries her home. To let go of the oars, to stop rowing, to let herself be carried inward, that is the coming. And on the other side, where she was certain there was the fall, there is the bank, there is solid ground, there is the lap.

The cure, then, is one, and it is a movement of return. It is not to do better, nor to fill better. It is to stop pushing, and to return. To stop proving that you deserve, to stop covering what you feel, and to stay, in the hollow, long enough to discover that it holds you. That is the coming. That is the whole path of this book.

I want to make clear to you, before we go in, what this movement does not ask of you, because it is as important as what it does ask. It does not ask you to stop caring for those you love, nor to become a cold woman, closed off, turned only towards herself. What we are going to put down is not your love, it is the fear that mixed itself in with it. It is not the giving, it is the obligation to give until there is nothing left of you. It is not the companionship, it is the terror of being alone. You will go on caring, perhaps even better, because you will come to care by choice and by liking, and not out of terror of ceasing to be needed. And you will go on having your people, perhaps in a more whole way, because you will no longer be in them to cover a hole. Coming does not take you out of life. It gives you back to yourself, within it. Everything that follows, the two faces we are going to look at, the root they share, the protocol, the practices, the seven days, is one single thing said in many ways: come back to yourself, and discover that the lap you return to has been open all along.

## The one who carries everything

Let us begin with the most visible face of the knot, the one the world applauds: the one who carries everything. There is a scene that repeats itself in many homes, and perhaps in yours too. You spent the whole day taking care of everyone. You fetched, you carried, you remembered, you solved, you held. And at night, finally seated, someone says to you, with the best of intentions, "rest a while, everything is fine". And instead of relief, an irritation you yourself do not understand rises up in you. Or a tightness. Or a sudden urge to get up and go and tidy something, fold a towel, get ahead with tomorrow's breakfast. Because rest, for your body, is not safe. To stop is to risk. It is to be in plain view. It is to lose the excuse of doing to justify that you deserve to be there, on that sofa, taking up that space.

Deep down lives an old agreement you never consciously signed, but which you fulfil every day, without fail, like someone paying rent. The agreement goes like this: if I do enough, I get to stay. If I stop, they stop loving me. It is a silent equation, learned early, and so stuck to your skin that it no longer even seems a belief. It seems the truth. It seems, quite simply, who you are. And everything, in your life, turns around this agreement. It was always easier for you to give than to receive, to serve than to be served, because to receive meant believing something that frightens you: that you were enough without offering anything in return. That, the simplest of all, was always the hardest account to settle.

How do you recognise this face of the movement, active in you? You recognise it by the guilt that rises every time you rest, as though repose were a debt to be paid later. By saying "yes" with your mouth while your body shouts "no", and only realising it too late, when you have already committed to more than you can bear. By it being far easier for you to give than to receive, to the point where a compliment or a helping hand leaves you uncomfortable, not knowing where to put yourself nor what to say. By anticipating others' needs before your own, often before they themselves feel them, as though you had an antenna permanently switched on for what is missing for those around you. By a little background voice that whispers that your worth depends on what you produce, on what you solve, on how much you are needed. And by the silent sorrow, which you rarely allow yourself to feel, of always being the one who holds, and almost never having anyone to hold you.

There is also the tiredness, which is the symptom that usually, at last, brings these women to seek help, when nothing else manages to cover it any longer. It is not the tiredness of one who worked hard, that passes with a good night's sleep. It is a tiredness that sleeps eight hours and wakes the same, an exhaustion that comes from having been, for years, holding a weight that is not only physical. It is the weight of feeling responsible for the happiness of others, of going about with a constant monitoring running beneath everything, checking whether everyone is all right, whether no one is upset, whether nothing is lacking to anyone. Of never being able to lower your guard. There are women who only grasp the true scale of this when the body forces them to stop, with an illness, a burnout, a collapse that cannot be put off. The body says, in its stubborn way, what the mouth never allowed itself to say out loud: enough, I need too, I count too. To release this chain before you reach that point is one of the greatest acts of self-love I know, and one of the hardest, because you have to do it against years of praise, against a whole life of hearing that your best quality was that you never stopped.

This face has disguises so praised that it is hard to catch. See whether you recognise any. The disguise of the indispensable one, who organises everything and then complains, exhausted, that no one helps, without realising that she trained everyone not to help, because no one does it as well or as fast as she does. The disguise of the one who never asks, who would rather go without than give someone the trouble of caring for her, and who calls this independence when it is, deep down, fear of depending and being let down. The disguise of the saviour, who surrounds herself with people to rescue, because as long as there is someone needing her, she knows what her place is and feels safe in the world. And the most subtle disguise of all, that of the one who gives in order to control, because to give much creates a silent debt, and one who owes does not abandon. There is no malice in any of these disguises. There is, at the bottom of each one, the same little girl, who learned that free love was not for her, and that she would have to pay for it all her life, always in advance.

There is a form of this face so fine that it hides inside the very gestures of love, and I want to show it to you, because it is the hardest to see. Sometimes, even in the most luminous, most generous gestures, an almost imperceptible weight slips in. The serving seems clear, but it carries a dense silence, as though it expected something in return. And in that secret corner the effort settles, not in the act of offering itself, but in the hidden expectation of being recognised, seen, repaid. The giving no longer rests in itself, it begins to measure reactions, to watch for responses, to wait for signs that it was worth it. And what should expand freely begins to shrink, and you, even as you offer, feel the burden of sustaining. To recognise this is not to discover that your love was false. It is to discover where it grew tired, where it tied itself to an exchange no one guaranteed you, and to be able, at last, to give it back its lightness. Love that expects nothing in return is the only one that does not exhaust the one who loves.

Here is the cruellest part of this face, the one that makes it so hard to see: it is the only one the world applauds. They call it devotion. Generosity. Being reliable. Being a rock. You receive praise precisely for the chain that binds you. No one tells you that you are exhausted, because, seen from outside, you seem tireless. And you yourself take pride in it, even as, on the inside, you go on fading. The whole world has an interest in your continuing to wear this layer, because it is comfortable to have nearby someone who carries everything and never presents the bill. But keep this question, which we will open later, because it is the hinge of this whole book: and what if this endless doing, this race that never stops, were also the most perfect way of never having to stop and stay with yourself, in the silence we have not yet looked at?

## The fear of emptiness

Let us now look at the other face, the one that hides beneath the first: the fear of emptiness. There is a place inside you that you avoid at all costs, and you do not always know that you avoid it. It does not appear with its face uncovered, it does not announce itself. You notice it, rather, between the lines of your gestures. In the hurry to switch on the television the moment you walk in, before you have even put down your keys. In the phone you pick up for no reason at all, with nothing to look at, only out of the need to keep your eyes occupied. In the task you invent when, for a rare instant, no one needs you and there is nothing to solve. The moment silence settles in, a dull anguish rises, without form, and you rush to fill it before you have even felt it fully, like someone fleeing a dark room without waiting to see whether there is, in fact, anything there.

Perhaps you have never stopped to notice that it is always the same gesture, repeated in a thousand different forms. The background noise you never switch off. The people you call when you are left alone, even the ones who tire you. The extra work you say you hate and on which, deep down, you depend in order not to stop. The relationship that does not end, though it long ago stopped feeding you, because the emptiness after it frightens you more than its lack of life. And notice carefully, because it is here that the two faces touch: the task you invent, the extra work, the endless holding of everyone, are also forms of filling. The effort is your noblest, most applauded way of never having to reach the silence. You carry everything, and in carrying you never stop, and in never stopping you never have to discover what is in the hollow. The two faces are one single flight.

How do you recognise this face active in you? By the difficulty of being at home alone, in silence, without television, music or phone covering the depths. It is not that you do not like silence in theory. It is that, in practice, the moment it settles, something in you stirs and needs to break it. By the tendency to fill your diary until there is no time left at all that is only yours, and to call that having a full life, when there is a difference between a full life and a cluttered one, and you, deep down, know which of the two is yours. By staying in relationships or commitments that no longer do you any good, only so as not to face the emptiness that would come once you let them go. By a restlessness that rises every time there is nothing to solve, as though peace itself were threatening. By confusing solitude with failure, as though being alone were public proof that something in you failed. And by rarely knowing how to answer, when you are asked, what it is that you, only you, truly like, because it has been so long since you stopped to listen to yourself that you no longer even know the answer.

There is a fear beneath this face that is worth naming, because it is the engine of everything: the fear that, if you stop and look inward, you will find nothing. That the silence will reveal a hollow, a lack of substance, the secret confirmation that you are no one interesting when you are not doing or pleasing. Notice how this closes the circle with the previous face. If you learned that you are worth only for what you do, then to stop is not only dangerous because of the love you might lose. It is dangerous because, without the doing, you fear there is nothing left of you. Emptiness, to you, is not only absence. It is accusation. It is the voice that asks, deep down: and now, with nothing to do, who are you, and what worth do you have?

To see this face is to grasp, with some surprise, that what frightens you is not emptiness itself. It is the fear of it, and the memory stuck to that fear. They are distinct things, and that distinction is the whole door. Notice carefully, because it changes everything: never, as an adult, have you actually stayed in the emptiness long enough to know what it is. You always fled before, by doing, by filling, by occupying. What you know is not the silence, it is the panic of the moment before it. You react to the door of a room you never went into, convinced you know what is there, when all you know is your fear of going in.

Stay with this image of the well, because it says everything. When we fall into a well, or when we are afraid of falling, we thrash. We churn the water, we kick, we scream, we do everything we can not to sink. And that thrashing is exactly what stops us from discovering a simple truth: the well has a floor, and it has water that holds us up, and if we stopped thrashing we would float. Your endless doing and your constant filling are that thrashing. The hurry to cover the silence is the kicking of one who is certain she will sink if she stops. But try, once, to stop thrashing. Stay still in the water. And discover that it holds you, that it always held you, and that what you took for drowning was only the fear of it. The bottom was not the abyss you feared. It was solid ground, and there was fresh water, and you always knew how to swim.

Emptiness, when you finally dare to stay in it without fleeing, does not swallow you. It stays there, with you, patient, and after a few minutes it begins to show itself within as what it always was, and what no one taught you to see. Not the abyss. The earth. The silent, dark field where the seed germinates with no witnesses, far from sight, in its own time and not in ours. Notice that nothing living is born in agitation. The seed needs the dark of the earth in order to open. The child forms in the dark of the womb, nine months without light and without hurry. Everything that comes to flower grows first in a place without light and without applause, in an apparent emptiness that is, in truth, pure gestation. What seems to you like lack is, so often, room for something of yours that has not yet been born.

Before we go on, we must distinguish two things that look alike from outside and are opposites within, because without this distinction this movement can be badly read. There is a solitude that hurts, that of one who wanted company and does not have it. The solitude of isolation, of exclusion, of real abandonment, of one who was left out and feels it as an open wound. That is real, and it is not the one I speak of when I say that emptiness is fertile. Not everything is solved within. There are those who truly need more bonds, more world, more real people in their life, and to that person one does not say that she feels better alone. One says that she deserves to belong, and that going to seek company is an act of health and not a flight. But there is another thing, that many women have never known: the chosen withdrawal. Being alone by liking and not by lack. The inhabited silence. The morning hour that is only yours before the house wakes. It is not abandonment, it is repose. It is not lack, it is plenty. And the irony, and it is a great irony, is that it is only she who learns to be well alone who stops clinging to relationships out of fear, and comes to be in them by choice. Well-inhabited emptiness does not push you away from others. It frees your relationships from the weight of having to use them as flight.

## The root

To release this knot, you first need to see where it came from, because a pattern that is understood stops being a flaw of yours and becomes a story with a beginning. And the two faces of this movement share, almost always, the same root: a child who grasped, with no one telling her, that love was not guaranteed. It was earned. That to exist at rest, without doing and without pleasing, was to risk ceasing to be seen.

Perhaps you had to take care of those who should have taken care of you. Perhaps you were the small adult of a household in disorder, the one who held the siblings together, the one who read the moods, the one who kept the fragile peace of a table where one never knew how dinner would go. Perhaps affection, in your home, only arrived wrapped in praise for what you did, for the grade, the help, the washed plate, never for what you simply were. There is a name for this, in psychology, and hearing it usually brings a strange relief, the relief of discovering that you were not alone nor did you invent your story. It is called the parentified child: the child who had to play the adult too soon, who cared instead of being cared for, who set down her own needs to take care of others', because someone had to do it and it was her. Those skills, which cost you your childhood, are today your greatest strength and your greatest prison. They made you capable, attentive, reliable. And they made you incapable of stopping, of needing, of letting yourself be cared for.

Notice how the same root feeds the two faces at once. The child who learned that she was only loved when she was useful learned, in the same gesture, that to stop was dangerous, and that the silence where no one needed her was a place of abandonment. Sometimes it is born of an early solitude that hurt too much: a child left alone too long, emotionally or physically, without enough lap, without anyone coming when she called, who learns early that silence is abandonment and that being alone is being in danger of not being held. Other times it is born of the opposite, in a house so full, so noisy, so demanding, that there was never room for the child to hear herself, and she grew up never having learned to be with herself. In either case, the way out was the same: to do, to fill, to occupy, so as never to have to stay in the hollow. The little girl deduced a double equation in order to survive. If I am useful, I get to stay. If I never stop, I do not have to feel the emptiness where I was left. And the woman you are goes on solving that same equation, decades later, without noticing that the conditions have changed.

There is a deeper layer still, and it was systemic family constellation that opened it to me in a way I did not expect. Behind you there is almost always a line of women. Grandmothers and mothers who only knew how to be worth something by what they gave, who confused love with sacrifice because that was what they were taught, who faded away so that others might live and never knew any other way of existing. Women who could never stop, because stopping, in their time, was a dangerous luxury or a door to pains that were better left unopened. That gesture, of giving until there is nothing left and never staying still, passes from hand to hand like a sacred inheritance, with no one deciding it, like someone honouring a debt she never knew she had signed. Much of your tiredness, and much of your horror of silence, may be, in the end, a loyalty: your body continuing a renunciation that comes from far back, keeping faith with a whole line of women who only knew how to love by wearing themselves out, and who never had the safety to inhabit a silence.

Notice, before we go on, the difference between two ways of seeing that look alike and are opposites. There is the seeing that is thinking about, analysing, dissecting childhood, finding the guilty, building the perfect theory about why you are as you are. That, often, is just the mind using introspection as one more form of control, one more way of not feeling, and you can spend a lifetime understanding yourself without ever actually seeing yourself. There are women who know everything about their tiredness and have never felt it, because the knowing was one more way of not stopping. Seeing, in the sense of this method, is something else, simpler and harder. It is a quiet noticing, almost without words, in which you catch the gesture in the act, at the exact moment it happens, the hand reaching out to do before anyone asked, or reaching for the phone the moment silence arrives, and you stay with it without rushing to judge it or fix it. It is the difference between talking about the sea and getting your feet wet. Seeing hurts a little, always, because it takes from you the anaesthetic of busyness, and beneath it lies, waiting, everything the doing and the filling served to keep you from feeling. But it is that small burning that, in the end, begins to heal.

When you see this, something releases. Because it stops being your fault, it stops being your flaw, and it becomes a chain that you can, at last, recognise and put down. You can honour them without having to repeat them. You can say to them, within, with all tenderness: I saw what you carried, I know what it cost you, I honour you for it. And from me on, this weight rests. At first glance, resting may seem almost a disloyalty, as though by putting down the weight you were saying they were foolish for having carried it. But it is exactly the opposite. The greatest honour you can pay a mother who wore herself out is not to wear yourself out too, in line, behind her. It is to live the rest she did not have. It is to let her sacrifice have served for something, to have opened, in you, the possibility of a lighter life. The women who came before you do not need you to repeat their pain in order to feel honoured. They need the pain to stop in someone. And that someone can be you. Perhaps you are the first of your line able, at last, to come back to yourself, and to stay, in the silence, without fleeing.

I want you to grasp this well, because it is what takes away the guilt, and without taking away the guilt there is no seeing that is not one more way of punishing yourself. The child who learned these equations was not foolish nor weak. She was, on the contrary, astonishingly competent. She looked at the world she had, grasped what guaranteed love and safety there, and organised herself wholly around it. If love came when she was useful, then she became useful. If silence hurt, then she learned never to let it settle. There is nothing pathological in this, there is an admirable survival intelligence, the same one that brought you here whole. The problem is not that you learned the equations. It is that you go on solving them in a world that no longer asks them. And to recognise this is not to renounce the clever girl you were. It is to thank her for what she did, and to tell her, with tenderness, that she can rest now, that now it is you, the adult, who takes care of you both.

There is also, in the root of this movement, a particular fear worth naming, because it tends to appear just when you begin to stop. When stillness settles in after a loss, a fall, the end of a phase, it is not the emptiness that weighs most. It is the subtle presence of a faceless fear. It does not shout nor block, it only remains, like a discreet shadow watching from afar, and it circles the edges of every gesture like a mist that does not lift. This fear is not born of a concrete threat, there is no enemy in front of you nor real danger. It grows from absence, from the vast space that opened when the old forms, the doing and the filling, stopped covering you. And what it guards, deep down, is the old habit of surviving clenched around what has already ended. Notice this, because it changes everything: what haunts you is not what you lost, it is your attachment to what you lost, the hand that goes on closed around something that is no longer there. And a hand closed around nothing can, at last, open. Not to lose more. To be able to receive again.

There is something you need to know about your body, because it takes the guilt off you. Your nervous system does not distinguish past from present. It distinguishes one single thing: safe from threat. When something in today touches that old wound, a request, someone else's need, a silence where it seems to you that you ought to be doing something, it does not ask what year it is nor what house you are in. It reacts as it reacted the first time, and rushes to what it knows, which is to do, to hold, to fill, to anticipate. That is why your hurry to serve, and your hurry to cover the silence, almost never present themselves as error. They present themselves as the most natural, the most obvious, the most you thing to do in that instant. Only later, exhausted, do you think yet again, and you do not understand how you came to carry everything once more, how you came to flee an afternoon that could have been yours. It was not a lack of judgement. It was an old body protecting you from a threat that is no longer there. And it is to that body, not to your willpower, that the protocol which follows is addressed.

## The protocol of coming

Here is the heart of this book, the serious method you can use whenever you feel the movement going off: that urgency to do, to solve, to anticipate, or that hand reaching for the phone, for the remote, to fill the silence. The two are the same impulse, the flight from the hollow, and so they have a single remedy. I call it the protocol of coming, because it is a protocol of return. It is five steps, each with its own name, and together they fit into a couple of minutes. You do not need this book in front of you to do them. With repetition, the body learns them by heart, and you begin to catch the flight earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you.

The golden rule, first of all: this protocol is done with tenderness, never with hardness. It is not one more task to do well, one more exam you can fail. That would be effort itself creeping into the remedy. It does not ask you to resist the flight by willpower, because hardness is incompatible with staying. It asks you something else, more subtle and deeper: to see the flight, to welcome it with tenderness, and to stay just one instant longer than you usually do, in order to discover, with your own body, that stopping does not kill you. It is not a fight against the doing nor against the phone. It is a reunion with yourself. And do not expect to do it cleanly the first times. You will remember it too late, with the dishes already in hand, with the phone already in hand, already in the middle of the flight. That is fine. Even in the middle of the gesture, even afterwards, you can stop and name, and that counts.

I want you to grasp how this works within, because knowing the mechanism helps you not to be frightened by it. Your body learned, long ago, that stopping brought danger, and that silence brought abandonment, and it engraved those lessons in a deep place, beneath words. One does not unlearn such a lesson with an argument, however true. One unlearns it with repeated experience, in the same way it was learned. Each time you stop, and the alarm sounds, and you stay, and nothing bad happens, the body records a new piece of proof: I stopped, I stayed in the silence, and I was loved just the same, and I did not sink. One piece of proof is not enough, because decades of proof to the contrary weigh heavily. But the second adds to the first, and the third to the two, and slowly the scales begin to turn. It is not magic, it is not willpower. It is giving your body, time after time, the chance to discover that today's world is not that of long ago. That is why the path is so patient, and so unspectacular. You are re-educating, drop by drop, a whole body that learned, as a child, that it had to earn the place where it was and to flee the hollow in order to survive.

**First step: Stop and name.** In the instant you feel the urgency to do, to leap, to solve, or the hand reaching out to fill the silence, stop. Just for a second. And say, within, without judging yourself: "here is the flight". Give it the name. Ask yourself, even, which of the two faces it is today: am I fleeing into the doing, or into the noise? It seems little, but it is everything, because a named pattern no longer acts in the dark. The moment you name it, there is already in you someone who observes it, and the one who observes is not wholly taken. This is the first step of any return: to stop the race for the space of a second, and to see where you were running to.

**Second step: Feel the body.** Before you think anything at all, take your attention to the body. Where is the urgency? Almost always it is in the chest, in the throat, in the stomach, in a tightness of the shoulders, in a hurry that lives somewhere beneath the ribs. Do not analyse it. Just locate it, and stay with it for three breaths, slowly. Put, if you like, your hand on the place where it lives. This matters because the flight is not born in the head, it is born in the body, in a physical alarm, and it is in the body that you have to find it in order to calm it. And notice: these three breaths are already the silence you were covering. You do not have to like it. Only not to flee it, for three breaths.

**Third step: Do not do, and stay.** This is the step of the method, and the hardest, because it is the heart of the return. The thing the urgency orders you to do, do not do it. For now. Do not take the dishes from the hand of someone capable of lifting them. Do not answer the message in the exact second. Do not get up to tidy. Do not pick up the phone, do not switch anything on, do not invent a task. Stay. And let the discomfort that comes right after rise up, the guilt, the restlessness, the urge to justify yourself, the dull anguish of the silence settling in. Do not cover that discomfort with another task, nor with noise. Just see it, with your hand on your chest, as one who watches over a frightened child. It is the movement protesting at being interrupted. Each time you stay, without obeying, it loses a little of the power it had over you. And it is here, exactly here, in the staying, that the coming happens. To stop pushing outward, and to come back inward.

**Fourth step: Read the hollow as a lap.** Now, instead of reading the silence and the stopping as threat, offer your fear a new reading. Say, within: this is not the abyss, it is earth. This is not danger, it is space. And more, because it is the deepest truth of this book: this is not abandonment, it is a lap. Put a hand on your chest and feel your own warmth, the heart beating, the body alive beneath the clothes. This gesture is not symbolic, it is practical: it gives your nervous system the signal of safety it was seeking out there, in the doing and the noise and the people, and that was after all here, in you, all along. There is a frightened child behind all of this, and this is the lap you give her. Say to her, slowly, as one who calms someone in the middle of the night: I am not being abandoned. I am with myself. And notice that to say it is already a small miracle: you are, at the same time, the child who was afraid and the woman who now holds her. The hands that all your life held others are now holding you.

**Fifth step: Receive, and listen.** To close, instead of giving more or fleeing, make the opposite gesture: receive. Receive something, however small. Notice a good thing you already have and that you did not have to earn. Accept, that day, a help you would normally refuse. Let yourself stay on the sofa five more minutes, with nothing justifying your presence there. And, if you stayed in the silence, ask it the question you have been fleeing from: and I, what is it that I need? Then, the hardest part, do not answer with the head. Listen. Let the answer rise from the depths, or not come today, without forcing. Say to yourself, slowly, as a truth you are training yourself to believe: I deserve to be here without doing anything to deserve it. You do not have to receive everything at once, nor believe the whole sentence right away. Receive a thread, just one, and leave the rest for what you cannot yet manage. Tomorrow you receive another.

Five steps: stop and name, feel the body, do not do and stay, read the hollow as a lap, receive and listen. That is all, and it fits into a minute or two. Repeat it until the body knows it on its own, and one day, with no fanfare at all, you will notice that you stopped without guilt, that you stayed in the silence without fleeing, that you received without discomfort, and that the sky did not fall. You do not have to do all five each time, especially at the start. There are days when you will manage only the first, stop and name, and that is already much. There are days when you stop at the second. That is fine. The protocol is not an exam to pass, it is a path to walk, step by step, until the body learns that on the other side of the flight there was, waiting, your own lap.

Let me show you how this happens within, in some scene of your day, so that it does not seem abstract. Dinner is over, and the dishes left on the table begin to call you. You feel your body already leaning to get up, almost without thinking. First step: you stop, a second, and you say within, here is the flight, I am leaping into the doing. You have seen it. Second step: you take your attention to the body, and you notice that the urgency lives in your chest, in a hurry that tightens, and you rest your hand there and breathe three times. Third step: you do not get up. You stay. And you let the tightness rise, the guilt, the urge to justify yourself, and, beneath it, the silence you were covering with the task. You do not cover them, you just see them, hand on your chest. Fourth step: you read the hollow as a lap, you say within, this is not danger, it is space, I am not being abandoned, I am with myself, and you feel your own warmth under your hand. Fifth step: instead of serving, you receive a thread, you let someone else get up, or you simply stay seated drinking the rest of your tea, and you ask, slowly, and I, what is it that I need? The dishes wait ten minutes, and the world does not end. That is it, in real life. Small from outside, immense within.

You will have relapses, I want to warn you now, so they do not catch you off guard nor do you take them for failure. There will be days when you do the five steps calmly, and days when the hand reaches for the task or the phone and you only realise half an hour later, already in the middle of the flight. That is not going back to square one. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before, you did not even realise, and now you see yourself doing it, perhaps still in the middle of the gesture. That seeing, earlier and earlier, is the progress, though it does not have the shape you expected. The movement does not disappear from one day to the next. It keeps losing strength, it catches you fewer times and for less time, until one day, with no fanfare at all, you notice that you stopped without even having had to remember to allow it. Be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades, and generations, to weave.

## The practices

Before the practices, stay with these questions, and do not hurry to cover what they stir. They are not for answering quickly, they are to sit with you for a few days. What do you fear will happen if you stop doing, solving, anticipating? What does the silence at home make you flee from? If you stayed in the emptiness without filling it, what would you be afraid to discover inside? Who would take care of you if you stopped, for a moment, being the one who takes care of everything? And from whom did you learn, back then, that your place was to give until there was nothing left of you, and that to stop was danger? Notice which of these questions tightens you most. It is usually, precisely, the one that points to your root.

The protocol is for the moments when the movement goes off, in the fire of urgency. These practices are for the calmer days, to soften the knot from within, slowly, away from the urgency. You do not have to do them all. Choose one, live with it for a few days, and only then move to another. The body learns by repetition, not by quantity. And do me a favour, before you begin: do not turn these practices into a new list of tasks, a new duty at which you can grade and fail yourself. It would be the perfect irony, effort taking hold even of what came to undo it. Do one, now and then, when you remember, with curiosity and not obligation. If you forget it for a whole week, nothing bad has happened.

**One. The thing you do not do.** This one is small from outside and immense within, and it is perhaps the hardest practice in this book. Choose one small thing you would do out of habit to serve, to please or to get ahead with others' work, and today, only today, do not do it. Just one. Then, stay attentive to the discomfort that rises almost at once, and do not solve it, do not cover it with another task nor with noise. Just see it, with your hand on your chest, three breaths. Notice that, beneath the guilt, is the silence the doing used to cover. Stay with that too. On the other side of that discomfort, and closer each time you practise, is your rest.

**Two. The two minutes with the silence.** It is the opposite face of the previous practice, and perhaps the most powerful of all. The next time the silence brings you that familiar anguish, do not fill it. Do not pick up the phone, do not switch anything on, do not invent a task. Stay two minutes, just two, with a clock beside you if you need to know they will end. Put your hand on your chest, feel your own warmth, and say, slowly, as one who calms a child in the middle of the night: this is not danger, it is space; this is not abandonment, it is a lap. Let the emptiness be earth, and not abyss. Two minutes, and then you can get up, if you like. You will discover, perhaps by the second or third time, that it did not swallow you, and that two minutes that seemed impossible after all fit inside you. With time, and only with time, you can let the two become five, and the five ten.

**Three. The training of receiving.** Once a day, let yourself receive something without giving it back. A compliment you accept with a simple thank you, without deflecting it or diminishing it. A help you accept without paying it back twofold straight away. A door someone opens for you, and you go through, without ten thanks. Notice what this does to you within, the tightness, the urge to compensate. Stay with it. You are teaching your body a new truth, and it is the central truth of the return: that you can receive without owing, that being cared for does not diminish you, it completes you, and that there is a lap you can return to without having to deserve it first.

**Four. The hands in the lap.** A practice for the body, simple and deep, and it is the heart of everything this book wants to give you. Sit in silence, with nothing to do, and rest both hands, open, on your own lap. The hands that all your life caught others before the fall, now resting on you. Stay like that for the space of a few slow breaths, and in each one feel that you are doing nothing, and that everything is fine. If restlessness rises, let it rise and pass, without getting up. If the silence you usually cover rises, stay with it too. You are teaching your hands, and your whole body, that there is a place where they can, at last, rest, and that that place is you. The hands that held, finally held by your own lap.

**Five. The question of the spring.** Once a week, in a moment of stillness you choose, sit with paper and pen and ask yourself the question this movement makes you flee, by doing or by filling: what is it that I, only I, truly like? And what is it that I need? And write, without censoring, whatever comes, however small it seems. A tea at a certain hour. A piece of music. A walk. A morning silence. It is not to solve your life, it is to hear your own voice again, the one that falls quiet when you are always full of people and tasks. The spring was always at the bottom of the silence you were covering. This practice is the rope you lower down to it.

**Six. The hand that opens.** This one is for the days of loss, of endings, of falling, when stillness comes accompanied by that faceless fear. Sit a moment and notice what your hand, within, still holds. What ended and that you go on clinging to. The relationship that already ended, the time that already passed, the life that already was and does not return, the role of the indispensable one you are afraid to give up. Imagine your hand closed around that, for so long that it has begun to hurt. And, slowly, without hurry, open one finger. Just one. Not everything, not by force. Say within: I can open my hand, not to lose more, but to be able to receive again. A hand closed around nothing cannot hold anything new, nor can it rest in a lap. Open one finger, today, and leave the rest for the days to come.

**Seven. The meal in silence.** This one is simple and everyday, and it fits into any life. Choose one meal in the week, just one, and have it in silence and without a screen. No television, no phone resting beside the plate, no radio. Just you, the food, and your own presence. Notice the taste, which usually escapes you when you eat while doing something else. Notice how your hand pulls you to the phone at the first pause, and do not give in. It teaches you, mouthful by mouthful, that you can be with yourself for fifteen minutes with nothing over the top of it, and come out whole. Where you learn to be alone eating, you go on learning to be alone living.

**Eight. The minute of the veil.** Once a day, at night perhaps, stop a minute and ask: where was the flight in front of my eyes today? Where did I run to do when I could have stayed, where did I cover the silence when I could have stayed in it? Not to correct yourself, only to see. Name the moment, thank the old movement for the ancient protection it once gave you, and release one thread. One minute a day. It is enough that, after a few weeks, you begin to catch the flight in the act, earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you.

**Nine. The letter to the women who came before.** This one is slower, and it is done once, or from time to time, when you need it. Write a letter, by hand, to a woman of your family who wore herself out giving, or who could never stop, your mother, a grandmother, one you never even knew but heard spoken of. Tell her that you saw. That you know what she carried, in what time, in what house, with no choice, and that you know she never had the safety to rest nor to inhabit a silence. Thank her for what her sacrifice opened for you. And then, at the end, say to her the sentence that frees: from me on, this weight rests. I honour you by living the rest you did not have. You do not have to send this letter to anyone. It is only yours. But notice what moves in you as you write it, because it is there, more than in the head, that the old loyalty begins to release.

## Seven days returning

Here is a short programme, one gesture a day, for seven days. Each day builds on the one before, and together they trace the whole movement of coming: to stop pushing, and to come back to yourself. They are small on purpose, because that is how the body learns, slowly and in safety, without one more effort for it to resist. You do not have to do them perfectly. You only have to do them. And at night, if you can, write two lines about the day, not to grade yourself, only to notice what moved: what discomfort rose, and where in the body; what fear crossed your mind when you stopped; what small good thing happened when you did not do what you usually do. To write is not one more task, it is a way of seeing.

**Day one: name the flight.** Today you change nothing. You only notice. Throughout the day, catch yourself in the act each time you rush to push: anticipating, solving what no one asked of you, switching on the television the moment you walk in, picking up the phone for no reason, inventing a task in the instant of peace. Say within, each time: "here is the flight". Do not judge yourself, only count. At bedtime, you know how many times you fled, and to where. It is your map. One does not release what one has not first seen.

**Day two: the body.** Today, besides naming, take your attention to the body each time. Where does the urgency live? In the chest, in the throat, in the shoulders? Put your hand there, and breathe three times before doing anything at all, before giving in to the flight. You are learning to find the movement in the place where it actually is born, and to give yourself three breaths of silence where before there were none.

**Day three: not doing one thing.** Today you choose one small thing you would do out of habit, and you do not do it. Just one. Let the discomfort rise and stay with it, hand on your chest, without covering it. Notice that nothing bad happened, and notice, above all, the silence that opened in the place of the task. Do not flee it. Note down, at night, how it went.

**Day four: staying in the silence.** Today you do the two-minute practice, once, in the moment the silence brings you anguish, or when you arrive home, without switching anything on. Hand on your chest, and stay. This is not danger, it is space; this is not abandonment, it is a lap. Two minutes with a clock if you need it. Notice how the anguish, which seemed to rise without end, at a certain point begins to settle. It is your first piece of proof, in the body and not in the idea, that the hollow does not swallow you.

**Day five: receiving.** Today you let yourself receive one thing without giving it back. A compliment, a help, a gesture of care. Accept it with a simple thank you, and stay with the tightness that comes after, without compensating. Today you train the hardest account to settle, and the central gesture of the return: to let them reach you, instead of always being the one who reaches others.

**Day six: the question and the lap.** Today, in a moment of stillness you choose, sit with your hands resting in your own lap and ask, and listen: and I, what is it that I need? And what is it that I, only I, truly like? Write whatever comes, however small. Not to solve, to hear yourself. Today you give the voice back to the one who was kept quiet, and you give yourself the lap you were seeking out there.

**Day seven: resting.** Today, ten minutes only for you, with nothing to do, with nothing justifying your presence there, with no noise over the top. Open hands in your lap. Breathe slowly. And let rise the truth you have been training all week: that your worth is not at stake, that there is no account to settle, that the silence is not an abyss, and that you deserve to be here without doing anything to deserve it. Notice how you are no longer quite the same woman as on the first day. You have come back a little to yourself. And the lap you came back to has been open all along.

At the end of these seven days you will not be cured, because you are not ill, and because a pattern of decades does not undo itself in a week. But you will have begun. You will have stopped without the sky falling, you will have stayed in the silence without it swallowing you, you will have received without dying. And that, however small it seems, is the whole door. The rest releases in its own time, as you repeat it, with tenderness, until the body believes. And if one week is not enough, repeat it, whenever life fills up again and the return becomes a stranger once more.

## What changes

What changes, when this movement is complete, is first of all your body. The chronic tiredness begins to ease, and not because you came to do less, but because you stopped carrying the permanent vigilance of being responsible for everyone's life, and because you stopped living in flight, and flight is tiring even when it does not call itself that. The weight that sleep did not cure was that one, and it loosens when you set down the sentinel and stop running away from yourself.

You stop being afraid of yourself. The silence, which was threat, becomes refuge. You come to know how to be alone without feeling abandoned, and to be at rest without feeling useless, and those are two of the greatest freedoms a woman can win, because they free everything else. You learn to receive, and you discover, with surprise, that to receive does not diminish you, it completes you, that to let yourself be cared for does not take away your worth, it gives you company. You begin to say "no" without a whole speech of justification behind it, and the sky does not fall.

Your relationships, curiously, improve. Because they stop resting on your exhaustion and your terror of emptiness, and come to rest on your true presence. When you no longer cling to anyone out of terror of being alone, and no longer hold everyone out of terror of ceasing to be needed, you come to be with people by choice and not by flight. Those around you stop having before them an exhausted woman who carries everything and charges for it in silence, or a woman who hangs on with a weight that smothers, and come to have a whole woman, who is there because she likes to be. Clinging love drives away. The love of one who is whole, even alone, is the only one that breathes.

You find again the liking for simple things that are only yours, that you had forgotten by being always full of people and tasks. The quiet morning before the house wakes. The walk with no one. A bath without the phone beside it. And you will notice that you were not, after all, a woman without likings nor a woman without depth. You were a woman who had buried herself beneath so much doing and so much noise that she no longer heard herself call.

Your relationship with rest also changes, and it is a deep change. Where before rest brought you guilt or alarm, it begins to bring you relief. You stop needing to deserve the pause, to justify it with extreme tiredness, to steal it like one doing something forbidden. You discover the difference, which you had never known, between the solitude that is suffered and the withdrawal that is chosen. That morning hour that is only yours, that late afternoon seated at the window with nothing to fill it, stop being abandonment and become plenty. And you discover, too, that the rest I speak of is not sleeping more nor taking a holiday, though you may need both. It is a way of being, and its heart is to be able to exist, for a few instants at least, without justifying your presence with any usefulness, and without fleeing it into the noise. It can happen in a two-minute pause in the middle of a full day. And it may not happen in three weeks of holiday, if the three weeks are one more thing to manage. Rest is not a question of how long you stop. It is a question of, in stopping, allowing yourself to come back to yourself.

Love, which for so long you confused with doing, with serving, with holding, with always being available, reveals itself in the end to be something much simpler, and much harder for you: being. Love stops being carrying. It becomes being. Being whole, proving nothing, fleeing nothing, beside those you love and beside yourself. And, at the bottom of that emptiness you so feared, and at the end of all that race that never stopped, you find not the abyss, but a lap. The hands, which all your life caught others before the fall and never stopped moving, can finally rest. And for the first time, you, who all your life held, are held. By no one else. By yourself. And you discover that that lap, your own, was open all along, waiting for you, while you ran everywhere except to it.

## To close

You have come this far, and perhaps you did it in a rush, wanting to finish the book as you finish tasks, to cross it off the list. If that was so, smile, and see in it the last wave of the old movement, still trying to turn even this into something to do well and fast. And then put the book down, without finishing it the way one fulfils a duty. Inhabit it. Come back to it. The books that matter are not read once, they accompany us, and they tell us new things at each age of the soul.

I want to leave you with the whole truth of this movement, condensed into a single line you can carry with you: you do not have to do to be loved, nor to fill so as not to feel alone. You never had to. The doing and the filling were the cleverest strategy of a little girl who needed to survive, and they fulfilled their function with honour. But the threat has passed, and you can, at last, stop pushing and come back to yourself. What you were seeking out ahead, at the bottom of one more list, and what you feared finding in the silence, were the same thing, and the same surprise: a lap. It was here all along, within, still, waiting for you.

This is the second of three movements. First the **seeing**, recognising what binds you. Then this one, the **coming**, coming back to yourself and putting down. And finally the **living**, inhabiting life again, free, from the lap out into the world. The three make a single crossing, and the veils we looked at here, the Effort and the Desolation, are interwoven with the others, with the identity that defines itself wholly by a role, with the mind that never switches off, with the horizon that postpones life to a later that never comes. To remove this knot usually loosens the others. The whole map, the seven veils and the way they connect, is in *The Seven Veils*, the mother-book from which this one was born, and it waits for you, in whatever time is yours. There is no right order for returning home.

If there is a single gesture you take from these pages into your life, let it be this, and it is the smallest and the hardest of all: the next time that familiar urge rises in you to get up and do one more thing, or to cover the silence with something, stay. Just that. Stay, with your hand on your chest, one instant longer than usual. Not to prove anything, not even to do this book well. Stay because you deserve to, and because you always deserved to, even in the years when no one told you. Each time you stay, you come back a little to yourself, and you teach your body, better than a thousand words, that the danger has passed, that you can stop, and that there is a lap waiting for you. Your own.

I am with you, at the same pace. And I wish for you, from the depths, the simplest and hardest thing I know: that you stop pushing, that you come back to yourself, and that you discover, on the other side of the fear, that you were, all that time, waiting for yourself, with open arms.
