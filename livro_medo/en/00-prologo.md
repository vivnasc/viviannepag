# PROLOGUE
## The Invisible Gravity

There is a force you never see and that, even so, bends almost everything you do.

You do not feel it as fear. You feel it as good sense when you do not take the risk, as responsibility when you stay, as love when you sacrifice yourself, as high standards when you refuse to rest. It always has a beautiful name, and that is its greatest talent: to pass itself off as virtue so as not to be recognized.

Most books promise to help you conquer fear. This one does not. To conquer presumes an enemy on the outside, and what we are about to see is something else. Fear does not stand before you like an obstacle. It lies beneath you, like the ground, so present that you have stopped noticing it. It does not only arrange the moments when you tremble. It arranges the whole days in which you feel perfectly calm and yet always choose what is familiar over what is possible.

The question of this book is not how to rid yourself of it. It is more uncomfortable, and therefore more useful: how much of what you call your personality is, in the end, an adaptation to fear? When you say "this is just how I am," how much of that is you and how much is the shape you found, very early on, so as not to be left out, not to be undone, not to be erased?

We will see that fear has six faces you have learned to call by other names, and one root that gives rise to them all. And we will see that this root always takes the same form: the dread of separating, of ceasing to belong, of ceasing to be. Before I name it, let me only make one request of you. Do not read this to correct yourself. Read it to see yourself. Seeing is the one thing fear cannot do to itself, and that is why it lasts so long.
