# CHAPTER 4
## Uncertainty · The Fortress

Think of the last time someone said to you "let's see what happens" and notice what you felt. For many people, those words are a relief. For you, they may have been a small jolt, an immediate urge to make a plan, to close the matter, to know now. If you know that jolt, you know this face. It fears losing no one and nothing in particular. It fears something more abstract and therefore harder to name: it fears the unknown. It fears not knowing. It fears the ground of the future, which is always made of mist, and it spends its life trying to build upon the mist something solid where it can set its feet. I call it the Fortress.

### What It Protects

Uncertainty fears the loss of the predictable, of the map, of the firm ground to walk on without jolts. It is a fear that seems very civilised, very grown-up, and yet it comes from the same ancient place as the others. An animal that does not know what comes next is an animal in danger. Knowing where the water is, where the predator comes from, when the rain arrives, that is the difference between living and dying. The body learned that predictability is safety and that surprise is threat, and so the unknown, even the harmless unknown, sets off in it a muffled alarm. Your need to know is not a quirk. It is an ancient system doing the work it has always done, which was to keep you alive in a world where not knowing could cost you your life.

Catch the Fortress on any evening, at its most common work. Everything is fine. The day went well, the people you love are home, there is no threat in sight. And yet you do not rest. Your mind has already gone off to tomorrow, to the week, to what could go wrong, and it runs through, without your asking, a list of things left unresolved, of scenarios to guard against, of possible dangers. The body is on the sofa, the mind is in an emergency meeting about a future that has not yet arrived and that perhaps will never arrive as you imagine it. You lose the evening, which was good, preparing a day that is not yet, and when that day comes, you will lose it too, preparing the next one. Notice the gentle absurdity of this. There is no fire, and the Fortress keeps you on guard beside the bucket, all night long, against a blaze that is not burning. This is the most constant price of this face, not the great crises, but the thousand calm evenings it will not let you inhabit, because it is always watching what is coming, and what is coming, when it comes, is almost always less than what it feared, and the peace of the evenings, that peace, does not come back.

What changed was the world, not the system. Today most of the uncertainties you face do not kill you. Not knowing how a conversation will go, a project, a year, a decision, none of this is the predator in the thicket. But the body cannot tell it apart from the predator, and it treats the mist of the future with the same urgency it would treat a real threat. The Fortress is what you build so as not to have to feel that urgency. Walls of plans, of routines, of control, of rules, all to create the illusion that you know what is coming, and so to keep the alarm quiet one more day.

Notice a difference between this face and the others, because it explains why the Fortress is so hard to let go of. The other faces fear the loss of something concrete, the group, the person, the bread. This one fears something that cannot be eliminated at all, because uncertainty is not an accident of life, it is the very condition of being alive in time. No one knows what comes next, no one, however much they plan, and that is a law of existence, not a flaw to be corrected. The Fortress has declared war on something that cannot be defeated, and so its war has no end. It can win battle after battle, cover scenario after scenario, and uncertainty is still there, intact, because it was never in an enemy that can be beaten, it was in the nature of the future, which is made of mist by definition and not by carelessness.

It is worth distinguishing two things the Fortress confuses, because it is in that confusion that it loses itself: control and influence. There are things over which you have influence, and there are things over which you have control, and they are very different. You can influence your health with what you eat and how you live, but you do not control it, because a body does what it does. You can influence the outcome of a piece of work with your effort, but you do not control it, because a thousand things depend on it that are not in your hands. You can influence how a child grows, but you do not control it, because he is a free person. Almost everything that matters in life is in the realm of influence, not of control, and health lies in doing your part well, the part of influence, and handing over the rest, the part that was never yours. The Fortress does not accept this division, it wants to control what it can only influence, and so it lives in a perpetual frustration, trying to hold in its hands a thing that, by definition, slips through its fingers. Peace is not in controlling more. It is in knowing, with clarity, what is yours to do and what is not, in putting all your strength into your part and in letting go, with a kind of trust, of everything else, which was never at your disposal however much you fret.

And there is an irony at the heart of this face that matters to see, because it disarms its own promise. The Fortress promises you that, if you control enough, you will be safe, and yet the people who control the most are among the most anxious there are, not the most tranquil. If control gave the safety it promises, whoever controlled most would be whoever lived most at peace, and it is exactly the opposite: the more you control, the more anxious you become, because the more things come to depend on you, the more fronts you have to watch, the more you notice everything that could still go wrong. Control does not cure anxiety, it feeds it, because each thing you bring under your control becomes one more thing you are responsible for, one more thing that could fail you. The safety the Fortress seeks in control lies, after all, in the opposite place, in the capacity to let go, to trust, to let a part of life run without your supervision, and that is the thing the Fortress least knows how to do, and so it spends its life building walls in search of a peace that would only come from letting them fall.

### The Turn

What you call being organised, responsible, prudent, having your feet firmly on the ground, is often the Fortress governing you. And it is one of the most praised faces, because uncertainty terrifies everyone and whoever controls it seems strong. We admire the one who has everything planned, who is never caught off guard, who has an answer for every eventuality. We do not see the price that person pays within, the tiredness of never being able to lower the guard, the rigidity that prevents her from welcoming the unexpected when the unexpected brings, as it so often does, the best of life.

Look at what the Fortress makes you refuse. It refuses you spontaneity, because spontaneity is uncertainty coming in through a door you did not control. It refuses you change, even good change, because all change is a passage through the unknown. It refuses you the rest of not knowing, which is a deep rest and one known only to those who have made peace with the mist. The person who lives in the Fortress is always working, even when she seems still, because her mind is permanently anticipating scenarios, preparing responses, plugging holes through which the unforeseen might enter. It is an invisible and infinite work, because the future produces uncertainty faster than any plan can eliminate it.

And here is the turn that hurts. The Fortress promises you control, and what it gives you, in truth, is a smaller life. Because the only way to reduce uncertainty is to reduce life, and the Fortress reduces life without telling you. You do not take that trip, because it is unpredictable. You do not accept that opportunity, because you did not plan it. You do not meet that person, you do not change that thing, you do not risk the new, because the new is always uncertain. So, battle by battle, the Fortress goes on walling in your life, and every wall that protects you from a surprise is also a wall that closes off a possibility, and after years you live in a fortress so safe and so small that it is almost no longer a life, it is a refuge, and the refuge, which should have guarded life, ends up replacing it.

And notice what the Fortress does to the good surprise, because it is here that you see best what it costs you. Life brings, now and then, gifts you did not ask for or plan, a chance meeting, an unexpected invitation, a turn of events that opens a door you did not even know existed. Almost everything best that has happened to you, if you think about it, arrived that way, without your having planned it, through a crack that opened in the plan. But the Fortress does not know how to receive a surprise, even a good one, because a surprise is uncertainty, it is something that came in without passing through control, and so its first reaction to the unexpected, even the good unexpected, is alarm, mistrust, the urge to refuse it or to fit it quickly into some plan that tames it. So, without knowing it, the Fortress closes the very door through which the greatest joys of life would enter, joys that almost never come through the expected door, and condemns itself to an existence without surprises, which is the same as saying without almost anything that makes a life worth living, because the predictable, however safe, is rarely what fills us, and what fills us, almost always, is what we did not see coming.

### How It Operates Within

The mechanism of this face lives on a promise that is never kept: the promise that, if you plan enough, you will finally arrive at certainty. The Fortress believes that uncertainty is a problem with a solution, and that the solution is more information, more preparation, more control. So it plans, and the plan soothes for a moment, and then the future presents a variable the plan did not foresee, and the Fortress's response is not to accept that the future is like this, it is to make a bigger plan. It grows wall upon wall, and the mist is never left out, because the mist is not out there, it is in the very nature of the time that has not yet come.

There is a cruel mechanism inside this face, and it is called rumination. The mind caught in uncertainty does not rest examining a problem, it circles it without advancing, it produces the same thought over and over with the illusion that, by giving it one more turn, it will reach a certainty that closes it. It never reaches it, because the question has no right answer, it has only possible futures, and no amount of thought turns a possible future into a known future. Rumination is the Fortress building walls inside its own head, corridors and corridors of worry that protect against nothing and that consume the energy real life was asking for something else.

It is worth seeing the trick that rumination plays on you, because it is subtle. While you think and rethink a problem, you feel you are doing something about it, working to solve it, and so rumination disguises itself as responsibility, as taking care of the matter. But there is a difference between thinking about a problem and ruminating on it, and it is easy to see: thought advances, arrives at a decision, at an action, at a step, and then it stops. Rumination does not advance, it circles, returns to the same point, repeats itself, and after an hour you are exactly where you began, only more tired and more frightened. Thought is a tool, you use it and put it down. Rumination is the tool running empty, wearing you out without cutting anything, giving you the sensation that you are working when you are only being consumed. Recognising the difference is halfway to being able to switch off the machine that turns without grinding.

Rigidity is the other cost, and it is more visible from outside than from within. Whoever lives in the Fortress needs things to be a certain way, and the deviation from that way, however small, provokes a disproportionate unease. The plan that changes at the last minute, the routine that is broken, the rule someone does not respect, all of this is not a minor inconvenience, it is a crack in the wall, and each crack lets in a thread of the mist the Fortress exists to keep out. That is why these people seem, at times, difficult, controlling, incapable of flowing. They are not. They are terrified of something no one around them sees, because the thing is not in the room, it is in the future, and they live more in the future than in the present they have in front of them.

There is one last piece, and it is the saddest, because it concerns the lost present. The Fortress lives so busy protecting the future that it almost never inhabits the now. You are at a dinner with those you love, and a part of you is organising the week. You are on holiday, and your mind is already preparing the return. You are living a good moment, and instead of living it, you worry about how to keep it, how to ensure it continues, what to do if it ends. So the present, which was the only thing you truly had, drains away through your fingers, spent managing a future that, when it arrives, will also be lived managing the next future. It is a whole life spent in the antechamber of life, waiting for uncertainty to end so that it can finally be lived, without seeing that uncertainty never ends, and that the antechamber where it waited was, itself, life passing by.

There is a phrase that is the secret engine of this face, and perhaps you recognise it because you say it often within: what if. What if it goes wrong, what if he gets sick, what if I lose my job, what if I say the wrong thing, what if I am not able. The what if is the Fortress's factory of futures, and it works without rest, producing scenarios of disaster with a realism that makes them seem probable when they are only possible. Notice what the what if does to your body. It does not distinguish an imagined danger from a real one, and so, when you imagine disaster vividly, your body reacts as if it were happening, and you suffer the pain of a thing that did not happen and that, in the overwhelming majority of cases, will never happen. So whoever lives in the what if suffers a thousand disasters that never come, and pays in advance a price for catastrophes that life, almost always, does not charge. It is said that whoever fears suffers twice, and it is literally true: once in the imagination, always, and again in reality, rarely, and the second almost never arrives, but the first never fails. The Fortress believes that anticipating disaster prepares it for disaster. What it does, in most cases, is make it live the disaster in advance, with none of the advantages of its being real, with all the pain and none of the facts.

There is a second piece in the mechanism, and it is the one that makes the Fortress so exhausting without its seeing why: control is insatiable exactly as comparison and recognition were, in the other faces. Each thing you manage to control soothes for an instant, and in the next instant your attention leaps to the next thing to control, and there is never a point of arrival, because the number of things that can go wrong is infinite and your control is finite. It is a war on many fronts at once, in which closing one front opens attention to another, and peace never comes because peace would require all the fronts to be closed at once, and that, in an uncertain world, never happens. That is why the Fortress does not rest even when everything is fine, because its work is not to respond to a present danger, it is to watch all possible dangers, and that watch has no end, it consumes it from within without there being, on the outside, any threat to justify so much wear.

### The Gallery of Masks

In practical life, the Fortress is the calendar with no free days, the house where each thing has its exact place and where disorder provokes an anxiety that far exceeds the disorder, the trip planned to the minute that leaves no room for chance, where the best of a trip usually dwells. We call these people organised, and they are, but organisation, when it is Fortress, has stopped being a tool at the service of life and become a cage in which less and less of life fits.

In the head, the Fortress produces a mask that has become epidemic: the thirst to know now. You cannot bear an open question, a matter without an answer, a doubt left hanging. You need to resolve, to close, to know now, and if you do not know, you search, you research, you ask, until you obtain an answer, whatever it may be, because the uncertainty of the open question is more unbearable than a hasty answer, even a wrong one. We live surrounded by instruments that feed this hunger, that promise an immediate answer to everything, and the Fortress clings to them, consults without stopping, informs itself compulsively, in the illusion that more information will finally put it safe from not knowing. It does not. Most of life's questions do not close with information, they stay open because it is in their nature to stay, and the thirst to know now, by demanding an immediate closure, either contents itself with false answers that quiet it for an instant, or circles endlessly in search of a certainty that does not come. Learning to leave a question open, to live with a doubt without rushing to close it, to bear not knowing without filling it with hasty answers, is one of the deepest disciplines against this face, because it is the exact opposite of what it asks, and it is where the patience it never had resides.

And there is a mask of the Fortress within thought itself, subtle and dangerous: black-and-white. Because nuance is uncertain, because the maybe is uncomfortable, because grey gives no firm ground to set the foot, the Fortress prefers simple certainties to complex truths. It divides the world into good and bad, right and wrong, ours and theirs, because that sharp division gives a safety that reality, full of shades, denies. It is easier to have a firm and closed opinion than to sustain the uncertainty of a question that has reason on several sides, and so the Fortress clings to absolute positions, to certainties that let no doubt in, not because they are true, but because they are firm, and firmness soothes the alarm better than truth. Notice how this impoverishes thought and hardens the heart: a person who needs simple certainties becomes incapable of understanding the complexity of others, of changing her mind, of bearing the discomfort of not having a closed opinion about everything. The maturity of thought, like that of life, is measured by the capacity to inhabit the grey without anguish, to sustain two truths in tension, to say I do not know when one does not know, and that maturity is precisely what the Fortress sacrifices in the name of the safety of easy certainties.

In health and in the body, this face dresses itself in a vigilance that is confused with care. It is the one who reads every symptom as a threat, who needs to know everything, who does not rest until she has a diagnosis that closes the uncertainty, even when waiting would be wiser. The body is, by nature, a territory of uncertainty, no one knows for sure what their body will do tomorrow, and that is one of the deepest sources of anguish for whoever lives in the Fortress. The obsession with controlling health is often less about health and more about the intolerance of not knowing. And there is a cruelty in this mask, because anxious vigilance over the body does not make it healthier, it makes it more tense, and tension is, itself, one of the things that most sickens the body. The Fortress, in trying to secure health by controlling it, undermines it with the very anxiety of control, and so, once again, it fabricates a little of what it feared.

There is an ancient mask of the Fortress that survives even in people who think themselves rational: rituals and superstitions of control. It is the gesture you have to make for it to go well, the order you cannot break, the small private ceremony that gives you the illusion of holding the uncontrollable. Touch wood, do not say that out loud for it brings bad luck, it has to be done this way or something happens. These rituals have no power over the world, and a part of you knows it, but you do them anyway, because they do not serve to control the world, they serve to soothe the anguish of not controlling it, they give your hands something to do before the mist, an action that replaces impotence with a gesture. They are harmless in small doses, and almost everyone has their own. They become a prison when they grow, when the person becomes hostage to them, when the order has to be followed to the letter or the anxiety spikes, because then they are no longer a small comfort, they are the Fortress building magic walls against an imagined danger, an attempt to buy safety from a universe that does not accept that kind of payment.

And there is the most productive mask of all, the one that disguises itself as virtue and so no one suspects it: eternal preparation that never passes into action. It is the person who studies endlessly before beginning, who takes one more course, who reads one more book, who researches a little more, always preparing to act one day, and the day does not come, because to act is to enter uncertainty and to prepare is to stay in the safe. The Fortress loves this mask because it looks like responsibility, it looks like rigour, it looks like the person is doing things well, when what she is doing is indefinitely postponing the leap, using preparation as a wall against the uncontrollable moment when one has to act without guarantees. No preparation, however great, eliminates the uncertainty of action, because action happens in the real world, which is unpredictable, and not in books, which are safe. At a certain point, one has to act without being fully ready, to leap without being sure of the landing, and it is precisely that leap which the Fortress, disguised as a diligent student, spends its life postponing, gaining knowledge and losing life, ever more prepared for an action that, from being so prepared, never comes to happen.

In decisions, the Fortress produces its most paralysing mask: the inability to choose without certainty. Because no important decision comes with a guarantee of outcome, the person caught in this face postpones, weighs indefinitely, seeks more information, asks for more opinions, and life passes while she waits for a certainty that no decision ever offers. Not to decide is also a decision, that of letting time choose for you, but the Fortress prefers it because that way it does not carry the responsibility of having chosen in the dark, which is the only way real choices are ever made. And the price of this indecision is high, because doors do not stay open forever waiting for the Fortress to gather the certainty that will never come. They close with time, one by one, and the person who did not choose for fear of choosing badly discovers, later, that life chose for her, and almost always worse than she would have chosen, because it left her with the scraps of what remained after so much postponing.

With money, the Fortress has a mask that is confused with that of scarcity but is another, and it is worth distinguishing them. Scarcity fears going without; the Fortress fears not knowing. One wants to gather against the winter; the other wants to foresee, to control, to guarantee against surprise. It is the person who needs to have everything planned to the last cent, who cannot bear an unforeseen expense not because she cannot pay it but because it escaped the plan, who protects herself against every possible risk, who multiplies the insurances, the emergency funds, the precautions, beyond what any real risk would justify, in an attempt to buy a financial certainty that life does not sell. And, as with everything else, it is never enough, because the number of things that can go wrong with money is infinite, and each one guarded against leaves another unguarded, and the person lives in an endless financial vigilance that prevents her from enjoying what she has, not out of greed, but out of intolerance to uncertainty. The difference is felt: the miser does not spend because he fears going without; the one who lives in the Fortress does not spend without having everything foreseen because she fears surprise, loss of control, the unforeseen that escapes the plan. One is cured by learning that there is enough; the other, by learning that one can live without foreseeing everything, that a part of money, like a part of life, has to be left to chance, and that whoever wants to control every cent of the future spends the present doing sums instead of living it.

In faith, and this is a subtle mask, uncertainty can disguise itself as rigid religiosity, of the kind that needs to have every answer closed and cannot bear mystery. There is a spirituality that flourishes in uncertainty, that makes of not knowing a form of humility and openness, and there is another that uses religion exactly as it uses everything else, to build a wall of certainties against the unknown, to turn mystery, which is the heart of any living faith, into a list of guaranteed truths where no question fits any longer. The first is faith. The second is the Fortress dressed as faith, and it is recognised because it fears questions, and living faith has never feared questions. A faith that needs everything answered is not strong, it is fragile, because a single unanswered question threatens to make it collapse, and so it has to forbid questions to hold itself up. True faith is the one that can live with mystery without closing it, that sustains not knowing about the last things with a trust that needs no certainties, and that is the exact opposite of the Fortress, it is the courage to inhabit the mist instead of walling it out.

And there is, as always, the nearest mask, the one you may be feeling at this very moment. It is the difficulty of being in a present you do not control. It is the inability to enjoy a good day without already worrying about the next one. It is the background tension that will not leave you, that sense that you must always be ready for something, even if you do not know what for. That tension is the Fortress mounting guard against an attack that does not come, and the price it charges is your capacity to inhabit your own life while it happens, instead of spending it all preparing one that has not yet arrived.

In love, the Fortress has a mask that ruins many relationships without anyone realising what happened. It is the need to know where the relationship is going, to define it, to guarantee its future before being able to live it. Where is this going, what are we, are we going to stay together forever, promise me you will not change. These are human questions, and some need an answer, but when they become a permanent demand for certainty, they push out of the present the only place where love in fact happens, which is now. No one can guarantee the future of a relationship, because people change, life changes, and a living love is precisely something that is recreated every day, not a contract signed once and for all. The Fortress, by demanding the guarantee, asks of love something love cannot give, and by not receiving it, is anguished, and from anguish is born control, and from control suffocation, and the love one wanted to guarantee forever dies in the present from an excess of future. To love is, in great part, to accept loving without a guarantee, to give oneself to a free person whose tomorrow you do not control, and that is one of the highest courages there is, precisely the one that whoever lives in this face lacks.

And if you are a mother, the Fortress has waiting for you perhaps its most painful trap, because a child is the greatest territory of uncertainty a person can love. You do not know who he will be, what he will suffer, what path he will follow, and that not-knowing, about the person you love most in the world, is almost unbearable for one who needs to know. So the maternal Fortress tries to control the uncontrollable: it plans the child's life, chooses for him, protects him from all uncertainty, tries to guarantee the outcome of a person, which is the least guaranteeable thing there is. And the child, raised within this control, either rebels against the cage, or, worse, internalises it, and becomes himself incapable of bearing not knowing, inheriting the Fortress the way one inherits the colour of one's eyes. The most mature mother's love does the opposite of what fear asks: it prepares the child to live in uncertainty, not to avoid it, gives him tools for the unknown instead of trying to eliminate the unknown, because it knows it will not always be there to wall his path, and that the greatest gift it can give him is not a path without surprises, it is the confidence that he will be able to face them when they come, as they will.

### The Edge

Now the caution, without which this lens would become an accusation against every prudent person. Not all planning is fear. Not all organisation is Fortress. Intelligent foresight is one of the greatest achievements of the human mind, and to despise it would be a romantic foolishness.

To plan is good. To prepare is wise. To think before acting is what distinguishes us and what allows us to build things that last longer than an impulse. A person who organises her life, who guards against real risks, who makes plans and keeps them, is not necessarily afraid, she may simply be using well the intelligence she has. There is a genuine joy in order, a legitimate pleasure in having things in their place, a real competence in anticipating problems before they become crises. None of this is illness. To confuse all competence with fear would be to replace one tyranny with another.

The sign that distinguishes the Fortress from wise planning is, once again, interior, and it has to do with what happens after the plan is made. Whoever plans with wisdom, after planning, rests, because she did her part and hands over the rest to what she does not control. Whoever lives in the Fortress never rests, because no plan soothes the alarm for more than an instant, and the proof is this: as soon as one scenario is covered, the mind leaps immediately to the next uncovered one, and there is never a moment when preparation is enough. If you plan and then live, it is wisdom. If you plan and never come to live because you are always planning, it is the Fortress, and the Fortress, in protecting you from every possible future, robs you of the only real time there is, which is now.

I want to make one thing very clear, so that this edge does not bend. There is nothing more evolved in being spontaneous, nor more backward in being organised. There are people for whom to plan is a joy, a pleasure, a form of love even, when they plan a party, a trip, a future for those they love. These are not caught in the Fortress, they are using a gift. The question is never to plan or not to plan, it is whether the plan serves you or you serve the plan, whether organisation is a tool you put down when you do not need it or a cage you cannot get out of. A free person plans and improvises as life asks, has both registers at hand. The Fortress has only one, and by having only one it is condemned to wall everything in, even what needed no wall, even what only asked to be lived without a plan.

And there is an edge finer still, which one must have so as not to confuse all restlessness with illness. Not all worry is Fortress. There is a restlessness that is a signal, that warns you of a real danger, that makes you look at something that really needs attention, and that is wisdom, not fear. A person who worried about nothing would not be free, she would be reckless, she would go about colliding with the dangers that healthy worry would make her avoid. The problem was never worry itself, it was the worry that leads nowhere, the one that circles without resolving, the one that settles in even when there is no danger, the one that continues after you have already done everything there was to do. There is a question that separates one from the other, and it is worth asking whenever the restlessness tightens: is there anything I can do about this? If there is, one does it, and the restlessness has fulfilled its function, it was a signal, it pointed to an action, and then it can fall silent. If there is nothing to do, then the restlessness is no longer a signal, it is noise, it is the Fortress circling in the void, and what it asks is not one more turn around the problem, it is to be let go, handed over, released, because continuing to worry about what cannot be changed is not responsibility, it is useless suffering disguised as zeal.

### What Disarms This Face

Relational ontology brings to this face an unexpected medicine, one that comes not from control but from its opposite, from trust. Where one lives the I am because we are, no one faces the future alone, and so no one needs to control all of it. Uncertainty is bearable when it is shared, when you know that, whatever happens, you will not be facing it alone. The Fortress is the response of one who feels alone before the mist, and so it has to be itself the wall, the map and the guard. Where there is a bond, the mist stops being frightening in the same way, because safety no longer comes from knowing what is coming, it comes from knowing who you are with when it comes.

There is here a wisdom older than our anxiety of control, and many cultures have kept it better than ours. The wisdom that life is not mastered, it is accompanied. That the future is not an enemy to subdue with plans, it is a river you are part of and in which you learn to swim, not to build dams. There are peoples whose relationship with time is not our anxious relationship with the clock and the calendar, but a trust that things have their time and that forcing them does not hasten them, it spoils them. In that way of being, not knowing is not a flaw to be corrected, it is the natural condition of whoever is alive, and one makes peace with it early, because to fight it is to fight life itself.

Notice the difference between swimming and damming, because it is the image that best disarms this face. Whoever builds a dam against the river spends an immense energy resisting the current, and the dam, however strong, is always under pressure, always threatening to give way, and on the day it gives way it takes everything with it. Whoever learns to swim does not resist the current, enters it, uses it, lets herself be carried where it carries and swims hard where she needs to go against it, and spends much less, and gets much further, and does not live in fear that the dam will give way, because she built none. The Fortress is a life spent building dams against the river of time. Relational wisdom is a life spent learning to swim in it. It does not free you from the current, no one is free of the current, but it transforms your relationship with it, from an exhausted struggle against the inevitable into a dance with whatever comes, and that dance, unlike the struggle, does not exhaust you, it feeds you.

I am not asking you to bring down your walls all at once, you would be exposed and the exposure would only feed the fear. I ask you something smaller and more possible. The next time uncertainty tightens and you feel your hand racing for the plan, for control, for the certainty that closes the matter, stop just an instant before, and ask yourself whether what you are about to control is in fact controllable, or whether you are only trying to quiet the alarm by building one more wall against the mist. Often you will discover that the thing was not in your hands, and that the energy you were about to spend controlling it would be better spent living the present while the future decides, on its own, what it always decided on its own. And try, just once, leaving something unplanned on purpose. An afternoon with no schedule, a trip with an open day, a night without knowing beforehand how it will go. Notice what happens. Almost always, the mist you feared was carrying, after all, something good that no plan of yours would have let in.

There is an expression heard in many cultures that have kept better than ours the art of living with the unknown, and that the Fortress cannot bear to hear: we shall see. God willing, if life allows, we shall see what the day brings. To the ear of the Fortress, this sounds like irresponsibility, like passivity, like a lack of rigour. But it is not. It is an ancient wisdom about the right proportion between what is ours and what is not, between the part that is ours to do and the part we have to hand over because it was never ours. The Fortress confuses everything with its part, thinks everything depends on its control, and so it carries on its shoulders the weight of the whole world, a weight that is not for anyone's shoulders. The we shall see returns things to their owner. One does what one has to do, with care, with commitment, and then one hands over the rest, not out of laziness, but out of lucidity, because the rest was never in your hands, and to grasp it is only to tire yourself with an illusion of possession over what is, and always was, of the domain of the unforeseeable.

And there is, beneath the we shall see, the same truth that runs through the whole book. The Fortress is the response of one who feels alone before an immense and uncontrollable future, and has to be, alone, the wall against it. Where the person feels part of something larger than herself, bound to others, woven into a community and a time that precedes her and continues her, uncertainty stops being a personal abyss to be defended with walls and becomes the shared condition of all who are alive, something crossed together, leaning on one another, and not a threat each one faces alone in her fortress. It is not that the future becomes less uncertain for whoever feels connected. It is that uncertainty stops being terrifying in the same way, because you do not face it alone, and the difference between facing the unknown alone and facing it accompanied is all the difference in the world, it is the difference between a wall raised in fear and a hand given in trust.

### The Final Turn

The question with which you close this chapter is not how to have more certainties, because that question is the very engine of the Fortress and leads nowhere. It is another, braver: of how much life are you depriving yourself so as not to have to feel that you do not know what comes next?

Stay with it without hurrying to answer. Do not use it to force yourself into a feigned spontaneity nor to drop all prudence at once, for that would only be the fear putting on the opposite clothes. Use it with patience. The next time you refuse a good thing only because you did not plan it, that you tense up because the day went off script, that you spend a whole night ruminating on a future it is not yours to control, do just this: notice the Fortress. See the walls. Feel the hand building. And know, for the first time, that what you called being responsible is, in that instant, fear of not knowing. To see the wall is the beginning of being able to open a window in it.

And notice something the Fortress never believes until it experiences it: life usually holds you up even without the walls. Think of the things you feared and that happened anyway, despite all your control. They hurt, and yet you bore them, you survived them, often you discovered in yourself strengths you did not know you had, precisely because life took the ground from under you and you, against your own prediction, kept standing. The Fortress lives convinced that only control keeps it from falling, and so it never discovers that it is capable of falling and getting up, that it has, beneath the walls, a resilience the walls prevent it from knowing. Every time you survive an unforeseen thing you did not control, you gain a proof, against the thesis of the Fortress, that you do not need to know what is coming in order to bear it when it comes, and it is from those proofs, and not from more control, that the only true safety is born, the safety of one who knows, by experience and not by theory, that she holds herself up whatever the mist may bring.

There is more for you than this endless vigil. There is a way of living that holds the helm without demanding to know the whole sea, that plans what is to be planned and hands over the rest with a kind of trust that is not naivety, it is maturity. You have tasted it, in rare moments, when something went well without your having controlled it and you felt, for an instant, that perhaps life would hold you up even without the walls. I see that trust in you, beneath the endless preparation. And when it grows, you will discover that the next face no longer fears the mist of the future, it fears something more intimate and more naked: it fears being seen. It fears the gaze of others upon you, the assessment, the judgement, the moment you step out of the shadow and stand exposed to the light.
