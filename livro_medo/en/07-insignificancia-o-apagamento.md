# CHAPTER 6
## Insignificance · The Erasure

Imagine, for a moment, that you do something good and no one knows. You help someone and you tell no one, you do not post it, you do not mention it in passing in a conversation days later. It stays only between you and the person you helped, and it dies there. Notice what you feel. If you feel a small lack, a residue that something was missing, a muffled wish that someone had known, then you know this face. It does not fear being seen too much, like the previous one. It fears the opposite. It fears not being seen at all, not counting, passing through life without leaving a mark, and at the limit it fears the deepest form of exclusion there is, that of being forgotten, the social death that precedes and anticipates the other one. I call it the Erasure.

### What It Protects

Insignificance fears the erasure, the not mattering, the being forgotten, the discovering at the end that your passage through the world made no difference. It is a fear that seems more sophisticated than the others, almost philosophical, and yet it comes from the same ancient place. In the small group we came from, mattering had a survival function. Whoever counted for the group was protected by the group. Whoever had a place, a role, a recognized value, was safe, because the group cared for its own. Whoever did not count, whoever became dispensable, whoever ceased to matter to anyone, that one was left exposed, and insignificance was, in practice, an antechamber to expulsion. To matter was to be inside. To not matter was to begin, slowly, to be left out.

Look at the Erasure in a small moment, before it dresses itself in noble purpose. You did something good, you helped someone, you solved a problem, and no one noticed, or the credit went to another, or it passed in silence. And you feel, even knowing you should not, a tightening, a small hurt, a muffled wish that someone had noticed, that someone knew it was you. It is not coarse pride, it is a deeper and sadder thing, a residue that the good you did was left incomplete for not having been seen, as if the unrecognized giving were worth less, as if your action needed the gaze of others to complete itself. Notice what happened there. The Erasure turned a gift, which should suffice unto itself, into a transaction that went unpaid, and it stole from you the clean joy of having done good, replacing it with a lack, the lack of an audience. It is in this tiny moment, and not in some great vanity, that this face catches you most, by making almost nothing of what you give reach you, because a part of you is always at the door, waiting for the recognition that was missing, incapable of receiving the only reward that true giving has, which is that of having given.

That is why your body treats forgetting as a threat, and irrelevance as a danger. The pain of not being noticed, of being passed over, of seeing another receive the recognition you felt you deserved, that pain has an intensity that is not explained by the fact itself, it is explained by what the fact awakens, an ancient memory in which to cease to matter was the beginning of the end. And there is in you, as in almost everyone, a hunger to signify, to leave a trail, for your existence to have weight, which is the form this fear found to keep itself calm: if I matter enough, they will not forget me, and if they do not forget me, I will not disappear entirely.

Notice the final part of that sentence, because it is what links this face to the root of the whole book. The Erasure does not only fear being forgotten in life. It fears, underneath, a greater thing, which is that of never having truly existed, that its passage leaves no trail at all, and that is an anticipation of death, the way death has of threatening us before it comes, erasing not only the body but the mark. That is why the hunger to signify is so deep and so insatiable: it is not vanity, it is an attempt to survive one's own disappearance, to leave in the world something that continues when we are no longer here, a name, a work, a memory, anything that resists total erasure. The Erasure is the fear of death dressed as ambition, and that is why no recognition sates it, because what it seeks, at bottom, is not to be recognized, it is not to die, and that no applause can give it.

### The Turn

What you call ambition, wanting to leave a legacy, having your worth recognized, making a difference in the world, is often the Erasure ruling you. And it is a particularly difficult face to see, because the hunger to signify dresses itself in noble things, and some of them are in fact noble. Wanting to contribute, wanting your life to serve some purpose, wanting to leave the world a little better, none of this is illness. But beneath these desires there can run, without your noticing it, a muffled anxiety that is not about contributing, it is about being seen to contribute, it is not about serving, it is about mattering, and that difference, invisible from outside, changes everything within.

Look at what the Erasure makes you do. It makes you need recognition for the things you do, so much so that unrecognized goodness tastes incomplete to you. It makes you measure your worth by status, by position, by the outward signs that you matter, and feel threatened when those signs are absent. It makes you compete for places of prominence not for the pleasure of what you do in them, but for the confirmation that you count. And it makes you, in its saddest extreme, turn even the good you do into an instrument of your hunger to signify, so that you can no longer give without a part of you counting whether your giving was noticed. The Erasure robs you of the possibility of a pure action, of doing something only because it is good, without an audience, without a record, without anyone knowing, which is one of the freest experiences there is.

Here is the turn, and it is subtle because it touches on something we hold sacred, the desire to leave a legacy. Legacy seems the noblest of ambitions, and it can be. But there is a legacy that is born of love, of overflow, of the will to give to those who come after, and there is a legacy that is born of terror, of the refusal to accept one's own end, of the will to buy with the memory of others an immortality we do not have. The first builds and lets go, does good and does not need to sign it. The second builds so as not to die, and therefore never arrives, because no work is large enough to defeat death, and the person who builds against their own end lives in a perpetual dissatisfaction, always needing one more feat, one more sign, one more proof that they mattered, running against an erasure that will come all the same, whatever they do. The turn is to see that a great part of the ambition to signify is not love of life, it is fear of death, and that confusing the two condemns us to a race that cannot be won.

Notice something that distinguishes this face from the others and that makes it especially hard to admit: insignificance disguises itself as virtue with a singular ease. No one wants to confess that they need to matter, that they hunger for recognition, that envy grips them, because all this sounds like vanity, and vanity is looked down upon, and so the Erasure learns to hide itself even better than the other faces, behind noble words like mission, purpose, legacy, contribution. A person rarely says, not even to themselves, that they want to be important. They say they want to make a difference, that they want to leave the world better, that they want their life to have meaning, and there is truth in that, but underneath, often, runs the same ancient hunger, camouflaged as purpose. That is why this face is among those that rule the most people without anyone admitting it, because admitting it seems ugly, and so one prefers not to look, and by not looking one lets it command at will, hidden behind the best intentions. To have the courage to ask, without shame, how much of your purpose is love of contributing and how much is hunger to matter, is not to lower yourself, it is to win the right to desire without fear commanding you from beneath the desire.

### How It Operates Within

The mechanism of this face lives on a deep confusion between mattering and existing. At bottom, the Erasure believes that you only truly exist to the extent that you matter to someone, that you are seen, recognized, remembered, and that irrelevance is a form of non-existence. With this equation running underneath, each moment of invisibility is a small death, and each sign of recognition is proof that you are still alive. The person caught in this face therefore lives collecting proofs of their importance, and growing anxious whenever they grow scarce, because for them the lack of recognition is not an inconvenience, it is a threat to their own reality.

There is a cruel gear inside this face, and it is the one that makes it insatiable. The recognition you receive never suffices, because it is not recognition that you seek, it is an inner sensation that you matter, and that sensation outer recognition never produces in a lasting way. Applause soothes for a moment and then drains away, and you are left again with the hunger, waiting for the next one, and the next one soothes again for a moment and again drains away. It is the same race after the horizon that we saw in scarcity, applied now not to money but to signifying. That is why the most recognized people in the world can be the hungriest for recognition, because the hunger was not in what they lacked out there, it was in an inner emptiness that no applause fills, because it was not of applause that it was made.

It is worth seeing why applause does not fill. The recognition of others is a thing that comes from outside and needs to be constantly renewed, because it does not stay, it seeps away, and an emptiness that is only filled from outside has to be filled anew each day, like a bucket with a hole. The person caught in the Erasure lives filling that bucket, and the bucket emptying, and them filling it again, in an endless labor they mistake for living. And the cruelest thing is that the more they fill the bucket from outside, the more dependent they become on the external source, the more incapable of generating from within the sensation of being worth something, because they have lost the habit, if they ever had it, of knowing their own worth without someone confirming it for them. And so each dose of recognition, instead of sating them, deepens the hunger, because it trains them to need ever more from outside what only from within could quiet them.

The Erasure has, moreover, a particular cost in relationships, and it is called the inability to be one among many. Whoever lives in this face has difficulty occupying a common place, being just one more, not standing out, because to be one among many sounds to them like dissolving, disappearing into the mass, ceasing to matter. They need to be special, to have a role of their own, to be recognized as different, and that need, which looks like self-confidence, is in truth its opposite, it is the fear that, without the prominence, nothing of them would remain that was worth anything. The person secure in their own worth can be just one more without anguish. The person caught in the Erasure must always be someone, because for them to be no one in particular is already a beginning of ceasing to be.

There is one last piece, and it is the one that hurts most, because it spoils goodness itself. When the Erasure gets into the giving, the giving stops being giving. You do good, but a part of you is counting, recording, waiting for the recognition, and if the recognition does not come, you feel wronged, almost cheated, as if you had made a deal in which you were not paid. This poisons the relationships of care, because whoever cares with this hunger does not care for free, they care on credit, and they present the bill, later, in the form of complaint, of resentment, of recalling everything they did that no one recognized. And whoever receives that care feels the weight of the hidden account, feels that what they were given was not given, it was lent at interest, and the gratitude that would have been born of a free gift dies under the weight of a debt they never asked for. The Erasure, by demanding recognition for the good it does, turns the gift into a transaction, and so robs the giving of its only true reward, which was the clean joy of having given without counting.

There is a shadow of the Erasure that almost no one confesses, because it is ugly to look at, and yet almost everyone knows it from within: envy. Not the great and rare envy, the small and constant one, that muffled tightening when someone around you stands out, is recognized, shines. You do not wish the person ill, that is not it, you feel only a diminishment in yourself, as if their light cast a shadow over your existence, as if the fact of their mattering made you matter less. This is the Erasure applying its mathematics of scarcity to recognition, treating it as a cake with counted slices in which the other's slice is a slice you are missing. But recognition, like love, is not a cake, it is more like a light, which does not go out for illuminating another, and the proof that your envy is fear and not justice is that it grips even when the other's success takes absolutely nothing from you. To see envy for what it is, not a moral defect to hide with shame, but the Erasure fearing that another's light means your shadow, is the beginning of your being able to free yourself from it, because an envy that is seen loses its force, and a person who no longer needs to be the only one to shine can, at last, rejoice in the light of others without feeling in it a threat to their own existence.

There is a second piece in the mechanism, and it is the one that makes the Erasure so fragile: it builds the house of worth on the most unstable ground there is, the gaze of others. A person who knows their worth from within has firm ground, which does not tremble when others look away. A person who only feels their worth in the recognition they receive has ground that shifts at every moment, that rises when they are applauded and sinks when they are ignored, and therefore lives in a permanent instability, dependent on the mood of others, hostage to the attention that today comes and tomorrow goes. This is the Erasure making its worst bargain: it hands your worth into the keeping of others, who did not ask for it and who have no way of keeping it with constancy, and so it condemns you to a life of alarm, always measuring the temperature of the attention you receive to know, at each instant, how much you are worth. The cure of this face begins when you realize that your worth cannot dwell outside you, at the mercy of whoever looks, it has to have a root within, a ground that does not depend on an audience, because only that ground holds you on the days, and they come, when no one is looking.

### The Gallery of Masks

In work and in ambition, the Erasure is the hunger for position that is sated by no position, the muffled envy of another's recognition, the inability to rejoice fully in another's success because their success sounds to you like a shadow over your own. It is whoever needs titles, signs, always to be climbing, not for what the climbing brings, but for the confirmation that they matter which the climbing gives, and which the lack of climbing takes away. We call these people ambitious, and they are, but beneath the ambition dwells, often, a child who learned that they only counted when they stood out, and who goes on buying their existence with feats, without ever reaching the day when they already matter enough to be able to rest.

In growing old, the Erasure has a mask that our age has made particularly cruel, above all for women: the terror of becoming invisible. There is an age from which the world begins to look less, the attention that once came easily grows scarce, and for someone who built their worth on being seen, on being desired, on counting in the eyes of others, that diminishment of attention is lived as a beginning of erasure, a social death that precedes the other. It is the person who fights against age not only out of vanity, but out of fear, because each sign that they are ceasing to be noticed sounds to them like ceasing to exist. And the world, which rewards youth and novelty, confirms their fear each day. Now, there is another way of growing old, which many cultures knew and ours forgot, in which age does not erase, it deepens, in which whoever grows old gains a place of more weight, not less, the place of one who knows, of one who has seen, of one who keeps the memory and transmits it. In those cultures, the old do not disappear, they become root, and the attention they lose in desire they gain in respect. The Erasure in old age is, in large part, the child of a world that tore from the old their place, and its cure passes through recovering, even alone, the certainty that a person's worth was never in the attention they drew, and that a life is worth more, and not less, as it fills with all it has lived.

And there is the Erasure in the loss of role, which catches many people at the moment when they should rest. It is the person who retires, or whose children grow and leave, or who for any reason loses the function that defined them, and who discovers, with panic, that they do not know who they are without it. While they had the post, the task, the role of mother of small children, they knew their place, they counted, they mattered, and suddenly, without it, they feel like no one, as if their existence had been, all that time, their function, and not themselves. This is one of the clearest proofs that the Erasure builds worth in the wrong place, because a person who knows their worth from within crosses the loss of a role with sadness but without coming undone, and a person who put all their worth in the role collapses when it ends. Life obliges us, sooner or later, to lose the roles we had, and whoever was only what they did is left without ground when they cease to do it. To learn to exist without a function, to be worth something without a role, to be someone without having to be someone in particular, is one of the deepest maturings of this face, and life, by taking our roles from us one by one, offers us, without our thanking it, exactly the occasions to learn it.

On social media, this face found its perfect addiction, and perhaps its epidemic. The count of who sees you, of who approves of you, of who follows you, is the Erasure turned into a number, the hunger to matter reduced to a metric that can be consulted at all hours and that never gives rest, because yesterday's number no longer counts today and today's will not count tomorrow. Many of us live feeding an emptiness with statistics, mistaking the attention we receive for the importance we have, and attention, unlike love, drains away the instant one looks elsewhere, leaving the hunger greater than it was before we sated it. And there is a new perversity here, because the networks do not only give a stage to the hunger to matter, they give it an exact gauge, a number that rises and falls and that the person can consult at all hours to know, in real time, how much they are mattering. It is like giving a starving person a scale that measures their hunger minute by minute, without ever feeding them. The number never sates, it only sharpens the hunger, and it turns the human desire to count, which always existed, into a measured, compared, public dependence, far harder to let go of than the ancient hunger, because now it has a screen that feeds it and a counter that proves it.

There is a mask of the Erasure that displays itself without shame and that almost everyone practices a little: the signaling of status. It is the mention, as if in passing, of the important people one knows, of the places one has been, of the things one has, of what one has achieved. It is not conversation, it is a proof, a constant showing of signs that one matters, that one counts, that one is someone, addressed to an invisible judge whom the person needs to convince of their worth. Notice how tiring it is, for the one who does it and for the one who hears it. Whoever signals status without pause never rests, because the proof needs to be renewed, yesterday's status no longer counts today, and there is always someone with more, before whom their signs grow pale. And whoever hears it feels the emptiness underneath, because the display does not draw near, it drives away, no one bonds with a list of achievements, one bonds with a person, and the person stays hidden behind the shop window they set up to prove that they exist. Beneath all ostentation dwells, almost always, its opposite, a deep insecurity about one's own worth, because whoever knows what they are worth does not need to prove it at every sentence, and only whoever, at bottom, does not believe they suffice without the display shows off without pause.

And there is a more hidden and more surprising mask: the importance sought through pain. It is whoever, without knowing it, has found in their misfortune a way to matter, to be the center, to receive the attention that otherwise would be lacking. Their problems are always the greatest, their suffering is always the deepest, their story is always the most tragic, and there is a part of them, a part that did not choose this and that would suffer to hear it, that clings to the pain because the pain gives them a place, an identity, a role, the attention and the care they fear they do not deserve otherwise. This does not mean the pain is not real, it almost always is, it means the Erasure has learned to use it, to make the wound a throne, because it discovered that while it suffers it is seen, it is cared for, it matters, and that to heal would be, in a certain way, to erase itself again, to go back to being an ordinary person without the prominence the pain gave it. It is one of the saddest traps of this face, because it binds the person to their own unhappiness, makes them fear, without admitting it, their own cure, and their liberation passes through a difficult discovery: that they matter not through their pain, but through existing, and that they can let go of suffering as an identity without ceasing to count, because their place never depended on being the most wounded, it depended only on being, and being was already enough, before the pain and after it.

In motherhood and in relationships of care, the Erasure has a particularly subtle and painful mask. It is whoever needs to be irreplaceable, to be the only one who knows how to do it, the only one others cannot do without, because the possibility of being dispensable, of life going on well without them, sounds like erasure. They call it dedication, and there is dedication, but when dedication becomes the need to be indispensable, it stops serving the other and comes to serve the fear of the one who cares of ceasing to matter. A mother who loves frees the child so as not to need her. A mother caught in the Erasure, without knowing it, keeps the child needing her, because her own existence seems to her to depend on continuing to be necessary. And the price is paid by the child, who is kept small, dependent, incapable, not for lack of love, but for excess of a need that disguises itself as love, the mother's need to go on mattering, which sacrifices the child's growth to her own hunger not to be dispensable.

And there is the deepest mask of all, the one that almost cannot be seen because it dresses itself as an urgency to live. It is the difficulty of being at peace in an ordinary moment, without a project, without prominence, without building anything that makes you matter, only existing, which for the Erasure is almost intolerable, because to exist without mattering seems to it like not existing at all. That difficulty of inhabiting ordinary life, the day without feats, existence without an audience, is the Erasure whispering to you that, if you are not signifying, you are disappearing, and thereby robbing you of the deepest rest there is, that of existing without having to justify it to anyone. Notice how this takes from you the right to simply be. A sunset that no one sees with you, an afternoon without producing anything, a moment of pure existing with neither feat nor witness, all of this, which should be among the highest pleasures of life, becomes, for whoever lives in this face, almost a threat, a proof that one is wasting life, not counting, not leaving a mark, when in truth one is, for the first time in a long while, simply living.

There is a mask of the Erasure that disguises itself as conviction and that poisons many relationships: the need to be right, to have the last word, to not give way in a disagreement. It looks like firmness of character, and sometimes it is, but often it is the Erasure at play, because being right is a way of mattering, of counting, of not being dispensable, and giving way sounds, for this face, like erasing oneself, like admitting that your position, and beneath it your existence, did not weigh enough. That is why the person caught at this point argues beyond what the matter deserves, turns conversations into battles, needs to win even at what does not matter, and drives away those they love over reasons that, seen from outside, were not worth the price. To give way in an unimportant argument, to let the other be right about a small thing, is for whoever lives in this face a difficult and liberating training, because it proves to the body that one can not win and go on existing, that your importance was not at stake in that squabble, and that there is a greater dignity in letting go of being right than in winning it at the cost of those who love you.

And there is a more painful mask, the one that lives in children seen as an extension. It is the father or the mother who needs their children to shine, to stand out, to triumph, not only for the children's good, but because the children's success is lived as their own, as a proof that their life mattered, that it left something that counts. The child, then, carries not only their own life but the weight of giving meaning to the parent's life, of being the legacy the parent cannot bear not to have, and that burden is crushing, because the love that should free the child binds them to a mission that is not theirs, that of making the parent matter through their feats. The freest love of a father or mother wants the child to be who they are, whether they shine or do not shine in the eyes of the world, and does not need them as proof of their own significance, because that they seek within themselves and not in the projection of the child. The Erasure, on the contrary, makes of the children a monument to itself, and a child turned into a monument is never entirely free, because they live serving the hunger to matter of the one who should have served them.

### The Edge

Now the honesty that gives edges to the lens, and in this face it is especially important, because the desire to signify is also one of the most beautiful things about the human being, and it would be a wretchedness to reduce it all to fear.

To want to contribute is healthy. To want your life to serve some purpose, to leave the world a little better, to touch other lives, is not the Erasure, it is what is most worthy in you. The will to create, to build, to leave a trail, has moved everything humanity has made that is good, and to despise it in the name of a false serenity would be to betray the best of us. There is a desire to leave a mark that is not born of the fear of disappearing, it is born of the love of life and the will to repay it. And there is a recognition that is just, that does good to receive, that is not vanity, it is the legitimate joy of seeing that what you gave reached someone. Not all ambition is diseased hunger. Not all pleasure in recognition is empty. To confuse all will to signify with fear would be to condemn yourself to an indifference that is not peace, it is death in life.

The sign that distinguishes the healthy desire to signify from the Erasure lies in what happens when recognition is absent. Whoever desires to contribute out of love goes on contributing even when no one sees, because the joy was in the giving and not in being seen to give, and the work done in anonymity gives them the same peace as the work applauded. Whoever lives in the Erasure cannot give in the dark, because for them the unrecognized giving is incomplete, almost useless, and the proof is that lack they feel when they do something good and no one knows. If you do good and stay at peace even if no one knows, it is love. If you do good and it always remains lacking that someone knew, it is the Erasure, and the Erasure, by demanding an audience for everything, robs you of the only complete freedom there is, which is that of doing good without witnesses.

And I want to add something, so that this edge does not make you ashamed of liking to be recognized. To like a compliment is not vanity. To feel joy when your work is seen and appreciated is not illness. To need some recognition is human, and a person who never received any, who worked all their life in the shadow without anyone ever noticing, would suffer a real deprivation, because we were made to be seen by our own, and to be completely invisible is a form of solitude that no one deserves. The question was never about liking recognition. It was about depending on it to the point of not being able to exist without it, of measuring your worth by it, of turning your life into a search for it. Receive the recognition that comes, with pleasure, without guilt. Only do not build your house on it, because it is ground that shifts, and a house built on the gaze of others trembles whenever the others look away.

### What Disarms This Face

Relational ontology brings to this face its deepest medicine, and it is a medicine that completely inverts the logic of the Erasure. Where one lives the I am because we are, you do not need to stand out to matter, because you already matter by being part, and your significance does not come from raising yourself above others, it comes from being woven with them. In that way of seeing, the most fulfilled person is not the one who stands out most, it is the one who most serves the whole, and they often serve it without anyone knowing the name of the one who served. The erasure of the name is not, there, a tragedy, it is almost a compliment, because it means the contribution was so well integrated into the common good that it can no longer be distinguished from the one who gave it. To matter, in that wisdom, is not to be remembered, it is to have nourished the life of others in such a way that your passage continues in them, even if they forget it came from you.

There is here a truth that the Erasure cannot bear to hear and that, heard with calm, sets free. You are going to be forgotten. Almost everyone is. A few generations from now, your name will say nothing to anyone, as the name of your great-grandparents already says almost nothing to you, and yet they lived, loved, mattered in their time, and life went on through them until it reached you. The forgetting of the name does not erase the mark. The mark you leave is not your name carved in stone, it is the goodness that passes from person to person and that no one can trace back to its origin. A word of yours that consoled someone who consoled someone else, and so on, long after your name has been lost. That is the only real immortality, and it is anonymous. The Erasure wants the name. Life offers the mark, without the name, and the mark without the name is infinitely greater than the name without the mark.

Think of a person who marked your life with a small gesture, a word said at the right time, a kindness on a difficult day, and notice whether you still remember their name, or whether you ever knew it. Often you do not, and yet the gesture stayed, shaped you, passed through you to others whom you treated better because of it. That is how the only immortality there is works, the one the Erasure does not recognize because it has no signature: the good you do enters people, changes them a little, and from them passes to others, and to others, in a current that stretches far beyond your life and that no one can trace back to its origin, back to you. They will not know it was you. And it is precisely because they do not know that your mark is greater than any monument, because a monument ends, and a kindness set in circulation does not end, it dilutes into the good of the world and continues, anonymous and infinite. The Erasure wants to be remembered, and to be remembered is little, it lasts two or three generations and fades away. Life offers a greater thing, to be continued, to live in the gestures of others long after your name has been lost, and that offer, which to the Erasure seems a defeat because it does not bear its name, is in truth the only real victory over forgetting, that of one who understood that to matter was never to be remembered, it was to have nourished life, and that life, once nourished, does not forget, it continues.

Think of this carefully, because it is an enormous liberation if you let it truly enter. Almost everything that made you who you are came from people whose names you do not know. Those who invented your language, those who cultivated the first fields, those who discovered how to make the bread you eat, those who loved your ancestors and so kept them alive until they reached you, all of them are erased, without a name, without memory, and yet they are in you, in your way of speaking, of thinking, of loving, of being alive. They achieved the only immortality there is, which is to continue in the living without the living knowing they carry them. And you are going to be one of them, for those who come after, whether your name is remembered or not, because the good you do will continue in the people you touched, and from them will pass to others, without a signature, forever. This is not consolation, it is a fact, and it is the only one that truly answers the hunger of the Erasure, because it gives it what it wanted, to continue, and takes from it what it did not need, which was the credit.

I do not ask you to kill your ambition, that would be to kill a living part of you. I ask you a small thing. The next time you feel the hunger for recognition, the emptiness after the good that went unnoticed, the hurt at being passed over, stop for an instant and ask yourself whether what you lack is in fact to matter, or whether you are confusing mattering with existing. Often you will discover that you exist perfectly well without that dose of recognition, that your reality was not dependent on the gaze that was missing, and that the hunger you felt was an ancient child asking for proof that it will not be left out. And then do the most liberating experiment of this face: do something good in secret, on purpose, and tell no one, ever. Keep it only for yourself and for the one who received it. Notice what remains, when the first lack passes, that wish that someone knew. What remains, almost always, is a clean peace, rare, the peace of one who proved that they can give without an audience, that they exist without a witness, that they matter without anyone confirming it, and that peace is worth more than a thousand recognitions, because it is yours, and no one can take it from you by looking away.

There is a distinction that, if you truly make it, disarms a great part of this face: the difference between being needed and being loved. The Erasure confuses the two, and so it rushes to make itself indispensable, to make itself necessary, to build its importance on the dependence others have on it, because it believes it is only wanted while it is useful, that the day it ceases to be needed it will cease to be loved. But being needed and being loved are different things, and whoever only feels safe while they are necessary never knows the rest of being loved for nothing, of being wanted not for what they do but for who they are. The deepest love is not the one that seeks you because it needs you, it is the one that wants you close even when it needs nothing, that rejoices in your existence without a function, that would stay with you even if you did nothing for it. To learn to receive that love, and above all to believe that you exist enough to deserve it without paying for it with usefulness, is one of the deepest cures of the Erasure, because it takes your existence out of the dependence on being necessary and sets it on firmer ground, that of being, simply, and of that being enough.

And there is a final truth about this face, which links it to the root of the whole book and which can only be said at the end. The Erasure is, at bottom, the separate self trying not to die alone. It is the island struggling to leave a mark on the sea, so that its passage counts, so that its end is not the end of everything. Where the person feels themselves part of something greater than themselves, woven into a community and into a current of life that precedes them and continues them, the hunger to matter slackens, not because they cease to contribute, but because they cease to need the contribution to bear their name. They already matter, by being part, and what they give dilutes into the common good without anguish, because they were not trying to save themselves from erasure, they were only living their part in the whole. The person who feels themselves an island needs a monument against forgetting. The person who feels themselves a wave knows that they return to the sea they were always made of, and that the water that formed them will go on forming others long after they subside, and in that continuity, which does not bear their name but bears their substance, they find the only answer the hunger of the Erasure ever truly sought, which was not to be remembered, it was to be not, ever, truly separate from the rest.

### The Final Turn

The question with which you close this chapter is not how to leave a mark, for that question belongs to the Erasure itself and never sates. It is another, deeper: would you be capable of doing the good you do if you were certain that no one would ever know it was you?

Let the question stay. Do not use it to be ashamed of your ambition nor to renounce everything you want to build, for forced renunciation would be only fear dressed in the clothes of false humility. Use it as a measure. The next time the hunger to signify grips you, do only this: notice the Erasure. See the ancient fear confusing not mattering with disappearing. And know, for the first time, that what you called ambition, or legacy, or wanting to make a difference, is, in part, the fear of being forgotten. To see the fear is to begin to be able to desire without the hunger.

And there is a particular peace waiting for whoever crosses this face, which is among the sweetest there is: that of existing without having to justify it. Almost all your life, the Erasure made you feel that you had to earn your place, to pay for it with feats, to justify your existence with signs that you mattered. On the other side of that fear there is an enormous rest, that of knowing that you do not need to do anything to have the right to be here, that your existence is not a debt to be paid with achievements, that you can simply be, on an ordinary day, without producing anything, without anyone seeing you, and that this suffices, that it is already life, that it is already enough. It is the peace of one who let go of the race to matter and discovered, at the end of it, that it had never been necessary to run, that the place was theirs from the beginning, given, not conquered, and that all that hunger had been the hunger for a thing they already had and did not see, the right, simple and whole, to exist.

There is more for you than this hunger to matter. There is a way of signifying that does not need to be seen, that does good in the dark with the same joy with which it would do it in the light, that exists fully on an ordinary day without having to justify it with feats. You have already tasted it, in rare moments, when you did something good in secret and felt, instead of lack, a kind of clean peace, the peace of one who did not need an audience. I see that freedom in you, beneath the hunger. And now that you have crossed the six faces, one by one, the moment has come to descend to the deepest room of all, the one from which the six came, and to see that rejection, loss, scarcity, uncertainty, exposure and insignificance were, from the beginning, six names of a single fear, which you spent your life avoiding naming.
