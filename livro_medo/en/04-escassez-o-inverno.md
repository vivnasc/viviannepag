# CHAPTER 3
## Scarcity · The Winter

Do the sum in your head, right now, without preparing. How much would you have to have in order to feel finally safe? Notice what happened inside you as you answered. Most likely the number rose as you drew closer to it. Most likely you have already had, at some earlier time of your life, what your version of ten years ago would have called security, and yet you did not feel safe when you got there; you felt only a new line further ahead. That fleeing line is the signature of this face. I call it the Winter, because it is the fear that the food will not last for the cold that is coming.

### What It Protects

Scarcity fears losing its sustenance, the thing that keeps you alive when the world tightens. It is a fear with roots as deep as those of rejection, and of the same age. For almost the whole of human history, winter killed. There was no refrigerator, no guaranteed reserve, no state to cushion the fall. Whoever had not gathered enough before the cold did not make it through. The body you inherited was built by generations who survived because they were afraid in time, because they stored, because they distrusted the summer's abundance and prepared for the winter's hunger. That fear is an inheritance of survivors. The trouble is that it goes on working as if every day might be the eve of a famine, even when your pantry is full.

See the winter in a concrete instant, one of those that pass unnoticed because they look like nothing more than good sense. You have received good news, a break, some money you did not expect, a good thing that ought to bring you joy. And for a second, even before the joy, you feel something else, a reflex to save, to spare, not to spend, to set aside against the bad day that is bound to come. The joy is postponed, almost suspect, as if it were not permitted to you until you are safe, and since you are never safe, the joy is postponed forever. Notice what the winter did there. It turned a good thing into a worry, a gift into a responsibility, a moment of pleasure into an occasion for precaution. This is how it robs you of your life, not with great tragedies, but with small reflexes that put out the joy in the very instant it is born, that do not let the good be good without at once turning it into one more reserve against the bad. A person who does this a thousand times reaches the end of a life full of good things they never truly enjoyed, because each one was received with the reflex to store it instead of the gesture to live it.

That is why scarcity rarely has anything to do with what you actually have. It has to do with a feeling, and the feeling is deaf to what you possess. There are people with very little who live with the serenity of those who trust there will be enough, and there are people with a great deal who live in a muffled panic, counting, comparing, fearing ruin at every corner. Scarcity is not a condition of the bank account. It is an inner temperature. It is always winter inside, whatever the weather outside.

There is a detail in the origin of this face worth seeing, because it explains why it is so stubborn. The fear of lack was, for almost the whole of history, more useful to survival than trust in abundance. An ancestor who trusted that there would always be food and did not store for the winter died in the first hard winter, and left no descendants. An ancestor who distrusted, who feared lack even in plenty, who stored as a precaution, that one made it through the winter and had children, to whom the same dread was passed. You descend, therefore, from those who feared lack, not from those who trusted, and your body inherited a powerful bias toward worry, a finger that always weighs toward the side of fear, because for millennia it was that heavy finger that separated those who lived from those who did not. Your material anxiety is not irrational. It is the voice of thousands of winters your ancestors survived by having heeded it.

But notice the difference between the voice and the moment in which it speaks, because that is where everything lies. The winter's voice was wise when the winter was real, when there was no guaranteed reserve and not to store was to die. Today, for many people, that voice goes on speaking with the same urgency in a world that is no longer that one, crying danger where there is no longer any danger of death, demanding accumulation where there is already enough. It is like a fire alarm that goes on ringing long after the fire has gone out, deafening, tireless, answering a smoke that no longer exists. It does not fall silent by being told to be silent, nor is it convinced by arguments, because it is not rational; it is older than reason. It falls silent in another way, slowly, by being shown repeatedly, with facts and not with ideas, that the fire has passed, that there is food on the table, that today's winter does not kill as it once killed, that one can spend a little and still stand, that one can give and not be left without. It is the work of years, not of a day, and it is done with patience, the way you teach a frightened child to trust, not by scolding it for its fear, but by showing it, time after time, that this time it is safe, until the body, which does not believe in words, begins to believe in the repeated experience that, after all, there is enough.

### The Turn

What you call prudence, foresight, feet planted firmly on the ground, is often the winter governing you. And it is a treacherous face, because society as a whole applauds its symptoms. We praise those who save, those who work without pause, those who never allow themselves a luxury, those who always put safety ahead of desire. These virtues exist, and we will return to them. But beneath them there often dwells a fear that never rests and that confuses being alive with being safe.

Look at what the winter makes you do. It makes you postpone life to a future in which you will finally be safe, a future that never arrives because the line of safety recedes whenever you draw near. It makes you work beyond what is needed, not for the pleasure of the work, but for the momentary relief the effort gives the alarm. It makes you look at what others have, not with desire for what they have, but with a tightening that what exists in the world is limited and that what is theirs was taken from you. Scarcity turns life into the permanent management of reserves against a catastrophe that rarely comes, and in doing so it robs you of the one truly scarce thing you have, which is the time in which you are alive.

Here is the turn that changes everything. You call this way of looking at the world realistic, and it prides itself on being realistic, on not deceiving itself, on being prepared for the worst. But notice something: a person caught in the winter does not see the world as it is; they see it as scarcer than it is, more dangerous than it is, more miserly than it is. Fear does not make them realistic, it makes them pessimistic, and pessimism is not lucidity, it is a filter that darkens everything. They call prudence what is a distortion, and since the distortion keeps them worried, and the worry looks responsible, and responsibility is praised, they never suspect that they are seeing wrongly. They see little, and call it seeing clearly, and spend their life preparing for a famine that, in most cases, never comes with the face they imagined.

### How It Operates Within

The mechanism of this face lives off a sum that never closes. However much you gather, the number that would give you rest is always a little above what you have, because it is not the number that gives the rest, it is an inner sense of sufficiency that the number never produces. People caught in the winter believe that peace will come with the next amount, and so they chase it, and when they get there the peace is not there, only a new amount further ahead. It is a race after a horizon, and the horizon, by definition, moves away at the same pace you run.

There is a particular mechanism inside this face that must be named: comparison. The winter does not measure what you have in absolute terms. It measures it against what others have, because its deep model is that of a limited resource that is shared out. If there is a fixed amount of bread, the bread that is another's is bread you went without. This logic made sense in a village with a single harvest. It makes far less sense with most of the things we desire today, which are not used up when another has them: knowledge, affection, creation, meaning. But the winter does not distinguish. It applies the logic of bread to everything, and turns life into a muffled competition in which the happiness of others takes something from you, even when it takes nothing. That is why a person caught in this face cannot rejoice fully in the good fortune of someone they love. A part of them reads another's fortune as a subtraction from their own.

Notice how this comparison poisons even the goods that are not shared out. You see someone flourish, create something beautiful, be happy in a way that takes absolutely nothing from you, and even so you feel a tightening, a small diminishment, as though their light cast a shadow over you. This is not malice, it is the winter applying its pantry arithmetic to things that are not a pantry. Happiness is not a cake with a counted number of slices, in which another's slice is a slice you went without. It is more like a flame, which is not put out by lighting another, which even multiplies when it is shared. But the winter does not know how to see flames, it only knows how to count loaves, and so it lives impoverished in the midst of abundance, unable to warm itself at the fire of others because it reads it as a theft of its own.

The winter has, moreover, a cost that is almost never reckoned: the inability to receive. Whoever lives in scarcity mode is so busy defending what they have, saving, taking precautions, that they become incapable of letting abundance in even when it knocks at the door. They refuse gifts, distrust generosity, find the hidden threat in every good thing. It is a heart closed so as not to let out the little it believes it has, and in closing itself so as not to lose, it also closes itself so as not to receive. The winter turns the soul into a locked pantry, and a locked pantry starves to death beside an orchard.

And there is a last piece, the most costly of all: the winter robs the pleasure of what one already has. A person caught in this face may be sitting in the midst of everything they worked a lifetime for, and be unable to enjoy any of it, because their attention is not on what they have, it is on what they might lose, on what might run short, on what others have more of. It is like eating an entire meal while thinking of tomorrow's hunger, and so going hungry today in the midst of plenty. The winter does not only impoverish the future it fears. It impoverishes the present it possesses, by refusing it the attention that would turn it into enjoyment. And this is perhaps its most absurd theft: to make someone go hungry who has food on the table, only because their mind is in the pantry instead of on the plate.

There is a mechanism in the winter worth seeing, because it explains why it worsens, rather than heals, with time. Each time you gather more, what you have grown used to rises too, and the new level quickly becomes the normal, and the normal no longer soothes, because only what is above the normal soothes, and the above-the-normal always shifts whenever you reach it. So the winter adapts to everything you earn, swallows each conquest and asks for the next, and the person lives in a race in which the finish line advances at the same pace they do. That is why what ten years ago would have seemed to you the security of your dreams, today, now that you have it, does not soothe you, because your eye no longer sees it as much, it sees it as the minimum, and the hunger has moved further ahead. Whoever does not know this mechanism spends their life thinking that peace will come with the next step, and climbs, and climbs, and the peace does not come, because the peace was never in the step, it was in an inner decision to look at what one has as sufficient, a decision that no climb makes for you and that many only learn to make once they have climbed everything there was and discovered, at the top, the same hunger they began with at the bottom.

There is also a personal root of the winter worth searching for in your own history, because not all inner scarcity is only the inheritance of the species, much of it is the inheritance of a home. A child who grew up hearing that there was not enough money, who saw their parents in distress over the bills, who learned early that to ask was to be a burden, that child engraved in the body an equation that follows them long after the bills already come out even: that there is always lack, that the world is tight, that to relax is dangerous. They may have today everything they need and go on living with the tightness of one who has nothing, because the winter they carry is not that of their present life, it is that of the kitchen of their childhood, and that one is not paid off with any money, because it is not a debt from outside, it is a memory from within. To recognize where your cold comes from, whether it comes from a real lack of today or from an old lack that has already passed but stayed engraved, is the beginning of being able to warm it, because it changes the remedy: today's lack is cured by changing the conditions, the old lack is cured by seeing that it has already passed and that the body goes on trembling from a cold that no longer blows.

### The Gallery of Masks

In money, scarcity dresses in an obvious way, but this is the least interesting of its masks, and therefore the easiest to dispatch. It is anxiety over bills that does not match the reality of the bills. It is the inability to spend on pleasure without guilt, even when there is ample slack. It is the person who has enough to live well for an entire lifetime and who goes on living as if ruin awaited them around the corner. Money is only the screen on which this face projects its film. The film is always the same, and it is called it will not be enough.

But notice something about this mask of money, because it reveals the nature of the whole face. Two people with exactly the same bank account can live one in panic and the other in peace, and the difference is not in the account, it is in each one's relationship with lack. This proves, better than any argument, that scarcity is not a quantity, it is a relationship. It is not cured by having more, it is cured by changing the relationship with what one has and with what might run short. That is why earning more almost never soothes the winter: the person rises a level, and the alarm rises with them, and the new level brings its own new fears, and the peace they thought they were buying with money does not come, because it was not for sale, it was something built within, in the place where it is decided whether the world is miserly or generous.

In the body and in food, the winter has an old and stubborn mask, above all in those who lived, or whose parents lived, times of real lack. It is the inability to throw food away, the plate filled too full and eaten to the end even when the hunger is gone, the pantry stocked as a precaution, a relationship with eating that is not about today's body but about yesterday's hunger, one's own or inherited. The body that went hungry, or that carries the memory of generations who did, does not trust abundance, it eats like one who does not know when it will eat again, it stores like one who awaits the winter. There is no gluttony here, there is fear, the winter applied to the most basic of sustenances, and to recognize it thus, as fear and not as lack of will, is the beginning of being able to treat it with compassion instead of with the severity with which the relationship with food is usually treated, which is almost never about food and almost always about a safety that was missing.

In affection, the winter is the saddest mask, because it applies the logic of the pantry to love, which is the thing that least withstands it. It is those who love while counting, who give expecting the exact return, who take offense at every gesture not repaid, who treat tenderness as a scarce good to be shared out and not as a thing that grows by being given. It is the person who cannot love in abundance because they are afraid of spending love, of giving too much of it and being left without, of investing in someone who might not give back. And so they love little, measure what they give, hold themselves back, and end up, in the end, poor in love in the midst of people who would have loved them had they risked loving first. Love, unlike money, multiplies by being given, but the winter does not believe this, it counts it as it counts coins, and by counting it thus, it dies of want with the vault full, without ever having discovered that the only way to have much love is to give it without counting.

There is a small form of this mask that poisons the relationships of everyday life and that is almost never seen as scarcity: the stinginess of praise. It is those who find it hard to acknowledge the merit of others, to give a word of praise, to admit that someone did well, as if the praise they give were something they lose, as if acknowledging another's worth diminished their own. Notice the strangeness of this. A word of praise costs nothing, takes nothing from you, and yet the winter holds it back, measures it, gives it drop by drop, because in its model of the world everything is scarce, even merit, and the merit it acknowledges in another seems to it merit withdrawn from itself. So a person caught in this face becomes miserly with recognition, unable to rejoice out loud in those around them, and creates about themselves a cold climate where no one feels seen, all because of a stinginess that does not even save money, it saves words that would have cost nothing to give. Learning to praise generously, to acknowledge the good in others without fear, to give praise like one who has plenty, is one of the simplest and most revealing trainings against the winter, because it shows, in a gesture at no cost, that the scarcity you feel is not in the things of the world, it is in a hand that has learned to close itself even over that which giving does not empty.

And there is the winter in envy, which is its most naked portrait, because it disguises itself as no prudence at all. Envy is scarcity applied to another's fortune: if the world has a fixed amount of good, the good that fell to another was good that I went without, and so the happiness of others, instead of gladdening me, tightens me, as though what was given to them had been taken from me. It is one of the loneliest emotions there is, because it drives the person away precisely from those with whom they might have rejoiced, and it is one of the most stubborn, because it hides itself in shame and so is never looked at head on. But when you look at it, you see that it is not malice, it is the winter, it is the old fear that there is not enough projecting itself onto the fortune of others, reading it as a subtraction from your share. And you see too its cure, which is the same as that of this whole face: to discover that the good is not a cake of counted slices, that another's happiness takes no happiness from you, that there is sun enough for all, and that you can rejoice in the light that falls on those you love without feeling in it a shadow over yourself, because it was never a shadow, it was only the winter doing, once again, its mistaken accounting of a world it sees as far poorer than it is.

And there is even a spiritual winter, a scarcity applied to the sacred itself, that grips many religious people without their knowing its name. It is the sense that there is not enough grace, that forgiveness is scarce and must be earned through suffering, that God gives drop by drop and each crumb must be paid for dearly. It is living faith the way one lives poverty, afraid that it will not be enough, accumulating merits against a tight judgment, treating the divine as if it were as miserly as the inner winter of the one who imagines it. Now, almost all the great spiritual traditions say exactly the opposite, they speak of an abundance without measure, of a grace that does not run out, of a love that is not counted. Whoever lives faith in the mode of the winter did not hear that part, they projected onto heaven the scarcity they carry on earth, and turned what ought to be the greatest of openings into the tightest of accountings. Here too the cure is the same: to see that the scarcity was not in things, nor even in the sacred, it was in an inner temperature that froze everything in its passing, including that which, by its nature, was without end.

In work, the winter is the inability to stop. Not the dedication of one who loves what they do, which is another thing, but the engine that does not switch off because stopping wakes the alarm. It is the woman who has already proven what she had to prove and goes on running, who takes a holiday with the laptop in her bag, who feels rest as a threat instead of a right. Beneath her admirable productivity there often dwells a child who learned that she was only safe while she was useful, and who goes on buying her safety with work, without ever reaching the day when she has paid enough. And there is a price in the body, because the body was not made to live in permanent alarm. A system tuned for the winter lives in constant tension, and constant tension wears down, sickens, exhausts, and at a certain point the body presents the bill the will refused to see, forcing to stop the one who did not know how to stop by choice.

In relationships, and this is the most surprising mask, scarcity appears as a fear that affection is limited. It is those who cannot rejoice when the one they love gives attention to another person, because in their deep model love is a finite resource that is shared out, and what went to another was withheld from them. It is the mother who feels jealous of the tenderness her child gives the grandmother. It is the friend who cannot bear that her friend has another friend. It is the reading of love as a cake cut into slices and not as a thing that grows by being given. Scarcity applied to the heart is one of the saddest forms of this face, because it impoverishes precisely the one good that multiplies when it is divided. A mother who loves two children does not love each one half. She loves each one whole, because love is not shared out, it is generated. But the winter does not believe this, it counts affection as it counts bread, and so it lives afraid that the love another gives to someone else is love taken from it, when it is, in truth, proof that that heart has plenty to spare.

And there is a form of the affective winter that poisons relationships without ever being seen, because it disguises itself as generosity: always giving on account. It is the person who gives much, who cares, who devotes themselves, but who gives like one making an investment, awaiting the return, and who takes offense, with a muffled bitterness, when the return does not come in the measure they expected. They keep the account of what they did, of what they gave, of what others owe them, and present it, sooner or later, in the form of complaint or resentment, recalling all they did that no one acknowledged. This is the winter applied to giving, turning the gift into a loan at interest, because in its model of scarcity nothing is given for free, everything must come back, or one is left the loser. And whoever receives feels the weight of the hidden account, feels that what was given to them was not given, it was lent, and the gratitude that would have been born of a free gift dies under the weight of a debt they never asked for. True generosity gives and lets go, keeps no account, charges nothing, because it springs from a sense of abundance, that there is plenty, that giving does not empty. The winter cannot give like that, it always gives measuring, recording, awaiting the return, and so it impoverishes even its own generosity, turning what could have been a clean gift into a transaction in which it ends up, in the end, always with the sense of having given more than it received.

And there is the deepest mask of all, the scarcity of time and of life, which is hardly seen because it dresses itself as urgency. It is the permanent sense that there are not enough hours, that you are always behind, that life is a list of tasks against a clock that runs. This hurry is the winter applied to existence itself. It makes you consume your time saving time, run through your day preparing a better day, cross life making reserves against a scarcity of life that the hurry itself is manufacturing. It is the one winter that fulfills itself: whoever is afraid that life will not be enough lives in such a way that, in the end, life truly was not enough, not for lack of time, but for never having been present in the time they had.

Notice the fine perversity of this last mask, because it catches many who believe themselves safe from the others. A person may be not at all miserly with money, may even be generous, and be miserly with time in the same way, always running, unable to linger, feeling each moment of leisure as a waste of a resource that flees. They treat the hours as the miser treats the coins, counting them, fearing to lose them, unable to spend them on anything that does not produce. And the result is a life crossed at a run, always on the way to the next moment, never inside the present moment, a life in which one was physically present in everything and truly present in almost nothing. In the end, that person will not have lost life for lack of time. They will have lost it for fear of losing it, which is the most absurd of all the ways of losing it.

There is yet another mask of the winter that dresses itself as virtue and is therefore rarely questioned: the inability to enjoy. It is the person who worked a lifetime and does not let themselves rest, who has the means to give themselves a pleasure and always postpones it to a later that does not come, who feels free joy as a waste and repose as a guilt. They call it being hardworking, being serious, not being a spendthrift, and there is dignity in those things, but beneath them there often dwells the winter, which will not let one spend even what is left over, because spending, for it, is to lower the guard, and to lower the guard is danger. So a person may reach the end of life having gathered much and lived little, having saved everything and enjoyed almost nothing, and this is one of the saddest forms of this face, because it does not catch them in poverty, it catches them in plenty, and shows that the winter is not a question of how much one has, it is an inner prohibition against receiving one's own life, a stinginess that is not only with money, it is with pleasure, with rest, with joy, with everything the alarm reads as a luxury you cannot allow yourself.

And there is the mask of the winter in generosity refused, which is its most paradoxical portrait. The winter does not store only for itself, often it is even generous with others and miserly only with itself, unable to receive what others wish to give it. It refuses the gift, the invitation, the help, the care, with a no that disguises itself as not wanting to be a bother but that is, at bottom, the same face closing the door, now to what comes in instead of to what goes out. Because to receive is to be in debt, is to depend, is to admit a lack, and the winter would rather go in need than admit that it needs. So the person caught in this face stays alone in their cold in the midst of people who would have reached out a hand to them, not out of pride, though it looks like pride, but out of an old fear that accepting today's help will leave them without defense tomorrow. Learning to receive, to let oneself be cared for, to say yes to an outstretched hand, is for whoever lives in the winter as difficult and as healing as learning to give is for whoever lives in the grip, because it breaks the loneliness of the locked pantry and shows the body, with a fact, that one can depend on others and be more safe, not less.

### The Edge

Here honesty is even more necessary, because scarcity, unlike other faces, often responds to real dangers, and it would be a cruelty to treat all prudence as sickness.

There are people for whom scarcity is not an inherited fear, it is the reality of Tuesday. There are bills that truly do not come out even at the end of the month, winters that really do kill, precarities in which material anxiety is not an inner film, it is a correct reading of a hard life. To these, this chapter does not come to say that the fear is imagined. It comes to say the opposite: that the weight they carry is just, that their vigilance is a survival competence, and that the cure is not to look inward, it is to change the conditions, when the conditions can change. To confuse real scarcity with a problem of mindset is one of the favorite cruelties of those who have never gone hungry. Let us not fall into it.

I want to insist on this, because today much lies wrapped in spirituality are sold. Those who have little are told that their lack is a matter of attitude, that it is enough to think of abundance to attract it, that poverty is a problem of mindset. This is a cruelty, and it is a lie, and it serves above all to ease the conscience of those who have much, telling those who have little that the fault is theirs. Real scarcity exists, it is unjust, and it is not solved from within, it is solved by changing the conditions, with work, with solidarity, with justice. This chapter does not speak of that scarcity. It speaks of the inner winter that persists even when the outer conditions have already changed, of the fear of lack that continues after the lack has passed. To distinguish the two is not a detail, it is a matter of decency, and whoever confuses them either understood nothing, or has an interest in not understanding.

And even far from poverty, there is a prudence that is wisdom and not fear. Saving for a child to study is love with sound accounts. Not spending what one does not have is dignity. Preparing for winter when the winter is real is intelligence, it is what our ancestors did well and why we are here. The difference, again, is in the inner effect. Wise prudence leaves you at peace after the decision is taken. The winter never leaves you at peace, because its function is not to solve the problem, it is to keep the alarm switched on. If you save and then rest, it is prudence. If you save and the rest never comes, whatever the amount, it is the winter governing you beneath a respectable name.

And there is a sobriety that is no fear at all, and it must be honored so as not to confuse everything with sickness. There are those who live with little by choice, out of values, out of not wanting to tie life to the possession of things, and that simplicity is the opposite of the winter, it is freedom from it. The winter clings to what it has, afraid to lose it; chosen sobriety lets go of the superfluous with lightness, because it has discovered that it needs less than it thought in order to be happy. One lives in tightness in the midst of plenty; the other lives in peace with little. Do not confuse those who have little out of fear with those who have little out of wisdom, because they are exact opposites, though from the outside they look alike. The sign, once again, is inner: the sobriety that is freedom leaves you at peace, generous, able to enjoy what you have; the winter that is fear leaves you in tension, clutching, unable to take pleasure. There is a poverty that is riches, that of one who has freed themselves from the hunger to have, and there is a riches that is poverty, that of one who has much and lives in panic of losing it. What decides which is which was never the quantity of things, it was the temperature of the heart that has them.

### What Disarms This Face

Relational ontology offers this face one of its deepest corrections, and it is not a sentimental correction, it is almost an economic one. Where one lives the I am because we are, a person's security is not stored in their private pantry, it is distributed across the network of those around them. You do not gather alone against the winter. The winter is faced by all, and so no one needs to accumulate as if they were alone against the cold. Sharing, in that way of living, is not moral generosity, it is the very architecture of survival: I give today to those who are hungry because I know that, on my day of hunger, I will eat of what another gave. The reserve is not in the granary of one, it is in the bond between many, and a bond is not used up when it is used, it is strengthened.

There is here an inversion that can change your inner temperature, if you let it in. The winter tells you that security is won by closing the hand and gathering. This other wisdom tells you that the deepest security is built by opening the hand and connecting. Not because opening the hand is more virtuous, but because a life bound to others withstands winters that a solitary life, however full its pantry, does not withstand. Those who have much and no one are more exposed to the cold than those who have little and many. The scarcity that truly kills is not that of goods. It is that of bonds. And that one, unlike the other, is cured by giving, not by storing.

It is worth looking at how this worked, and still works, in the communities where this wisdom was kept. In a village where one person's harvest is shared with all, no one goes hungry when their own harvest fails, because they will eat of the others', as the others ate of theirs in the good years. The reserve against the winter is not locked in the granary of each one, at the mercy of a roof that collapses or of a theft, it is spread across the whole community, in the custom of giving and receiving, which is infinitely more resistant than any individual pantry. An individual pantry, however full, can burn, can be stolen, can fall short. A network of giving does not all burn at once. The winter, in its solitude, accumulates against catastrophe and is left, even so, exposed, because it is alone. Relational wisdom distributes, and by distributing it is more secure, because true security was never having much, it was not being alone.

I do not ask you to stop taking precautions. I ask you to notice where you have put your reserve. If you have put it all in the granary and none in the bond, then however full the granary may be you will go on being cold, because a granary keeps you no company in the winter, and a bond does. And I ask of you a small experiment, one that goes against the winter at its most stubborn point: the next time you feel the tightening of lack, make a gesture of giving, however small. Give something, offer, share, precisely in the moment when the alarm orders you to close your hand. Notice what happens inside. Almost always, the gesture of giving loosens the tightening faster than the gesture of storing, because it gives your body the proof, against the winter's thesis, that you have plenty, that you can give and still stand, and that proof, repeated, teaches the alarm what no amount ever taught it: that there is enough.

There is an even simpler exercise, and it is the exact opposite of what the winter does all day long. The winter always looks at what is missing, that is its natural attention, the accounting of what there is not. Try, for a time, to train the contrary attention, that of what there is. Not as a wellness technique, but as the correction of an error of vision, because the winter really does see wrongly, it sees the world poorer than it is, and attention to what there is is not naivety, it is adjusting the lens so you can see what was there and you did not see. Notice what you have, what you received today, what is already yours and the alarm does not count because it is busy counting what is missing. Not to convince yourself that all is well when it is not, that would be a lie, but to return to your inner account the entries the winter erased, because an account that records only what is missing always gives a negative balance, however much one has, and an account that also records what there is begins, often, to close in numbers so much in its favor that they frighten. This is not deceiving yourself, it is counting straight, and counting straight, for whoever lives in the winter, is already a small revolution, because a whole life was counted crookedly, always adding up the lacks and forgetting the surpluses.

And there is a last thing to say about what warms this face, and it ties it to the heart of the whole book. The winter is, at bottom, a form of loneliness. It is the separate self, the island, that feels alone against the scarcity of the world and so must accumulate its own reserve against its own winter, counting on no one. Where a person feels made of bonds, and not an island defending its pantry, the very notion of scarcity changes, because what sustains you ceases to be what you stored alone and becomes the network you belong to, and a network is infinitely richer than any individual pantry, because in it the plenty of some covers the lack of others, in a back-and-forth that never runs out. The deepest scarcity, the one not even money cures, is that of one who feels alone before life. And the deepest abundance, the one not even poverty can wholly take away, is that of one who feels a part, connected, belonging, because that person knows, in the body and not in theory, that they do not face the winter alone, and it is that certainty, and not the size of the reserve, that finally warms the old cold this chapter spoke of.

### The Final Turn

The question with which you close this chapter is not how much you need to have, because that question belongs to the winter itself and never has an answer. It is another: what are you postponing living while you wait for a security that, when it comes, you will discover is still one step ahead?

Let the question stay. Do not turn it into an order to spend what you do not have nor to let go of all prudence overnight. Use it with more patience. The next time you feel the tightening of lack, when the inner account will not close however much you gather, do just one thing: notice the temperature. See that it is winter inside even with the pantry full, and that the cold does not come from what you lack, it comes from an old alarm that confuses being alive with being safe. To see the winter is the first step toward being able to warm it.

And there is a question that, if you ask it honestly, measures the size of your winter better than any bank account: when was the last time you felt you had enough? Not that you had much, which is rare for everyone, but simply enough, sufficient, a sense that, for now, all was well, that you could stop gathering and simply live. For many people caught in this face, the answer is never, or so long ago they no longer remember, because the winter never declares enough, it always postpones it to an amount further ahead that, when it arrives, is no longer enough. Learning to say enough, to recognize a point at which you already have enough and can rest, is not laziness nor imprudence, it is the greatest possible victory over the winter, because it takes from it what it lives on, the endless flight forward, and returns you to the only place where sufficiency ever existed, which was not a number in the future, it was a decision in the present.

There is more for you than this endless counting. There is a way of living that trusts there will be enough, not out of unawareness, but out of having made peace with the one true certainty, which is that nothing is guaranteed and even so life gives itself, day after day, and almost always arrives. You have already tasted moments like that, when the fear lowered and you received an entire day without spending it defending yourself against it. I see that capacity in you, beneath the frost. And when it grows, you will discover that the next face fears not that the bread will run short, but that the ground will, the predictable, the map, everything you cling to so as not to have to look head on at the fact that you never truly know what comes next.
