# INTRODUCTION
## This Is Not a Book About Conquering Fear

I want to begin by taking something out of your hands, so the reading sits more lightly on you. You will not find here a method for ceasing to be afraid. Not because that is difficult, but because it would be a lie, and a lie that has been sold to you many times already. Fear is not a disease to be cured nor an enemy to be defeated. It is one of the oldest things you carry with you, older than your language, older than your name. A body with no fear at all is not a free body. It is a body that does not make it to the end of the day.

What I am proposing to you is different, and more interesting. We are not going to try to erase fear. We are going to learn to recognize it when it disguises itself as something else, which is nearly always. Because the fear that halts your life does not usually show up trembling. It shows up calm, sensible, well dressed. It arrives wearing the face of prudence, of loyalty, of humility, of love. It sits at your table as though it were family, and you pass it the bread without suspicion, because it has the face of a virtue you learned to admire.

Notice something, before we go any further. Almost everyone knows what they are afraid of in moments of danger. You know you are afraid when the car skids, when the phone rings before dawn, when the doctor pauses before speaking. That fear is not the subject of this book; that one is plain to see and it passes. The fear I am telling you about is another, and it is much harder to catch, because it does not come in moments of danger. It comes on ordinary days, in the small choices, in the way you decide what to say in a conversation, in what you accept and what you refuse, in what you risk and what you postpone. It does not make you tremble. It makes you choose. And because it does not tremble, you never called it fear. You called it your way of being.

### The Thesis, Spoken Softly

Here is the idea that holds up everything that follows, and I would rather tell it to you early, without ceremony, so that you can go on testing it against your own life as you read.

A great part of what you call your personality is not your temperament. It is your defense. It is the way you found, very young, when you still had no words for what you felt, to protect yourself from something that seemed unbearable to you. That way worked. It kept you inside, it kept you safe, it kept you standing. That is why it stayed. You grew around it the way a tree grows around a wire that was tightened on its trunk when it was small, and today you no longer know where the wood ends and the metal begins.

Conformity, perfectionism, control, the rush to please, the difficulty in stopping, the need to become important. We tend to read these things as traits of character, some good, some bad. This book reads them differently: as strategies. Each one answered, one day, a question your body asked without consulting you. And the question was always a variation of the same one: how do I keep from being separated from what I need in order to exist?

Notice the word, because it is the key to the whole book. Separation. We are going to see that fear, beneath all its disguises, fears only one thing, declined in seven ways. It fears losing the group. It fears losing the one it loves. It fears losing its sustenance. It fears losing the firm ground of its days. It fears being seen and judged. It fears disappearing without leaving a mark. And, at the bottom of all this, in a room we almost never open, it simply fears ceasing to be. The final separation, the one all the others rehearse, is the one that awaits us all and that we spend our lives pretending does not exist.

There is a consequence of this thesis that I want you to hear carefully, because it is what makes the book useful and not merely curious. If what governs you is not your temperament but your defense, then there is a part of you that is not fear, a part prior to the defense, which the defense is protecting. The child who had to defend herself existed before she learned to defend herself. That part did not disappear. It lies beneath the layers, and it is this part that the book remembers on every page, when it tells you there is more to you. I am not coming to point out the flaw. I am coming to remind you that beneath the flaw, which turns out to be a defense, there is a whole person that the defense has spent a lifetime hiding, even from you.

Let me give you an example of what I mean, so the thesis does not stay in the abstract. Imagine a woman whom everyone calls organized, controlling, someone who needs to have everything planned. We can read this in two ways. The first, the common one, says she is like that, it is her temperament, some people are more organized than others, and it stops there. The second, the one of this book, asks something else: what is she protecting herself from when she controls? And if we look closely, we almost always find a child who grew up in an unpredictable world, in a house where you never knew what mood the father would come home in, or where the ground was missing, or where the only way to be safe was to foresee everything so as not to be caught by surprise. Her control is not temperament; it is the armor that child forged to survive a world she could not predict, and that the woman goes on wearing long after the world has changed. Do you see the difference between the two readings? The first files her away in a drawer and gives her no way out. The second shows her the defense beneath the temperament, and the instant she sees the defense, she is no longer condemned to be that way, because a defense, unlike a temperament, can be recognized, thanked, and slowly loosened, when it is no longer needed. This is what this book does, six times, with the six faces: shows you the defense beneath what you took to be your character, to give you back the choice that character denied you.

### Six Faces and One Root

I chose to organize fear into seven faces, and there is a reason they are not eight or four. The first six are the ones you recognize in daily life, the ones that pull your strings without your knowing they are being pulled. Rejection, loss, scarcity, uncertainty, exposure, insignificance. Each one has its chapter, its way of operating, its favorite hiding places in your home, in your work, in your bed, in your relationship with God and with money.

The seventh is not beside the others. It is beneath them. It is the root from which the six spring, and that is why its chapter comes at the end, when you have already recognized the masks one by one and are ready to see that they all hid the same face. That root is the fear of separation carried to its limit, the fear of ceasing to exist. I call it the Abyss, and I ask you to have patience with it until then, because it is what gives meaning to everything else. A book about the leaves that never speaks of the root is a book that entertains you. I want this one to serve you.

Perhaps you wonder why I should speak of seven and not ten, or three. The answer is that I did not invent a round number to make it look neat. These are the fundamental separations by which a person can feel threatened, and each one corresponds to something without which, in one age or another of human history, one did not survive: the group, the bond, the sustenance, the predictability of the environment, secure standing among others, the memory that guarantees we are not left behind. Take any one and you will see that it fits, in the end, into one of the six. Add the root, the fear of ceasing to be, and you have the whole map. It is not a typology to file you away. It is a map of the rooms through which the same fear moves, changing its clothes according to the room.

There are those who have walked here before me, and it would not be honest to pretend I arrived alone. Fritz Riemann, in a 1961 book on the basic forms of anxiety, proposed that there were four essential fears, tied to four types of personality. He was a psychiatrist, and his reading is clinical, made for the consulting room and for his time. Karen Horney, before him, spoke of a basic anxiety born in the child when the world around it ceases to be trustworthy. Ernest Becker, later, had the courage to look at the whole of human civilization and see in it a gigantic response to the terror of dying. I owe something to all three, and I say so with an open chest. Here, however, you will find something else. Not a typology that files you into a drawer, but a map of the faces we all share. Not a clinical reading of illness, but a relational reading of ordinary life. Not the past of the child you were, but the present of the woman who today chooses, postpones, yields, and insists. Where these authors illuminate me, I say so. Where I go by another path, I say that too.

There is one more difference I want to mark, because it changes the temperature of everything you are going to read. Riemann looked at the four fears as forms of personality, almost as diagnoses, and there was in that a coldness of one who classifies from outside. I do not look at you from outside. I write this from inside the same fear, because I too know the six faces from within, one by one, and I recognized them first in myself before seeing them in others. I do not speak to you from the height of one who has been cured. I speak from the side of one who learned to see, which is the only cure this book knows and the only one that does not lie.

### What This Book Promises You, and What It Does Not

Be fair with me and I will be fair with you. I do not promise you peace. I do not promise that, once the book is closed, fear will let go of your ankles. What I promise you is sight. At the end of each chapter, you will recognize one of the faces better, and you will recognize it first in yourself, then in the people you love, and at a certain point in the whole world, because once you learn to see this gravity you can no longer unsee it. That changes everything, even if it gives you no rest. Seeing is not the same as solving. It is the beginning of another relationship with what moves you.

Perhaps you will ask what use seeing is, if seeing does not solve. It is of great use, and you will feel it before you understand it. As long as you confuse fear with your way of being, you obey it without choice, because one does not choose against what one takes to be oneself. The instant you recognize that this sensible voice is fear, a space opens, small, between the voice and your response. You need do nothing in that space. Its mere existence already changes who is in charge. It is the difference between being led in the dark and driving with your eyes open on a road that still has the same curves. The curves do not disappear. You come to see them arriving.

There is here, too, a discipline of honesty that I want you to notice, because it is what separates this book from a sermon. I will show you, more than once, the places where my own lens does not serve. Where generosity is really generosity and not fear of displeasing. Where staying is fidelity and not the inability to leave. Where stopping is rest and not disguised flight. If I told you that everything you do is fear, I would be doing to you what I accuse fear itself of: reducing you, shrinking you, explaining you from below. That is not what I want. An instrument that cuts everything alike is not a lens; it is a blind blade. I would rather give you a lens with edges, one that shows where it sees and admits where it does not reach.

I make you this promise for a weighty reason: a lens that explains everything loses the authority a lens gains when it knows where to stop. If after reading this book you come to see fear in every gesture of yours and of others, to suspect all kindness, to distrust all love, I will have failed, and turned a tool of freedom into a new prison, more subtle than the one I wanted to take you out of. Fear has six faces, that is true. But not everything that resembles them is fear, and knowing how to distinguish fear from what merely resembles it is half the work of seeing.

A word about whom this book addresses, because I write in the feminine and I do not want that to close doors. I write to you, woman, because it is from within that experience that I speak, and because many of the faces of fear press upon women in particular ways, shaped by a world that asked them, for centuries, to please, to care, not to be a nuisance, to efface themselves. But fear has no sex, and a man who reads these pages will find himself in them as much as you do, because the six faces are human before they belong to anyone, and the root, the fear of ceasing to be, is the most shared of all experiences, the only one that absolutely no one escapes. If you are a man and have read this far, you are not in the wrong place. Translate the feminine into your own life without effort, because the gravity I speak of bends your choices exactly as it bends hers, only with sometimes different masks, and the work of seeing, that one, is the same for all who were born knowing they will die.

### How to Read This

You can read the chapters in order, and that is how I wrote them, because each face prepares the next and all of them prepare the root. You can also, if one of them calls to you more loudly, begin with that one, because each stands on its own. I ask only one thing of you, the same thing I asked in the prologue and that I will ask you over and over, because it is the only true instruction in this book. Do not read to repair yourself. Do not underline your flaws with a severe pen. Read like someone drawing close to a person she loves and finally wants to understand. That person is you, and she has spent her life defending herself against ghosts that can no longer hurt her the way they hurt the child who invented them out of need.

You will notice that each chapter follows more or less the same path, and that is on purpose. It comes in through a scene from daily life, something you have already done or said. Then it turns, and shows you that what you called virtue or temperament was, after all, that face of fear wearing a mask. It opens the mechanism, so you can see how it works from within. It walks with you through the rooms where it hides, the family, love, the children, work, faith, money, and finally you. It finds the deepest layer, an ancient wisdom that disarms that face at the root. It makes an honest pause to show you where that face is not fear. And it closes, not with a lesson, but with a question larger than the one that opened the chapter. It follows this path six times, and on the seventh it descends to the root from which the six came.

One last thing, before we turn to the first face. You are going to read many sentences about fear, and your instinct may be to frighten yourself at the quantity of fear you will recognize in yourself. Do not be frightened. The quantity of fear you are able to see is exactly the measure of the courage you already have to look at it. No one who was not strong would have opened this book. Now come with me to the first room, the oldest of them all, the one where fear learned to call itself belonging.
