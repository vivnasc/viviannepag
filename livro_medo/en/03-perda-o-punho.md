# CHAPTER 2
## Loss · The Grip

There is a gesture you make with your hands without noticing, and it tells your story better than any word. When something is precious to you, you close your hand. A child does this with a toy in front of another child. An adult does the same with a person, except the hand that closes is invisible and goes by a thousand names. Too much attachment. Jealousy. Needing to know where she is. Feeling wounded when she has a life that does not include you. We call it intense love, and sometimes love is intense. But there is an intensity that is love and another that is a grip, and this chapter is about the second.

Think of a night when the one you love went out without you. A dinner with his friends, a work trip, a night that was hers and not yours. Notice what happened inside you during the hours she was far away. If there was peace, if the absence was only absence and you managed to inhabit your own night, then this face does not govern you at that point. But if there was a dull unease, a phone checked too often, a mind inventing scenes, a relief too great when she finally came back, then you know the grip, and you know it does not require great tragedies to close. A single night in which the one you love exists without you is enough.

### What It Protects

Rejection feared losing the group. This face fears losing a single person, the one in whom you have placed so much that their absence seems like the end of you. It is a more concentrated fear and therefore a more violent one. The group is diffuse, it dilutes itself, there was always another group. The beloved is unique, irreplaceable, and the body knows it. When you love in earnest, the other stops being someone out there and becomes a part of your inside. To lose them stops seeming like a sorrow and starts seeming like an amputation. That is why grief hurts in the body, not only in the chest: because a part of you, which dwelled in that bond, was suddenly left with nowhere to be.

It is worth not shaming this fear, because it is born of the best in you. Only those who know how to love fear losing. People who protect themselves from loving too much never know this grip, and they also never know the thing of which it is the shadow. I am not proposing that you love less. I am proposing that you look at what happens when love, afraid of ending, turns into the very thing that hastens the end.

Let me show you the grip closing in a small gesture, one of those you make without noticing, because that is where it lives, not in the grand scenes. The one you love is telling you, happily, about something good that happened to them, a new plan, a person they met, a longing they discovered. And instead of the joy that their joy ought to kindle in you, you feel a tightening, a shadow, a quick calculation of where that leaves you, whether that piece of news includes you or threatens you, whether that new life he wants has room for you. You say nothing, or you say something lukewarm that dims his light a little, and he notices, without knowing what, that his joy was not well received, and the next time he tells you less. Notice what happened there, in that tiny instant. The grip read the beloved's own life, the part of him that does not pass through you, as a threat, and reacted by shrinking it, by dimming it slightly, by teaching him, without a single word, that it is safer not to shine too brightly at your side. That is how the grip tightens, not with chains, with shadows, and that is how it slowly impoverishes the person it most wanted to see grow, because it cannot bear to have them grow in a direction that is not its own.

The opposite of this gesture even has a name, and it is one of the most reliable proofs of love there is: the capacity to rejoice, truly, in the good fortune of the one you love, above all in the part of that good fortune that does not come from you and does not pass through you. To notice the other's light and be kindled by it, rather than reading it as a shadow over yourself, is the exact opposite of the grip, and it is rare, because it demands that your love be free enough of fear to want the other's good more than your own security in them. When you can rejoice without reservation in a joy of the other's that does not include you, you know that at that point you love with an open hand, because the grip can never do it, the grip always reads the beloved's own life as a subtraction from its own, and the open hand reads it as what it actually is, a proof that the person you love is alive and whole, which is the only thing that makes it worth loving them.

This face too has roots that descend very deep, deeper than your personal history. The human being is born more defenceless than almost any other animal, and depends, for years, on someone not letting go. The child you once were inherits from thousands of generations the knowledge, in the body, that to be let go is to die, because for almost all of history it was exactly so, an abandoned human infant did not last. The bond, for us, was never an emotional luxury, it was a condition of survival, and the alarm that fires when the bond seems threatened is the same, in its origin, as the one that fired when the mother strayed too far on the savannah. That is why the loss of the one we love, or merely its threat, strikes us with a force that seems disproportionate to those who look from outside. It is not disproportionate. It is proportional to an ancient truth: there was a time when to be let go was to die, and your body goes on living as if it still were.

### The Turn

What seemed the supreme proof of your love reveals itself, so often, as the supreme proof of your fear. This is the hardest chapter to accept, because it touches the point where we are most convinced of our own nobility. We think that the more we grip, the more we love. That jealousy measures passion. That wanting to know everything is caring. That suffering greatly over someone is the measure of wishing them well.

Look at what the grip actually does, when you bring it into the light. Jealousy does not protect the relationship, it corrodes it. Control does not hold the other, it drives them away. Vigilance does not give you certainty, it gives you a dose that needs renewing hour by hour, like any addiction. And the person you love, inside that grip, does not feel loved. They feel gripped, which is a very different thing and a much lonelier one, because to be gripped is to be bound to another person's anxiety, and no one flourishes bound to another person's anxiety. The love that closes its hand always obtains the opposite of what it wants. It grips so as not to lose, and it is the grip that makes it lose.

There is a cruel irony at the heart of this face, and I want you to see it head on, because it is what makes it so tragic. The grip exists to prevent loss, and it is one of the most reliable causes of loss there is. The gripped person suffocates, and whoever suffocates seeks air, and seeking air means, sooner or later, moving away from the one who takes the air. So the grip manufactures exactly the disaster it fears, and then reads the disaster as proof that it was right to grip, when the disaster was the child of the grip. It is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Whoever fears being abandoned grips, the grip pushes the other away, the other withdraws, and the one who feared abandonment concludes: I was right to be afraid, see how they leave me. He does not see that it was his own fear, turned into a grip, that created the distance he then wept over.

### How It Operates Within

The mechanism of this face lives off an illusion you may never have suspected: the illusion that it is possible to guarantee the other. That there exists a sufficient quantity of knowing, of presence, of proof of love, that will finally put you beyond the reach of loss. The grip spends its life trying to reach that quantity, and never reaches it, because it does not exist. The other is free. It was that freedom that made you fall in love, and it is that same freedom you can never abolish without killing the very thing you love.

Notice the mechanics of jealousy, which is the purest expression of this face. It does not begin in the other. It begins in an image your mind produces, an invented scene of loss, and your body reacts to the scene as though it were real. From there, you search the world for proofs that confirm the scene, and the mind, generous, always finds them, because a frightened mind finds what it seeks. A delay becomes a sign. A phone turned face down becomes a confession. A joy that did not come from you becomes a threat. You are not reacting to the other's life. You are reacting to the film your own mind projects, and then you punish the other for the script of a film he does not even know is playing.

There is a cog in jealousy that makes it insatiable, and it is the same one that makes all fears insatiable: the proof you seek is never enough to settle you. You ask for a guarantee, and you receive it, and the guarantee calms you for an hour, and then the doubt returns, stronger, and you ask for another, and another, because what you are seeking is not in fact a guarantee about the other, it is a cure for an insecurity that is yours and that no external proof heals. That is why the jealous person seems never to be satisfied, however faithful and present the other may be. It is not the other they need. It is an inner peace they confuse with a certainty about the other, and because they seek within the other a thing that exists only within themselves, they seek forever and never find, and in that search they wear away the love they claimed to want to protect.

Control is jealousy without the face of a rival. It is the same fear applied to everything that might change. You need to know the plans, to approve the decisions, to have nothing move without passing through you, and you call this being organised, being present, taking care of the home and the family. Part of that is competence, and we will return to it in the edge. But part of it is the grip closing over uncertainty, because everything you do not control is a door through which loss may enter. To live like this is exhausting, and not only for you. It is exhausting for whoever lives with you, who feels they breathe within the limits of your fear and that any piece of news they bring is first filtered through the alarm before it can simply be a good thing.

There is one more piece I want you to see, because it is the most hidden: the grip confuses intensity with love. Because it suffers greatly, because it thinks of the other at every hour, because it feels the absence with a force that consumes it, the grip concludes that it loves deeply, more deeply than those who love in peace. But intensity is not the measure of love, it is often the measure of fear. What burns in that intensity is not chiefly love for the other, it is terror of losing the other, and the two things look alike from outside and are very different within. Love wants the other's good, even when the other's good does not pass through you. The terror of loss wants the other close, safe, yours, even when that shrinks them. Learning to tell these two flames apart, the flame of love and the flame of fear, which burn so close together that they almost blur into one, is a great part of the work of this face.

It is worth asking where, in each of us, the force of this grip comes from, because it does not grip everyone with the same hand. There are those who love in peace, who bear absence without drama, who trust without effort, and there are those who live love in permanent alarm, and the difference is not one of character, it is one of history. The grip tightens hardest in those who, very early, learned that love was not to be trusted. The child whose lap sometimes came and sometimes did not, without her understanding why, learned to cling with all her strength when she had it, because she did not know when she would have it again. The child who was left, or who felt she could be left, grew up with a radar tuned to the signs of abandonment, a radar that now fires at the slightest hint, a phone that takes too long, a colder tone, a night alone. You are not exaggerating when your body enters panic over a small thing. You are reliving, without knowing it, an ancient insecurity about whether love stays or goes, and treating the person of today with the fear you earned from another, long before them. Seeing this does not excuse the grip, but it explains it, and the explanation is the beginning of compassion for yourself, because you stop thinking yourself mad with jealousy and begin to recognise a wound that has age, history and reason to be, and that, because it has reason to be, can also, with patience, be healed.

There is a second thing in the mechanism worth seeing, because it is what makes the grip so convincing from within. The grip produces relief. Every time you check, that you confirm, that you make sure, that you draw the other closer, you feel a momentary relief, the anxiety drops, and that relief is a prize that teaches your body to repeat the gesture. You send one message too many, and he answers, and you settle, and your body learns that sending the message settles you. You ask the jealous question, and he reassures you, and you settle, and your body learns that the question brings peace. So the addiction sets in, not through weakness, but through learning, and because the relief is immediate and the cost is slow, the cost of suffocating the other and the relationship, the body chooses the relief again and again, until the grip becomes a habit you no longer choose, only carry out. That is why it is not enough to promise yourself you will not control. You have to see the secret prize that control gives you, that brief peace, in order to stop chasing after it blindly, and to learn to bear the anxiety without going to relieve it in the other, which is, at bottom, what it means to open the hand.

### The Gallery of Masks

In love, you have now seen the grip head on. But it lives in more places of your life than you suppose, and each door must be opened.

In motherhood, this face has its most powerful disguise, because the nearness between a mother's love and the fear of losing the child is so great that there is almost no gap between them. A mother who fears losing her child can spend her life protecting him from what he needs. From the fall that would teach him to get up. From the no that would give him structure. From the world that would give him world. The maternal grip always calls itself love, and it is love, but a love contaminated by a fear that is not the child's, it is the mother's, the fear of seeing him suffer, which she turns into not letting him live. The child raised within a grip learns two things at once: that he is greatly loved, and that the world is too dangerous for him. He grows up needing love and fearing everything, exactly like the person who raised him, and the grip passes from generation to generation dressed as devotion.

Notice what happens when the child grows, because that is where the maternal grip hurts most. Every child must leave, that is the task of life, to become a person of one's own who no longer needs the mother as she once needed her. A mother who loves toward freedom feels that departure with a sweet sorrow, the fitting sorrow of one who has done her task well and now watches its fruit go. A mother caught in the grip lives the departure as an abandonment, almost a betrayal, and without meaning to she holds it back, with guilt, with complaints, with a need that binds the child by a thread of obligation. The child, then, is divided between his own life and the fear of hurting his mother, and either he leaves with a guilt that pursues him, or he does not leave at all and stays, held fast, living half a life so as not to forsake the one who should have launched him. The highest maternal love is the one that prepares, from the very start, to become unnecessary. The maternal grip is the one that needs to go on being necessary, and sacrifices the child's freedom to that need, calling it love.

In friendships, the grip appears as the disproportionate hurt when the other person has a life without you. The friend who made a plan and did not call you. The group that gathered and you found out afterward. The pain this gives you, when it is too great for the size of the fact, is not about the friendship. It is the grip tightening, reading the other's autonomy as abandonment, unable to bear that the one you love exists outside your field of vision. Healthy friendships breathe. They come and go, they absent themselves and return, and trust is precisely the ability to bear the absence without reading it as an end. The grip does not trust. The grip needs to be always watching, because for it what it does not see is disappearing.

There is a grip turned toward the other's past, and it is one of the strangest and most painful, because it grips a thing that can no longer be changed. It is retrospective jealousy, the inability to bear that the one you love had a life before you, other loves, other stories, a past that did not include you. You ask questions whose answers will hurt you, you dig up what is already gone, you suffer over things that happened before you even knew the person, and you do not see the absurdity of this, because the grip does not reason, it feels, and what it feels is that any part of the other's life that is not yours is a threat, even the part that ended long before you met. This jealousy protects nothing, because there is nothing to protect in the past, it only corrodes the present with a useless pain, and poisons a good relationship with the ghost of relationships that no longer exist. Beneath it dwells the same fear as always, now in its most impossible form: the desire to possess the other whole, not only their present and their future, but their past as well, to have all of them, with no remainder, without a single piece that belonged to someone else, a desire no human being can satisfy, because no one comes into our hands without a whole life already lived before.

And there is the grip that tightens through guilt, which is the most silent and the most effective of all, because it does not look like control at all. You give no orders, you make no scenes, you forbid nothing. You are hurt, and you show that you are, and your hurt becomes an invisible chain that binds the other far better than any prohibition. He stops doing the things that hurt you, not because you forbid them, but because he cannot bear your sadness, your heavy silence, your quiet disappointment. So, without a single order, you control the movements of the one you love, through the guilt your hurt makes him feel, and you call this being sensitive, being easily hurt, when it is, at bottom, the grip in its finest form, the one that tightens without touching, that binds without bars, that gets the other to limit himself out of fear of wounding you. It is a control that disguises itself as fragility, and it is one of the hardest to let go of, because whoever exercises it feels, sincerely, the victim, and does not see that her hurt, real and all, has become the hand that closes, the instrument with which, without admitting it, she shrinks the freedom of the one she claims to love.

At work and with money, the same face dresses itself as a difficulty in delegating, in letting go of a project, in trusting others to do it their way. You cling to control of the process with the same hand you cling to people, because there too, underneath, dwells the fear that if you let go it will be lost. She is the boss who redoes the whole team's work and then complains of being overloaded, without seeing that the overload is the price of the grip, the cost of never managing to trust enough to open the hand. And there is a price she does not see: a team entrusted with nothing never grows, never gains the hands it would gain if it were allowed to err and learn, and so the grip, which tightens to guarantee that all goes well, manufactures around itself people smaller than they could be, and then complains that she has to do everything alone, without seeing that it was her own grip that prevented others from growing enough to help her.

With ageing parents, the grip has a particularly poignant mask, because it reverses the roles and no one taught her to do it. A day comes when the parents begin to decline, to need, to walk toward the end, and the grip, faced with this, refuses. It does not let them grow old, it does not accept their frailty, it fights against every sign that they are leaving, and it calls this caring, and there is care in it, but there is also a refusal to let go that deprives them of the dignity of their own end. A father has the right to grow old in his own way, to decide about his life while he can, to draw near to death without being managed like a child by a son who cannot bear to lose him. The grip, afraid of the loss that approaches, grips the father in his final years as it gripped the others, and turns the time that remained for encounter into a time of control, robbing both of the serene farewell they would have had if the hand had opened in time. To accompany the one who departs, without binding them, to let them go with tenderness rather than hold them with anguish, is perhaps the hardest gesture of this face, and the most loving, because it is loving even at the point where love has to accept the very separation it most feared.

And in faith, the grip appears in a form that many religious people have never recognised as fear: that of demanding guarantees from God, or from life. It is the prayer that is, at bottom, a negotiation, a plea that nothing change, that no one fall ill, that loss not come, as if faith were an insurance against separation. It is clinging to a certainty about the beyond, or about divine protection, with the same hand with which one clings to people, so as not to have to feel the uncertainty and the loss that are part of being alive. But a faith that is a grip is not trust, it is trust's forgery, because true trust demands no guarantees, it gives itself without them, it accepts that life has loss within it and loves it all the same. The faith that holds without gripping does not ask God to abolish separation, it asks for the courage to pass through it without shattering, and that is a faith of open hands, unlike the other, which prays with a closed fist asking that nothing it loves ever be taken from it, a plea that life, sooner or later, cannot grant.

And there is the grip turned toward the past, which is the most silent of all. It is the inability to let go of what has already gone. The relationship that ended years ago and still occupies the best room in your mind. The person who died and whose place you keep intact, not out of memory, which is sacred, but out of a refusal to go on living that you confuse with fidelity. There is an enormous difference between honouring the one who is gone and staying clung to the pain of the loss like someone clinging to the last thing that still ties them to you. The first frees you to love again without betraying anyone. The second keeps the fist closed over an absence, and a hand closed over emptiness can receive nothing more.

Let me linger on this grip of the past, because it is one of those that binds most people without their knowing its name. When we lose someone, whether by death or by the end of a relationship, the pain of the loss becomes, strangely, the last tie that binds us to the person. As long as it hurts, they are still here, in some way, present in the very absence. And so a part of us clings to the pain, is afraid to let it go, because to let go of the pain seems to let the person depart for good, seems a second loss on top of the first, almost a betrayal. So one spends years feeding a suffering that no longer serves the love, it serves only the fear of letting go. But the truth, and it is a hard and liberating truth, is that to honour the one who is gone is not to stay bound to the pain of their absence, it is to let their passage through us go on bearing fruit in the life we go on living. The dead we loved do not ask for our paralysis. They ask, if they ask anything, that what we were with them go on living in us, and a heart paralysed by pain goes on with nothing, it only halts.

There is yet another mask of the grip that almost no one recognises as a grip, because it wears the most touching clothes of all: worry. It is the grip that tightens through anxiety. It does not control the other with orders, it controls them with its own fear. You stay awake until he arrives, and he knows it, and that loving vigil weighs on him like a chain. You fall ill with dread when the child takes a risk, and the child learns to hide his risks from you, not because he does not love you, but to spare you the suffering, and so worry, which gave itself as love, drives away instead of drawing near, teaches the other to lie out of kindness. A person who worries too much seems the most devoted of all, and that is why this mask is so hard to remove: who would tell her that her dread, so clearly love, is also a form of holding? But notice the effect, which is the test as always. The worry that is love strengthens the other, trusts him, accompanies him without weighing on him. The worry that is a grip weakens him, transmits to him that the world is dangerous and that he is not capable, and binds him to a dull guilt at making her suffer every time he lives. The highest love for the one who departs into the world is not to worry without rest. It is to trust, which is the hardest and most generous form of loving, because it demands that you let go of the illusion that your dread protects him, when what it does is weigh upon his shoulders his whole life long.

And there is the grip that disguises itself as indispensability, the most cunning of all, because it does not grip the other, it binds them through dependence. Instead of holding the one you love with your hands, you make it so they cannot live without your hands. You take charge of everything, you solve everything, you become the only one who knows where things are, how they are done, what is needed, and so, without an order, without a visible jealousy, you guarantee that the other cannot leave you because they do not know how to be without you. You call this caring, and there is much care in it, but underneath dwells the same fear of loss, making its coldest calculation: if he needs me, he will not leave me. It is an invisible grip, made not of tightening but of manufactured dependence, and its price is double, because it shrinks the other, who never learns to fend for himself, and it imprisons you, who become bound to being indispensable, with no right to tire, no right to fall ill, no right to stop, because you have built a house that collapses if you leave it one day. Whoever loves toward freedom wants to become, in time, a little dispensable, because they know that the love that stays by choice is worth infinitely more than the love that stays for not knowing how to leave.

### The Edge

Now the honesty, without which this lens becomes cruel. Not all nearness is a grip. Not all care is control. Not all pain of losing is a sick fear.

Love truly binds, and that true binding makes loss hurt. That is not the grip, it is the just price of having loved, and whoever never wants to pay that price ends up loving nothing with weight. To want to be near the one you love is healthy. To feel the absence is healthy. To weep a loss with a pain that seems not to fit in the body is healthy, it is the most healthy thing there is, it is love showing its size after the end. Do not confuse grief with the grip. Grief is the hand opening slowly, hurting because it opens. The grip is the hand that refuses to open and calls its rigidity fidelity.

There is also a care that holds without binding, and one must know how to see it so as not to shame it. The mother who protects her child from a real danger is not the grip, it is the work of being a mother. Whoever cares for someone ill and organises everything around them is not control, it is love in action. The sign, once again, is in the effect. The care that is love leaves the other larger, more capable, more free in time. The care that is a grip leaves them smaller, more dependent, more diminished from year to year. Look at the one you care for and ask in which direction that person is growing at your side. The answer tells you which of the two hands you have been using.

And there is something that must be said in favour of the very pain of loss, so that this face does not make you ashamed of suffering. To suffer a loss is not weakness, nor lack of faith, nor sick attachment. It is the price of love, and it is a just price, and whoever wants to avoid it at all costs ends up loving nothing with weight, living a lukewarm life in which nothing hurts because nothing matters. Grief is not the enemy of this chapter. The grip is. Grief is the hand opening slowly, hurting because it lets go, and that pain is sacred, it is love paying its last homage to the one who departed. Do not confuse your legitimate sadness with the sickness we spoke of. You can weep a loss for years and have no grip at all, if that pain does not paralyse your life nor keep you from going on loving. And you can shed not a single tear and have the whole grip, if you refuse to let go, if you control so as not to lose, if you suffocate the one you love with the fear of losing them. The measure was never how much it hurts. It was whether your hand, in the end, opens or closes, whether your love frees or imprisons, whether what you feel serves the other's life or only your own dread.

And I want to add something that saves this edge from turning into hardness. Feeling jealousy, once in a while, does not make you a person caught in the grip. Jealousy is a human emotion, it visits almost everyone, and feeling it is not a defect, it is being part of the species. The question is never feeling, it is what you do with what you feel. There is an enormous difference between feeling a pang of jealousy and recognising it as an alarm of your own, an insecurity that is yours to tend, and feeling jealousy and turning it into an accusation, an interrogation, a control that makes the other pay for your fear. The first is emotional honesty, and it can even draw you closer, when you say with humility that you are feeling insecure and that you know it is yours. The second is the grip. The same emotion, two opposite destinies, and what separates them is only this: whether you tend to your fear yourself, or hand it to the other as a bill to be paid.

And it must be said, so that this face does not make you fear connection itself, that needing others is not the grip. We are beings who depend on one another, and that dependence is not weakness, it is the condition of our species, it is what kept us alive and what makes us human. A person who prides herself on needing no one is not cured of the grip, she is on the other side of the same fear, so afraid of losing that she has decided not to bind herself, so as to have nothing to lose. That is not freedom, it is a worse prison, that of one who defends herself against love in order to defend herself against loss, and ends up safe from both, alone and dry. Health is not needing no one, it is needing without gripping, it is depending and letting others depend without that turning into possession, it is the gentle interdependence of one who leans on others and lets them lean on her, without binding anyone. There is an enormous difference between needing the other and clutching them. To need is human, it is the fabric of shared life. To clutch is fear turning need into a chain. You can need the one you love greatly and have an open hand, and you can need little and have a closed fist, because the grip is not measured by need, it is measured by the tightening, and there are those who love with great need and no tightening at all, which is, in the end, the most human way to love.

### What Disarms This Face

The relational ontology we have already met has, about this face, a particularly liberating word, and it must be said with care so as not to turn it into cheap consolation. Where it is understood that I am because we are, the person you love is not a possession you can lose. She is a part of the fabric of which you too are made, and that fabric does not come undone because she has a life of her own. On the contrary, it is her own life that keeps alive the thread that binds you. In that way of seeing, no one belongs to anyone, and yet everyone belongs to everyone, because belonging is not clutching, it is being made of the same. The grip tightens because it believes the other is its own and can flee from it. Where the bond is understood as fabric and not as possession, there is nowhere to flee, because there is not the separation the grip spends its life trying to prevent.

There is here a reversal that can change the very nature of your love, if you let it in. The grip loves out of lack, it loves to fill a hole, it needs the other so as not to feel an emptiness, and so the other becomes a function, a hole-filler, and the love is held hostage to the fear of the hole reappearing. There is another way to love, born not of lack but of fullness, that loves out of overflow and not out of want, that wants the other not to complete itself but to share a life that was already whole before they arrived. That way does not grip, because it does not need, and by not gripping it lets the other breathe, and by letting them breathe it is precisely the one that retains most, because people stay where they can breathe and flee from where they suffocate. It is one of the deepest paradoxes of the life of the heart: whoever grips least is whoever loses least, and whoever grips most is whoever, by gripping, manufactures the very loss they fear.

There is a barer truth still, which these cultures never hid and which we spend our lives hiding from ourselves: everything you love, you will lose. Not as a threat, as a fact. People depart, phases end, children grow and go, bodies die, including yours. The grip lives fleeing from this truth, and it is that flight that wears it out. On the day you look at it head on, without drama, like one who accepts the law of the house, a strange thing happens. The hand begins to open, not out of despair, but out of wisdom. You love better what you know you will lose, because you stop trying to keep it and begin to be present while it lasts. The awareness of loss, looked at without terror, is the greatest teacher of presence there is. The grip robs you of the present by trying to hold the future. Open it, and the present is given back to you whole.

I am not asking you to accept this truth all at once, that would be asking too much and too soon. I ask you a smaller thing, one that fits in a gesture. The next time you feel your hand closing over the one you love, the jealousy rising, the urge to control, the panic of an absence, stop for an instant and ask a simple question: what do I really want for this person, their good, or my own security? And if the honest answer is your own security, do not condemn yourself, only notice, and then make a small gesture in the opposite direction, let her go to that plan, do not ask the question you were about to ask, bear the absence without filling it with messages. Each time you open the hand one finger, even afraid, you teach your body that one can love without gripping and that the world does not end, and it is so, finger by finger, and not all at once, that a hand closed for a lifetime learns, slowly, to hold without binding.

There is, about the deepest loss of all, that of the death of those we love, a wisdom in those same cultures that disarms the grip in a way our own way of seeing scarcely allows. Among us, the dead one goes, is cut off, disappears, and grief is the solitary management of that absolute cut. In many African traditions it is not so. The one who dies does not stop being part of the community, they change place within it. They continue to be named, they continue present, they continue to belong to the living as an ancestor, and as long as there is someone who remembers them and includes them, they are not separated, they are here in another way. The bond is not broken by death, it is transformed, and love is not left without a recipient, it continues to have somewhere to go. I do not bring you this as a doctrine to adopt, nor do I know what lies beyond death, I have said it already and I say it again. I bring it to you as another possible way to feel loss, one in which the one who departs does not fall into an absolute void but passes into a different presence, and in which your role is not to cling to the pain to keep them with you, it is to go on weaving them into your life, to include them, to let them be what they can no longer be but continue to be in you. The grip grips the dead in pain because it believes that only pain retains them. This other way of looking tells it that there are sweeter ways to retain, made of living memory and not of paralysed suffering, and that to let go of the pain is not to let go of the person, it is to let them pass from the place of wound to the place of root.

This is, perhaps, the deepest lesson this face has to give about love entire, and not only about grief. Where the person feels made of bonds, and not an island that possesses a few bonds, loss changes its nature. It goes on hurting, and it should hurt, but it does not threaten the existence of the one who remains in the same way, because the one who remains has not built their whole identity on the possession of an other. An island that loses its only bridge is isolated and fears sinking. A person woven of many bonds who loses one of them is wounded, but remains woven, remains held within the fabric, and so can weep the loss without being destroyed by it, can open the hand over the one who departed without feeling left without ground. The grip is the gesture of one who feels themselves an island and treats each bond as the only bridge. The cure is to discover that you were never an island, that you are made of many connections and not the hostage of a single one, and that love, when it is not the only thing holding you upright, can be loved with open hands, because it no longer carries, alone, the impossible weight of keeping you from falling.

### The Final Turn

I will not ask you to love less, that would be asking you to live less. The question with which you close this chapter is another, and more delicate: does your love, today, make the beloved larger or smaller? Does it let her breathe and grow and have a life that does not all pass through you, or does it grip her slowly in the name of not losing her?

And if the answer hurts you, do not use it against yourself. Use it to notice, the next time you feel your hand closing, the fear that lies beneath the gesture. It does not need to be fought. It needs to be seen, called by its right name, and to know that what you are about to call love is, in that instant, the fear of losing. That alone already changes the temperature of the hand. To see the grip is the beginning of being able to open it.

And do not deceive yourself into thinking that opening the hand is loving less. It is the opposite. It takes more love to hold someone without gripping them than to bind them, because opening the hand demands that you bear your own anguish without discharging it onto the other, that you trust where you were afraid, that you let live the one you long to keep. The grip is the easy path, the one fear takes on its own. The open hand is the hard path, the one only love manages when it decides to be greater than the fear that accompanies it. So, each time you open the hand, you are not loving less, you are loving better, loving in the highest form there is, the one that wants the other's good above its own peace, and it is that, and not the tightening, that is the true measure of how much you love.

There is more for you than this tightening. There is a way of loving that does not live haunted by the end, that holds without binding, that rejoices in the other's life instead of watching over it. That way is not strange to you. You have felt it already, in rare moments, when the fear lowered its guard and you loved someone with no reckonings and no conditions, only for the joy of their existing. I see that capacity in you. It is beneath the grip, waiting for you to open the hand. And when you open it, you will discover that the next face of fear does not fear losing a person, but losing the very ground you need in order to live, the bread, the sustenance, what keeps you upright when no one is watching.
