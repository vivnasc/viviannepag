# CHAPTER 7
## Separation · The Abyss

We have come to the room that was always beneath the others.

You have travelled through six faces of fear, and perhaps, along the way, you felt a suspicion grow, that sense that, however different they seemed, you were always looking at the same thing seen from different angles. You were right. Rejection, loss, scarcity, uncertainty, exposure, insignificance, are not six fears. They are six faces of a single one, six tongues in which it speaks itself, six doors into the same dark room. This chapter is about what is in that room, and it is the hardest of them all, because it is the only one with no mask. The others disguised themselves as virtues. This one does not disguise itself. It only hides, behind the others, so as not to be looked at directly. I call it the Abyss, and it is the fear of separating from everything, the fear of ceasing to be.

### The Root That Generates the Faces

Notice how each of the six faces feared a separation, and how each separation was a rehearsal, a fragment, a bearable version of the total separation.

Rejection feared separating you from the group. Loss feared separating you from those you love. Scarcity feared separating you from the sustenance that keeps you alive. Uncertainty feared separating you from the firm ground where you rest your feet. Exposure feared separating you from the safety of anonymity, laying you open to a judgment that could exclude you. Insignificance feared separating you from the memory of others, being forgotten. Six separations. And beneath them all, the same one that none of them dared to name: the separation from everything, the end of connection, the ceasing to exist. Each face was a way of managing, in miniature, the terror you could not look at in full. You were afraid of being left out because, deep down, being left out sounds like disappearing. You were afraid of losing those you love because, deep down, the death of the other is the rehearsal of your own. You were afraid of not mattering because, deep down, ceasing to matter is the antechamber of ceasing to be.

The Abyss is the root, and the six faces are the branches. That is why, when you cut one branch, another was born. You worked on your fear of rejection and it reappeared as control. You cured the control and it came back as perfectionism. Not because you did the work badly, but because you were pruning the tree by its leaves, and the root remained intact, always feeding new branches. That is why this chapter comes at the end. Only after recognising the six masks did it make sense to descend to the root, and to see that working on it is the only way for the whole tree to change.

I want you to keep this image of the tree, because it explains something that may have frustrated you your whole life. You have surely tried, at some point, to correct one of your fears. You worked on it, you made an effort, and perhaps you even managed to improve on that point, to stop being so jealous, or so dependent on approval, or so controlling. And then, with time, either the same fear returned by another route, or a different fear took the place of the one you had tamed. It was not for lack of commitment. It was because you were dealing with a branch, and the energy that ran through that branch, unable to leave by there, found another way to run. The root was not touched, and an intact root produces branches indefinitely. That is why I do not propose, in this chapter, to correct one more fear. I propose that you descend, at last, to the root from which all of them spring, because it is the only work that changes the whole tree instead of trimming one leaf at a time.

### The Fear That Does Not Let Itself Be Looked At

The Abyss has a characteristic the other fears do not have: you cannot feel it directly for very long. You can think about death, you can speak of it, you can even believe you have made your peace with it, but there is a difference between thinking about your non-existence and truly feeling it, and that second thing the mind cannot bear for more than instants. Try, now, to truly feel that you will cease to exist, not as an idea, but as an imminent fact. Notice how your mind flees, changes the subject, finds a distraction, turns the thing abstract. It is not weakness. It is protection. The Abyss is so deep that to look at it directly for very long would disorganise your capacity to live the day, and so the mind built, over the course of a whole life, an entire system of detours so that you would not have to look at it.

The six faces are that system. They are the way you found to keep the Abyss at a distance, giving the fear smaller and more manageable targets. It is easier to fear that a friend will not call you than to fear dying. It is easier to worry about money, about the judgment of others, about recognition, than to feel the emptiness that waits at the end of everything. The faces are, in a strange sense, a mercy. They spread the terror of the Abyss across a thousand small and bearable fears, so that you do not have to feel it all at once. The problem is not that you have this system. The problem is that you live your whole life inside it without knowing it exists, governed by a fear whose root you never looked at, putting out fires that keep relighting because the ember beneath is intact.

There is something our age does that almost no other age did, and that makes this fear more powerful, not less. We hid death away. We took it out of the home, we put it in hospitals, out of sight, and we organised life so as to almost never meet it. The generations before us lived with death at the door, saw it up close, accompanied it, and so, in a certain way, they knew it, and what is known frightens in another way, a gentler one. We, who hid it away so well, became the most frightened of all, because we have with it the relationship one has with everything one refuses to look at, a relationship of pure terror, with no intimacy to temper it. And because we do not look at it directly, it comes out on every side in disguise, in our anxieties, in our hurry, in our incapacity to stop, in our hunger for signs that we are still alive and still counting. The more you hide the Abyss, the more it governs you from below, because a hidden fear does not stop acting, it only stops being seen to act.

And here is the deepest paradox of this face, the one that gives meaning to looking at it directly instead of hiding it: those who make their peace with death live more, not less. It seems the opposite of what instinct says. Instinct commands you to hide death, not to think about it, to distract yourself from it, and it promises that this way you will live better. But the experience of almost everyone who has been near the end, and survived, says the opposite. Those who have touched their own death, in an illness, in an accident, in a loss that brought them close to the abyss, often return transformed, not more frightened, but more alive, more present, more able to enjoy what they used to waste, freer from what used to bind them. Because the consciousness of death, looked at directly instead of hidden, does something nothing else does: it shows what matters. Before the end, small things shrink and great things grow, what was vanity falls away and what was essential appears, and the person sees, suddenly, with a clarity that distracted life never gave them, where they had been wasting their counted time. I do not propose that you live obsessed with death, that would be another sickness. I propose that you let it, from time to time, enter the room, because its presence, far from taking your life away, gives it back to you, by reminding you that it is brief, that it is counted, that it is precious for that very reason, and that the time you spend defending yourself from it is time that the defence itself is stealing from you, from the only life you have.

It is worth seeing how the cultures that did not hide death used it, unlike us, as a teacher and not as a monster. There were whole traditions that put death in plain sight on purpose, that remembered it in their rituals, in their art, in their everyday words, not out of morbidity, but out of wisdom, because they understood that to remember that one is going to die is the strongest remedy against the postponed life. A person who remembers, every day, that they are mortal does not postpone what matters, does not save love for later, does not leave the essential waiting for a future that may not come. The denial of death, which our age has taken further than any other, promised to spare us the fear and gave us, in exchange, infinite postponement, the illusion that there is always time, and that illusion is, itself, a way of already being half dead, of crossing life as if it were not going to end, and for that very reason without the urgency of living it. To look at death is not the opposite of loving life. It is, very often, its condition, because only what is known to end is lived with the intensity of one who knows it does not come back.

### Why Separation Is the Deepest Terror

It is worth asking why separation, and not something else, is at the root. Why the ceasing to be is the ultimate terror, the one of which all the others are a rehearsal.

The answer lies at the beginning of everything. You came from a unity. Before you were born, there was no separation between you and that which sustained you, you were part of a body, without frontier, without lack, without absence. Birth was the first separation, the first cut, the first time you became a distinct being, with a skin that separated you from the rest, with a hunger that was only sated if another came, with an underlying solitude you would spend your life trying to remedy. All individual existence begins with a separation, and carries with it, from the very first instant, the memory of a lost unity and the presentiment of a final separation. To be alive, as an individual, is to be separated, and the fear of the Abyss is, at bottom, the fear of that condition taken to the extreme, the total and definitive separation that is death.

That is why we spend our lives trying to return to the unity, without knowing that is what we are doing. We seek in love the fusion that would undo the separation. We seek in the group the belonging that would mend the solitude. We seek in fame the permanence that would deny the end. We seek in a thousand things the same one, a return to that state prior to the frontier, in which there was no lack because there was no separation. And all those searches, when they come from fear, fail for the same reason: they try to undo the separation from the outside, by accumulating connections, possessions, recognitions, when the separation that hurts is a condition from within, that of being a distinct being in a world of other distinct beings, and that one is not resolved by accumulating, it is resolved in another way, which is the only thing this book, in the end, has to offer you.

Notice how this illuminates your whole amorous and social life in another way. That intensity with which you seek fusion in love, the wish to be one with the other, to dissolve the frontiers, that is not only passion, it is the ancient attempt to undo the original separation, to return to that state without frontier from before you were born. That hunger to belong to a group, to dissolve yourself into a cause, into a crowd, into a shared identity, is the same attempt by another route. Our whole life of connection is, in part, an attempt to mend the cut of birth, to recover the lost unity. And there is nothing wrong with seeking connection, it is one of the most beautiful things we do. The error lies in seeking it in order to abolish the separation, because the separation cannot be abolished, it is part of being alive as an individual, and whoever tries to abolish it through fusion ends up suffocating the other and themselves, asking a person to do what no person can do, which is to erase the frontier that makes you be you. Mature connection does not abolish separation. It coexists with it, it unites without fusing, and that is why it gives peace where fusion gives only vertigo.

It is worth seeing how this fear is built in a child, because it explains why it is so universal and so deep. A baby, in the first times, does not distinguish itself from the world around it, does not know where it ends and the mother begins, lives in a continuity without frontier that is the remnant of that unity prior to birth. And then, slowly, the self is formed, the child discovers that it is a distinct person, separate, with a body of its own that feels hunger and cold, and that discovery, which is necessary and good, because that is how we become people, brings with it the first terror, that of being alone, of being able to be left, of the mother going and not returning. Every child goes through this, this anguish of separation, and the way it goes through it, whether it was held or left, whether the lap returned or was missing, marks the force with which the Abyss will grip it its whole life. But no child escapes entirely, because to become a separate self is, in itself, the original wound, the price of existing as an individual, and we all paid it, and we all carry, beneath the adult life, the memory of that first terror of being left alone in the world, which is the seed from which the six faces will sprout.

And it is worth seeing, too, that almost everything humanity built of its highest was, in part, a response to this fear. The religions, which promise that death is not the end, that there is a continuity, that you are not alone in the universe. Philosophy, which for millennia has turned around the question of what we are and what happens when we cease to be. Art, which tries to fix what passes, to halt time, to leave a mark against oblivion. Love, which takes us, for instants, out of the solitude of the island. None of this is flight, it is response, it is what a creature conscious of its death invented in order to live with that knowledge. And there is a grandeur in this, not only a fear: the same terror that generates the small anxieties of the six faces also generates the cathedrals, the poems, the great questions, the great loves. The Abyss is not only our curse. It is, looked at directly, the source of almost all our depth, because a creature that did not know it was going to die would have no art, no religion, no philosophy, no urgency to love, it would live as an animal lives, without the weight and without the height that that weight, borne, gives us.

### Rereading the Six in the Light of the Root

Come back now, with me, to each of the faces, and see it again with the Abyss beneath.

That yes you gave out of fear of rejection, do you remember it? See it now deeply. You did not only fear displeasing. You feared, beneath, that displeasing would separate you from the group, and that, separated from the group, you would begin to cease to exist, because your ancient body knows that outside the group one dies. Each time you gave in to please, you were buying, with a piece of yourself, the guarantee of remaining inside, of continuing to be. Rejection was the Abyss dressed as a living room.

That fist you closed over those you love, do you remember? See it deeply. You did not only fear losing a person. You feared that losing them would take from you a piece of yourself that existed only in the connection, and that losing pieces of yourself was a rehearsal of losing yourself entirely. Grief hurts so much because it is a small death, the death of the part of you that dwelt in the other. Loss was the Abyss dressed as love.

The endless counting of scarcity, the winter that never warmed however much you gathered, see it deeply. You did not only fear the lack of bread. You feared that the lack would leave you exposed to an end, that without reserves the world would erase you, that material insecurity was an antechamber of the ultimate insecurity, that of not holding yourself alive. Scarcity was the Abyss dressed as a bank account.

The walls of the Fortress, the need to know what is coming, see them deeply. You did not only fear surprise. You feared that the greatest surprise of all, the only truly uncontrollable one, awaited you at the end of all your plans, and you built walls against the small unknown so as not to feel the total unknown, the one no plan covers. Uncertainty was the Abyss dressed as a schedule.

The hiding place from the Light, the fear of being seen and judged, see it deeply. You did not only fear the judgment of others. You feared that the judgment would exclude you, that being seen from below or from above would separate you from the safe milieu, and that, separated, you would be left exposed to the end. And, deeper still, total exposure, that of being left completely in view with no defence whatsoever, is what happens in death, the moment when you can no longer manage the image you give. Exposure was the Abyss dressed as a stage.

And the hunger of the Erasure, the need to matter, to leave a mark, see it deeply. It was not only the wish to signify. It was the terror that, forgotten, it would be as if you had never existed, and oblivion is the way death has of going on killing you after you are dead, erasing not only your body but your trace. Insignificance was the Abyss dressed as a legacy.

Six faces, a single fear. Do you see now why working on them one by one never sufficed? You were arguing with the branches. The root was here, in this room, waiting to be finally looked at.

And notice something that only becomes visible after this rereading. The six faces are not only masks of the same fear, they are also attempts to resolve it, each in its own way, and all condemned to the same failure. Rejection tries to defeat death by belonging, as if being in the group would save us from the end. Loss tries to defeat it by grasping, as if holding the other would hold us. Scarcity tries to defeat it by accumulating, as if having enough would put us out of danger. The Fortress tries to defeat it by controlling, as if knowing what comes would prevent what comes. The Light tries to defeat it by being perfect, as if having no flaws would make us immortal. The Erasure tries to defeat it by leaving a mark, as if being remembered were not dying. All of them promise a victory over the Abyss, and all of them lie, because the Abyss cannot be defeated, not by belonging, not by possession, not by reserve, not by control, not by perfection, nor by fame. And that is why none of the faces satisfies: not because you do too little of each one, but because each one pursues an impossible victory, a salvation from death that none of them can give.

Notice what this changes in the way you can treat yourself, because this is where compassion is born. While you looked at each face as a defect of yours, a bad trait of your character, you treated yourself with the severity of one who has a flaw to correct. Now you see something else. You see that each of the six was an attempt, clumsy but moving, to save yourself from something terrifying, the separation, the end, the ceasing to be. Your jealousy was not pettiness, it was terror of losing. Your hunger for approval was not weakness, it was fear of being left out and disappearing. Your control was not obsession, it was panic before the ultimate uncontrollable. Your vanity was not emptiness, it was a struggle against oblivion. Seen this way, your faces cease to be reasons for shame and become reasons for tenderness, because they are what a mortal creature conscious of its death invented in order to live despite knowing it is going to end. There is nothing here to despise. There is a person doing what they can with the heaviest weight there is, that of knowing they are finite, and that does not ask for judgment, it asks for compassion, the same you would feel for any frightened creature defending itself from the dark.

And you see, too, why this fear is the most universal of all, the one that truly chooses no person. The other faces grip some people more than others, according to each one's history. This one grips everyone, because we are all going to die, and we all know we are going to, and we are, as far as we know, the only creature that carries that knowledge its whole life. An animal flees from death when it threatens, and the rest of the time it lives, without the weight of knowing it. We know it always, even on good days, even in health, even in joy, and it is that knowing, and not death itself, that haunts us, because death comes only once and the knowledge of it lasts a whole life. This could be our curse, and for whoever does not look at it, it is, turned into the thousand anxieties of the six faces. But it can be something else, for whoever looks at it: the source of our depth, of our art, of our conscious love, of everything great we do precisely because we know it is brief. The same knowing that generates the terror also generates what we have that is highest, and the difference between one thing and the other lies not in the knowing, which is equal in all, it lies in hiding it or looking at it.

There is a teacher of the Abyss that almost everyone meets, sooner or later, and that one does not choose: the loss of those we love. Each time you lose someone, you touch separation in its most naked form, and grief is, at bottom, a rehearsal of your own death, an anticipation in miniature of the end that awaits you. That is why grief so transforms whoever goes through it seriously, without fleeing from it. Whoever lost someone they loved, and looked at the loss directly instead of anaesthetising it, often returns changed, with a new clarity about what matters, with a gentle hurry to love those still here, with a strange lightness in relation to the small things that used to bind them. The death of those we love opens a window onto the Abyss, and through that window, though it hurts as few things hurt, a light also enters, the one that makes each day more precious and each love more urgent. I do not wish you losses, no one wishes them. But when they come, and they will, you can live them in two ways: fleeing the window they open, closing it quickly with distractions and hurry, and then you suffer the loss without gathering its wisdom; or looking through it, with all the pain, and letting the nearness of the Abyss teach you, as only it teaches, the terrible and precious value of being alive for a counted time, among others you love and whom you too will lose, and whom for that very reason you must love now, while they last, while you last.

### The Edge, Here Too

And even in this room, the deepest of all, the lens must be given edges, because here too it can blind if it means to explain everything.

Not all attachment to life is a sick fear of death. The will to live is the cleanest force there is, it is life itself wanting to continue, and there is nothing pathological in loving being alive, in not wanting to depart, in holding to what you have with gratitude. The fear of death, in its just form, is the wisdom of the body, it is what makes you swerve from the car, take care of yourself, protect those you love. Not all consciousness of the end is terror. There is a serene consciousness of death that does not paralyse, it deepens, that makes each day more precious precisely because it is counted. To confuse all love of life with fear of the Abyss would be the final error, that of turning even the will to live into a symptom. It is not. The will to live is health. What this book looked at was something else, it was the fear that, instead of making you live more, makes you live less, protecting you from an inevitable end at the cost of all the time you have before it.

There is still something the lens does not reach and that must be said with humility. I do not know what there is after. No one knows, however much many claim to know. This book does not come to tell you that death is the end, nor that it is not, that is not its matter. It comes to say only this: whatever there may be after, the fear of the Abyss is stealing the before from you, the life you have now, and it is over that one that you have some power. What comes after will come. What there is before, you are living it, or avoiding it, at this moment.

And let me tell you where this lens, that of the whole book, also has its limit, because a book that knows where it stops deserves more trust than one that explains everything. Not everything in you is fear of separation. There is in you a joy that is no defence at all, a laughter that protects from nothing, a love that manages no terror, a curiosity that flees from nothing, a pleasure of being alive that is prior to any fear and deeper than it. If this book left you with the impression that you are, at bottom, only a frightened creature defending itself from the end, then I failed, and I must correct myself now, in the last room. Fear is a great part of you, a part worth knowing, and it is that part we spoke of, because it was that part that was governing you unseen. But beneath the fear, and prior to it, there is something that does not fear, that only lives, and it is of that one the epilogue will speak, because it is that one the fear went about hiding and it is to that one I want to return you.

### What Makes the Root Loosen

Throughout the whole book, one same wisdom returned in each face, and it was not by chance. I saved it for the end, because it is here, at the root, that it yields its greatest fruit. Relational ontology, the I am because we are, is not merely a remedy for rejection, for loss, for each face separately. It is the deepest answer to the Abyss itself, because it attacks the root of the root, which is the illusion of separation.

Follow with me carefully, because it is here that the book decides itself. The Abyss is the fear of separating from everything. But what if separation, which you took from birth as your condition, were in part an illusion? What if you were not, after all, an isolated being that exists alone and fears losing its connections, but a knot in a fabric that precedes you and continues you, made of the same matter of which others, the earth, time, are made? In that way of seeing, you are not connected to others, you are the connection, you do not have relations, you are made of relations, and what you call your separate self is like the crest of a wave that thinks itself separate from the sea for an instant, without seeing that it was never anything but sea rising and falling. The wave does not die when it falls. It returns to that of which it was always made.

The person who experiences themselves this way fears the Abyss less, not because they believe a consoling doctrine, but because they never thought themselves so separate that the final separation would terrify them as it terrifies whoever thought themselves, all their life, an island. Whoever always felt themselves an island lives death as the shipwreck of the whole island, the end of everything. Whoever felt themselves a wave lives it in another way, as a return, a falling into that of which they were always a part. I do not ask you to believe this the way one adopts a religion. I ask you to notice how much of your life rested on the contrary supposition, that you are an island, separate, alone against the end, and how that supposition, which you never examined, is the fuel of all six faces. If it loosens, even a little, all the faces loosen with it, because they all fed on the same underlying solitude, that of feeling yourself a separate self in a universe of separate things.

Notice what happens to each of the six faces when the root loosens, because it is here that the whole book gathers itself into a single image. Rejection, which feared being cast out of the group, quietens in one who feels they already belong by being made of others, and turns into the capacity to speak the truth without terror, to belong without asking permission. Loss, which gripped the one it loved out of fear of losing them, quietens in one who feels the bond is fabric and not possession, and turns into a love with open hands, that holds without binding. Scarcity, which gathered against the winter in solitude, quietens in one who knows that security is in the net and not in the pantry, and turns into generosity, into the confidence that there is enough. Uncertainty, which walled the future alone, quietens in one who knows they do not face it alone, and turns into confidence, into the courage to swim rather than to bar. Exposure, which hid from the judgment of a tribunal, quietens in one who sees companions where they saw judges, and turns into the freedom to show themselves whole. And insignificance, which ran so as not to be forgotten, quietens in one who knows they continue in others even without their name, and turns into the joy of giving with no audience. They are not six different cures. It is a single one, applied in six places, the same passage from fear to connection, from island to sea, and that is why working on the root changes the whole tree, because each branch, watered from the same new source, yields a different fruit from the one it yielded when it was watered by fear.

And there is a truth about love that only fits here, at the root, because it is here that it answers the ultimate terror. Love is the nearest experience we have of undoing separation without the fusion that suffocates. When you love truly, and are truly loved, you touch, for instants, something that is beyond your island, a state in which you are not separated and yet have not ceased to be you, in which the frontier becomes porous without being erased. It is no accident that almost all the traditions that thought about death linked it to love, that they said, in a thousand ways, that love is stronger than death. I do not know if it is true in the literal sense, and I have already said I promise nothing about the after. But I know it is true in the sense of lived experience: a loved and loving life fears the end less, not because it knows more about it, but because it knew, in life, the undoing of the separation that death seemed to threaten, and so death ceased to be, for it, the absolute shipwreck of the island, and became one more passage into that of which love had already given it a taste that it was part. The answer to the Abyss is not an idea about death. It is a life lived in connection, because only whoever lived connected knows, in the body, that separation was never the whole truth about them.

There is an experience almost everyone has already had, and that points to this without needing any doctrine. Moments in which the frontier of your separate self loosened on its own, without your wanting it. Before an immense landscape, and for an instant you ceased to feel separate from it. In the midst of a crowd singing the same thing, and for a moment you did not know where you ended and the others began. Holding a newborn child, or losing yourself in a work you love, or in a deep silence, and for seconds the tight self you usually are dissolved into something larger, and what you felt was not terror, it was peace, an enormous peace, almost of home. Those moments are not flights from reality. They are, perhaps, glimpses of a reality more true than that of the island, brief cracks through which you see that the separation you take for absolute is not the bottom of things, that beneath it there is a continuity in which you are whole, and of which your separate self is only a passing wave. You do not need to build a faith on these moments. I only ask that you not despise them, because they are the proof, in your own experience, that the island is not the whole truth about you, and that there is in you a memory of sea that fear, tight in its island, spent a life forgetting.

It is worth noticing how the great traditions of humanity, which almost never agree on anything, strangely converge on this point. Some speak of a God in whom we are all one. Others speak of a web of existence in which nothing is separate, in which everything depends on everything, in which the illusion of the isolated self is the root of suffering. The African traditions speak of the I am because we are, the person made of persons. The mystics of almost all religions, when they describe the highest they reached, describe almost always the same thing, the undoing of the frontier of the separate self, the dissolution of the island into a larger unity, and they describe it always not as terror, but as peace, as home, as the end of a solitude they thought was the very condition of existing. I do not bring you this as proof of anything, because the traditions could all be wrong, and it is not the matter of this book to decide it. I bring it to you as a curious and perhaps significant fact: that people so different, in times and places so distant, without knowing one another, arrived, by paths so various, at the same intuition, that separation is not the bottom of things, that beneath it there is a unity, and that to touch that unity is what most deeply cures the fear of ceasing to be. When so many people who disagree on everything agree on one thing, it is at least worth not discarding it without looking.

And there is a simple practice, that does not require you to believe anything, and that works this root better than any theory. From time to time, in the middle of the day, stop, and notice how much of what you are came from outside, how much of you is made of others. The language in which you think, which you did not invent, which reached you from thousands of mouths before yours. The body you have, made of the food the earth gave and that other hands harvested. The ideas you take for yours, which you caught from people, from books, from conversations. The love you know how to give, which you learned from those who loved you. Notice how almost nothing in you began in you, how you are, literally, made of everything that preceded you and surrounds you, a meeting point of a thousand currents that pass through you and will continue after you. This simple noticing, done many times, slowly loosens the illusion of the island, not with an argument, but with an evidence you have before your eyes at all hours and that fear taught you not to see: that you were never a separate thing, you were always a knot of connections, and a knot does not die as an island dies, it comes undone back into the threads of which it was made, which continue, woven into other knots, long after yours has come untied.

There is a simple way to say what disarms this root, and it is the same one that ran through the whole book, now at its highest point. The opposite of the fear of separation is not the certainty of not dying, which no one has. It is connection. It is living so woven with others, with life, with what precedes you and continues you, that the final separation ceases to seem to you the fall of an island into the void and comes to seem the return of a wave to the sea. I do not give you this as a proof, because I do not have it, no one has it. I give it to you as an orientation for your life, here and now, before any end: the more connected you live, the less the Abyss governs you, and the more isolated, the more it grips you. That is why the people most frightened by death are often the loneliest, and those most serene before it are often the most connected, not because they know more about what there is after, but because they feel less separate before. The cure for the fear of ceasing to be is not in proving that you will not cease to be. It is in ceasing to live yourself as a separate thing that fears the end, and beginning to live yourself as part of something that did not begin in you nor end in you, and of which you are, for a time, a living expression.

And there is one last thing, the most practical of all, for you to take from this chapter. You do not need to resolve the Abyss, and just as well, because it cannot be resolved. You only need to recognise it when it is beneath one of the six faces, and that recognition, on its own, changes everything. The next time jealousy grips you, or the hunger for approval, or the anxiety of the future, descend for an instant and recognise what is at the bottom: it is not only this, it is the fear of ceasing to be, it is the ancient solitude of the island. Not so that you frighten yourself more, but so that you see, with clarity, the true size of what moves you, and so that you treat with tenderness, and not with judgment, the mortal creature that you are, doing what it can so as not to feel the emptiness. To call things by their true name is the most powerful thing there is against fear, because fear lives on the dark and on disguise, and a thing named, looked at, recognised, loses the power to govern you from below. It is only this I ask of you, in the end: not the victory over the Abyss, which does not exist, but the courage to look at it when it appears, because looking at it is the one thing it does not know how to do to you, and it is, for that reason, the one thing that disarms it.

And notice what this does to your relationship with all the rest of the book. You will no longer need to fight six fears, one by one, in an endless war in which each victory generates a new enemy. You will only need to remember, when any of them grips, to descend to the root and recognise it, and to treat yourself, in that recognition, with the tenderness owed to a mortal creature defending itself from the dark. It is not a work of force, it is a work of looking. And it is a work that lasts the whole life, it is not done all at once, because the Abyss always returns, disguised as a thousand things, and a thousand times you will have to recognise it anew. But each time you recognise it, it governs you a little less, and the life it was shrinking for you widens a little more, and it is in that widening, slow and endless, and not in a sudden cure that will never come, that everything this book can give you dwells.

And there is a place where the Abyss never reaches you, and it is worth knowing which one, because it is toward it that all this work, at bottom, leads you: the present. Notice that the fear of ceasing to be always lives in the future, it is always about an end that is to come, about a loss that has not yet arrived, about a tomorrow in which you will no longer be. In the now, in the exact instant in which you are alive, the Abyss has no power at all, because at this moment you are not dying, you are living, and death, however certain, is not here, it is always in another time. That is why whoever learns to inhabit the present, to be in fact where they are, to live the instant instead of spending it fearing the next, escapes the Abyss not by defeating it, but by leaving the only terrain where it operates, which is the time that has not yet come. A sunset truly looked at, a meal savoured, a conversation in which you are whole, a work that absorbs you, an embrace in which you think of nothing else, all these are places where the fear of dying does not enter, because they are full of present life, and where there is present life to the brim there is no room for the fear of the end. I do not propose that you flee from the Abyss into the present, that would be one more flight. I propose that you discover that the present, lived deeply, is the one thing death can never take from you while you have it, because death takes the future, all the future, but it cannot touch the instant you are living now, and it is in that instant, and only in it, that the whole of life has always happened.

### The Final Turn

The question with which you close this chapter, and with it almost the whole book, is the greatest of all, and it has no answer, it has only direction. If you ceased to live yourself as an island threatened with shipwreck, and began to live yourself as part of something that precedes you and continues you, what would you still be afraid of, and what would you do with the life that fear went about saving you for nothing?

Do not answer quickly. This question is not answered with the head, it is answered with the years, and each face you learn to see over the course of life will be a partial answer to it. I only ask that you take it with you. The next time one of the six faces grips you, rejection, loss, scarcity, uncertainty, exposure, insignificance, do a new thing, which you can now do because you know the root. Instead of arguing with the branch, descend for an instant to the root and recognise it: beneath this is the Abyss, is the fear of ceasing to be, is the ancient solitude of the island that thinks itself separate from the sea. You do not need to resolve it. Only to recognise it. And in that recognition, strangely, there is peace, because to look at the Abyss directly, even for an instant, is the one thing it does not know how to do to you, and what is looked at directly loses the power to govern you from below.

There is more to you than this fear, and now you know it is the same more that appeared at the end of each face, because it was always the same, seen from different angles. There is a way of existing that does not live against the end, but in the full consciousness of it, and that for that reason lives more, not less. A way of loving without a fist, of belonging without asking permission, of giving with no audience, of not knowing without terror, of showing yourself without fear of judgment, of existing without having to matter. You have already tasted it, in flashes, over the course of your life and over the course of this book. It is not a foreign land. It is your own life, when fear lowers its guard. And it is toward that life that I want to lead you in the last pages, not to promise you that fear ends, which it does not, but to ask you who you would be if your choices, at last, ceased to be organised by protection.
