# viver.soltar
### living: entering your own life
*Method VS · See and Release · Vivianne dos Santos*

> *The premiere is not next year. It is today, barefoot, with your feet on the ground that is already yours.*

## Who this book is for

This book is for you, if for a long time you have lived a step ahead of where you are, always leaning towards a point further on where, finally, you will be able to start really living. When you lose the weight. When he changes. When the children grow up. When you have the house, the money, the peace you keep putting off. And it is for you if, at the same time, you cling with both hands to an old version of yourself, to a sentence you have said about who you are for so long that it now seems to be your own face, afraid that to change would be to betray yourself.

It is for you if real life always seems to be somewhere else and at some other time, never here, never now. If you reach a goal and, instead of peace, you feel a brief emptiness and then at once the craving for the next one. If you carry an identity that already pinches you like clothes from a body that has since changed, and you defend it anyway, because who would you be without it. If you live, at the same time, waiting for what has not yet come and held back by what has already passed, and so you are almost never where your life is actually happening, which is exactly here, in this instant you barely notice because you are already in the next one.

It is not a self-help book with easy promises, nor a ten-step manual for a perfect life scheduled for later. It is the opposite of that. It will not ask you to run faster nor to arrive sooner anywhere. It will ask you only two things: that you look, with more honesty and less harshness, at the way you postpone your own life in two directions at once, and that you discover, beneath the rush and the armour, that you are already here, that you always were, and that the only place where you can finally live is this one, now, with no further condition to fulfil.

There is a question that has stayed with me for years, and it began in me. Why does someone lucid, who sees clearly that life is passing her by, keep pushing it towards a later that never comes? And why does that same person, who feels an old identity suffocating her, keep putting it on every morning, afraid of who she would be without it? For a long time I called this weakness, lack of character, lack of wanting it enough. I looked at my own cycles and concluded that, if I were more conscious, more disciplined, I would stop tripping on the same stone. And still I repeated. That was when I realised I was looking in the wrong place. The problem was not that I could not see. It was that I saw and, even so, could not do otherwise. We do not postpone life out of laziness, nor cling to who we were out of stubbornness. We do it because, at some point, running towards the future and holding on to the past were the most intelligent answers a body found in order to survive, to belong, to be loved. The conscious mind learns quickly, with a single sentence. The body learns slowly, and unlearns more slowly still. That is why knowing is not enough.

I am not writing to you from a place of arrival, and in this book that sentence carries a special weight, because the book is precisely about the illusion of arrival. I write from a path still under way, my own. I am someone who lived years with her bags packed at the door of a train that never came, always preparing a life instead of living it, and who at the same time clung tooth and nail to an image of herself. I still study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as someone who collects certainties, but as someone who needs names for what she feels. Everything I share here passed first through me, and only then did I recognise it in what echoes in so many women going round the same loops, convinced they are the only ones.

This is one of the movements of a path I call Method VS, See and Release. The initials are mine, Vivianne dos Santos, but they are also the only two gestures of the whole method: to see what holds you, to release what makes you repeat. And this book in particular is the third of three sister movements, the whole crossing told in three times: see, come, live. First you learn to see what holds you. Then you let what was underneath come. And finally, in this one, you enter your own life, which is where all of this always meant to arrive. You can read this book without ever having read the others, and use it whole. It is a short, deep manual, meant to be read in one breath and carried into life, which is where everything is decided. Read it slowly. Underline. Go back. And above all, carry the method into your days.

A word about how to read this, because the way you read is part of the method. Do not read in a hurry, in one sitting, to get to the end, because that would be to read this book with the very veil it helps you release, running to the last page as if life were there. This is not a book to finish, it is a book to inhabit. Read slowly, let each part work in you for a few days, notice where, in your concrete life, the postponing and the armour appear. Do the practices, even the ones that seem too small to you, because it is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn how to enter the present. And come back. Come back to these pages in a few months and reread them, and you will find them different, because you will be different. The books that matter are not read once. They keep us company, and tell us new things at each age of the soul.

There is one condition without which none of this works, and I want to make it clear before anything else: tenderness towards yourself. Most of us learned to change by force of harshness, of demand, of inner criticism. We point out what is missing, we scold ourselves as no one from outside would dare. And then we are surprised that nothing truly changes. Harshness is, itself, a veil, perhaps the one that clothes all the others, and with it on there is no seeing that is not judgement. So whenever, throughout these pages, you catch yourself judging yourself for postponing, for running, for clinging, swap harshness for curiosity, shame for tenderness. Treat yourself as you would treat your dearest friend learning this. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself, and the rest becomes possible.

## The movement of living

Living seems the simplest thing in the world, and yet it is what costs us most. Not living in idea, not living in plans, not living in what is still to be or in what has already been, but really living, with the whole body inside the instant that is happening. To live is to enter. It is to stop watching your own life through the window and to cross the door into the room where it is, where it always was, happening without you.

There is only one movement in this book, and it is the movement of entering. The other veils of the method each have their own gesture, but this one asks for something that seems small and is the hardest of all: to arrive where you already are. To stop projecting yourself forward and to stop holding yourself back, at the same time, and to discover that, in the middle of those two forces, there is a here that was always available and that you almost never inhabited.

Because there are two forces, and that is what makes this movement different from the others. Your life is being pulled from two sides at once, and the two of them rob you of exactly the same thing: the present. On one side, the future pulls you. You live in a "when", always waiting for a condition that has not yet been met before you start really living, and that postponing is what I call the Horizon. On the other side, the past holds you back. You cling to who you used to be, to the roles you learned to wear, to a fixed identity you defend as if your life depended on it, and that clinging is what I call Permanence. One pushes you forward, the other holds you behind. And you, in the middle, are stretched between the two like washing on a line, never set down, never whole anywhere.

Notice how they work together, because it is rare for them to come alone. You postpone your life for a better future and, at the same time, that better future is almost always a more accomplished version of the same character you already are. You will be happy when you are even more the strong one, even more the one who holds everything up, even more the one who fixes it all. The horizon you chase does not free you from permanence, it perpetuates it, because it is made in the image of the role you already defend. And permanence, in turn, needs the horizon to justify itself: you have to go on being the strong one because you have not yet arrived, because there is still so much to hold up, because rest is for later. One feeds the other. The future promises that you will finally be able to set down the armour, but only further on, and the further on never comes, and so the armour is never set down.

And between the two, crushed, is the only time where life happens. The present is not a passage between what was and what will be. It is the only place you ever had, the only one you will ever have. The past no longer exists except as a memory you retell, the future does not yet exist except as an idea you project, and you live almost always in one of those two places that do not exist, letting pass the only one that does. To live is the gesture of coming back here. Of stopping fleeing forward and stopping clinging behind, and discovering that here, in this ordinary instant, with the air going in and out of you without your having to ask for it, is the whole life you were saving for later.

Perhaps you wonder why something so simple is so hard. If the present is the only place that exists, why is it so difficult to stay in it? It is difficult because the present, unlike the future and the past, cannot be controlled. The future is the mind's territory: there you can plan, rehearse, foresee, feel that you hold the reins. The past is memory's territory: there you can retell, justify, build the story that explains who you are. But the present cannot be thought, it can only be lived, and so the mind, which learned to keep you safe through control, flees from it to the only two places where it feels in command. To live in the now demands something the mind fears above all: to let go of the helm. To trust that life happens without your having to anticipate it whole nor explain it whole. That is why entering the present feels, to a body trained to stay on guard, almost like danger. And that is why tenderness is so important here: one does not enter life by force, one enters slowly, like someone winning the trust of a frightened animal, showing it that here, now, there is none of the threat it flees from.

Notice too something about time that most of us never stopped to see. You have never lived, not even once, in the future. Not even once in the past. Your whole life, from your first cry to this exact instant, has always, always happened in a present. The happy moments you remember were lived in their present. The plans you carried out were carried out in a present. There was never, in your entire existence, a single second that was not now. And yet you spent a vast part of those nows dreaming of others or chewing over those that had passed. Your whole life has always been happening in the one time you were absent from. To live is, simply, to be present in what already is, and always was, the only real time.

There is also a difference worth keeping, between this movement and the quick fixes you may already have tried. The quick fixes promise you presence fast, as one more technique to master, one more well-being goal to reach, and so, without noticing, they turn living in the present itself into one more horizon to chase. Repeat that you are present, force the calm, reach serenity. They work for a few days, and then the rush returns, intact, because it was never seen, only painted over. This movement is the opposite, and that is why it is slower and more solid. It does not give you a perfect present to conquer in place of the perfect future you were chasing. It teaches you to stop chasing anything, and to enter the imperfect instant you already have. From outside it may look as though you are going slowly, while others promise lightning transformations. Inside, you are building the only kind of change that lasts, the kind that rests on seeing and not on flight.

I want you to notice something strange and freeing at the same time: these two forces, which seem opposite, are in fact the same refusal. She who runs towards the future refuses to be here. She who clings to the past refuses to be here. The directions are opposite, but what both avoid is identical, and it is the present. It is as though the here were, of the two, equally unbearable, and you had learned to live in any time but this one. That is why it is not enough to work one of the faces without the other. If you released only the horizon and stayed trapped in permanence, you would stop running towards the future but you would go on defending a character who does not let you inhabit the now. If you released only permanence and stayed in the horizon, you would set down the armour but you would go on pushing life towards later. It is the whole knot that has to loosen, because it is the whole knot that takes you from home.

And there is a deeper reason still to treat them together, which has to do with the fear that lives beneath both. Under the rush of the horizon there is a fear: that, as you are, you are not enough, and so you have to arrive somewhere to deserve your place. Under the firmness of permanence there is the same fear, in other clothes: that, without the armour, you are not enough, and so you have to go on being a certain version of yourself to deserve to be loved. It is the same "you are not enough" speaking through two mouths. Running and holding on are the two attempts that fear found to resolve the same wound, and that is why the healing is also a single one: discovering, in the body and not only in the idea, that you are enough now, just as you are, before any achievement and with no armour at all. It is that discovery the movement of living brings you, not as a pretty sentence to repeat, but as a ground you feel beneath your feet when you finally stop fleeing in both directions.

That is why this book treats the two veils as a single knot, because that is how they are lived. I will not ask you to resolve first the horizon and then permanence, like two tasks in a queue. I will show you the whole knot, the two forces pulling you in opposite directions, and the single movement that loosens both at once: entering your life, now, just as you are and just as it is, with no condition to fulfil and no armour to defend. To that state of arrival, at the end, I give a name: barefoot. But we will come to that. For now, it is enough that you see the knot. The two chapters that follow look at each of its faces, first the postponed life, then the prison of who you used to be, so that you recognise it whole before you begin to untie it.

## The postponed life

You live leaning forward, always a little ahead of where you are, towards a point further on where, finally, you will be able to be happy. There is always a "when" and there is always a "there", and it is towards that luminous there that you keep pushing life, while real life, the only one that exists, goes on happening whole in this hurried here you barely notice, because you are already in the next one. This is the first face of the knot, the face of the Horizon: the belief that life begins later, when everything is in place, and that for now this is only a waiting room.

I think of all the times you said "next year". Next year I will slow down, I will travel, I will take care of myself, I will do the thing I have always put off. There are women who say this for thirty years, with all sincerity, every year, and reach a certain age with the strange feeling that life passed them by while they were preparing it to begin. They were not exactly unhappy. It was worse, in a way: they were almost always halfway to a happiness scheduled for later, always with their bags packed at the door of a train that never came. Life was not in the destination. It was in the waiting room, and no one had told them that the waiting room was the journey.

This veil promises you an end. A finish line where everything is resolved at once and from which you will be, at last, complete, at peace, ready to live. The mind needs that promise to keep you moving, and so it always paints a new horizon, in lovely colours. The problem, which perhaps you have already felt without naming it, is that every time you draw near, the horizon recedes. You reach the weight you wanted and the body still falls short. You settle one debt and the next appears. You reach the goal you thought would bring you rest and, within a few days, the restlessness moves on to the next objective, as if it had never happened. It is not that you miss your targets. It is that the "there" is, by its nature, a place one does not reach, because it was made to be always ahead, like the line where the sky touches the sea and that recedes with every step we take towards it.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By living in "when" mode, always waiting for a future condition before you start living for real. By reaching a goal and feeling, instead of peace, a brief emptiness and then at once the craving for the next. By the difficulty of savouring what you already have, because your attention has already jumped to what is missing. By postponing rest, pleasure, presence, with the excuse that first you have to deserve, to resolve, to arrive. By a background dissatisfaction that no achievement silences for long. And by that sometimes frightening sense that life is passing quickly and you are always a little beside it, preparing it instead of living it. To recognise this veil does not take your dreams away. It gives you back the only place where they, and you, can happen: here.

This veil has a sophistication worth seeing, because it is the one that deceives you most: it dresses up as virtue. We call it ambition, focus, dream, personal growth, and all of that can be healthy. The difference lies in a subtle point. There is a walking that sets out from fullness and there is a running that sets out from lack. You can want to grow because life calls you to more, gladly, without ceasing to be whole on the way. Or you can run because you feel that, as you are, it is not enough, you are not enough, and only further on, with the objective achieved, will you finally have the right to rest and to like yourself. The first is movement. The second is flight disguised as movement. From outside they look the same, two women working towards an objective. Inside, one is at home while she walks, the other has been homeless for years, waiting for an arrival that postpones the right to live.

There is a price to this veil that few see in time, and it has to do with people. When you live always in the next step, you are not truly present with whoever is in front of you now. The children grow up while you wait to have more time for them. Friendships wither while you postpone meeting for a less busy phase. Love dries up for lack of presence, because presence is the only thing that feeds it, and you were always half absent, already in the future. To postpone life is not only to postpone your own pleasure. It is to postpone the people you have, in exchange for the ones you hope to have, and to discover too late that the ones you had were your whole life.

And there is a particular way this veil robs you, worth naming, because it is the quietest. It does not take your happiness all at once, in one great blow. It takes it in small doses, instant by instant, crossing each moment with you without ever letting it settle. You drink your coffee already thinking of what you will do next, and so you never really drink the coffee. You embrace the one you love already with your head on the day's list, and so the embrace does not reach you. You live a whole life of crossed moments, none of them lived completely, and after years the sum of all those half-lived moments is a strange hunger, a sense that despite everything you did and had, you did not get to live. Not because you lacked life. Because you were always a step ahead of it.

There is a wider vision that opens when this face loosens, and it deserves to be said slowly. Our rigid maps of arrival, the calculated goals, the grand endings we imagine, are all made in the image of a mind that needs a destination in order to feel safe. But life, when you look at it without that map, has no final point. It is not a system to conquer nor a goal to fulfil. It is a living field of possibilities, in movement, where each instant is already full in itself, like a river that discovers itself to be sea at every bend, without having to wait for the mouth. What you seek is not at the end of the road, it is in the quality of your presence at each step. There is no there where life finally begins. There is this here, always open, always whole, waiting only for you to stop running long enough to see it. And when you stop, a barely perceptible voice begins to rise, which is your best guide for the rest of the journey. It is not the voice of the head, with its calculations and its fears of the future. It is an inner call, a silent thread that guides without effort, and that speaks only when the noise of the running quietens. The more you listen to it, the less you need maps and goals to know which way to go, because you come to be guided from within, and not pushed from outside.

Where is the postponed life born? Often in a childhood where love and approval came after the achievement, never before. Where only the good mark, the good behaviour, the result, earned the smile of those who mattered. The child learns that the present never arrives, that there is always a next bar to clear in order to deserve to be loved, and grows up living in the future, always a step away from finally being enough. But we keep the longer look at the root for the chapter that comes next, because it is a shared root, and is only seen whole when one looks at both faces at the same time.

## Held by who you used to be

And while one part of you runs forward, another part holds on behind with the same force. You introduce yourself, and out comes always the same sentence. The name, what you do, whose daughter you are, what you have already lost, what you became good at. You have said it so many times that it stopped being an introduction and became an outline, and inside that outline you learned to fit. You are the strong one. You are the responsible one. You are the one who holds out, who does not collapse, the one others come to when the ground shakes. This is the second face of the knot, the face of Permanence: the belief that you are a fixed thing, an identity you have to carry and defend as if your life depended on it.

And there is some hour, perhaps late at night when the house falls asleep, when you feel that sentence pinch, like clothes from a body you no longer have. But you hold it with both hands, because who would you be without it. Notice the cruel irony of the two faces together: you run towards a future where you will finally be able to rest and be something else, but the character who runs is exactly the one who does not know how to rest nor to be something else. You carry with you, into the luminous future, the very armour that keeps you from living it.

I think of a woman, and she could be so many. She was, all her life, the pillar of the family, the one who solved things, the one everyone came to in crises and no one came to on good days. She always lived for the next objective, always postponing for herself, always saying she would rest when the children grew up. When the children left and she was alone, she did not first weep their absence. She wept something else, harder to confess: she did not know who she was without someone to hold up, and the "when the children grow up" she had awaited all her life had arrived, and she did not know what to do with it. She had spent so long being the pillar, and so long waiting to be able to finally live, that she had forgotten she was also a house, with rooms to inhabit inside, and that those rooms were empty because she had never entered them. She was not broken. She was only discovering, late, that she was much more than the function that made her indispensable, and that the later she had waited for was, after all, the now.

How do you recognise this face active in you? By introducing yourself always with the same sentence, the same roles, as if outside them no one were left. By feeling that to change course, opinion, life, would be to betray yourself or to disappoint those who count on you. By how hard it is for you to ask for help or to show fragility, because your place was always that of the strong one, the one who holds out, who does not collapse. By an identity so glued to a function, mother, daughter, professional, the one who fixes things, that the mere idea of letting it go opens an ache of emptiness in you. By defending who you are with a firmness that, seen from outside, looks like security, and that inside is, so often, fear of not knowing who you would be without it. And by feeling, in certain silences, that the sentence you repeat about yourself already pinches you like clothes from a body that has since changed.

This face does not harm you out of cruelty. It harms you out of loyalty to a time that has already passed. At some point, being a fixed and firm version of yourself was the most sensible thing a body found in order to belong, to be loved, to be safe. The veil protected you, and it did so well. The problem is not that it existed. It is that it goes on dressing you long after the threat has disappeared, and choosing for you in places where you could, at last, choose for yourself. And there is an added problem when this face goes hand in hand with the other: the armour you wear to survive becomes the excuse never to arrive. As long as you are only the strong one, the one who fixes things, there is never time to stop, to change, to live differently, because there is always more to hold up. The fixed identity not only holds you to the past, it helps to postpone the future indefinitely, because the character you defend is precisely the one who cannot rest.

This face has ways of hiding worth knowing, because they tend to pass for virtue, and so they are the hardest to catch. There is the disguise of the strong one, who never trembles, who confuses rigidity with character and dreads being seen fragile. There is the one who defines herself entirely by a role, mother, professional, carer, and who, when that role changes or ends, feels she has ceased to exist. There is the consistent-at-all-costs one, who would rather stay the same all her life than admit she has changed her mind, as if changing were lying about who she was. And there is the quietest disguise of all, that of loyalty, which I will speak of in the root, because many women defend a hard identity because they inherited it from mothers and grandmothers who had to hold themselves whole at all costs. There is no malice in any of these disguises. There is a little girl who learned, long ago, that only one version of her was safe, and who goes on protecting it as best she can.

There is also a difference worth seeing, because all the freedom of this face lives in it: the difference between having a form and being the form. Think of a tree. The roots keep where you come from, the trunk holds up the roles, the leaves are the facets that change with the seasons and that will fall. If a branch breaks, if the leaves drop, the tree does not cease to be a tree. You are not the leaves. You are what remains when they fall. What this face of the knot did to you was to make you confuse yourself with the leaves, and so every autumn seems to you the end of the world, and so you cling to leaves that should already have fallen, afraid that without them you will cease to be a tree. But the tree is not afraid of autumn. It lets them fall, stands bare, rests, and in spring gives new leaves, and none is less true because the previous one fell.

To release this face often has the taste of a small grief, and I want you to know this so it does not frighten you. Even the prisons we grow used to have the comfort of the known, and to let go of a hard identity, or a role that defined you for decades, is also to say goodbye to who you were inside it, the one who held out, the one no one saw tremble. Let what has to hurt, hurt. The sadness, here, is not a sign that it went wrong. It is a sign that something true is finally being freed. And there is, in this releasing, a brief emptiness worth announcing to you, so that you do not read it as a mistake. When you let go of a version of yourself that defined you for a long time, there is a strange interval in which you are no longer the old one and not yet anything new. The mind, used to always knowing who it is, does not know how to inhabit that open space, and may take fright, may think it has lost itself. Do not be frightened. It is an emptiness of gestation, not of lack. It is the fertile territory between the form you released and the life you have not yet named, and it is precisely there, without the comfort of the old certainties about yourself, that something truly new can, for the first time, begin to be born.

See how the two faces lock each other in. To change would seem to betray who you always were, so you do not change, and you stay held behind. To live now would seem irresponsible, because you have not yet arrived, so you postpone, and you stay flung forward. And between the fear of betraying the past and the rush to reach the future, the woman who is here, in this instant, alive, never gets to be invited to her own life. It is to her that this book speaks. It is she whom we are going, slowly, to seat at the table.

## The root

To understand why this knot keeps its hold, even against your will, even after you have seen it, it helps to look at its root. Because seeing, alone, rarely is enough the first time, and that is where so many women judge themselves weak when they were only human. The two faces of this knot, the postponed life and the prison of who you used to be, almost always drink from the same source. And the source tends to be a single one: a home where love and approval came afterwards, and where only one version of you was welcome.

Where is the postponed life born? Often in a childhood where love and approval came after the achievement, never before. Where only the good mark, the good behaviour, the result, earned the smile of those who mattered. The child learns that the present never arrives, that there is always a next bar to clear in order to deserve to be loved, and grows up living in the future, always a step away from finally being enough. Other times it is born of homes where the present was too hard to inhabit, and dreaming of later was the only way to survive the now, a habit of flight that became a way of life. Notice what this root already carries: if only achievement brought love, then you could never simply be, you had to be on the way to something. The right to exist was conditional, and the condition was always further ahead.

And where is the prison of who you used to be born? Almost always in the same home, seen from another angle. The child who receives love when she is strong learns to hide her fragility. The one who is only seen when she stands out learns that to exist is to perform. The one who had to grow up fast clings to the role of the responsible one, because it was that role that secured her a place. You did not build a self out of vanity, you built it to survive, to belong to those you needed to want you. Do you see how the two roots are the same? In a home where love was earned through achievement, you learned two lessons at once: that you had to arrive somewhere to deserve love, which flung you towards the horizon, and that you had to be a certain version of yourself to receive it, which held you to permanence. The postponed life and the fixed identity were born twins, from the same conditional love, and that is why it is so natural that they always go together.

And often neither the rigidity nor the rush is yours. It was here that systemic family constellation opened my eyes in a way I did not expect: much of what we carry was not born in us, it crossed generations before reaching us. There is the inheritance of rigidity: women behind you who had to hold themselves whole at all costs, in times and homes where stepping out of line was dangerous, and who passed the gesture to you without words, like someone passing down a sacred inheritance. To defend your identity today tooth and nail may be, without your knowing it, a loyalty to them. And there is the inheritance of postponing: whole generations who had to put everything off, to live for the children, for retirement, for a better day that for many never came. Women who never allowed themselves a present pleasure because life demanded sacrifice of them, and who passed to you, without words, the habit of saving happiness for later. When you see this root, you understand that neither postponing life nor holding on to who you were is a flaw of yours. They are old, deep patterns, and to stop running is not to give up your dreams, just as to change is not to betray your own. It is to let the current stop with you, so that it does not have to go on running in those who come after.

Notice how, deep down, living lightly and early, stopping running, resting barefoot in a present without conditions, can sound almost like abandoning those women. As if resting were accusing them of not having known how to rest. As if living today were saying they were wrong to postpone life entirely. As if changing were betraying the path of stone they walked. There are women who stay rigid and hurried all their lives out of a faithfulness they never named, held by a family fate of sacrifice and postponing that they confused with identity and with duty. And the body fulfils that loyalty long before the head understands it. That is why, even when you see clearly that your life is passing you by, it is so hard for you simply to stop and enter it: because stopping seems, deep down, a disloyalty to those who never could.

But there is a very particular rest that only comes when you tell them, inwardly, slowly: I saw what you had to bear and what you had to save for later. I honour you. And, from me onward, the part that is no longer needed can be set down, and I can live today what you had to keep for a tomorrow that did not come. This is not betrayal. It is exactly the opposite. It is to let the current of rigidity and of postponing stop with you. To honour where you come from does not oblige you to repeat what hurt there nor to go on saving life for later. You can keep their strength as inheritance and live, in your name and perhaps in theirs too, the present they were never able to inhabit. There is, in this, a beauty worth feeling whole: when you finally sit down, barefoot, at the table of your life, and savour an ordinary instant without postponing it or armouring it, you are in a way living also for all of them, honouring their sacrifice in the only way that redeems it, which is not to continue it, but to gather its fruit. They did not run and hold out so that you would run and hold out the same. They ran and held out so that someone, one day, in their line, could finally stop and live. That it should be you, that woman, is not betrayal. It is the reason for everything.

The third root is the one least seen, and so the one that governs most: what stays in the shadow. As long as you do not look at the rush and at the armour, they decide for you in the dark, dressed as good sense, as responsibility, as who you are. The postponing presents itself as prudence. The rigidity presents itself as character. And it is only when you name them, when you give them an outline and see their origin, that they stop acting with their backs to your attention. That is why the first liberation, in this knot as in everything in the method, is never to change. It is to see. A pattern seen no longer catches you in the same way, because it has stopped acting in secret. Do not hurry, then, to correct anything yet. For now, it is enough to begin to see where you run and where you hold on, and where, far back, you learned to do it.

So, before you go on, stay a moment with these questions, in no hurry to answer. From whom did you learn, far back, that love came after the achievement, never before? And from whom did you learn that only one version of you was welcome at home? What woman before you had to postpone her whole life for a better day that never came? And how much of the rush and the firmness you feel as yours is, deep down, a loyalty to those who no longer need you to carry them? Do not answer from your head. Let the answers arrive over the days, often when you least expect them.

## The protocol of living

So far, we have seen the knot. Now you need something concrete to do with it when it appears, because the knot does not tighten while you are sitting reading, calm. It tightens in the middle of an ordinary day, when you catch yourself pushing life towards later and holding the armour at the same time. It is in those instants, and not in these pages, that the method proves itself. So this is the serious heart of this book: a protocol, with named steps, for entering your life in the exact moment you find yourself outside it.

I call it the protocol of living, and it has five times. It fits into a minute, and is used whenever you catch yourself postponing the present or defending who you used to be, which are, after all, the same gesture of not being here. The steps are named on purpose, because a gesture with a name returns more quickly to the hand. Memorise them, not so as to do them perfectly, but so that they are there, available, the next time you feel life slipping from you ahead or behind.

Before I give you the steps, I want you to understand what the protocol does, deep down, because then you will use it with more meaning and less mechanics. It undoes the knot in the order in which the knot tightens. First it calls you back from the future, with the anchor-sentence that tells you that you have already arrived. Then it eases the past, taking off one piece of the armour you defend. Then it lights up the root of both, asking whose the rush is. Then it honours the old function, because nothing is released until it is thanked. And only then, with the future stopped and the past set down, does it hand you to the present, to the concrete gesture of entering a small, real thing. It is not by chance that the entering comes at the end. It is that you can only enter your life after you stop fleeing it in both directions. The first four steps open the door. The fifth is you crossing it.

**One: You are already here.** Stop, even if only for a second, in the middle of whatever you are doing, and say inwardly, slowly: I am not on the way anywhere, I am already here. This is the anchor-sentence of this veil, and it is the first gesture of any return. You are not in the waiting room. There is no train leaving without you. Life is not a rehearsal for a premiere that is always still to come. Say "you are already here" and notice that, just from saying it, something in you sets down, like someone arriving home after a whole day on her feet.

**Two: Take off one piece of the armour.** Ask, quietly: what role am I wearing right now that I do not need to wear now? The strong one, the one who holds out, the one who is always right, the one who cannot show herself tired. And take off one piece only. Not the whole armour, not by force. Say a small sentence that contradicts it without tearing it off: "I don't know", "I've changed my mind", "I need help", "I can't today", "you can do it". One only. And notice that you remain whole even without the piece, even without being right, even without being the strong one. The armour was for a danger that has already passed. Here, now, you can set it down a little.

**Three: Ask whose "when" it is.** Ask the root question: for what "when" am I postponing my life, and who taught me that it only begins later? What woman before me saved happiness for a tomorrow that did not come? Often you will feel, with a shiver, that the rush is not yours, it is borrowed, just like the rigidity. And what is borrowed can be returned with respect, so that you no longer have to go on paying it for them.

**Four: Honour what this protects.** Before releasing, honour. Ask: what am I trying to protect by running forward and holding the armour? Almost always you will find, beneath the rush and the firmness, a child who learned that she would only be loved if she arrived somewhere and if she were a certain version of herself. Do not scold her. Put, inwardly, a hand on her shoulder. The knot was protection before it was prison, and one only releases what one first thanks.

**Five: One present, small thing.** And now enter. Choose one present, small thing, the light falling on a wall, the warmth of the cup in your hands, the air going in and out of you without your having to ask for it, the voice of whoever is in front of you. Linger on that tiny thing for the length of three breaths, as if it were the first time you saw it and the last. It is not a relaxation technique. It is the concrete gesture of entering the life that is happening now, instead of the one you wait for or the one you defend. It was for this that you were waiting, all this time, without knowing it. And it was already here.

This is the protocol, and it is only this. You are already here. Take off one piece of the armour. Ask whose "when" it is. Honour what this protects. One present, small thing. It is not a complicated ritual, it is a simple gesture, repeated until the body knows it on its own, until you catch it earlier and earlier, until you see the flight coming before it catches you.

Let me show you the protocol working in a real situation, the kind life brings. Imagine a Sunday afternoon. Everyone has stopped, and you, instead of resting, feel the restlessness rising, the list of what is left to do, the idea that you can only relax when this and that are resolved, and at the same time you get up to tidy something, because stopping makes you feel guilty, because your place is that of the one who does. It is the whole knot tightening: the horizon pushing you towards "when everything is done" and permanence holding you in the role of the one who never stops. First, you say inwardly: I am already here, I am not on the way anywhere. The restlessness eases for half a second. Then you take off one piece of the armour: instead of getting up to tidy, you stay seated, and you say, even if only to yourself, "today I am not going to do this". Then you ask whose "when" it is: from whom did I learn that resting was for after everything was resolved. Perhaps you remember a mother who never sat down. Then you honour what this protects: I am trying not to feel useless, not to feel disposable if I stop doing. And finally, you choose one present, small thing: the sun at the window, the silence of the house, three breaths. And you notice that the world does not end, that life was not at the end of the list, it was there, in that afternoon you almost crossed at a run. It was a minute. And in that minute you entered your own life.

Let me give you a second example, because the knot tightens in many ways. Imagine that you have finally reached something you waited a long time for, an objective, a recognition, a goal you thought would bring you peace. And instead of the promised peace, you feel a brief emptiness, and almost at once the mind is already painting the next horizon, the next thing you will have to achieve in order to finally be enough. It is the horizon receding, as it always does, and beneath it permanence whispering that you must go on proving your worth to deserve your place. First, you say inwardly: I am already here, I have arrived, I am not on the way anywhere else. Then you take off one piece of the armour: instead of running already to the next target, you allow yourself not to know what it is, you say "for now, this is enough". Then you ask whose "when" it is: from whom did I learn that no achievement was ever enough, who taught me that love was always at the next bar. Then you honour what this protects: I am trying to deserve a place I feel is not yet mine. And finally, you choose one present, small thing: you let yourself, for three breaths, feel what you achieved, instead of crossing it already on the way to the next goal. And you notice that the peace you were seeking in the next objective was, after all, available in this one, if you stopped long enough to feel it.

Notice that the protocol does not ask you to stop feeling the rush nor the urge to hold the armour. Those impulses will go on coming, with the same force, for a long time, because the body learned them decades ago. What changes is that, between the impulse and the flight, you open a millimetre of space, and it is in that millimetre that you live your freedom. With repetition, the millimetre widens. At first you will remember the protocol only at night, when the day has already passed in a rush, and that is fine, that too is the path. Then you will remember halfway through. And one day, without warning, you will notice that you are simply living an ordinary afternoon without pushing it anywhere, without needing to deserve it. It was for that instant that this whole protocol worked.

And there is one last fold of the protocol, perhaps the most important of all, and it is this: use it on the protocol itself too. Because the horizon is so clever that it slips even into your path of healing, and you will catch yourself treating "entering life" as one more goal to achieve, one more place you have to reach, one more thing you can fail at. You will want to be already present, already barefoot, already arrived, and that rush to reach presence is what I call the horizon coming in through the back door. When you catch it, do the protocol on it: you are already here, you do not have to reach a perfect presence, this imperfect breath is already being here. There is no advanced student in this school, and there is no level of living you have to reach. There is only this instant, and the possibility, always open, of entering it just as it is.

## The practices

The protocol is for the instant when the knot tightens. The practices are for serene training, away from the heat of the moment, so that the body slowly learns a new way. Do them with tenderness, not as one more task you can fail at, and above all not as one more horizon to reach, because that would be the very veil slipping into the path. Choose the ones that call you. Repeat them. And remember that there is no advanced student in this school, because the destination is exactly where you already are.

The practices that follow have a gentle order, but not an obligatory one. Some train the return to the present, against the postponing of the horizon. Others train the setting down of the armour, against the rigidity of permanence. And there are the ones that touch the root and the inheritance, because that is where the two faces cross. Do one at a time. You do not need more than a few minutes a day, and a practice done with presence, which in this book is almost a true pun, is worth more than five done in a rush to get them out of the way, because to rush a practice of living in the present is to deny it in the very gesture of doing it.

**Practice one: you are already here.** This is the smallest and the most fundamental, and you can do it several times a day, in seconds. Stop what you are doing, in the middle of any task, and say, in silence, slowly: I am not on the way anywhere, I am already here. Then choose one present thing, the light, the warmth of a cup, the air going in and out, and linger on it for the length of three breaths, as if it were the first time you saw it and the last. Do not chase any special sensation. The practice is not to feel anything spectacular, it is to train the return. The more you do it, the more quickly the body learns the way back to the present, until one day it returns on its own.

**Practice two: the inventory of "when".** On a sheet of paper, write all your sentences that begin with "when" or "next year" or "one day". When I lose the weight. When I have time. When things calm down. Write them all, without censoring. Then read them slowly and ask each one: and if this "when" never comes? What am I postponing while waiting for it, and what of that could I live, even in small measure, today? It is not about giving up your objectives. It is about ceasing to make them the condition for having the right to live now. Choose one only of those postponed things and bring a fraction of it into this week. Not "I will travel when I can", but an afternoon to a new place this very Saturday. The present does not ask you to arrive. It asks you to enter.

**Practice three: the tree.** This one works the face of permanence. On a sheet of paper, draw a simple tree, without worrying about the drawing. In the roots, write where you come from, the names of those who came before you. In the trunk, write the roles that hold you up today, mother, daughter, professional, what you are to others. In the leaves, write the facets that have already changed over your life, the ones that fell, the girl you were and no longer are, the tastes that changed, the certainties you let drop. Then sit looking at the trunk and the roots, without the leaves, and ask: what of me would remain if all the leaves fell at the same time? That remainder is what stays, who you are beneath the roles. You are not the leaves that fall with the seasons. You are the tree that stays when they drop. And a tree does not postpone spring nor cling to the leaves of autumn. It lives each season whole, in its turn.

**Practice four: returning the inheritance.** This one is slower and asks for silence. Sit alone and bring to mind a woman of your line who had to postpone her life or to hold herself whole at all costs, a grandmother, a mother, a great-grandmother you were told about. Imagine her in front of you. Say to her, inwardly, unhurried: I saw what you had to bear and what you had to save for a later that did not come. I honour you. And I tell you that, from me onward, there is no longer any need to postpone nor to be of stone. I can live today, I can tremble, I can change, and that does not betray you. Notice what moves in you as you say these words. Often it is here, and not in willpower, that the rush and the rigidity finally begin to set down.

**Practice five: the whole meal.** This one steps off the page and into life, and it is the concrete heart of living. Choose, once a day, a small, ordinary thing you usually cross at a run, already thinking of the next: a meal, a bath, the way home, five minutes with someone you love. And do only that thing, with your whole body inside it, without your phone, without planning what comes next, without treating it as a step towards something else. Eat the whole meal knowing you are eating. Walk the whole way knowing you are walking. When the head jumps to the future, and it will jump, bring it back with tenderness, without scolding yourself, like someone calling a distracted child. This practice teaches the body, and not only the head, that each instant is already whole in itself, that it does not have to be a means to reach another. It is in the ordinary, lived whole, that the postponed life finally returns.

**Practice six: the minute of the veil.** This is the only one I ask of you every day, and it is the smallest of all. Once a day, at night perhaps, stop for a minute and ask: where today did I postpone my life for later, and where did I hold the armour when I could have set it down? Where was I everywhere but here? Not to correct yourself, only to see. Name the moment, thank the veil for the old protection, and enter, for an instant, the present you have, if only the breath of that minute. One minute a day. It is enough so that, after weeks, you begin to catch the flight earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you. One minute a day changes a life, not because it is much, but because it is every day.

## Seven days of living

Seven days do not untie a knot forever, and that is not the promise, which would itself be one more horizon. What they do is teach the body a way, one small movement a day, so that it is there, available, the next time life slips from you ahead or behind. Do one a day, unhurried, without grading yourself. If one day asks for more time, stay in it. There is no possible delay on this journey, because the destination is exactly where you already are.

Each day asks of you a few minutes and a single gesture. It is not a programme to carry out with rigour, it is an invitation to inhabit your own present long enough to discover that it is enough. If you can, keep a notebook of your own to hand, not to find right answers, but to see yourself living. And remember, each day, the golden rule: first see, without judging yourself. The entering comes after, and comes more on its own than you expect.

**Day 1, see the flight.** Today you only notice. Walk through the day with a loose question in your pocket: where am I not here? Change nothing. Just note the times you postpone the present for a "when", the times you cross a moment at a run already thinking of the next, the times you hold the role of the strong one when you could have set it down. Notice, without scolding yourself, how often your head is in the future or in defending who you used to be, and how rarely it is in the instant you have. Seeing is the first movement, and for today it is enough.

**Day 2, you are already here.** Do the first practice, several times through the day. Stop, say "I am not on the way anywhere, I am already here", and linger on a small, present thing, three breaths. Carry with you, through the rest of the day, the memory that the here was always available and that it is enough to come back to it.

**Day 3, take off one piece of the armour.** Today you say a small sentence that contradicts permanence: "I don't know", "I've changed my mind", "I need help", or you leave, without picking it up, a task you would do out of habit to show that you hold out. One only. Notice the discomfort that rises, and notice that you remain whole, and that the world does not fall because you set down one piece.

**Day 4, bring a "when" into today.** Today you take something you have long postponed for a better future and bring a fraction of it into today. Not the whole thing, just a segment. A small pleasure you were saving for when you deserved it. Notice the resistance, the voice that says it is not yet time, that first you have to resolve the rest. And live it anyway. Life does not begin later. It begins when you enter it.

**Day 5, whose it is.** Today you look at the root. Choose a rush of yours, or a firmness, and ask: whose is this? What woman before me had to postpone life, or to be of stone in order to survive? You do not need the right answer. It is enough to open the question and let it work.

**Day 6, return the inheritance.** Do the practice of returning the inheritance, with the woman of your line who comes to mind. Tell her, inwardly, that you honour what she bore and postponed, and that, from you onward, the current can set down, that you can live today what she saved for a tomorrow that did not come. Notice what lightens.

**Day 7, the whole meal.** Today close the week by entering, with your whole body, an ordinary thing. Do the practice of the whole meal with a moment of your choosing, lived without rush, without future, without armour. And then sit with the question of this book, not as conclusion but as seed: and what if the premiere I waited for all my life were today? Write, if you wish, a short letter to yourself, to read in a few months, with what you discovered about entering your own life. Keep it. You will need it again, and it will be there, patient, waiting for you, in this present that is the only place where anything can wait for you.

## What changes

What changes, when this knot releases, is that life happens again in the present, the only time where it ever happens, and that you fit again, whole, into the woman you already are, without pushing her forward nor holding her behind. And there is a word for that state, and it is the word I kept for the end: barefoot.

Barefoot, because you set down the armour. You no longer walk through life armoured, permanently on guard, defending an image you have to keep intact at any cost. You can change course at forty, at fifty, at sixty, without feeling that you betray yourself, because you understand that to change is the nature of one who is alive. Losses come to hurt without destroying you, because you discover in yourself a depth that no loss reaches. You set down the guard, that weariness of one who has to prove who she is full time, and you discover a rest you did not know: that of not having to be the strong one, because there is no longer any danger that you would be less without it.

And barefoot, because your feet finally touch the ground that is yours, this one, present, real, instead of the imagined ground of a there where life would finally begin. You stop postponing happiness for a "when" that always recedes, and you begin to find it in small things you had the habit of crossing at a run. You still have projects and dreams, but you stop living them as conditions for having the right to breathe. You become more present with those you love, because you are no longer half absent, flung into the next step. And there is a deep rest in realising that you are not late for anything, that there is no train leaving without you, that your life is not a rehearsal for a premiere that is always still to come. The premiere is today. It always was. You got up from the waiting room and sat down, at last, at the table of your own life.

And I want to linger a moment on that word, barefoot, because I kept it the whole book and it carries everything that has been said here. Think of the gesture of taking off your shoes. You take them off when you reach home, when the day outside has ended and you no longer need the protection of shoes for the world out there. You take them off to feel the ground, the texture of the earth or the wood or the grass, that the shoes hid from you. You take them off when you are at peace, in safety, in your own place, with no one left to impress. To be barefoot is, at the same time, to have set down the armour, which is releasing permanence, and to have arrived, which is releasing the horizon. It is the body saying, without words, that it has stopped running and that it is, finally, home. For years you walked shod for a world you defended yourself from and for an arrival you chased. To be barefoot is the proof, in the body, that you have stopped defending yourself and stopped arriving, because you are already here.

The identity, which was prison, becomes flux. You stop being a statue bent on staying the same and become a river again, able to change course without ceasing to be water. You are not a photograph you took of yourself at a certain moment and confused with the whole river. You are the river. And a river does not postpone its course for later nor cling to the bend it has already passed. It runs, now, whole in every metre of its bed. You give yourself permission to be many, over time, without ceasing to be one, and to live each age of your life in its turn, instead of spending it waiting for the next or grieving the previous.

You get back, above all, time, and not because the day gained hours. The rush of the horizon and the guard of permanence consumed, together, an enormous slice of your attention, always half spent anticipating what is missing and defending who you are. When that slice is freed, there is suddenly more of you available for what is actually happening. The same day that seemed not enough for anything begins to seem wider, more inhabitable, not because the tasks shrank, but because you stopped crossing them at a run and carrying them as proof that you are worth something. The woman who does not have to arrive anywhere nor prove who she is has, finally, time to live the time she has.

The change here does not have the dramatic form you may expect. You will not wake up one day transformed, with the knot undone and a permanent serenity, and just as well, because to expect that would be to fall back into the horizon. What you will notice, after months, is more subtle and more solid than that. You will notice that you savoured a whole Sunday without crossing it at a run to resolve it. That you changed plans without the old guilt. That you were with those you love without being half absent in the future. That you set down the armour in a conversation and remained whole. That you said "maybe next year" and, for the first time, instead of postponing life, you decided to live a part of it already. They are small victories, almost invisible, and often you only notice them later, when you realise you are living, simply, without condition and without armour. True transformation is not a lightning flash, it is a tide that rises so slowly that you only notice it when you see that the whole landscape has changed.

And something also changes in the body. The permanent rush and the permanent guard spend, together, a silent energy you did not even notice you were spending, that of one who runs and one who defends herself at the same time, twenty-four hours a day. When you set them down, there is a deep weariness that begins to give way, the shoulders drop, the breath widens. There are women who only realise how stretched they were between the future and the past when, for the first time, they allow themselves to stay whole in a present afternoon, barefoot, and feel the body release a tension they had had since childhood and had long confused with their natural posture.

Your relationship with people also changes, and this is perhaps what will surprise you most. When you stop being half absent, flung into the next step, and when you set down the armour that kept you on guard, you become, finally, available to whoever is in front of you. Your relationships stop resting on your function or your firmness and come to rest on your true presence. Those who love you discover that they can, at last, reach you, because you are no longer always a little elsewhere and at some other time. And you discover that being present with someone, with nothing to prove and nothing to rush, is a form of love you did not know, simpler and deeper than all the doing with which you confused love.

And there is, above all, a reconciliation that often happens without being sought: with those who came before you. When you see that you inherited the rush and the rigidity without choosing them, that those women ran and held themselves of stone because life demanded running and stone of them, you stop judging them and begin to understand them. And in that understanding something is also freed in you. You are no longer loyal to their postponing nor to their hardness, you are loyal to the love that was underneath, and that love does not ask you to go on running nor holding on. It asks you, perhaps, for exactly the opposite: that you be the first to be able, at last, to stop, to live, and to rest barefoot in the life they saved for a tomorrow that did not come.

A word about relapses, because you will have them, and I do not want them to catch you by surprise nor for you to take them as failure. This is not a straight line, it is a spiral, and on a spiral you pass several times through the same point, but never at the same height. You will recognise yourself again pushing life towards a "when", putting on the old armour, crossing a whole Sunday at a run to resolve it, on a bad day, in a fright, with tiredness. And you will be tempted to think you have learned nothing, that you are back to square one. You are not. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before, you did not even realise, now you see yourself doing it, perhaps still mid-gesture, perhaps already halfway. That seeing, earlier and earlier, is the progress, even if it does not have the form you expected. The knot does not undo from one day to the next as if by magic. It keeps losing force, it catches you fewer times and for less time, until one day, with no celebration at all, you notice that you are now living more than postponing. Be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades and generations to weave. And one thing I want to make clear, because it matters: this path does not replace the support you may need. There are wounds that ask for professional hands, and to seek that help is not weakness, it is, itself, a gesture of seeing and of caring.

## To close

This was the third of three movements, and with it the crossing is complete. First came seeing, learning to recognise what holds you. Then coming, letting what was underneath come up, waiting for you. And now living, entering your own life, which was where the other two always pointed. To see without living would be to stay looking at what holds you without ever crossing the door. To come without living would be to let everything rise and have nowhere to set it down. It is living that closes the gesture, because it serves nothing to see clearly and to let come with courage if it is not, in the end, to inhabit the only place where all of this happens, which is here, now, barefoot, with your feet on the ground that is yours.

These two veils, the postponed life and the prison of who you used to be, do not live alone. They interweave with the others, they take their hands. Permanence goes with effort, because the identity of the strong one sustains itself by doing, by holding up, by proving. The horizon goes with desolation, because sometimes the running towards the future is the most applauded flight from the emptiness of the present. And beneath all of them there is a deeper veil, the sense of separation, that you are alone in having to defend who you are and to conquer your place, and it is from this that all this rush and all this firmness, deep down, are born. When you work these two, you will feel the others loosen on their own, because the web does not come undone knot by knot through force, it comes undone when you stop tightening it.

You do not have to cross them all, nor in order, nor all at once. You pulled these threads because these are the ones that called you, and as you release them you will feel the others loosen. The whole map, the seven veils and the path between them, and the veil of Duality, which lives at the root of them all and to which everything returns, is in *The Seven Veils*. When you want to go deeper, or when another veil begins to tighten, it stays waiting for you, patient, like a friend who is in no hurry, in this present that is the only place where anyone can, truly, wait for you.

For now, stay only with this, which is the heart of all that has been said here. You do not have to arrive anywhere to have the right to live, and you do not have to go on being who you were to have the right to belong. Life is not in the destination nor in the image you defend. It is here, in this ordinary instant, waiting only for you to stop running forward and holding on behind long enough to enter it. Get up from the waiting room. Set down the armour. And sit, barefoot, at the table of your own life, which has long been laid, and where your place never, at any moment, stopped waiting for you.

I do not wish for you to undo this knot forever, nor to reach a perfect and definitive presence, because that would be to hand you one more horizon. I wish for you something else, smaller and truer: that, the next time you catch yourself pushing life towards later or putting on the old armour, instead of the old ache, you feel an almost tenderness for yourself, and think, with a tired smile, "ah, I'm out of the house again". And that you come back. And that you enter, if only for a single breath, the life that is, that always was, happening here.

See. And release. And live, one instant at a time. And remember, on the days when the rush and the armour tighten again, that to recognise them is already half the path, and that on the other side of them, waiting for you for a long time, barefoot and serene, is your own life.

With all my care,
Vivianne
