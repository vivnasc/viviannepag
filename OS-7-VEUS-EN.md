### Dedication

For Nana.

There was a time when certain parts of me rested, not out of forgetting, but because the path asked for silence. The girl I once was, with a name made of laps and a seed of presence, stayed kept somewhere between the gestures and the days. Today, I do not seek you: I recognise you. You are the old breath stirring again in me, not as a return, but as the continuation of what I always wanted. And I write to remember that we are always more than what the world taught us to be.

### Who this book is for

This book is for you, if you have ever caught yourself repeating what you swore you would not repeat, and felt like a failure for it. If you carry more than you should and do not know how to set it down. If you see clearly what harms you and, even so, go back to doing it. If, beneath a life that on the outside may even look full, there is a restlessness that does not pass, a tiredness that sleep does not cure, the sense that there is more of you than what you manage to live.

This is not a self-help book with easy promises, nor a ten-step manual for a perfect life. It is slower and deeper than that, and for that very reason more truthful. It will not ask you to try harder, you will see that effort is, itself, one of the veils. It will ask you only two things, across these pages: that you look at yourself with more honesty and less harshness, and that you trust that what you are, beneath everything you learned to be, is enough.

Read it slowly. Underline. Go back. Argue with me inside when you do not agree. And, above all, carry it into your life, because it is there, and not on these pages, that the unravelling happens. I am with you, at the same pace, in every veil.

There is a question that has accompanied me for years, and it began in me. Why does someone lucid, who sees clearly what harms her, go back to doing it? Not once, but over and over. The same kind of love already known by heart. The same exhaustion of one who gives until there is nothing left. The same way of erasing herself to fit into the space that remains. The person understands, swears that next time it will be different, and next time it happens again, with another face and the same root.

For a long time I called this weakness. Lack of character, lack of constancy, lack of wanting it enough. I looked at my own cycles and concluded that, if I were stronger, more disciplined, more aware, I would stop tripping over the same stone. I even collected books, phrases, decisions made at midnight with all the certainty in the world. And still I repeated. That was when I realised I was looking in the wrong place. The problem was not that I did not see. It was that I saw and, even so, could not.

We do not repeat for lack of will, nor of awareness. We repeat because, at some moment, that was the most intelligent answer a body found to survive, to belong, to be loved. And the body does not forget what once kept it safe. Awareness learns quickly, with a phrase, with a reading. The body learns slowly, with repetition and with time, and unlearns even more slowly. That is why knowing is not enough. Between what you know and what you do there is an interval where everything you did not choose lives, and which, even so, governs you.

This was the part that confused me. I saw, and still I repeated. I recognised the pattern and it did not stop. There was something happening before the decision, before the promise, often before the thought itself. A reaction already underway when reason arrived, late, trying to explain what the body had already done. What I called weakness had, after all, a whole logic of its own. It just was not the logic of reason. It was the logic of something older, that learned to protect me long before I knew my own name.

I do not write to you from a place of arrival. I write from a path still underway, my own. I am someone who saw herself repeat, who grew tired of judging herself for it, and who went looking to understand, with the stubbornness of one who needs to, not of one who is merely entertained. I still study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as one who collects certainties, but as one who needs names for what she feels. Each thing I share here passed first through me. I recognised it in myself, sat with it, and only then went on finding it, repeated, in what echoes in so many women going round the same turns, convinced they are the only ones, convinced it is a flaw only theirs.

So I do not invite you to follow me. I invite us to walk together, at the same pace, towards what is already yours and that perhaps you have only forgotten. There is no step here on which I stand and you do not. There is a path, and on it we go side by side.

This book does not promise you a cure, nor a method to fix yourself, because you are not broken. I want this to be clear from the first page, because it is the opposite of almost everything they will tell you: there is nothing in you to repair. You will not find here a manual to become another person, more productive, more positive, more anything. You will find a way to see what makes you repeat, and to release, slowly, what no longer serves you, so that what you always were may finally have room to breathe. One thing at a time, without haste, without noise. That is how the unravelling happens, and it is the only way I know that lasts.

I call a veil a conditioning so old that it already seems your own face. It is not a flaw, not an illness, not a fault. It is a thin layer that was placed between you and what you are, early, by hands that often did not choose to place it either, because they too had inherited it. A belief about who you have to be. A role you took on without noticing. A fear you learned before you knew what you were afraid of. A way of loving you mistook for love. The veil dressed you before you knew you had a body, and you grew up confusing the veil with your skin. After so many years, it stops being seen as a veil. It is seen as truth. As "this is how I am".

The veil is not bad. That is the part no one tells you, and the one that changes everything. It was useful. It protected you when you needed it, made you fit where you had to fit, spared you pains you would not have borne at the time. Pleasing may have been what kept you safe in a house where love was conditional. Staying silent may have avoided a punishment. Controlling everything may have been the only way to survive the unpredictable. There are no enemies here. There are old strategies, intelligent ones, that fulfilled their function and were never told that the danger had passed. The problem is not that the veil existed. It is that it keeps dressing you long after the threat has disappeared, and choosing for you in places where you could, finally, choose on your own.

The question of this book is not "what is wrong with me?". It is "how did I survive?". The veils are not flaws to correct, they are the forms you found to keep yourself standing. To see them is the first step to no longer needing them.

To understand why these veils hold on, even against your will, even after you see them, it helps to look at three things. They are the three reasons why seeing, on its own, rarely suffices, and why so often you judged yourself weak when you were only human.

The first is the body that learned. Your nervous system does not distinguish past from present. It distinguishes only one thing: safe from threatened. When something in the present touches an old wound, a tone of voice, a silence, a door that closes, it does not ask what year it is. It reacts as it reacted the first time, and resorts to what it already knows, not to what is healthy, but to what is familiar. That is why repetition almost never presents itself as an obvious mistake. It presents itself as the most sensible thing to do in that instant, as internal coherence, as instinct. Only later, when the dust settles, do you think "again", and you do not understand how you fell once more.

The second is the inheritance that is not yours. It was here that family constellation opened my eyes in a way I did not expect. Much of what we carry was not born in us. It crossed generations before reaching you. We repeat gestures, loyalties, renunciations and even misfortunes that belonged to those who came before, without anyone having decided them, like one who honours a debt she never knew she signed. There are patterns of yours that, when you listen to them with true attention, have the voice of another person. A grandmother's renunciation. A mother's fear. The empty place of someone who was excluded or forgotten, and which you, without knowing, went to fill. There are women who carry all their lives a weight that is not even theirs, and who only rest when they finally understand this: this is not mine, I was only loyal.

The third is what you do not see. What stays in the shadow is precisely what governs us most. We do not choose what we hide from ourselves, and that is why the shadow rules so much, because it acts with its back to our attention. The veil lives, by definition, where you do not look. While you do not look, it decides. And looking at those zones costs, because it was exactly so as not to feel them that you hid them. But it is only there, in what you avoid, that the key lies. That is why the first liberation is never to change. It is to see.

It is for all this that the path of this book has two times, and an order that does not reverse. First, to see. To recognise the pattern without judging yourself. Seeing is not condemning, it is locating. It is giving a name to what had no name, giving contour to what was fog, discovering where and when it was born, and understanding that it made sense. What stops being invisible stops being destiny. A pattern seen no longer catches you in the same way, because it stopped acting in the dark. Then, to release. To let go of what you saw. Not by force, not by tearing, because what is torn out with violence grows back with a deeper root, and you wake up fighting against yourself. Releasing is more like slowly opening a hand that has been closed so long it had begun to hurt, and discovering what it was clenching, and what lay beneath, waiting for you.

There is no releasing without seeing. Write this into yourself if you can, because it is the heart of everything. The haste to change without recognising is, itself, repetition dressed in good intention. It is wanting to tear out the symptom without looking at the root, and then being surprised that it returns. So, if there is a single request this book makes of you, it is this: do not run to the solution. Resist the temptation to fix yourself right away. Stay first in the seeing, even when it is uncomfortable, even when your head screams for a technique, a step, a way out. Releasing comes on its own, and comes more whole, when the seeing is ripe. There are things that let go of themselves at the exact instant we finally see them completely.

There is a condition without which none of this works, and I want to make it very clear before we enter the veils: tenderness with yourself. Most of us learned to change through harshness, through demand, through inner criticism. We point out what is missing, we correct, we scold ourselves as no one from outside would dare to scold. And then we find it strange that nothing truly changes, or that it changes only for a few days until everything returns. The thing is that harshness is, itself, a veil, perhaps the one that dresses all the others, and with it on there is no seeing that is not judgement and no releasing that is not tearing. One does not awaken through blows. A frightened child is not calmed with shouts, she is calmed with a lap, and there is in you a child like that behind every pattern. So, across these pages, whenever you catch yourself judging yourself for what you see, make of that too a veil to lift, and trade harshness for curiosity, shame for tenderness. Treat yourself as you would treat your best friend learning this. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself, and the rest becomes possible.

So that the method fits with you anywhere, without this book in front of you, I leave you the pocket protocol, four times you apply to any veil, at any hour. One, stop and name: "there is a veil here", and give it whatever name you can, without judging yourself. Two, see the root: where does this come from, from what time, from what house. Three, ask whose it is: is this really mine, or am I being loyal to someone who came before, and what am I trying to protect by acting this way? Four, release one finger: not everything, not by force, open the hand just a little, breathe three times, and leave be what today you cannot let go of. That is all, and it fits in a minute. Seeing and releasing is not a complicated ritual, it is this simple gesture, repeated until the body knows it by heart.

I propose to you, too, a single habit for this method, and I call it the minute of the veil. Once a day, in the evening perhaps, stop for a minute and ask: which veil was in front of my eyes today? Not to correct yourself, only to see. Name it, thank it for its old protection, and release one finger. One minute a day. It is enough so that, after weeks, you begin to catch the veils in the act, earlier and earlier, until you see them coming before they catch you. One minute a day changes a life, not because it is a lot, but because it is every day.

There are seven veils. Each one hides a belief and, on lifting, reveals what it was covering. They are not a staircase to climb to a summit, nor stages to complete in order, nor levels of a game. They are a spiral that opens. You will recognise yourself far more in some than in others, and that is fine, it is natural, each life has its thicker veils. You will return to the same veil at different moments and find it each time a little thinner. You do not need to remove them all, nor all at once, nor in the order they appear here. It is enough to begin to see. The rest releases in its own time, veil by veil, as you are ready, and not an instant before.

Perhaps you wonder why seven, and not five or ten. It is not a magic number nor a closed formula. It is that, over a long time listening to the turns I take and the ones I have seen in others, these were the seven knots that appeared over and over, beneath a thousand different stories. The identity that defends itself. The past that imprisons. The mind that drowns. The effort that exhausts. The emptiness that terrifies. The horizon that postpones. And the separation that is at the root of all. There could be other names, other divisions, and there are, in other books and other traditions. But these seven cover, in my experience, almost all the terrain where a woman loses herself. If you feel that one is missing for you, or that one of yours does not quite fit any, trust your experience more than my map. The map serves the journey, never the other way round.

As for the order, it has a soft logic, though it is not obligatory. We begin with the most visible, identity and story, what we show and what we tell. We descend then to what stirs inside, the mind and effort. Deeper still, we touch what we avoid, the emptiness and the illusion of arrival. And we end at the root of all, separation, because it is from it that the other six were born and it is to it that they return. But, I repeat, this is a spiral, not a staircase. You will enter through whatever door your life opens for you, and you will return to the same veils at different times, finding them each time a little thinner. There is no right order to return home, there is only your own step.

I also want you to know that I invented none of this. The intuition that we are more than our conditioning, that there is in us a presence the thoughts do not reach, that we suffer for judging ourselves separate when we never were, is among the oldest of humanity. It crosses centuries, geographies and traditions, spoken in a thousand different languages. The African Ubuntu sums it up in a phrase, I am because we are. The contemplative traditions of east and west, each in its own way, speak of the same unravelling of illusions and the same silent centre beneath the noise. And the deepest psychology itself, in speaking of shadow, of inheritances, of patterns, is, with another vocabulary, pointing to the same. I bring you, therefore, no theory of mine. I bring you a very old knowing, which I recognised first in myself, translated into the language of the veils and into the concrete life of a woman of today. If it resonates in you, it is not because I convinced you. It is because you already knew it, in some old place, and these pages only helped you remember.

There is something you will feel, from the first veil to the last, beneath all of them. You have never been so alone as it seems. What looks like separation, that glass between you and life, is the thinnest and the oldest of all the veils, and when it falls there remains what was always there, the simple belonging to the life that breathes in everything. As the old knowing says, I am because we are. We will get there, in the seventh veil. For now, it is enough to know that, even in this reading, even on your worst days, you do not walk alone.

Let me say one more word about seeing, because it is half the method and the part most underestimated. Seeing is not thinking about. It is not analysing yourself to exhaustion, dissecting childhood, finding the guilty ones, building the perfect theory about why you are as you are. That, often, is only the mind using introspection as one more form of control, and you can spend your life understanding yourself without ever truly seeing yourself. Seeing is another thing, simpler and harder. It is a quiet noticing, almost without words, in which you catch the pattern in the act, in the moment it happens, and you stay with it without rushing to judge it or fix it. It is the difference between talking about the sea and wetting your feet. There are women who know everything about their wounds and have never seen them, because the knowing was one more way of not feeling. Seeing hurts a little, always, because it takes away the anaesthetic of explanation. And it is that small burning that, after all, heals.

A word now about releasing, because here too there is a common mistake. Releasing is not an effort, it is not one more thing you have to do well, it is not tearing out of yourself what you dislike by force of will. That would be the veil of effort infiltrating even the path of healing, turning your own growth into one more task at which you can fail. Releasing is, at bottom, ceasing to hold. It is stopping feeding the pattern with your attention and your fear, and discovering that, without that fuel, it loses strength on its own. Often there is not even a dramatic moment of liberation. There is rather a day when you notice that what bound you for years no longer grips you as before, that you reacted in another way without even having tried, that the thing came undone in the dark while you were living. Releasing has, frequently, the taste of a small mourning, because even the prisons we grew used to have the comfort of the known, and to let go is also to say goodbye to who we were inside them. Let what must hurt, hurt. Sadness, here, is a sign that something true is being freed.

About time, and about relapses, because you will have them, and I do not want them to catch you off guard nor for you to take them as failure. This is not a straight line. It is a spiral, I have said it, and on a spiral you pass several times by the same point, but never at the same height. You will recognise yourself repeating a pattern you thought resolved, and you will be tempted to think you have learned nothing, that you are back to square one. You have not gone back. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before you did not even realise, and now you see yourself doing it, perhaps still in the middle of the gesture, perhaps already halfway. That seeing, earlier and earlier, is the progress, though it does not have the form you expected. The pattern does not vanish from one day to the next as if by magic. It keeps losing strength, it catches you fewer times and for less time, until one day, with no celebration at all, you notice that it hardly comes anymore. Be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades and generations to weave.

A note, still, on how to read this, because the way you read is also part of it. Do not read in haste, in one sitting, to get to the end. This is not a book to finish, it is a book to inhabit. Read one veil at a time, and then close it and let it work in you for a few days. Notice where, in your concrete life, that pattern appears. Do the practice, even if it seems too small to you, because it is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here. If you can, write, in a notebook only yours, what each veil stirs. Not to resolve it, to see it. And return. Return to this book in a few months and reread the same veil, and you will find it different, because you will be different. The books that matter are not read once. They accompany us, and tell us new things at each age of the soul.

## Where to begin

The seven veils appear here in an order, but your life does not live them in any order. In each phase, there is one that is thicker, more active, pulling the strings with more force. Beginning with that one, the one that grips you most now, is usually more fertile than following the numbering. So, before you go on, let me help you find it, with a few simple questions. Read slowly and notice which of them makes you say, inside, "this is me".

If what weighs on you most is not knowing who you are outside your roles, if you cling to an image of yourself for fear of losing it, if changing seems a betrayal of yourself, your veil right now is probably the first, **Permanence**.

If you live bound to your story, if "I have always been this way" is your phrase, if the past chooses for you before you choose, begin with the second, **Memory**.

If your head does not stop, if anxiety and rumination govern you, if you feel you are your emotions instead of having them, your veil is the third, **The Whirlwind**.

If you exhaust yourself caring for everyone, if rest gives you guilt, if you learned that love is earned by doing, go straight to the fourth, **Effort**. It is, perhaps, the most common among us.

If you flee from silence and solitude, if you fill your life so as not to feel the emptiness, if being alone seems a danger, your veil is the fifth, **Desolation**.

If you live always waiting for a "when", if you postpone life for a later that never comes, if no achievement brings you peace for long, begin with the sixth, **The Horizon**.

If, beneath everything, you feel a separation and a deep solitude that no company cures, if the world always seems to you on the other side of a glass, your deepest veil is the seventh, **Duality**, and perhaps it will make sense for you to read it last, because it is the one that holds all the others.

Did you recognise yourself in more than one? It is natural, the veils intertwine, and removing one usually loosens the others. Do not worry about choosing correctly. Choose the one that called you, read that veil, and let it work. The book waits for you for the others, in whatever time is yours. There is no wrong order to return home.

## Veil 1 · Permanence

> *There is something in you that remains when all the rest dissolves.*

You introduce yourself, and the same phrase always comes out. The name, what you do, whose daughter you are, what you have already lost, what you have become good at. You have said it so many times that it stopped being an introduction and became a contour, and inside that contour you learned to fit. You are the strong one. You are the responsible one. You are the one who endures, who does not collapse, the one others seek when the ground trembles. And there is some hour, perhaps late at night when the house falls asleep, when you feel that phrase tighten, like the clothes of a body you no longer have. But you hold it with both hands, because who would you be without it? This is the first veil, and the most stubborn: the belief that you are a fixed thing, an identity you have to carry and defend as if your life depended on it.

I think of a woman, and it could be so many. She was, all her life, the pillar of the family, the one who solved things, the one everyone sought in crises and no one sought on good days. When the children left and she was alone, she did not first weep their absence. She wept something else, harder to confess: she did not know who she was without someone to hold up. She had spent so long being the pillar that she had forgotten she was also a house, with rooms inside still to be inhabited. She was not broken. She was only discovering, late, that she was much more than the function that had made her indispensable.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By introducing yourself always by the same phrase, the same roles, as if outside them no one remained. By feeling that changing course, opinion, life, would be betraying yourself or disappointing those who count on you. By finding it hard to ask for help or to show fragility, because your place was always that of the strong one, the one who endures, who does not collapse. By an identity so glued to a function, mother, daughter, professional, the one who solves things, that the mere idea of letting it go opens in you a tightening of emptiness. By defending who you are with a firmness that, seen from outside, looks like security, and that inside is, so often, fear of not knowing who you would be without it. And by feeling, in certain silences, that the phrase you repeat about yourself already grips you like the clothes of a body that has since changed. Recognising this takes nothing from what you are. It only loosens, a little, the knot with which you hold it.

Where is this veil born? Almost always in a house where only one version of you was welcome. The child who receives love when she is strong learns to hide fragility. The one who is only seen when she stands out learns that to exist is to perform. The one who had to grow up fast clings to the role of the responsible one, because it was that which secured her a place. You did not build a self out of vanity, you built it to survive, to belong to those you needed to want you. And often the rigidity is not even yours: you inherited it from women who had to stay whole at all costs, where stepping out of line was dangerous. Defending your identity today tooth and nail may be, without your knowing, a loyalty to them.

Seeing this veil is recognising that the problem was never that you have an identity, it is that you confused yourself with it, to the point where letting go of a role seems like dying. But there is an enormous difference between having a form and being the form. Think of a tree. The roots keep where you come from, the trunk sustains the roles, the leaves are the facets that change with the seasons and that must fall. If a branch breaks, if the leaves drop, the tree does not stop being a tree. You are not the leaves. You are what remains when they fall. And you can prove it now: whenever you notice a thought, there is in you the one who notices, and that one is not the thought. That one who sees has no name nor function, and need not be defended, because what cannot be lost does not need defence.

Releasing this veil is not throwing away who you are nor ceasing to be capable. It is returning identity to the place from which it should never have left, clothing and not skin. You remain a daughter, mother, professional, friend, everything you are. You only stop dying a little each time one of those roles changes, ends or is taken from you. The children leave and you do not collapse with the empty nest, because you were not only the nest. And there is an immense rest on the other side: if you are not the name you defend, then you do not have to defend it full time, in permanent guard. You can lower your arms, make mistakes, not know, change your mind, without that being the end of you.

This veil has ways of hiding that are worth knowing, because they usually pass for virtue. There is the disguise of the strong one, who never trembles, who confuses rigidity with character and is terrified of being seen as fragile. There is that of the one who defines herself entirely by a role, mother, professional, carer, and who, when that role changes or ends, feels she has stopped existing. There is that of the consistent one at all costs, who would rather stay the same all her life than admit she changed her mind, as if changing were lying about who she was. And there is the most silent disguise of all, that of loyalty. Many women defend a hard identity because they inherited it from mothers and grandmothers who had to stay whole at all costs, in times and houses where stepping out of line was dangerous. Staying rigid may be, without your knowing, a way of not betraying them. And there is a rest that only comes when you tell them, inside: I saw what you had to endure, I honour you, and from me on I can set down the part that is no longer needed.

What changes, when this veil lifts, is that life becomes lighter. You can change course at forty, at fifty, at sixty, without feeling you betray yourself, because you understand that to change is proper to one who is alive. The losses come to hurt without destroying you, because you discover in yourself a depth that no loss reaches. You regain curiosity about yourself, you stop being a statue intent on staying the same and become a river again. You do not become no one. You become free within who you are.

*Defending who you were was how you kept your place when having it seemed at risk. Thank you, identity. I can exist now without wielding you.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, without rushing to answer. Who would you be if you let go, for a single day, of the role that most defines you? What do you fear would happen if you stopped being the strong one, the one who endures, who solves everything? From whom did you learn, back then, that only one version of you was welcome at home? And what in you remains, even when all the roles change, end or are taken from you?

**The practice.** Find a moment of calm, alone, and say your name in a low voice, slowly, three times. On the third, instead of thinking about the name, notice the one who is hearing it. Stay with that one who listens for the length of three breaths, doing nothing, changing nothing. And, over the week, write three phrases that begin with "I am" and, beside each one, ask what would happen if it stopped being true. The one that tightens your chest is not your identity. It is the veil you most defend, and the first to learn to set down.

## Veil 2 · Memory

> *You are not your narrative. You are a living now, always to be written.*

There is a phrase you have been saying about yourself for so long that you no longer notice you say it. "I have always been this way." "After that, I became like this." "With me it is always the same thing." It comes out with the naturalness of one stating the colour of her eyes, as if it were a fact and not a choice of every day. Your story walks with you as proof and as sentence at the same time, and it is the one that decides, before you decide, who you love, what you flee from, how much you ask for, what you think you deserve. Without noticing, you live looking backwards, confirming who you are by what has already happened to you, like someone driving an entire car while looking in the rear-view mirror.

Notice a small and common scene. Someone takes a while to answer a message of yours, and in seconds you already have the story assembled: they are drifting away, it was like the other time, it is always like this with me, I am no one's priority. The person was only in a meeting. But you have already lived the whole afternoon inside an old film, with characters from twenty years ago, feeling in today's body a pain that belongs to another time. You did not react to the present. You reacted to the memory.

There is a woman who used to say, laughing but in earnest, that she was "terrible at choosing men". She repeated it like one who repeats a worn joke, and each new disappointment confirmed the sentence, there, all neat. Only much later, looking calmly, did she understand that she was not choosing badly. She was always choosing the same, the same kind of distance, the same promise that was not kept, because that was the love she knew from home, and the known, even when it hurts, has the treacherous comfort of the familiar. The phrase "I am terrible at choosing" was not a diagnosis. It was a spell she herself renewed in each relationship. The day she stopped saying it, and looked at the pattern instead of the sentence, she began, for the first time, to be able to choose in another way.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? You catch yourself using the words "always" and "never" a great deal when you speak of yourself, as if they were laws of physics. You justify the present with the past almost without noticing, "it is just that I, with the childhood I had". You react to situations of today with an intensity that, if you are honest, belongs to another era and to other people. You have stories about yourself that you always tell in the same way, with the same words, worn from being so repeated, and that you have never questioned. You feel a kind of loyalty to your suffering, as if letting it go were betraying those who lived it with you or before you. And you project the future as an inevitable continuation of what has already been, closing doors before you even approach them. Recognising this does not erase your story. It only takes you out of inside it long enough that you can, at last, tell it in another way.

Where is this veil born? In the way they taught you to read your own life, before you were old enough to read it on your own. Children do not receive only facts, they receive interpretations. "You are like this", "with us it is always like this", "this family has bad luck", "love hurts". Each of these phrases, repeated, becomes a lens, and from then on you spend your life confirming what they told you you were. And there is the deeper inheritance, that of the stories that were not even yours. When a family carries an unwept loss, a secret, an injustice that no one noticed, those who come after usually repeat the plot without knowing, as if completing a phrase left half-said by those who came before. There are women sad with a sadness that was their mother's, loyal to a destiny they received at birth like one who receives a name. To look at your narrative through this lens is to discover that part of the script you did not write, and that, for that very reason, you have the right not to continue it.

This veil makes us believe we are the sum of our past, and that carrying the story is the same as being faithful to it. It even seems a form of honesty, of coherence. But there is here a strange thing that matters for you to see, because it changes everything: memory does not keep the past as it was. It retells it. Each time you return to an old scene, it comes back slightly different, tinged with today's emotion, patched by what you have learned since, and you live that re-stirred version as if it were the pure fact. You do not remember what happened. You remember the last time you remembered. Memory is less an archive and more a room where the story is staged anew, every time, with small alterations to the script.

The past is real, and it is not about denying it nor pretending it did not hurt. It is about something subtler and freer: the past happened once, but the reading you make of it happens every day, and that, yes, you can change. The same childhood can be told as proof that you are not capable, or as proof that you survived much. The same facts, two stories, two different lives out of the same material.

Releasing this veil is not erasing what you lived, nor rewriting the story in a rose-coloured version you do not believe. It is to stop letting what has been always have the last word over what may yet be. You are not your narrative. You are the living now that is telling it, and that can, from today, tell it in another way. Not as a sentence, but as a root. And there is an enormous difference between the two. The sentence binds, defines, closes the case. The root sustains, nourishes, and lets the tree grow in any direction the light asks for. When the story stops being a cage and becomes a root again, the present opens, free, perhaps for the first time in a long time, and you discover that the future does not have to resemble the past just because it always has.

This veil has a particularly stubborn way of holding on, and it has a name in family constellation: loyalty. Notice that, often, letting go of your old story seems almost a betrayal. If your mother suffered and you built your identity around that shared suffering, to be happy can sound, deep down, like abandoning her. If in your family one was always poor, or unlucky in love, or ill, to prosper can bring a strange guilt, as if you were stepping out of the line, leaving your own behind. There are women who sabotage, without realising, everything that goes well for them, faithful to a family destiny they confused with identity. Seeing this is liberating, because it allows a new choice: I can honour where I come from without having to repeat what hurt there. Belonging to my family does not oblige me to inherit its sadness.

There is still a confusion to undo. Releasing the narrative is not lying about the past, pretending it was all good, or falling into that empty positivity that orders you to turn the page without having read it. It is the opposite. It is looking at the whole story, including what hurt, with today's eyes and not those of back then. A child interprets what happens to her with the resources of a child, and almost always concludes that the fault is hers, that she was not enough, that something in her was wrong. To reread that same story with the eyes of an adult woman is, often, to free the girl from a sentence she never deserved. The facts do not change. What changes is who reads them, and that changes everything.

There is also, in this veil, a subtler form of prison, worth looking at calmly. We walk through life as if an invisible thread guided each step, in search of an explanation that justifies us, of a meaning that gives order to what we lived. And that search, so human, can itself become a chain: the more we pursue the answer about who we are and why what happened to us happened, the further we move from the stillness that already pulses in the present, here, with no explanation at all. When we finally release the narrative that defined us, a strange space opens. The self no longer leans on the past, but has also not yet found firm ground in the now, and the mind, used to continuity, does not know how to inhabit that interval. An emptiness arises. Do not be frightened by it, because it is an emptiness of gestation, not of lack. It is the fertile territory between the story you let go of and the life you have not yet named, and it is precisely there, without the comfort of the old certainties about yourself, that something truly new can, for the first time, begin to be born.

What changes when this veil lifts is your relationship with time. You stop living backwards, confirming who you are by what has been, and begin to live forwards, open to what is not yet written. The same memories remain there, but they lose the power to govern you, because you stopped treating them as a sentence and began to hold them as learning. You react to the people of today by what they are, and not by what others did to you. And there happens, often, an unexpected reconciliation with those who came before, because, in seeing that you inherited their pain without choosing it, you stop blaming them and begin to understand them, and in that understanding something in you is also freed. You become lighter of a weight you carried without knowing it could be set down.

*Holding on to the past was how you protected yourself from repeating an old pain. Thank you, memory. The pain was from another time.*

Before the practice, sit a moment with these questions, without haste. What story do you tell about yourself that you never went back to question? If it were not true, or were not the whole truth, who could you be? To whom are you being loyal when you repeat that story, and what would you fear to lose if you let it go? And if you looked at your childhood with today's eyes, instead of the eyes of the girl who lived it, what would you see differently, what would you forgive, to whom would you return a guilt that was never yours?

The practice is this, and it asks of you a gentle courage. Choose a phrase you have said about yourself for years, one of those that begin with "I never" or "I always". Write it on a piece of paper. Read it aloud, slowly, and then ask only this, without rushing to answer: whose voice is this? Stay with the question. Often you will discover, with a shiver, that the voice is not yours. It is a tired mother's, a demanding father's, a teacher's, someone who spoke first and that you continued to repeat inside for decades, doing their work. When you recognise it, in silence, return the phrase to them. You do not have to fight it nor hate it. Only to notice that it was a loan, and that you no longer have to keep paying it for them.

## Veil 3 · The Whirlwind

> *There is in you a presence that observes, and is not caught by what stirs.*

Your head does not stop. You anticipate conversations that have not yet happened and you relive, point by point, the ones that already have. You rehearse perfect answers for imaginary arguments, you defend yourself from attacks no one made, you solve ten times a problem that has not even arrived and that perhaps never will. At night, when the body finally stops, the mind accelerates, as if it took advantage of the silence to dump everything it held back during the day. And you feel each thing with such intensity that you become what you feel: you are not sad, you are the whole sadness, from head to foot; you do not have fear, you are fear from one end to the other. When the whirlwind catches you, it seems there is nothing else beyond it, and that it is you.

Notice how the whirlwind disguises itself as responsibility. You tell yourself you are only thinking, preventing, solving in advance, and so you let the wheel spin as if it were useful work. A woman told that she spent her nights planning conversations with her boss, anticipating each criticism, rehearsing defences for attacks that never happened. In the morning she woke exhausted, as if she had lived the argument ten times, and the real argument, when it finally came, lasted two minutes and went well. She had suffered everything, except what actually happened. The whirlwind was not protecting her from anything. It was only robbing her of her nights in the name of a security she never came to feel.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By the head that does not switch off, especially at night, when the body finally stops and the mind takes advantage of the silence to dump everything. By the tendency to anticipate the worst and to rehearse scenarios that rarely happen. By feeling the emotions as waves that submerge you whole, with no margin on which to stand. By the difficulty in being simply present, without commentary, at a meal, in a conversation, in a good moment, because the head is already analysing, judging, planning the next. By confusing worry with love or with responsibility, thinking that, if you stopped fretting, you would be being negligent. And by a background sense of being almost always reacting, rarely choosing. None of these signs is illness nor flaw of character. They are the invitation to discover, beneath the noise, who it is in you that never moves.

Where is this veil born? Often in environments where the child had to be always on alert. Where the climate of the house was unpredictable, where it was necessary to read the adults' moods to protect oneself, where danger, physical or emotional, could come without warning. A nervous system that grows like this learns to keep watch as one learns to breathe, and the hypervigilance, which was protection, becomes later anxiety without object, a head that does not switch off because switching off, in times past, was risky. Adult rumination is, often, the old child still trying to predict the unpredictable so as not to be caught by surprise again. And there is also an inheritance here: mothers and grandmothers who lived in times or houses of affliction pass on, without words, a state of alert that no longer corresponds to today's life. When you see that your agitation has this root, you stop fighting it as an enemy and begin to treat it as what it is: a part of you, loyal and tired, that can finally know the danger has passed.

We live in an age that feeds this veil as never before. Everything cries out for your attention, everything asks of you an immediate reaction, and your nervous system, already trained to keep watch for danger, rarely finds a moment of rest. But the whirlwind is not, in truth, a problem of too many thoughts. It is a problem of identification. You confuse what stirs inside with what you are, and so each wave carries you off, without resistance, as if there were no margin on which to stand.

But there is a margin. There is here a door, small and almost secret, and it is this: if you can see a thought, then there is in you something that is not that thought. Notice the almost silly simplicity of this truth, and the force it has. The one who observes the sadness is not totally taken by it, otherwise she could not observe it. The one who notices the fear has, in that same instant, one foot outside the fear. The storm is real, the wind is real, the rain wets. But you are not the storm. You are the sky through which it passes, and no sky, however black the day becomes, was ever torn by a cloud. The clouds pass. The sky stays.

Seeing this veil is discovering that presence that attends, silent, beneath all the noise. It is always there, even on the worst days, even when you swear you cannot find a centimetre of peace. It does not ask you to silence the mind by force, because fighting against thoughts is only more thought, more whirlwind trying to fight whirlwind. It does not ask you to stop feeling, because the emotion has the right to pass through you. It asks of you only one thing, and it is a change of place: that you stop being inside the wave, soaked, and begin to see it from the shore. The emotion still comes, with the same force, but you are no longer dragged by it to the bottom. You look, you let it come, you let it pass.

Releasing this veil is to stop treating each thought as an order you have to obey and each emotion as a final truth about reality. A thought of fear is not a prophecy. A wave of anger is not an instruction. They cross you, say what they have to say, and go, if you let them go instead of grabbing them to interrogate them. Little by little, with practice and with patience, the silence that exists beneath the noise stops being a weekend rarity at a retreat, and becomes a place you know how to return to in the middle of the day, in traffic, in the queue, in the kitchen. Not because your life became suddenly calm. But because you found, within yourself, the one who is not altered by it.

It is worth understanding how the whirlwind feeds itself, so you stop adding wood to it without meaning to. It grows above all from two roots. The first is fusion: believing that everything the mind says is true and deserves an immediate answer. A thought appears, "and what if the worst happens", and you treat that phrase as a real warning, and you mobilise, and the mind, seeing that you took it seriously, produces ten more like it. The second root is resistance: wanting not to feel what you feel, fighting against the anxiety, getting irritated for being irritated. And there happens the cruel paradox of all inner life, what is called, rightly, the law of the inverse: everything you resist, persists. The emotion to which you say no stays at the door knocking harder. The emotion you let in, sit down and be felt, usually stays a short time, because that was all it asked for, to be felt.

There is a common misunderstanding, and I want to undo it, because it can make you feel like a failure for no reason. Coming out of the whirlwind is not reaching an empty mind, without thoughts, flat as a lake in the morning. That does not exist, except in meditation photographs, and pursuing it only creates one more effort, one more horizon. The mind will keep producing thoughts, it is its function, as the stomach produces gastric juice. Freedom is not having no thoughts. It is to stop being dragged by them. It is to be able to have a full head and, at the same time, a calm centre that observes that full head without drowning. The sky does not need the clouds to stop in order to keep being sky.

It is honest to tell you one thing about this veil, so you do not become frightened when it happens. The clarity that opens between you and the thoughts does not settle as a permanent rest, a smooth peace forever. When the light touches the deepest layers, it does not reveal only serenity. It also exposes the shadows that were hidden in the swirl of the mind. Many imagine that quieting the head brings constant calm, and soon realise that, in crossing the whirlwind, the wind also lifts the dry leaves of what was unresolved. There is no crossing without that trembling. The silence that seemed firm ground finds, suddenly, sleeping memories, old wounds, beliefs that guided you without being seen. And that is not a sign that it went wrong. It is a sign that it went deep. The light did not come only to calm you, it came to show you what still binds you, and to show is the first gesture of any liberation. So, when the stillness brings up what you did not expect, do not flee back into the noise. Stay. It is your own depth trusting you enough to let itself finally be seen.

There is a place, in the depth of this veil, that one reaches when one lets go even of the eagerness to cross it well. While the mind sustains a role, even the role of one who seeks, who meditates, who wants to awaken, there is still effort, there is still agitation disguised as path. But at a certain point that mask too releases, and what opens is not an arid emptiness, it is a living soil where the breath gains another texture. The silence that springs forth is no longer absence imposed by force, it is natural presence, that dilates in the body like a breath with no owner. Life stops needing to fit into narratives and into frames. There is, beneath thought, a discreet and whole vibration that asks no proof nor demands a name, it only pulses. You do not have to fabricate it. You only have to make silence enough to notice that it was always there.

What changes when this veil lifts is that you gain a centre. The mind keeps producing its noise, but you stop living inside it full time. You come to have a calm place from which you observe the storm without being the storm, and that changes everything in daily life. The decisions stop being made in panic and begin to come from a more serene place. You feel the emotions with the same intensity, but they no longer drag you to the bottom, because you know they are waves and that you are the sea. You sleep better, because the head learns, slowly, that not every thought deserves an emergency meeting at three in the morning. And you discover, perhaps for the first time, the taste of being simply present, without commentary, in a moment that does not need to be analysed in order to be lived.

*Anticipating everything was how you kept yourself safe. Thank you, vigilance. I am safe now, and thinking is not solving.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, slowly. When your head accelerates, what story is it trying to protect you from feeling? If you believed half of what you think about the future, how much of your energy would you be spending defending yourself from things that will never happen? Can you, right now, find in you the one who is reading these lines, behind all the noise? And what would change in your day if you treated your thoughts as passing clouds, and not as orders to obey?

The practice is short and you can use it today, in the middle of a small crisis. The next time the head accelerates, do not fight nor try to think better to solve the thought. Say inside, slowly, almost like one commenting on the weather: I am noticing that I am thinking this. That simple "I am noticing" already takes you out of inside the wave and puts you on the shore, because to notice a thing you have to be, for an instant, outside it. From there, breathe three times, slow, and in each breath feel who it is that breathes. You are not the whirlwind that passes through your head. You are, and always were, the one who sees it pass.

## Veil 4 · Effort

> *It is not in the running, but in the rest, that the truth is revealed.*

You always arrive first. You anticipate what others will need before they themselves know, you solve before they ask, you hold what is falling before it falls. You please, you care, you do your part and then, silently, you do the others' part too, and still you apologise for not having done more. To sit with nothing to do gives you an immediate restlessness, a dull guilt, as if rest were a debt you will have to pay. And deep down, in a place you rarely look at directly, lives an old agreement you never consciously signed but that you fulfil every day: if I do enough, I stay. If I stop, they stop loving me.

There is a scene that repeats in many houses. The woman spent the whole day taking care of everyone, and at night, finally seated, someone says to her, with the best of intentions, "rest a bit, everything is fine". And instead of relief, an irritation rises in her that she herself does not understand, or a tightening, or a sudden urge to go tidy something. Because rest, for her body, is not safe. To stop is to risk, is to be in plain sight, is to lose the excuse of doing to justify that she deserves to be there. It was easier for her, all her life, to give than to receive, to serve than to be served, because receiving meant believing she was enough with nothing to offer in exchange. And that, the simplest of all, was always the hardest account to settle.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By the guilt that rises whenever you rest, as if rest were a debt to be paid later with interest. By saying "yes" with your mouth while your body screams "no", and only realising too late. By it being far easier for you to give than to receive, to the point where a compliment or some help leaves you uncomfortable, not knowing where to put yourself. By anticipating others' needs before your own, often before they even feel them. By a little voice that whispers that your worth depends on what you produce, on what you solve, on how much you are needed. By the chronic exhaustion that rest does not cure, because it is not the body that is tired, it is the vigilance that never switches off. And by the silent hurt of always being the one who holds, and rarely having anyone who holds you. If this touches you, you are not being needy nor weak for feeling it. You are only, finally, hearing a part of you that had been silent for too long.

Where is this veil born? It usually is born early, in a child who realised that love was not guaranteed, it was earned. Perhaps you had to care for those who should have cared for you, to be the small adult of a house in disorder. Perhaps affection only arrived wrapped in praise for what you did, never for what you were. Perhaps you learned, without anyone telling you, that if you stopped being useful you became invisible. The girl deduced an equation to survive, and the woman keeps solving it decades later, without noticing that the conditions changed. And there is almost always a line of women behind: grandmothers and mothers who only knew how to be worth something by what they gave, who confused love with sacrifice because that was what was taught them, and who passed that gesture on to you like one who passes a sacred inheritance. When you see it like this, you can finally honour them without having to repeat them, and tell them inside: I saw what you carried, and from me on, this weight is set down.

This is, perhaps, the most common veil among the women who carry everything, and at the same time the hardest to see, because it is the only one the world applauds. They call it dedication, generosity, being trustworthy, being a rock. You receive praise precisely for the chain that binds you. No one tells you that you are exhausted because, seen from outside, you seem tireless. And you yourself take pride in it, while, inside, you go on emptying, with the bitter sense that no one holds you, that you are the one who holds and no one else notices that you too needed hands.

Seeing this veil begins with a tenderness you perhaps do not usually give yourself. No one sat down with you to explain that your worth had to be earned. You deduced it, as a child, in the most intelligent way a child has, which is by observing. At some moment you learned that you would be more loved if you were useful, safer if you were needed, less subject to abandonment if you became indispensable. Perhaps you grew up caring for those who should have cared for you. Perhaps love, in your house, always came wrapped in a task. Your body kept the lesson as if life depended on it, and so your goodness is, at the same time, true and strategic. The two things, together, with no contradiction. It is genuine love and it is also an old way of ensuring they do not leave you. It is no hypocrisy at all. It is a clever girl doing everything she knew to be loved.

Releasing this veil is the most risky experience your body knows, and I want you to know this so you do not become frightened when the fear rises. It is to stop doing, for a moment, and see that you continue to be loved. Not because you hold everything. Not because you are useful. Because you exist, and that is all. The fullness you keep pursuing up ahead, always at the end of one more task, at the bottom of one more list, is not in the running. It never was. It is in the rest you are afraid to allow yourself, because it is exactly in the rest, when you stop proving, that you discover your worth was nowhere waiting to be conquered. It was already here, whole, from always, beneath everything you do. The hands, which all your life caught others before the fall, can finally rest on your own lap, without ceasing to love anyone. Loving stops being carrying. It becomes being.

This veil has disguises so praised that it is hard to catch it. See if you recognise any. The disguise of the indispensable one, who organises everything and then complains, exhausted, that no one helps, without realising she trained everyone not to help, because no one does it as well nor as fast as she. The disguise of the one who never asks, who would rather go without than give the trouble of being cared for, and who calls this independence when it is, at bottom, fear of depending and being disappointed. The disguise of the saviour, who surrounds herself with people to rescue because, as long as there is someone needing her, she knows what her place is and feels safe. And the most subtle disguise of all, that of the one who gives in order to control: giving much creates a silent debt, and one who is in debt does not leave. There is no malice in any of this. There is a girl, deep down, who learned that free love was not for her, and that she would have to pay for it all her life in advance.

I want to speak too of the tiredness, because it is the symptom that usually brings these women to seek help. It is not the tiredness of one who works much, that passes with rest. It is a tiredness that sleeps eight hours and wakes the same, an exhaustion that comes from being, for years, holding a weight that is not only physical. It is the weight of being responsible for others' happiness, of constantly monitoring whether everyone is well, of not being able to lower the guard. There are women who only grasp the scale of this when the body forces them to stop, with an illness, an exhaustion, a collapse. The body says, in its own way, what the mouth never allowed itself to say: enough, I too need, I too count. Releasing this veil before reaching that point is one of the greatest acts of self-love I know, and one of the hardest, because you have to do it against years of praise.

There is a form of this veil so fine that it hides inside the very gestures of love, and I want to show it to you, because it is the hardest to see. At times, even in the most luminous gestures, an almost imperceptible weight insinuates itself. The serving seems limpid, but it carries a dense silence, as if it expected something in return. And in that secret corner the effort settles, not in the act of offering, but in the hidden expectation of being recognised, seen, repaid. At first almost nothing betrays itself: the gesture repeats, the presence keeps the appearance of spontaneity. But beneath the surface a vigil grows. The giving no longer rests in itself, it begins to measure reactions, to watch responses, to wait for signs that it was worth it. And what should expand freely begins to shrink, and the soul, even while offering, feels the burden of sustaining. To recognise this is not to discover that your love was false. It is to discover where it grew tired, where it tied itself to an exchange no one guaranteed you, and to be able, at last, to return its lightness to it. The love that expects nothing in return is the only one that does not exhaust the one who loves.

In the depth of this veil, there is a point at which the search out there begins, on its own, to fall silent. Not out of giving up, nor out of one more effort of will to stop making an effort, which would be only the same veil gripping in other clothes. It is rather the fading of an eagerness that emptied itself of fuel, like a fire to which they stopped adding wood. The body, before agitated in the incessant search for validation, is surprised by a rest it did not expect. The mind, once entangled in promises that you would be worth more after this and that, releases and settles on what simply is. There are no trumpets nor spectacular revelation. There is only a discreet softness, like the wind that ceases suddenly and leaves the whole space vibrating in silence. And in that silence you understand, without drama, that you never needed to run outwards to find what was already, quiet, within.

What changes when this veil lifts is, above all, your body. The chronic tiredness begins to give way, not because you started doing less, but because you stopped carrying the permanent vigilance of being responsible for everyone's life. You learn to receive, and you discover that receiving does not diminish you, it completes you. You begin to say "no" without a whole speech of justification behind it, and the sky does not fall. Your relationships, curiously, improve, because they stop resting on your exhaustion and begin to rest on your true presence. And love, which for so long you confused with doing, with serving, with holding, reveals itself after all in something much simpler and much harder for you: being. Being whole, without having to prove anything, beside those you love and beside yourself. The hands, finally, can rest.

*Doing without stopping was how you felt loved and needed. Thank you, effort. I am enough before I do.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, with tenderness for yourself. What do you fear would happen if you stopped doing, solving, anticipating? If love did not have to be earned, what would you do differently today? Who would care for you if you stopped, for a moment, being the one who takes care of everything? And from whom did you learn, back then, that your place was to give until there was nothing left of you?

The practice is small from outside and enormous inside, and it is perhaps the hardest in this book. Choose one small thing you would do by habit to serve, please or get work ahead for others, and today, only today, do not do it. Do not take the dishes from the hand of someone who is able to lift them. Do not answer the message in the exact second. Do not solve the problem that is not yours. And then stay attentive to the discomfort that rises almost immediately: the urge to justify yourself, the guilt, the idea that they will think badly of you. Do not solve that discomfort. Do not cover it with another task. Only stay watching it, with your hand on your chest, three breaths, like one who watches over a frightened child. It is the veil protesting for being lifted. On the other side of it, and closer each time you practise, is your rest.

## Veil 5 · Desolation

> *Emptiness is not absence. It is the silent field where the soul germinates.*

There is a place inside you that you avoid at all costs, and you do not always know that you avoid it. It does not appear with its face uncovered. You notice it in the lines between your gestures: in the hurry to turn on the television the moment you enter the house, in the phone you pick up for no reason, in the task you invent when, for a rare instant, no one needs you and there is nothing to solve. The moment silence settles, a dull anguish rises, without form, and you run to fill it before you even feel it fully, like one who flees a dark room without waiting to see whether there is, in fact, anything there.

A woman realised this on a weekend in which, by chance, she stayed home alone, with no plans, with no one to care for. Instead of the rest she said she had wanted for months, she felt a growing discomfort, almost panic, and found herself calling friends, inventing outings, filling the hours with anything. On Sunday night, exhausted from so much fleeing, she finally sat on the sofa with the silence. The first minutes were almost unbearable. Then, slowly, the anguish began to settle, like dust that comes to rest, and beneath it appeared not the abyss she feared, but a kind of forgotten peace, and some gentle tears, and the simple question she had been fleeing for years: and I, what is it that I need? Emptiness had not swallowed her. It had given her voice back.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By the difficulty in being home alone, in silence, without television, music or phone covering the background. By the tendency to fill the calendar until no time is left for you alone, and to call this having a full life. By staying in relationships or commitments that no longer do you good, only so as not to face the emptiness that would come after letting them go. By a restlessness that rises whenever there is nothing to solve, as if peace itself were threatening. By confusing solitude with failure, as if being alone were public proof that something in you failed. And by rarely knowing how to answer, when asked, what it is that you, only you, truly like, because it has been so long since you stopped to hear yourself. Recognising this does not condemn you to isolation. It opens to you the possibility of a company you may never have had: your own.

Where is this veil born? Almost always in an early solitude that hurt too much. A child left alone for a long time, emotionally or physically, learns that silence is abandonment and that being alone is being in danger of not being supported. Other times it is born the opposite, in a house so full, so noisy or so demanding that there was never space for the child to hear herself, and she grew up without ever having learned to be with herself, terrified of the emptiness because she never inhabited it in safety. And there are inheritances here too: families that fled from silence, that had to be always doing, that treated stillness as laziness or as threat, and that passed on that horror of emptiness without ever naming it. When you see where your fear of desolation comes from, it loses part of its power, because it stops being a truth about emptiness and becomes a memory about you. And memories, unlike truths, can be revisited with new eyes.

This veil makes you read emptiness as a threat. As absence, abandonment, proof that something is missing in you or that you fell behind while others had their full lives. You learned, somewhere, perhaps very early, that being alone was being helpless, and ever since you confuse stillness with loss. They are such different things, and the wound does not distinguish them. So you fill. You fill with people who do not even do you good, with background noise, with occupation, with relationships that do not want to end, with anything that covers that silent depth where you are afraid to fall and never stop falling.

Seeing this veil is to understand, with some surprise, that what frightens you is not emptiness itself. It is the fear of it, and the memory glued to that fear. They are distinct things, and that distinction is the door. Emptiness, when you finally dare to stay in it without fleeing, does not swallow you. It stays there, with you, and after a few minutes it begins to show itself from within as what it always was, and which no one taught you to see. Not the abyss. The earth. The silent and dark field where the seed germinates without witnesses, away from the eyes, in the time that is its own and not ours. Notice that nothing living is born in agitation. The seed needs the dark of the earth. The child forms in the dark of the womb. Everything that comes to flower grows first in a place without light and without applause, in an apparent emptiness that is, in truth, pure gestation.

Releasing this veil is, simply, to stay. To stay in the silence long enough to discover the difference, which is enormous, between being alone and being with yourself. One is abandonment, it is the absence of others lived as lack. The other is encounter, it is your own company, which perhaps you had been avoiding for years without knowing. When you stop fleeing from emptiness, it stops being a threat and reveals itself as the only place quiet enough for you to hear yourself. The desolation you so feared shows itself fertile, and what seemed lack was, after all, space. Space for something of yours, long postponed, finally to breathe and begin to take form. Many women only discover what they want from life after they stop covering the silence where that answer always was, waiting, patient.

It is important to distinguish two things that look alike from outside and are opposite inside: imposed solitude and chosen withdrawal. There is a solitude that hurts, that of one who wanted company and does not have it, that of isolation, of exclusion, of abandonment. That is real and it is not the one I speak of here, and not everything is resolved within, there are those who really need more bond and more world. But there is another thing, that many women have never known: the withdrawal one chooses, being alone out of liking and not out of lack, the inhabited silence. One who never learned to be well with herself tends to flee from both, and to fill life with relationships and occupations precisely so as never to have to discover whether she can bear her own company. The irony is that it is only one who learns to be well alone who stops clinging to relationships out of fear, and begins to be in them by choice. Well-inhabited emptiness does not move you away from others. It frees your relationships from the weight of having to use them as escape.

There is a fear beneath this veil worth naming, because it is the engine of everything: the fear that, if you stop and look inside, you will find nothing. That the silence will reveal a hollow, a lack of substance, the confirmation that you are no one interesting when you are not doing or pleasing. It is exactly the opposite that happens. What seems hollow, when you stay there, reveals itself full. What seemed absence of you reveals itself as your presence, finally without interruptions. No one told you that the bottom of the well, when you stop struggling, has water. It has always had it.

There is, in this veil, a particular fear, and it is different from the panic we know. When stillness settles after a loss, a fall, the end of a phase, it is not the emptiness that weighs most. It is the subtle presence of a fear without a face. It does not shout nor block, it only remains, like a discreet shadow keeping watch from afar. It does not stop you from walking, but it does not leave you free either, it prowls the margins of each gesture like a mist that does not disperse. This fear is not born of a concrete threat, there is no enemy ahead nor real danger. It grows from the absence, from the vast space that opened when the old forms collapsed. And what it guards, at bottom, is no longer your body nor your identity. It is the old habit of surviving clinging to what has already ended. Notice this, because it changes everything: what haunts you in desolation is not what you lost, it is your attachment to what you lost, the hand that keeps closed around something that is no longer there. And a hand closed around nothing can, finally, open. Not to lose more. To be able to receive again.

What changes when this veil lifts is that you stop being afraid of yourself. The silence, which was a threat, becomes a refuge. You come to know how to be alone without feeling abandoned, and that frees all your relationships of an enormous weight, because you no longer cling to anyone out of terror of the emptiness, and you come to be with people by choice and not by escape. You rediscover the liking for simple things that are only yours, which you had forgotten by being always full of people and tasks. And, at the bottom of that emptiness you so feared, you find not the abyss, but a spring, a quiet place from which spring the answers you were looking for out there. One who learns to inhabit her own silence is never again truly alone.

*Filling the emptiness was how you bore it without breaking. Thank you. The silence is no longer an abyss, it is a lap.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, without hurrying to cover what they stir. From what does the silence at home make you flee? If you stayed in the emptiness without filling it, what would you fear to discover in there? When was the last time you were alone out of liking, and not out of lack? And if stillness were not abandonment, but the only company that never leaves you, would you have the courage to sit with it?

The practice is brief and asks only that you not flee. The next time silence brings you that familiar anguish, do not fill it. Do not pick up the phone, do not turn anything on, do not invent a task. Stay two minutes, only two, with a clock if you need. Put your hand on your chest, feel your own warmth, and say, slowly, like one who calms a child in the middle of the night: this is not danger, it is space. Let the emptiness be earth, and not abyss. Two minutes, and then you can get up if you want. But you will discover, perhaps by the second or third time, that it did not swallow you. That on the other side of the fear of being alone was, waiting for you, your own company.

## Veil 6 · The Horizon

> *There is no end to reach. There is a flow always open, and it is now.*

You live leaning forwards, always a little ahead of where you are, towards a point further on where, finally, you will be able to be happy. When you lose weight. When he changes. When the children grow. When you have the money, the house, the diagnosis resolved, the peace you keep postponing. There is always a "when" and there is always a "there", and it is to that luminous there that you keep pushing life, while the true life, the only one that exists, keeps happening fully in this hurried here that you barely notice, because you are already in the next.

Think of all the times you said "next year". Next year I will slow down, I will travel, I will take care of myself, I will do what I have been postponing forever. There are women who say this for thirty years, with all sincerity, every year, and reach a certain age with the strange sense that life passed them by while they prepared it to begin. They were not unhappy, exactly. It was worse, in a way: they were almost always halfway to a happiness scheduled for later, always with their bags packed at the door of a train that never came. Life was not in the destination. It was in the waiting room, and no one had told them that the waiting room was the journey.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By living in "when" mode, always waiting for a future condition to begin living for real. By reaching a goal and feeling, instead of peace, a brief emptiness and then at once the eagerness for the next. By the difficulty in savouring what you already have, because the attention has already jumped to what is missing. By postponing rest, pleasure, presence, with the excuse that first you have to deserve, to resolve, to arrive. By a background dissatisfaction that no achievement silences for long. And by that sense, sometimes frightening, that life is passing fast and you are always a little beside it, preparing it instead of living it. Recognising this veil does not take away your dreams. It returns to you the only place where they, and you, can happen: here.

Where is this veil born? Frequently in a childhood where love and approval came after the achievement, never before. Where only the good mark, the good behaviour, the result, deserved the smile of those who mattered. The child learns that the present never comes, that there is always a next bar to deserve being loved, and grows up living in the future, always a step from finally being enough. Other times it is born of houses where the present was too hard to inhabit, and dreaming of the after was the only way to survive the now, a habit of escape that became a way of life. And there is the whole cultural inheritance, of generations that had to postpone everything, to live for the children, for retirement, for a better day that for many never came. When you see this root, you understand that postponing life is not a flaw of yours, it is an old and deep pattern, and that to stop running is not to give up on the dreams, it is to stop making of them the condition for having the right to live today.

This veil promises you an end. A finish line where everything resolves at once and from which you will be, at last, complete, at peace, ready to live. The mind needs that promise to keep you in motion, and so it always paints a new horizon, with beautiful colours. The problem, which perhaps you have already felt without naming, is that each time you approach, the horizon recedes. You reach the weight you wanted and the body still does not arrive. You resolve a debt and the next one appears. You reach the goal you thought would bring you rest and, in a few days, the restlessness moves to the next objective, as if it had never happened. It is not that you fail at your targets. It is that the "there" is, by nature, a place one does not reach, because it was made to be always ahead, like the line where the sky touches the sea and that recedes with each step we take towards it.

Seeing this veil is catching yourself, with some tenderness and some fright, in the repeated gesture of postponing presence for later. It is recognising how many times in life you said "when this passes" and how many times "this" was, after all, your life passing while you waited for it to begin. The illusion of a definitive arrival is perhaps the most polite thief of all, because it robs you smiling, always in the name of a better future, of the only place where anything can really happen, which is this instant, always this, the only one you ever had and the only one you will ever have.

Releasing this veil begins with a piece of news that seems bad and is the best of all: there is no arrival. There is no final point where everything is resolved forever, where life stops asking and you can finally sit down. And if there is no there towards which you run, then notice what that means, let it really settle: you are not late for anywhere. You can stop running. Life is not a straight line with a goal at the bottom to be conquered, it is a continuous flow, always open, without conclusion, in which each moment is whole in itself and not only a step towards the next. When you release the mirage of the horizon, you give up nothing that matters, you do not stop having dreams nor projects. You only bring back the happiness you were saving for "later" to the only time it was ever available, which is now. You keep walking. Only now you no longer walk fleeing the present.

This veil has a sophistication worth seeing: it dresses itself in virtue. We call it ambition, focus, dream, personal growth, and all that can be healthy. The difference lies in a subtle point. There is a walking that departs from fullness and there is a running that departs from lack. You can want to grow because life calls you to more, with liking, without ceasing to be whole on the way. Or you can run because you feel that, as you are, it is not enough, you are not enough, and only up ahead, the objective conquered, will you finally have the right to rest and to like yourself. The first is movement. The second is escape disguised as movement. From outside they look the same, two women working towards an objective. Inside, one is at home while she walks, the other has been homeless for years, waiting for an arrival that postpones the right to live.

There is a price to this veil that few see in time, and it has to do with people. When you live always in the next step, you are not truly present with the one who is in front of you now. The children grow while you wait to have more time for them. The friendships waste away while you postpone the meetings to a less busy phase. Love dries up for lack of presence, because presence is the only thing that feeds it, and you were always half absent, already in the future. Releasing this veil is, at bottom, returning yourself to the people and the moments you have, instead of the ones you wait to have. It is the difference between watching your own life through the window of what is missing, or finally entering the room where it is, always was, happening.

There is a wider vision opening when this veil loosens, and it deserves to be said slowly. Our rigid maps of arrival, the calculated goals, the grandiose endings we imagine, are all made in the image of a mind that needs a destination to feel safe. But life, when you look at it without that map, has no final point. It is not a system to conquer nor a goal to fulfil. It is a living field of possibilities, in movement, where each instant is already full in itself, like a river that discovers itself sea at every bend, without having to wait for the mouth. The words we give to what we seek, purpose, fulfilment, completeness, still carry the trace of that logic of arrival. And there is another truth, simpler, beneath them: what you seek is not at the end of the path, it is in the quality of your presence at each step. There is no there where life finally begins. There is this here, always open, always whole, waiting only for you to stop running long enough to see it.

When the horizon stops pulling you forwards, a field without margins opens where life does not demand arrival, only presence. It is in that field that a voice almost imperceptible begins to rise, and it is worth knowing of it, because it is your best guide for the rest of the journey. It is not the voice of the head, with its calculations and its fears of the future. It is an inner call, a silent thread that orients without effort, a whisper that returns you to what matters. To listen to it is not a task of conquest, it is a task of pause. It speaks when the noise quiets, when the body rests in its own rhythm, when you stop running for the next "when". It does not compete with your reason, it does not demand immediate clarity, it only vibrates like a serene spark at the bottom of the chest. And the more you listen to it, the less you need maps and goals to know which way to go, because you come to be guided from within, and not pushed from outside.

What changes when this veil lifts is that life comes to happen again in the present, the only time where it ever happens. You stop postponing happiness for a "when" that always recedes, and begin to find it in small things you used to cross at a run. You keep having projects and dreams, but you stop living them as conditions for having the right to breathe. You become more present with those you love, because you are no longer half absent, projected into the next step. And there is a deep rest in understanding that you are not late for anything, that there is no train leaving without you, that your life is not a rehearsal for a premiere that is always to come. The premiere is today. It always was.

*The "when" was how you kept walking through the days without meaning. Thank you. The waiting room was the journey.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, and notice which of them bothers you most. For what "when" are you postponing your life at this moment? If you knew there was no arrival at all, that there is no there where everything resolves, what would you change today? What are you losing, of those you love and of what you have, while you live always a step ahead? And what would it be to live as if you had already arrived, because, in truth, you were always here?

The practice is a return, and you can do it several times a day, in seconds. Stop what you are doing, in the middle of some task, and say, in silence, slowly: I am not on my way anywhere. I am already here. Then choose only one present and small thing, the light falling on a wall, the warmth of the cup in your hands, the air entering and leaving you without your having to ask for it. Linger on that minuscule thing for the length of three breaths, as if it were the first time you saw it and the last. It was for this that you were waiting, all this time, without knowing. And it was already here.

## Veil 7 · Duality

> *There remains the undivided centre, where you and life are not two.*

However surrounded you are, however full the house, there is always, in a corner of you, a glass. You on one side, the rest of the world on the other. Life as something that happens to you, coming from outside, against which you defend yourself, for which you fight, that you try to control so it does not hurt you. And at the bottom of everything, beneath the people and the days and the conversations, an old and deep solitude that no company fills completely, as if there were always missing something you cannot name and that you have already given up looking for.

It is a solitude that is not cured with people around, and that is why it is so disconcerting. There are women surrounded by love, with a full house and friends and family, who confess, in a low voice and with guilt, that they feel alone in the midst of it all. The guilt comes from thinking they have no right, with so much that is good. But the solitude of this veil is not lack of company. It is the sense of being always, even so, on the outside, separated by a thin glass that no one sees. There is no lack of people around. There is a lack of the experience of belonging, of not being an island, and that is not resolved by adding more people to the island. It is resolved in another way, deeper, which is the one this last veil opens for us.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By a background sense of being always, to some degree, on the outside, even in the midst of the people you love. By living the relationships with a discreet calculation, how much I gave, how much I received, who is in debt, always a little on guard. By feeling the world as something that happens to you from outside, against which you defend yourself or for which you fight, and rarely as something of which you are a part. By a difficulty in asking for help or in letting yourself be cared for, because depending seems dangerous. By moments of inexplicable peace, by the sea, in nature, with music, in which that separation loosens and you do not quite know why you feel better. And by a background longing, without object, for a belonging you cannot name. These signs are not a sentence. They are the seventh veil, the thinnest, announcing that it is about to lift, and that the home you seek was never far.

Where is this veil born? It is born earlier than all the others, in the first time when, as a baby, you felt separated from the one who sustained you, in the first absence, in the first fright of being alone in an enormous world. It is the original wound, the one that precedes language, and that is why it is the one we least know how to name. Each child crosses it in some way, and each culture feeds it in its own way, teaching us to see ourselves as isolated individuals, in competition, responsible alone for saving ourselves. So this veil is not only yours nor only your family's, it is almost the air everyone breathes. And it is also for this reason that lifting it is the deepest return of all: not to a childhood home, but to something prior to any home, to the belonging that existed before you felt a separate self. You do not have to build it. Only to remember it, beneath all the forgetting that life kept laying on top.

This is the thinnest veil and, at the same time, the oldest of all: the belief that you are separate. Separate from yourself, from others, from life, from the whole. It is so old that you do not even feel it as a belief, you feel it as an obvious fact, as the backdrop of all your experience. And yet it is from it that, ultimately, all fears are born, because only one who judges herself alone and apart truly fears, a small island in an immense and indifferent sea. Look now backwards, at the six veils we have already crossed together, and you will recognise it hidden in all of them. You clung to your identity because you felt alone in having to defend it. You stayed bound to your story because it was all that seemed yours. You drowned in the head, you filled the emptiness, you ran after the horizon, you made an effort to exhaustion to be loved, and all this, at bottom, were different ways of answering the same first wound, that of feeling yourself separate, alone, afraid. The other six veils were, all of them, children of this one.

Seeing this veil is the most subtle task of the book, because here there is not properly a wrong pattern to correct, there is an illusion to dissolve. And an illusion is not fought, one sees through it, and it undoes itself. It does not dissolve with effort, which would be more separation, one more self trying to conquer unity. It dissolves with simple attention. Notice: the air you breathe now was, moments ago, inside another person's lungs, and will be in a little while inside a tree. The body you are was entirely made of the body of others, fed by earth, sun and rain you did not fabricate. The very words with which you think, which you feel so much your own, were given to you, one by one, by people who came before. So at what point, exactly, do you end and the world begin? Look for that border with honesty. The more you look for it, the less you find it, because it never existed except as an idea.

Releasing this veil is not reaching unity, as if it were a rare and elevated state to be conquered at the end of many years of practice. It is to remember that it was never broken, that it was here the whole time, beneath the idea of separation as the sky is beneath the clouds. When this veil lifts, even if for an instant, there does not remain a bigger and more enlightened self, more special than the others. There remains the opposite of the self: there remains the undivided centre, where you and life are not two things that meet, but a single one that never came to divide. As the old African knowing says, and they say in a thousand ways all the traditions that drank deep, I am because we are. You were never apart. You were already, from your first cry, before you had a name or story or role, pure belonging. Awakening, at the end of everything, is not becoming someone special, enlightened above the others. It is exactly the opposite. It is the humble return to the common ground, to the life that breathes the same in you and in everything, and the serene discovery, almost banal from being so true, that never, at any moment, did you walk alone.

I want this last veil not to stay in the abstract, because it has very concrete consequences in the way you live. When you feel separate, the other is always, to some degree, a threat or a resource: someone from whom you have to defend yourself, or someone you have to conquer to fill your lack. The relationships become transactions, even the ones of love, and there is always an account to make, a fear of losing, a calculation of how much you gave and how much you received. When this veil loosens, even if for instants, the other stops being a threat or a resource and becomes, simply, another form of the same life that you also are. You do not have to defend yourself from yourself, and fear, which was the bottom of everything, loses the ground on which it rested. It is not that you become naive or without limits. It is that you stop living at war.

A woman described the moment in which this veil, for instants, lifted. She was in a garden, on an ordinary day, tired of the usual feeling, that of walking through life on the outside of a glass, alone even surrounded. She sat looking at a tree, expecting nothing. And, without warning, she felt herself part. She did not think "I am connected to everything", as a pretty idea. She felt it in the body: the air she breathed had been in the tree, the light that warmed her warmed also the grass, and for an instant there was not her and the world, there was only life, happening, and she was one of the places where it happened. It lasted little. The glass returned. But something stayed, a memory of belonging that no later solitude managed to deny completely. She had touched, for instants, the home beneath all the veils. And one who touches that home once, even if she forgets it, knows, from then on, that it exists, and that she can return.

Here, in the last veil, there is a truth that is not conquered, it is lived. Integration is not a place apart from the world, a peak of serenity far from common life. It is the way you learn to walk within life with open eyes. It is no longer only about dissolving illusions or crossing fear. The invitation, now, ripens into an art simpler and harder: to live within form without losing yourself in it. The spirit and the matter, which so many traditions set at war, stop being contrary forces and come to breathe as two faces of the same breath. And then each gesture of the day becomes a bridge. Working, cooking, resting, caring for those you love, none of this stays outside the awakening. The silence you found in the rare hours comes to inscribe itself in the detail of the ordinary, and you discover that the simple is already an altar. As Ubuntu says, being does not flower alone. To integrate is to understand, in the body and not only in the idea, that each gesture of yours links you to all the rest, that there is no life of yours separate from life, and that the home you sought seven veils long was, after all, the ground you always trod.

In the depth of the last veil, the inversion that gives meaning to the whole journey takes place. At the start it seemed the path was leading you far away: dismantling beliefs, crossing fears, opening horizons, releasing everything. It seemed a journey outwards, in search of something you lacked. But at this threshold the direction inverts, and you understand that you were not walking outwards at all. Everything you took away was only what covered what was already inside, whole, from always. The centre you reach is not a distant goal. It is the place from which you set out, and to which, even without knowing, you always returned, even when the mind swore it was walking in a straight line far from itself. To reach the last veil is not, after all, to reach an end. It is to recognise an old call, and to answer it with the simplest thing of all: to come home, where you never truly ceased to be.

What changes when this veil lifts is the backdrop of your whole life. Fear, which was the background tone of everything, loses the ground, because fear is born of separation and you stop feeling yourself a surrounded island. The relationships stop being transactions with accounts to settle and become encounters between forms of the same life. You feel yourself part, and that belonging does not depend on having many people around, it depends on an inner discovery that no one can take from you. Death, even death, becomes less terrifying, because it stops being the absolute end of an isolated self and comes to fit into a larger image, that of the wave returning to the sea from which it never truly separated. And, in daily life, you are left with a background serenity, a gentle confidence that, whatever happens, you belong, and that belonging is your original state, not a conquest.

*Making yourself an island was the first way to survive an enormous world. Thank you. You were never, in fact, separate.*

Before the practice, stay with these questions, letting them rest more than answering them. In what moments of your life did you feel, even if for instants, that you were not separate from all the rest? Where do you end and the world begin, if you really look for that border? From what would you have to defend yourself if you knew, in the body and not only in the idea, that you belong? And if the peace you seek were not something to reach up ahead, but something to remember, that is already yours from always?

The practice is the simplest and perhaps the deepest. Out in the open, if you can, choose a single living being and linger on it: a tree, a bird, the grass between the stones, a person in the distance you do not know. Instead of thinking, as always, "me, and that thing there, in front of me", let another thing rise slowly, without forcing it: we are of the same. The same air, the same earth, the same single life crossing different forms for a little while. Stay three breaths within that belonging, without needing to believe in anything, only feeling. It is not an idea to collect. It is a home, your home from always, to which you can return when the glass of separation becomes thick again and the old cold knocks at the door.

## When the veils cross

I presented the seven veils to you one by one, so you could see them with clarity. But in real life they rarely appear alone. They intertwine, they hold hands, they reinforce one another, and that is why sometimes you feel caught in a web and not in a single thread. It is worth seeing some of these crossings, because recognising them spares you much suffering and much confusion.

Notice how effort and desolation usually go hand in hand. You make an effort to exhaustion, you fill life with doing and with caring, and one of the reasons, the most hidden, is not having to stop and face the emptiness. Effort is, often, the most socially applauded escape from desolation. So, when you finally stop, the anguish you were avoiding rises all at once, and you run to find more to do. Do you see the cycle? One veil feeding the other.

Or notice the horizon and permanence. You live always projected into a "when will I finally be happy", and at the same time clinging to an identity that no longer serves you, and the two together rob you of the present completely: one, throwing you into the future; the other, binding you to what you already were. Or the whirlwind and memory, in which the head that does not stop is almost always brooding over the past or rehearsing a future in the image of that past, rarely in the now.

Notice that these crossings are not accidents. They are, at bottom, the three movements of a single passage. There is a time of seeing, in which the head that does not stop and the story that imprisons grow quiet when you begin to watch them from the shore. There is a time of coming back, of return, in which you stop making an effort and filling the emptiness and allow yourself, at last, to be held. There is also a time of living, of integration, in which you step out of the waiting and the armour of the roles and enter your own life. See, return, live: first one sees, then one returns, finally one embodies. It is not a rigid order, it is a rhythm, and you enter wherever your life opens the door. For whoever wants to walk each movement in depth, there is a path devoted to each one, but the whole map is this book, and it is enough for you.

There is still the veil that lives beneath all of them, duality, the sense of separation. I have already told you, and I repeat it here because it is the key: it was separation that made you cling to identity, bind yourself to the story, fill the emptiness, run for the horizon, make an effort to be loved. When you work any one of the other six with enough depth, sooner or later you reach this one, because it is the common root. And when this one loosens, all the others lose strength at the same time, like leaves of a branch that finally lets go.

Let me show you the web at work in a single situation, one of those life brings. Imagine the end of a relationship. At the same time, and all at once, the seven can light up. Permanence cries out, "who am I now, if I am no longer his woman". Memory assembles the story, "it is always like this with me, I always end up alone". The whirlwind does not let you sleep, brooding over what you said and what you should have said. Effort whispers that, if you had done more, given more, pleased more, perhaps he would have stayed. Desolation terrifies you with the emptiness of the suddenly silent house. The horizon promises that you will only be happy when another person appears. And, beneath everything, duality, the background solitude, the glass, the sense of being alone against life. No wonder a rupture brings you so low: it is not one veil that catches you, it is seven at the same time, in chorus. And when you do not know this, you feel only an enormous and shapeless pain, and you conclude that there is something very wrong with you. When you know it, you can begin to separate the threads, to see one at a time, and the pain, without ceasing to hurt, stops being a nameless monster and becomes something you can look at and, little by little, release.

What I want you to take from here is simple and liberating: you do not have to resolve everything at the same time, nor to know exactly which veil is which. Pull the thread that calls you, with patience, and you will feel the others loosen on their own. The web does not undo itself knot by knot through force. It undoes itself when you stop clenching it.

## Living the method, day to day

The seven veils we crossed are not to read and put away. They are to live, and life does not happen in retreats nor on pages, it happens in the kitchen, in traffic, in the middle of an argument, in bed at three in the morning when fear grips. It is there that the method proves itself, and it is there that it is simpler than it seems, because it reduces, at bottom, to two gestures you can make anywhere and at any hour: to see, and to release.

Imagine an ordinary day. You wake already in debt, with the list running in your head before you put your feet on the floor. Instead of embarking on the automatic race, you do a small thing: you notice. Ah, here is the effort, the hurry to prove I deserve the day. You do not censure yourself for it, you do not force yourself to change, you only see. That seeing, on its own, already opens a millimetre of space between you and the pattern, and it is in that millimetre that all the freedom you keep gaining lives. Later, someone does not answer a message, and you feel the old story assembling itself: they are drifting away, it is always like this. And you notice again. Ah, here is memory rewriting the present with the ink of the past. You see, and the story loses strength, because it stopped acting in hiding.

It is like this, and not in some grander way, that a life transforms. Not in a day of revelation, but in thousands of small instants in which you choose to see instead of react, and to release instead of hold. At first you have to remember, and you forget almost always, and only realise hours later, or the next day. That is fine, that is the path, not a detour from it. With time, the seeing comes earlier, sometimes still in the middle of the gesture, and one day, without warning, it comes before, and you see the pattern approaching and can, for the first time, choose something else. It was for that instant of choice that this whole book worked.

There are days you will manage, and there are days you will fall into all seven veils before lunch. The bad days do not erase the road walked, just as a storm does not erase a whole spring. On those days, the method itself has one last fold, the most important: to see, and release, your own demand to do it well. Because the haste to awaken fast, to be already the serene and free woman you imagine, is only the veil of effort and the veil of the horizon joining to rob you of the present again. There is no advanced student in this school. There are only women walking, each at her own pace, seeing and releasing what they can in the day they have.

There is a difference worth keeping, between this and everything you have perhaps already tried. The quick solutions promise to take away your pain fast, and so they ask of you not to look at it, to cover it with positive thinking, with occupation, with one more goal. They work for a few days, and then the pain returns, because it was never seen, only silenced. The method is the opposite, and so it is slower and more solid. It does not take away your pain by force, it teaches you to look at it until it stops governing you. It does not give you a destination without suffering, it gives you a new relationship with whatever suffering there is, in which it crosses you without defining you. From outside, it may seem you are going slowly, while others promise lightning transformations. Inside, you are building the only kind of change that lasts, the one that rests on seeing and not on escape. Trust that slow rhythm, especially on the days when the old haste tells you that you should already be cured. That haste is, itself, one more veil. See it. And release it too.

It is worth telling you what happens over time, because the change here does not have the dramatic form you perhaps expect. You will not wake one day transformed, with all the veils on the ground and a permanent serenity. What you will notice, after months, is subtler and more solid than that. You will notice that a situation that before would have brought you low for a week now only catches you for a day. That you reacted with calm where before you would have exploded, and you did not even notice it was different until later. That you said no without the whole speech of guilt. That you stayed in silence without fleeing. They are small victories, almost invisible, and it is precisely because they are small and daily that they are real. The true transformation is not a lightning bolt, it is a tide that rises so slowly that you only notice it when you see that the whole landscape has changed. Trust that tide, even on the days when the sea seems still.

If there is a single thing you take with you when you close this book, let it be this, said with all care: be on your side. Your whole life you learned to be hard on yourself, to demand, to correct, to point out what is missing. The method only works if it is practised with tenderness, because harshness is, itself, one more veil, perhaps the one that dresses all the others. Treat the woman you are as you would treat a much-loved friend who was learning, slowly, to free herself from everything she was taught she had to be. With her, you would have patience. With her, you would not give up. Be that friend to yourself.

## Doubts along the way

Over the years, some questions repeat themselves always, and they deserve an honest answer. Perhaps they are yours too.

I do the practice and feel nothing? Wonderful. The practice is not to feel anything special, it is to train the looking. Often there are no fireworks, there is only a millimetre of space opening between you and the pattern, and that millimetre is not felt, it is understood later, when you notice that you reacted in another way. Do not pursue experiences. Do the gesture, simple, and let the rest work in the dark.

I begin to see and I feel worse? It can happen, and it is not a bad sign, it is a sign of depth. When a layer lifts, what was beneath comes to the surface, and sometimes what was beneath was sleeping pain. Go slowly. Do not dig everything at once. If the pain is very great, do not cross it alone, and this brings me to the next question.

Does this replace therapy or professional support? No, and I do not want you to think it does. This book is a path of awareness, not a treatment. There are wounds, traumas and sufferings that ask for professional hands, and seeking that help is not weakness, it is wisdom, and it is, itself, a gesture of seeing and of caring. Use this book as a companion, not as the only crutch. If you feel you need more than it can give you, listen to that sign.

How long does it take? The question is, itself, the veil of the horizon speaking. There is no end to reach, there is a way of living to practise. Some veils loosen fast, others take years, others return. You are not late for anything. The right time is yours.

Do I have to believe in something spiritual for this to work? You do not have to believe in anything. I do not ask you for faith, I ask you for attention. Seeing and releasing work whether you call it presence, awareness, soul or simply the part of you that observes. Stay with the language that is true for you and ignore the rest.

Is this only for women? I wrote thinking of women, in their voice and in mine, because it is what I know from inside and because they are the ones who sat with me in the same turns. But the veils have no gender. Anyone who recognises themselves in them can cross them. If a man finds a mirror here, he is welcome to walk too.

I feel I am doing everything wrong, that I do not understand, that I am not capable? Then you are doing well, because you just caught a veil in the act, that of demand, that of having to be a good student even at freeing yourself. There is no doing this wrong. There is no grade. You read a page, you notice something of yours, you breathe. That is already the whole path. The rest is the old harshness wanting to come in through the back door.

I cannot stop repeating? You will repeat, I assure you, and that is not failing. The difference is that, now, you will repeat seeing. And seeing, earlier and earlier, is the whole path. Be patient and be on your side. You are undoing with tenderness what took generations to weave.

## The call of the centre

There is a curious thing that happens when one crosses these veils, and I want to leave it with you before the end, because it changes the way you will understand this whole journey. At the start, it seems the path takes you far away: dismantling beliefs, crossing fears, letting go of stories, opening horizons. It seems a journey outwards, in search of something you do not yet have. But at a certain point the direction inverts, and you understand that you were not walking outwards at all. You were walking inwards, and everything you took away was only what covered what was already there. The centre you reach is not a distant goal you conquered. It is the place from which you always set out, and to which, even without knowing, you always returned.

That centre makes no noise. It does not announce itself with spectacular revelations nor with extraordinary states, and that is why so often it passes unnoticed by those who seek the grandiose. It is recognised more as a rest than as a discovery, more as a return home than as an arrival at a new place. It is what in you needed none of the seven veils to exist, and which was there, whole and quiet, beneath all of them, waiting, with the patience of the things that are in no hurry because they have no end.

From that centre there begins to rise, with time, a voice. Not the voice of the head, with its fears and calculations, but another, deeper and more serene, that is recognised before it is even understood. Some call it intuition, some call it the inner call, and the names matter little. It is the silent thread that orients you without effort, that knows what is yours and what is not, that points you the direction when reason loses itself in turns. It does not compete with the mind nor shout louder. It speaks low, and you only hear it when the noise quiets, when the body rests, when you stop enough that the silence may have voice. This whole book was, at bottom, a way of lowering the noise enough for you to hear it again.

To learn to listen to it is the art of a whole life, and it is not taught on pages. But it always begins in the same way: by quieting. Each veil you lift lowers the noise a little more. Each practice of this book is, seen this way, a way of making silence so that the centre can speak. And the more you listen to it, the more you trust, and the more you trust, the less you need to defend the identity, to bind yourself to the story, to drown the head, to make an effort, to fill the emptiness, to run for the horizon. Because you stop feeling yourself an orphan trying to get by alone in a hostile world, and come to feel yourself guided by something that is, at the same time, the most intimate of you and larger than you.

It is this that is on the other side of the seven veils. Not a perfect woman, serene forever, free of all pain. A whole woman, at home within herself, able to hear her own centre in the midst of the noise of life, and to return to it whenever she loses herself. It is not a destination one reaches. It is a relationship one cultivates, with yourself, for the rest of your days.

## To close

You removed some veils in reading this book, and perhaps you wept on some page without quite knowing why, because there are recognitions that hurt before they relieve. Other veils will settle again tomorrow, with tiredness, with a fright, with a bad day, and that is not failing, not retreating, not proof that you learned nothing. Awakening is not a crown one conquers at once and displays forever. It is common ground. It is an unravelling that keeps happening as long as there is life and as long as there is love, and that is never properly finished. You will recognise yourself repeating again, because the old patterns do not vanish as if by magic, they only lose, little by little, the strength as they stop catching you in the dark.

When that happens, and it will, remember that you already know what to do, and that you never need, ever again, to punish yourself for it. Do not run to the solution. Do not demand of yourself to change by force, which is only one more veil, that of effort, dressing itself as personal growth. Do only what you learned here, slowly, one thing at a time, with your hand on your chest if it helps: see, and release. See first, without judging yourself, until the pattern stops being fog and gains a contour. And then release, without violence, opening the hand. Veil by veil, at your rhythm, and not an instant before you are ready.

Trust this, even on the days when it is hard to believe: beneath everything that still releases in you, beneath each veil you have yet to lift, there is something that was never bound. Something whole, serene, that needs not be conquered nor deserved, and that waited for you all this time, with the patience of the things that are in no hurry because they have no end. We do not walk alone. And there is, at the centre of each of us, a place so quiet and so full that, on arriving there, you understand at last the simplest and hardest thing of all: in truth, we never came to walk. We were always, from the start, exactly where we needed to be.

# Appendix · The notebook of the seven veils

### A crossing of seven weeks

Reading is the first gesture. Living is what comes after, and it is there that everything is decided. This notebook is your companion to carry the book from understanding into the body, over seven weeks, one for each veil. It is not a rigid programme nor a test. It is an invitation to slow down and to inhabit each veil long enough to know it from within, and not only from ideas.

How to use it: dedicate a week to each veil, in whatever order you want, beginning with the one that most calls you today. You do not need more than a few minutes a day. If a veil asks for more time, stay in it two weeks, three, however long. There is no possible delay on this journey, because the destination is exactly where you already are. If you can, keep at hand a notebook only yours, not to find answers, but to see yourself think. And remember, each week, the golden rule: first see, without judging yourself. Releasing comes after, and comes on its own.

Each week has four times. **To see**, some questions to open the looking. **The practice**, the central gesture of the week. **A gesture for the days**, a micro-action to carry with you. And **at the end of the week**, a reflection to gather what moved.

---

## Week 1 · Permanence

The focus of this week is to notice who you think you have to be. Walk through the week with a loose question in your pocket: where am I defending an image of myself?

**To see.** In what situations this week did you feel the need to defend who you are, to prove, to correct the image others have of you? What role would it cost you most to let go of, and what do you fear would happen if you let it go? Was there any moment in which you felt yourself, without having to be anything for anyone? Do not answer from the head. Notice over the days and let the answers arrive. And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Once a day, in a moment of calm, say your name in a low voice, three times, and on the third notice who hears it, that one who is behind the name and needs no defence. Stay three breaths with him. And, mid-week, write three phrases that begin with "I am", and beside each one ask what would happen if it stopped being true. The one that tightens your chest is your most defended veil.

**A gesture for the days.** Choose a small occasion to change your mind out loud, or to say "I do not know", without justifying yourself. Notice that you do not collapse. That you stay whole even without being right, even without knowing.

**At the end of the week.** What did you discover about who you are beneath the roles? Was there any moment in which you felt that you remained, even when the form changed? Note it, not as a conclusion, but as a seed.

---

## Week 2 · Memory

The focus of this week is the story you tell about yourself. Walk attentive to the phrases you repeat, especially those that begin with "I always" and "I never".

**To see.** What story about yourself did you repeat this week without questioning it? When you reacted with more force than the situation asked for, to what other time, to what other people, did that pain, in fact, belong? Whose is the voice you hear when you diminish or limit yourself? Listen with attention, because often it is not yours. And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Choose an old phrase you say about yourself, write it, and read it aloud. Ask: whose voice is this? When you recognise to whom it belongs, in silence, return it to them, with respect and without rancour. Repeat this with a different phrase mid-week.

**A gesture for the days.** Once a day, when you catch yourself telling the old story, add to it softly four words: "until now, it was so". The "until now" opens the door that the "always" had closed.

**At the end of the week.** What part of your story did you understand to be interpretation, and not fact? And what part is perhaps not even yours, but inheritance? Write what lightened.

---

## Week 3 · The Whirlwind

The focus of this week is your relationship with the thoughts and the emotions. Not to silence them, to see them pass.

**To see.** In what moments did your head accelerate this week, and what was it trying to protect you from feeling? Did you notice the difference between having a thought and being dragged by it? Did you manage, at any time, to find the one in you who observes the whirlwind without being the whirlwind? And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Several times a day, especially when the mind fires off, say inside: "I am noticing that I am thinking this". And breathe three times from that one who notices. It is a short return to the shore, repeated until the body learns the way.

**A gesture for the days.** Choose one simple task a day, washing the dishes, walking, drinking the tea, and do only that, without phone, without planning the rest. When the head flees, bring it back to the gesture, with sweetness, without scolding yourself.

**At the end of the week.** Did you find, even if for instants, the silence beneath the noise? How was it to be there? Note the sensation, not the explanation.

---

## Week 4 · Effort

The focus of this week is how much you make an effort to be loved, and the fear that lives beneath the rest.

**To see.** Where did you catch yourself doing too much this week, anticipating, saving, pleasing? What did you feel when someone offered you help or rest? Did you manage to receive, or did you stay uncomfortable? And the guilt, when you stopped, where did it come from? And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Once a day, choose a small thing you would do by habit to serve or get ahead, and do not do it. Stay with the discomfort that rises, your hand on your chest, three breaths, without resolving it. It is the veil being lifted.

**A gesture for the days.** Receive one thing a day without repaying it immediately, a compliment, a favour, a care, and train saying only "thank you", without giving it back, without minimising it. Let yourself be cared for.

**At the end of the week.** What happened when you stopped doing? Were you less loved, or only less busy? Write what you discovered about your worth when you were not proving it.

---

## Week 5 · Desolation

The focus of this week is your relationship with silence and emptiness. Not to fill them, to inhabit them.

**To see.** From what did you flee this week when the house became silent? What noises, screens or tasks did you use so as not to feel the emptiness? And when, by chance, you stayed alone with nothing to do, what rose? And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Once a day, stay two minutes in the silence, without filling it. Without phone, without music, without task. Your hand on your chest, and inside: "this is not danger, it is space". Let the emptiness be earth, not abyss. If emotion rises, let it come. That was all it asked for.

**A gesture for the days.** Do one thing alone out of liking, and not out of lack, this week. A coffee, a walk, sitting to look at the sky. Notice the difference between being alone and being with yourself.

**At the end of the week.** Did the emptiness swallow you, or reveal something to you? What question of yours, long silent, perhaps began to make itself heard in the silence? Write it.

---

## Week 6 · The Horizon

The focus of this week is to notice how much you live waiting for an "after", and to bring life back to the present.

**To see.** For what "when" are you postponing your life at this moment? How many times this week did you say, inside or out loud, "when this passes"? Did you reach something you wanted and feel, instead of peace, the eagerness for the next step? And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Several times a day, stop and say: "I am not on my way anywhere, I am already here". Then linger on a small and present thing, the light, the warmth of the cup, the breath, three breaths. It was for this that you were waiting.

**A gesture for the days.** Choose, each day, a small pleasure you usually postpone for when you have time, and give it to yourself now, without deserving it first. Life is made of these nows.

**At the end of the week.** What changed when you brought the happiness from "after" to today? What did you discover you were losing, of those you love and of what you have, by living always a step ahead? Note it.

---

## Week 7 · Duality

The focus of this last week is belonging. To notice where you feel separate, and to remember that you are not an island.

**To see.** Where did you feel yourself on the outside this week, separated by a glass, even in the midst of people? Did you notice yourself living the relationships as accounts to settle, who gave more, who is in debt? And were there moments of inexplicable peace, in nature, in music, in affection, in which that separation loosened? And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** Once a day, out in the open if you can, choose a living being, a tree, a bird, a person in the distance, and instead of "me and that" let rise "we are of the same". The same air, the same life in different forms. Stay three breaths in that belonging.

**A gesture for the days.** This week, let yourself depend on someone in a small thing, ask for help, accept a lap, share a weight. Train belonging, instead of being self-sufficient out of fear.

**At the end of the week.** In what moments did you feel that you were not alone, that you were part? What changes in your fear when you remember that you belong? Write, to recall later.

---

## Week 8 · The whole crossing

You crossed the seven. This last week has no veil of its own, because it is the week to let them converse among themselves and to practise the method as a whole, in the midst of real life, with no set theme.

**To see.** Look at the week that passed. Which of the seven veils appeared most? Did you notice any of them calling another, effort covering the emptiness, the whirlwind brooding over the past, the horizon fleeing the present? And were there moments, even small, in which you saw a veil in time and chose something else? Notice them, because they are your progress, however discreet. And, every week, the same two questions: what survival was this? To whom was this strategy loyal, to me, or to someone who came before?

**The practice of the week.** With no fixed theme. Only, over the day, two pocket questions, whenever something grips you: what is it that I am seeing here? And: can I release a little of this, open just one finger of the hand? You do not need to know the name of the veil. It is enough to see, and to release what you can.

**A gesture for the days.** At the end of each day of this week, instead of evaluating what you did, ask only: today, at some moment, was I awake? Did I see myself? That is the only progress that counts.

**At the end of the week.** Write a short letter to yourself, to read in a few months. Tell her what you learned in these weeks, and what you want to remember on the days when you forget everything. Keep it. You will need it, and it will be there.

### On closing the notebook

Seven weeks do not remove seven veils forever, and that was not the promise. What they do is teach the body a new way, that of seeing and releasing, so that it is there, available, the next time a veil settles. And there will be a next time, many, because this is a spiral and not a line. When you return to an old pattern, return also to this notebook, to the week you need, without shame and without haste. The books that matter accompany us all our lives, and tell us new things at each age of the soul. May this be one of those for you.

See. And release. Veil by veil.

# The words of this book

Some words appear many times in these pages, and it is worth keeping them clear, because they are the tools of the method.

**Veil.** An old conditioning, learned or inherited, that was placed between you and what you are, so early that it came to seem your own face. It is not a flaw nor a fault. It was protection that became prison. There are seven, and crossing them is the path of this book.

**To see.** The first movement of the method. To recognise the pattern without judging yourself, to give it a name and a contour, to understand where it is born. It is not to analyse to exhaustion, it is a quiet noticing, almost without words. What stops being invisible stops being destiny.

**To release.** The second movement. To let go of what you saw, not by force, but by stopping holding it and feeding it with your attention and your fear. Begin by thanking: a strategy is not let go of by rejecting it, the protection it was is honoured, and only then does the hand open. Releasing is gratitude, not rupture. Releasing comes on its own when the seeing is ripe. There is no releasing without seeing.

**Pattern.** The repeated form of feeling, reacting or loving that the veil makes you live on automatic. What makes you repeat, even when you already know.

**Shadow.** What you hid from yourself, and which precisely because you do not see it governs you more. The veil lives in the shadow. To see is to bring light to that zone.

**Inheritance.** What you received without choosing, from those who came before you. Loyalties, fears, renunciations and gestures that cross generations. Much of what you carry is not yours, it is faithfulness. Seeing this allows honouring without repeating.

**Presence.** What in you remains when all the rest changes. The one who observes the thoughts without being the thoughts, who attends the roles without being the roles. It has no name, it cannot be lost, it needs no defence. It is what the veils cover and to which you can always return.

**Spiral.** The form of the path. Not a staircase one climbs to a summit, but a turn one repeats at different heights. You pass several times by the same veil, and each time thinner. That is why there is no relapse that is failure, nor any possible delay.

Belonging, in the end, is the word that lies beneath all of them. To remember it is the seventh and last veil lifting. You were already, from always, belonging.
