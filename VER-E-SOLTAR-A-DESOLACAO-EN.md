# See and Release the Desolation
### the fear of emptiness
*Method VS · See and Release · Vivianne dos Santos*

> *Emptiness is not absence. It is the silent, dark field where the soul germinates, far from view, in its own good time.*

---

## Who this book is for

This book is for you if, the moment you walk into the house, you rush to turn on the television, not because you want to watch anything, but because the silence that would remain without it tightens your chest in a way you cannot name. It is for you if you pick up your phone for no reason at all, dozens of times a day, looking for something, anything, that will cover over a depth you would rather not touch. It is for you if you fill your diary until there is not a single minute left that is only yours, and you call that having a full life, when underneath that full life there is a restlessness that never passes, a hurry to be always doing, an almost physical discomfort whenever, for one rare instant, there is nothing left to solve.

It is for you if you stay in relationships that no longer do you any good, in commitments that have become a weight, in conversations that say nothing to you anymore, only so as not to face the emptiness that would come once you let them go. It is for you if you confuse being alone with having failed, as though solitude were public proof that something in you was no good. And it is, above all, for you if, when someone asks what you, only you, truly love, you go blank, because it has been so long since you stopped to listen to yourself that you no longer even know the answer.

This is not a self-help book with easy promises, nor a ten-step manual for never feeling alone again. It is slower and deeper than that, and for that very reason more truthful. It will not ask you to fill yourself better, to find more distractions, to plug the emptiness with nobler things. It will ask you the opposite: that you learn, slowly and without violence, to stay with the silence you so avoid, and to discover, on the other side of the fear, that what you took for an abyss was in fact fertile ground, and that the company you had been fleeing for years was your own.

This is one of the seven veils I write about in the parent book, *The Seven Veils*. It is called the Desolation, and it is the fear of emptiness. You do not need to have read the other book to use this one. Here I take you to the bottom of this one veil, with a method you can apply, one that fits into your real life. Read slowly. Underline. And, above all, carry it into your silences, because it is there, and not on these pages, that the unravelling happens. I am with you, at the same pace.

Let me tell you straight away, before anything else, what makes the difference between this veil and almost everything you will be told about solitude. I am not going to teach you to be less alone. I am not going to give you techniques for filling your hours better, nor advice for finding more people to surround yourself with. The path of this veil is, in a sense, the opposite of all that. It is learning to stay with what you have been fleeing your whole life, and discovering, with surprise, that it was not an enemy. The world out there wears itself out teaching you to flee faster and with more style. I am going to ask you to stop fleeing, slowly, one silence at a time, and to see what is in fact on the other side of the door you never opened.

I do not write to you from a place of arrival. I write from a path still under way, my own. I too fled the silence, for years, without ever calling it a flight. I too filled my life with noise and people and tasks, convinced I was merely a busy woman, a woman with a full life. I still study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as one who collects certainties, but as one who needs names for what she feels. Everything I share here passed first through me. I recognised it in myself, sat with it, and only then went on finding it, repeated, in what echoes in so many women going round the same circles, convinced they are the only ones. So I do not invite you to follow me. I invite you to walk together, at the same pace, towards a silence that is, after all, yours, and that you may only have forgotten.

A note on how to read this, because the way you read is part of it too. Do not read in a hurry, in one sitting, to reach the end. This is not a book to finish, it is a book to inhabit, and it would be ironic to rush through it when what it asks of you is precisely that you learn to stop. Read slowly. Stop when a sentence stirs you, and stay with it a moment instead of pressing straight on. If you can, keep a notebook of your own beside you, and write in it whatever each part stirs, not to solve it, to see it. And when you reach the practices and the seven-day programme, do not only read them. Do them. It is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here, and the body only learns by doing, slowly, with repetition and with time.

A word before we begin, because it is the condition without which none of this works: tenderness towards yourself. One does not learn to inhabit silence by force of demand, nor does one calm an old fear with harshness. There is a child in you who learned, very early, that emptiness was danger. A frightened child is not soothed with shouting, she is soothed with a lap to sit on. So, throughout these pages, whenever you catch yourself judging yourself for fleeing the silence, swap shame for curiosity, and harshness for tenderness. Treat yourself as you would treat your best friend learning this. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself.

## The veil of desolation

There is a place inside you that you avoid at all costs, and you do not always know that you are avoiding it. It does not show its face openly, it does not announce itself. You notice it, rather, between the lines of your gestures. In the hurry to turn on the television the moment you walk in, before you have even put down your keys. In the phone you pick up for no reason, with nothing to look at, only out of the need to keep your eyes occupied. In the task you invent when, for one rare instant, no one needs you and there is nothing to solve. The moment silence settles, a dull, formless dread rises, and you rush to fill it before you have even felt it whole, like someone fleeing a dark room without waiting to see whether there is, in fact, anything there.

And perhaps you have never stopped to notice that it is always the same gesture, repeated in a thousand different forms. The background noise you never switch off. The people you call when you find yourself alone, even the ones who tire you. The extra work you say you hate and on which, deep down, you depend so as not to stop. The relationship that will not end, though it long ago ceased to nourish you, because the emptiness after it frightens you more than its own lifelessness. They are all the same flight. For years, you have been fleeing a place inside you, and you have built an entire life so full that you never, not for one minute, have to visit it.

Notice how cunning this veil is. Unlike others, which show themselves in obvious suffering, this one disguises itself as something good. A full life, a packed diary, a woman in demand, someone always available to others, someone who never stops. From the outside, it looks like dedication, energy, generosity. No one tells a woman like that that she might be fleeing, because her flight has the exact shape of what the world applauds. And she herself does not see it, because she is too busy to notice what, after all, she is keeping herself busy with. It takes an imposed pause, an illness, a weekend alone, a child leaving home, for the noise to drop enough that she hears, for the first time in years, the silence she had been covering over. And it is in that unexpected silence that so many women find themselves, with a start, face to face with this veil.

I think of a woman, and she could be so many. She realised this on a weekend when, by chance, she found herself alone at home, with no plans and no one to look after. Instead of the rest she had said for months she longed for, she felt a growing discomfort, almost panic, and caught herself calling friends, inventing outings, filling the hours with anything at all, whatever it was. On the Sunday evening, exhausted from so much fleeing, she finally sat down on the sofa with the silence. The first few minutes were almost unbearable. Then, slowly, the dread began to settle, like dust coming to rest, and underneath it appeared not the abyss she feared, but a kind of forgotten peace, and a few gentle tears, and the simple question she had been running from for years: and me, what is it that I need? The emptiness had not swallowed her. It had given her back her voice.

Notice what changed in that woman's life, because it is the key to everything. She did nothing. She solved nothing, she made no great decision, she did not force herself to change. She simply did not flee, once, for long enough. She stayed where she had always run from. And what she had taken for an abyss, for that terrifying hollow that made her call everyone on a Sunday afternoon, revealed itself, when she finally stayed in it, to be something else entirely. Not her end. The beginning of a conversation with herself, postponed for decades. The emptiness was not the problem. The problem was the whole life she had built so as never to have to visit it.

This is the fifth veil, the Desolation. The belief, ancient and rarely seen, that emptiness is a threat, and that to be alone is to be in danger. It is no flaw of yours, no weakness, no lack of character. It is an old, intelligent strategy that once protected you from a pain you could not have borne, and that goes on dressing you long after the threat has passed. Take the guilt off first, because without that there is no seeing that is not judgement. The child who learned to flee the silence did so because the silence, in her time, really did hurt. The woman who goes on fleeing is not weak, she is loyal to that child, and to the body that protected her. There is nothing here to correct in a hurry. There is, first, something to see.

### How do you recognise this veil active in you?

By the difficulty of being at home alone, in silence, without television, music or phone covering the depth. It is not that you dislike silence in theory. It is that, in practice, the moment it settles, something in you stirs and needs to break it.

By the tendency to fill your diary until no time at all is left that is only yours, and to call that having a full life. There is a difference between a full life and a crammed one, and you, deep down, know which of the two is yours.

By staying in relationships or commitments that no longer do you any good, only so as not to face the emptiness that would come once you let them go. You prefer the company that empties you to the silence you fear.

By a restlessness that rises whenever there is nothing left to solve, as though peace itself were threatening. At rest, instead of rest, you feel a kind of dull alarm, and your hand reaches out on its own for the phone.

By confusing solitude with failure, as though being alone were public proof that something in you fell short, that you were not loveable enough to always have someone at your side.

And by rarely knowing how to answer, when you are asked, what it is that you, only you, truly love. Not because you have no loves, but because it has been so long since you stopped to listen to yourself that you have lost contact with your own voice.

Recognising yourself in these signs does not condemn you to isolation, nor is it a sentence on who you are. It is only the veil becoming visible. And a veil that is seen no longer catches you in the same way, because it has stopped acting in the dark. On the contrary, recognising this opens you to the possibility of a company you may never truly have had: your own.

## The root

Where is this veil born? Almost always in an early solitude that hurt too much. A child left alone too long, emotionally or physically, without enough holding, without anyone who came when she called, learns very early a lesson that stays in her body for life: silence is abandonment, and to be alone is to be in danger of not being held. It was not an idea she had. It was a truth her body recorded before there were words to tell it. And the body does not forget what once frightened it. So, decades later, when silence settles over a room, that body, now a grown woman's, reacts as it reacted the first time, and rushes to fill the silence before reason has even arrived.

Other times, and this surprises those who hear it, this veil is born of the opposite. In a house so full, so noisy, so demanding, that there was never any room for the child to hear herself. A house where people always spoke loudly, where there were always others around, where the television was never switched off, where stillness was treated as laziness or as failing. That child grew up never having learned to be with herself, because she was never given a safe silence in which she could do so. And today, a woman, she is terrified of emptiness not because it ever hurt her, but because she never inhabited it, and she fears what she has never known. Some flee silence because they were once left alone in it. And some flee it because they have never, not even once, been at peace there.

It is worth noticing which of these two stories looks more like yours, because the way out has different shades for each. If your fear is born of an early solitude that hurt, then your path is to discover, slowly and in safety, that today's silence is not the one of back then, that you are no longer that unsupported child, that now there is a lap to sit on, and that this lap, surprisingly, you can give to yourself. If your fear is born of a full house where you never heard yourself, then your path is one more of discovery than of healing: you never knew the good silence, and you are going to know it now, for the first time, like someone tasting a new food with caution and then being amazed to like it. In one case, you return to a silence in order to heal it. In the other, you enter a silence in order to have it for the first time. In both, the gesture is the same: to stay.

And there is something you need to know about your body, because it takes the guilt off you. Your nervous system does not tell past from present. It tells one thing only: safe from threat. When silence settles today and touches that old wound, it does not ask what year it is, nor notice that you are an adult now and the house is safe. It reacts as it reacted the first time, and sets off the alarm, and orders you to fill, to flee, to switch on, to call. That is why your flight from emptiness almost never presents itself as an error. It presents itself as the most sensible thing to do in that instant, as instinct, as need. Only afterwards, when the dust settles, do you perhaps think that once again you fled an afternoon that could have been yours. It is not lack of will. It is a body protecting you from a danger that no longer exists. And it is that body, not your willpower, that the protocol addresses.

And there are inheritances here too, because much of what we carry was not born in us. This is where systemic family constellation opened my eyes in a way I did not expect. There are whole families who fled silence, who had to be forever doing, who treated stillness as a threat or as wasted time, and who passed on that horror of emptiness without ever naming it, from mother to daughter, from grandmother to granddaughter, like someone handing down an inheritance no one chose to receive. Perhaps your restlessness is not only yours. Perhaps it is the echo of a woman who came before you, who could never stop, because stopping, in her time, was a dangerous luxury or a door to pains it was better not to open. When you listen to your hurry with true attention, sometimes it has another person's voice.

There is a third way this veil comes in, and it is the most treacherous, because it comes disguised as virtue. The child who was given attention only when she was doing something, when she was helping, when she was useful, learns that to exist at rest is not to exist at all. That only she who is producing, caring, solving, deserves to be in the world. That child grows into a woman who does not know how to be without doing, not out of horror of silence itself, but because rest brings her an old guilt, as though to rest were to steal a place she has not yet paid for. For that woman, emptiness is not only a threat, it is an accusation. It is the voice that says, deep down: and now, with nothing to do, who are you, and what worth do you have? Notice if this touches you, because it changes the way you will use what follows.

When you see where your fear of desolation comes from, it loses part of its power. Because it stops being a truth about emptiness, and becomes a memory about you, or about those who came before you. And memories, unlike truths, can be revisited with new eyes. Silence is not dangerous. You were taught, early and without words, that it was. These are very different things, and grasping the difference is where everything begins.

And there is here a gesture of healing that family constellation taught me, and that is worth many explanations. If your hurry to fill everything is, in part, inherited, then there is a sentence that loosens it, said within, slowly, to those who came before you: I have seen what you could not stop doing, I honour the life that gave you no rest, and from me onward I can learn to stay still. You do not betray them by resting. On the contrary, you may be the first of your line who can, at last, inhabit a silence they never had the safety to inhabit. What in them was survival, in you can be, at last, repose.

## See

This veil makes you read emptiness as a threat. As absence, abandonment, proof that something is lacking in you or that you were left behind while others had their full lives. You learned, somewhere, perhaps very early, that to be alone was to be unsupported, and ever since you have confused stillness with loss. They are such different things, and the wound does not tell them apart. So you fill. You fill with people who do you no good, with background noise, with endless busyness, with relationships that will not end, with anything that covers over that silent depth where you are afraid of falling and never stopping.

To see this veil is to realise, with some surprise, that what frightens you is not emptiness itself. It is the fear of it, and the memory glued to that fear. They are distinct things, and that distinction is the whole door of this veil. Look closely, because it changes everything: never, as an adult, have you actually stayed in the emptiness long enough to know what it is. You always fled first. What you know is not the silence, it is the panic of approaching it. You react at the door of a room you never entered, convinced you know what is inside, when all you know is your fear of going in.

Emptiness, when you finally dare to stay in it without fleeing, does not swallow you. It stays there, with you, patient, and after a few minutes it begins to show itself from within as what it always was, and what no one ever taught you to see. Not the abyss. The earth. The silent, dark field where the seed germinates with no witnesses, far from view, in its own good time and not in ours. Notice that nothing alive is born in agitation. The seed needs the dark of the earth in order to open. The child is formed in the dark of the womb, nine months without light and without hurry. Everything that comes to flower grows first in a place without light and without applause, in an apparent emptiness that is, in truth, pure gestation. What seems to you like lack is, so often, room for something of yours that has not yet been born.

There is a fear beneath this veil worth naming, because it is the engine of it all: the fear that, if you stop and look within, you will find nothing. That the silence will reveal a hollow, a lack of substance, the secret confirmation that you are no one interesting when you are not doing or pleasing. The exact opposite is what happens. What seems hollow, when you stay in it, reveals itself to be full. What seemed like the absence of you reveals itself to be your presence, at last without interruption. No one told you that the bottom of the well, when you stop thrashing, has water. It always has had.

Stay with this image of the well, because it says everything. When we fall into a well, or when we are afraid of falling, we thrash. We churn the water, we kick, we cry out, we do everything we can not to sink. And that thrashing is exactly what stops us discovering a simple truth: the well has a bottom, and it has water that holds us up, and if we stopped thrashing we would float. Your constant filling is that thrashing. The hurry to cover over the silence is the kicking of one who is certain she will sink if she stops. But try, just once, to stop thrashing. Stay still in the water. And discover that it holds you, that it always held you, and that what you took for drowning was only the fear of it. The bottom was not the abyss you feared. It was solid ground, and there was fresh water, and you always knew how to swim.

There is also, in this veil, a particular fear, and it is different from the panic we know. When stillness settles after a loss, a fall, the end of a chapter, it is not the emptiness that weighs most. It is the subtle presence of a faceless fear. It does not shout or block, it merely remains, like a discreet shadow keeping watch from afar, and it circles the edges of every gesture like a mist that will not lift. This fear is not born of a concrete threat, there is no enemy ahead and no real danger. It grows from the absence, from the vast space that opened when the old forms collapsed. And what it guards, deep down, is no longer your body or your identity. It is the old habit of surviving clutching what has already ended. Notice this, because it changes everything: what haunts you in the desolation is not what you lost, it is your attachment to what you lost, the hand still closed around something that is no longer there. And a hand closed around nothing can, at last, open. Not to lose more. To be able to receive again.

There is a detail of this veil that few women see in time, and it is its greatest cunning: it robs you of contact with yourself. When you fill every hour, when there is never a silence without sound over it, it is not only boredom you keep away. It is your own voice. Your true loves, the ones no one taught you and that are heard only in stillness, lie buried under the noise. Your real tirednesses, which speak only when you stop, are smothered before they can tell you what you need. Your longings, your wishes, your small truths, all of it lives in the same place you flee. That is why there are so many women who, at a certain point in life, find themselves not knowing who they are outside their roles. It is not that they have no self. It is that they built it so full that they never heard it again.

Notice, too, a difference that changes the way you will use everything that follows: the fear of emptiness is almost never, in truth, fear of emptiness. It is fear of what you think the emptiness will show. You are afraid that, still and quiet, a sadness you had been covering over will appear. Or a tiredness you did not want to feel. Or a truth about your life, about a relationship, about a path, that while you run you manage not to see. The noise does not protect you from the emptiness. It protects you from what the emptiness would let rise. That is why, when you finally stay, what appears first is rarely peace. It is what you had been fleeing. Let it appear. It is not the emptiness that brings you the pain, it is the emptiness that gives you, at last, room to feel it and let it pass. And what is felt, unlike what is avoided, in the end passes. What is avoided stays, waiting, forever.

To see, here, is not to think about your fear of emptiness, to analyse it to exhaustion, to find the exact scene from childhood that explains it. That would be the mind using introspection as one more form of flight, one more way of not feeling. There are those who know everything about their wounds and have never seen them, because the knowing was one more way of not feeling. To see is something else, simpler and harder. It is a quiet noticing, almost wordless, in which you catch the gesture of flight in the act, the hand reaching for the phone, the hurry to switch on the noise, and you stay with it for a moment without judging or fixing it. It is the difference between talking about silence and staying in it. It hurts a little, always, because it takes from you the anaesthetic of busyness. And it is that small burning that, in the end, begins to heal.

## Release

To release this veil is, simply, to stay. To stay in the silence long enough to discover the difference, which is enormous, between being alone and being with yourself. One is abandonment, it is the absence of others lived as lack. The other is encounter, it is your own company, which you may have been avoiding for years without knowing. When you stop fleeing the emptiness, it stops being a threat and reveals itself as the only place quiet enough for you to hear yourself. The desolation you so feared shows itself to be fertile, and what looked like lack was, after all, space. Space for something of yours, long postponed, to finally breathe and begin to take form. Many women only discover what they want from life after they stop covering over the silence where that answer always was, waiting, patient.

To release is not an effort, it is not one more thing you have to do well. It is not filling yourself with silence practices with the same craving with which you filled your diary with tasks, turning your own rest into one more project at which you can fail. That would only be swapping one flight for another. To release is to stop holding on. It is to stop feeding the fear of emptiness with your attention and your hurry, and to discover that, without that fuel, it loses force on its own. Often there is not even a dramatic moment of liberation. There is, rather, a day when you notice that you stayed home alone all afternoon and did not rush to turn anything on, that the silence found you and you did not flee, that you reacted differently without even having tried.

To release often has the taste of a small grief, and I want to prepare you for that, so that you do not mistake it for failure. When you begin to let go of the noise, the excess busyness, the relationships that served only as a lid, you will feel, along with the relief, a sadness. It is natural. Even the prisons we grew used to have the comfort of the familiar, and there is a self we were inside them, a woman forever full, forever in demand, forever doing, from whom you are, in part, taking your leave. Let what must hurt, hurt. The sadness, here, is not a sign that something went wrong. It is a sign that something true is being released, that you are in fact letting go and not merely talking about letting go. Cry, if you need to. The gentle tears that appear in the silence are usually the ones that had been dammed up behind all that noise.

### Imposed loneliness and chosen solitude

Before we go on, we must distinguish two things that look alike on the outside and are opposite within: imposed loneliness and chosen solitude. This distinction matters, and I do not want you to skip it, because without it this veil can be misread.

There is a loneliness that hurts, the loneliness of someone who wanted company and does not have it. The loneliness of isolation, of exclusion, of real abandonment, of someone who was left out and feels it as an open wound. That is real, and it is not what I mean when I say that emptiness is fertile. Not everything is solved from within. There are those who genuinely need more bonds, more world, more true people in their lives, and to such a person one does not say she feels better alone. One says she deserves to belong, and that to go and find company, help, community, is an act of health and not a flight. To inhabit silence is not an invitation to isolation, and it would be a violence to read these pages as though it were.

But there is another thing, which many women have never known: the withdrawal that is chosen. Being alone by wish and not by lack. Inhabited silence. The morning hour that is only yours before the house wakes. The walk with no one to interrupt your thoughts. The late afternoon sitting at the window with nothing to fill it. It is not abandonment, it is repose. It is not lack, it is abundance. And the difference between the two is not in the outer circumstances, it is within: one is suffered, the other is chosen.

She who never learned to be well with herself tends to flee both. And, unable to tell them apart, she fills her life with relationships and busyness precisely so as never to have to find out whether she can bear her own company. The irony, and it is a great irony, is that it is only she who learns to be well alone who stops clinging to relationships out of fear, and comes to be in them by choice. Withdrawal well inhabited does not push you away from others. On the contrary: it frees your relationships from the enormous weight of having to use them as a flight from emptiness. When you no longer need anyone in order not to be alone, you can at last be with someone because you want to, and not because you fear them. Emptiness well inhabited is what makes love possible without desperation.

That is why it matters so much that you know, about yourself, which of the two is yours. For some women, this veil asks for more silence, more withdrawal, more courage to be alone, because the life they built is a flight from emptiness and the path is to stop fleeing. For others, the truth is different: they are in fact alone, isolated, with a real lack of bonds, and for these the path is not more silence, it is more world, more people, more connection. The one is sometimes confused with the other, and that is why I want you to look honestly. Ask yourself: is my solitude one I choose and flee, or one I suffer and resign myself to? The answer tells you whether your work is to learn to stay, or to learn to seek. Almost always, in truth, it is a little of both, in different proportions across a life. What does not change is the compass: the withdrawal that nourishes is the one you choose, and the loneliness that wounds is the one imposed on you. Learn to give more space to the first, and more courage to seek company where the second is real.

## The protocol of emptiness

Here is the method, the serious heart of this book. When the fear of emptiness fires, when silence settles and your hand is already reaching for the phone, for the remote, for someone's number, you have a path to follow. It is not a complicated technique or a long ritual. There are five movements, each with its own name, that you apply in the exact instant the flight begins. I call it the protocol of emptiness. Learn it slowly, practise it many times, until your body knows it by heart and it comes on its own, without your having to think.

Before I give it to you, a word about what it is not, because it is easy to confuse. It is not a screen-time technique, one of those that teach you to use your phone less. It is not discipline, it is not control, it is not one more rule to keep. If you use it as one who forces herself, with harshness, you turn the protocol into the veil of striving creeping even into the path of healing, and it stops working, because harshness is incompatible with staying. The protocol does not ask you to resist the flight by willpower. It asks you something else, more subtle and deeper: that you see the flight, welcome it with tenderness, and stay just one instant longer than you usually do, to discover, with your own body, that the silence does not kill you. It is not a fight against the phone. It is a reunion with yourself.

**One. Catch the hand.** Before anything, before anything at all, notice the gesture of flight in the moment it happens. The hand reaching for the phone. The urge to turn on the television. The sudden wish to call someone you do not even miss. The plan you invent for the evening that was going to be yours. That is the golden instant, and it almost always slips by you. You do not have to resist yet, you do not have to do anything heroic. Only to catch the hand in the act and say, within, slowly: here is the veil. Here is the flight from emptiness. To name it is already half the way, because what has a name stops governing you in the dark.

**Two. Stay three breaths.** Do not fill it yet. Before you give in or solve anything, stay where you are and breathe three times, slowly, with the silence you so wanted to cover. Just three breaths. It is little, it is bearable, and it is enough to break the automatism of the flight. In these three breaths you do not have to like the silence, you do not have to find it beautiful. Only not to flee it for three breaths. Most of the time, the dread that seemed unbearable begins to settle right here, like dust coming to rest when you stop stirring it.

**Three. Put your hand on your chest.** Bring a hand to your chest and feel your own warmth, your heart beating, your living body beneath your clothes. This gesture is not symbolic, it is practical: it gives your nervous system the signal of safety it goes looking for outside, in the noise and in other people, and that was here all along, in you. The touch of your own hand tells the body, in a language older than words, that it is not alone and that there is no danger. There is a frightened child behind this veil, and this is the lap you offer her. Say to her, slowly, as you would soothe someone in the middle of the night: this is not danger, it is space. I am not being abandoned. I am with myself. And notice that to say it is already a small miracle: you are, at the same time, the child who was afraid and the woman who now holds her. That lap you may have lacked as a little girl, you can, at last, give to yourself.

**Four. Read the emptiness as earth.** Now, instead of reading the silence as absence, read it as earth. Say, within: this is not the abyss, it is the field where something germinates. Remember that nothing alive is born in agitation, that the seed needs the dark, that this apparent emptiness is, perhaps, room for something of yours you do not yet know. You do not have to believe this with all your might. Only to offer it to your fear as a new reading, another possibility besides the only one you knew. Notice that your body learned a single equation, emptiness equals danger, and that it learned it early, without words, and so believes it without questioning. This step is where you begin to write a new equation over the old one, slowly, with patience, like someone teaching a child that the dark of the bedroom has no monsters. Emptiness is earth, not abyss. Repeat it until your body begins, slowly, to suspect it may be true.

**Five. Ask, and listen.** If you stayed, and only if you stayed, then put to the silence the question you have been running from: and me, what is it that I need? And then, the hardest part, do not answer with your head. Listen. Let the answer come from the depth, or not come today, without forcing. This is the moment when emptiness stops being an enemy and becomes the only quiet place where your voice is heard. It does not answer every day. But it only answers here, in this silence you did not cover, and never in the noise where you had been looking for it. And if no answer comes today, it is not failure. Sometimes the question is enough. You planted it in the silence, and it germinates in its own time, in the dark, far from your eyes, like everything alive. Days later, washing the dishes or walking, you may find yourself knowing something you did not know. It was not your head that found it. It was the silence that gave it back to you.

Five movements: catch the hand, stay three breaths, put your hand on your chest, read the emptiness as earth, ask and listen. You do not have to do all five at once, especially at first. There are days when you will manage only the first, catching the hand, and that is already a great deal. There are days when you stop at the second. That is fine. The protocol is not an exam to pass, it is a path to walk, movement by movement, until the body learns that silence does not kill it, and that on the other side of the fear of being alone there was, waiting, your own company.

And you will have relapses, I do not want them to catch you off guard. There will be days when you do the five movements calmly, and days when your hand reaches for the phone and you only notice half an hour later, already mid-flight. That is not back to square one. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before you did not even realise, and now you catch yourself doing it, perhaps still mid-gesture, perhaps already halfway there. That seeing, ever earlier, is the progress, even if it does not have the shape you expected. The fear of emptiness does not vanish from one day to the next. It loses force, it catches you fewer times and for shorter spells, until one day, with no celebration at all, you notice that you spent a whole afternoon alone and it did not even occur to you to flee. Be patient with yourself. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades, and perhaps generations, to weave.

## The practices

Before the practices, stay with these questions, and do not hurry to cover over what they stir. They are not for answering quickly, they are for sitting with you a few days. What does the silence at home make you flee from? If you stayed in the emptiness without filling it, what would you be afraid of discovering inside? When was the last time you were alone by wish, and not by lack? And if stillness were not abandonment, but the one company that never leaves you, would you have the courage to sit with it? Notice which of these questions pinches you most. It is usually, precisely, the one that points to your root.

The protocol is for the moments when the fear fires. The practices are for the calm days, to train your body to inhabit silence before it catches you by surprise. They are, for this veil, the equivalent of the minute of the veil I speak of in the parent book: small gestures, repeated, that after weeks change the body more than any understanding. Choose one, begin with one alone, and do it until it is yours. Do not pile them all on at once, for that would be the hurry creeping back in even into rest. Do the practice, even if it seems too small to you, because it is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here.

**The two minutes with the silence.** It is the simplest practice and the most powerful, and it is where one begins. The next time silence brings you that familiar dread, do not fill it. Do not pick up the phone, do not turn anything on, do not invent a task. Stay two minutes, only two, with a clock beside you if you need to know they will end. Put your hand on your chest, feel your own warmth, and say, slowly, as you would soothe a child in the middle of the night: this is not danger, it is space. Let the emptiness be earth, and not abyss. Two minutes, and then you can get up, if you wish. You will discover, perhaps as soon as the second or third time, that it did not swallow you, and that two minutes that seemed impossible do, after all, fit inside you. In time, and only in time, you can let the two become five, and the five ten.

**The silent homecoming.** Choose the moment of coming home to train yourself, because that is where the flight is most automatic. The next time you arrive, do not turn on the television, do not put on music, do not rush to the phone. Come in, put down your keys, and stay in silence for a few moments, inhabiting your own home with no noise over it. Notice how your body begs for the background sound, and do not give in yet. Let the house be quiet with you. It is only one gesture, repeated every day, but it is the exact gesture where your veil tends to win, and each time you do not let it win, it loosens a little.

**The screen-free hour.** Set aside, once a day, an hour, or half of one, or a quarter of one if that is what you can bear, with no screen at all. No phone, no television, no computer. Not to do anything productive in that time, for that would be swapping one busyness for another. It is deliberately empty time. You can look out the window, you can sit, you can wander the house, you can do nothing. The aim is exactly that: to learn to be without the screen that covers the depth, and to discover what appears when the noise ceases. At first it will be uncomfortable. Stay with the discomfort. It is the veil losing force.

**The question at the spring.** Once a week, in a moment of stillness you choose, sit down with paper and pen and put to yourself the question this veil makes you flee: what is it that I, only I, truly love? And write, without censoring, whatever comes, however small it may seem. A tea at a certain hour. A song. A path. A morning silence. It is not to solve your life, it is to hear your own voice again, the one that falls quiet when you are forever full of people and tasks. Little by little, you will rediscover the taste of simple things that are only yours, which you had forgotten. The spring was always at the bottom of the emptiness. This practice is the rope you lower down to it.

**The opening hand.** This one is for days of loss, of endings, of falling, when stillness comes accompanied by that faceless fear. Sit for a moment and notice what your hand, within, is still holding. What has ended and what you are still clutching. The relationship that is already over, the time that has already passed, the life that already was and does not return. Picture your hand closed around it, for so long that it ached. And, slowly, without hurry, open one finger. Only one. Not everything, not by force. Say within: I can open the hand, not to lose more, but to be able to receive again. A hand closed around nothing can hold nothing new. Open one finger, today, and leave the rest for the coming days.

**The meal in silence.** This one is simple and you can begin it today. Choose one meal in the week, just one, and have it in silence and without a screen. No television, no phone resting beside the plate, no radio, not even a conversation if you are with someone and the other person agrees. Only you, the food, and your own presence. Notice the taste, which usually escapes you when you eat while doing something else. Notice how your hand pulls you towards the phone at the first pause, and do not give in. It is a small, everyday practice, one that fits into any life, and that teaches you, forkful by forkful, that you can be with yourself for fifteen minutes with nothing over it, and come out of it whole. Where you learn to be alone while eating, you go on learning to be alone while living.

**Thanking the veil.** This one is less a gesture and more a way of treating yourself, and it runs through all the others. Whenever you catch your flight from emptiness, instead of scolding yourself for it, thank it. Yes, thank it. Say within: thank you, this once protected me. Because it really did. That need to fill everything kept safe a little girl who could not bear the silence alone. It was never an enemy, it was an old guard who did not know the danger had passed. When you thank it, instead of fighting it, you take from it the struggle that feeds it, and it loosens far faster than with any effort. Harshness towards yourself is, itself, one more veil. To lift it, here, is to swap the fight for gratitude.

## Seven days with the silence

I offer you a week. Not to solve this veil, which is solved neither in a week nor in seven, but to begin unravelling it with small gestures, one a day, one at a time. Seven days, seven gentle moves towards the silence you have been avoiding, each resting on the one before, with no hurry to arrive anywhere. Do only the gesture of the day, calmly, without piling them up. Do not try to be perfect, do not try to do everything, do not turn this week into one more project at which you can fail. If one day you fail, it is neither relapse nor failure, it is only one day. You begin again the next, without harshness. And if one week is not enough, repeat it. There are women who make of these seven days a rhythm that returns, from time to time, whenever life fills up again and the silence becomes a stranger once more.

**Day one. Catch the flight.** Today you do not have to change anything, nor to stay in any silence. Only to notice. Throughout the day, catch yourself in the act each time you rush to fill the emptiness: the television you turn on the moment you walk in, the phone you pick up for no reason, the task you invent in the instant of peace. Do not judge yourself, only count. By the end of the day, you know how many times you fled. It is your map. One cannot release what one has not first seen.

**Day two. Three breaths.** Today, just once, when you catch the flight, do not fill it straight away. Stay three breaths before you give in. Just three. You can give in afterwards, that is agreed. What matters is that, for three breaths, you stay with the silence instead of running from it. It is the first time you choose to stay, even if only briefly. Notice what you feel in those three breaths, and notice, above all, that you survived them.

**Day three. Two minutes.** Today you do the two-minute practice, once, in the moment silence brings you dread. Hand on chest, and stay. This is not danger, it is space. Two minutes with a clock if you need one. Notice how the dread, which seemed to rise without end, at a certain point begins to settle. It is your first proof, in the body and not in the idea, that the emptiness does not swallow you.

**Day four. The quiet house.** Today, when you come home, do not turn anything on. Come in, put down your keys, and stay in silence for a few minutes before you break the silence with anything. Inhabit your own home with no noise over it. It is a small gesture, but it is exactly where your veil tends to win every day. Today you do not let it.

**Day five. The question.** Today, in a moment of stillness you choose, sit down and ask, and listen: and me, what is it that I need? And then, what is it that I, only I, truly love? Write what comes, however small. Not to solve, to hear yourself. Today you give the voice back to the one who had fallen quiet.

**Day six. Alone by wish.** Today, choose to be alone. Not by lack, but by wish. Set aside a time, half an hour even, in which you are alone on purpose, not because no one is available, but because you chose it. A coffee alone, with no one across from you and no phone in your hand. A walk. An hour at the window. On purpose, do something you used to do only in company, and do it with yourself. Notice the difference between this chosen solitude and the one you usually fear. One is abandonment, it is the absence of others lived as lack. This is encounter, it is your own company, offered to you as one offers it to a dear friend. Today you taste withdrawal, instead of suffering loneliness, and you may surprise yourself by finding it, against all you expected, pleasant.

**Day seven. The opening hand.** Today, on the last day, notice what you are still holding, for so long that it aches, and that may be the root of your fear of emptiness: the attachment to what has already ended. And, slowly, open one finger. Only one. Not to lose more, but to be able to receive again. And then, stay a moment in silence, without fleeing, and notice how it is no longer quite the same silence as the first day. You have changed a little. The rest releases itself in its own time, day by day, as you become ready, and not one instant before.

## What changes

I do not promise you a cure, nor a day when the fear of emptiness disappears for good and never returns. That is not how it works, and whoever promises you that is selling you another flight. What I promise you is more modest and more truthful: that, as you practise, the silence will catch you fewer and fewer times and for shorter spells, that your first impulse will slowly cease to be flight, and that one day, with no celebration at all, you will notice that you have changed. Here is what changes.

What changes when this veil lifts is that you stop being afraid of yourself. The silence, which was a threat, becomes a refuge. You come to know how to be alone without feeling abandoned, and that is one of the greatest freedoms a woman can win, because it frees everything else.

It frees, first, all your relationships from an enormous weight. When you no longer cling to anyone out of terror of the emptiness, you come to be with people by choice and not by flight. You leave the relationships that no longer serve you, because you no longer need them in order not to be alone. And you are more wholly present in the ones that are worth it, because you are not in them to plug a hole, you are in them to share an abundance. Love, without the desperation of the emptiness beneath, becomes at last possible without clutching.

Notice what this does to the people around you, because it is great. A woman who fears emptiness clings. She hangs on relationships with a weight that smothers, she demands constant presence, she feels each absence of the other as abandonment, she keeps accounts, she lives on guard. Not out of malice, but out of terror of what would come if she were left alone. When the emptiness no longer terrifies her, that weight falls away. She no longer needs the other in order not to sink, and so she can love him more lightly, give him space, let him go and return without drama. Paradoxically, it is when you learn not to need others in order to survive the silence that you become better company for them. Clinging love drives away. The love of one who is whole, even alone, is the only kind that breathes.

You rediscover the taste of simple things that are only yours, which you had forgotten because you were forever full of people and tasks. The quiet morning before the house wakes. The walk with no one. The late-afternoon silence that no longer frightens you. A book read slowly, a song heard to the end with nothing else being done, a bath without the phone beside you. Small things that have flavour again, because you finally stop long enough to feel them. And you will notice that you were not, after all, a woman without loves. You were a woman who had buried them under so much noise that she no longer heard them call.

Your relationship with rest changes too. Where before rest brought you guilt or alarm, it begins to bring you relief. You no longer need to deserve the pause, to justify it with extreme tiredness, to steal it like someone doing something forbidden. To rest becomes a right of yours, simple, with no bill to pay. And that is a freedom that spreads through everything, because a woman who knows how to stop is a woman who treats herself with the tenderness she used to give only to others.

Your relationship with life as a whole changes, in the end. A woman who cannot bear emptiness lives always in flight, and flight is tiring, even when it does not go by that name. There is a background tiredness in one who can never stop, an exhaustion that sleep does not cure, because what tires is not what one does, it is the never being able to not do. When silence stops being a threat, that tiredness loosens. Not because you do less, but because you stop doing in flight. And life, which was a destinationless race away from yourself, becomes something else, slower, more inhabited, more yours.

And, at the bottom of that emptiness you so feared, you find not the abyss, but a spring. A quiet place from which there well up, slowly, the answers you had been looking for outside, in the noise and in other people, where they never were. You discover what you want, what you love, who you are when you are not doing or pleasing. It is not that you become another person. It is that you find again the one who was beneath all that full life, waiting for a silence in which to show herself. She who learns to inhabit her own silence is never truly alone again, because she carries with her, wherever she goes, the one company that never leaves her.

## To close

This was one veil, of seven. The Desolation, the fear of emptiness, is only one of the layers that came to sit between you and what you are, and it may not even be the thickest in your life. There are others. The identity you defend and that already pinches. The story you got caught in, the "I have always been this way". The head that will not stop, the whirlwind that drowns you. The striving of one who exhausts herself to be loved. The horizon forever postponed, the "when I get there". And, beneath them all, the oldest, the sense of separation, of being always on the outside of a pane of glass.

The veils intertwine, and removing one tends to loosen the others. Perhaps you noticed, reading this one, that your fear of emptiness touches your striving, your hurry to arrive, your difficulty in resting. That is natural. They are all kin, and they are born, deep down, of the same root. So, if this veil called to you, let it work in you for a few days, do the practices even if they seem small to you, come back to these pages in a few months and read them again, because you will find them different, since you will be different.

And do not expect a straight line. This is not a path you walk once and finish. It is a spiral. You will go back to fleeing the silence in the more tiring phases of life, you will find the phone in your hand again without noticing, you will have whole weeks so full that emptiness becomes a stranger once more. You have not gone back to square one. On a spiral one passes the same point several times, but never at the same height. Next time, you will catch yourself sooner. You will know the name of what is happening to you. You will have a protocol, and some practices, and the memory, in the body, that you have stayed in the silence before and it did not swallow you. That changes everything, even when life pushes you back into the noise.

Do you remember the woman from the beginning, the one with the weekend alone? She did not become another person that Sunday evening. She went on having a full life, people, tasks, noise. But one thing stayed with her and never left: the memory, in the body, that she had stayed in the silence and that it had given her back to herself, instead of swallowing her. From then on, every now and then, she chose to stay. Not always, not perfectly, with relapses and flights in between. But she chose. And each time she chose, the emptiness became a little more earth and a little less abyss, until one day, with no celebration, she found herself longing for an afternoon alone, she who for years had feared them more than anything. This is what I wish for you. Not that you stop being who you are, nor that you abandon your life and your people. Only that you gain, in the midst of all that, the freedom to stay with yourself whenever you wish, without fear.

And when you want the whole map, the seven veils and the way they connect, it is in *The Seven Veils*, the parent book from which this one was born. There you will find the full path, and the pocket protocol and the minute of the veil that serve them all. For now, this is enough: learn to stay with the silence. Discover, on the other side of the fear of being alone, your own company. And remember, always, that emptiness was never absence. It was always the silent, dark field where something of yours, long postponed, was waiting for its time to germinate.
