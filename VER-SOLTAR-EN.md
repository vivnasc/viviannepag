# ver.soltar
### seeing: stepping out of the storm
*Method VS · See and Release · Vivianne dos Santos*

> *The water keeps passing. You are no longer in it: you are on the bank, and you see.*

## Who this book is for

This book is for you, if your mind never stops. If you wake already tired of a day that has not yet begun, because you lived it all through the night. If you relive, point by point, a conversation that ended years ago, and rehearse, point by point, a conversation that may never happen. If you feel each emotion so strongly that you stop having it and become it, head to foot. If there is an old film playing inside you almost all the time, with characters from twenty years ago, and you reacting to the present with a pain that belongs to another time.

This is not a book to teach you to empty your mind, because the mind does not empty, and anyone who promises you that is only handing you one more race to lose. Nor is it a manual of techniques to "control anxiety", as if it were an enemy to be put down. It is something else, simpler and deeper. It is about a single movement, perhaps the most freeing one I know: stepping out of the storm and watching it from the bank. Not silencing the wind. Not stopping the rain. Stepping out of it, and discovering that there is a place in you the storm does not soak.

This is the first of three books that are, in truth, three movements of a single crossing. **See, return, live.** This one, the first, is the movement of **seeing**, the movement of consciousness. And it begins with the loudest of the veils, the one that steals the now from both ends: the mind racing forward, forever rehearsing what is to come, and the mind reaching backward, forever chewing over what has already been. I call it, in truth, two veils that always travel hand in hand: the Whirlwind and the Memory. But you will see, over these pages, that they are two faces of the same storm, and that they are crossed with a single gesture.

Read it slowly. Not to reach the end, but to carry it into your life, because it is there, in traffic, in the queue, in bed at three in the morning, that everything is decided. I will not ask you to try harder. Effort, here, is part of the problem. I will ask you only to look, with more honesty and less harshness, at the water that carries you, and to discover, with some surprise, that there has always been a bank, and that it is yours.

I want to tell you, right here, what this book will not do, so that you do not reach the end with the sense of having failed. It will not give you a calm, flat mind, like a lake in the morning, because that does not exist except in meditation photographs, and chasing it is just one more race. It will not promise you that you will never again have a sleepless night, nor ever again relive, with that tightening, an old scene. The mind will go on doing what the mind does. What this book gives you is something else, more discreet and more solid: a place to stand, from which to watch all of it pass without going in. Not the absence of storm, which is not in your hands. The bank, which is.

I want to make clear, also from the first page, that there is nothing in you to fix. You are not broken, you are not ill, you are not a problem to be solved. Your mind that never stops is not a manufacturing fault. It is, you will see, an old intelligence that learned to protect you in a way that no longer serves, and that no one told the danger has passed. You do not come to these pages to repair yourself. You come to see, with new eyes and gentler hands, what has carried you off for so long. And seeing, on its own, is already half of freedom.

I know from the inside the mind I speak of. There was a time in my life when bedtime was the worst hour of the day, because it was there, in the dark and the silence, that the mind took charge of everything. I relived conversations, rehearsed others, made and unmade decisions, anticipated catastrophes that never came, and woke in the morning more tired than I had lain down, as if the night, instead of rest, had been one more shift of work. For a long time I thought the problem was me, that I was weak, that I had no mental discipline, and I tried everything to stop the mind by force, which, you will see, is like trying to calm water by hitting it with your hands. Only when I stopped trying to stop it, and began to learn to step out of it, did something, at last, give way. I do not write this from the bank, settled and serene forever. I write it as someone who knows the way back to the bank, and who loses it many times, and who has learned to return. That is all I bring you: the way back.

I do not write to you, then, from a place of arrival. I write from a path still under way, my own. I am someone who watched herself drown in her own mind, who grew tired of judging herself weak for it, and who went looking to understand, with the stubbornness of one who needs to. I still study, to this day, transpersonal psychology and systemic family constellation, not as someone collecting certainties, but as someone who needs names for what she feels. Everything I share here passed first through me. I do not invite you to follow me, I invite us to walk at the same pace, side by side, to the bank.

## The movement of seeing

There is an old mistake worth undoing straight away, because it is the door to everything. Most of us believe the problem is the quantity of thoughts, and that the solution would be to have fewer. We think like this: if I could just calm the mind, empty it, stop it, I would be at peace. And so we chase silence as if it were one more goal, and when it does not come, we conclude that we are failing even at resting. But the problem was never that you have thoughts. The mind produces thoughts the way the stomach produces gastric juice, it is its function, and it will keep doing so until your last day. The problem is another, more subtle one: that you are inside it. That you confuse yourself with what churns, that you live inside the wave instead of watching it pass.

Seeing is exactly this, and nothing grander than this: stepping out from inside. Notice that I do not ask you to stop thinking, nor to stop feeling. The emotion has the right to pass through you, and the mind will think for as long as you are alive. I ask of you only a change of place: that you stop being inside the wave, drenched, and come to watch it from the bank. The wave keeps coming, with the same force, but you are no longer dragged by it to the bottom. And that small change, which seems almost nothing, is the difference between a life lived in fright and a life with a centre. It is not thinking about. It is not analysing yourself to exhaustion, dissecting your childhood, building the perfect theory of why your mind is the way it is, because that, very often, is just the mind using introspection as one more way to keep busy, one more turn of the wheel. Seeing is a quiet noticing, almost wordless, in which you catch what is happening in the moment it happens, and you stay with it without rushing to judge it or fix it. It is the difference between talking about the sea and getting your feet wet. And it is, at once, the simplest and the hardest thing, because it takes away the anaesthetic of always doing something with what you feel, and asks of you only that you see.

Here is the heart of this book, the reason the Whirlwind and the Memory come together: they are the same storm. Your mind never stops for a reason, and the reason is this: it is forever reliving the past or rehearsing the future, and in either case it is outside the now, which is the only place where life actually happens. The Memory is the mind turned backward, chewing over what was, confirming who you are by what has already happened to you, like someone driving a whole car looking in the rear-view mirror. The Whirlwind is the mind turned forward, anticipating, preventing, defending itself against attacks no one made, solving ten times over a problem that has not even arrived. One steals your present through the past, the other through the future. And between the two, in the middle, lies the now, squeezed to almost nothing, crossed at a run, barely seen.

Notice how they feed one another, because that is where the web is. The Whirlwind rehearses the future in the image of the past: you fear it will happen again, and that "again" is a memory. The Memory, in turn, does not stay quiet in the past, it projects itself forward, "it is always like this with me, and it always will be". One pulls the other, and the wheel turns, and you, in the middle, exhausted, living a whole life that is not happening, while the one that is happening passes you by. There are women who suffer for years everything that never happened to them, and lose, without noticing, almost everything that actually did.

Let me give you an example of how the two work together, in chorus, in a single day. In the morning, someone makes a curt remark at work. The Memory lights up first, without asking permission: this is how it has always been, no one respects me, it is the story of my life. Within seconds, you have built from a remark a verdict about your place in the world, and that verdict has the voice of things far older than the colleague who spoke. Then, at the end of the afternoon, the Whirlwind comes in, and takes up the baton: and what if this gets worse, and what if there are more remarks, I must prepare, I must rehearse what I will say, I must foresee every scenario. At night, in bed, the two join hands and dance: the Memory brings the proof of every time you felt like this, the Whirlwind uses that proof to frighten you about the future. And you, in the middle, awake at three in the morning, living a war that is happening nowhere except inside your own head. Do you recognise this day? Almost all of us do. It is not weakness. It is the storm working exactly as it learned to work.

The movement that frees you is a single one, and that is why these two veils fit in one book. It is not stopping the past, nor controlling the future. It is stepping out of the water and onto the bank. From the bank, you watch the river pass, and it keeps passing, with the same force, with the same dark water, but you are no longer in it. You watch the memory arrive and you recognise, ah, it is the past painting the present. You watch the whirlwind begin and you recognise, ah, it is the future stealing my night. And in that recognising, in that simple seeing, there is already a millimetre of space between you and the storm, and it is in that millimetre that all the freedom you slowly gain lives.

There is a question that may occur to you, and it is a fair one: if I am only actually here, in this instant, why do I spend so little time here? The answer is that the now, on its own, rarely feeds the mind. The present is, in most moments, surprisingly simple: you are sitting, you breathe, there is light, there is the sound of something far off. There is, in this exact second, no drama at all. And the mind, trained to watch for danger, finds the present boring, or dangerously calm, and flees to where there is material, which is the past, full of wounds to reopen, and the future, full of catastrophes to prevent. That is why the wheel turns: it feeds on everything that is not here. And that, too, is why returning to the now disarms it. In the now there is no firewood for the whirlwind. That is why it hates the present, and why the present is your greatest freedom.

Notice that the bank is not coldness, nor the distance of someone who has stopped feeling. I am not teaching you to numb yourself, to look at your life from above, shrunken, untouched by anything. It is the opposite. When you are inside the water, carried off, you feel everything in a confused way, unable to tell what is from now and what is from twenty years ago, and so you suffer double, today's pain added to all the old pains it woke. From the bank, you feel with more clarity, not less. You watch the wave of sadness arrive, and you can let it pass through you with all its force, and even weep with it if you need to, but without drowning, because you know you are the one who sees it, and that it will pass, as all of them pass. The bank does not take away your feeling. It takes away your drowning.

I want to say a further word about what seeing is not, because the confusion between seeing and thinking is the one that most delays the women I know. Seeing is not understanding yourself. You can spend your life understanding yourself, building your perfect biography, knowing by heart the reasons you are the way you are, and never having actually seen yourself, because the knowing was, itself, one more way of not feeling. There are women who know everything about their anxiety, have read everything, have all the vocabulary, and go on drowning in it exactly as before, because the knowing stayed in the head and never came down to the body. Seeing is coming down to the body. It is the mute instant in which you catch the storm assembling itself, still at the start, and you stay watching it instead of joining it. There is no theory there. There is only presence, and a small burning, because seeing always hurts a little, it takes away the anaesthetic of explanation. And it is that small burning that, in the end, frees you.

I do not ask you to believe this. I ask you to notice one thing, right now, as you read: if you can see a thought, then there is in you something that is not that thought. The one who sees the sadness is not wholly taken by it, otherwise she could not see it. The one who notices the fear has, in that very instant, one foot outside the fear. The storm is real. But you are not the storm. You are the one who sees it. And it has always been so, even when you forgot.

Notice the almost silly simplicity of this truth, and the force it has, because it is the stone on which all that follows rests. You do not have to do anything to make it true. You do not have to win it with years of practice. It is already the case, in this very moment. Behind all the noise that may be happening inside you as you read, there is someone who is reading. There is a quiet place, nameless, ageless, that watches. That place was not born with your first wound and will not die with the next. It was there on your worst day, even if you could not find it, just as the sky stays behind the clouds even when not a single patch of blue can be seen. This whole book is, at bottom, a way of taking you back to that place, and of showing you the way back so that you can do it alone, in the kitchen, in traffic, in the dark.

## The mind that never stops

Let us look first at the face of the future, the Whirlwind, because it is the loudest and the one you most easily recognise. Your mind never stops. You anticipate conversations that have not yet happened and you rehearse perfect replies to imaginary arguments. You defend yourself against attacks no one made. You solve ten times over a problem that has not arrived and perhaps never will. At night, when the body finally stops, the mind speeds up, as if it were using the silence to dump everything it held in during the day. And you feel each thing so intensely that you become what you feel: you are not sad, you are sadness whole; you are not afraid, you are fear from one end to the other. When the whirlwind catches you, it seems nothing else exists beyond it, and that it is you.

Notice how it disguises itself as responsibility, because that is how it stays unpunished. You tell yourself you are only thinking, preventing, solving ahead of time, and so you let the wheel turn as if it were useful work. I knew of a woman who spent her nights planning conversations with her boss, anticipating every criticism, rehearsing defences for attacks that never came. In the morning she woke exhausted, as if she had lived the argument ten times over, and the real argument, when it finally came, lasted two minutes and went well. She had suffered everything, except what actually happened. The whirlwind was not protecting her from anything. It was only stealing her nights in the name of a safety she never came to feel.

I think of another, and there could be so many, who lived with her phone in her hand checking things. Tomorrow's temperature, the traffic for a journey a month away, the symptoms of an illness she did not have, the bank account three times a day, though nothing had changed since the last. She called it staying informed, staying prepared, being careful. Underneath, it was a child trying to make sure the world would never again catch her off guard, as it had caught her once. Each check soothed her for thirty seconds and returned her, right after, to the same unease, because the unease did not come from outside, it came from within, and no amount of information could close it. The whirlwind is like this: it promises you that, if you control just a little more, you will be at peace, and the peace always recedes one step further, like the horizon that is never reached. There is only one way out of it, and it is not by controlling more. It is by stepping out of the water.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? By the mind that will not switch off, especially at night, when the body finally stops and the mind uses the silence to dump everything. By the tendency to anticipate the worst and rehearse scenarios that rarely happen. By feeling emotions as waves that submerge you whole, with no bank to stand on. By the difficulty of being simply present, without commentary, at a meal, in a conversation, in a good moment, because the mind is already analysing, judging, planning the next. By confusing worry with love or with responsibility, believing that if you stopped fretting you would be neglectful. And by an underlying sense of being almost always reacting, rarely choosing. None of these signs is illness or a flaw of character. They are the invitation to discover, beneath the noise, who in you never moves.

There is a disguise of the whirlwind worth naming, because it is what makes it so hard to release: it convinces you that it is love. You tell yourself you fret because you care, that if you stopped anticipating the worst you would be abandoning those you love to chance. And so worry seems to you a duty, almost a form of magical protection, as if, by suffering a misfortune in advance, you were somehow preventing it. But notice honestly: has your worry ever changed a single event out there? Has a night spent rehearsing a conversation ever made the conversation go better? The whirlwind protects no one. It only consumes you, and steals from you, on top of that, the real presence with those you say you love, because you are always half absent, already in the next feared scenario. True love is not the worry that robs you of sleep. It is the presence that is, whole, with whoever is in front of you now.

It is worth understanding how the whirlwind feeds itself, so that you stop throwing it wood without meaning to. It grows mainly from two roots. The first is fusion: believing that everything the mind says is true and deserves an immediate reply. A thought appears, "and what if the worst happens", and you treat that sentence as a real warning, and you mobilise, and the mind, seeing that you took it seriously, produces ten more like it. The second root is resistance: wanting not to feel what you feel, fighting against the anxiety, getting irritated at being irritated. And there happens the cruel paradox of all inner life, the one rightly called the law of reversal: everything you resist persists. The emotion you say no to stays at the door knocking harder. The emotion you let in, sit down and be felt, usually stays a short while, because that was all it was asking, to be felt. Notice that these two roots ask the opposite of one another: fusion asks you to bring your gaze close, to put your head inside the thought, and resistance asks you to flee, to push the emotion away. Seeing does neither one nor the other. It does not stick, it does not push. It stays at the exact distance of the bank, where one sees without going in, and where one lets pass without pushing away.

The whirlwind is not, in truth, a problem of too many thoughts. It is a problem of identification. You confuse what churns inside with what you are, and so each wave carries you, without resistance, as if there were no bank to stand on. We live, on top of all this, in an age that feeds this veil as never before. Everything shouts for your attention, everything asks of you an immediate reaction, and your nervous system, already trained to watch for danger, rarely finds a moment of rest. You carry in your pocket a small device designed, to the millimetre, never to let you rest your head, to give you always one more thing to fear, to compare, to rehearse. It is not weakness of yours to be like this. It is an old whirlwind living in a world made to stir it up. And that is precisely why the bank, today, is not found by chance. It has to be chosen, and trained.

There is a misunderstanding I want to undo straight away, because it can make you feel a failure for no reason. Stepping out of the whirlwind is not reaching an empty mind, without thoughts, flat as a lake in the morning. That does not exist, and chasing it only creates one more effort, one more horizon never to be reached. The mind will go on producing thoughts, it is its function, as the stomach produces gastric juice. Freedom is not having no thoughts. It is no longer being dragged by them. It is being able to have a full head and, at the same time, a calm centre that observes that full head without drowning. The sky does not need the clouds to stop in order to keep being sky. But the bank exists, and has always existed, even on the days when you swear you cannot find a centimetre of peace. The storm is real, the wind is real, the rain wets. But you are not the storm. You are the sky it passes through, and no sky, however black the day turns, was ever torn by a cloud. The clouds pass. The sky stays.

## Bound to your story

Now the other face, the face of the past, the Memory, quieter than the Whirlwind but as powerful, and often the secret spring the Whirlwind drinks from. There is a sentence you have said about yourself for so long that you no longer notice you say it. "I have always been like this." "After that, I became like this." "With me it is always the same." It comes out with the ease of someone stating the colour of their eyes, as if it were a fact and not a reading you repeat every day. Your story walks with you as proof and as sentence at once, and it is the story that decides, before you decide, whom you love, what you flee, how much you ask, what you think you deserve. Without noticing, you live looking backward, confirming who you are by what has already happened to you, like someone driving a whole car looking in the rear-view mirror, and then wondering why they always hit the same walls.

This veil makes us believe we are the sum of our past, and that carrying the story is the same as being faithful to it. It even seems a form of honesty, of coherence. But you are not your narrative, and it is not even the faithful record of what happened. It is a reading, made and remade, and the one who makes it is you, today, and not the girl who lived the facts. The difference between treating your story as a sentence or as a root is the difference between two lives. The sentence imprisons, defines, closes the case. The root sustains, nourishes, and lets the tree grow in any direction the light asks. When the story stops being bars and becomes root again, the present opens, free, perhaps for the first time in a long while, and you discover that the future does not have to resemble the past just because it always has.

Notice a small, common scene. Someone takes a while to reply to a message of yours, and within seconds you have the whole story built: they are drifting away, it is just like the last time, it is always like this with me, I am no one's priority. The person was only in a meeting. But you have already lived the whole afternoon inside an old film, with characters from twenty years ago, feeling in today's body a pain that belongs to another time. You did not react to the present. You reacted to the memory. And do you see how the two faces touch? That same episode, at night, turns into whirlwind, and there you are rehearsing the conversation you are going to have, defending yourself against an abandonment no one announced. The memory set the stage, the whirlwind acts out the play.

How do you recognise this veil active in you? You catch yourself using the words "always" and "never" a great deal when you speak of yourself, as if they were laws of physics. You justify the present with the past almost without noticing, "well, with the childhood I had". You react to today's situations with an intensity that, if you are honest, belongs to another era and to other people. You have stories about yourself you always tell the same way, with the same words, worn from repetition, and which you have never questioned. You feel a kind of loyalty to your suffering, as if releasing it would betray those who lived it with you or before you. And you project the future as an inevitable continuation of what already was, closing doors before you even draw near them. Recognising this does not erase your story. It only takes you out from inside it long enough to be able, at last, to see it from outside.

There is a strange thing here worth seeing, because it changes everything: memory does not keep the past as it was. It re-tells it. Each time you return to an old scene, it comes back slightly different, tinged with today's emotion, patched by what you have learned in the meantime, and you live that reworked version as if it were the pure fact. You do not remember what happened. You remember the last time you remembered. Memory is less an archive and more a room where the story is staged anew, every time, with small changes to the script. The past happened once. The reading you make of it happens every day, and that, yes, you can see, and in seeing, change.

The past is real, and it is not a matter of denying it or pretending it did not hurt. It is a matter of something more subtle and freer: the past happened once, but the reading you make of it happens every day, and that, yes, you can change. The same childhood can be told as proof that you are not capable, or as proof that you survived a great deal. The same facts, two stories, two different lives out of the same material. And it is not empty positivity, the kind that orders you to turn the page without reading it. It is the opposite. It is looking at the whole story, including what hurt, with today's eyes and not those of back then. A child interprets what happens to her with a child's resources, and almost always concludes that it is her fault. To re-read that same story with the eyes of a grown woman is, very often, to free the girl from a sentence she never deserved. The facts do not change. The one who reads them changes, and that changes everything.

I think of a woman who used to say, laughing but in earnest, that she was "terrible at choosing men". She repeated it like a worn-out joke, and each new disappointment confirmed the sentence, neat and round. Only much later, looking calmly, from the bank, did she realise she did not choose badly. She always chose the same, the same kind of distance, the same promise that went unkept, because that was the love she had known at home, and the familiar, even when it hurts, has the treacherous comfort of the known. The sentence "I am terrible at choosing" was not a diagnosis. It was a spell she herself renewed in every relationship. The day she stopped saying it, and looked at the pattern instead of at the sentence, she began, for the first time, to be able to choose otherwise.

There is a still more stubborn way this face keeps itself, and it has a name in family constellation: loyalty. Notice that, very often, releasing your old story feels almost like a betrayal. If your mother suffered and you built your identity around that shared suffering, being happy can sound, deep down, like abandoning her. If in your family people were always sad, or unlucky in love, prospering can bring a strange guilt, as if you were stepping out of the line, leaving your own behind. There are women who sabotage, without realising, everything that goes well for them, faithful to a family fate they confused with identity. Seeing this is freeing, because it allows a new choice: I can honour where I come from without having to repeat what hurt there. Belonging to my family does not oblige me to inherit its sadness.

There is one last subtlety in this face, worth naming because it is the one that most frightens when it appears. When you finally release the story that defined you, a strange space opens. The self no longer leans on the past, but it has not yet found firm ground in the now either, and the mind, used to telling itself as a continuity, does not know how to inhabit that interval. A void arises, and you may take fright at it, think you have lost something, run back to the old story just to have known ground under your feet again, even if it hurt. Do not run. That void is not lack, it is gestation. It is the fertile ground between the story you released and the life you have not yet named, and it is precisely there, without the comfort of the old certainties about yourself, that something truly new can, for the first time, begin to be born. Who are you when you stop being the story you always told? You do not know yet, and that is well. The not knowing, here, is openness, not loss.

## The root

Where does all this come from, this mind that keeps watch without rest, this past that refuses to stay behind? It is worth looking at the root, not to lose yourself digging up your childhood, but because seeing where a thing comes from takes away half its power. What seemed a flaw of yours reveals itself, almost always, as an old strategy, intelligent, that a body found in order to survive, and which was never told that the danger has passed.

Here is something it really matters to understand, because it changes the way you will treat yourself from here on: your nervous system does not distinguish past from present. It distinguishes a single thing, safe or threatened. When something in the present touches an old wound, a tone of voice, a silence, a door that closes, it does not ask what year it is. It reacts as it reacted the first time, and sets off the whole alarm, with the same force as back then, even if there is no danger today. That is why your reaction so often seems excessive, and why you yourself do not understand it, and scold yourself for it. It is not excessive for the time it belongs to. It is the right measure for a wound of thirty years ago, set off today by an echo. When you see this, you stop treating yourself as hysterical or difficult, and begin to treat yourself as someone carrying an old alarm, too sensitive, that takes fright at shadows. And at an alarm like that you do not shout. You calm it with tenderness, and with the proof, repeated many times, that this time there is no fire.

The Whirlwind is often born in settings where the child had to be always on alert. Where the climate of the house was unpredictable, where one had to read the moods of the adults in order to be safe, where danger, physical or emotional, could come without warning. A nervous system that grows up like this learns to keep watch the way one learns to breathe. Hypervigilance, which was protection, later becomes anxiety with no object, a mind that will not switch off because switching off was, once, a risk. Adult rumination is, very often, the old child still trying to foresee the unforeseeable so as not to be caught by surprise again. When you see that your agitation has this root, you stop fighting it as an enemy and begin to treat it as what it is: a part of you, loyal and tired, that can finally know the danger has passed.

The Memory, for its part, is born in the way you were taught to read your own life, before you were old enough to read it for yourself. Children do not receive only facts, they receive interpretations. "You are like that", "with us it is always like this", "this family is unlucky", "love hurts". Each of these sentences, repeated, becomes a lens, and from then on you spend your life confirming what you were told you were. A child interprets what happens to her with a child's resources, and almost always concludes that it is her fault, that she was not enough, that something in her was wrong. And that conclusion, drawn at six years old, still governs a woman of forty who has never reviewed it.

There is, deepest of all, the root that systemic family constellation opened to me in a way I did not expect: the inheritance that is not yours. Much of what you carry was not born in you. It crossed generations before it reached you. Mothers and grandmothers who lived in times or houses of distress pass on, without words, a state of alarm that no longer matches today's life. There are sorrows that are not yours, they are your mother's, and you were loyal to them like someone honouring a debt she never knew she had signed. There are families that carry an unmourned loss, a secret, an injustice, and those who come after repeat the plot without knowing, as if completing a sentence left half-finished. When you listen with true attention to certain sentences you say about yourself, or to a certain alarm that lives in your body, you realise they have someone else's voice. And there a new possibility opens, a freeing one: I can honour where I come from without having to repeat what hurt there. I can give back the alarm to the one who needed it, and set down what was never mine.

I want to dwell a moment on this inheritance, because it is the part that most relieves once it is finally seen, and the hardest to believe at first. I am not speaking of a pretty metaphor. I am speaking of something concrete, that is felt in the body. There are women who live in permanent alert, the heart always half racing, awaiting a danger that never comes, and who, when they look back over their own life, find no event that justifies so much fright. Their life was, on the outside, relatively safe. And yet the alarm is there, deep, old, as if the body remembered a danger it never lived itself. Often it is because it did not, in fact. The mother lived it, or the grandmother, in a time of war, of hunger, of flight, of a house where one had to be always ready for the worst. That state passed from body to body, without words, like someone passing on the colour of their eyes, and reached you already without the danger that gave it meaning. You carry the watch of a sentry from another generation, at a post where the war ended long ago.

When this is seen, with clarity, something rare happens: the alarm begins to be able to rest. Not because you fought it, but because you finally recognised it as what it is, a loan, a loyalty, and not your truth. You can, inside, with respect, say to those who came before: I see what you had to endure, I honour your vigilance, it kept you alive. And from me on, in this safer time, I can set down the part that is no longer needed. This is not forgetting, nor dishonouring. It is giving the weight back to whom it belonged, with love, so that you can finally breathe more slowly.

Notice that, seen by the root, the two faces of the storm reveal the same fear beneath: the fear of being caught by surprise, helpless, with no defence. The Whirlwind tries to foresee the blow before it comes. The Memory tries to confirm who you are so that nothing surprises you about yourself. Both are forms of an old child trying to keep control of a world that was, once, genuinely unpredictable. Seeing this is the first step toward telling her, inside, with tenderness: it has passed. You can set down the watch now. I look after us now.

## The protocol of seeing

Before I give you the method, stay a moment with these questions, without rushing to answer, letting them rest more than answering them. When your mind speeds up, what story is it trying to protect you from feeling? If you believed half of what you think about the future, how much of your energy would you be spending defending yourself against things that will never happen? Which of your reactions, of the ones that repeat, is too big for the situation that sets it off, and to what other time does that pain, in fact, belong? And can you, right now, find in you the one who is reading these lines, behind all the noise? Do not answer from your head. Notice, over the days, and let the answers come.

Here is the heart of this book, the serious part, the method you can carry with you anywhere, without these pages before you. I call it **the protocol of seeing**, and it has five steps. It is not a technique for emptying the mind, I say it again, because that does not exist. It is a path for stepping out of the storm and onto the bank, five steps you train until the body knows them by heart and does them almost on its own, in the middle of a small crisis, in traffic, in bed at three in the morning. Read them calmly. Each does one thing, and the order matters.

**First: name it. "I am noticing."** The next time the mind speeds up, do not fight it or try to think better in order to solve the thought, because fighting a thought is only more thought, more whirlwind battling whirlwind. Say inside, slowly, almost like someone remarking on the weather: "I am noticing that I am thinking this." Or: "I am noticing that I am reliving that." Notice the almost silly simplicity of these few words, and the force they have. That "I am noticing" already takes you out from inside the wave, because to notice a thing you have to be, for an instant, outside it. You do not have to stop it. Only to notice it. Naming is the first step to the bank, and sometimes it is the only one you need. Notice the difference, which seems small and is enormous, between "I am sad" and "I am noticing that I am sad". In the first, you are the sadness, taken by it, with no way out. In the second, there is the sadness, and there is the one who notices it, and those are two things, and between them the millimetre of air has opened where all freedom fits. You did not change the sadness. You changed your place in relation to it. You stopped being the wave and became the one who sees it arrive.

**Second: step onto the bank.** Now that you have noticed, take a step back, inwardly. Instead of being inside the thought, living it, stay watching it pass. Imagine, if it helps, that you are sitting on the bank of a river, and that each thought, each wave of emotion, is water passing before you. You do not push it, you do not hold it, you do not jump in. Let it come, let it pass. The emotion keeps coming, with the same force, but you are no longer dragged by it to the bottom. You watch. This is the change of place that changes everything: not silencing the water, but changing your position in relation to it. If the image of the river does not reach your body, use another, whichever is yours. Some imagine themselves watching the clouds pass in a sky that stays. Some feel thoughts as trains that stop at the station and which they can choose not to board, letting them go on. Some simply bring a hand to the chest and feel the body, the weight, the breath, and that is enough to step out of the head. The image is only a bridge. What matters is the gesture beneath it: that you stop being the water and become the one who sees it.

**Third: ask what time this belongs to.** Now, from the bank, ask a simple question of what you are watching: is this from now, or from another time? Does what I am feeling belong to this situation, or is it an old pain this situation woke? Is it the unanswered message that hurts me, or a memory of abandonment from twenty years ago? Is it this meeting that frightens me, or every time I was caught by surprise? Almost always you will find that the intensity does not fit the present, that the water comes from a much older spring. And that discovery, on its own, eases things, because it takes the present out of the courtroom where the past had put it. There is a simple rule that helps to catch this: when your reaction is far bigger than the situation deserves, it is almost certain you are reacting to another time. A small setback that puts you down for the whole day is not the small setback, it is everything it woke. The present, on its own, rarely justifies so much. When you feel it out of proportion, know that the storm comes from far off, and that the person in front of you, today, is paying a bill they did not open.

**Fourth: ask whose voice this is.** This is the deepest step, and sometimes the most chilling. Listen to the sentence the mind repeats, the alarm the body sets off, and ask: whose voice is this? Often you will find, with a shiver, that the voice is not yours. It is a tired mother's, a demanding father's, a grandmother's who lived in fright, someone who spoke first and whom you went on repeating inside for decades, doing their work for them. When you recognise whose it is, in silence, give the sentence back. You do not have to fight it or hate it. Only to notice it was a loan, and that you no longer have to keep paying it off for them. I can honour those who came before without inheriting their alarm. There is here a freedom few expect: the moment you recognise that the voice is not yours, it loses almost all its force. A sentence of ours seems absolute truth, law. A sentence we realise belongs to someone else becomes only that, someone's opinion, said in another time, under other pains, and which we do not have to keep taking as our own. You do not fight the voice. You simply stop confusing it with yourself, and in ceasing to confuse it with yourself, you cease to obey it.

**Fifth: breathe, and return to the now.** Finally, return to the only place that actually exists, which is this one. Breathe three times, slowly, and in each breath feel who it is that breathes. Then choose a single present, small thing, the warmth of the cup in your hands, the light on a wall, the air coming in and out of you without your having to ask for it, and linger on it for the length of three breaths. This is not flight, it is return. The past happened once, the future may never happen, and you were living in both except the only one you have. Returning to the now is returning home. And notice: the now, almost always, is bearable. It is the imagined future and the chewed-over past that are unbearable. In this very instant, with the cup in your hands, there is rarely anything happening that you cannot get through. Suffering lives almost entirely outside the present. Returning to the now is, very often, discovering that you are well, here, even in the middle of a difficult life.

I want to say a word about the order of these five steps, because it is not arbitrary. The first two, naming and stepping onto the bank, take you out from inside the water: they are urgent, they are always done, and sometimes they are enough. The two in the middle, asking what time it belongs to and whose voice it is, are the deeper work, the one that undoes the storm at the root rather than just enduring it: you do not always have the calm to do them at the peak of a crisis, and that is all right, you can do them later, cool, in the night practice. The last, breathing and returning, closes the gesture and gives you back to life. It is not a rigid staircase. It is a path the body learns, and which, with time, no longer needs numbered steps, because it becomes a single fluid thing, almost instant: see, and be back on the bank.

Five steps: name it, step onto the bank, ask what time it belongs to, ask whose voice it is, breathe and return. It fits in a minute. You do not have to do them all every time. There are small crises that resolve with the first alone, the "I am noticing". There are nights when you will need all five. And there are days when you will forget every one and only realise hours later. That is all right, that is the path, not a detour from it. Each time you notice, however late, the noticing comes a little earlier the next time. And one day, without warning, you see the storm approaching before it catches you, and you can, for the first time, choose the bank before falling into the water.

Let me show you the protocol working in a whole scene, one of those life brings, so that you see it in motion and not only in steps. Imagine you have sent a message to someone you care about, and the hours pass with no reply. You feel the tightening rise in your chest, the old story assembling itself: that is it, they are going to leave me, it was just like always. The mind shoots forward, rehearsing the break-up conversation that may never happen, and at the same time reaches backward, gathering proof of every other time you felt like this. You are in the middle of the water, carried off. First step: you stop, and say inside, "I am noticing that I am making a whole film out of a silence". Just this, and there is already a millimetre of air. Second step: you take a step back, and instead of being inside the tightening, you stay watching it, like someone watching a wave pass in front of them. Third step: you ask what time this fear belongs to, and you realise, clearly, that it is not from this message, it is from a very old waiting room, from a girl who waited and was not collected. Fourth step: you ask whose voice it is that says "they will always leave you", and you hear, with a shiver, that it is not yours, it is a sadness that was not born in you. Fifth step: you breathe three times, you rest a hand on your chest, and you return to what is really here, to the light on the wall, to the ground beneath your feet. The message is still unanswered. But you are no longer in the water. You are on the bank, watching the fear pass, and when the reply comes, an hour from now, with a "sorry, I was in a meeting", you will already be whole to receive it.

Notice an important thing in this example: nothing out there changed. The message was not answered any faster because you did the protocol. What changed was only your place in relation to the water. And that is all you will ever be able to change, and it is enough. You do not have to control the world, nor the past, nor the future, to stop drowning. You only have to learn to step out of the water. The bank does not take your life out of your hands, it gives it back to you.

## The practices

The protocol is for moments of crisis, for the middle of the day. These practices are for training, for the calm moments in which you teach the body, slowly, the way to the bank, so that it is there, available, when the water rises. Do one at a time. Do not stack them. The body learns slowly, and it is precisely the body, and not the mind, that needs to learn what is said here.

**Practice one · The river from the bank.** Sit for a few minutes, alone, in a calm moment. Close your eyes if you like. Instead of trying not to think, do the opposite: let the thoughts come, and imagine each one as a leaf that falls onto the water of a river and passes, before you, carried by the current. You are sitting on the bank. You do not leap after any leaf, you do not hold them, you do not push them away. You watch each one arrive, pass, and go. When you catch yourself inside the water, carried off by a thought, and you will, notice it without scolding yourself, and return calmly to the bank. That returning, repeated, is the whole practice. You are not learning to stop the river. You are learning to stay on the bank.

**Practice two · What time it belongs to.** Choose a moment of the day when you felt a strong, disproportionate emotion, one of those that caught you by surprise by its intensity. At night, calmly, write the situation in one line. Then ask the paper: how old was I, the first time I felt exactly this? Do not think too much, write the first number that comes. Almost always a small number comes. Then write: what did the girl of that age need to hear, in that moment, and never heard? And say it to her now, inside. This practice shows you, time after time, that much of today's storm is an old child asking for what she lacked, and not the present being the monster it seemed.

**Practice three · Whose voice this is.** Choose a sentence you have said about yourself for years, one of those that begin with "I never" or "I always". Write it on a piece of paper. Read it aloud, slowly, and then ask only this, without rushing to answer: whose voice is this? Stay with the question as long as it takes. When you recognise whose it is, a mother's, a father's, a teacher's, a grandmother's, in silence, give the sentence back, with respect and without resentment: this was yours, I honour what you lived, and from me on I can set down what I no longer need to carry. Repeat with a different sentence on another day. You will discover you carried, inside, whole voices of people who no longer even remember having said them.

**Practice four · One thing at a time.** This is the simplest and the most underestimated. Choose, each day, one small task, washing the dishes, walking from the car to the house, drinking your tea, and do that alone, without your phone, without planning the rest, without reliving anything. Only that, only that one thing, with all your attention. The warm dishes, the water, the sound. When the mind flees forward or backward, and it will flee many times, bring it back to the gesture, gently, without scolding yourself. You are teaching the mind that there is a now, and that it is enough. It is the humblest training of the bank, and perhaps the one that most changes the days.

**Practice five · The night you do not let be stolen.** This is for the whirlwind in bed, that hour when the body finally stops and the mind uses the silence to dump everything. When you catch yourself awake, chewing over or rehearsing, do not fight the thoughts, which is only giving them more rope. Instead, take your attention to the body, and not to the head. Feel the weight of your limbs on the mattress, the air coming in and out, some point where the body touches the bed. Each time the head pulls you back to the wheel, and it will, return, gently, to the weight and the breath. You are not trying to fall asleep, because trying to fall asleep is more whirlwind. You are only coming down from the head to the body, from the water to the bank. Often sleep comes as a by-product, when you stop chasing it. But even if it does not come, you have won the night all the same, because you spent it on the bank and not being carried off.

**Practice six · The minute of the veil.** I offer you, finally, a single habit to fix all of this, and I call it the minute of the veil. Once a day, at night perhaps, stop for a minute and ask: today, where did my mind take me out of the now? Did it go backward, chewing over, or forward, rehearsing? Not to correct yourself, only to see. Name what happened, thank the part of you that was only trying to protect you, and breathe three times back to the present. One minute a day. It is enough that, after weeks, you begin to catch the storm in the act, earlier and earlier, until you see it coming before it catches you. One minute a day changes a life, not because it is much, but because it is every day.

There is a request I make about these practices, and it is important: do the gesture, even if it seems too small, even if your head says it is not worth it, that it is too simple to change anything at all. It is precisely the body, and not the head, that needs to learn what is said here, and the body learns slowly, with repetition, not with understanding. You can understand everything in this book in an afternoon, and it changes nothing. What changes you is doing, day after day, the small gesture of stepping out of the water and returning to the bank, until the body knows it by heart and does it almost on its own. The difference between the women this frees and those who stay exactly the same is not intelligence, nor the depth of what they understood. It is only one thing: some do the gesture, every day, and others stay thinking about it. Be one of those who do.

A word about how to use these practices, because the form too is part of it. Do not do them all at once, nor turn them into one more list to complete and where you can fail, which would be effort sliding into the very path of healing. Choose one, live with it a few days, let the body learn it, and only then move to the next. And if one day you do none of them, do not punish yourself. Harshness with yourself is, itself, one more whirlwind, perhaps the one that clothes all the others. Treat yourself as you would treat your dearest friend learning this slowly. With her, you would have infinite patience. Be that friend to yourself, and the rest becomes possible.

## Seven days of seeing

Here is a short crossing, of seven days, one movement a day, for the body to begin to know the bank from inside and not only as an idea. It is not a test, it is not a rigid programme, and no delay is possible. Do each day calmly, and if a day asks for more time, stay in it. A few minutes a day are enough. Remember, on each one, the golden rule: first see, without judging yourself. The release comes after, and it comes on its own.

Each day trains, on purpose, one of the steps of the protocol, so that, by the end of the week, you have them all in the body and can join them in a single gesture. Do not worry about doing it all well. There is no doing this well, there is no mark. If one day you forget the exercise entirely and only remember at night, that remembering is already the path. And if there is a day when the water is too high and you cannot reach the bank, it is not failure, it is only a day of storm, and a whole spring is not erased by one storm. Return the next day. What matters is not the perfection of the crossing, it is beginning to know, with the body, that there is always a bank, and that you know the way back to it.

**Day 1 · Noticing that you notice.** Today, only today, your single task is to catch yourself thinking. Several times, throughout the day, when you can, stop and say inside: "I am noticing that I am thinking." Change nothing, stop nothing, only notice. You are training the first step of the protocol, the one that takes you out from inside the wave. At the end of the day, notice only this: to be able to notice is already to be, for an instant, on the bank.

**Day 2 · Past or future.** Today, each time you catch the mind racing, ask it a single question: are you backward or forward? Are you chewing over what was, or rehearsing what is coming? You do not have to do anything else, only label it: "past", "future". You will discover, with some surprise, how few times the mind is actually here. And you will begin to see the two faces of the same storm.

**Day 3 · The bank.** Today you train the step onto the bank. Once, in a calm moment, do the river practice: sit for a few minutes and let the thoughts pass like leaves on the water, without leaping after any. And throughout the day, when a wave catches you, remember: I can watch it from the bank instead of being in it. Take a step back, inwardly, and watch the water pass.

**Day 4 · What time it belongs to.** Today, choose a strong emotion that appears, and instead of living it at a run, ask it what time it belongs to. Does it belong to now, or is it an old pain this woke? How old was I, the first time I felt this? You do not need a perfect answer. The question, on its own, opens space between you and the wave, and takes the present out of a guilt that was not its own.

**Day 5 · Whose voice it is.** Today, listen to a harsh sentence you say about yourself, and ask whose voice it is. If you recognise whose it is, in silence, give it back, without resentment. Honour those who came before, and set down what you do not need to keep carrying for them. Go through the day lighter by a weight that, after all, was never yours.

**Day 6 · One thing at a time.** Today, choose a small task and do it with all your attention, only that, without your phone, without planning, without reliving. And choose a small, present pleasure, give it to yourself, and linger on it for three breaths, as if for the first time. Today you train the return to the now, the fifth step of the protocol. Notice how rare it is, and how rich, to be whole in a single moment.

**Day 7 · The whole crossing.** Today you put it all together. Whenever something tightens you, do the pocket protocol, even in fits and starts: name it, step onto the bank, ask what time it belongs to, ask whose voice it is, breathe and return. It does not need to come out perfect. At night, write yourself a short sentence, to read in a few months, about what has changed in you over these seven days. Not as a conclusion. As a seed.

Seven days do not lift this veil forever, and that was never the promise. What they do is teach the body a new path, that of stepping out of the water and returning to the bank, so that it is there, available, the next time the storm rises. And it will rise, many times, because this is a spiral and not a straight line: you pass several times through the same point, but never at the same height. When you return to an old pattern, return also to these seven days, to the day you need, without shame and without haste. There is no relapse that is failure here, there is only the water rising again and you, faster and faster, remembering the way back to the bank.

## What changes

You will not wake one day with the mind silent forever, and I want to tell you this clearly, so that you do not feel a failure for not arriving at a place that does not exist. The mind will keep producing thoughts, it is its function. What changes is not the quantity of water in the river. It is that you discover the bank, and learn to return to it. You gain a centre.

You come to have a calm place from which you observe the storm without being the storm, and that changes everything in daily life. Decisions stop being made in panic and begin to come from a more serene place. You feel emotions with the same intensity, but they no longer drag you to the bottom, because you know they are waves and that you are the sea. You react to today's people for what they are, and not for what others did to you, because the memory has stopped painting the present with the paint of the past. You sleep better, because the mind learns, slowly, that not every thought deserves an emergency meeting at three in the morning. And you discover, perhaps for the first time, the taste of being simply present, without commentary, in a moment that does not need to be analysed in order to be lived.

There is also something that opens, deeper than all of these, and it is worth knowing about because it is the best guide for the rest of the way. As the noise lowers, there begins to be heard, beneath it, a voice that was always there and that the whirlwind drowned out. Not the voice of the head, with its calculations and its fears. Another, deeper and more serene, that is recognised even before it is understood. Some call it intuition, some call it the inner call, and the names matter little. It is the silent thread that orients you without effort, that knows what is yours and what is not, that points you the way when reason gets lost in circles. It does not compete with the mind nor shout louder. It speaks low, and you hear it only when the noise grows quiet. All the learning of this book was, at bottom, a way of lowering the storm enough for you to hear it again. And the more you listen to it, the less you need to chew over the past or rehearse the future in order to know where to go, because you come to be guided from within, and not pushed from without.

There also happens, very often, an unexpected reconciliation with those who came before. As you see that you inherited their alarm without choosing it, that the sorrow was your mother's and the fright was your grandmother's, you stop blaming them and begin to understand them, and in that understanding something is freed in you too. You grow lighter by a weight you carried without knowing it could be set down.

There is, too, a change that touches your relationships in a way you may not expect. When you stop reacting to the present with the paint of the past, the people in front of you come to be themselves, and not the ghosts you projected onto them. That tendency of yours to build stories out of a silence, to read abandonment into every delay, to defend yourself against attacks no one made, slowly comes undone, and the relationships breathe, because they stop carrying the weight of all the old relationships you put on top of them without meaning to. The people who love you will notice, even without being able to name it, that you have become easier to love, more present, less on guard. Not because you became someone else. Because you stopped living with them out of an old film, and began, at last, to be with whoever is really there.

You will notice, too, something almost physical: the body slows. The permanent vigilance, that underlying state of alert you may never have recognised as such because it was always there, begins to give way. You notice your shoulders have dropped, your breathing has grown deeper, you no longer wake in the middle of the night with your heart racing over a thought. It is not that your life suddenly grew calm. It is that you stopped living it all in emergency mode. The nervous system, which learned long ago to treat each day as a possible threat, learns, slowly and with repeated proof, that the danger has passed, and that it can, at last, rest the watch.

It is honest to tell you still another thing about this crossing, so that you do not take fright when it happens. The clarity that opens between you and the thoughts does not settle as a smooth rest forever. When the light touches the deeper layers, it does not reveal only serenity. It also exposes the shadows that were hidden in the spinning of the mind. Many women imagine that quieting the head brings constant calm, and soon realise that, in crossing the whirlwind, the wind also lifts the dry leaves of what was unresolved. The silence that seemed firm ground suddenly meets sleeping memories, old wounds. And that is not a sign that it went wrong. It is a sign that it went deep. The light did not come only to calm you, it came to show you what still binds you, and showing is the first gesture of any liberation. So, when the stillness brings to the surface what you did not expect, do not flee back to the noise. Stay on the bank. It is your own depth trusting you enough to let itself, at last, be seen.

That place you reach, the centre you gain, I call the bank. It is honest to tell you it is not a permanent rest, a smooth peace forever. There are days when the water rises high, and you will go back into it, and you will be tempted to think you have learned nothing. It is not true. The proof is that, this time, you noticed. Before you did not even realise, and now you watch yourself doing it, perhaps still in the middle of the gesture, perhaps already halfway to the bank. That seeing, earlier and earlier, is the progress, though it does not have the shape you expected. The bank is not the absence of storm. It is knowing where it is, and knowing how to return.

The change here does not have the dramatic shape you might expect, and I want to tell you so that you recognise it when it comes, because it comes disguised as smallness. You will not wake one day transformed, with the mind at peace forever. What you will notice, after months, is more subtle and more solid than that. You will notice that a night that once took your whole sleep now takes only half an hour. That an unanswered message, which once put you down the whole afternoon, now gives you a tightening that passes in minutes. That you relived an old scene and, halfway through, noticed you were reliving it, and stepped out. They are small victories, almost invisible, and it is precisely because they are small and everyday that they are real. True transformation is not a lightning bolt, it is a tide rising so slowly that you only notice it when you realise the whole landscape has changed. Trust that tide, especially on the days when the sea seems still.

I want to answer, before we close, a few questions that always recur, because they may also be yours. I do the protocol and feel nothing special, am I doing it wrong? No, you are doing it well. The protocol is not for feeling anything special, it is for training the gaze. Often there are no fireworks, there is only a millimetre of space opening between you and the whirlwind, and that millimetre is not felt in the moment, it is realised after, when you notice you reacted differently. Do not chase experiences. Do the gesture, simple, and let the rest work in the dark.

I begin to look within and I feel worse, is that a bad sign? It can happen, and it is not a bad sign, it is a sign of depth. When a layer lifts, what was underneath comes to the surface, and sometimes what was underneath was sleeping pain. Go slowly, do not dig everything up at once. And if the pain is very great, do not cross it alone. This book is a path of consciousness, not a treatment, and there are wounds that ask for professional hands. To seek that help is not weakness, it is wisdom, it is, itself, a gesture of seeing and of caring. Use this book as a companion, never as your only crutch.

Do I have to believe in something spiritual for this to work? You do not have to believe in anything. I do not ask you for faith, I ask you for attention. The bank works whether you call it presence, consciousness, soul, or simply the part of you that observes. Keep the language that is true for you and ignore the rest.

But what if I keep falling into the water, time after time? You will fall, I promise you, and that is not failure. The difference is that, now, you will fall seeing. Before you did not even realise you were in the current, and now you notice, and the noticing, earlier and earlier, is the whole path. There is no advanced pupil in this school. There are only women walking, each at her own pace, seeing and releasing what they can on the day they have. Be patient with yourself, and be on your own side. You are undoing, with tenderness, what took decades and generations to weave.

## To close

You have reached the bank, at least for instants, and perhaps you already know, in the body and not only as an idea, that it exists and that it is yours. You have learned the movement of seeing: stepping out from inside the water, watching the storm pass, asking what time it belongs to and whose voice it is, and returning to the only place that actually exists, which is this one. It is a simple gesture, and you will repeat it your whole life, because the water does not stop running, and that was never the promise. Freedom is not the river drying up. It is knowing there is a bank, and returning to it whenever you find yourself in the current again.

But this is only the first movement of three. Seeing is where everything begins, because nothing is released until it is seen, and consciousness is the door to the whole crossing. After seeing comes **returning**, the movement of homecoming, of setting down what you carried without knowing, of giving back to those who came before what was theirs and making peace with your own story. And at last comes **living**, the movement of inhabiting the whole of life, present, without the glass, at home inside yourself and in the world. See, return, live. Three movements, one single crossing, the crossing back to who you always were beneath everything you learned to be.

If you want the whole map, the seven veils that interweave and join hands, the complete web of all that makes us repeat, it is in *The Seven Veils*, the mother-manual from which these companion books were born. Here you have been left with one, the loudest, the one that stole your now from both ends. You have seen it. And the next time the storm comes, you already know what to do. Do not run to the solution, do not demand of yourself that you silence the mind by force. Do only what you have learned, slowly, with a hand on your chest if it helps: notice, go to the bank, and watch the water pass.

I will not wish you to lift this veil forever, nor to arrive at a definitively peaceful mind, because that would be giving you back to the race, to the promise of an arrival that does not exist. I wish you something else, smaller and truer: that, the next time you catch yourself chewing over or rehearsing, instead of the old tightening and the old shame, you feel an almost tenderness for yourself, and think, with a tired smile, "ah, here you are again, my old mind, forever trying to protect me". And that you notice. And that you go to the bank. And that you be, above all, on your own side.

The storm will return, and you will step out of it again, and in that there is no failure at all, there is only a life being lived awake, which is the one thing I have ever asked of you. Return to this book when you need it. It stays here, patient, like a friend who is in no hurry.

You do not walk alone. And there is, beneath every storm you have yet to cross, a sky that no cloud has ever torn, and which has waited for you all this time, quiet, whole, waiting for you on the bank.

With all my care,
Vivianne
